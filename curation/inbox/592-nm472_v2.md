## Summary
This study developed and validated the **Japanese version of the Sensory Experience Questionnaire (SEQ) 3.0**, a caregiver-report measure of sensory features in **autism spectrum disorder (ASD)**. The SEQ 3.0 captures **sensory experiences** across multiple modalities that are clinically important in autism but often poorly measured by standard instruments. The validation involved both autistic and non-autistic populations to examine **measurement invariance** and structural differences. Using **confirmatory factor analysis (CFA)** and related psychometric methods, the study evaluated the factor structure, reliability, and validity of the Japanese translation. Results demonstrated that the Japanese SEQ 3.0 maintains the **core factor structure** of the original English version while revealing some **structural differences** between autistic and non-autistic populations — suggesting that sensory experiences are organized differently in autistic individuals. The study contributes a much-needed **culturally validated instrument** for assessing sensory features in Japanese clinical and research contexts, where gold-standard tools for sensory assessment in autism have been lacking.

## Clinical Insight
Sensory sensitivities and atypical sensory processing are among the most prevalent and impairing features of autism, affecting up to 90% of autistic individuals, yet clinicians have lacked validated instruments in many languages including Japanese. The SEQ 3.0 Japanese version provides a practical tool for **clinical sensory profiling** that can inform **occupational therapy planning**, **environmental accommodations**, and **individualized support strategies**. The finding of **structural differences** between autistic and non-autistic populations is theoretically important — it suggests that sensory experiences are not simply more intense in autism but are **qualitatively different in their organization**, which has implications for how clinicians interpret sensory assessment results. The caregiver-report format makes it accessible for use with children and non-speaking individuals. Clinicians working with Japanese-speaking autistic populations now have a validated alternative to informal sensory questioning.
