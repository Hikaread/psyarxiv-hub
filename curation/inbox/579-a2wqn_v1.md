## Summary

This theoretical paper, situated within **critical psychology**, identifies a recurring clinical phenomenon termed the **Masking Paradox**: people who become **less anxious** and more composed through therapy or wellbeing interventions while remaining **disconnected from others**. The author argues this pattern cannot be resolved within frameworks that locate the problem inside the individual. Drawing on **critical psychology**, **capability theory** (Nussbaum, Sen), and **recognition theory** (Honneth), the paper makes two linked conceptual moves. First, it reframes **non-transactional connection** as a contested **social capability** rather than an internal disposition or skill. Second, it introduces the concept of **prosthetic capability**: institutional and relational scaffolding that makes connection achievable for subjects whose formation under capitalist conditions has rendered it precarious. **Nonviolent Communication (NVC)** is examined as a worked example, including its potential to reproduce the Masking Paradox when taught as an individual skill without addressing structural conditions. The paper argues that contemporary mental health discourse increasingly converges on the **calm, self-managing subject** as an unquestioned ideal, inadvertently producing alienated wellbeing. ## Clinical Insight

For therapists, the **Masking Paradox** names a familiar clinical pattern: clients who learn to regulate their emotions effectively — becoming calm, composed, and self-managing — while reporting persistent feelings of emptiness, disconnection, or inauthenticity in their relationships. This is particularly relevant for **ACT** practitioners who may observe clients achieving strong **cognitive defusion** and **acceptance** skills while struggling with **self-as-context** and genuine interpersonal connection. The paper's concept of **prosthetic capability** suggests that therapists should look beyond individual skill-building to consider the **relational and institutional scaffolding** their clients need. For example, a client who has learned to self-soothe through mindfulness may still lack the social conditions — safe relationships, non-transactional spaces, institutional support — that make genuine connection possible. The critique of NVC is particularly incisive: teaching communication skills as individual techniques without addressing power dynamics and structural barriers may leave clients more skilled at appearing calm while remaining fundamentally alienated. For **clinical supervisors**, this paper challenges the tendency to equate symptom reduction with therapeutic success and encourages a broader view of wellbeing that includes relational authenticity.
