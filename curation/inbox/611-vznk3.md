## Summary

This conceptual paper proposes an integrative **process model** linking trauma exposure to worker outcomes across **trauma-exposed occupations** including emergency services, healthcare, social work, child protection, and psychotherapy. The model differentiates **extraordinary trauma events** (DSM-5 A1/A2/A3) from ordinary **task-related exposure** (DSM-5 A4) and introduces **trauma dose** — comprising type, duration, density, and intensity — as a distinct stressor category. Four interdependent mechanisms connect exposure to trauma sequelae including **PTSD**, **complex PTSD**, **burnout**, and **empathic distress**: (1) **autonomous stress responses** mediated by biological stress physiology; (2) **controlled emotion regulation** and self-regulation drawing on Gross's extended process model; (3) **cumulative resource dynamics** governed by **Conservation of Resources (COR) theory**, featuring both loss spirals and gain spirals; and (4) **emotional labour climate** as the "cause of the cause" — the organisational policies and practices that build resources or fail to buffer exposure. The model specifies 17 causal paths and yields four testable propositions. It integrates five major theoretical traditions: clinical stress and trauma models, emotional labour theory, COR theory, the **Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) framework**, and work design theory. Drawing on cited evidence from over 272,000 police personnel showing a pooled **PTSD prevalence of 14.2%** (Syed et al., 2020), and UK data showing 47.7% of PTSD-positive police staff also meeting criteria for depression and anxiety (Stevelink et al., 2020), the paper argues that the strongest leverage for reducing trauma sequelae lies in organisational mechanisms rather than individual resilience training. Four organisational leverage points are identified: (1) selection and self-selection through realistic job previews; (2) work design that reduces regulation load through autonomy and task coherence; (3) recovery protection including psychological detachment and normalised help-seeking; and (4) making emotional labour climate visible through concrete, observable practices rather than aspirational statements.

## Clinical Insight

Clinicians working with trauma-exposed professionals — including fellow mental health practitioners — will find this model directly applicable to both case formulation and occupational wellbeing interventions. The distinction between **extraordinary** and **task-related** trauma exposure addresses a long-standing recognition gap in **DSM-5 Criterion A**, where workers' compensation and occupational health practice have struggled to validate ordinary task-related confrontation as qualifying trauma exposure. The model's emphasis on **loss-spiral prevention** as the primary leverage point, consistent with COR theory's primacy of loss (Hobfoll et al., 2018), has direct implications for therapeutic approaches: clinicians should prioritise preventing resource depletion before it cascades, rather than focusing solely on building new resources. The paper's integration of **emotional labour theory** — particularly the novel distinction between **emotion-feeling-rule dissonance** (felt vs. to-be-felt emotion) and classical emotion-display-rule dissonance — offers clinicians a more nuanced framework for understanding the inner emotional conflicts experienced by trauma workers. The **i-frame vs. s-frame distinction** (Chater and Loewenstein, 2023) is critical: i-frame solutions such as resilience training and mindfulness apps alone cannot offset structural exposure conditions, reinforcing that clinicians advocating for trauma-exposed workers should also engage with organisational and systemic change. The concept of **trauma dose** as a measurable composite of type, duration, density, and intensity provides a framework for clinicians to assess cumulative risk more precisely than event-counting alone.

## Relevant For

- Clinical psychologists treating trauma-exposed professionals (police, firefighters, healthcare workers, therapists)
- Occupational health psychologists designing wellbeing interventions
- Supervisors and managers in trauma-exposed organisations
- Researchers studying PTSD, complex PTSD, and burnout in high-risk occupations
