## Summary

The FOCUS study protocol describes a design to map cognitive functioning in forensic outpatients (n=100) compared to a matched normative group (n=100). Participants complete a comprehensive neuropsychological battery covering seven domains: intelligence, attention, language, executive functioning, social cognition, memory, and decision making. Aggression is assessed via self-report (RPQ) and behavioral task (CRTT). The study aims to: (1) compare cognitive profiles between forensic outpatients and matched controls, (2) examine correspondence between self-reported and task-based aggression, (3) examine associations between neuropsychological scores and aggression, and (4) examine associations between neuropsychological scores and treatment outcomes including recidivism risk, treatment non-attendance, dropout, and actual recidivism.

## Clinical Insight

This protocol is directly relevant for therapists working in forensic settings. It applies the Risk-Need-Responsivity (RNR) framework by examining how cognitive functioning maps onto treatment-relevant outcomes like dropout and recidivism. If cognitive deficits in specific domains predict treatment dropout or non-response, therapists could use cognitive screening to identify clients who need adapted treatment approaches. The comprehensive multi-domain assessment approach also models thorough cognitive case formulation in forensic populations, going beyond single-test evaluations.

## Relevant For

- Forensic and correctional therapists
- Clinicians conducting cognitive assessments in forensic settings
- Therapists working with antisocial behavior and aggression
- Clinical psychologists interested in the RNR framework and treatment planning
