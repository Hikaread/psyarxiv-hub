## Summary

This study examined how specific **listener engagement behaviors** (nodding, gaze, smiling) influence speakers' **autonomic arousal** during dyadic **emotional self-disclosure** in **N = 160 participants** (data collected 2023–2024). Dyads interacted either **face-to-face** or via **video call**, with listeners either fully attentive or distracted by a **mental arithmetic task**. Engagement behaviors were tracked alongside speakers' **autonomic arousal** (ECG-derived) at both encounter-level and moment-to-moment timescales.

Attentional disengagement significantly reduced all three engagement behaviors. **Nodding** decreased under disengagement in both media (face-to-face d = 0.21, p = .010; video call d = 0.24, p = .003). **Gaze dispersion** increased dramatically under disengagement (face-to-face d = -0.82, p < .001; video call d = -1.09, p < .001), with a significant medium interaction (ηp² = .05). **Smiling** decreased under disengagement in video calls (d = 0.36, p < .001) but not face-to-face (p = .266). At the encounter level, listener nodding during disengagement showed a trend toward reducing speakers' arousal (β = -0.081, p = .081). At the moment-to-moment level, **nodding, gaze diversion, and smiling all transiently increased speakers' arousal**. The effect of **gaze diversion** on arousal was **weaker in video calls**, while the effect of **smiling was stronger under full attention**.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists, these findings have direct implications for understanding how nonverbal behavior shapes the therapeutic encounter. The dual timescale finding—that engagement behaviors **transiently increase** arousal moment-by-moment while active nodding during disengagement may **reduce** overall arousal—suggests that listener behaviors serve complex regulatory functions. In therapy, a therapist's **gaze and nodding** patterns may directly modulate a client's physiological state during disclosure. The finding that **video calls attenuate the impact of gaze diversion** on speaker arousal helps explain why teletherapy can feel different: some nonverbal channels (smiling) remain effective while others (gaze) are dampened. Therapists conducting teletherapy should be particularly intentional about **nodding and smiling** as compensatory engagement signals when gaze cues are less effective.

## Methodology Note

This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 160 participants in dyadic design provides 80 dyads.
The moment-to-moment analysis benefits from the high temporal resolution of continuous behavioral and physiological measurement.
For the encounter-level analysis, 80 dyads with 2 conditions × 2 media = 4 cells provides modest power for detecting small effects (the nodding effect trended at p = .081).

__Preregistration__: Not explicitly mentioned in the available text.

__Effect Sizes__: Attention effect on gaze dispersion: ηp² = .65 (very large).
Attention effect on nodding: ηp² = .09 (small-to-medium).
Attention effect on smiling: ηp² = .10 (small-to-medium).
Medium × attention interaction on gaze: ηp² = .05.
Within-group effect sizes for gaze: d = -0.82 to -1.09 (large).
Nodding: d = 0.21–0.24 (small).
Smiling: d = 0.36 (small-to-medium) in video calls.

__Statistical Rigor__: Appropriate use of **linear mixed-effects models** for the nested data structure (observations within encounters within dyads).
**FDR correction** was applied to pairwise comparisons.
Both within-person and between-person effects were examined.
The dual-timescale analysis (encounter-level and moment-to-moment) is a methodological strength.
The moment-to-moment analysis appropriately models temporal contingencies between listener behavior and speaker arousal.

__Measurement Validity__: **Autonomic arousal** was measured via ECG (heart rate-derived).
Engagement behaviors were measured via video coding (nodding, gaze orientation/dispersion, smiling).
The mental arithmetic distraction task was validated: listening difficulty was significantly higher during disengagement (p < .001, ηp² = .61), with participants completing a mean of 8 operations per subtask.
The emotional self-disclosure task provides ecological validity for studying sensitive interpersonal communication.

__Open Science__: Data and analysis code availability are not explicitly stated.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 160 participants, data collected 2023–2024 at the University of Helsinki.
Dyadic design with both face-to-face and video call conditions.
Specific demographic characteristics are not available in the reviewed text.

## Notes

The finding that moment-to-moment listener behaviors transiently increase speaker arousal while overall nodding during disengagement may reduce it suggests that engagement behaviors have both activating (immediate social cue) and regulating (sustained supportive presence) functions. This distinction may be important for understanding therapeutic process.
