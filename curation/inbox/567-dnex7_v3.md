## Summary

This companion paper to the mindfulness ceiling analysis (see p8e75_v3) provides a more detailed structural account of the **manas layer** — the Yogacara concept of a background self-grasping process that persists beneath conscious awareness. The paper reconstructs the classical **eight-consciousness doctrine** as a five-layer mental architecture: **L1** (sensation-perception), **L2** (conceptual-reasoning), **L3** (background self-grasping), **L4** (latent-structural), and **L5** (post-transformation non-grasping). This model is brought into systematic dialogue with contemporary self models, mindfulness mechanisms, and psychotherapy frameworks. The central argument is that standard **MBIs** operate primarily at L1-L2 (enhancing attentional and metacognitive skills) while leaving L3 self-grasping largely untouched. The paper outlines **testable implications** for nonattachment measures, intervention design targeting L3 processes, and theoretical integration with **acceptance-based therapies**. Full text extracted from a 43-page PDF. The paper is noted as submitted to Mindfulness (Springer) and currently under peer review.

## Clinical Insight

Where the companion paper diagnoses the problem (the mindfulness ceiling), this paper provides a constructive framework for addressing it within therapeutic practice. The **five-layer mental architecture** offers clinicians a more granular model for understanding why clients may show improvement in **attention regulation** and **emotional awareness** while continuing to experience **self-critical rumination**, **shame**, or **identity-based suffering**. For therapists using **third-wave CBT approaches**, the L3 (background self-grasping) concept helps explain the clinical observation that insight alone is often insufficient for lasting change — clients can understand their patterns intellectually while remaining structurally identified with them. The paper's dialogue with contemporary self-models connects the Yogacara framework to measurable constructs like **self-referential processing**, **interoceptive prediction errors**, and **default mode network** activity, providing potential neurocognitive biomarkers for tracking therapeutic change at this deeper level. The **testable implications** for intervention design are particularly valuable: therapists could explore practices that specifically target the transition from L3 to L4-L5, such as **radical acceptance** exercises, **loving-kindness meditation** directed at the sense of self, or **experiential exercises** that reveal the constructed nature of self-narratives — approaches already present in **ACT**, **CFT**, and advanced **MBSR** but potentially undertheorized.
