## Summary

This study examines the **convergent validity** of coding **psychological inflexibility** in **written emotional disclosure (WED)** narratives and its associations with **posttraumatic stress (PTS)** symptomology in a community sample of **N = 415** participants. Grounded in **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)** theory, the research investigates whether linguistic markers of psychological inflexibility extracted from expressive writing samples correspond to established self-report measures and predict trauma-related outcomes beyond what self-reports alone can explain. Results confirmed **negative associations** between coded written psychological inflexibility and self-reported psychological flexibility, alongside **positive associations** with PTS symptoms and **anxiety-related distress and impairment**. Critically, written psychological inflexibility predicted PTS-related outcomes **over and above** the variance explained by self-report psychological flexibility, cumulative exposure to potentially traumatic events, and narrative word counts. This extends the small but growing literature validating the utility of evaluating **psychological inflexibility** features in natural language, demonstrating that written language analysis can capture clinically meaningful information about trauma-related processes that standardized questionnaires may miss.

## Clinical Insight

The finding that **written psychological inflexibility** accounts for unique variance in **PTS symptomology** beyond self-report measures has significant implications for clinical assessment and case formulation. Clinicians working with trauma survivors often rely on self-report instruments such as the **Acceptance and Action Questionnaire-II (AAQ-II)**, yet these may be subject to response biases or limited introspective awareness. The ability to code psychological inflexibility from **expressive writing samples** offers a complementary, implicit assessment pathway that could enhance case conceptualization within ACT and other mindfulness-based approaches. The WED paradigm itself has established efficacy in reducing PTS symptoms, and this study suggests that the **language people produce during disclosure exercises contains detectable markers of the same psychological processes** that maintain distress. For therapists using **narrative exposure** or **written disclosure** techniques, monitoring shifts in psychological flexibility markers across sessions could serve as a process measure of therapeutic change. Additionally, the study bridges assessment science and clinical practice by demonstrating that linguistic analysis of open-ended writing — a low-burden, easily administered format — can provide incremental predictive validity for trauma outcomes, potentially enabling earlier identification of individuals at risk for chronic PTSD who might otherwise be missed by standard screening tools.

## Relevant For
- Trauma therapists using ACT or expressive writing interventions
- Clinical psychologists assessing posttraumatic stress symptomology
- Researchers studying psychological inflexibility as a transdiagnostic process
- Assessment developers seeking implicit or language-based clinical measures
