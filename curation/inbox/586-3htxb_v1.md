## Summary
This narrative review synthesises structural and functional **MRI correlates of loneliness**, a subjective experience of perceived social disconnection that is robustly associated with poor mental and physical health across the lifespan. Structural MRI studies, including large-scale UK Biobank analyses (~40,000 participants), implicate subtle **gray matter volume** variation within the **default mode network (DMN)**, **medial temporal structures** (amygdala, hippocampus, parahippocampal gyrus), and **prefrontal cortex**. Associations are **heterogeneous** and context-dependent, varying by sex, depression history, and whether loneliness co-occurs with social isolation. Functional MRI studies reveal that lonely individuals show heightened neural responses to **negative social stimuli** (insula, basal ganglia, orbitofrontal cortex) and attenuated responses to **positive social stimuli** (ventromedial prefrontal cortex, ventral striatum). **Resting-state functional connectivity** differences consistently involve the DMN, with loneliness associated with altered connectivity between medial prefrontal and **temporoparietal junction** regions. The review identifies critical limitations: over-reliance on single-item measures, cross-sectional designs restricting causal inference, concentration on older adults, and insufficient integration with **genetic** and **environmental** perspectives.

## Clinical Insight
Loneliness is increasingly recognised as a clinical risk factor, with meta-analytic evidence linking it to depression, anxiety, cognitive decline, and mortality. For mental health clinicians, understanding its neural substrates informs both assessment and intervention. The consistent involvement of the **default mode network** suggests loneliness may reflect altered self-referential processing and internally directed thought — processes central to rumination and depressive cognition. The dissociation between loneliness (subjective) and social isolation (objective) is clinically important: a patient may be socially integrated yet feel profoundly lonely, or may be socially isolated yet content. Neural findings suggest these represent partially distinct phenotypes, with the combination of both associated with accelerated **brain aging**. The review's call for **longitudinal developmental designs** is particularly relevant for clinical work with adolescents and young adults, among whom loneliness is rising most sharply. For therapists, these neural findings provide a scientific basis for psychoeducation about loneliness and may guide interventions targeting **social cognition**, **self-referential processing**, and **reward processing**.

## Relevant For
- Clinical psychologists treating depression and anxiety where loneliness is a maintaining factor
- Neuropsychologists interested in social cognition and brain-behaviour relationships
- Mental health researchers studying psychosocial risk factors
- Clinicians working with older adults at risk of cognitive decline
