## Summary

This well-powered RCT (N=716 meditation-naive adults) examined dose-response effects of daily mindfulness meditation across four assigned durations (4, 10, 20, or 30 minutes per day) over a 28-day online intervention. Primary and secondary outcomes included wellbeing, mindfulness-related constructs, and mental health symptoms. All groups showed significant improvements across outcomes (SMD = 0.29-0.56), but no dose-response effect was found: longer assigned durations did not produce greater benefits. Adherence declined steeply at higher doses, with only 48.5% completing two-thirds of sessions in the 30-minute group compared to 70.9% in the 10-minute group. In exploratory analyses, actual practice engagement, goal confidence, and time predicted outcomes regardless of assigned dose.

## Clinical Insight

These findings have direct implications for how therapists recommend mindfulness to clients. The common clinical assumption that more practice time equals more benefit is not supported here: within the first 30 days, 4-10 minutes of daily practice produced benefits comparable to 30 minutes, while higher doses led to poorer adherence. For clients who struggle to maintain a meditation practice, therapists can confidently recommend short sessions (5-10 minutes) without worrying that they are offering an insufficient dose. This is particularly relevant for clients with depression or anxiety who may find extended sitting meditation aversive or overwhelming. The strong role of goal confidence in predicting outcomes also suggests that therapists should invest time in building clients' belief that mindfulness will help them, rather than focusing on the duration of practice. The study population was non-clinical, so extrapolation to clinical groups requires caution, but the adherence finding is likely even more important in clinical populations where motivation is often lower.

## Relevant For

Therapists who incorporate mindfulness into their clinical work, whether as a standalone intervention or as part of broader protocols such as MBCT, ACT, or DBT
