## Summary

This computational modelling paper introduces the **ACU (Allocating Commitment under Uncertainty)** framework, which formalises intention formation as the allocation of **behavioural commitment** under uncertainty. An intention's effectiveness depends on three interacting components: (1) **subjective value** of the goal, (2) **context specificity** (gamma), and (3) **commitment precision** (m) — how selectively commitment is enforced by suppressing non-focal execution. The model was fit to five experiments from three published studies using **maximum likelihood estimation** and compared against three alternative models via **AIC/BIC**. The ACU model won best fit in **4 out of 5 experiments**. Key findings include: implementation intentions improved performance when planned cues were available but **impaired** performance when cues were omitted — only ACU captured this crossover interaction. With multiple goals (6 goals), implementation intentions lost their advantage and **reduced target accuracy** — only ACU reproduced this actual decrement (Experiment 3). After outcome revaluation, implementation intentions impaired performance across **all** value conditions, not just revalued ones — again only ACU captured this generalised impairment. Individual-level parameter estimates (Experiment 5) showed **context specificity for implementation intentions** (gamma = 2.08) was nearly double that for goal intentions (gamma = 1.07), and **commitment precision** (m = 3.70) was significantly greater than zero, confirming mutual suppression of concurrent intentions.

## Clinical Insight

This formal framework has direct relevance to **CBT** and **behavioural activation** practices where **implementation intentions** (if-then plans) are widely used to help patients translate therapeutic goals into action. The finding that implementation intentions can **backfire** — impairing performance when planned cues are absent, when too many goals compete, or when outcomes are revalued — provides a principled explanation for a common clinical observation: patients who form very specific if-then plans sometimes become rigid or fail to adapt when circumstances change. The identification of **commitment precision** as the core control process suggests a clinical analogue: patients who overcommit to specific plans (high m) may sacrifice behavioural flexibility, while those who undercommit preserve flexibility but lose efficiency. This maps onto clinical distinctions between **rigid** and **under-controlled** presentation styles. For therapists, the framework suggests that helping patients calibrate commitment precision — matching their intention strategies to the reliability of their environment and their own executive capacity — may be as important as helping them form specific plans. The model's prediction of a **non-monotonic effect of context specificity** (an initial dip in goal pursuit at low-to-moderate specificity) cautions against assuming that more specific plans are always better. The integration with **metacognitive calibration** theories (Nelson and Narens, 1990) suggests that therapists should attend to patients' metacognitive awareness of when their strategies are and are not working.
