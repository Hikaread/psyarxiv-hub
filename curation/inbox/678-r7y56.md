## Summary
This study (N = 161; 81 with **Social Anxiety Disorder**, 80 controls) examined attentional mechanisms linking **post-interaction state anxiety** to **24-hour post-event rumination** using eye-tracking after a **naturalistic dyadic social interaction**. Socially anxious participants showed a significant **attentional bias toward negative self-evaluation** (prolonged dwell time on how they believed their partner evaluated them negatively), an **exaggerated self-critical bias** (underestimating partner's actual evaluation by M = -4.95 vs. controls' M = -2.56, d = 0.52), and elevated post-event rumination. An associational variable analysis revealed that attentional bias to negative self-evaluation **partially accounted for** the association between state anxiety and 24-hour rumination in both groups, though the indirect association did not differ significantly between SAD and control participants. Data and code are available on OSF.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides empirical support for key mechanisms in the **cognitive-behavioral model of social anxiety** with improved ecological validity. The finding that socially anxious individuals show **preferential gaze toward negative self-evaluative information** within the first hour after a social interaction maps directly onto clinical observations of clients who **fixate on perceived social failures** after encounters. The exaggerated **self-critical bias** (d = 0.52) — underestimating how positively others see them — is a quantifiable version of the common clinical presentation. For therapists, this supports interventions that target **self-focused attention** and **negative self-appraisal** after social situations, such as **video feedback** to correct distorted self-imagery, **attention training** to redirect from negative self-evaluation, and **behavioral experiments** to test negative beliefs about social performance. The finding that the attention-rumination link operates in both groups suggests these processes exist on a continuum, supporting transdiagnostic approaches to rumination.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 161 (81 SAD, 80 controls) provides adequate power for the primary ANOVA analyses.
The moderated indirect association analysis may have been **underpowered for interaction effects**, which the authors acknowledge.
The study was not designed to be sufficiently powered for detecting group differences in the indirect pathway.

__Preregistration__: The study had a **pre-registered analysis plan** on OSF.
One deviation was noted: the pre-registered plan specified exclusion for any missing data, but the authors used **person-mean single imputation** for minimal missingness (3 participants, 4 instances).

__Effect Sizes__: The self-critical bias showed a **medium effect** (d = 0.52, p = .002).
The Group x AOI interaction for dwell time yielded **eta-squared partial = .03** (small-to-medium).
Attentional bias dwell time correlations with state anxiety and rumination were significant at **p < .001**.
Conditional indirect effects were reported with 95% bias-corrected CIs.

__Statistical Rigor__: Holm-Bonferroni corrections were applied for multiple comparisons.
Greenhouse-Geisser corrections were used when sphericity was violated.
A **transposed model** was tested to evaluate alternative variable orderings, which is methodologically commendable.
The authors appropriately frame the indirect association as **associational rather than causal**, following Weems (2025).

__Measurement Validity__: Trait social anxiety was assessed with **SIAS and SPS** (both Cronbach's alpha = .95), which is excellent.
Post-event rumination was measured with the **SARQ** (alpha = .94), selected for superior psychometric properties over alternatives.
State anxiety used the **STAI-S** (alpha = .94–.95).
Eye-tracking was conducted at 500 Hz with a validated EyeLink 1000 Plus system.
The performance evaluation task was custom-designed with no prior validation, which is a limitation.

__Open Science__: **Data and R code** are available on the OSF project page.
The preprint is openly available on **PsyArXiv**.
The study was **preregistered** on OSF.
This represents strong open science practices.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 161 (83 dyads, 3 excluded for unreliable gaze data).
81 participants met diagnostic criteria for **SAD**, 80 were healthy controls.
Symptom levels in the SAD group were mainly in the **moderate range**, limiting generalizability to severe presentations.
No demographic breakdown (age, gender, ethnicity) was available in the extracted text.
