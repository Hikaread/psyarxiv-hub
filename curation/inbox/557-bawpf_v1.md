## Summary
**PRISMA-guided systematic review** examining the bidirectional relationship between **pain** and **body ownership** through the **Rubber Hand Illusion** (RHI) paradigm. Following systematic searches of PsycINFO and PubMed, **30 experimental papers** were included and qualitatively synthesized (meta-analysis was pre-registered but abandoned due to substantial between-study heterogeneity in pain induction methods, outcome measures, and ownership operationalizations). The review identified a context-dependent relationship: when the embodied hand appeared **healthy** and **multisensory stimulation** was congruent, RHI was more frequently associated with **analgesic effects** (reduced pain intensity, increased pain threshold). Conversely, when the embodied hand appeared **injured**, **threatening**, or **sensitized**, embodiment tended to increase pain sensitivity or unpleasantness (**hyperalgesic effects**). **Null findings** were also common, suggesting that ownership induction alone is insufficient to reliably modulate pain. Importantly, the review found that RHI can be induced not only by innocuous touch but also by **painful or nociceptive stimulation**, and that pain itself can serve as an informative somatosensory signal that helps maintain body representations when tactile or proprioceptive input is reduced — challenging the view that pain solely disrupts body representation. Proposed mechanisms include **multisensory integration**, **affective-motivational**, and **predictive coding** processes.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have direct implications for treating conditions characterized by **body representation disturbances** and **chronic pain**, including **functional neurological disorder** (FND), **complex regional pain syndrome**, **phantom limb pain**, and **fibromyalgia**. The context-dependent nature of pain modulation by body ownership suggests that therapeutic approaches using **virtual reality embodiment** or **body-focused interventions** should carefully control the visual appearance of the embodied body part — presenting healthy, non-threatening body representations may produce analgesic effects, while exposing patients to injured or threatening body images could paradoxically increase pain. The finding that nociceptive input can support body representation challenges clinical approaches that assume pain is purely disruptive, suggesting instead that appropriate sensory feedback (including some pain signaling) may help maintain integrated body representations in conditions where sensory input is diminished. For clinicians using **VR-based exposure** or **graded motor imagery** for chronic pain, the review supports the use of congruent multisensory stimulation and positive body representations as active therapeutic ingredients rather than mere delivery vehicles.

## Relevant For
- Pain psychologists and physical therapists using VR or embodiment-based interventions
- Clinicians treating FND, CRPS, phantom limb pain, and fibromyalgia
- Researchers studying body representation in chronic pain conditions
- Therapists using graded motor imagery or virtual reality for pain management

## Notes
Systematic review of 30 papers. Meta-analysis planned but abandoned due to heterogeneity. Pain induction methods varied widely (thermal, mechanical, electrical, laser). Ownership measures also heterogeneous (questionnaire, proprioceptive drift, composite indices). Future studies need larger samples, clinical populations, and standardized measures.
