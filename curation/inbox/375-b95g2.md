## Summary

This study tests whether Big Five personality traits predict new-onset common mental disorders (CMDs) using biobank data and machine learning approaches. Personality traits are examined as risk predictors measurable long before clinical presentation, leveraging their temporal stability and conceptual continuity with psychopathology. The study investigates both broad traits and personality nuances (facets and items) to determine whether finer-grained personality assessment improves prediction of depression, anxiety, and related disorders.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists, this research supports the routine use of personality assessment as a screening and case formulation tool. If personality traits reliably predict mental disorder onset, therapists can use personality profiles to identify clients at elevated risk before clinical symptoms fully emerge, enabling earlier intervention and preventive strategies. The finding that personality nuances improve prediction suggests that brief personality measures in intake assessments could serve a dual purpose: informing case formulation while also flagging future risk. This is particularly relevant for therapists in primary care or screening settings who need efficient assessment tools.

## Relevant For

Therapists conducting intake assessments, those working in prevention and early intervention, clinicians using personality assessment in case formulation, therapists in integrated primary care settings.

## Notes

Biobank data provides large sample but may not represent clinical populations. Machine learning approach allows testing of incremental predictive value. Personality nuances outperforming broad traits has implications for assessment tool selection.
