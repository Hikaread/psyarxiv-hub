## Summary
This set of four studies (total N across studies = 3,227) compared the quality of cognitive reappraisals produced by GPT-4 versus trained human laypeople. In Study 1 (N=868), GPT-4 reappraisals of negative vignettes were consistently rated higher on effectiveness, empathy, and novelty by third-party raters compared to human reappraisals. Study 2 (N=386) replicated these findings in real-time interactions where recipients rated the reappraisals they received. Study 3 (N=1,477) demonstrated that incentivising humans with monetary bonuses increased time spent on reappraisals but did not close the quality gap with GPT-4, suggesting the difference is driven by skill rather than effort. Study 4 (N=496) found that labeling reappraisals as AI-generated reduced participants' evaluations of effectiveness, though GPT-4 was still rated higher than humans even when labeled. Language analysis revealed that GPT-4 used more lexically complex language and a distinct vocabulary profile; humans who used characteristic GPT-4 vocabulary produced higher-quality reappraisals, and including this vocabulary-origin interaction rendered the main effect of origin insignificant.

## Clinical Insight
These findings carry important implications for clinical practice as patients increasingly turn to AI for emotional support. Cognitive reappraisal is a core technique in CBT, DBT, and other evidence-based therapies, and this research shows that AI can produce reappraisals rated as more effective and empathic than those of laypeople. However, several cautions apply for clinical contexts. First, the comparison was with laypeople, not trained therapists, and the authors note that some humans outperformed GPT-4, suggesting skilled clinicians retain an advantage. Second, the label effect in Study 4 means that patients who know they are receiving AI-generated reappraisals may discount their value, a relevant consideration for any clinical tool that incorporates AI suggestions. Third, the linguistic analysis suggests that specific vocabulary choices drive perceived quality, which has implications for therapist training: teaching clinicians the specific language patterns associated with effective reappraisal could improve therapeutic communication. Finally, the authors emphasise that quality of a single response does not equate to the long-term relational support that characterises effective therapy, and privacy concerns remain significant when clients share emotional content with commercial AI systems.

## Relevant For
CBT practitioners interested in understanding AI capabilities and limitations for emotional support, therapists whose clients use AI chatbots, clinical researchers studying emotion regulation strategy delivery, and developers of AI-assisted therapeutic tools.
