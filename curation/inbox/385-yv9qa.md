## Summary
This study surveyed 58 university students in Sri Lanka (39 female, 19 male) aged 18-23 to investigate gender differences in academic stress and academic pressure. Using validated scales and SPSS analysis including correlation, t-tests, and ANOVA, the study found that 45% of participants experienced academic stress with a strong positive correlation between stress and pressure. No significant gender differences emerged, contradicting some Western findings. The main academic stressors were high workload, peer pressure, and worrying about grades.

## Clinical Insight
The strong correlation between academic stress and academic pressure suggests these constructs operate as a unified stress system for young adults. For therapists, this means that addressing one component (e.g., workload management) may not be sufficient without also addressing the perceived pressure to perform. In Sri Lankan and similar collectivist cultures where educational achievement carries significant family and social weight, academic stress may present differently than in individualistic Western contexts, potentially manifesting through somatic complaints, family conflict, or withdrawal rather than through explicit anxiety reports.

## Relevant For
Therapists working with university students, young adult mental health services, and culturally informed practice.

## Notes
Small sample size (N=58) with non-probability sampling limits generalisability. Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality. Cultural specificity of findings warrants caution when applying to other contexts.
