## Summary
This critical commentary re-analyses data from a meta-analysis by **Kekic, Rose, and Bayley (2026)** that evaluated **Oxevision** (now rebranded as LIO) — a **video-based monitoring technology** used in NHS mental health inpatient bedrooms. The original study claimed "notable reductions" across all five patient safety outcomes (self-harm, falls, assaults, physical restraint, and rapid tranquilisation). Porter's re-analysis demonstrates that after excluding incomplete or unreliable data, **four of five outcomes showed no statistically significant association** with Oxevision installation. The **confidence intervals** for restraint (95% CI: -50.2%, 15.2%) and rapid tranquilisation (95% CI: -60.1%, 10.3%) cross zero, indicating non-significance. The assault finding became non-significant after correcting for missing patient IDs. For **self-harm**, six of twelve wards saw **increases** post-installation. For **falls** — the only significant outcome — data from wards where Oxevision was **not installed in all bedrooms** was included, contaminating results. The author highlights that **video-recording patients in their bedrooms is an infringement of privacy** that must be proportionate to its aim, yet the evidence does not support the technology's safety claims.

## Clinical Insight
For mental health professionals working in **inpatient settings**, this analysis raises serious concerns about the **evidence base** supporting a technology that has been deployed in **over a third of NHS Mental Health Trusts** in England. The finding that **self-harm increased** in half of the wards studied after Oxevision installation is particularly concerning and warrants urgent attention from clinical governance teams. The author — a person with lived experience of psychiatric detention — highlights the **privacy and dignity implications** of continuous video monitoring in bedrooms, arguing that the **burden of proof** should lie with demonstrating benefit, not merely the absence of harm. For clinicians, this underscores the importance of **critically evaluating** technologies promoted as safety solutions, particularly when the evidence comes from **service evaluations conducted "in partnership" with the company** rather than independent research. The reliance on **relative percentage changes** (comparing intervention to control wards) rather than absolute rates can obscure the fact that self-harm rates on Oxevision wards remained very high (29.49 per 1,000 bed days). Clinicians should advocate for **independent, preregistered research** before adopting monitoring technologies, and should be aware of the **Lampard Inquiry** findings on inpatient deaths in Essex where Oxevision was used.

## Methodology Note
This is an other-type paper (critical re-analysis/commentary).

__Measurement Validity__: The re-analysis uses the same data as the original meta-analysis, which relies on service evaluation data from six NHS Trusts.
The original data has significant limitations: inconsistent definitions across Trusts, missing patient IDs, and variable reporting periods.
The author appropriately highlights that the original study used fixed-effects models despite substantial heterogeneity, where random-effects would have been more conservative.

__Open Science__: Published as an open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
The original study's data limitations are transparently critiqued.
The author declares their position as a founding member of the Stop Oxevision campaign and a person with lived experience.

__Participant Transparency__: The original meta-analysis aggregated data across multiple NHS Trusts with varying patient populations and ward types.
No demographic breakdown of affected patients is provided.
The "older adult wards" for the falls analysis are not differentiated by diagnosis (functional vs. dementia).
