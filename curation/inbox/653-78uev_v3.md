## Summary
This study presents the most comprehensive examination to date of **moderators and mechanisms of change** in **CBT for procrastination**, using secondary analyses of pooled data from **two RCTs** (N = 459) comparing a 5-session group CBT intervention with wait-list control.
The authors introduce an **Integrative Cognitive-Behavioral model of Procrastination (ICB-P)** synthesizing key mechanistic accounts including **Temporally Motivation Theory (TMT)**, emotion regulation frameworks, and self-regulation models.
Measures were collected at **pre-, mid-, and post-intervention**, covering procrastination severity, task value, success expectancy, delay discounting, self-control (NAS-50), emotion regulation (DERS-SF), attributional style (MMCS), fear of failure (PFAI), time perspective (ZTPI), reward/punishment sensitivity (SPSRQ-SF), and personality disorder beliefs (PBQ).
**Moderation findings**: Participants with greater baseline **emotion regulation difficulties (DERS)** benefited more from CBT, while **dysfunctional beliefs associated with cluster B personality disorders (PBQ)** predicted lower improvements.
Baseline depression, anxiety, or ADHD symptoms did **not** moderate outcomes, suggesting broad applicability of CBT for procrastination across comorbid conditions.
**Mechanism findings**: Increased **task value** and **proactive control** mediated both contemporaneous and lagged reductions in procrastination.
Contemporaneous mediators included increased positive and decreased negative **task-related affect**, improved perseverance, **emotional clarity**, and **internal attributions of success**.
The paper has been accepted for publication in *Behaviour Research and Therapy*.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides directly actionable guidance for clinicians treating procrastination, a prevalent self-regulation difficulty affecting an estimated **15-20% of the general population** and up to **75% of students**.
The finding that **emotion regulation difficulties predict greater treatment benefit** suggests that clients who struggle most with emotional responses to tasks have the most to gain from CBT — this should encourage therapists not to exclude seemingly "emotionally dysregulated" clients from procrastination treatment.
The key mechanism — shifting **task-related affect** from negative to positive — aligns with **ACT-based** approaches that emphasize willingness and values-based action.
Clinicians should prioritize interventions that increase **task value** (meaningfulness, enjoyment) and **proactive control** (planning, sub-goaling) as these were the strongest and most consistent mediators of change.
The **ICB-P model** provides a useful formulation framework: procrastination is maintained by low task value, low success expectancy, high delay discounting, poor self-control, and negative task-related affect.
The null moderation effects for depression, anxiety, and ADHD suggest that **comorbid psychopathology should not be considered a contraindication** for procrastination-focused CBT.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The pooled sample of **N = 459** (two RCTs) provides adequate power for moderation and mediation analyses.
The study examines a **wide range of candidate mechanisms** (over 20 measures), which increases the risk of **multiple comparisons** and false-positive mediation findings.
No explicit **a priori power analysis** for the mediation analyses was reported.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of **longitudinal structural equation models** with both contemporaneous and lagged mediation paths is methodologically rigorous.
Distinguishing within-wave (contemporaneous) from cross-wave (lagged) effects provides stronger evidence for temporal precedence of mechanisms.
The **pooled analysis** of two RCTs increases generalizability and statistical power.
A limitation is the **wait-list control design** — without an active control condition, it is unclear whether mechanisms are specific to CBT or reflect non-specific treatment factors.

__Effect Sizes__: The paper reports detailed mediation effects including indirect effect sizes and confidence intervals.
Moderation effects for emotion regulation difficulties and cluster B beliefs are quantified.
The **medium-to-large CBT effects** on procrastination are consistent with prior meta-analytic findings.
Both **Cohen's d** and **longitudinal mediation coefficients** are reported.

__Measurement Validity__: A comprehensive battery of validated measures was used, all with reported internal consistencies (McDonald's omega) that were generally adequate (most above .70).
Notable measures include **DERS-SF** (omega = .877), **NAS-50** subscales (.713-.813), **PFAI** (omega = .947), and **PBQ** subscales (.799-.927).
A limitation is that the SPSRQ-SF reward sensitivity subscale had low internal consistency (omega = .579).
The reliance on **self-report measures** for both predictors and outcomes introduces potential **common method bias**.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint** and has been accepted for publication in *Behaviour Research and Therapy*.
The data comes from two prior RCTs, but it is unclear whether the original datasets or analysis code are publicly available.
The comprehensive reporting of measures and analyses supports transparency.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample comprised **459 participants** (pooled from two RCTs) who were students with academic procrastination.
Detailed demographic characteristics were reported in the full text.
The student sample limits generalizability to clinical populations seeking treatment for procrastination in occupational or other life domains.
