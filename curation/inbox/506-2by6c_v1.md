## Summary
This study challenges the dominant model-based versus model-free (MB-MF) dichotomy in reinforcement learning theories of addiction by examining multi-task learning in individuals with substance use disorders (SUD). Rather than the traditional single-task paradigm, participants (SUD group and demographically matched healthy controls) completed a two-step, multi-task reinforcement learning experiment requiring navigation through a tree-structured environment with varying reward contingencies across tasks. The paradigm was designed to assess how people generalize learning across multiple goal contexts.

Results showed that across both groups, behavior was not well explained by canonical MB or MF accounts. Instead, participants acquired successor features (SFs) that serve as reusable representational components for generalization across tasks. SF learning occupies a middle ground between MB and MF approaches: it does not construct a full environmental model (unlike MB) but captures shared structure across tasks (unlike MF). Leveraging information theory, the authors developed a resource-rational model that balances expected reward and cognitive cost in multi-task learning.

Critically, individuals with SUD showed increased sensitivity to cognitive cost and preferentially adopted simplified policies that they reused across tasks, at the expense of reward maximization. While both groups used SF learning, the SUD group's behavior was primarily driven by minimizing cognitive effort rather than maximizing rewards. Individual differences in cost sensitivity and inferred cognitive effort significantly predicted addiction severity, providing a direct link between computational parameters and clinical symptoms. These findings suggest that addiction involves a systematic shift in how cognitive resources are allocated during learning, rather than a simple degradation from model-based to model-free control.

## Clinical Insight
This study reconceptualizes addictive behavior not as a failure of goal-directed control (MB) versus habitual control (MF), but as a resource allocation problem where individuals with SUD disproportionately minimize cognitive effort at the cost of reward optimization. This has practical implications: treatment approaches that reduce cognitive demands of adaptive decision-making may be more effective than those that simply target impulse control or reward sensitivity.

The finding that cognitive cost sensitivity predicts addiction severity suggests that assessments of how patients allocate cognitive effort in learning contexts could serve as a novel biomarker or treatment target. Interventions that make adaptive strategies less cognitively costly, or that gradually increase cognitive engagement through scaffolding, might address this core mechanism. The resource-rational framework also explains why individuals with SUD often appear rigid: they reuse simplified policies because developing new ones is experienced as too costly.

## Relevant For
Researchers studying computational models of addiction, clinical neuroscientists, and therapists working on cognitive rehabilitation approaches for substance use disorders.

Sophisticated computational modeling approach. Multi-task paradigm is more ecologically valid than traditional single-task RL designs. SUD group included methamphetamine users in treatment. International collaboration across Chinese and UK institutions.
