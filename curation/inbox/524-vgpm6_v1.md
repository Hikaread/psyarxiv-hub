## Summary

This **quasi-experimental study** (N = 249 first-year psychology students) examined whether **online** versus **in-person briefing** for a 7-day **experience sampling method** protocol affected **compliance**, **careless responding**, **participant burden**, and **briefing sufficiency**. Online briefing was associated with higher initial **compliance** (estimated 81% vs 73% for males) that declined significantly over the study period, while in-person briefing showed a non-significant flat trajectory. By the final day, both conditions converged to approximately 66% compliance. There were no significant differences in any **careless responding** indicator (within-beep standard deviation, response time per item, percentage of items at the mode), either in mean levels or temporal trends. **Participant burden** and **perceived briefing sufficiency** also did not differ between conditions. Several careless responding indicators worsened over time regardless of briefing modality, and **momentary burden** unexpectedly decreased over the study period.

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: The study addresses a practically important but empirically neglected question in **ESM methodology**. It uses multiple well-validated indicators of **careless responding** from Jaso et al. (2022), including both behavioural (response time, response variability) and self-report measures. The **generalised linear mixed-effects models** with random slopes for day number appropriately account for the hierarchical data structure. The preregistration on OSF and available analysis code on OSF support transparency. The inclusion of both time-invariant (retrospective) and time-varying (momentary) measures of burden and carelessness provides a comprehensive picture.

__Limitation__: The quasi-experimental design assigns participants to conditions by **recruitment phase** rather than randomisation, introducing potential **cohort confounds** (different academic years, 2024-2025 vs 2025-2026). The groups are highly unbalanced (online n = 209; in-person n = 40), and the in-person group may be underpowered to detect temporal trends. The sample consists entirely of **first-year psychology students** at a single university, limiting generalisability to clinical or community ESM samples. The study was powered for overall compliance differences, not for the **modality-by-time interaction** that emerged as the primary finding, meaning it should be considered preliminary. The 7-day protocol is relatively short, and the question of whether findings extend to longer ESM designs remains open.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians and researchers using **ESM in clinical populations**, this study supports the feasibility of **online briefing** as a viable alternative to in-person sessions, at least for relatively straightforward protocols of short duration. This has practical implications for making **intensive longitudinal monitoring** more accessible to geographically dispersed patients, reducing barriers related to travel, scheduling, and researcher time. However, the finding that **compliance declined faster** with online briefing warrants caution for longer clinical monitoring protocols, where sustained engagement is critical for treatment planning and outcome tracking. The finding that **careless responding** increased over time regardless of briefing modality suggests that post-briefing strategies — such as **reminders**, **gamification**, or **incremental incentives** — may be more important than briefing format for maintaining **data quality** in clinical ESM research.

## Relevant For

- Researchers conducting experience sampling studies with clinical populations
- Clinicians using ESM for idiographic assessment and monitoring
- Research coordinators designing ESM study protocols
- Methodologists studying data quality in intensive longitudinal designs
