## Summary
Four **preregistered experiments** (total N across studies ranging from 72 to 120+ participants) investigated how **trait cognitive anxiety** (worry, thought intrusions, distractibility) and **trait somatic anxiety** (physiological arousal, muscle tension) differentially affect **visual working memory** for feature bindings. Using the **Mental State Inventory** (MSI) to dissociate cognitive and somatic anxiety dimensions, Experiments 1-2 used cued recall of sequentially presented colour-shape combinations to test effects of **executive attention** (Experiment 1: prioritization instructions) and **perceptual distraction** (Experiment 2: visual suffix). No significant anxiety effects emerged in either experiment. Experiments 3-4 used **single-probe recognition** at different memory loads (3 or 4 items). Experiment 3 compared binding recognition with individual feature recognition: higher **trait cognitive anxiety** was associated with improved feature recognition accuracy, while higher **trait somatic anxiety** was associated with reduced binding recognition accuracy. Experiment 3 also found that higher somatic anxiety predicted faster but less accurate binding responses overall. Experiment 4 confirmed that the somatic anxiety–binding deficit persisted under **cognitive load** (number repetition vs backward counting), with higher somatic anxiety again predicting worse binding recognition. These results suggest that **somatic anxiety** may be a more sensitive indicator of visual working memory disruption than cognitive anxiety, particularly for the binding process that integrates separate visual features into unified object representations.

## Clinical Insight
The dissociation between **cognitive** and **somatic anxiety** dimensions has direct implications for clinical assessment and case formulation. Patients presenting with prominent somatic anxiety symptoms — such as those with **panic disorder**, **health anxiety**, or **GAD** with strong physiological arousal — may experience specific deficits in integrating complex visual and environmental information, which could manifest as difficulties in social situations requiring rapid processing of facial expressions, body language, or other multi-feature stimuli. In contrast, the counterintuitive finding that cognitive anxiety may enhance single-feature recognition suggests that the worry-driven hypervigilance characteristic of cognitive anxiety can improve performance on some specific cognitive tasks. Clinicians should assess both anxiety dimensions separately (using tools like the **MSI** rather than composite anxiety scores) to predict different cognitive profiles and tailor interventions: patients with dominant somatic anxiety may benefit more from interoceptive exposure and physiological regulation techniques, while those with dominant cognitive anxiety may benefit from worry management and cognitive defusion strategies. The findings also align with **Attentional Control Theory**, supporting the role of bottom-up perceptual disruption in anxiety-related cognitive impairment.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians assessing anxiety subtypes using cognitive vs somatic distinctions
- CBT practitioners treating GAD, panic disorder, and health anxiety
- Neuropsychologists assessing working memory in anxiety disorders
- Researchers studying anxiety-cognition interactions using experimental paradigms

## Notes
All 4 experiments preregistered on OSF. Sample sizes adequate for small-to-medium effects but larger samples needed for cognitive × somatic anxiety interactions. Key clinical measure: Mental State Inventory (MSI) for dissociating anxiety dimensions.
