## Summary
This two-study paper develops and initially validates the first workplace well-being assessment battery specifically designed for workers with intellectual disabilities in sheltered employment. The instrument is grounded in three theoretical frameworks: the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model, the DISC-R model, and recovery theories (the DRAMMA model). The 80-item battery covers four well-being outcomes (burnout, need for recovery, vigor, job satisfaction), job characteristics (demands and resources), and off-duty recovery (six activity types plus recovery experiences including psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, control, and affiliation). In Study 1, a 9-member expert panel including both academic researchers and frontline practitioners (neuropsychologists, integration coordinators, workplace supervisors, special educators) conducted four rounds of iterative review using the Lynn content validation method, achieving I-CVI = 1.00 for all items. In Study 2, 29 PwID in two French sheltered employment centres completed the questionnaire with researcher support available, with a mean completion time of 28 minutes. Approximately 20% of items received dont-know responses and 6 participants missed at least one item, suggesting generally good comprehension. Descriptive scores revealed moderate levels of burnout, low need for recovery, and moderate-to-high vigor and job satisfaction.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists supporting adults with intellectual disabilities, this instrument provides a structured way to differentiate between workplace stressors that require organizational-level intervention versus those requiring individual clinical support. The JD-R framework means that a PwID showing high burnout alongside low job resources can be referred for job redesign or supervisor training rather than individual therapy. The finding that most participants achieved good comprehension with supported administration (but not independently) has direct implications for service delivery: clinicians and case managers working with PwID should plan for assisted completion of any standardized assessments rather than assuming self-report capability. The tool also opens research possibilities for examining how workplace wellbeing intersects with mental health outcomes in the PwID population, an area that has been severely understudied due to the lack of appropriate measurement instruments.

## Relevant For
Therapists and case managers working with adults with intellectual disabilities in supported employment, occupational psychologists, vocational rehabilitation specialists, and researchers studying wellbeing in neurodivergent populations.

## Notes
No psychometric validation beyond content validity has been conducted yet — construct validity, internal consistency, and test-retest reliability require larger samples. The pilot sample (N=29) was occupationally homogeneous (79% in conditioning workshops). PwID were not involved in the instrument development phase itself. Severity of intellectual disability was not formally recorded. The instrument requires in-person supported administration and cannot yet be used as an independent self-report measure.
