## Summary

This study reports the **cross-cultural adaptation** and **psychometric validation** of the **Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire** for Brazilian Portuguese (CAT-Q-BR) in a large sample of N=1393 (923 autistic + 470 non-autistic adults) recruited online across 26 of Brazil's 27 federative units.
**Confirmatory factor analysis** supported the original three-factor structure (Compensation, Masking, Assimilation) in the autistic subsample with acceptable fit (CFI=0.964, TLI=0.96, RMSEA=0.076, SRMR=0.066), and fit was excellent in non-autistic and combined samples.
**Internal consistency** was adequate in the autistic group (α=0.88) and higher in non-autistic (α=0.93) and combined (α=0.95) samples.
Notably, no significant association emerged between CAT-Q-BR total scores and the **AQ-10**, while higher **camouflaging** was associated with self-reported **anxiety** (d=0.49) and **depression** (d=0.72).
**Gender-diverse** autistic participants reported significantly higher camouflaging than both cisgender men and cisgender women, whereas no significant difference was found between cisgender men and women.

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: The sample size substantially exceeds the planned target of 250, with N=1393 providing strong statistical power for **CFA** and subgroup comparisons.
The translation process followed the **ITC-TARES** framework and included a commendable participatory element: five autistic adults served as expert judges alongside a clinician, enhancing **experiential validity** of the adapted items.
Data and materials are **openly available** on OSF, and the **Content Validity Index** (CVI=0.78) was formally assessed.
Effect sizes (Cohen's d) are reported for group comparisons, which aids interpretation.
The study fills a genuine gap as the first **camouflaging** validation study from Latin America.

__Limitation__: The RMSEA of 0.076 in the autistic sample exceeds the conventional ≤0.06 threshold for good fit cited by the authors themselves (Hu & Bentler, 1999), and the paper does not address this discrepancy or explore alternative models via **EFA** or **ESEM**.
Four items showed poor **factor loadings** (≤0.43) in the autistic group, and Item 18 had consistently negligible loadings (≤0.16) across all groups, raising questions about its suitability in the Brazilian context.
Item 12 displayed **negative factor loadings** in the non-autistic and combined samples, suggesting problematic functioning of this reverse-scored item outside the autistic group.
No **measurement invariance** testing was conducted across diagnostic groups or gender categories, which is a notable omission given that the authors themselves note the lack of mean gender differences may indicate differential item functioning.
**Test-retest reliability** was not assessed, so temporal stability remains unknown.
**Anxiety and depression** were measured via a single open-ended self-report question rather than standardized instruments, substantially limiting the precision of these validity estimates.
The sample lacks demographic characterization for education, **socioeconomic status**, and race/ethnicity, which are particularly salient in the Brazilian context.
The non-autistic comparison group was heavily concentrated in São Paulo (76.8%), limiting its representativeness.
No **preregistration** is mentioned, and only **Cronbach's alpha** was used (no omega), with no subscale-level reliability reported.
The planned recruitment target of 250 was far exceeded, suggesting the sample size was not power-calculated a priori.

## Clinical Insight

The CAT-Q-BR provides the first validated tool for assessing **camouflaging behaviors** in Brazilian autistic adults, enabling clinicians and researchers to explore how compensatory strategies interact with **mental health outcomes** in a previously understudied cultural context.
The finding that higher camouflaging is associated with self-reported **anxiety** and **depression** aligns with international evidence and suggests that the mental health burden of sustained social masking extends across cultures, though the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference.
The lack of association between CAT-Q-BR scores and the AQ-10 in a diagnosed autistic sample reinforces concerns that the AQ-10 may lack **sensitivity** in adults who actively camouflage, potentially contributing to **diagnostic delays**.
Elevated camouflaging among **gender-diverse** participants highlights a particularly vulnerable subgroup that may benefit from targeted clinical attention, as the compounding effects of minority stress and autistic camouflaging could amplify **mental health risks**.
However, clinicians should interpret individual CAT-Q-BR scores cautiously given the identified item-level problems (Items 12 and 18) and the absence of **Brazilian normative data** or clinical cut-off scores.

## Relevant For
- Autism researchers conducting cross-cultural camouflaging studies
- Clinicians assessing autistic adults in Brazilian and Latin American contexts
- Researchers interested in gender diversity and camouflaging in autism

## Notes

This is the first camouflaging validation study from Latin America, filling an important geographical gap. The open data availability on OSF is a strength, but the lack of measurement invariance testing and the reliance on a single open-ended question for mental health assessment limit the validity evidence. The authors themselves note several of these limitations and recommend ESEM, IRT, and qualitative follow-ups.
