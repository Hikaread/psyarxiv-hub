## Summary
This study systematically compared **six idiographic time-series methods** for identifying individual-level process-outcome relationships in psychological treatment data: **bivariate i-ARIMAX**, **multivariate i-ARIMAX**, **GIMME** (network approach), **indSEM** (network approach), **Boruta** (tree-based), and a novel **tsBoruta** (Time Series Boruta). Through **simulation studies** varying linear/nonlinear effects, autoregressive patterns, trends, interactions, predictor counts, and sample sizes, the authors evaluated methods using **sensitivity**, **specificity**, **precision**, and **F1 scores**. For **linear effects**, **i-ARIMAX**, **iBoruta**, and **tsBoruta** achieved the highest F1 scores and specificity. **Tree-based approaches** performed best at detecting **nonlinear effects** and **interactions**. Methods failing to account for **time-series dependencies** showed significant performance drops. In an **empirical application** to loneliness and depressed mood data, **i-ARIMAX** and **tsBoruta** replicated the group-level loneliness-depression link while revealing **substantial individual-level heterogeneity** and **distinct predictor patterns**, suggesting that a **combined i-ARIMAX + tsBoruta workflow** offers a practical approach to data-driven **idiographic treatment planning**.

## Clinical Insight
This paper provides clinicians and researchers with **actionable guidance** on selecting statistical methods for **personalizing psychological treatment**. The key clinical message is that different idiographic methods capture different aspects of a client's process — **ARIMAX models** excel at identifying **linear temporal relationships** (e.g., "when my loneliness increases, my depressed mood rises the next day"), while **tree-based methods** like **tsBoruta** detect **nonlinear patterns and interactions** (e.g., "social isolation only predicts depressive spikes when combined with sleep disruption"). The recommended **combined workflow** allows clinicians to build a more complete picture of each client's unique symptom dynamics. For **therapists using EMA or diary data** in practice, these methods could identify **personalized treatment targets** — for instance, determining whether intervening on sleep, social connection, or activity level would most effectively disrupt a specific client's depressive cycle. The emphasis on **individual-level heterogeneity** challenges the one-size-fits-all approach to case formulation and supports the growing **process-based CBT** movement. However, these methods require **sufficient repeated measurements** (typically 30+ observations per person) and **statistical expertise** that may not be available in routine clinical settings.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental study (simulation + empirical application).

__Statistical Power__: Simulations systematically varied sample sizes and conditions, providing comprehensive evaluation of method performance.
The empirical application used intensive longitudinal data with adequate observations per person for idiographic analysis.
Power was not formally calculated for the empirical data, as the study focuses on comparing method performance.

__Preregistration__: No mention of preregistration for the simulation study or empirical analysis.
This is a limitation for a methodological comparison study where analytic choices could influence results.

__Effect Sizes__: Performance metrics (sensitivity, specificity, precision, F1) are reported for each method across simulation conditions.
These provide clear, comparable effect size analogs for method performance.
The empirical application reports individual-level coefficients but does not aggregate them into summary effect sizes.

__Statistical Rigor__: The simulation design is comprehensive, systematically varying six data-generating conditions.
Performance is evaluated across multiple metrics rather than a single index, providing a nuanced picture.
The empirical application appropriately uses the methods in a confirmatory manner after simulation evaluation.

__Measurement Validity__: The empirical application uses EMA-style self-report measures of loneliness and depressed mood.
Specific psychometric properties of these measures in the idiographic context are not discussed.
The study does not address whether the measures have adequate reliability at the within-person level for time-series analysis.

__Open Science__: The paper is available as a preprint on PsyArXiv.
No mention of data, code, or materials being publicly available.
For a methodology paper, open code would be particularly valuable for reproducibility and adoption.

__Participant Transparency__: The empirical application sample is described but specific demographic details are limited in the abstract.
The simulation component does not involve human participants.
The study focuses on method performance rather than sample characteristics.
