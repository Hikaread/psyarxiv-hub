## Summary
This study employed a rigorous **split-sample design** (exploratory n=359, replication n=588) to identify **cognitive-computational markers** that differentially relate to **anxiety-depression** versus **anhedonia-apathy** symptom dimensions. Participants completed four established cognitive tasks: the **Probabilistic Instrumental Learning Task (PILT)**, the **Volatility Task (VT)**, the **Facial Expression Recognition Task (FERT)**, and the **Foraging Fish Game (FFG)**. Factor analysis of mood questionnaires (BDI, STICSA, AMI, TEPS) confirmed a two-factor structure: **Anxiety-Depression** and **Anhedonia-Apathy** (r=0.33). Key replicated findings included: **anhedonia-apathy** was associated with **better reinforcement learning** and **increased foraging** versus hiding, while **anxiety-depression** was linked to **impaired reinforcement learning** and **deficient emotional processing** (reduced positive bias on the FERT). A combination of parameters from three tasks significantly predicted the relative severity of anxiety-depression versus anhedonia-apathy (**R-squared = 0.122, p = 0.0034**), outperforming demographics alone.

## Clinical Insight
This study advances **computational psychiatry** by demonstrating that objective **cognitive task parameters** can differentiate between two core transdiagnostic symptom dimensions that often co-occur in clinical practice. The counterintuitive finding that **anhedonia** is associated with *better* reinforcement learning challenges simplistic assumptions about reward processing deficits and suggests that anhedonic individuals may actually be more sensitive to reward contingencies in structured tasks, possibly reflecting compensatory over-engagement. For clinicians, this implies that patients presenting with similar overall symptom severity may have fundamentally different **cognitive profiles** requiring tailored treatment approaches -- for instance, patients with dominant anxiety-depression may benefit more from interventions targeting **emotional processing biases** (e.g., **attentional bias modification**), while those with dominant anhedonia-apathy may respond better to interventions addressing **motivation and approach behavior**. Although the effect size for multivariate prediction was small, the study provides proof of principle for **transdiagnostic cognitive phenotyping** that could eventually complement symptom-based assessment.

- Therapists designing targeted interventions for anhedonia vs. anxiety
