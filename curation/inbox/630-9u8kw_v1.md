## Summary
This EEG study (N = 34 undergraduates) used **encoding-retrieval similarity (ERS)** analyses to test the timing of **neural recapitulation** for emotional memories. Participants encoded line-drawing/full-image pairs and later completed a recognition memory task using line drawings as retrieval cues. Consistent with the **NEVER model** (Negative Emotional Valence Enhances Recapitulation), **encoding-retrieval similarity was greater for negative images** than for positive or neutral images. Critically, this increased recapitulation for negative memories began **within 200 ms** after retrieval cue presentation, overlapping temporally with early **ERP old-new effects** and preceding the initial old/new decision. This early onset suggests that valence-dependent recapitulation is not merely a consequence of post-retrieval processing but may influence the nature and success of ongoing retrieval. The study used **representational similarity analysis (RSA)** applied to EEG data, cluster-based permutation tests, and mass univariate ERP analyses, providing converging evidence from two complementary approaches.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have direct relevance to understanding the neurocognitive mechanisms underlying **emotional memory disturbances** in clinical conditions such as **PTSD**, **depression**, and **anxiety disorders**. The demonstration that negative memories show **enhanced and earlier neural recapitulation** provides a plausible neural mechanism for why **intrusive memories**, **flashbacks**, and **rumination** are so persistent in these disorders — the brain more rapidly and strongly reactivates the original encoding state for negative material. The early timing (within 200 ms) suggests these processes operate at a **pre-conscious level**, which has implications for therapeutic approaches: **EMDR**, **imaginal exposure**, and **cognitive restructuring** may need to account for this rapid, automatic reactivation rather than treating negative memory retrieval as a purely strategic process. The NEVER model's prediction that negative memories are more vivid and sensory-specific is directly relevant to clinical phenomenology — clinicians working with **trauma-related disorders** can use this framework to psychoeducate clients about why their negative memories feel so vivid and intrusive. The use of **EEG** (rather than fMRI) opens possibilities for developing **biomarkers** of maladaptive emotional memory processing that could eventually be used to track treatment response, though the current undergraduate sample limits immediate clinical generalisability.

## Methodology Note
__Statistical Power__: N = 34 is typical for EEG studies but small; the authors note adequate power (≥ .80) for medium effects in conventional within-subject analyses and that N exceeds prior fMRI studies using similar paradigms, but no formal power analysis for cluster-corrected permutation tests was possible due to the novel ERS-in-EEG approach. __Preregistration__: The study was not preregistered. __Effect Sizes__: The primary analyses used cluster-based permutation tests (which provide p-values but not traditional effect sizes for significant clusters) and RSA correlation-based similarity measures; ERP effect sizes were reported via mass univariate analyses. __Statistical Rigor__: Appropriate EEG preprocessing (ICLabel for artefact removal, 1 Hz high-pass filter), cluster-based permutation tests controlling for multiple comparisons across time/electrodes, and converging evidence from both RSA and mass univariate approaches strengthen the findings; however, the study is under review and results should be considered preliminary. __Measurement Validity__: Stimuli were drawn from three established affective image databases (EmoMadrid, OASIS, NAPS) and a separate rating study (N = 300) was conducted to validate the line-drawing stimuli; the EEG paradigm was adapted from established fMRI studies (Kark & Kensinger, 2015). __Open Science__: The paper is open access on PsyArXiv and is currently under peer review (noted as preliminary version). __Participant Transparency__: All 34 participants were Westminster College undergraduates (age 18–22, M = 19.56); 8 male, 26 female; 29 White, with limited racial/ethnic diversity; three participants excluded (excess artefact, poor performance, technical issue); this is a homogeneous, non-clinical sample limiting generalisability.
