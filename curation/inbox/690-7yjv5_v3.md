## Summary
This longitudinal study examined whether children's **affective and cognitive empathy** moderates the association between **parental burnout** and children's **internalizing, externalizing, and trauma-related symptoms** in a context of war. Participants were **104 mother-child dyads** assessed at two time points during the Israel-Hamas conflict (children aged approximately 3.7 and 4.7 years at T4 and T5 respectively). Results showed that parental burnout predicted child maladjustment, and critically, **higher affective empathy amplified** the negative impact of parental burnout on children's trauma-related symptoms. This counterintuitive finding challenges the prevailing view of empathy as uniformly protective, suggesting that in high-stress environments, children who are more emotionally attuned to their parents' distress may be **disproportionately affected** by it.

## Clinical Insight
This study overturns a common assumption in clinical work with families: that empathic children are better protected from parental distress. The finding that **affective empathy amplifies** the transmission of parental burnout to children's trauma symptoms has important implications for **family-focused interventions** during crises. For clinicians working with families in conflict zones or other high-stress contexts, assessing a child's empathic sensitivity is not just relevant for social development — it may identify children at **heightened risk** for trauma-related problems when parents are burned out. The clinical implication is that interventions should not merely aim to reduce parental burnout (important as that is) but should also help **highly empathic children** develop boundaries and regulation skills that protect them from **absorbing parental distress**. This is particularly relevant for young children (ages 3–5 in this study) who may lack the metacognitive capacity to distinguish their own emotions from those of their parents.

## Methodology Note
This is a longitudinal study with two measurement points during an active armed conflict.

__Design__: Longitudinal design with two time points (T4 and T5) during the Israel-Hamas war.
104 mother-child dyads (52.8% male children).
Parental burnout measured with validated instruments.
Child outcomes: internalizing, externalizing, and trauma-related symptoms.
Child empathy: affective and cognitive components measured via validated parent-report measures.
Moderation analysis testing empathy as a moderator of the parental burnout → child outcome pathway.

__Limitations__: Very young children (3–5 years); empathy measured by parent report rather than behavioral or physiological measures.
Mother-only reporting for both predictors and outcomes, introducing shared method variance.
War context limits generalizability to non-crisis settings.
Sample size (N = 104 dyads) limits statistical power for detecting small moderation effects.
Only mothers assessed; paternal burnout and father-child dynamics not examined.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
Data collected during an active conflict, providing rare real-time evidence on family processes under extreme stress.

## Notes
The finding that empathy can be a vulnerability factor in young children is both novel and clinically important. The very young age of the sample (3–5 years) makes this particularly concerning, as these children may lack the cognitive resources to process their empathic experiences. The war context, while limiting generalizability, provides a powerful natural experiment for understanding how family processes operate under extreme stress.
