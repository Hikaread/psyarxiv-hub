## Summary
Two exploratory studies examined intolerance of uncertainty (IU) using a novel experiential uncertainty task where participants inserted their hands into an opaque box to feel an unknown object, with subjective affective reactions and physiological reactivity assessed in anticipation. Across both studies, individuals higher in IU demonstrated less parasympathetic nervous system reactivity during uncertainty compared to those lower in IU, suggesting blunted vagal withdrawal responses. In Study 2, this association was disrupted by biofeedback presentation, particularly false biofeedback indicating rapid heart rate. Participants high in IU also reported heightened nervousness, worry, and stress during experiential uncertainty (Study 2). These findings indicate that high-IU individuals not only report unfavorable attitudes toward uncertainty on retrospective questionnaires but also experience real-time uncertainty differently both subjectively and physiologically.

## Clinical Insight
These findings directly inform the treatment of anxiety disorders and OCD, where intolerance of uncertainty is a central transdiagnostic maintaining factor. The demonstration that IU manifests in blunted parasympathetic reactivity during live uncertainty provides a physiological marker that clinicians could use to track treatment progress beyond self-report. The biofeedback disruption finding is particularly clinically relevant: if simply receiving external physiological information (even false feedback) can alter the IU-physiology relationship, this suggests both potential therapeutic opportunities (using accurate biofeedback to help patients recalibrate their interoceptive responses to uncertainty) and cautions (patients may be vulnerable to misinterpreting bodily signals during exposure-based treatments). The task itself could serve as an in-session experiential exercise for exposure-based therapies targeting IU, bridging the gap between questionnaire-based assessment and lived experience.

## Relevant For
Clinicians treating GAD, OCD, and other IU-related conditions; those using exposure therapy; therapists interested in interoceptive exposure and biofeedback; researchers studying the psychophysiology of uncertainty.
