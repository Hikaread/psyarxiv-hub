## Summary
This study developed and **psychometrically validated the Azerbaijani version of the 35-item Attitudes Towards Mental Health Problems Scale (ATMHPS-AZ)** among **946 university students**. Following **WHO translation guidelines**, the scale was translated and culturally adapted. Analyses included **internal consistency** (Cronbach's alpha = .954, McDonald's omega = .956), **EFA and CFA** supporting the original **seven-factor structure**, **convergent validity** with the Self-Compassion Scale, and **criterion-related validity** with the Stigma-9 Questionnaire. The seven factors are: **Disclosure Concerns**, **Family Attitudes**, **Family Burden**, **Workplace Attitudes**, **Friends' Attitudes**, **Professional Help-Seeking**, and **Self-Attitudes**. Results indicated **moderate overall stigma**, with the highest levels in **Disclosure Concerns** and the lowest in **Family Attitudes** and **Family Burden**. **Year of study** emerged as the only statistically significant (albeit weak) predictor of stigma, with later-year students reporting slightly higher levels. The ATMHPS-AZ demonstrated excellent reliability and supported the original factor structure, establishing it as a valid instrument for **stigma research** and **reduction efforts** in the Azerbaijani cultural context.

## Clinical Insight
The ATMHPS-AZ provides a **culturally validated tool** for assessing mental health stigma in Azerbaijan and similar **post-Soviet, collectivistic cultural contexts** where family expectations and social norms strongly shape help-seeking behavior. For clinicians working in these settings, the finding that **Disclosure Concerns** represent the highest stigma domain suggests that patients' fear of social consequences of disclosing mental health problems is a primary barrier to treatment engagement. This aligns with clinical observations that many patients in collectivistic cultures delay seeking help until crises occur. The relatively lower stigma in **Family Attitudes** and **Family Burden** domains suggests that families may be more supportive than patients anticipate, which clinicians can leverage in **family psychoeducation** and **engagement strategies**. The weak but significant effect of **year of study** on stigma (later years = slightly higher stigma) is counterintuitive and may reflect growing awareness of stigma-related consequences as students approach the workforce. The scale's **seven-factor structure** allows clinicians to identify specific stigma domains to target in **anti-stigma interventions** rather than addressing stigma as a monolithic construct. The strong convergence with **self-compassion** suggests that interventions enhancing **self-compassion** may also reduce mental health stigma.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric study (quantitative_experimental).

__Statistical Power__: The sample of N = 946 is large and provides excellent power for CFA and regression analyses.
The sample size exceeds the recommended minimum of 10 participants per item for factor analysis.
However, the university student sample limits generalizability to clinical or community populations.

__Effect Sizes__: Convergent and criterion validity correlations are reported between ATMHPS-AZ and established measures.
The regression analysis for demographic predictors reports effect sizes, though they are described as weak.
Specific correlation coefficients and R-squared values should be examined in the full text for clinical significance.

__Statistical Rigor__: Both EFA and CFA were conducted, with CFA supporting the seven-factor structure from EFA.
Multiple fit indices were likely used for CFA evaluation, though the abstract does not specify which.
The Holm correction or similar multiple comparisons adjustment for the regression analysis is not mentioned.

__Measurement Validity__: The original seven-factor structure was supported by CFA in the Azerbaijani sample.
Convergent validity with the Self-Compassion Scale and criterion validity with the Stigma-9 were established.
Test-retest reliability was not assessed, which is a notable limitation for a new adaptation.
Measurement invariance across gender or other subgroups was not examined.

__Open Science__: Published as a preprint on PsyArXiv.
No mention of data, code, or analysis materials being publicly available.
For a validation study, open data would strengthen confidence in the reported psychometric properties.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample consists of 946 Azerbaijani university students.
The gender distribution, age range, and field of study are not detailed in the abstract.
University students represent a specific demographic that may differ from the general population in stigma levels.

## Notes
Strong validation study with large sample. Key limitation is student-only sample. The finding that family-related stigma is lowest is interesting and may be culture-specific. Test-retest reliability is needed.
