## Summary
This concise conceptual paper (702 words) clarifies three practical meanings of **clinically significant change** in clinical practice and medical education. The first meaning, **recovery**, asks whether a patient's current state has moved from a **dysfunctional to a functional range** (e.g., crossing a symptom cut-off on a standardized measure), drawing on the classic **Jacobson and Truax** framework. The second, **response**, asks whether the patient has **improved enough compared to their starting point** to matter — particularly relevant when full recovery is unrealistic and meaningful relief is the clinical goal. The third, **additional reference points**, integrates **patient experience**, **clinical judgment**, **functional observations**, **relative/caregiver perspectives**, and **follow-up course** to determine whether measured changes are clinically meaningful. The authors organize these three definitions in a practical table to guide clinicians in choosing the right framework for the clinical question at hand.

## Clinical Insight
This paper is directly useful for any clinician who needs to communicate about treatment progress — whether in **inpatient settings**, **outpatient therapy**, or **clinical supervision**. The distinction between **recovery** (crossing a clinical threshold) and **response** (meaningful improvement from baseline) addresses a common source of confusion, particularly in **chronic or severe conditions** where full remission may not be achievable. The third definition is especially valuable: it validates the clinician's role in integrating **multiple information sources** (patient report, ward behavior, family observations, trajectory) rather than relying solely on symptom scale scores. This is particularly relevant for **multidisciplinary team discussions** where different professions may prioritize different indicators of change. The paper also implicitly warns against **over-reliance on the Reliable Change Index (RCI)** in isolation, consistent with the authors' prior work critiquing misunderstandings of the Jacobson-Truax method.
