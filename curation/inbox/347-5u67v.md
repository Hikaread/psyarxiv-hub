## Summary

This 60-day experience sampling study (N=79 adults) examined how emotion intensity and perceived controllability predict the use of six emotion regulation strategies (distraction, rumination, cognitive reappraisal, expression, suppression, and relaxation) in daily life. Replicating prior work, expression, relaxation, and rumination increased on days with more intense and uncontrollable emotions. The key novel finding was evidence for quadratic relationships: suppression and cognitive reappraisal were most likely on days with average levels of emotional intensity and controllability, and less likely on both low-intensity and high-intensity days. Additionally, people were more likely to switch to atypical strategies on days where emotions were less or more controllable than usual.

## Clinical Insight

These findings challenge the common therapeutic assumption that clients should be encouraged to use adaptive regulation strategies like cognitive reappraisal in proportion to emotional difficulty. Instead, reappraisal and suppression appear to be strategies people deploy in a middle zone of emotional activation, not when emotions are most overwhelming. When emotions are either too mild (no need to regulate) or too intense (capacity to reappraise may be overwhelmed), people are less likely to use these cognitive strategies. This has important implications for therapy: teaching cognitive reappraisal skills in session may not translate to use during acute emotional crises, and therapists should consider helping clients develop strategies for the high-intensity end (such as grounding, distraction, or soothing techniques) alongside cognitive approaches. The finding that people switch to atypical strategies when controllability deviates from normal also suggests that therapy should build a diverse repertoire of regulation strategies rather than relying on a primary approach, so clients have options when their usual strategies no longer fit the situation.

## Relevant For
Therapists helping clients develop emotion regulation skills, particularly those working with emotional dysregulation in the context of personality disorders, anxiety, or mood difficulties
