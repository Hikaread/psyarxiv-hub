## Summary
This study tests the hypothesis that **household SES** mediates the relationship between **childhood adversity** and **decision-making under uncertainty**, as measured by the **Cambridge Gambling Task (CGT)**. Past research has implicated both **childhood adversity** and **poverty** in impaired **reward processing** and **risk-taking behavior**, but the mediating role of socioeconomic circumstances in this relationship has been underexplored. The study employs **latent change mediation analysis** to decompose the pathway from early adverse experiences through socioeconomic conditions to adult decision-making patterns.

By using the CGT, which captures multiple dimensions of gambling behavior including **risk adjustment**, **quality of decision-making**, **deliberation time**, and **risk taking**, the study moves beyond simple risk-preference measures to assess the cognitive and affective components of decision-making under uncertainty. The **latent change mediation** approach allows for modeling change processes rather than relying on cross-sectional associations alone, providing a more dynamic picture of how childhood adversity may shape decision-making through its impact on socioeconomic trajectories.

## Clinical Insight
Understanding the pathway from **childhood adversity** to impaired **decision-making** through **household SES** has implications for assessing and treating individuals with adverse childhood experiences. If low SES mediates the relationship between early adversity and poor decision-making, interventions that address **economic stability** and **resource access** may be as important as trauma-focused therapies in improving cognitive functioning in this population. The CGT measures (risk adjustment, quality of decision-making) capture constructs relevant to **impulse control disorders**, **substance use**, and **behavioral addictions**, where decision-making under uncertainty is frequently impaired. The latent change mediation framework provides a model for understanding how **cumulative disadvantage** transmits from one generation to the next through cognitive pathways.
