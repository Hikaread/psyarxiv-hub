## Summary
This paper presents a **critical reanalysis** of two influential studies (Foody et al., 2013; Foody et al., 2015) that claimed **hierarchical framing** in ACT exercises (seeing thoughts as contained by a larger self) was more effective at reducing distress than **distinction framing** (seeing thoughts as separate from the self). Hussey identifies several serious problems: the central claim did not match the reported analyses, the failed replication (Foody et al., 2015) was misleadingly described as supporting the original findings, and **uncritical citation** of these studies in major textbooks (including the Oxford Handbook of ACT) has propagated the unsupported claim. A **multiverse analysis** extracting summary statistics from both studies found **null results under every analytic condition** when the analysis properly mapped onto the stated claim. The paper documents how these studies have been cited over **134 times** and featured prominently in ACT training materials despite lacking adequate evidential support.

## Clinical Insight
This reanalysis has direct implications for **ACT training and practice**. The claim that hierarchical self-as-context exercises are superior to distinction exercises has influenced how therapists are trained and how they explain ACT concepts to patients. Hussey is careful to note that this does not mean ACT is ineffective or that self-as-context is invalid — only that this **specific empirical claim** is unsupported. However, clinicians should be aware that a widely taught distinction in ACT practice (hierarchy > distinction) rests on **flawed evidence**. The paper also serves as a broader reminder about the importance of **critical evaluation of foundational evidence** in evidence-based practice. Clinicians who have been taught this hierarchy/distinction distinction should reconsider how strongly they weigh it in clinical decision-making. The pattern of **uncritical citation** — where failed replications are ignored and original claims are accepted at face value — is a systemic issue that affects how clinical techniques are disseminated.

## Methodology Note
This is a critical reanalysis and methodological critique (not an original empirical study).

__Design__: Multiverse reanalysis of summary statistics extracted from Foody et al. (2013, 2015).
Examined all reasonable analytic combinations that map onto the stated claim.
Also conducted a citation analysis of how the original studies have been represented in subsequent literature.

__Strengths__: The multiverse approach is transparent and exhaustive — every reasonable analytic specification was tested.
Detailed comparison of original methods, claims, and analyses reveals substantial discrepancies.
Citation analysis documents the propagation of unsupported claims through influential textbooks.

__Limitations__: Reanalysis relies on summary statistics rather than raw data.
Cannot fully account for all design choices in the original studies.
The critique targets a specific claim, not the broader ACT framework or evidence base.
Single-author reanalysis without independent verification.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
The multiverse analysis approach itself is fully transparent and reproducible.
The author explicitly bounds the claims, avoiding overgeneralization.
