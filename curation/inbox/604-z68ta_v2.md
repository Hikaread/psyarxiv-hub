## Summary
This qualitative study examined **self-generated behavioral goals** set by veterans with significant **PTSD symptoms** and **moral injury** distress within a web-based, self-guided positive psychology intervention called **MOVED** (Moral elevation-based intervention). The sample included **24 veterans** enrolled in a VA healthcare system who completed eight online sessions incorporating elevation-eliciting exercises, reflection, and **SMART goal setting**. Inductive coding of qualitative responses revealed that the most common self-generated goal themes were **prosocial behavior** (e.g., helping other veterans), enhancing **family relationships**, and **self-improvement**. Goal success rates ranged from 46% (self-improvement) to 80% (positive thinking). Reactions to successful goal completion most frequently included **positive emotions** (22.9%) and **noticing positive impact on others** (13.1%). Common barriers included **other responsibilities** (20.4%), **interfering negative emotions** (18.4%), and **lack of motivation** (12.2%). Veterans identified strategies such as **seeking support**, **planning ahead**, and **practicing perseverance** to overcome barriers.

## Clinical Insight
This study offers a novel window into how veterans with trauma distress approach **goal setting** independently, which has direct implications for **trauma-focused therapy**. The predominance of **interpersonal goals** (prosocial behavior, family relationships) over symptom-reduction goals suggests that veterans intrinsically value **psychosocial growth** and connection, aligning with **self-determination theory** and pointing to the potential of **positive psychology approaches** in trauma recovery. The finding that some veterans experienced **negative emotions** even upon successful goal completion (5.9%) highlights how PTSD symptoms can infiltrate positive experiences, a phenomenon clinicians should anticipate and normalize. The high success rate for **family relationship goals** (78.6%) suggests these may serve as particularly feasible and meaningful targets in early-phase trauma treatment. Barriers like interfering negative emotions and lack of motivation reflect ongoing **trauma-related impairments** that may require **therapist scaffolding** in self-guided interventions. These findings support incorporating **collaborative goal setting** informed by veterans own priorities into trauma treatment planning.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians treating veterans with PTSD and moral injury
- Therapists incorporating goal setting into trauma-focused interventions
- Developers of self-guided digital mental health tools
- Positive psychology practitioners working with trauma populations
