## Summary
This protocol paper describes a two-group randomised controlled trial (RCT) evaluating a CBT-infused, therapeutically applied role-playing game (TA-RPG) intervention using Dungeons and Dragons (D&D) for individuals with complex mental health needs at a community mental health centre in Australia. The 8-week group program embeds cognitive behavioural therapy techniques within the D&D gameplay structure, targeting anxiety and depressive symptomatology. An active control group will play standard board games for a comparable duration. Outcome measures are collected pre- and post-intervention for both groups, with the intervention group also completing 12-month follow-up assessments. The trial is registered on the Australian New Zealand Clinical Trials Registry (ACTRN12625000296437). The protocol represents an important step in building evidence for an emerging therapeutic modality that has shown early promise but lacks robust controlled evidence, particularly for populations with comorbid complex mental health presentations.

## Clinical Insight
TA-RPGs represent a novel therapeutic approach that harnesses the inherent structure of tabletop role-playing games to deliver psychotherapeutic content in an engaging, low-stigma format. The D&D framework naturally provides opportunities for social skills practice, behavioural activation, cognitive restructuring, exposure, and interpersonal learning within a group setting. For community mental health populations with complex needs who may be disengaged from traditional therapy, game-based interventions can improve attendance and therapeutic alliance. The use of an active control (board games) strengthens the design compared to earlier waitlist-controlled trials, allowing researchers to isolate the specific therapeutic ingredients of the TA-RPG approach beyond nonspecific group factors. If efficacy is demonstrated, this protocol could provide a template for integrating TA-RPGs into existing community mental health group programs, potentially improving treatment engagement for hard-to-reach populations.

## Relevant For
Clinicians working in community mental health settings seeking innovative group-based interventions, therapists interested in gamification of CBT techniques, and researchers investigating therapeutic applications of role-playing games for anxiety and depression.
