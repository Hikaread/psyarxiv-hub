## Summary
Across **6 experiments** (2 preregistered, total N = 1,271), this paper tested whether **empathy** and **mental imagery** underlie the **contagious spread of anxiety**. Study 1 (N = 166) established a novel paradigm: participants viewed anxiety ratings and written responses ostensibly from another participant who was either worried or calm about viewing distressing pictures. Self-reported **anxiety contagion** was demonstrated via a significant time x condition interaction, F(1, 164) = 14.06, p < .001, with effect size of .08 partial eta-squared, and replicated in Studies 2a (N = 220) and 2b (N = 224), with Study 2b showing the largest effect, F(1, 218) = 62.38, p < .001, with .22 partial eta-squared.

Studies 2a and 2b assessed **empathy components** — empathic concern, personal distress, and perspective taking — and **mental imagery** (vividness of flashforward images). All three empathy measures were elevated in the worried partner condition (d = 0.29 to 0.92). **Personal distress** showed the strongest correlation with anxiety change (r = .44 to .55 in the worried condition). Imagery vividness was strongly correlated with anxiety contagion only in the worried condition (r = .58 in both studies). **Serial mediation models** supported a process model: perspective taking leads to vivid flashforward imagery which leads to heightened anxiety.

Studies 3a-3c provided **causal evidence** by testing whether blocking working memory eliminates anxiety contagion. Study 3c (N = 442, preregistered) used four conditions: **Tetris** (visual WM load), **D-Corsi** (visual WM task), **digit span** (auditory WM), and passive control. Anxiety increased in the control condition (d = 0.23) and digit span condition (d = 0.35) but was eliminated in both visual WM conditions (Tetris d = 0.17, p = .083; D-Corsi d = 0.13, p = .151), demonstrating that **blocking visuospatial working memory** — but not auditory working memory — reduces contagious anxiety. Study 4 meta-analyzed trait measures across studies (N = 562), showing that anxiety contagion was related to trait **personal distress** (r_weighted = .26), **spontaneous use of imagery** (r_weighted = .17), **worry** (PSWQ; r_weighted = .19), and **trait anxiety** (STAI; r_weighted = .13).

## Clinical Insight
This work has significant translational potential for anxiety intervention. The finding that **visuospatial working memory tasks** can disrupt anxiety contagion provides a mechanistic rationale for deploying **imagery-competing interventions** (e.g., Tetris, D-Corsi) in settings where anxiety spreads socially — schools, workplaces, and clinical groups where **corumination** is common. The process model of **empathy leading to mental imagery leading to anxiety** suggests that "empathy regulation" strategies could help individuals in helping professions or those prone to **vicarious distress**. The clinical relevance is underscored by the mini-meta-analysis showing that individuals higher in worry and trait anxiety are more susceptible to contagious anxiety, suggesting this paradigm captures processes relevant to real-world anxiety pathology. The paradigm itself could serve as an experimental testbed for screening interventions aimed at interrupting the social transmission of anxiety.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: Total N = 1,271 across 6 studies provides adequate power for the main effects reported.
Study 2a suffered from suspected bot contamination, limiting its utility, but Study 2b served as a preregistered replication with N = 224.
Individual study sample sizes (N = 111-224) provide adequate power for medium effects.

__Preregistration__: Studies 2b and 3c were preregistered on OSF.
Study 1 was not preregistered.
Deviation from preregistered mediation models is transparently reported (personal distress removed due to conceptual overlap with the outcome).

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes are consistently reported with 90% or 95% confidence intervals.
Anxiety contagion effects range from small (d = 0.23 in Study 3c control) to medium (d = 0.42 in Study 2b).
Mini-meta-analytic correlations are small in magnitude (r = .13-.26).

__Statistical Rigor__: The multi-study design with replication and causal manipulation (Study 3c) is methodologically strong.
Multiple mediation approaches including parallel and serial models are used appropriately.
The manipulation of working memory systems (visual vs. auditory) provides a strong causal test.

__Measurement Validity__: Empathy measures adapted from established methods (Interpersonal Reactivity Index).
Mental imagery vividness measured using adapted Craving Experience Questionnaire items.
LIWC used for linguistic analysis of anxiety-word use.

__Open Science__: Two studies preregistered on OSF with links provided.
Data and analysis code availability not explicitly stated.
Competing interests of EAH (ANEMONE trademark) are transparently disclosed.

__Participant Transparency__: Demographics reported in Table 1 for each study.
Participants recruited via Prolific (online platform).
Exclusion criteria (bots in Study 2a) are clearly described.

## Notes
Classification: Social sciences / Psychological and Cognitive Sciences. The paradigm of exposing participants to a worried peer's fabricated responses is novel and could be adapted to study contagion of other internalizing symptoms. The connection between empathy (typically viewed as prosocial) and the cost of vicarious distress is theoretically important.
