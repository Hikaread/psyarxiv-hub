## Summary
This position paper argues that **clinical psychology training** must integrate **Open Science practices** to address the field's **replication and credibility crisis**. The authors document the **limited uptake** of open science practices in clinical journals and graduate training programs despite the availability of tools such as **preregistration**, **Registered Reports**, and **data/materials/code sharing**. They propose a **competency-based model** for embedding open science principles into doctoral education, arguing that transparency is both a **methodological and ethical imperative**. The paper reviews factors contributing to the crisis, outlines the role of transparency in distinguishing rigorous from flawed research, and calls on programs, journals, and accrediting bodies to make transparency a **core requirement** for restoring credibility and advancing trustworthy clinical science.

## Clinical Insight
While primarily aimed at the research-training interface, this paper has direct implications for **evidence-based practice**. Every clinician who reads a journal article to inform treatment decisions is relying on the credibility of that evidence. The replication crisis means that some published findings that have entered clinical guidelines may not be reliable. The authors' call for **preregistration** and **Registered Reports** is relevant to practitioners who want confidence in treatment efficacy data. For **clinical supervisors**, the competency-based model provides a framework for training trainees not just in open science but in **critical evaluation of evidence quality**. The emphasis on transparency as an **ethical obligation** reinforces that open science is not merely an academic preference but a matter of **patient welfare** — clinicians practicing based on flawed or unreproducible evidence may inadvertently harm patients.
