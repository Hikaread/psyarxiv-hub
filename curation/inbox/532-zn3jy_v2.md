## Summary
This study (N = 157 psychology students) investigated whether empathic disequilibrium — the imbalance between cognitive and emotional empathy components — can be modified through a brief mindfulness intervention. Participants were assigned to 10 days of daily 10-minute guided mindfulness training (n = 89) or a control condition involving emotion regulation lessons and daily mood ratings (n = 68). Empathic disequilibrium was calculated as the absolute difference between standardized cognitive empathy (Perspective Taking + Fantasy subscales) and emotional empathy (Empathic Concern + Personal Distress subscales) from the Interpersonal Reactivity Index. A significant Group x Time interaction (F(1,157.16) = 10.64, p < .01, partial eta-squared = .06) showed that the mindfulness group experienced a significant reduction in empathic disequilibrium magnitude from T1 to T2 (p = .002, d = .34), while the control group showed no significant change (p = .10). The reduction reflected genuine balancing between the two empathy components rather than a directional shift. Emotional reactivity (ERS) also decreased in the mindfulness group but did not mediate the reduction in empathic disequilibrium. Bayesian and propensity-score sensitivity analyses confirmed robustness.

## Clinical Insight
Empathic disequilibrium has been consistently linked to psychopathology across conditions including schizophrenia, anxiety, depression, psychopathy, and eating disorders. This study provides the first evidence that this interpersonal imbalance is modifiable through a brief, low-intensity mindfulness intervention in a non-clinical sample. For clinicians, this suggests that mindfulness-based approaches may help patients who experience empathy-related difficulties — whether feeling overwhelmed by others' emotions (emotional empathy dominance) or feeling detached (cognitive empathy dominance). The finding that emotional reactivity did not mediate the effect suggests that mechanisms beyond simple emotional regulation are at work, possibly involving changes in attentional or interoceptive processing. The brevity of the intervention (10 minutes daily for 10 days) makes it potentially feasible as an adjunctive skill-building exercise in clinical settings, though clinical population trials are needed.

## Relevant For
Clinicians working with patients experiencing empathy-related interpersonal difficulties; therapists incorporating mindfulness into treatment; researchers studying empathy in psychopathology; those interested in brief adjunctive interventions for conditions marked by empathic imbalance.

## Notes
Study used a student sample, limiting direct clinical generalizability. The state-based IRI adaptation and Hebrew back-translation are methodological considerations. Future research should test in clinical populations and examine the phenomenology of empathic disequilibrium.
