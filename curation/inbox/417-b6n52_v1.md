## Summary
This large-scale metascientific audit examined **transparent reporting** and **open research practices** across **2,431 psychological Experience Sampling Method (ESM) studies** published before October 2023, combining an **automated annotation pipeline using GPT-5** with manual validation and verification. The automated extraction demonstrated **high consistency** with manual coding. Only **4.6%** of studies declared a (pre- or post-) registration, **9.6%** declared open code, and **9.8%** declared publicly available data — with actual accessibility rates closely matching declarations (4.4%, 9.2%, and 9.2% respectively). Open science practices showed an **increasing trend over time**, particularly after 2018. Core ESM design features (study duration, sampling frequency, sampling scheme) were generally reported with adequate transparency. However, **ESM compliance reporting** (e.g., response rates, adherence) and **sample-size justifications** were notably less common. The study provides what the authors describe as **lower-bound estimates** of open science prevalence in ESM research and demonstrates a **scalable LLM-augmented workflow** for large-scale metascientific audits that could be applied to other research domains.

## Clinical Insight
ESM is widely used in clinical psychology to study **moment-to-moment dynamics** of symptoms, affect, and behaviour in conditions such as depression, anxiety, psychosis, and substance use. The finding that only 4.6% of ESM studies are registered and fewer than 10% share data or code has direct implications for **evidence-based practice**: clinicians should be aware that the majority of ESM-based findings they encounter in the literature lack the transparency safeguards needed to fully evaluate their credibility and robustness. The particularly low rates of **compliance reporting** are concerning for clinical ESM, where non-response and missing data patterns can substantially bias estimates of symptom dynamics. When clinicians interpret ESM-derived clinical findings — for example, the temporal relationship between negative affect and substance use — the low prevalence of preregistration and open data means that many of these findings may be subject to undisclosed analytic flexibility. The LLM-augmented audit workflow itself could be adapted by clinical research teams to systematically evaluate reporting standards within their own subfields.

## Methodology Note
Paper type: other (meta-science audit). __Measurement Validity__: The key methodological contribution is the demonstration that **LLM-based automated annotation** (GPT-5 via OpenAI API) can achieve high consistency with manual coding for metascientific data extraction, enabling audits at a scale (2,431 studies) that would be infeasible manually. The authors appropriately treat their estimates as lower bounds and implement manual verification of LLM outputs. __Statistical Rigor__: The search strategy covered three major databases (Scopus, Web of Science, PubMed) with ESM-specific terminology. The combination of automated and manual extraction, with reported consistency between the two, provides a credible methodological approach. However, the lower-bound framing acknowledges that some open science practices may be declared in supplements or elsewhere without being detected. __Open Science__: The study's OSF repository (osf.io/89pbn) is declared, and the paper models the transparency it advocates by auditing the field's practices. __Participant Transparency__: Not applicable — this is a bibliometric audit of published studies, not a study of human participants.

## Relevant For
- Clinical researchers using ESM/EMA designs who want to improve their reporting practices
- Meta-scientists studying transparency and reproducibility in clinical psychology
- Research teams considering LLM-augmented approaches for systematic reviews or audits
- Journal editors and reviewers establishing ESM reporting standards

## Notes
The finding that open science practices increased notably after 2018 may reflect the influence of registered report availability and changing funder/journal policies. The distinction between declared and actually accessible materials is valuable — declarations closely matched reality in this sample, suggesting researchers who declare openness follow through.
