## Summary

This qualitative meta-synthesis analyzed 88 published case studies comprising 133 individual cases of gaming-related health problems. Using deductive coding and thematic synthesis, the authors identified 845 distinct problems and 35 comorbidities. The resulting thematic network describes a pathway: a spectrum of vulnerabilities places the individual on a high-stress life trajectory; self-medication through gaming becomes both the coping mechanism and the problem; and prolonged gaming or critical life events lead to treatment-seeking or hospitalization. Most treatment-seeking was driven by social compliance rather than intrinsic motivation. Notably, most case studies did not formally diagnose gaming disorder but instead described instances where gaming intersected with mental health difficulties in varied ways. The authors highlight that many findings align with coping theories of gaming disorder.

## Clinical Insight

The coping model that emerges from this synthesis should reshape how therapists assess and treat problematic gaming. Rather than treating gaming as the primary pathology, therapists should explore what underlying vulnerabilities and stressors the gaming is helping the individual manage. The finding that most people seek treatment due to external pressure (social compliance) rather than personal concern has direct implications for motivational engagement: many clients will present ambivalent about change, and a directive or confrontational approach is likely to be counterproductive. The sheer variety of presentations across the 133 cases, including many that did not meet formal gaming disorder criteria, suggests that therapists should avoid applying a one-size-fits-all framework and instead conduct a thorough functional analysis of what gaming provides for the specific client. The identification of a vulnerability-stress-coping pathway also implies that successful treatment often requires addressing the original stressors and building alternative coping resources, not just reducing screen time.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with gaming disorder, behavioral addictions, or young adults presenting with problematic gaming patterns and comorbid difficulties
