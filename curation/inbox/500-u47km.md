## Summary
This qualitative **thematic analysis** of 52 aphantasic adults (mean VVIQ=16.5, age range 16-81, from a larger sample of 6,436 respondents) is the first systematic investigation of the **general lived experiences** of aphantasia — the inability to generate visual imagery when awake. Four main themes were identified: (1) Communicating and understanding the "mind's eye" in a world of visualisers, including frequent **disbelief and invalidation** from others; (2) The diverse experience of knowing without seeing, with some participants experiencing no imagery while others had faint or modality-specific imagery; (3) The **protective and disruptive ways** aphantasia shapes life and thoughts, with some reporting protection from trauma-related flashbacks while others described therapeutic techniques as ill-suited; (4) The search for meaning, including questioning whether aphantasia resulted from childhood emotional trauma. A critical finding was that many participants reported **standard therapies (CBT, exposure therapy, EMDR) were ineffective** because they rely heavily on imagery-based techniques. The study also found that some participants suspected their aphantasia was a **psychogenic response to childhood trauma**, though this remains unproven. Published in **Neuropsychologia**.

## Clinical Insight
This study has profound implications for clinical practice. Many standard evidence-based therapies for depression, anxiety, social anxiety, **PTSD, and OCD** rely on **imagery-based techniques** — yet for the estimated 3.9% of the population with aphantasia, these techniques may be inaccessible or ineffective. Clinicians should routinely assess clients' imagery ability, particularly when standard protocols (imaginal exposure, guided imagery, visualization-based CBT) are not working. For aphantasic clients, therapists may need to adapt techniques toward **sensory or verbal modalities** rather than visual imagery. The finding that some aphantasic individuals report a **protective effect against trauma-related flashbacks and intrusive images** suggests that aphantasia may mask or alter PTSD presentation — potentially leading to **underdiagnosis** since DSM-5 criteria include recurrent intrusive visual memories. Clinicians should also be aware that discovering one has aphantasia can cause **identity distress**, particularly when individuals realize therapies were not designed for their neurocognitive profile. Importantly, the study notes that aphantasia frequently co-occurs with **autism, ADHD, and dyslexia**, suggesting that neurodivergent clients may be disproportionately affected.

## Methodology Note
This is a qualitative-type paper (thematic analysis).

__Measurement Validity__: Thematic analysis was conducted on open-ended survey items plus unprompted email testimonies, providing rich qualitative data.
The VVIQ confirmed aphantasia in participants (mean=16.5, floor range).
The dual data collection method (survey + emails) limits standardization but increases ecological validity.

__Participant Transparency__: Sample of 52 aphantasic adults from 6,436 survey respondents, with age range 16-81.
20 UK residents and 18 USA residents, with others from various countries.
14 women, with gender distribution not fully reported.
Participants were selected from a larger quantitative study (Digard et al., 2026) that identified subtypes.

__Open Science__: Published in Neuropsychologia (peer-reviewed, DOI: 10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2026.109464).
Data available from corresponding author on reasonable request.
No preregistration (typical for qualitative research).

## Relevant For
- All clinicians using imagery-based therapeutic techniques
- Trauma therapists using EMDR, imaginal exposure, or prolonged exposure
- CBT practitioners whose protocols include visualization
- Clinicians working with neurodivergent populations (autism, ADHD, dyslexia)
- Therapists assessing treatment non-response
