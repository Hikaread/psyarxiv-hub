## Summary
This **mixed-methods systematic review** investigated the **experiences, barriers, and needs** of families of young people who **self-harm**. Recognizing that family support plays a crucial role in the young person's prognosis while caregiving itself takes a significant toll, the review synthesized both quantitative and qualitative evidence across multiple domains. The review mapped families' **emotional responses** (shock, guilt, helplessness, fear), their **information and support needs** (crisis management strategies, communication approaches, understanding self-harm), and the **barriers** they face (stigma, lack of professional guidance, fragmentation of services). The mixed-methods design allowed integration of prevalence data on family impact with rich qualitative descriptions of lived experience, providing a comprehensive picture of the **family caregiving context** in youth self-harm.

## Clinical Insight
Family members are often the **first responders** when a young person self-harms, yet they frequently receive little to no guidance on how to respond. This review consolidates evidence that families experience significant **psychological distress**, **caregiver burden**, and **information deficits** that can paradoxically undermine their ability to provide effective support. For clinicians, the review highlights the importance of **family-inclusive care** — not just family therapy, but practical psychoeducation about self-harm, **crisis management training** for family members, and acknowledgment of caregivers' own mental health needs. The identification of specific barriers (stigma, service fragmentation) points to **system-level interventions** alongside individual family work. The review also underscores that supporting families is not merely adjunctive to treating the young person — it is a **primary therapeutic target** in its own right.
