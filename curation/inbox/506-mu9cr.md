## Summary

This computational psychiatry study tested whether autism-related differences in social adaptation are better captured by **generative models of latent strategy use** or by **model-free temporal dynamics of choice**. A sample of **51 participants** (27 autistic, 24 healthy controls; children and adolescents) completed a **repeated hide-and-seek task** under social and non-social framing, against artificial opponents of graded sophistication (zero-order, biased, first-order). The same behavior was analyzed at three levels: aggregate performance, **Bayesian model selection** over candidate strategies, and **Volterra history kernels** measuring how current choices depended on recent self-actions and opponent-actions.

The key finding was that **fixed lag 1:8 history-kernel features outperformed both behavioral summaries and model-based strategy features** in individual-level ASD-HC classification (**balanced accuracy = .819, AUC = .824**), while clinical variables remained the strongest representation (balanced accuracy = .875, AUC = .968). ASD participants showed **stronger immediate self-history dependence** in simpler opponent conditions (non-social lag-1: d = 1.31, p = .004), while HC participants showed **stronger lag-2 self-history and opponent-history dependence** under first-order opponents requiring adaptive tracking (social self-history lag-2: d = -1.30, p = .002). Bayesian model selection revealed that in the social first-order condition, HC participants predominantly used **adaptive opponent-modeling strategies** (Ef = .838) versus ASD participants who primarily used **heuristic policies** (Ef = .771).

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians and researchers in autism assessment, this study proposes that the clinically relevant signal in social adaptation may reside not in which **cognitive strategy** a person uses, but in the **temporal organization of their choices** over time. The finding that autistic participants relied more on their own immediately preceding actions in simpler conditions, while controls engaged in deeper **recursive opponent-tracking** when required, maps onto the clinical observation that autistic individuals may have particular difficulty with adaptive social adjustment in dynamically contingent interactions. The **history-kernel approach** is computationally lightweight (standard regression) and could potentially complement existing diagnostic pathways as a **scalable, quantitative phenotyping tool**, though the authors explicitly note this was not designed as a diagnostic test.

## Methodology Note

This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample size is modest (27 ASD, 24 HC), and the authors acknowledge this as a limitation.
**Leave-one-subject-out cross-validation** was used for classification, which is appropriate for small samples but may yield optimistic estimates when the feature space is rich relative to N.
The fixed lag 1:8 kernel representation included 90 features for 51 participants, raising potential overfitting concerns despite ridge regularization.
The classification analysis is explicitly described as a "comparative probe of feature informativeness" rather than diagnostic validation.

__Preregistration__: No preregistration is mentioned for this specific analysis.
The study uses an overlapping sample from previously reported molecular and cognitive studies (Godazgar et al., 2023a, 2023b).

__Effect Sizes__: Key ASD-HC differences in early-window history kernels: social self-history lag-2 d = -1.30 (p = .002), d = -1.21 (p = .004).
Non-social opponent-history lag-3 d = -.84 (p = .045).
Non-social self-history lag-1 d = .98 (p = .056).
Aggregate accuracy difference in the social first-order condition: OR = 1.53 (p = .014).
Timeout rates were significantly elevated in ASD across several conditions (ORs = 0.24–0.50).

__Statistical Rigor__: The three-level analytical framework (behavioral, model-based, kernel-based) is conceptually strong and methodologically innovative.
**Ridge-regularized logistic regression** with leave-one-subject-out cross-validation was used appropriately.
**Generalized estimating equations** clustered by participant were used for aggregate behavioral analyses with Holm correction.
**Random-effects Bayesian model selection** was used for strategy inference.
The study transparently acknowledges that combining feature sets did not improve upon kernel features alone, suggesting the temporal information is the primary signal.

__Measurement Validity__: The repeated hide-and-seek task dissociates formal decision structure from social framing, allowing examination of social-cognitive processing under controlled conditions.
Clinical measures included the **Childhood Autism Rating Scale (CARS)** and **Happé's Strange Stories** for ToM assessment.
The OXTR rs2268498 and rs53576 polymorphisms and CNTNAP2 expression were measured in overlapping work.
The task itself is brief, non-verbal, and requires minimal language, making it accessible across diverse populations.

__Open Science__: Data and analysis code availability are not explicitly mentioned.
The study references supplementary materials extensively (S1–S16 Tables, S1–S15 Figures).

__Participant Transparency__: N = 51 (27 ASD, 24 HC), children and adolescents.
Ethics approval: IR.KHU.REC.1399.021, Kharazmi University, Iran.
The Iranian context and specific age range are described but detailed demographics (age distribution, sex ratio, IQ, verbal ability) are in supplementary materials not available here.
The modest sample and single-site recruitment in Iran limit generalizability.

## Notes

The "depth-before-strategy" framing offers a useful conceptual bridge between computational models of Theory of Mind and observable behavioral phenotyping. The connection to OXTR and CNTNAP2 molecular findings in the overlapping sample is theoretically interesting but underpowered for formal testing in this study. Future work should test kernel features in larger, more heterogeneous samples.
