## Summary

Two studies examined how secrets affect well-being through mind-wandering. A 14-day daily diary (N=240) found that worries about secrets intruded spontaneously, while deliberate mind-wandering involved fantasizing and daydreaming. A 7-day experience-sampling study (N=207) showed that spontaneous (but not deliberate) mind-wandering to secrets predicted greater negative emotion both in the moment and approximately 2 hours later. The key finding is that it is the involuntary, spontaneous intrusion of secret-related thoughts, rather than deliberate rumination, that drives the well-being costs of secrecy.

## Clinical Insight

Secrets are common in therapy, whether clients hold personal secrets or, in the case of therapists, professional ones. This research clarifies the mechanism: it is not that clients choose to think about their secrets but that the thoughts intrude unbidden, causing sustained negative affect. For therapists, this suggests that helping clients reduce spontaneous intrusions, perhaps through mindfulness or attentional retraining, may be more effective than encouraging them to deliberately process or disclose the secret. The finding that the negative affect persists for approximately 2 hours after intrusion also helps explain why secrets can feel chronically burdensome even when not actively being concealed.

## Relevant For

- Therapists working with clients who hold burdensome secrets
- Clinicians interested in rumination and intrusive thought processes
- Therapists using mindfulness-based approaches for unwanted thoughts
- Practitioners addressing shame and concealment in therapy
