## Summary
This study examined the network structure of non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) behaviours and their relationships to suicidal thoughts and behaviour (STB) in 327 autistic and 260 non-autistic adults pooled from two online surveys. Using cross-sectional network analysis, the authors identified three distinct NSSI clusters in autistic people compared to one cluster in non-autistic people, revealing fundamentally different patterns of self-harm co-occurrence. In both groups, some NSSI behaviours were only indirectly linked to STB via other NSSI behaviours. Cutting and ingesting dangerous substances or sharp objects emerged as most strongly related to suicide attempts. In longitudinal analyses of autistic participants across two time-points one year apart (N=168), LASSO regression showed that self-biting, breaking bones, and fighting to get hurt remained significant predictors of STB-related outcomes after controlling for baseline STB, while cutting, self-burning, and self-hitting did not. Measures included the Non-Suicidal Self-Injury: Assessment Tool (NSSI-AT), Self-Injurious Thoughts and Behaviors Interview (SITBI), PHQ-9 for depression, and the Interpersonal Needs Questionnaire-10 (INQ-10) for perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have significant implications for clinical risk assessment in autistic adults. The differential network structure of NSSI in autistic versus non-autistic people suggests that standard risk assessment approaches developed in non-autistic populations may not adequately capture suicide risk in autistic clients. The identification of specific NSSI behaviours (self-biting, bone-breaking, fighting to get hurt) as longitudinal predictors of STB in autistic people, distinct from the cross-sectional prominence of cutting, indicates that clinicians should broaden their assessment beyond the most visible or common forms of self-harm. Qualitative distinctions made by autistic people between sensory/meltdown-driven self-injury and emotionally motivated self-harm are supported by these quantitative network findings. Clinicians should remain aware that all forms of NSSI warrant serious clinical attention, even those that appear less directly linked to suicidality in cross-sectional analyses, as they may contribute to risk through indirect longitudinal pathways including impacts on psychosocial functioning, emotional dysregulation, and psychopathology.

## Relevant For
Clinicians assessing and managing self-harm and suicide risk in autistic adults, researchers studying NSSI network structures, and mental health services developing autism-inclusive safety planning and risk assessment protocols.
