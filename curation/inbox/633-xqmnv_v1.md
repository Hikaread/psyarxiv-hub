## Summary
This study (under peer review) evaluates a novel **norm-based dimensional rating approach** for assessing **early life adversity (ELA)** across four theoretical dimensions — **threat, deprivation, unpredictability, and uncontrollability** — and tests their associations with **emotion regulation** and **psychopathology**. In Sample 1 (N = 200, Prolific), normative ratings were developed by averaging 82 ELA item ratings from independent raters, then compared with participants' own self-ratings. In Sample 2 (N = 291, community), the Sample 1 norms were applied out-of-sample to test **replicability**. Across both samples, **norm-based ratings** explained more variance in emotion dysregulation and psychopathology than self-ratings (norm: R² = 0.09–0.23 vs. self: R² = 0.01–0.09). **Deprivation** was the dominant predictor of **depression, PTSD, and emotion dysregulation** (B = 0.40–1.89, ps < .01) and the only dimension negatively associated with **cognitive reappraisal** (B = −0.05, p = .007). Threat showed no unique positive associations and, due to extreme **multicollinearity** with deprivation (VIFs > 100), exhibited suppression effects. The authors conclude that ELA dimensions are highly **entangled at the person level**, preventing clean dimensional specificity in naturalistic samples, and that norm-based ratings offer a scalable tool for applying dimensional models to existing datasets.

## Clinical Insight
Understanding how different types of **early adversity** shape later emotion regulation difficulties is central to clinical formulation and treatment planning. This study has several implications for clinicians. First, the finding that **deprivation** — the absence of expected environmental inputs like emotional support and cognitive stimulation — was the dominant predictor across depression, PTSD, and emotion dysregulation suggests that clinicians working with trauma-exposed clients should pay particular attention to histories of **emotional and cognitive neglect**, not just threat-based trauma. The **DERS (Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale)** scores were most strongly predicted by deprivation, indicating that clients with deprivation histories may present with particularly pervasive regulatory difficulties. Second, the high collinearity between threat and deprivation (VIFs > 100) means that in clinical practice, these dimensions rarely occur in isolation — a client reporting physical abuse (threat) is also very likely to have experienced neglect (deprivation). This supports **transdiagnostic treatment approaches** that target common underlying regulatory mechanisms rather than dimension-specific interventions. Third, the demonstration that **norm-based ratings** outperform self-ratings for predicting outcomes suggests that clients' subjective appraisals of their adversity may be influenced by their current emotion regulation tendencies (e.g., clients who use cognitive reappraisal may perceive events as more controllable), which has implications for how clinicians interpret client narratives about their childhood experiences.

## Methodology Note
Paper type: quantitative_experimental.

__Statistical Power__: Two independent samples (N = 200 and N = 291) provide adequate power for the regression analyses conducted. The norm-based approach was tested with both mean and maximum scoring strategies. However, the study is cross-sectional, limiting causal inference about the direction of ELA–outcome associations.

__Preregistration__: The study was preregistered on OSF (osf.io/vybf8/). Deviations from preregistered analyses were transparently reported — sum scores and the dimensions of uncontrollability and unpredictability were excluded from regression models due to multicollinearity (VIFs > 50).

__Effect Sizes__: Regression coefficients (unstandardized B values) and R² values were consistently reported with significance tests. Norm-based deprivation explained R² = 0.09–0.23 of variance in outcomes, representing small-to-medium effect sizes. Confidence intervals were not reported for individual regression coefficients.

__Statistical Rigor__: The study used appropriate regression models with sensitivity analyses including age and gender as covariates (neither was significant). The multicollinearity issue (VIFs > 100) was acknowledged as a meaningful finding rather than a methodological failure, consistent with emerging field consensus (Pollak & Smith, 2026). Preregistered analyses were deviated from transparently. The cross-sectional design is a notable limitation.

__Measurement Validity__: Established measures were used — the **CTQ (Childhood Trauma Questionnaire)** and ABCD Study ELA items for exposure assessment, the **DERS** for emotion dysregulation, the **ERQ** for cognitive reappraisal, the **BDI-II** for depression, the **SCAARED** for anxiety, and the **PCL-5** for PTSD. The novel norm-based rating approach was validated across two independent samples with different recruitment methods (Prolific vs. community flyers). Norms did not differ significantly by race/ethnicity, though a small set of items differed by sex.

__Open Science__: Preregistration, study materials, and item-level normative ratings are publicly available on OSF (osf.io/vybf8/). De-identified data and analysis scripts are available from the corresponding author upon request rather than fully open.

__Participant Transparency__: Sample 1: N = 200 via Prolific (M_age = 23.06, 40.5% female, 48% racial minorities). Sample 2: N = 291 via online advertisements and community flyers (M_age = 22.40, 59.5% female, 60.5% racial minorities). Both are non-clinical community samples of young adults, which limits generalisability to treatment-seeking or clinical populations — acknowledged as a key limitation.
