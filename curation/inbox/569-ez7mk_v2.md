## Summary

This study examined links between **Cannabinoid Hyperemesis Syndrome (CHS)** symptoms and **suicidal thoughts and behaviours (STB)**, **proximal suicide risk factors**, and **non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI)** using data from the **National Firearms, Alcohol, Cannabis, and Suicide survey**, a nationally representative survey of **N = 7,034 US adults**. Four comparison groups were constructed: daily cannabis use with CHS symptoms (n = 191), daily cannabis use without CHS symptoms (n = 882), past-year cannabis use but not daily (n = 1,288), and no past-year cannabis use (n = 4,673). Those with CHS symptoms reported the highest lifetime and past-12-month prevalence of **suicidal thoughts and behaviours**, with nearly all being significantly higher than daily cannabis users without CHS symptoms. CHS was also associated with elevated **proximal suicide risk factors** and **NSSI**, even after controlling for cannabis use frequency. The findings identify CHS as a marker of elevated suicide risk among heavy cannabis users that extends beyond the effects of cannabis use itself.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians conducting **suicide risk assessments**, particularly in substance use and emergency settings, this study identifies CHS as an underrecognized clinical marker of elevated risk. The key clinical finding is that CHS symptoms — severe nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain in heavy cannabis users — signal a subgroup with significantly elevated **suicidal ideation**, **suicide attempts**, and **NSSI** beyond what cannabis use frequency alone would predict. This is critical because CHS is frequently misdiagnosed in emergency departments (often as cyclic vomiting syndrome), meaning the mental health comorbidities may go unaddressed. Clinicians working in **addiction treatment**, **emergency psychiatry**, or **primary care** should screen for CHS symptoms in heavy cannabis users and, when present, conduct thorough **suicide risk assessment** including proximal risk factors and NSSI history. The large, nationally representative sample (N = 7,034) strengthens confidence in the generalisability of findings to US adult populations. The association between a physical health condition (CHS) and suicidality also illustrates the importance of **integrated care** models that address both physical and mental health symptoms in substance-using populations. For therapists using **safety planning interventions**, awareness of CHS as a risk marker could prompt more intensive monitoring and earlier intervention.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians conducting suicide risk assessments in substance use settings
- Addiction treatment providers working with heavy cannabis users
- Emergency psychiatry and crisis intervention teams
- Primary care physicians screening for mental health in chronic cannabis users
- Public health researchers studying cannabis-related harms

## Notes

PDF was not available; curation based on the full abstract description. The cross-sectional design limits causal inference — it is unclear whether CHS directly increases suicide risk or whether shared vulnerability factors (e.g., impulsivity, trauma history) drive both. The CHS group (n = 191) is relatively small for subgroup analyses. The nationally representative sample is a major strength. The survey did not include structured clinical interviews for CHS diagnosis, relying on symptom self-report. Author Mark Ilgen is a prominent suicide and addiction researcher.
