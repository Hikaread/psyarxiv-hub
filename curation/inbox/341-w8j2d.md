## Summary

This pilot parallel-group RCT evaluated a self-guided, web-based single-session intervention (SSI) called Project EMPOWER, delivered to parents of children ages 4-13 with anxiety or OCD who were on a waitlist at a hospital-based CBT clinic. The SSI targets parental accommodation, a well-established family factor that can worsen child anxiety and undermine CBT efficacy. Parents (N=47) were randomized to receive the SSI (N=24) or waitlist as usual (N=23). At 2-week follow-up, parents who received the SSI reported significant reductions in accommodation (d=0.59), child anxiety symptoms (d=1.25), and significant increases in parent agency (d=0.68). Effects were maintained until children started CBT (mean wait of 13.54 weeks). Parents rated the SSI as highly acceptable per preregistered benchmarks.

## Clinical Insight

Single-session interventions for parents offer a practical, scalable solution to one of the most frustrating systemic problems in child mental health: long waitlists that leave families without support for months. For clinicians running waitlisted services, this study provides evidence that a brief, self-guided web module targeting parental accommodation can meaningfully reduce both accommodation behaviors and child anxiety symptoms while families wait for formal therapy. The effect size on child anxiety (d=1.25) is strikingly large for such a minimal intervention, though the small sample size warrants caution. The focus on parental accommodation is well-chosen: it is one of the most modifiable and clinically impactful factors in child anxiety, and reducing it before CBT begins may actually improve subsequent treatment outcomes. Clinicians should consider recommending SSI resources to families on their waitlists, and those in private practice can use the core principles (psychoeducation about accommodation, guided exercises to reduce it) as take-home materials in the very first session.

## Relevant For

Clinicians working with child anxiety in community or hospital settings, particularly those managing long waitlists, and therapists interested in single-session intervention approaches
