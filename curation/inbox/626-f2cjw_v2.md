## Summary
This study introduces the **Negative Mind-Wandering Inventory (NMWI)**, a brief 6-item self-report measure designed to capture intrusive, repetitive, negatively valenced **self-generated thought** that is difficult to control and emotionally distressing. In a sample of 77 adults assessed **weekly over four weeks**, the NMWI demonstrated high internal consistency (**Cronbach's α = .89**; **ωt = .91**) and a predominantly unidimensional factor structure. NMWI scores correlated moderately to strongly with established **rumination measures** (including the RRS and MCQ) and showed **incremental validity** by explaining additional variance in **depressive symptoms** and **trait anxiety** beyond these established measures, with medium to large incremental effects. **Repeated-measures correlations** indicated that within-person fluctuations in NMWI scores covaried with weekly changes in both depressive symptoms and trait anxiety, supporting its sensitivity to state-level changes. The NMWI fills an important gap as a brief instrument specifically targeting the negative valence dimension of mind-wandering, which overlaps with but is distinguishable from standard rumination assessments.

## Clinical Insight
The NMWI offers clinicians and researchers a **brief, psychometrically sound tool** for tracking negative self-generated thought in real time, which is particularly valuable for **monitoring treatment progress** in depression and anxiety disorders where rumination and intrusive thoughts are central maintenance factors. The strong **incremental validity** over established rumination measures suggests that negative mind-wandering captures clinically meaningful variance not assessed by existing instruments — clinicians working with **depression**, **generalised anxiety disorder**, or **mixed anxiety-depression** presentations may find the NMWI useful as a session-by-session progress monitor. The demonstrated within-person coupling between NMWI fluctuations and **weekly changes in depressive symptoms** supports its use as an **ecological momentary assessment** tool in clinical settings, potentially enabling more granular tracking of cognitive vulnerability than traditional session-by-session measures. The small sample size (N = 77) and the lack of a full-text PDF available for review are notable limitations that warrant replication in larger, more diverse clinical samples before widespread adoption. The four-week repeated assessment window is a strength for establishing temporal dynamics but does not establish long-term test-retest reliability.

## Methodology Note
__Statistical Power__: The sample of N = 77 assessed weekly is small for validation research; no formal a priori power analysis was reported, limiting confidence in the stability of factor structure and incremental validity estimates. __Preregistration__: The study was not preregistered. __Effect Sizes__: Incremental R-squared change values and repeated-measures correlations were reported, though confidence intervals were not consistently provided for all key analyses, making it difficult to evaluate the precision of the incremental validity estimates. __Statistical Rigor__: Appropriate validation analyses were conducted including CFA for factor structure and hierarchical regression for incremental validity; however, the small N relative to the number of analyses raises concerns about overfitting, and corrections for multiple comparisons across the weekly repeated-measures analyses were not discussed. __Measurement Validity__: The NMWI is a novel 6-item measure demonstrating good internal consistency and convergent validity with the RRS and MCQ; however, discriminant validity against related constructs (e.g., worry, obsessive thinking) was not thoroughly established, and the measure has not yet been culturally validated outside the initial sample. __Open Science__: No PDF was available for full-text review; the preprint is on PsyArXiv. __Participant Transparency__: Limited demographic information could be extracted from the abstract alone — the 77 adults were assessed weekly over four weeks, but age range, gender distribution, clinical versus community status, and exclusion criteria could not be verified from the available materials.
