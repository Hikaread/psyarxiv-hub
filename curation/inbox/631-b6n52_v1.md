## Summary
This large-scale metascientific audit examined **transparent reporting** and **open science practices** across 2,431 psychological studies using the **Experience Sampling Method (ESM)** published between 2010 and 2023. Data extraction combined manual coding (89 papers) with **GPT-5-based automated extraction** (95.8% overall accuracy; 100% precision for focal open science variables). Key findings: only **4.6%** of ESM studies declared **preregistration** (4.4% accessible); **9.6%** declared **open code** (9.2% accessible); **9.8%** declared **open data** (9.2% accessible), with a further 16.5% offering data upon request. **Sample size justification** was provided in only 19.5% of papers (8.5% via power analysis). **ESM design transparency** was generally high (98.1% reported study duration; 94.8% sampling frequency), but **compliance rates** were reported in only 74.4%. **Software versions** were reported in 79.9% of studies specifying software, but **package versions** were specified in only 35.7% of R/Python studies naming packages. Temporal trends show growing adoption of open practices beginning around 2018–2019, with declared prevalence increasing ~2.4 percentage points per year for open data and ~2.5 for open code.

## Clinical Insight
ESM is increasingly central to **clinical assessment and treatment research** in mental health — used to study **affective dynamics in depression**, **craving in addiction**, **suicidal ideation trajectories**, **panic symptom patterns**, and **therapeutic change processes** in real time. This audit reveals that the evidential foundation of ESM-based clinical research is undermined by poor transparency: over 90% of studies lack preregistration, and fewer than 10% share data or code. For **clinicians interpreting ESM-based evidence** to inform practice (e.g., whether momentary assessment shows that a particular therapeutic mechanism operates as claimed), the low reproducibility infrastructure means these findings may be less trustworthy than they appear. The finding that **package versions are reported in only 35.7%** of R/Python studies is particularly concerning for clinical research replication, as numerical results can change across package versions. The audit provides a benchmark for the field and suggests that journal editors, reviewers, and funding bodies should mandate stricter transparency requirements for ESM-based clinical research. The **innovative use of GPT-5 for large-scale metascientific data extraction** (with 100% precision on focal variables and manual verification of all positives) demonstrates a scalable approach for future audits.

## Methodology Note
This is an **other-type** metascience paper (systematic audit), so Statistical Power, Preregistration, and Effect Sizes dimensions are not applicable in the traditional sense.

__Statistical Rigor__: The audit used rigorous methods including 95% Wilson confidence intervals for all proportions, manual verification of all declared open materials (checking actual accessibility), LLM-human comparison on 89 papers, and manual resolution of all LLM "check" outputs; the combination of automated and manual extraction is a methodological strength.

__Measurement Validity__: The codebook was designed with conditional coding logic; LLM extraction achieved 95.8% accuracy overall and 100% precision (zero false positives) for the three focal open science variables; the lower-bound nature of the prevalence estimates (due to potential false negatives) was transparently acknowledged.

__Open Science__: Exemplary — the OSF repository (osf.io/89pbn) includes the full extraction script, prompts, LLM parameters, grading files, and supplementary materials; the paper itself models the transparency it advocates.

__Participant Transparency__: The audit covered 2,431 ESM studies published between 2010–2023 across psychology and neuroscience; searches were conducted in Scopus, Web of Science, and PubMed with clear inclusion/exclusion criteria; publication year distribution and database coverage were reported.
