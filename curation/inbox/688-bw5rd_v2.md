## Summary
This study explored **biological correlates of psychopathology** in **1,229 children and adolescents** (ages 10–15) from the ABCD Study who completed both the **CBCL 6–18** and laboratory blood tests (CBC, HDL, cholesterol, ferritin). Using OLS regression and hierarchical clustering of correlation matrices, the study found that **background auto-inflammation** (elevated platelet count, WBC, monocytes, neutrophils), **anemia** (likely secondary to inflammation), and **male sex** were robust correlates of total psychopathology load. **Neutrophilic inflammation** correlated more strongly with the **internalizing dimension**, while **male sex** correlated with the **externalizing dimension**. **Eosinophils** correlated specifically with **ADHD**. The paper advocates for the **Sigma-factor model** — conceptualizing psychopathology as **network-level dysregulation** across interconnected physiological systems, not isolated categorical disorders.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians, this study supports an **integrative view of child and adolescent psychopathology** that bridges psychological and immunological domains. The key clinical message is that a routine, inexpensive **complete blood count** can reveal inflammation patterns associated with specific psychological presentations — internalizing symptoms linked to neutrophilic inflammation, ADHD linked to eosinophils. This does not mean CBC should be used diagnostically, but it strengthens the case for **screening for medical comorbidities** in youth presenting with psychological difficulties. The finding that inflammation and anemia are associated with the general psychopathology factor (p-factor) aligns with growing evidence that **physical and mental health are deeply intertwined** in young people. Clinicians should consider that persistent psychological symptoms in children may have **inflammatory components** that warrant medical evaluation, and that treating comorbid medical conditions (e.g., iron deficiency) could potentially improve psychological outcomes.

## Methodology Note
This is a secondary data analysis of the ABCD Study (cross-sectional).

__Design__: Secondary analysis of ABCD Study Release 5.1 data.
1,229 unique cases with simultaneous CBC and CBCL measurements at 24-, 36-, or 48-month follow-ups.
OLS regression with CBCL Total Problems as dependent variable.
Hierarchical clustering of Spearman correlation matrices with bootstrapped 95% CIs.

__Measurement Validity__: CBCL 6–18 is a well-validated, widely used measure of child psychopathology.
Good internal consistency for syndromic scales (α = 0.89) and DSM-5 oriented scales (α = 0.87).
CBC parameters are objective laboratory measures with established clinical reference ranges.
Multiple testing concern given the large number of correlation analyses conducted.

__Limitations__: Cross-sectional; cannot determine directionality (does inflammation cause psychopathology, vice versa, or both?).
ABCD subsample with CBC data (N = 1,229) may not represent the full ABCD cohort or general population.
Race not included as covariate per current recommendations, but ACS Raked Propensity Score was included.
Age range 10–15 limits generalizability to younger children or adults.
Single author study; no independent replication.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
Uses publicly available ABCD Study data (NIMH Data Archive).
Analysis code and approach clearly described, supporting reproducibility.

## Notes
The Sigma-factor framework offers a compelling alternative to purely categorical diagnostic thinking. The differential inflammatory profiles for internalizing vs. externalizing dimensions could eventually inform personalized treatment approaches, though this is speculative at this stage. The practical value of CBC as a screening tool for inflammatory contributions to psychopathology deserves further investigation.
