## Summary

This paper presents the **ACCENT (Agenda for Culturally Competent Neuropsychological Research and Testing) Checklist**, a concise, practical tool designed to guide ethical, culturally sensitive, and methodologically rigorous **cross-cultural neuropsychological research** from design through dissemination. The checklist was developed through **stepwise synthesis** of a comprehensive literature review and the authors' combined experience conducting neuropsychological research in diverse **Global South contexts**, including South Africa. ACCENT is organized into **six domains**: (a) **research team composition**, (b) **community and participant engagement**, (c) **study design**, (d) **test selection and adaptation**, (e) **cultural competency training**, and (f) **transparent reporting**. The checklist contains **19 items**: 15 classed as required, two as conditional, one as aspirational, and one as conditional-aspirational. The paper situates ACCENT within the context of the **Nieuwoudt et al. (2020) retraction** — a high-profile case of culturally insensitive cross-cultural neuropsychological research — and addresses dimensions of **epistemic justice** and **structural equity** that prior guidelines leave unaddressed. A planned **multi-method validation programme** comprising Delphi consensus, content validity, cognitive interviewing, and simulation studies is described. Full text extracted from a 45-page PDF.

## Clinical Insight

For **clinical neuropsychologists** and **assessment practitioners** working with culturally diverse populations, ACCENT provides a much-needed practical framework for ensuring that cognitive assessments are conducted and interpreted responsibly. The checklist directly addresses a critical problem in clinical practice: the routine use of **Western-normed cognitive tests** with populations for whom those norms are inappropriate, leading to potential **misdiagnosis** of cognitive impairment, intellectual disability, or neurocognitive disorder. The **test selection and adaptation** domain (Domain D) is particularly relevant for clinicians, as it prompts systematic consideration of whether tests have been validated for the specific cultural and linguistic group being assessed, whether translated versions maintain psychometric equivalence, and whether local normative data are available. The **community and participant engagement** domain (Domain B) has implications for **culturally responsive assessment** practices more broadly — clinicians should consider whether their assessment approach respects local understandings of cognition, health, and help-seeking. For **clinical supervisors** and **training programs**, ACCENT can serve as a teaching tool for developing cultural competence in trainees. The checklist's emphasis on **epistemic justice** — ensuring that knowledge produced about diverse populations does not perpetuate colonial power dynamics — challenges clinicians to examine how their assessment practices may inadvertently pathologize cultural difference.

## Relevant For

- Training programs in clinical neuropsychology

- Test publishers and adaptation teams

## Notes

Full 45-page PDF was extracted. The checklist is proposed but not yet validated — the planned validation programme (Delphi, content validity, cognitive interviewing, simulation studies) is described but not yet completed. The Nieuwoudt et al. retraction as a motivating example provides powerful context. The Global South expertise of the authors (particularly South Africa) is a strength. The 19-item checklist is appropriately concise for practical use. Some items may be challenging to implement in resource-limited settings (e.g., community co-design of studies). The distinction between required and aspirational items helps with feasibility.
