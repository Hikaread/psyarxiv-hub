## Summary

This exploratory study examined whether a 12-session yoga psychology intervention could shift primal world beliefs (fundamental assumptions about the world) and improve well-being in adults with mild-to-moderate depression (N=31). Using a single-group pre-post design, results showed significant improvements in well-being across multiple measures (all p<.001), increases in positive primal beliefs (Safe, Enticing, Alive, Good), changes in Triguna (increased sattva, reduced rajas and tamas), and decreased depressive symptoms. Informant ratings paralleled self-reports, suggesting observable change.

## Clinical Insight

This study introduces an interesting therapeutic angle: addressing fundamental world beliefs through yoga-based interventions. For therapists, this suggests that helping clients shift deeply held beliefs about the world (e.g., from seeing it as dangerous to safe) may accompany broader improvements in well-being. While the single-group design limits causal inference, the convergence of self-report and informant data adds credibility. Therapists could incorporate elements of yoga psychology or body-based interventions when working with depressed clients who hold deeply negative world assumptions, particularly as a complement to evidence-based approaches like CBT.

## Relevant For

- Therapists working with mild-to-moderate depression
- Clinicians interested in integrative or mind-body approaches
- Practitioners incorporating yoga or somatic interventions
- Therapists addressing maladaptive core beliefs about the world
