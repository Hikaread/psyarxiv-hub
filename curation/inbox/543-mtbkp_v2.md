## Summary
This ecological momentary assessment (EMA) study examined affective reactivity to racial discrimination and general stress in 493 African American adults (Mage = 65.8, SD = 5.5, range 55-75; 72% female) from The Heart of Detroit Study. Participants reported momentary negative and positive affect, experiences of racial discrimination, and other stress up to four times daily during a seven-day protocol, analyzed with residual dynamic structural equation models. Both general stress and discrimination showed concurrent associations with increased negative affect and decreased positive affect. However, when considered jointly, only general stress maintained its concurrent association — discrimination effects did not survive adjustment. A novel cyclical pathway emerged: elevated negative affect predicted higher intraindividual stress four hours later, and elevated positive affect predicted lower intraindividual stress, while general stress did not prospectively predict future affect. Between-person depressive symptoms and neuroticism were associated with higher mean stress, higher mean negative affect, lower mean positive affect, and greater intraindividual variability in positive affect.

## Clinical Insight
The finding that affect drives future stress (rather than the reverse) has important implications for therapeutic intervention. For African American adults, interventions that increase positive affect or reduce negative affect in the moment may create a downward cycle of reduced stress exposure over time, rather than merely managing stress's aftermath. This supports the incorporation of affect-regulation strategies and behavioral activation into treatment for this population, rather than focusing solely on stress management or coping skills. The finding that racial discrimination's effect on momentary affect was subsumed by general stress suggests that for clinical work, the broader stress burden may be a more important treatment target than discrimination-specific interventions, though this does not diminish the systemic importance of addressing discrimination itself. The between-person associations linking depression, neuroticism, and positive affect variability highlight positive affect as both a marker of vulnerability and a potential intervention target in midlife and older African American adults.

## Relevant For
Clinicians working with African American adults; those treating late-life depression and anxiety; therapists incorporating EMA-informed approaches; researchers studying affect-stress relationships; practitioners interested in health disparities and discrimination.

## Notes
Sample is middle-aged and older African American adults (55-75), which may limit generalizability to younger populations. The EMA protocol assessed discrimination and stress concurrently, making causal claims about their unique contributions tentative.
