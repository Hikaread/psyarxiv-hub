## Summary

This pre-registered RCT evaluated Clever Kids, a nine-week socio-emotional wellbeing programme developed specifically for upper primary school children with dyslexia. Seventy-eight children (mean age 10.19 years, 54% female) with clinically diagnosed dyslexia were randomized to the programme (n=38) or a wait-list control (n=40). Outcomes included coping skills, emotion regulation, self-esteem, perseverance, assertiveness, and internalising and externalising symptoms measured at pre-, post-, and three-month follow-up. Significant intervention-by-time interactions were found for six outcomes: child-reported coping, emotion regulation, academic and social self-esteem, and parent-reported anxiety and depression, all maintained at three-month follow-up. The programme focuses on building coping strategies and emotional skills within a peer group of children who share the experience of dyslexia.

## Clinical Insight

Children with learning difficulties such as dyslexia are frequently overlooked in mental health provision, yet they experience elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem that often persist into adolescence and adulthood. This study demonstrates that a targeted, nine-week group programme can produce meaningful improvements across multiple domains of functioning. For therapists who work with children or young people, the findings underscore the importance of screening for learning difficulties when a child presents with anxiety or low mood, as the two are closely intertwined and the learning difficulty may be the primary driver of the emotional difficulty. Rather than treating the anxiety in isolation, referring to or replicating structured programmes like Clever Kids that address coping, emotion regulation, and self-esteem in the context of the child's specific learning profile may be more effective. The group format also leverages the normalizing power of shared experience among children with dyslexia, which can itself be therapeutic.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with children and young people who have co-occurring learning difficulties and mental health needs, particularly in educational or school-based settings
