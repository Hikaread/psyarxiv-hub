## Summary

This study examines whether religious and spiritual experiences (RSEs) that follow depressive episodes resemble hypomanic or manic states. Using content analysis and phenomenological analysis of 249 first-person RSE reports associated with depression, the authors characterize the duration, emotional valence, and phenomenological features of these experiences. Findings reveal that some post-depression RSEs involve prolonged states (days to weeks) of positive emotion, grandiosity, and increased energy that closely parallel hypomanic/manic presentations.

## Clinical Insight

Differential diagnosis between spiritual experiences and manic/hypomanic episodes is a clinically important challenge, particularly in culturally diverse therapeutic settings. Therapists working with clients who report spiritual or religious experiences following depression need to carefully distinguish between genuine spiritual growth, normative religious experience, and potentially bipolar spectrum pathology. This study provides a phenomenological framework for making this distinction, which is directly applicable to clinical assessment and formulation. The findings support a more nuanced approach to cultural competence in mood disorder assessment, where spiritual experiences are not automatically pathologized but are also not reflexively accepted as benign.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with bipolar spectrum disorders, culturally diverse populations, clients integrating spiritual and psychological frameworks, and clinicians conducting differential diagnoses between spiritual experiences and psychopathology.

## Notes

N=249 first-person reports provides substantial qualitative data. Phenomenological approach captures subjective experience richness. Does not include structured clinical interviews to verify bipolar diagnoses.
