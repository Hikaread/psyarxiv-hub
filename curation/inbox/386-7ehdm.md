## Summary
This self-replication study re-examined whether measuring alcohol craving before an experimental task influences post-test craving levels, extending original COVID-lockdown findings to a post-lockdown context. Using 142 psychology students in a pre-post vs. post-only design with an alcohol-related video, the study confirmed that heavier drinking and greater video engagement predict craving. However, the key finding that repeated craving measurement inflates post-test levels did not replicate. Reanalysis of original data revealed a gender-specific pattern where the measurement effect was present in men but absent in women.

## Clinical Insight
The partial replication failure reminds therapists that psychological mechanisms observed under unusual circumstances (such as lockdown) may not generalise to normal conditions, a caution relevant to applying research findings in clinical practice. For addiction treatment, the robust finding that engagement with alcohol-related content predicts craving reinforces the clinical importance of identifying and modifying clients' patterns of engagement with alcohol cues in their environment. Gender differences in craving reactivity suggest that treatment approaches may need to be tailored, with men potentially more susceptible to measurement-induced cue reactivity.

## Relevant For
Addiction therapists, CBT practitioners treating alcohol misuse, clinical researchers studying craving mechanisms.

## Notes
Used student sample which limits generalisability to clinical populations. The gender interaction finding came from reanalysis of original data rather than the replication sample. Context of video exposure may differ from real-world craving triggers.
