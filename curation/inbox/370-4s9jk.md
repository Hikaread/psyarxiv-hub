## Summary

This study tested a one-week daily journalling intervention called "well done" journalling among 67 high-achieving university students. Participants wrote daily about small actions they had done well. While subjective wellbeing did not change significantly from an already elevated baseline, perceived work performance improved significantly. A dose-response pattern emerged: participants who journaled more frequently showed stronger performance improvements. Qualitative feedback highlighted perceived benefits in positive affect, self-esteem, and motivation.

## Clinical Insight

This study offers therapists a simple, accessible intervention tool for clients who struggle with self-criticism or perfectionism. The "well done" journalling approach shifts focus from what went wrong to what went right, which can be particularly helpful for high-functioning clients who tend to dismiss their accomplishments. The dose-response finding supports assigning it as between-session homework with daily practice. The fact that wellbeing did not improve (likely due to ceiling effects in this sample) should not discourage therapists from using it with clinical populations who typically present with lower baseline wellbeing where the effect may be more pronounced.

## Relevant For

- Therapists working with perfectionistic or self-critical clients
- Clinicians incorporating positive psychology interventions into practice
- Therapists assigning between-session homework tasks
- Practitioners working with high-achieving or burnout-prone professionals
