## Summary
This **pre-registered study** examined cross-national variation in **gender differences in emotion experiences** across **60 countries** (N = 19,936, M age = 37.26).
The study distinguished between **communal emotions** (compassion, gratitude, love) and **power-related emotions** (anger as a powerful emotion; fear, sadness, anxiety as powerless emotions), testing whether **economic development (HDI)** and socio-structural factors predict the magnitude of gender differences.
For communal emotions, greater economic development predicted **larger gender differences** favoring women — consistent with the **"gender equality paradox"**.
This pattern was partially explained by **decreased cultural collectivism** and higher **female labor force participation**, but NOT by occupational sex segregation.
For the powerful emotion (anger), the pattern was reversed: economic development predicted **narrower gender differences**, with development more strongly reducing anger among women.
For **powerless negative emotions** (fear, sadness, anxiety), women consistently reported higher levels than men **across all countries**, and economic development did NOT predict the size of the gender gap.
**Structural gender equality** (GGGI) also predicted larger gender differences in communal emotions, but this effect did not hold above and beyond HDI.
All findings remained robust after controlling for **data quality** and country- and individual-level demographics.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have direct implications for **culturally competent clinical practice**, particularly for therapists working with **emotion-focused therapies** such as **ACT, CBT, and emotion-focused therapy (EFT)**.
The universal finding that women report more **fear, sadness, and anxiety** across all 60 countries — regardless of development level or gender equality — suggests that clinicians should be attuned to gendered emotion presentation patterns while avoiding assumptions that these differences are purely cultural or socially constructed.
The **communion-power distinction** provides a useful framework for therapists to understand that gender differences are not uniform across all emotions.
In more economically developed, individualistic contexts, women may experience and express **communal emotions more intensely** than men, which could affect how emotional material is processed in therapy.
For **cross-cultural assessment**, clinicians should be aware that gendered emotion norms vary significantly by cultural context — what is considered normative emotional expression for men and women differs across societies, and this should inform **case formulation** and **treatment expectations**.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample of **N = 19,936** across **60 countries** provides substantial statistical power for individual-level analyses.
However, for **country-level moderator analyses** (testing whether HDI predicts the size of gender differences), the effective sample size is only 60 countries, which limits the detectable effect sizes and increases the risk of **Type II errors** for country-level predictors.
The multilevel models appropriately account for the nested data structure (individuals within countries).

__Preregistration__: The study was **pre-registered**, and hypotheses were clearly specified in advance.
The preregistration covered the main effects and moderation analyses, and the authors note that some exploratory analyses (individualism, labor force participation) were not preregistered.
Preregistration strengthens confidence in the reported findings, particularly the distinction between communal and power-related emotion patterns.

__Statistical Rigor__: **Multilevel modeling** was used to account for the nested data structure (individuals within countries), with cross-level interactions testing whether country-level factors moderate gender differences.
Controlling for **data quality** at the country level is a methodological strength that addresses concerns about measurement invariance across diverse samples.
The **Cronbach's alpha** for communal positive emotions was below .60 in 14 of 60 countries, which is a notable measurement concern — the authors addressed this by analyzing each emotion separately, finding consistent results.

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes were reported using multilevel model coefficients (B, SE, t, p) with **95% confidence intervals** for simple slopes analyses.
Country-level gender gaps were visualized with **95% confidence intervals**, providing clear communication of uncertainty.
The **POMP (percent of maximum possible)** framework was not used here but the raw coefficients with CIs allow readers to gauge practical significance.

__Measurement Validity__: Emotion experiences were assessed using established self-report measures.
A limitation is the potential **measurement non-invariance** across cultures — the same emotion words may carry different meanings in different languages and cultural contexts.
The low **Cronbach's alpha** for communal emotions in 14 countries raises concerns about the **internal consistency** of the composite measure in those contexts, though individual emotion analyses were consistent.
The **cross-sectional design** limits causal inference about the direction of relationships between cultural factors and emotion experiences.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint** and was pre-registered.
The large, cross-national dataset provides a valuable resource for replication and secondary analysis.
Data and analysis code availability were not explicitly mentioned in the available text.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample included **19,936 participants** across **60 countries** with a mean age of 37.26.
Individual-level demographic controls (age, gender, education) and country-level controls (data quality, GDP) were included.
The sampling strategy within each country was not detailed in the abstract.
The large and diverse sample is a major strength for cross-cultural generalizability.
