## Summary
Two large-scale surveys of **American knowledge workers** during the COVID-19 pandemic (Study 1: N = 32,245 across six waves; Study 2: N = 5,216 longitudinal, 48% retention) examined how **remote work** relates to **racial discrimination** and **well-being**. Using within-person analyses, workers of color reported significantly less **racial discrimination when working remotely** than in person (Study 1: beta = -.10, p < .001; Study 2: beta = -.18, p < .001). This effect was more pronounced among **women of color** (beta = -.21) than **men of color** (beta = -.14). Across both studies, greater **remote work frequency** predicted better well-being outcomes including work-life balance, job satisfaction, psychological safety, affective organizational commitment, and lower stress — effects that were largely consistent across racial groups. For most well-being outcomes, the benefits of remote work were **comparable for workers of color and White workers**, with a few exceptions where White workers benefitted slightly more. Results were robust to propensity score matching and demographic/organizational controls. All materials, data, and analysis code are available on OSF.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians whose clients experience **workplace racial discrimination** — a known contributor to **anxiety, depression, and stress-related disorders** — these findings provide empirical support for discussing **remote work arrangements** as a potential protective factor within a broader coping and wellbeing strategy. The intersectional finding that **women of color** experience the greatest reduction in discrimination when working remotely aligns with clinical observations that individuals at the intersection of multiple marginalized identities face compounded workplace stressors. The study also demonstrates that remote work confers **mental health benefits** (reduced stress and anxiety, greater psychological safety) without disadvantaging workers of other racial groups, which is important for clinicians advising clients navigating **return-to-office mandates**. Clinicians working in **organizational psychology** or **workplace mental health** can use these findings to advocate for flexible work policies as part of systemic interventions to reduce discrimination-related distress.

## Methodology Note
Paper type: quantitative_experimental. __Statistical Power__: Both samples are very large (N = 32,245 and N = 5,216), providing excellent power for detecting even small effects. No a priori power analysis was reported, though the sample sizes far exceed conventional thresholds. __Effect Sizes__: Standardized betas with 95% CIs are reported throughout, and simple slopes are provided for interaction effects, enabling evaluation of practical significance. __Statistical Rigor__: The use of within-person analyses for discrimination (RQ1) effectively controls for between-person confounds. Propensity score matching was used as a robustness check for well-being analyses (RQ2). Holm correction and control variables (age, organizational tenure, company size, seniority) strengthen causal inference. However, data were collected during COVID-19, potentially inflating remote work benefits. No preregistration. __Measurement Validity__: Study 1 used ad hoc single-item well-being measures; Study 2 improved upon this with validated scales (affective commitment alpha = .92/.93, turnover intentions r = .77-.78). The racial discrimination measure used only two items. __Open Science__: All materials, data, and code are publicly available on OSF. This is a notable strength. __Participant Transparency__: Study 1 recruited via Qualtrics panels with sampling quotas; Study 2 oversampled workers of color (54% of sample). Detailed racial/ethnic breakdowns are provided. Both samples are limited to American knowledge workers during COVID-19, restricting temporal and occupational generalisability.
