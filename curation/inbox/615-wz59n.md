## Summary

This single-case longitudinal study examined how **semantic similarity** among training items affects long-term vocabulary re-learning in two individuals with aphasia who present dissociated lexical retrieval deficit profiles: **XR** (56-year-old female, 22 years post-stroke, right hemisphere damage, **activation deficit**) and **QD** (65-year-old male, 12 years post-stroke, left MCA stroke, **inhibition deficit**). Over six weeks of one-on-one training (27 items per participant in related, unrelated, and mixed block conditions, integrating **repetition**, **retrieval practice**, and a variant of **Semantic Feature Analysis** using distinctive features), the study assessed retention at five timepoints up to 16 weeks post-training. The critical finding was a **double dissociation**: semantic similarity **hurt long-term retention** for QD (inhibition deficit) — related blocks were significantly worse than both mixed (z = 2.403, p = .016) and unrelated (z = −2.625, p = .009) at long-term follow-up — but had **no detrimental effect** for XR (activation deficit). Approximately **60% of trained items** were retained at 6 weeks. The study also found that **mixed blocks** (semantically related items spread across different blocks within a session) effectively ameliorated the harmful effect for the inhibition deficit, performing similarly to unrelated blocks. The authors explicitly reject **desirable difficulty** as an explanation: what is difficult during training does not become easier with time — it remains difficult.

## Clinical Insight

This study has immediate practical implications for **aphasia rehabilitation** and speech-language therapy. The key clinical message is that **deficit-specific treatment planning** matters: the effect of semantic similarity on vocabulary learning depends critically on whether the patient's primary difficulty is activating lexical items or inhibiting competitors. For patients with **inhibition deficits** — who produce multiple semantically related words and have impaired performance on the Simon, Miscue, and Stroop tasks — clinicians should avoid grouping semantically related words together in training sets. Instead, the simple strategy of **spreading related items across different training blocks within a session** substantially ameliorates the detrimental effect. For patients with **activation deficits** — who show slow naming with many omissions and unimpaired inhibitory control — semantic similarity is not a major concern and may even be slightly beneficial. The training protocol itself, integrating repetition, retrieval practice, and **distinctive semantic features** (rather than category-shared features like "it's a fruit"), achieved robust long-term retention. This distinctive-features approach is particularly important for inhibition-deficit patients because it enhances target-specific activation without broadly activating competitors. The theoretical framework of **flexible criterion** lexical selection, where the system adjusts inhibition levels based on precision demands, offers clinicians a way to understand why their patients may perform well in some contexts but struggle in others.
