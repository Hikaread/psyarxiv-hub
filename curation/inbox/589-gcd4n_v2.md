## Summary
This study analysed 140 police files on complex **delayed-reporting sexual abuse** cases in the Netherlands, examining whether three memory types — **continuous memories**, **spontaneously recovered memories**, and **recovered memories following an active search** — could be reliably differentiated and whether they differed in **corroborative evidence**. Memory type classification achieved satisfactory **interrater reliability**. Statements from the continuous memory group were corroborated significantly more often than those in the recovered-memory-following-search group, which were **rarely supported by strong evidence**. The comparison between actively searched and spontaneously recovered memories remained inconclusive. Exploratory analyses revealed that spontaneously recovered memories more often involved **single incidents**, whereas recovered memories following an active search frequently involved **extended abuse** including multiple perpetrators. The findings suggest memory retrieval context systematically influences the likelihood of corroborative evidence being available.

## Clinical Insight
This large-scale archival study provides the most comprehensive field evidence to date on the relationship between **memory type** and **evidentiary support** in delayed sexual abuse reports. For therapists and forensic evaluators, the findings carry nuanced implications. The lower corroboration rate for memories recovered during active searching does not mean such memories are false, but it does indicate greater involvement of **reconstructive processes** that may introduce inaccuracies. The clinical distinction between spontaneously recovered memories (single incidents, intermediate corroboration) and actively searched memories (extended abuse, low corroboration) is particularly important: therapists should be aware that therapeutic practices encouraging active exploration of possible past abuse may yield memories with different evidential characteristics than those that emerge spontaneously. This has implications for **therapeutic technique**, **informed consent**, and **documentation** practices. The findings also underscore that delayed reporting alone does not indicate unreliability — **continuous memories** reported after long delays still showed high corroboration rates, consistent with the well-established robustness of genuinely traumatic memories.

## Relevant For
- Forensic psychologists conducting statement validity assessments
- Trauma therapists whose clients report recovered memories
- Legal professionals evaluating delayed-disclosure abuse cases
- Clinical psychologists providing expert testimony in abuse proceedings
