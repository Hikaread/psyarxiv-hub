## Summary
This study investigated an 8-week, **health-insurance-provided group-based prevention program** combining weekly **outdoor physical activity (PA)** and **psychoeducational sessions (PsycEd)**. Using a naturalistic, non-controlled design, the study examined both **program-level outcomes** (pre- to post-program changes in mental health) and **session-level processes** (proximal changes within individual sessions and their relation to overall outcomes). The program was delivered in groups and represents a real-world implementation of a **prevention-oriented intervention** offered through the German healthcare system. The dual focus on PA and psychoeducation reflects the evidence base for both **exercise-based mental health promotion** and **structured psychoeducation** as prevention components, while the session-level analysis addresses the gap between knowing that programs work at the aggregate level and understanding **how they work** at the process level.

## Clinical Insight
Prevention programs that combine **physical activity** and **psychoeducation** are increasingly popular yet poorly understood at the process level. This study's key contribution is examining **what happens within sessions** — the proximal mechanisms through which a group-based prevention program exerts its effects. For service planners and clinicians, understanding session-level processes helps optimize **program design** and **facilitator training**. The naturalistic, insurance-provided context means findings are **ecologically valid** — they reflect what actually happens in community prevention programs rather than optimized research conditions. The group-based format also offers **social support** benefits that individual interventions lack. However, the non-controlled design limits causal inference about program effects.

## Relevant For
- Prevention program designers and coordinators
- Group facilitators in mental health prevention
- Health insurance program managers
- Clinicians involved in community-based prevention
