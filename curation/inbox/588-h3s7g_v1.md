## Summary
This single-blind, active-control, counterbalanced **crossover trial** (N = 27 healthy adults) tested whether **bilateral 30 Hz continuous theta burst stimulation (cTBS)** over the **occipitotemporal cortex (OTC)** modulates **affective appraisal** and **memory** for negative emotional stimuli. The OTC was selected based on fMRI evidence linking it selectively to successful encoding of aversive stimuli. Participants received cTBS over the OTC or an active control site in two visits 7-21 days apart, then viewed 72 standardised emotional pictures (positive, neutral, negative), rated them for valence and arousal, completed a **free-recall task**, and re-rated the pictures after approximately 30 minutes. The pre-registered primary outcome — **valence ratings of negative pictures immediately after stimulation** — showed no significant effect (R = 0.01, t(23.69) = 0.53, p = 0.60). All secondary outcomes (arousal ratings, free-recall memory, delayed valence ratings for all valence categories) were likewise non-significant after **multiple comparison correction**.

## Clinical Insight
This rigorously conducted null-result study has important implications for understanding the neural architecture of **emotional memory formation**, particularly relevant to **trauma-related disorders** such as PTSD where aversive memories persist maladaptively. Despite prior fMRI evidence that OTC activation correlates with successful encoding of negative stimuli, disrupting this region via cTBS did not alter affective appraisal or memory — suggesting the OTC may be **correlational rather than causally necessary** for negative emotional encoding. For researchers developing **neuromodulation-based interventions** for PTSD and other conditions characterised by intrusive negative memories, this result redirects attention toward prefrontal and medial temporal targets that have shown causal effects. The study also demonstrates the value of preregistered designs with active control conditions, which protect against both Type I and Type II errors. Clinicians should be cautious about interpreting neuroimaging correlations as evidence for causal mechanisms, as the failure of OTC disruption to affect emotional processing provides a clear example of how correlation does not imply causation in the emotion-memory network.
