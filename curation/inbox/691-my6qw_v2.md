## Summary
This **ecological momentary intervention** tested whether training individuals to use **wise empathy** on Instagram can improve well-being. Half of the **260 participants** were trained to wisely regulate empathy for positive and negative posts. Using **experience sampling** (5,902 observations), the study tracked empathy and well-being in daily life. The intervention **increased wise empathy online** — boosting appreciative joy, positive emotion sharing, and compassion for negative emotions — while **reducing negative emotion sharing, upward social comparisons, and personal distress**. When participants had recently browsed Instagram, the intervention also improved well-being. The findings suggest that **how** people engage empathically with social media content can be modified to support rather than undermine mental health.

## Clinical Insight
Social media use is frequently cited as a driver of poor mental health, particularly among young people. Rather than recommending reduced usage, this study offers a more nuanced approach: **changing how patients engage** with social media content through empathic regulation. The concept of "wise empathy" — feeling appropriate concern without being overwhelmed — maps directly onto clinical skills taught in **dialectical behavior therapy (DBT)** and **compassion-focused therapy (CFT)**. For clinicians whose patients report social media-related distress, this study provides an evidence-based framework for teaching specific **empathic regulation strategies** rather than blanket abstinence recommendations. The experience sampling design (5,902 observations) provides unusually **ecologically valid** evidence that the effects hold in real-world social media use, not just in the lab.

## Methodology Note
This is a randomized ecological momentary intervention with experience sampling.

__Design__: Ecological momentary intervention (EMI) with experience sampling methodology (ESM).
N = 260 participants randomized to wise empathy training vs. control.
5,902 ESM observations tracking real-time empathy and well-being during daily Instagram use.
Assessed both Instagram-specific and general well-being outcomes.

__Measurement Validity__: Experience sampling provides ecologically valid, real-time data rather than retrospective self-report.
Multiple well-being and empathy constructs assessed.
Large number of observations (5,902) provides robust within-person data.

__Limitations__: Abstract-only access; specific effect sizes, measures, and analysis details not fully assessable.
Instagram-specific; generalizability to other platforms uncertain.
Short-term intervention effects; durability unknown.
Self-selected sample of Instagram users.
The "wise empathy" construct requires further validation as a standardized clinical tool.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
