## Summary
This **systematic review** examines the genetic associations between **irritability-related traits** and diverse mental health disorders. Searching MEDLINE, EMBASE, PsycINFO, and Web of Science up to May 2025, the authors identified **30 studies** — six **twin studies** and 24 **genome-wide analyses** (GWAS and polygenic risk score studies). The most replicated finding was **shared genetic liability** between irritability and **internalizing disorders** (depression: **rg = 0.46–0.56**) and **externalizing disorders** (ADHD: **rg = 0.29–1.00**). Emerging but inconclusive evidence was found for associations with **neurodevelopmental disorders**, **psychotic disorders**, and **substance use disorders**, limited by the small number of studies in these domains. The review highlights that irritability operates as a **transdiagnostic genetic factor** bridging mood and behavioral psychopathology, and calls for research using **standardized irritability phenotypes** and more **ancestrally diverse samples**.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians, the transdiagnostic genetic architecture of irritability supports a **dimensional rather than categorical** conceptualization. A child or adolescent presenting with irritability may carry shared genetic risk for both depression and ADHD, which has direct implications for **case formulation** and **treatment planning**. The strong genetic correlation with depression (**rg up to 0.56**) suggests that irritability in clinical presentations should be taken seriously as a potential marker for **mood disorder risk**, not dismissed as purely behavioral. Conversely, the very high genetic correlation with ADHD (up to **rg = 1.00** in some studies) reinforces that irritability in ADHD contexts may reflect shared etiology rather than a distinct comorbid condition. Clinicians should be cautious about **over-pathologizing irritability** as a separate diagnosis when it may be better understood as an expression of underlying transdiagnostic vulnerability. These findings also support the use of **irritability-focused assessments** as part of broader risk screening, particularly in young people where irritability may precede more differentiated psychopathology.

## Methodology Note
This is a meta_analysis-type paper, so Statistical Power and Preregistration are not applicable.

__Effect Sizes__: Genetic correlations (**rg**) were the primary effect metric, ranging from **0.29 to 1.00** for externalizing associations and **0.46 to 0.56** for internalizing associations.
The wide range for ADHD (**rg = 0.29–1.00**) across studies indicates substantial heterogeneity in how irritability and ADHD were measured and analyzed.
Polygenic risk score (PRS) analyses were reported qualitatively rather than with standardized effect sizes, limiting comparability.

__Statistical Rigor__: The review followed **PRISMA** guidelines for systematic reviews, which is appropriate.
Heterogeneity across studies was substantial, driven by differences in irritability measures (Affective Reactivity Index, CBCL irritability items, GWAS of irritability items), analytical approaches, and sample characteristics.
No formal meta-analysis or publication bias assessment was conducted, which is a limitation given the small number of studies per disorder category.

__Measurement Validity__: A key finding was the **lack of standardized irritability phenotyping** across genetic studies.
Some studies used the **Affective Reactivity Index (ARI)**, others used CBCL/YSR irritability items, and some used item-level GWAS, making cross-study comparison difficult.
The review itself serves as a call for measurement harmonization.

__Open Science__: The preprint is openly available on **PsyArXiv**.
No protocol registration (e.g., PROSPERO) was explicitly mentioned, which would have strengthened the review's transparency.

__Participant Transparency__: The 30 included studies spanned diverse ancestry groups to varying degrees, but the authors noted that **ancestrally diverse samples remain underrepresented**.
Twin studies provided heritability estimates while GWAS/PRS studies provided molecular genetic data.
No aggregate participant demographic breakdown was provided across all 30 studies.
