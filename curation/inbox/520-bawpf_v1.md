## Summary

This **PRISMA-guided systematic review** synthesised 30 experimental papers (total N = 1,188; mean age 25.8 years) examining the bidirectional relationship between the **Rubber Hand Illusion** and **pain perception**. The review was originally pre-registered as a meta-analysis but was converted to a qualitative synthesis due to substantial **methodological heterogeneity** in pain induction methods (thermal, mechanical, electrical, laser, visual, endogenous), **pain outcome measures** (intensity, threshold, tolerance, unpleasantness), and **ownership measures** (questionnaires, proprioceptive drift, nociceptive drift). A key qualitative finding emerged: when the embodied hand was presented as **healthy** and **multisensory stimulation** was congruent, the RHI was associated with **analgesic effects**. Conversely, when the rubber hand appeared **injured**, **threatening**, or **sensitised**, embodiment tended to produce **hyperalgesic effects**. However, **null findings** were common, suggesting that ownership induction alone is insufficient to reliably modulate pain. Studies using painful stimuli to induce RHI (the **Nociceptive RHI** paradigm) showed that nociceptive signals can be integrated within **multisensory body-ownership** mechanisms. Risk of bias and transparency of methods were significantly correlated (rho = 0.60).

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: The review followed **PRISMA guidelines** with a pre-registered protocol on OSF. Two independent reviewers conducted screening and data extraction with reported **inter-rater reliability** (Cohen's kappa = 0.44-0.70). The quality assessment used both **Risk of Bias** and **Transparency of Methods** tools, providing a novel examination of how methodological transparency relates to bias. The inclusion of both directions of the RHI-pain relationship (RHI affecting pain; pain affecting RHI) is a strength.

__Limitation__: The most significant limitation is that the meta-analysis had to be abandoned due to extreme **heterogeneity** in experimental paradigms, preventing quantitative effect size estimation. The mean sample size per study was only 40 participants, and most studies used **healthy young adults** exposed to experimentally induced **acute pain**, limiting clinical generalisability. The literature is dominated by hand-specific paradigms, and the authors note the hand may not generalise to other body parts. Only one study included a **clinical population** (phantom limb pain). Publication bias could not be formally assessed given the lack of quantitative synthesis.

## Clinical Insight

The finding that the visual appearance of the embodied limb determines whether RHI produces **analgesia** or **hyperalgesia** has direct implications for **virtual reality**-based pain management. Clinicians using embodiment-based interventions should ensure that virtual body representations appear **healthy** and non-threatening to avoid inadvertently increasing **pain sensitivity**. The **Nociceptive RHI** paradigm suggests that even nociceptive input can support body representation when tactile and proprioceptive information is impaired — relevant for conditions like **spinal cord injury** or **peripheral neuropathy** where conventional sensory input is reduced. However, the predominance of null findings and the reliance on healthy samples means that embodiment-based pain interventions remain experimental and should not yet be adopted as standard clinical practice without further evidence from **chronic pain** populations.

## Relevant For

- Pain medicine specialists exploring novel analgesic mechanisms
- Clinical psychologists using virtual reality for pain management
- Researchers studying body representation in chronic pain conditions
- Neurorehabilitation specialists working with somatosensory impairment
