## Summary

This mixed-methods study gathered developmental expert perspectives (survey n=141, interviews n=15) to map the research landscape on adolescent AI chatbot use and inform a coordinated research and policy agenda. Experts prioritized studying use cases with severe or irreversible immediate harms, AI literacy, AI as a social/relational actor, AI-generated misinformation, and overreliance. Key barriers included the rapid pace of AI change, limited industry transparency, and funding gaps. Experts recommended three immediate guardrails: enforceable youth-centered regulation, developmentally informed product design, and multidimensional AI literacy, emphasizing that responsibility for safe AI should not rest primarily on adolescents and families.

## Clinical Insight

Therapists working with adolescents increasingly encounter clients whose emotional and social lives are intertwined with AI chatbots. This study provides a framework for understanding the clinical landscape: teens may form parasocial relationships with AI, be influenced by AI-generated content, or develop overreliance on AI for emotional support. Clinicians should assess clients' AI chatbot use as part of a comprehensive social history, particularly for vulnerable youth. The expert recommendation that responsibility should not rest solely on families aligns with a systemic therapeutic perspective—therapists can advocate for environmental changes rather than placing the burden on individual clients.

## Relevant For

- Child and adolescent therapists encountering AI-related issues in practice
- School counselors monitoring AI's impact on student wellbeing
- Clinicians working with youth social media and technology use
- Family therapists addressing technology-related family dynamics
