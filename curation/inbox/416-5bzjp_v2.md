## Summary
This perspective paper addresses four methodological problems that limit the informational value of **many-analysts studies** — designs where multiple independent analysis teams analyse the same dataset to reveal the extent of analytic variability in psychology. The authors propose four corresponding solutions, illustrated with data from the **Many-Analysts Religion Project** and the **OSSC19 Crowdsourced Replication Initiative**. **Problem 1** (design-goal mismatch): the lead team's research objective (mapping analytic diversity, testing robustness, or parameter estimation) should determine team recruitment strategy and research question breadth. **Problem 2** (loss of analyst expertise): structured reflection opportunities should be created so that analysts' qualitative insights and study limitations are captured alongside quantitative results. **Problem 3** (undifferentiated contributions): analytic contributions should be weighted by quality indicators (e.g., team expertise, preregistered analysis plans) or by **a priori plausibility** via Bayesian priors that shrink outlier estimates toward a common mean. **Problem 4** (opaque variability drivers): **sparse meta-regression models** and complementary approaches (machine learning, hierarchical Bayesian models) should be used to identify which specific analytic decisions drive result variability, rather than relying solely on standard meta-regression. The paper demonstrates that these approaches can extract substantially more information from many-analysts datasets than traditional forest plot interpretation alone.

## Clinical Insight
Many-analysts studies have direct relevance for **evidence-based practice** because they quantify the extent to which clinically relevant findings depend on arbitrary analytic choices rather than reflecting robust underlying effects. For clinicians who consume research to inform treatment decisions, understanding that analytic variability in many-analysts studies may be "as large as the variability observed in conceptual replications" underscores the importance of attending to **replication evidence and meta-analytic syntheses** rather than relying on single-study findings. The paper's proposed solutions — particularly **weighting analyses by a priori plausibility** and using **hierarchical Bayesian models** to integrate multiple sources of uncertainty — offer methodological advances that could improve the reliability of clinical research syntheses. Clinicians involved in research can apply these frameworks to design more informative many-analysts studies in clinical domains, such as testing whether treatment effect estimates for common interventions (e.g., CBT for depression) are robust across analytic approaches.

## Notes
All analyses in the paper use R and are presented within a Bayesian framework, though the authors emphasise the proposed solutions are not tied to any specific statistical framework (with the exception of Solutions 3b and 4c which are inherently Bayesian).
