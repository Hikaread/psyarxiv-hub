## Summary
This paper introduces Momentary Structured Networks (MSN), a novel EMA-based framework that combines theory-guided structure with self-reported causal relations to generate individualized network representations for case formulation. The approach was demonstrated in a sample of 46 individuals with PTSD (diagnosed or probable) who completed EMA over three weeks with 2-5 daily assessments (mean=42.6 plus or minus 36.5 observations per participant). Grounded in the Ehlers and Clark cognitive model of PTSD, MSN assesses five theory-driven constructs: negative appraisals, trauma memory characteristics, triggers, sense of current threat, and maladaptive coping strategies. Participants selected three personalized items per construct from validated questionnaires.

MSN networks consistently showed dense and complex structures even with limited data, in contrast to data-hungry traditional network approaches. Directional edges were estimated as conditional probabilities (e.g., P(A to B given A)), and MSN identified most relations found by association-based Ising models while capturing additional ones, resulting in higher network complexity. Network complexity was independent of the number of individual assessments, and structure stabilized rapidly: over 90% of edges were recovered using just 60% of the data. Directional edge preferences were largely consistent with theoretical predictions from the Ehlers and Clark model. A detailed clinical case study demonstrated how MSN can track changes in network dynamics during Cognitive Therapy for PTSD and generate hypotheses about the processes underlying clinical improvement.

## Clinical Insight
MSN offers a practical, theory-grounded approach to operationalizing case formulation that addresses key limitations of existing idiographic network methods. Unlike purely exploratory networks that require hundreds of observations, MSN produces stable networks with relatively few EMA assessments, making it feasible for routine clinical use. The rapid stabilization (90% of edges at 60% data) means clinicians can obtain reliable individualized formulations within the first 1-2 weeks of monitoring.

The clinical case study is particularly compelling, showing how MSN can track which therapeutic mechanisms are changing during treatment. This has the potential to make the often-invisible process of case formulation visible and quantifiable, enabling clinicians to see whether their interventions are targeting the right mechanisms. Although demonstrated in PTSD, the framework is explicitly designed to be generalizable to other conditions and theoretical models (e.g., depression, anxiety), offering a new paradigm for evidence-based case formulation.

## Relevant For
Clinical researchers interested in idiographic methods, network psychopathology, and case formulation; clinicians using EMA in practice; psychotherapy researchers studying mechanisms of change.
