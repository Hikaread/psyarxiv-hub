## Summary
Six experiments (2 preregistered, total **N = 1,271**) tested whether **empathy** and **mental imagery** underlie contagious anxiety. Study 1 demonstrated **anxiety contagion**: participants reported greater anxiety after exposure to a worried peer versus a calm peer. Studies 2a and 2b replicated this and showed that **perspective-taking** and spontaneous mental imagery mediated the effect. A process model was supported in which people take the perspective of anxious peers, visualize **catastrophic mental images**, and become anxious about those images. Studies 3a-3c provided **causal evidence**: blocking visual (but not auditory) working memory eliminated anxiety contagion, confirming the **visuospatial imagery pathway**. A mini-meta-analysis across studies showed that participants more prone to catching anxiety were higher in **trait empathy** and spontaneous mental imagery. Crucially, anxiety contagion in this paradigm was meta-analytically associated with higher **self-reported worry** and **trait anxiety**, demonstrating clinical relevance. Notably, participants never saw any aversive images directly — the negative mental images arose purely from exposure to others' reactions.

## Clinical Insight
This study has significant implications for understanding and treating anxiety disorders. The finding that anxiety spreads through a specific mechanism — **empathy-driven perspective-taking triggering catastrophic mental imagery** — suggests concrete intervention targets. Blocking **visual working memory** (Studies 3a-3c) eliminated contagion, which directly supports the clinical use of **imagery-competing tasks** (such as those developed by Holmes and colleagues) to interrupt the maintenance of anxiety through imagery. For **group therapy settings**, the findings highlight that anxious group members may inadvertently amplify anxiety in others through this contagion mechanism, suggesting therapists should be aware of and potentially manage **group emotional dynamics**. The link between contagion susceptibility and trait worry/anxiety suggests this paradigm may identify individuals at **heightened risk** for anxiety's social amplification. The model may also extend to other disorders where empathy-imagery interactions play a role, such as **suicidal ideation** and eating disorders.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians treating anxiety disorders, especially GAD and health anxiety
- Therapists running group therapy for anxiety
- Those using imagery-based interventions
- Researchers studying social transmission of psychopathology
- Clinicians interested in transdiagnostic mechanisms

## Notes
Laboratory paradigm uses induced anxiety from a picture-viewing task, which may not fully capture clinical anxiety contagion in naturalistic settings. The paradigm's clinical generalizability requires further testing in clinical populations.
