## Summary
The FEARFALL study investigated the **long-term effects** of a **multi-component intervention** on **concerns about falling (CaF)**, **stress physiology**, and **inflammation** in 160 community-dwelling older adults (aged 70+, mean age ~79.5 years, ~74% female). Participants were randomized to either a 4-month intervention combining **exercise training** and **cognitive-behavioral strategies** (adapted from "A Matter of Balance") or a **sham control group** (health education lectures plus light stretching). Assessments occurred at baseline (T0), post-intervention (T1), and 8-month follow-up (T2). The per-protocol sample included 87 intervention and 73 control participants.

The primary hypothesis was not supported: **no between-group differences** emerged on any outcome domain. Both groups showed significant **time effects** across psychological, functional, and biomarker outcomes. CaF measured by the **FES-I** decreased over time (T0-T2 estimate = 3.85, p less than .001), as did FES-I Avoidance Behavior (estimate = 2.12, p = .001) and **UP-CoF** improved (estimate = -1.68, p less than .001). **Geriatric Anxiety Scale** scores increased over time (T0-T2 estimate = 1.88, p = .003), and **perceived stress** also increased (T0-T2 estimate = 1.46, p = .010). **Handgrip strength** declined significantly (T0-T2 estimate = -2.43 kg, p less than .001) and **SPPB** total scores decreased (estimate = -0.41, p = .034).

For biomarkers, **cortisol AUCg** showed a complex pattern with decrease from T0-T1 then increase from T1-T2, yielding a net increase at T2 (estimate = 0.66, p = .017). **Alpha-amylase AUCg** increased over time (estimate = -10.07, p = .013). **Inflammatory markers** showed a non-cohesive picture: **IL-6** and **IL-1b** increased (p = .002 and p = .004), **TNF-alpha** decreased (p less than .001), and **CRP** and **IL-10** remained stable. Multiple testing was controlled via **Benjamini-Hochberg FDR** correction.

## Clinical Insight
The **null between-group finding** is clinically important, suggesting that the sham control condition (health education lectures plus light stretching) produced comparable improvements to the active multi-component intervention. This could indicate either that non-specific factors (social contact, attention, structured activity) drive CaF improvement, or that the active intervention components did not sufficiently exceed the control in targeting psychological mechanisms of fall concerns. The **decline in functional measures** (handgrip strength, SPPB) across both groups despite CaF improvements suggests that psychological and functional trajectories may diverge in this age group. The **increase in anxiety and perceived stress** over 12 months, alongside improved fall self-efficacy, is a paradoxical finding. Practitioners should not assume that reducing fall concerns will automatically improve broader psychological outcomes.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 160 randomized provides moderate power; per-protocol samples were 87 (IG) and 73 (SCG).
Power may have been insufficient to detect small between-group effects.
ITT analysis confirmed per-protocol findings, supporting robustness.

__Preregistration__: Registered at German Clinical Trials Register (DRKS00029171).
Study protocol published and preregistered on OSF.
Ethics approval obtained from Friedrich-Alexander-Universitat Erlangen-Nurnberg.

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes reported as estimated marginal mean differences with FDR-adjusted p-values.
Time effects were generally in the small-to-medium range for psychological outcomes.
No between-group effect sizes are reportable as no significant interactions emerged.

__Statistical Rigor__: Linear mixed-effects models with Type III F-tests and Satterthwaite-approximated degrees of freedom.
Random-intercept structure used throughout due to model instability with random slopes.
FDR correction applied at multiple levels (within-domain and across-domain via ACAT).

__Measurement Validity__: Well-validated measures used throughout: FES-I (alpha = 0.93, ICC = 0.94), FES-IAB, UP-CoF, GAS, GDS-15, PSS-10, EQ-5D VAS.
Biomarker assays used established protocols with CVs less than 10%.
SPPB and handgrip strength measured per EWGSOP2 recommendations.

__Open Science__: Preregistered on both DRKS and OSF.
Protocol published separately.
Supplementary results provided for all outcomes.

__Participant Transparency__: Detailed baseline characteristics reported for both groups showing no significant differences.
Inclusion criteria clearly specified (age 70+, FES-I score of 20 or above).
Exclusion criteria include anti-inflammatory medication use and cognitive impairment (MMSE less than 24).
High attendance rates reported (77% IG, 82.2% SCG meeting the 75% threshold).

## Relevant For

## Notes
The null finding for the primary hypothesis is a meaningful contribution. The long-term follow-up (8 months post-intervention) is a strength. Declining functional measures across both groups highlight the progressive nature of age-related physical decline regardless of psychological intervention.
