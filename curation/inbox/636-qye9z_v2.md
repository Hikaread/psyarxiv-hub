## Summary
This **systematic review** synthesises evidence on **sexual and reproductive health (SRH) care access** among people with **mental health conditions** across OECD countries (post-2012 literature). From 6,146 identified studies, only **10 met inclusion criteria** (5 quantitative, 5 qualitative), highlighting the significant evidence gap in this area. Findings indicate that mental health conditions — particularly **depression** and **severe mental illness** — are associated with **reduced or delayed SRH care utilisation**, including lower uptake of contraception, STI testing, and reproductive health services. Access was shaped by **intersecting factors**: stigma (both mental health-related and SRH-related), sexuality, ethnicity and culture, substance use, and age. System-level barriers included **lack of provider training** in addressing the intersection of mental health and SRH needs, and **limited service integration** between mental health and SRH care pathways. Identified facilitators included **low-barrier services, person-centred care, informal support networks**, and **integrated service models** with effective communication between mental health and SRH providers. The authors conclude that the evidence base remains critically limited and call for research addressing gaps in understanding effective strategies to improve access.

## Clinical Insight
This review reveals a consequential blind spot in clinical practice: people with mental health conditions face **significant barriers to accessing SRH care**, yet this intersection is understudied and poorly addressed in service design. For clinicians in both mental health and SRH settings, several implications are clear. First, mental health clinicians should routinely assess **SRH needs** as part of holistic care — patients with depression or severe mental illness may not be accessing contraception, STI screening, or reproductive care, and this is not merely a "lifestyle" issue but one with direct health consequences (higher STI rates, unintended pregnancies). Second, the finding that **provider training** is a key barrier suggests that many clinicians in both sectors feel unprepared to address the intersection — this is a training gap that can be addressed at the level of clinical education programs and continuing professional development. Third, the role of **intersecting stigma** means that patients who are already marginalised (e.g., LGBTQ+ individuals, ethnic minorities, people who use substances) face compounded barriers that require **culturally informed, low-threshold approaches**. Fourth, **integrated service models** where mental health and SRH providers communicate effectively were identified as facilitators — this supports the development of collaborative care arrangements that bridge these traditionally separate service sectors. For therapists, simply being aware that a client's mental health condition may be impeding their access to SRH care, and being willing to have non-judgmental conversations about this, could represent a meaningful intervention.

## Methodology Note
Paper type: other (systematic review).

__Statistical Rigor__: The review followed standard systematic review methodology, searching 10 electronic databases with clear inclusion criteria (OECD countries, post-2012, mental health conditions as the population of interest, SRH care access as the outcome). Study quality was assessed using the **Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT)**. The narrative synthesis approach is appropriate given the heterogeneity of included studies. The very small yield (10 studies from 6,146 screened) is itself an important finding about the state of the evidence base.

__Measurement Validity__: The review used the MMAT for quality assessment, which is appropriate for synthesising mixed-methods evidence. The heterogeneity of the included studies in terms of populations, definitions of mental health conditions, and SRH outcomes limits the specificity of the findings, but the authors acknowledge this transparently.

__Open Science__: The paper is open access on PsyArXiv. No protocol registration or data sharing statement is mentioned in the abstract.

__Participant Transparency__: The review covers studies from OECD countries published after 2012. Specific participant characteristics of the 10 included studies are not detailed in the abstract — the review covers diverse populations including people with depression, severe mental illness, and various SRH needs.
