## Summary

This preregistered experimental study (N = 87) compared three **CBM-I** (Cognitive Bias Modification-Interpretation) training conditions—**positive**, **negative**, and **neutral**—on interpretation biases (IBs) and stress reactivity markers in individuals with moderate **social anxiety** (SPAI-G scores 28–52). The sample was predominantly female (69%), German-speaking students (M age = 22.3 years). The CBM-I training used the **Mathews and Mackintosh (2000)** paradigm with 80 trials across ten blocks.

CBM-I successfully modified interpretation biases in a condition-congruent direction. Post-training, the positive group (d = -1.49 vs. negative) and neutral group (d = -1.08 vs. negative) reported stronger positive interpretations than the negative group. However, the positive and neutral groups did not differ significantly (p = .171). The within-group change for the positive condition was **d = .56**. Critically, **no transfer effects** were found to stress reactivity: self-reported stress, **heart rate (HR)**, **heart rate variability (HRV)** (RMSSD and HF-Power), and **salivary cortisol** all changed similarly across all three conditions following the **anagram stress task**. Self-reported stress and HR increased while HRV decreased post-stressor across all groups. **Post-event processing (PEP)** also showed no differential change between conditions, though PEP levels at post-stressor were comparable to those observed in clinical SAD samples.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with **social anxiety**, these findings reinforce a growing concern about the **limited transfer** of CBM-I effects to clinically meaningful outcomes. While CBM-I reliably changes interpretation bias on closely matched tasks, this cognitive shift did not translate to reduced **physiological stress reactivity** or decreased **post-event processing**—two core maintaining factors in SAD per the **Clark and Wells (1995) model**. The authors suggest that the mismatch between the social-evaluative content of CBM-I training and the performance-based nature of the anagram stressor may explain the null transfer effects. Clinicians should be cautious about expecting single-session CBM-I to produce broad therapeutic change and may need to consider **longer, more immersive protocols** that better align training content with real-world social stressors.

## Methodology Note

This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The study did not reach its targeted sample size (final N = 87 versus planned N > 87).
The authors acknowledge this limitation but note that the achieved sample was sufficient for detecting the robust IB effects (ηp² = .18 for the group × time interaction on IBs).
The study may have been underpowered for detecting transfer effects to physiological outcomes, which typically show smaller effect sizes.

__Preregistration__: The study was **preregistered on OSF** (https://osf.io/nqab6/).
Deviations from the protocol are reported in the paper and supplementary materials.

__Effect Sizes__: The primary IB effect was large: group × time interaction ηp² = .18, with between-group d values ranging from -1.49 to -0.77 for IB comparisons.
Within-group IB changes: positive training d = .56, negative training d = -.32.
No significant effect sizes for transfer outcomes (all interaction p-values > .10).
The IB change score ANOVA yielded F(2, 84) = 17.12, p < .001, ηp² = .29.

__Statistical Rigor__: The three-condition design (positive, negative, neutral) is methodologically stronger than the typical two-condition (active vs. sham) approach.
Mixed ANOVAs were used with Bonferroni-corrected post-hoc tests.
Nonparametric aligned-rank-transform ANOVAs were conducted as sensitivity analyses for IB outcomes.
The authors transparently report that nonparametric analyses weakened some findings.
SPAI-G internal consistency was low in this restricted-range sample (α = 0.63 vs. α = 0.96 in the broader screening sample), raising questions about measurement precision.

__Measurement Validity__: IBs were measured using the **Encoding Recognition Task (ERT)** with acceptable internal consistency (α = .67–.88 across versions and time points).
Social anxiety was screened with the **SPAI-G** (α = 0.63 in this sample; α = 0.96 in the screening study).
Depressive symptoms were screened with the **QIDS-SR** (α = 0.54 in this sample; α = 0.78 in the screening study).
The low SPAI-G and QIDS-SR reliability values in this moderate-anxiety, restricted-range sample are concerning and may reflect the homogeneity of the sample.
HRV was measured using both **ln RMSSD** and **ln HF-Power**.
Cortisol was assayed from saliva samples.
PEP was measured but details of the specific instrument are not fully specified.

__Open Science__: Preregistered on OSF with deviations reported.
Supplementary materials are referenced.
Data availability is not explicitly stated.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 87 (27 positive, 31 negative, 29 neutral), aged 18–35 years.
Recruited from a larger online screening of 394 participants.
Inclusion: SPAI-G score 28–52 (moderate social anxiety range).
Exclusions: QIDS-SR > 15 or suicidality item > 1, psychopharmacological treatment, BMI extremes, regular substance use, prior anagram task participation, prior CBM-I participation (2 years).
69% female, 89.7% students, 89.7% German nationality.
The sample was relatively homogeneous (German students with moderate social anxiety), limiting generalizability.

## Notes

The study ended before reaching the targeted sample size. The neutral control condition showed a modest (non-significant) increase in positive interpretations, possibly reflecting time/repeated-testing effects—this underscores the value of including neutral comparison conditions in CBM-I research rather than relying solely on sham training. The anagram task as a stressor may not have been well-matched to the social-evaluative content of the CBM-I training.
