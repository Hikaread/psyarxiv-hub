## Summary

Using data from the Changing How I Live Life Study (N=444), which evaluated a digital intervention targeting neuroticism reduction, this study examined intervention-induced personality change across three timescales: momentary, daily, and weekly. Neuroticism changed nonlinearly across the intervention period, with changes at each timescale reliably predicting one another. Bottom-up effects (momentary to daily to weekly) were often larger in magnitude than top-down effects. Intervention groups showed larger mean-level changes and coupled changes across timescales compared to the active control group. Results support both bottom-up and top-down change processes in personality modification.

## Clinical Insight

This study has important implications for therapists working with clients high in neuroticism. It provides evidence that neuroticism is modifiable through intervention and demonstrates the mechanistic pathways: momentary state changes accumulate into lasting trait change. This supports therapeutic approaches that target daily emotional experiences (e.g., emotion regulation skills, CBT techniques, mindfulness) as pathways to durable personality-level change. The finding that bottom-up effects dominate suggests therapists should prioritize helping clients change moment-to-moment responses rather than focusing solely on trait-level restructuring.

## Relevant For

- Therapists working with clients presenting high neuroticism or emotional instability
- Clinicians interested in personality change mechanisms
- Practitioners using digital or mobile interventions for mental health
- Researchers studying therapeutic mechanisms of personality change
