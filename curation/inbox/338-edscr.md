## Summary

This proof-of-concept study evaluated whether person-specific network analysis can guide individualized treatment target selection for chronic pain patients with comorbidities. Twelve patients (mean age 60.25) and nine therapists participated in an ABCD single-case experimental design with randomized baselines. Qualitative interviews explored feasibility and acceptability. Compliance reached at least 80% in seven participants, with a mean of 298 observations per person. Multilevel analysis showed an average decrease in disability scores from baseline to intervention phase. The authors conclude that the clinical value of data-informed personalization lies not in algorithmic precision but in facilitating patient-centered, reflective clinical reasoning in complex cases.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with chronic pain, this study supports using repeated self-monitoring data to collaboratively identify each patient's unique symptom network before selecting intervention targets. Rather than applying a standard protocol, the approach uses individual network visualization to help patients recognize patterns in their own daily experience, then matches intervention modules to the most central nodes in their personal network. The finding that structured data-informed reflection, rather than algorithmic output per se, drove therapeutic benefit suggests that network-informed case formulation can be integrated into existing clinical workflows without requiring specialized software. Therapists treating complex somatic presentations where comorbidities obscure the primary maintaining mechanism may find this approach particularly useful for identifying which target to address first.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with chronic pain and medically unexplained symptoms who want to integrate personalized formulation into treatment planning
