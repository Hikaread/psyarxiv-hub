## Summary

This **randomised controlled trial** (N = 135, iOS users of the **one sec** app) examined how a **digital friction** intervention affects social media use, momentary **affective states**, and broader well-being over a 7-day period using **ecological momentary assessment (EMA)**. Participants self-selected a target app (most commonly **Instagram**, 63.3% of interactions) and were randomly assigned to an affect-only condition, an added friction condition, or a timed friction condition. Across 18,796 app interactions and 4,209 mood reports, **linear mixed-effects models** revealed that total target app interactions decreased significantly over time (b1 = -1.06, p = .002), corresponding to approximately a 5% daily reduction. **Momentary valence** became more positive across the study period (b1 = 0.025, p < .001), while **arousal** showed no significant change. Participants dismissed 29% of app-opening attempts overall, with higher dismissal rates in friction conditions (26.4% and 30.0%) versus affect-only (18.9%). Pre-post survey analyses (n = 44) showed significant improvements in **stress** (Cohen's dz = -0.66, p < .001), perceived impact of phone use on **focus** (dz = 0.59, p = .003), **productivity** (dz = 0.38, p = .039), and **mood** (dz = 0.41, p = .034), all surviving FDR correction. Changes in **life satisfaction** and total phone use were non-significant.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with clients who report **problematic social media use** or difficulty regulating digital behavior, this study provides evidence for a concrete, accessible intervention that integrates behavioral and affective components. The finding that merely asking clients to report their mood before opening an app reduced app openings by 18.9% — even without any deliberate friction — demonstrates the power of **momentary self-awareness** and **measurement reactivity** as therapeutic mechanisms. This aligns with clinical approaches emphasizing **mindful awareness** of automatic behaviors. The significant stress reduction (dz = -0.66) observed in just one week suggests that even brief friction-based interventions can produce clinically meaningful shifts in affect, though the small post-survey sample (n = 44) warrants cautious interpretation. The study's EMA methodology — capturing affect at the point of decision — mirrors the **urge-surfing** technique used in addiction treatment, where patients learn to observe cravings without acting on them. Clinicians could recommend the one sec app or similar digital friction tools as an adjunct to therapy, particularly for clients whose social media use intersects with **anxiety**, **depression**, or **procrastination** patterns. Importantly, the affect-only condition's efficacy suggests that the simple act of pausing to check in with one's emotional state — a core mindfulness skill — may be the active ingredient, raising questions about whether apps are even necessary or whether therapists could cultivate this capacity through in-session practice.

## Relevant For
- Therapists treating clients with problematic social media or smartphone use
- Clinical psychologists working with anxiety, depression, or digital wellbeing
- Practitioners integrating digital tools into therapeutic practice
- Researchers studying behavioral interventions and digital mental health
- CBT clinicians addressing habit regulation and impulse control

## Notes

Authors include Frederik Riedel (developer of the one sec app) and David Gruning (organizer of research on the app), which represents a conflict of interest. The post-survey sample was small (n = 44 out of 135 EMA participants) due to attrition and technical issues, limiting the reliability of pre-post comparisons. The study duration of 7 days limits conclusions about sustained effects. No OS-level screen time data were collected, so substitution effects (reducing one app while increasing another) cannot be ruled out. The study was conducted with unpaid volunteers.
