## Summary
This naturalistic study investigated the effectiveness of the Greifswald Psychotherapy Navigation System (GPNS), a newly implemented routine outcome monitoring and feedback system for outpatient psychotherapy. The sample included 261 patients (mean age 33 years, 79% female) who completed individual cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) from 61 psychotherapists. Using Bayesian instrumental variable estimation to address non-random adoption of the feedback system, the study estimated the local average treatment effect (LATE) of using the GPNS on post-treatment symptom severity and personality impairment.

Using the GPNS significantly reduced post-treatment symptom severity (LATE = -0.11, 95% CrI: -0.18 to -0.03) and personality impairment (LATE = -2.65, 95% CrI: -3.72 to -1.58). Sensitivity analyses demonstrated that effects were robust to plausible residual confounding, strengthening confidence in a causal interpretation. The study employed instrumental variable methods to address the naturalistic design's limitation: therapists self-selected into using the GPNS, creating potential selection bias. The instrument (therapist-level variation in GPNS adoption) satisfied relevance and exclusion restriction assumptions, allowing estimation of the complier average causal effect.

The GPNS provides therapists and patients with real-time feedback on symptom trajectories, enabling data-driven treatment decisions including early identification of non-responders. The effect on personality impairment was notably larger than on symptom severity, suggesting that systematic monitoring and feedback may be particularly beneficial for addressing deeper-level personality-related difficulties that often go undetected in standard symptom-focused treatment monitoring.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides evidence that implementing measurement-based care (MBC) in routine outpatient CBT leads to modest but reliable improvements in treatment outcomes. The effect on personality impairment is particularly noteworthy and clinically meaningful, suggesting that systematic feedback may help therapists address characterological patterns that might otherwise persist beyond symptom resolution.

The Bayesian instrumental variable approach represents a methodological advance over previous naturalistic MBC studies, which have been limited by selection bias (therapists who adopt MBC may differ systematically from those who do not). While the effect sizes are small to moderate, they are consistent with meta-analytic findings on MBC and represent meaningful improvements at the population level when scaled across services. For clinic directors considering implementing MBC systems, these findings support the investment, particularly given the robust sensitivity analysis results.

## Relevant For
Clinic directors and service managers implementing measurement-based care, CBT practitioners, and health services researchers studying implementation of routine outcome monitoring.

## Notes
Naturalistic design with strong causal inference methods (Bayesian IV). The effect on personality impairment is a novel and important finding. GPNS is specific to the Greifswald context but principles are generalizable. 79% female sample limits generalizability.
