## Summary
This **theoretical manuscript** integrates the **Gender Role Strain Paradigm**, **Precarious Manhood Theory**, and **human-computer interaction research** to explain why men may be particularly drawn to **AI-based mental health support** — and how this affinity could be leveraged to improve men's engagement with mental health care. Men consistently **underutilize mental health services** despite substantial psychological burden, driven by **traditional masculinity ideologies**, **gender role strain**, and the precarious nature of masculine identity. The authors argue that **LLM-based tools** may circumvent key barriers to men's help-seeking: they eliminate the need to **disclose vulnerability to another person**, avoid **gender role threat** inherent in help-seeking, and provide **on-demand, anonymous** access that sidesteps scheduling and visibility concerns. The paper proposes a framework for designing **male-friendly AI mental health tools** and identifies both opportunities (increased engagement, psychoeducation, bridge to formal care) and risks (reinforcement of avoidance, lack of human connection, inappropriate self-treatment).

## Clinical Insight
This paper offers a compelling theoretical explanation for an emerging clinical phenomenon: men are engaging with AI chatbots for mental health support at significant rates. For clinicians, understanding this dynamic is essential — rather than viewing AI engagement as problematic avoidance, it may represent a **stepping stone** toward formal care for men who would otherwise never seek help. The integration of **Gender Role Strain Paradigm** with technology acceptance research provides a nuanced framework: AI tools reduce the **status threat** and **perceived weakness** that men associate with traditional help-seeking. However, clinicians should also be alert to the risk that AI tools may **reinforce avoidance** of necessary human therapeutic relationships. The paper's framework could inform **clinical guidelines** for when to recommend AI tools as adjuncts versus when to gently redirect toward human care.
