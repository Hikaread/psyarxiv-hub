## Summary

This study introduces a Bayesian multilevel vector autoregressive model (BmlVAR) for estimating person-specific dynamic network features with uncertainty quantification, and applies it to treatment selection and outcome prediction. In a preregistered simulation study comparing several network estimation methods, results showed that all methods performed mediocrely in estimating centrality measures in practically relevant settings. BmlVAR outperformed other methods in detecting associations with outcomes, but all methods required substantial data or very large effects. The study cautions that centrality-based treatment selection from dynamic networks is unlikely to work well with typical sample sizes.

## Clinical Insight

This is an important methodological cautionary paper for therapists and clinical researchers who have embraced network analysis for treatment planning. The finding that network centrality measures require impractically large amounts of data to reliably guide treatment selection challenges a growing trend in personalized medicine. For clinicians, this means that identifying "central" symptoms in an individual's network and targeting them therapeutically may not be as straightforward as some have suggested. Therapists should be cautious about over-interpreting network-based assessment tools and continue relying on comprehensive clinical judgment alongside any network-informed insights.
