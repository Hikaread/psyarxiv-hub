## Summary

This 28-day longitudinal study (N=143 adults with DSM-5 AUD) used smartphone-based multimodal monitoring to predict same-day nighttime drinking. Daily assessments covered three domains: app-based self-reports (mood, craving, meal intake, sleep), computational tasks (delay discounting, risk preference, ambiguity tolerance), and passive smartphone sensing (time at home, calls, steps). A multimodal model showed highest explanatory power (marginal R-squared=0.22), with within-person increases in delay discounting, alcohol craving, and positive mood, plus lower meal intake and less time at home, all associated with greater likelihood of nighttime drinking. Crucially, within-person delay discounting fluctuations remained predictive after accounting for other measures.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists treating alcohol use disorder, this study highlights the value of monitoring cognitive markers alongside self-reported states. Delay discounting (preference for immediate over delayed rewards) is not just a stable trait but fluctuates day-to-day and predicts drinking behavior within the same day. Clinicians could help clients recognize their personal high-risk patterns—days when they are more impulsive, crave alcohol more, or spend less time at home—and develop targeted coping strategies. The multimodal approach suggests that combining cognitive, emotional, and behavioral monitoring provides the most complete risk picture for relapse prevention.

## Relevant For

- Addiction counselors and therapists treating alcohol use disorder
- Clinicians interested in ambulatory assessment and digital monitoring for substance use
- Therapists incorporating behavioral economics concepts (delay discounting) into treatment
- Practitioners working on relapse prevention and harm reduction
