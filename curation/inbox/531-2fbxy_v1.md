## Summary
The Depressometer project is a multimodal, intensive longitudinal dataset collected between October 2018 and February 2021 at Bar-Ilan University's Psychotherapy Research Lab. It includes 58 patients diagnosed with Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) via the MINI 5.0, and 13 therapists (advanced trainees with 3-7 years experience). Patients underwent a manualized 16-session supportive-expressive psychodynamic psychotherapy. The dataset captures an exceptionally rich array of process and outcome measures across multiple temporal resolutions: treatment-level, session-level, and follow-up self-reports plus clinician assessments (HRSD, BDI-II), as well as fine-grained within-session data including linguistic features from transcripts, vocal prosody, facial expressions, body movement, physiological signals (ECG, EDA), and hormonal measures (cortisol). A subsample was manually coded using the MIND coding system for patients' affective/behavioral/cognitive experiences, therapists' interventions, and clinician session summaries. The sample was predominantly female (63.8%), with a mean age of 36.66 years (SD = 9.45, range 21-61). The majority (71.9%) met both HRSD and BDI-II threshold criteria at intake. Six patients dropped out before session 12, leaving the final analytic sample of 58.

## Clinical Insight
This dataset represents one of the most comprehensive multimodal psychotherapy process datasets available for depression treatment. For clinicians and researchers, its value lies in enabling investigation of how moment-to-moment therapeutic processes — including nonverbal behavior, physiological arousal, linguistic patterns, and relational dynamics — relate to treatment outcomes. The combination of supportive-expressive psychodynamic therapy with this level of multimodal measurement opens new avenues for studying mechanisms of change in psychotherapy that go beyond self-report alone. The human-annotated sessions are specifically designed to support LLM-based large-scale analysis, potentially enabling automated identification of change-promoting therapeutic patterns. Clinicians may benefit from future findings about which specific therapist behaviors and relational dynamics are most strongly associated with client improvement.

## Relevant For
Psychotherapy researchers studying mechanisms of change in psychodynamic therapy for depression; clinicians interested in evidence-based process-outcome relationships; researchers developing computational approaches to psychotherapy process analysis; those investigating the therapeutic alliance and interpersonal dynamics in MDD treatment.

## Notes
This is a methodology paper describing the dataset rather than reporting specific findings. The data are designed to support future analyses examining patterns, dynamics, and sequences that may facilitate or hinder treatment processes and outcomes.
