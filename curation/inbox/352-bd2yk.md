## Summary

This study extracted and categorized the active components of 109 psychological intervention arms from RCTs targeting stimulant (cocaine and/or amphetamine) use disorder, drawn from a network meta-analysis of 50 RCTs plus 10 additional RCTs identified in a search update. A total of 1,592 distinct intervention elements were identified from full intervention descriptions (protocols, manuals), then consolidated into 25 transtheoretical components. These components were further organized into 40 unique intervention profiles (distinct combinations of components). The four most frequently employed components were relapse prevention (present in 34 of 40 profiles), functional analysis (32), skill generalization (29), and therapeutic alliance (29). The least frequent were mindfulness and meditation (3 profiles) and subpopulation issues (4). Inter-rater reliability was assessed via Cohen's kappa on a random 35% subset: two components showed fair agreement (κ=0.20-0.40), four showed moderate agreement (κ=0.41-0.60), and eleven showed substantial agreement (κ>0.60). The taxonomy was developed through systematic extraction, iterative consensus coding, and refined in a 2.5-day expert meeting.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with stimulant use disorders, this taxonomy provides a concrete, evidence-based component checklist that goes beyond brand-name protocols. The dominance of relapse prevention (85% of profiles), functional analysis (80%), skill generalization (72.5%), and therapeutic alliance (72.5%) identifies a de facto core package for stimulant use — therapists should verify these four components are present regardless of the manual they are using. The relative rarity of mindfulness (7.5%) and subpopulation-specific components (10%) is a notable counterpoint to trends in other areas of mental health, suggesting that for stimulant users specifically, behavioral and skill-based components drive change rather than contemplative or identity-focused approaches. The therapeutic alliance appearing in nearly three-quarters of profiles reinforces that in addiction treatment, where dropout and disengagement are primary barriers, the relationship is not supplementary but central. Practically, therapists can use this taxonomy as a component-selection tool: rather than asking "Which manual?", ask "Which combination of these 25 components does this specific client need?" — for example, a client with strong social reinforcement for use may need more functional analysis and skill generalization, while one with poor self-efficacy may need more psychoeducation and coping skills training.

## Relevant For

Therapists treating stimulant use disorders, addiction counselors designing individualized treatment plans, clinical supervisors ensuring core components are delivered

## Notes

Full text not available (abstract only); taxonomy limited to stimulant use disorders and may not generalize to other substances; component identification relied on written descriptions which may not capture all intervention elements actually delivered; inter-rater reliability was variable across components with two showing only fair agreement; the 2.5-day expert meeting could introduce consensus bias.
