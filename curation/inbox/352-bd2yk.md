## Summary

This study extracted and categorized the active components of 109 psychological intervention arms from RCTs targeting stimulant use disorders, identifying 1,592 distinct elements consolidated into 25 transtheoretical components. These were further organized into 40 unique intervention profiles. The most frequently employed components were relapse prevention (34 profiles), functional analysis (32), skill generalization (29), and therapeutic alliance (29). The least frequent were mindfulness and meditation (3) and subpopulation issues (4). The taxonomy was developed through systematic extraction, iterative consensus coding, and a 2.5-day expert meeting, with inter-rater reliability ranging from fair to substantial across components.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with substance use, this taxonomy offers a practical framework for moving beyond protocol fidelity toward intentional component selection. Rather than asking "Which manual should I follow?", therapists can ask "Which combination of components does this client need?" The dominance of relapse prevention, functional analysis, and skill generalization across effective treatments suggests these are the core ingredients for stimulant use disorders specifically, and therapists should ensure these are present even when using a different primary modality. The finding that therapeutic alliance appeared in 29 of 40 profiles reinforces that regardless of theoretical orientation, the relationship remains central — this is particularly important in addiction work where dropout and disengagement are common. The rarity of mindfulness components (only 3 profiles) is noteworthy and cautions against assuming mindfulness is a universal add-on; for stimulant users, behavioral and skill-based components appear to be the primary drivers of change. Therapists can use this taxonomy as a checklist when designing individualized treatment plans.

## Relevant For

Therapists treating stimulant use disorders and those interested in evidence-based component selection for addiction treatment
