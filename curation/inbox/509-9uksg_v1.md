## Summary
This EMA study examined whether momentary inhibitory control, planned use, craving, and perceived control predict alcohol consumption in recreational drinkers. 142 participants (40 males, 102 females; M=33.30 years, SD=14.76) completed a four-day EMA protocol with three daily random prompts assessing alcohol use (drinks in past 2 hours or since waking), craving (0-100 VAS), perceived control over drinking (0-100 VAS), and planned use (estimated total drinks for the day). Each prompt was directly followed by a mobile Go/No-Go task (100 trials, 5 minutes) assessing behavioral inhibitory control.

Using Bayesian multilevel models, momentary results showed a positive association between planned use and drinking both between-persons (beta=0.18, 95% CrI [0.03, 0.31]) and within-persons (beta=0.31, 95% CrI [0.20, 0.42]). Counterintuitively, negative within-person effects emerged for craving (beta=-0.12, 95% CrI [-0.23, -0.02]) and perceived control (beta=-0.41, 95% CrI [-0.52, -0.28]). At the daily level, planned use was positively associated with daily drinking (beta=0.84, 95% CrI [0.44, 1.24]) and perceived control was negatively associated (beta=-0.78, 95% CrI [-1.21, -0.36]). Inhibitory control errors showed no credible association with drinking at either timescale.

The paradoxical negative within-person association between momentary craving and drinking likely reflects a mismatch in reference windows: craving was measured in the moment (right now), while drinking was measured retrospectively (past 2 hours or since waking). On days when participants were currently craving but had not yet drunk, they scored high on craving but low on drinking, creating a negative within-person association despite a positive overall trend. The lack of inhibitory control effects suggests that in recreational (non-dependent) drinkers, behavioral inhibition is not a meaningful driver of consumption at the momentary level.

## Clinical Insight
For recreational drinkers, alcohol use appears driven primarily by intentions (planned use) and self-regulatory beliefs (perceived control) rather than by momentary lapses in inhibitory control. This has implications for early intervention: rather than targeting impulse control (which may be more relevant in dependent populations), interventions for non-dependent drinkers should focus on modifying drinking intentions and enhancing perceived self-efficacy over drinking behavior.

The strong negative within-person effect of perceived control (beta=-0.41) suggests that momentary fluctuations in how much control people feel they have over their drinking are closely tied to whether they actually drink. This aligns with the Theory of Planned Behavior and suggests that brief interventions enhancing self-efficacy at the moment of decision-making could be effective. The null finding for inhibitory control, combined with prior evidence of dose-response relationships between alcohol exposure and inhibitory impairment, supports a progression model where inhibitory control becomes more relevant as drinking severity increases.

## Relevant For
Researchers studying self-regulation and alcohol use, clinicians developing brief interventions for non-dependent drinkers, and EMA methodologists interested in combining behavioral tasks with self-report.

## Notes
From Erasmus University Rotterdam. The craving paradox is an important methodological lesson for EMA studies about reference window alignment. The null finding for Go/No-Go is notable given mixed prior literature. Convenience sample limits generalizability.
