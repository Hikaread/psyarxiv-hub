## Summary
This seven-day experience sampling study (N = 174; 4,625 observations) investigated how momentary evaluations of social media use (SMU) — specifically perceived agency (control over use) and valence (perceived benefit vs. harm) — relate to well-being. Building on Lee and Hancock's (2024) social media mindsets framework, the study tested both behavioral and appraisal pathways. At the within-person level, more positive valence evaluations and greater perceived agency were consistently associated with lower negative affect. Mindless scrolling showed small associations with increased negative affect but did not reliably mediate the evaluation-wellbeing relationship. Valence evaluations moderated the SMU-negative affect link: when participants experienced their use as more beneficial, increases in use were less strongly associated with negative affect. The study found limited support for the behavioral pathway and partial support for the appraisal pathway, suggesting that subjective experience is part of the process through which digital behaviors relate to wellbeing.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians whose clients present with social media-related distress, this study suggests that how clients evaluate their social media use matters more than how much they use it. Rather than focusing treatment on reducing screen time — an approach with limited empirical support — clinicians may achieve better outcomes by addressing clients' beliefs about and subjective experiences of social media. Two targets emerge: increasing perceived agency (helping clients feel more in control of their social media behavior) and shifting valence evaluations (helping clients notice beneficial aspects of their social media engagement). The moderation finding is particularly important: the same amount of social media use has different affective consequences depending on whether the person experiences it as beneficial or harmful, suggesting that cognitive reframing of social media experiences could be a therapeutic intervention in itself.

## Relevant For
Clinicians addressing social media-related concerns in therapy; those working with depression and anxiety where social media plays a maintaining role; researchers interested in digital wellbeing; therapists incorporating behavioral activation or cognitive restructuring around technology use.

## Notes
The cross-sectional ESM design (evaluations and affect assessed concurrently) limits causal inference. The moderation finding for valence could reflect state-dependent appraisal rather than causal moderation. Sample characteristics and platform-specific effects were not extensively analyzed.
