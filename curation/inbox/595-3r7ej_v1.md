## Summary
This paper presents the **protocol for a qualitative study** exploring how **peer support** may influence **employment outcomes** for people living with psychotic disorders within Quebec's socio-cultural and economic context. Developed in collaboration with the **Société québécoise de la schizophrénie et des psychoses apparentées**, the study uses a **participatory approach** where workshops are led by **peer workers** addressing themes including coping with illness, disclosure, and social skills. Data collection involves **focus groups** with three stakeholder categories: service participants, peer workers, and employment counselors. Analysis will use **Braun and Clarke's inductive thematic analysis** supported by NVivo, with **member checking** and **reflective journaling** for rigor. The protocol details the theoretical rationale linking peer support to **recovery-oriented employment services** and the Quebec-specific context of supported employment programs.

## Clinical Insight
Although this is a protocol without results, it addresses an important gap: the role of **peer workers with lived experience** in **supported employment programs** for psychotic disorders is **poorly documented** despite the established effectiveness of peer support in mental health more broadly. The **participatory design** — co-developed with a community organization — is itself a model of how psychosis research should be conducted. For clinicians and service managers, this protocol outlines a framework for integrating **peer expertise** into vocational rehabilitation, which is particularly relevant given that **employment is a key recovery goal** yet most people with psychotic disorders remain unemployed. The focus on **disclosure** and **social skills** in employment contexts addresses real-world barriers that individual therapy alone may not resolve. The Quebec-specific context (distinct social programs, French-speaking services) adds cultural relevance for Francophone services.
