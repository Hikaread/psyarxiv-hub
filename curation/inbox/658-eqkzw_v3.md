## Summary
This paper describes an **error-adjusted approach** to interpreting discrepancies between **neuropsychological test scores**, addressing a widespread problem in clinical assessment.
Current practice often interprets score differences using **simple standard deviation thresholds** (e.g., 1 or 1.5 SD difference), but these approaches do not explicitly account for **measurement error**, leading clinicians to infer deficits where none may exist.
Drawing on **classical test theory**, the authors derive the **standard error of the difference** from the standard errors of measurement of the two tests being compared.
The resulting **Error-Adjusted Difference (EAD)** expresses the size of an observed discrepancy relative to the shared measurement error of the two tests.
A worked example illustrates how interpretation of the **same raw score difference** varies substantially as a function of test **reliability** — a difference that appears large on unreliable tests may be trivial on reliable ones.
The method is intended as a **practical complement** to existing approaches (normative comparisons, regression-based methods), particularly in contexts where those methods are not readily available.

## Clinical Insight
Neuropsychological **test discrepancy analysis** is a routine part of clinical assessment, used to identify cognitive strengths and weaknesses, lateralized brain dysfunction, and specific learning disabilities.
This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked problem: **reliability-driven misinterpretation** of score discrepancies.
For clinicians, the EAD provides a straightforward method to determine whether a score difference is **larger than expected given the imprecision of the tests themselves**, which should be the minimum threshold before attributing clinical significance to a discrepancy.
The worked example powerfully demonstrates that the same 10-point difference between two tests could be **clinically meaningful on highly reliable tests** but **entirely within measurement error on less reliable tests**.
This approach is particularly useful for **bedside or resource-limited settings** where comprehensive normative data or regression-based methods may not be available.
The method complements (rather than replaces) **demographically-adjusted norms** and should be adopted as a standard step in neuropsychological report writing.

## Methodology Note
This is an other-type paper (methodological/theoretical).

__Statistical Rigor__: The mathematical derivation of the **standard error of the difference** from classical test theory is well-established and clearly presented.
The **EAD formula** is a direct application of established psychometric principles, making the method both rigorous and accessible.
A limitation is that the method is demonstrated with a single worked example rather than validated across multiple test batteries or clinical populations.

__Measurement Validity__: The paper is fundamentally about improving the **validity of clinical interpretations** of neuropsychological test discrepancies.
By accounting for measurement error, the EAD reduces the risk of **false-positive discrepancies** (inferring deficits that do not exist).
The method does not address **construct validity** issues (whether the tests measure what they claim to measure) or **criterion validity** (whether discrepancies predict real-world outcomes).

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint**.
No data or code are associated with this methodological paper.
The method itself is simple enough to implement with basic spreadsheet software, promoting accessibility.

__Participant Transparency__: Not applicable to this methodological paper.
The worked example uses illustrative test scores rather than real patient data.
