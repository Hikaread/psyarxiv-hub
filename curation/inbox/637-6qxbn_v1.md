## Summary

This **systematic review** evaluates the effectiveness of **lay worker-delivered psychosocial interventions** for people who use illicit drugs, specifically when delivered **outside formal healthcare settings** — via community outreach, phone, or online platforms. Searching PubMed, Cochrane CENTRAL, Embase, APA PsycINFO, trial registries and grey literature, the reviewers identified **13 studies** (8 RCTs, 4 pre-post, 1 pre-post with controls) meeting inclusion criteria. Studies primarily enrolled people using opioids, cocaine/crack, or amphetamines, with 57% of all participants being women. The review synthesised outcomes from the 8 studies with low or moderate risk of bias.
Across studies, **substance use and mental health outcomes improved over time** in both intervention and control groups. However, evidence for **additional benefits of lay worker-delivered components** was inconsistent: three RCTs reported favourable between-group differences (primarily in **harm reduction** interventions using **brief, structured formats**), while three RCTs found no significant differences. Mental health outcomes (assessed in only two studies using the **PHQ-9** and **GAD-7**) showed non-significant improvements with wide confidence intervals, likely reflecting underpowered samples. **Quality of life** was measured in only one study, with no significant between-group differences.
The review highlights that **high attrition** (median 25% at first follow-up) and the frequent use of **active comparators** may have obscured the incremental value of lay worker components. Only one study incorporated elements of an evidence-based intervention (**motivational interviewing**). No consistent patterns were identified across population characteristics or intervention formats.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working in **addiction and substance use**, this review has several important implications. First, it confirms that people who use illicit drugs show consistent improvements over time regardless of intervention condition, suggesting that **natural recovery processes** and the non-specific effects of study participation contribute substantially to outcomes — a finding that should temper expectations about any single intervention's unique contribution. Second, the **accessibility argument** for lay worker-delivered interventions remains compelling: these interventions reach people who actively avoid formal healthcare due to **stigma, criminalisation, and legal concerns**. Even if the evidence for incremental effectiveness is currently weak, lay workers may play a critical role in **engaging and connecting** this hard-to-reach population to services.
Third, the finding that **brief, structured harm reduction interventions** showed the most promising between-group effects (e.g., 60–80% reduction in drug use days, lower opioid overdose rates at 12 months: 12.5% vs. 32.5%) suggests that clinicians and programme designers should prioritise **focused, time-limited interventions** over intensive, long-term formats when deploying lay workers. Fourth, the near-absence of **evidence-based therapeutic components** (only one study used motivational interviewing) represents a major gap — integrating established approaches like **MI, CBT, or contingency management** into lay worker training could substantially improve outcomes. Fifth, the high attrition rates underscore the importance of **retention strategies**, including contingency management, even at low cost, to improve both scientific rigour and clinical impact.

## Methodology Note

This is an other-type paper (systematic review), so Statistical Power, Preregistration, and Effect Sizes are not applicable in the traditional sense.

__Statistical Rigor__: The review followed PRISMA guidelines, used a rigorous two-stage machine-learning-assisted screening approach (DocTER), and applied NIH **Quality Assessment Tools** appropriate to each study design. Risk of bias was assessed by two independent reviewers. However, only 1 of 13 studies was rated as low risk of bias, 7 as moderate, and 5 as high — limiting the strength of outcome synthesis. Narrative synthesis was used due to heterogeneity, which is appropriate but precludes meta-analytic effect estimation.

__Measurement Validity__: Five validated substance use measures were identified across studies (MAP, SURE, SCID-MINI, TCU Drug Screen, COMM), alongside mental health measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7). The heterogeneity of outcome indicators limits cross-study comparability. No studies measured all three outcome domains (substance use, mental health, quality of life) simultaneously.

__Open Science__: Protocol registered on OSF (osf.io/8udv6).
Data extraction and risk of bias assessment tables available at osf.io/gnbv7/files/mwtdb.

__Participant Transparency__: 13 studies included a total of 2,842 participants (57% women; weighted mean age 39).
11 studies conducted in the United States, one in Scotland, one in Malaysia.
Populations included general drug users (8 studies), women (2), MSM (2), and chronic pain patients (1).
Primary substances were opioids, cocaine/crack, and amphetamines.
Sample sizes ranged from 8 to 725.
