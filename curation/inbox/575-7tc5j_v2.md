## Summary

This **ecological momentary assessment (EMA)** study examined how **active** and **passive social media use** relate to momentary **thought patterns** and **affect** in **N = 146 young adults** (aged 18-23) over 14 days, yielding **4,325 EMA reports** including **1,171 reports** of recent social media use (27% of prompts). **Exploratory factor analysis** of Multidimensional Experience Sampling (MDES) thought items identified three cognitive dimensions during social media use: **Episodic, social, memory-based thought**; **Intentional, detailed, task-focused thought**; and **Intrusive or negative thought**. **Intrusive or negative thought** showed the strongest associations with distress: higher **fatigue** (b = 6.65, SE = 0.98, p < .001), higher **high-arousal negative affect** (b = 8.93, SE = 0.65, p < .001), higher **boredom** (b = 3.17, SE = 0.97, p = .001), and higher **sadness**, alongside lower positive affect. Compared with passive use, **active use** was associated with higher Episodic thought, higher Intentional thought, and was significantly less likely when physically alone (OR = 0.43, 95% CI [0.32, 0.58]). **Multilevel models** revealed that within-person fluctuations in intrusive thought during social media use predicted momentary distress above and beyond use type. ## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with young adult clients who report social media-related distress, this study provides granular, momentary evidence that it is not social media use per se that drives negative affect, but the **quality of cognition** during use — specifically **intrusive or negative thought**. This finding supports therapeutic approaches that target **cognitive patterns** rather than behaviour (use frequency) alone. In **CBT** for depression or anxiety, clinicians can help clients identify the specific thought content that arises during social media use and apply standard cognitive restructuring techniques. The distinction between **active** (messaging, posting) and **passive** (scrolling, watching) use is clinically relevant: active use was associated with more adaptive thought patterns and was less likely when alone, suggesting it may serve a genuinely **social connecting** function. In contrast, passive scrolling may be more likely to trigger the intrusive, comparative cognition that drives distress. This maps onto **behavioural activation** principles — helping clients shift from passive consumption to active, purposeful engagement with digital environments. The finding that 10.3% of participants reported social media use in more than half their EMA prompts suggests a meaningful subgroup for whom **digital habits** may warrant dedicated clinical attention.

## Relevant For
- Therapists treating young adults with social media-related distress
- CBT practitioners targeting cognitive patterns in digital contexts
- Researchers studying digital mental health using EMA methodologies
- University counselling services addressing technology-related wellbeing concerns

## Notes

Full 38-page PDF extracted. The EMA design with 4 daily prompts over 14 days provides rich within-person data. The three-factor thought structure is a novel contribution that bridges social media research and cognitive science. The sample is limited to young adults (18-23), primarily university students, limiting generalisability. Self-reported social media use may be subject to reporting bias, though EMA reduces this compared to retrospective measures. The cross-sectional within-person design cannot establish causal direction between thought patterns and affect.
