## Summary

This secondary analysis of an online study (N = 300, 76.7% female, M age = 25.52, SD = 6.79) examined how trauma type (interpersonal vs. non-interpersonal) and coping style (problem-focused, emotion-focused, avoidant) relate to posttraumatic symptomatology. Participants had experienced a DSM-5 traumatic event within the past 18 months, assessed via the Life Events Checklist for DSM-5 (LEC-5). The sample included 99 participants with interpersonal trauma and 201 with non-interpersonal trauma. Sexual assault was the most common interpersonal trauma (n = 32), while severe human suffering was the most common non-interpersonal trauma (n = 53).

Interpersonal trauma was associated with significantly higher PTSD symptoms (d = .53, p < .001), depression (d = .44, p < .001), anxiety (d = .37, p < .01), and stress (d = .36, p < .01), plus lower life satisfaction (d = -.39, p < .01). The interpersonal trauma group also reported significantly higher avoidant coping (d = .31, p = .03) and lower problem-focused coping (d = -.34, p < .01), with no difference in emotion-focused coping (d = -.23, p = .07).

Within both trauma groups, avoidant coping was the only coping style significantly correlated with PTSD symptoms. In hierarchical regression, avoidant coping was the sole significant predictor of PTSD symptoms in both the interpersonal group (β = .61, p < .001) and the non-interpersonal group (β = .42, p < .001). Cross-group, trauma type itself predicted PTSD symptoms (β = .52, p < .001).

An exploratory mediation analysis (not pre-registered) found that avoidant coping partially mediated the association between trauma type and PTSD symptoms (indirect effect a*b = .15, 95% CI [.02, .28], p = .02), with the direct effect remaining significant (c' = .37, 95% CI [.15, .58], p = .001). Measures used: PCL-5 (α = .95), DASS-21 (depression α = .92, anxiety α = .84, stress α = .90), SWLS (α = .90), and Brief-COPE. Data collection ran September 2022 to October 2024. Both the original study and these secondary analyses were pre-registered on AsPredicted.

## Clinical Insight

The finding that avoidant coping partially mediates the relationship between interpersonal trauma and PTSD severity has direct implications for clinical assessment and treatment planning. Clinicians working with trauma survivors should routinely assess coping style using the Brief-COPE, as avoidant coping emerged as the strongest predictor of PTSD symptoms regardless of trauma type, with a notably larger effect in the interpersonal trauma group (β = .61 vs. β = .42). The small-to-medium effect sizes for group differences suggest that while interpersonal trauma confers additional risk, avoidant coping is a more proximal and modifiable target. The study supports CBT for PTSD, which directly targets avoidant coping strategies (Bourdon et al., 2019; Zang et al., 2017). The authors suggest that trauma-related cognitions may underlie the relationship between avoidance and PTSD, pointing to Cognitive Bias Modification-Appraisal (CBM-App) as a potential adjunctive intervention. The 18-month recall window for traumatic events and the retrospective assessment of coping are limitations that clinicians should consider when applying these findings.

## Relevant For

Trauma therapists, PTSD specialists, clinical psychologists conducting trauma assessments, CBT practitioners working with avoidance

## Notes

Secondary analysis of pre-registered data. Retrospective design limits causal inference. Sample was predominantly female and young (German university context). Mediation analysis was exploratory and not pre-registered. Brief-COPE emotion-focused subscale showed weak internal consistency.
