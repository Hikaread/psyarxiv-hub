## Summary

Four experiments (total N = 297) examined how **trait cognitive anxiety** and **trait somatic anxiety** differentially affect **visual working memory** for bound colour-shape features. Experiments 1 and 2 used **cued verbal recall** and found no effects of either anxiety dimension on **binding performance**, regardless of whether top-down attentional control or bottom-up perceptual distraction was manipulated. Experiments 3 and 4 switched to **single-probe recognition** and revealed a double dissociation: **trait cognitive anxiety** was linearly associated with improved **shape feature recognition** (F(1,170) = 17.90, p < .001) but did not impair binding, while **trait somatic anxiety** was associated with reduced **binding recognition** accuracy (F(1,170) = 9.27, p = .003) without affecting feature memory. This somatic anxiety effect on binding was observed across varying levels of **cognitive load** and was specific to set size 3 in Experiment 3. These results challenge **Attentional Control Theory**, which predicts that cognitive anxiety should impair executive-dependent processing, by showing that somatic anxiety may be the more sensitive predictor of **cognitive deficits**.

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: All four experiments were **pre-registered** on OSF with the same ethical approval. The systematic progression from cued recall (Experiments 1-2) to **single-probe recognition** (Experiments 3-4) is well-motivated by theory and allows direct comparison of response modalities. The use of the **STICSA** to separate cognitive and somatic anxiety dimensions is appropriate. **Bayes Factors** supplement frequentist analyses, providing evidence both for and against effects. The inclusion of **DASS-21** depression and stress as covariates strengthens specificity claims. Data are openly available on OSF.

__Limitation__: Data collection in Experiments 3 and 4 was terminated early due to **COVID-19 lab closures**, reducing power from the preregistered 96 to 86 and 68 participants respectively. The anxiety groups were created **post hoc** by splitting STICSA scores rather than through stratified sampling. **Articulatory suppression** was used throughout, which may have masked effects of cognitive anxiety that operate through subvocal worry. The sample consisted of university students (mean age approximately 22), predominantly female, limiting generalisability to clinical populations. The inter-stimulus interval differed between Experiments 1-2 and 3-4, introducing a confound. The inconsistent pattern across experiments (effects only in recognition, not recall) requires careful interpretation.

## Clinical Insight

The finding that **trait somatic anxiety** — not cognitive anxiety — predicts impaired **feature binding** in working memory has important implications for clinical assessment and case formulation. Patients presenting with somatic symptoms such as **muscle tension**, **racing heart**, and **body vigilance** may experience subtle **cognitive deficits** in integrating visual information that are not captured by standard cognitive assessments targeting worry. This is particularly relevant for **generalised anxiety disorder** and **health anxiety**, where somatic hyperarousal is prominent. Clinicians should be aware that self-reported somatic symptoms may index **attentional resource depletion** through internal body monitoring, potentially affecting daily functioning in ways that cognitive-focused anxiety measures would miss. The dissociation also suggests that treatment targeting **somatic arousal** (e.g., relaxation training, interoceptive exposure) may improve cognitive functioning independently of addressing worry.

## Relevant For

- Clinical psychologists assessing cognitive functioning in anxiety disorders
- Neuropsychologists evaluating working memory in anxious patients
- Researchers on attentional control theory and its clinical extensions
- Clinicians treating generalised anxiety disorder with prominent somatic symptoms
