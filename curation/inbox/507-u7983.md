## Summary

This **mixed-methods survey** examined accessibility barriers, participation, and wellbeing impacts among **149 autistic adults** and **97 parents/caregivers** of autistic people in Australia across seven everyday life domains: healthcare, employment, employment support, transport, retail, education/training, and community/leisure. Autistic adults reported on their own experiences; parents reported on behalf of the autistic person they supported. **998 open-text responses** from 208 participants (85% of the sample) were analyzed using **reflexive thematic analysis**.

Accessibility barriers were widespread: **64–82%** of autistic adults who had used each service reported difficulties. Barriers commonly occurred across multiple domains, and **more than half** of respondents across all domains rated the wellbeing impact as moderate or large. **Sensory environments** were the most frequently endorsed barrier type. Four themes were generated: (1) environments were **actively hostile** to autistic needs; (2) services were available but not always **accessible**; (3) access depended on whether people **understood autistic needs**; and (4) repeated inaccessibility **narrowed everyday life**. The qualitative data revealed that inaccessibility created an **access burden**—the ongoing work of planning, recovering, and managing uncertainty—that could reduce capacity for hours or days afterward.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians and service providers working with autistic individuals and families, this study provides compelling evidence that accessibility barriers are not isolated incidents but a **systemic, cross-domain burden** that directly impacts mental health. The concept of **access burden**—the hidden work of identifying suitable services, planning around sensory demands, preparing for unfamiliar environments, and recovering afterward—resonates with descriptions of **autistic burnout**. Clinicians should routinely assess the accessibility demands of their clients' daily environments and consider whether environmental barriers may be contributing to presenting difficulties. The finding that accessibility is **relational** (depending on staff understanding and flexibility) rather than merely physical suggests that even modest changes in service delivery could yield significant benefits. The neurodiversity-affirming framing—shifting responsibility from individual adaptation to environmental design—aligns with best practice in autism support.

## Methodology Note

This is a mixed_methods paper.

__Statistical Power__: Not applicable for the qualitative component.
The quantitative descriptive analysis included 246 participants (149 autistic adults, 97 parents/caregivers), which provides adequate descriptive precision for the reported frequencies.
No inferential statistics or hypothesis tests were conducted.

__Preregistration__: Not mentioned.

__Effect Sizes__: Not applicable (descriptive quantitative, qualitative).

__Statistical Rigor__: Descriptive statistics (frequencies, percentages) were used appropriately for the quantitative component.
The survey used **Qualtrics** with in-built bot detection.
Missing data were not imputed; available-case analysis was used.
The survey was not mandatory beyond eligibility screening, with partial responses retained (n = 18).
The qualitative analysis followed **reflexive thematic analysis** (Braun & Clarke, 2006, 2019) with inductive coding at both semantic and latent levels.
The lead author is an autistic researcher, which adds valuable experiential perspective but may also shape interpretive emphasis.
Coding was conducted by the lead author and discussed with the broader team, but no inter-coder reliability is reported—consistent with the reflexive TA approach but limiting claims about coding consistency.

__Measurement Validity__: Barrier types were assessed via researcher-designed items (not validated instruments): physical accessibility, sensory environment, communication/information, staff attitudes, unpredictability, cost, wait times.
Frequency was rated 1–4; wellbeing impact was rated 1–4.
These measures have not been validated, which is acknowledged as a limitation.
The survey was piloted by autistic staff at Aspect.

__Open Science__: Data are **not publicly available** because participants did not consent to public data sharing and the dataset includes sensitive open-text responses that may carry identification risk.
No preregistration.
The full survey is provided as supplemental material.

__Participant Transparency__: 246 participants: 149 autistic adults (136 formally diagnosed, 13 self-identified), 97 parents/caregivers (32 also autistic themselves).
Autistic adults: M age = 40.61 (SD = 12.93, range 18–87).
Children described by parents: M age = 14.61 (SD = 8.82, range 3–42).
Common co-occurring conditions among autistic adults: ADHD (74%), anxiety disorder (60%), mood disorder (42%).
The sample was not nationally representative—predominantly European/White, highly educated, recruited through autism organizations and social media.
The online format may have excluded those with limited internet access, intellectual disability, or communication needs not supported by text-based surveys.
Ethics: Griffith University HREC (2025/950).

## Notes

The concept of "access burden" developed here extends administrative burden theory into the sensory, social, and recovery domains. The findings support arguments by Mandy (2022) that the autism mental health crisis cannot be addressed without changing environments. The study was conducted by researchers at Aspect (Autism Spectrum Australia), and the potential conflict of interest regarding the organization's Autism Friendly consultancy service is transparently disclosed.
