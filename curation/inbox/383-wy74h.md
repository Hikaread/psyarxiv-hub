## Summary
Using data from the UK Household Longitudinal Study, this study prospectively examined whether household food insecurity assessed in 2021-2023 predicted eating disorder pathology two years later in adolescents aged 14-17. Controlling for socio-demographics, carer mental health, ethnicity, financial strain, and urban/rural residence, food insecurity showed only a weak association with later dieting and weight-loss behaviours (RR 1.07, 95% CI 0.99-1.16) and no association with appearance concerns. These findings challenge assumptions about food insecurity as a direct driver of eating disorder pathology.

## Clinical Insight
Therapists should be cautious about over-attributing eating disorder presentations to food insecurity in their clinical formulations. While food-insecure clients may present with disordered eating patterns, these behaviours may reflect economic necessity rather than psychopathology per se. The weak prospective association found here supports the need for comprehensive assessment that considers multiple pathways to eating pathology. This is particularly relevant when working with socioeconomically diverse caseloads where conflating food restriction due to poverty with eating disorder dieting could lead to misdirected treatment.

## Relevant For
Eating disorder therapists, adolescent mental health clinicians, and therapists working in socioeconomically disadvantaged communities.

## Notes
Analytical sample included 1,140 participants (52% female, 18.8% ethnic minority). The two-year follow-up window captures early adolescent development. Findings do not negate the importance of addressing food insecurity as part of holistic care but suggest it operates through different pathways than core eating disorder psychopathology.
