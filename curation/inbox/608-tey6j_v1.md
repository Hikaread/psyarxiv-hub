## Summary
This study investigated **olfactory disgust conditioning** using a within-subjects design with **71 participants** (54 women) across two sessions examining **delay conditioning** (2s CS-UCS overlap) versus **trace conditioning** (4s gap). Faces served as conditioned stimuli (CS) and individualized aversive odors as unconditioned stimuli (UCS). **Differential conditioning** (CS+ vs. CS-) was demonstrated across all **self-report measures** (valence, disgust, arousal, expectancy) and most **psychophysiological markers**: **respiratory latency (RSP)**, **heart-period (HP) modulation**, **skin conductance response (SCR)**, and **pupil dilation response (PDR)**. **Trace conditioning** was associated with attenuated responding in HP, SCR, and PDR, but did not reduce response differentiation (the ability to distinguish CS+ from CS-), with the only significant CS-type interaction emerging for RSP in the CS-locked window. This study provides the first evidence that **pupil dilation** can serve as a marker of conditioned disgust.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have direct implications for understanding the **learning mechanisms** underlying disorders where disgust plays a central role, including **specific phobias** (blood-injection-injury), **contamination-related OCD**, and **eating disorders**. The demonstration that disgust conditioning can be measured across multiple **autonomic response systems** -- including the novel use of **pupil dilation** -- expands the methodological toolkit for both research and potential clinical assessment of disgust learning. The finding that **trace conditioning** (with a temporal gap) produced attenuated but still present differential conditioning suggests that disgust associations can form even when the disgust-eliciting event is temporally separated from the cue, which may explain why patients develop intense disgust reactions to stimuli only indirectly associated with contamination (e.g., a door handle touched by a contaminated object). The relative robustness of disgust conditioning across both delay and trace paradigms implies that **extinction-based treatments** for disgust-mediated disorders may need to be particularly thorough, as the associations appear resilient to reduced temporal contiguity. Clinicians using **exposure therapy** for contamination OCD or specific phobias should consider that disgust-based associations may be more resistant to extinction than fear-based ones.

## Relevant For
- Therapists treating contamination-related OCD or specific phobias
- Researchers studying disgust conditioning and extinction
- Clinicians using exposure-based interventions for disgust-mediated disorders
- Psychophysiology researchers interested in autonomic markers of emotional learning
