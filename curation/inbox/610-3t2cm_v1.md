## Summary
This study, accepted for publication in **Nature Mental Health**, challenges a foundational assumption in psychopathology research using **Bayesian change point models** applied to four waves of the **ABCD Study** data (N = 11,868 youths). The authors demonstrate that covariation among higher-order psychopathology dimensions (**internalizing**, **externalizing**, **thought problems**, **attention problems**) is driven by **nomorbidity** (shared absence of symptoms) rather than **comorbidity** (shared presence). At Wave 1, a 1-point increase in internalizing at asymptomatic levels was associated with a **32% increase** in externalizing (B = .28), but only a **7% increase** at higher symptom levels (B = .07) -- a 4.6-fold difference. The 1-change point negative binomial model substantially outperformed standard linear regression. These patterns replicated across all four waves and all six pairwise dimension comparisons. The findings challenge the interpretation of the **p-factor** and covariance-based structural models as reflecting comorbidity.

## Clinical Insight
This study has profound implications for how clinicians interpret research on the structure of psychopathology. The **p-factor**, often cited as evidence for a general liability to mental disorder, may primarily reflect the statistical artifact that most people do not have significant psychopathology, rather than a meaningful clinical dimension. At clinically relevant symptom levels, psychopathology dimensions become **more differentiated** rather than more overlapping -- meaning that a patient with severe depression does not necessarily have elevated externalizing or thought disorder symptoms. This supports the clinical utility of **specific disorder-focused assessments** and treatments rather than purely transdiagnostic approaches. For clinicians, the key takeaway is that covariance-based research findings (including factor structures) may not accurately describe the relationships between disorders at the severity levels encountered in clinical practice. The study also validates the clinical intuition that patients with multiple diagnoses often have genuinely distinct conditions rather than manifestations of a single underlying liability, reinforcing the importance of **individualized case formulation** over categorical or purely dimensional classification.
