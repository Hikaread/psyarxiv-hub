## Summary
This paper introduces a **three-level linear mixed-effects autoregressive model** for estimating the **reliability of single-item measures** in **intensive longitudinal data (ILD)**. The model explicitly accounts for the **nested data structure** (moments within days, days within persons) and incorporates **two distinct autoregressive processes** at both the moment and day levels while **partitioning measurement error** from within-person variability. The authors delineate two approaches to defining reliability: a **variance partitioning approach** (how well the instrument distinguishes among individuals) and a **correlation approach** (consistency of measurements across occasions). This framework yields a **multifaceted set of reliability coefficients** that distinguish **between-person from within-person reliability** and assess reliability across **different timescales** (moment-to-moment vs. day-to-day). The model is applied to a study where students reported **negative affect** multiple times per day for nine days before and after receiving exam results. The paper demonstrates that traditional reliability metrics (e.g., a single Cronbach's alpha) are **inadequate for ILD** and can produce **misleading estimates** when serial correlation is ignored.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians and researchers using **EMA, experience sampling, or ambulatory assessment** — increasingly common in clinical psychology for monitoring symptoms in real-time — this paper addresses a fundamental but often neglected measurement issue. **Single-item measures** are widely used in EMA to minimize participant burden, yet their **reliability is rarely evaluated appropriately**. The framework demonstrates that a single-item negative affect measure can have **acceptable between-person reliability** (distinguishing among individuals) while having **much lower within-person reliability** (consistency across moments within a day). This distinction is clinically crucial: if a therapist is using EMA data to track a patient's **moment-to-moment symptom fluctuations**, low within-person reliability means that observed changes may reflect **measurement noise** rather than genuine clinical change. Conversely, if the goal is to **compare patients** (e.g., identifying who has higher average anxiety), between-person reliability is more relevant. The model also shows that reliability can **differ across timescales** — a measure may be reliable day-to-day but not moment-to-moment, or vice versa. For **treatment monitoring** using EMA, this framework helps clinicians determine whether observed symptom changes are **trustworthy** enough to inform treatment adjustments. The **R implementation** makes the approach accessible for applied researchers.
