## Summary
This longitudinal study used the population-based **Young in Norway** cohort (N = 2,215) followed over **28 years** to examine how **mental health trajectories** from adolescence relate to **midlife political preferences**.
**Latent class growth analysis (LCGA)** identified three trajectories for **internalizing problems** (low-stable 67.2%, elevated-decreasing 26.4%, elevated-increasing 6.4%) and two for **externalizing problems** (elevated-decreasing 87.7%, elevated-stable 12.3%).
Elevated levels of either problem type were associated with **lower political and social trust** in midlife.
Elevated initial **internalizing** problems, regardless of subsequent development, were related to **more left-wing political orientations** and lower likelihood of voting center-right.
Persistently elevated **externalizing** problems were associated with **more right-wing orientations**, higher opposition to social equality, greater immigrant threat perceptions, and **twice the likelihood of voting populist-right**.
Effect sizes for internalizing problems were medium for trust outcomes (social trust d = -0.59 for elevated-decreasing, d = -1.17 for elevated-increasing) and small to medium for political orientation (d = -0.36 to d = -0.42).
Analysis code is publicly available on **OSF**.

## Clinical Insight
While the primary focus is political, this study is clinically relevant because it demonstrates that **adolescent mental health trajectories have long-term consequences** for social functioning well beyond clinical symptomatology.
The finding that both internalizing and externalizing trajectories predict **lower social and political trust** in midlife has implications for understanding how early mental health problems may contribute to **social withdrawal, institutional distrust, and reduced civic engagement** — all of which are relevant to therapeutic goals around social connection and functioning.
For clinicians working with adolescents, these findings underscore the importance of **early intervention**: mental health problems in adolescence are not just about current distress but can shape **worldviews and social orientations** decades later.
The divergent political patterns associated with internalizing vs. externalizing trajectories may also inform **case formulation** — chronic externalizing problems were associated with threat perceptions and opposition to equality, which could manifest in therapy as resistance, hostility toward outgroups, or distrust of helping institutions.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample of **N = 2,215** with 28-year follow-up is substantial for a longitudinal study.
However, the **elevated-increasing internalizing class** had only **142 participants**, and the **elevated-stable externalizing class** had only **273 participants**, which limits the precision of estimates for these subgroups.
No **a priori power analysis** was reported.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of **latent class growth analysis (LCGA)** is appropriate for identifying heterogeneous developmental trajectories.
Model selection was guided by **BIC, LMR LRT, and entropy**, with the three-class solution for internalizing and two-class solution for externalizing being well-justified.
The **Lanza et al. (2013)** approach for distal outcomes (political preferences) is a rigorous method for testing class differences.
A limitation is that the study was **not pre-registered**.

__Effect Sizes__: **Cohen's d** was reported for continuous outcomes, with transparent reporting of both large (social trust d = -1.17) and small effects (political interest d = -0.06).
For categorical outcomes (party vote), **probability differences** were reported, showing that the elevated-stable externalizing class had **twice the probability** of voting populist-right.
**95% confidence intervals** were not prominently reported for all effect sizes, though the detailed tables provide means and standard deviations.

__Measurement Validity__: Mental health was assessed using validated measures of **internalizing** (depression, anxiety, somatic symptoms) and **externalizing** (impulsivity, disruptive behavior, substance use, aggression) problems within the established internalizing-externalizing framework.
Political outcomes covered 12 distinct measures across three domains (turnout, engagement, attitudes), providing comprehensive coverage.
A limitation is that mental health was measured at only a few time points across 28 years, which may not capture short-term fluctuations or accurately model nonlinear trajectories.

__Open Science__: **Analysis code is publicly available on OSF** (https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/98A54), supporting full reproducibility.
The study was **not pre-registered**, which is a limitation given the exploratory nature of some analyses.
The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv**, and the manuscript is published (doi: 10.1111/pops.70178).

__Participant Transparency__: The sample is from the **population-based Young in Norway cohort**, which tracked participants from adolescence (T1 mean age ~14) to midlife (T7 mean age ~43).
The sample is **Norwegian**, which is a relatively homogeneous, high-income welfare state context, limiting generalizability to other cultural and political systems.
Attrition over 28 years is a concern, though the original sample was population-based (N = 2,215 at T1, with outcome data available for most at T7).
Background characteristics (gender, parental education) were used as covariates in auxiliary analyses.
