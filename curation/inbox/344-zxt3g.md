## Summary

This re-analysis challenges the widely cited Chiang et al. (2017) meta-analysis that concluded CBT is effective for reducing depression and mania in bipolar disorder. Smailes and colleagues identified that Chiang et al.'s conclusions depended partially on a pair of implausibly large effect sizes extracted from a 2010 RCT, and that a second publication of the same RCT reported much more plausible (and smaller) effect sizes. Replacing the implausible values with the corrected ones eliminates the statistically significant effect of CBT on both depression and mania severity. The original meta-analysis has been cited over 200 times and has influenced treatment guidelines.

## Clinical Insight

This re-analysis is important for any therapist who works with bipolar disorder and has used the Chiang et al. findings to justify CBT as an adjunctive treatment. While CBT for bipolar disorder is widely recommended in clinical guidelines, the evidence base may be weaker than previously believed. This does not mean CBT is useless for bipolar clients, but it does suggest that therapists should be honest about the limitations of the evidence and avoid overstating the expected impact on core mood symptoms. Many of the benefits of CBT in bipolar disorder may lie in domains not captured by symptom severity measures alone, such as medication adherence, early relapse detection, coping self-efficacy, and functional recovery. When working with bipolar clients, therapists may want to focus on these functional outcomes rather than expecting large reductions in depression or mania ratings, and to communicate realistic expectations at the outset of treatment.

## Relevant For

Therapists providing CBT or other psychotherapies for bipolar disorder, and those involved in clinical training or guideline development
