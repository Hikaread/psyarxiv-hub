## Summary

This tutorial introduces **Nix** — a deterministic software package manager — and the **{rix} R package**, which provides an R-native interface to Nix, as a solution for **computational environment reproducibility** in simulation studies. The authors argue that current reproducibility tools in psychology (e.g., **{renv}**, **{groundhog}**, **Docker**) address only part of the problem: package managers stabilise R packages but not the R interpreter or **system-level dependencies**, while containerization requires Linux expertise and may suffer from **temporal drift**. Nix addresses these gaps by building each environment from a declarative specification that pins the R version, all R packages, and all system dependencies using a **cryptographic hash** of all inputs. The tutorial walks through a complete reproducible simulation study, from specifying the environment with a single rix() call to generating an **APA-formatted manuscript** with apaquarto, illustrating how the entire software stack can be frozen at a specific date and rebuilt years later. The authors note that Siepe et al. (2024) found that nearly two-thirds of 100 simulation studies in psychology journals provide no code.

## Methodology Note

This is a **methods tutorial**, not an empirical study, so traditional methodological strengths and limitations do not apply in the same way. As a tutorial, its value lies in the clarity and practicality of the step-by-step guidance and the novelty of introducing the **Nix** ecosystem to psychology researchers. A potential limitation is that one author (Bruno Rodrigues) is a co-author and maintainer of the **{rix} package**, creating a **conflict of interest**, though this is openly declared. The tutorial acknowledges that exact numerical reproducibility across different **operating systems** and processors cannot be guaranteed due to **floating-point arithmetic** differences, particularly with certain R packages that rely on system BLAS/LAPACK libraries. The tutorial may be more accessible to researchers already comfortable with the command line, though it is written for a general R audience.

## Clinical Insight

While not a clinical paper per se, this tutorial addresses a foundational issue that affects all clinical researchers who use **statistical simulations** — which are the primary tool for evaluating the **statistical methods** that inform clinical practice. The documented gap that two-thirds of simulation studies provide no code, and those that do rarely document the **computational environment**, means that many methodological recommendations guiding clinical practice research may not be fully reproducible. For clinical researchers running power analyses, simulation-based evaluations of **measurement invariance**, or **Monte Carlo** studies of diagnostic accuracy, the **{rix}** workflow offers a practical path to full **computational reproducibility** without requiring deep systems expertise. Adoption of these tools would strengthen the evidential foundation upon which clinical **assessment instruments** and **treatment evaluation** methods are built.
