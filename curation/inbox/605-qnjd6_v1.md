## Summary
This comprehensive **scoping review** (56 pages) integrates developments across four domains to outline a roadmap for **AI-informed precision brain stimulation**. First, it surveys large-scale **neuroimaging repositories** (UK Biobank, HCP, ABCD, ENIGMA) that provide the population-level data foundations for **biomarker discovery**. Second, it traces the evolution of **computational head models** simulating electric, magnetic, and acoustic field distributions across non-invasive modalities including **transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)**, **transcranial electrical stimulation (tES)**, **temporal interference stimulation (TIS)**, and **transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS)**, plus invasive techniques like **deep brain stimulation (DBS)**. Third, it highlights the emergence of **neuroimaging foundation models** (e.g., **NeuroSTORM**) that leverage large-scale pre-training for predictive modeling of brain states, treatment response, and individualized stimulation optimization. Fourth, it presents the concept of **individualized digital twin brains** -- computational avatars integrating neuroimaging, physics-based simulations, and AI to simulate and test stimulation strategies **in silico**. Clinical use-cases include precision targeting for **seizure suppression** in epilepsy and individualized stimulation for **craving reduction** in addiction.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians, this review signals a paradigm shift from **one-size-fits-all** brain stimulation protocols toward **individualized neuromodulation** guided by patient-specific neuroimaging and computational modeling. The concept of **digital twin brains** represents a future where clinicians could test stimulation parameters virtually before applying them, potentially improving outcomes for treatment-resistant **depression**, **OCD**, **PTSD**, and **addiction**. The review highlights that **AI-driven targeting biomarkers** -- derived from individual functional connectivity patterns -- could address the well-known heterogeneity in treatment response to **TMS** and **tDCS**. Clinicians should be aware that **foundation models** trained on large neuroimaging datasets are beginning to enable prediction of treatment response, which could transform how stimulation parameters are selected. However, the authors note significant **ethical and regulatory challenges**, including issues of **data privacy**, **algorithmic bias** in training datasets, and the need for **clinical validation** before these approaches enter routine practice.
