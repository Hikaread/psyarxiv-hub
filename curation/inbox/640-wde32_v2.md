## Summary

This study dissociates **state-like, trait-like, and remission-related alterations** in reinforcement learning and value-based decision making in depression, using a carefully designed four-group comparison. **Unmedicated adults** (N = 195) were recruited across four groups: current depression (n = 49), remitted from depression (n = 50), first-degree relatives of depressed individuals (n = 36), and healthy controls (n = 60). Participants completed a **4-armed bandit (4AB) reinforcement learning task** under volatile uncertainty and a **gambling task** assessing value-based decision making under risk. Computational parameters (reward/punishment learning rates, outcome sensitivity, decision noise, risk aversion, loss aversion, inverse temperature) were estimated using **hierarchical Bayesian modelling** with **Bayesian model averaging**.
The key finding was a **selective punishment learning deficit** in remitted participants: they showed significantly lower punishment learning rates (F(3,191) = 5.43, p = .001) and lower lapse rates (F(3,191) = 4.37, p = .005) compared to all other groups. By contrast, in the gambling task, depressed and at-risk participants showed higher **inverse temperature**, suggesting more consistent use of learned values to guide choices. These results suggest that **altered punishment learning may persist after remission** (a potential scar effect or trait marker), while more deterministic decision-making may characterise both active depression and vulnerability states.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with **depression**, this study offers a computationally precise way to understand why some clients seem to **repeat maladaptive patterns** even after symptom remission. The finding that remitted individuals show **reduced punishment learning** (i.e., difficulty updating behaviour in response to negative outcomes) has direct clinical implications: clients who have "recovered" from depression may still struggle to learn from negative consequences, potentially maintaining **vulnerability to relapse** through continued engagement in behaviours that produce harmful outcomes. This maps onto the clinical observation that some remitted clients describe feeling "fine" while still making choices that perpetuate problems in relationships, work, or self-care.
The distinction between **state effects** (present during active depression, normalise after remission) and **trait/scar effects** (persist after remission) is critical for treatment planning. If impaired punishment learning is indeed a residual marker, it suggests that **relapse prevention protocols** should specifically target negative feedback processing — for instance, through structured exercises that help clients notice and respond to negative consequences in real time, rather than assuming that symptom remission alone restores adaptive learning. The elevated inverse temperature in depressed and at-risk groups (more value-consistent choice) might seem adaptive but could reflect **cognitive rigidity** — an over-reliance on learned value representations that reduces behavioural flexibility.

## Methodology Note

Paper type: quantitative_experimental.

__Statistical Power__: N = 195 across four groups (49 current depression, 50 remitted, 36 first-degree relatives, 60 controls).
This provides adequate power for the ANOVA-based group comparisons reported.
The unmedicated requirement strengthens internal validity but may limit generalisability to medicated populations.
The sample was 68.7% female, mean age 27.3 (SD 8.9).

__Preregistration__: Not stated in the abstract.

__Effect Sizes__: The abstract reports F-statistics and p-values for group comparisons but does not provide effect sizes (e.g., partial eta-squared, Cohen's d) or confidence intervals in the available text.
No PDF was available for full-text review, limiting assessment of reported effects.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of **hierarchical Bayesian modelling** with **Bayesian model averaging** is a computationally rigorous approach that naturally handles individual differences and model uncertainty.
Symptom dimensions were derived from **confirmatory factor analysis**, strengthening the symptom-level associations.
The four-group design (current, remitted, at-risk, healthy) elegantly dissociates state from trait effects.
Limitations: no PDF available for full review; cross-sectional design limits causal inference about whether punishment learning differences are truly trait-like or reflect residual symptoms.

__Measurement Validity__: Computational modelling of the 4AB task provides **process-level measures** (learning rates, outcome sensitivity) that are more informative than aggregate performance metrics.
The gambling task captures distinct aspects of decision making (risk aversion, loss aversion).
Both tasks are well-established in the computational psychiatry literature.
The specific symptom measures used could not be verified without the full paper.

__Open Science__: Information limited to the abstract — no PDF was available for review.
Open science practices could not be assessed.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 195 unmedicated adults (68.7% female, mean age 27.3, SD 8.9).
Recruited 2013–2019.
