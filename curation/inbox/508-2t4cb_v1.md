## Summary
This study examined whether interaction modality (typing vs. speech) affects self-disclosure and depression screening accuracy in AI-based tools, and whether socioeconomic status (SES) moderates prediction calibration. The researchers developed M2SurveyBot, a multimodal chatbot designed with clinical psychologists that administers semi-structured mental health interviews. In a controlled study with 455 U.S. adults recruited via Prolific and CloudResearch (after quality checks from 831 initial participants), participants were randomly assigned to text or audio interaction in their first session and received the alternative modality in a follow-up session after 3 days, with counterbalanced order.

For self-disclosure (RQ1), audio produced more words (M=753 vs. M=463) and faster speaking rates, but depth of self-disclosure did not differ significantly between modalities across LIWC, RoBERTa-based classifiers, or GPT-4o rubric scoring. For depression prediction (RQ2), typed text (RoBERTa embeddings, r=0.496) and spoken transcripts (r=0.445) achieved comparable performance, while acoustic features alone showed near-zero predictive validity (r=-0.023, p=.691). Combining transcript and acoustic features did not improve over transcript alone. GPT-4o zero-shot estimation yielded r=0.583 for typed and r=0.522 for spoken transcripts.

The most clinically significant finding concerned SES moderation (RQ3): predictions tracked depression severity more closely for lower-SES participants across all models. For typed text, Elastic Net on RoBERTa achieved r=0.51 for low SES versus r=0.28 for high SES. GPT-4o showed the same pattern with larger effects (typed low SES: r=0.66 vs. high SES: r=0.36). The SES x prediction interaction was significant (beta=-0.355, p=.005), with a flatter prediction-to-actual slope for high-SES participants, suggesting compressed predictions. This gap was not modulated by interaction modality and persisted across both supervised and LLM-based models.

## Clinical Insight
The SES calibration gap is the most important clinical finding. AI depression screening tools are more accurate for lower-SES individuals because higher-SES individuals appear to express distress differently, possibly through more subtle or socially managed language patterns. This means that current AI screening tools may systematically underdetect depression in higher-SES populations, a group that already faces barriers to mental health care due to stigma and expectation of self-reliance.

For clinicians using or considering AI-assisted screening tools, this study offers two key takeaways: first, spoken interaction via chatbot is a viable alternative to typed text and may be more accessible for populations with typing difficulties, without sacrificing screening accuracy. Second, any deployment of AI screening tools should account for SES-related calibration differences, potentially by using SES-stratified models or adjusting thresholds. The acoustic finding is also important: adding voice analysis to text-based screening provides no additional benefit for depression detection, suggesting resources should focus on improving text-based models rather than pursuing multimodal approaches.

## Relevant For
Digital mental health developers, clinicians integrating AI screening tools into practice, and researchers studying algorithmic fairness in mental health applications.
