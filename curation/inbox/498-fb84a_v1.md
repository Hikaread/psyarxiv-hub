## Summary
This study investigated how specific narcissistic traits and gelotophobia (fear of being laughed at) predict changes in depressive symptoms and negative affect during the first three months of group psychotherapy for personality difficulties. The sample included 48 patients (72.9% with avoidant PD, 75% with borderline PD, 37.5% with narcissistic PD) participating in psychodynamic group psychotherapy.

Narcissistic neuroticism (vulnerability, hypersensitivity to criticism, and shame-proneness) emerged as the strongest predictor of early treatment outcomes. Higher narcissistic neuroticism predicted significant increases in depressive symptoms (beta=-0.501, p=.005) and negative affect (beta=-0.571, p=.001) after three months of therapy, indicating an initial deterioration effect. This finding suggests that vulnerable narcissistic patients may experience an exacerbation of symptoms when entering the relational intensity of group therapy.

Gelotophobia was correlated with worsening depressive symptoms and negative affect but did not add significant predictive value beyond narcissistic neuroticism in regression models, suggesting that the fear of being laughed at operates through the same underlying vulnerability pathway. Other narcissistic facets (grandiosity, leadership/authority, self-absorption) did not significantly predict early outcomes. The results highlight that it is not narcissism per se, but specifically the vulnerable, neurotic dimension that poses a risk for early deterioration in group therapy settings.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have important implications for treatment planning. Patients with high narcissistic neuroticism may be at particular risk for symptom deterioration during the early phase of group psychotherapy. Clinicians should consider assessing narcissistic vulnerability at intake and preparing these patients for the potentially challenging interpersonal dynamics of group settings. Pre-group preparation, individual sessions alongside group therapy, or modified group compositions may help mitigate the deterioration risk.

The null finding for gelotophobia beyond narcissistic neuroticism simplifies clinical assessment: rather than needing to screen for multiple constructs, focusing on narcissistic vulnerability may capture the key risk factor. The high comorbidity of avoidant, borderline, and narcissistic PD in this sample reflects real-world clinical complexity where patients rarely present with a single personality disorder.

## Relevant For
Group psychotherapists, personality disorder specialists, and clinicians working in psychodynamic or interpersonal therapy settings with patients who have narcissistic features.

## Notes
Small sample (N=48) limits statistical power and generalizability. Short follow-up (3 months). Important clinical signal about vulnerable narcissism and early group therapy deterioration despite methodological limitations.
