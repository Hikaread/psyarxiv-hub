## Summary

This qualitative study examines intervention fidelity in digital behavior change interventions (DBCIs), specifically an ecological momentary intervention (EMI) targeting unhealthy snacking through habit degradation strategies. Using semi-structured interviews and template analysis, the study explores participant experiences across three fidelity dimensions: delivery (was the intervention delivered as intended), receipt (did participants understand and engage with it), and enactment (did participants actually use the strategies in daily life).

## Clinical Insight

As therapists increasingly recommend or integrate digital therapeutic tools into their practice, understanding intervention fidelity becomes essential. This study reveals the specific ways that digital interventions can fail to achieve their intended effects: participants may receive the intervention but not deeply engage with it, or may understand the strategies but struggle to enact them in real-world moments. Therapists can use these fidelity insights to better prepare clients for using digital tools, set realistic expectations, and monitor not just whether a client used an app but whether they engaged with it meaningfully.

## Relevant For

Therapists recommending digital mental health tools, clinicians working with habit-based interventions, therapists integrating technology into treatment, supervision of digital tool use.

## Notes

Single author limits perspective. Focus on habit degradation for snacking is specific but fidelity findings generalize broadly. Template analysis provides clear thematic structure.
