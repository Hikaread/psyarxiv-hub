## Summary

This secondary analysis of **N = 1,229** participants from the **ABCD Study** (aged 10–15 years) examined biological correlates of psychopathology using routine **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** parameters, HDL, total cholesterol, and ferritin alongside the **CBCL 6-18**. **Hierarchical clustering** of laboratory measures revealed three distinct clusters: (1) an **Inflammation** cluster (WBC, neutrophils, platelets, HDL inverse); (2) an **Anemia** cluster (hemoglobin, hematocrit, RBC); and (3) the CBCL psychopathology cluster (internalizing and externalizing sub-clusters).

An **OLS regression** controlling for ACS propensity score and age found that the **Total Problems** scale was independently associated with the **Inflammation Factor** (β = 0.081, p = .005, 95% CI [0.022, 0.140]), the **Anemia Factor** (β = -0.070, p = .018, 95% CI [-0.128, -0.013]), and **male sex** (β = 0.199, p = .001, 95% CI [0.086, 0.315]). **Neutrophilic inflammation** correlated more strongly with the **internalizing** dimension, while **monocyte percentage** correlated exclusively with **externalizing**. The absence of immature granulocytes favored **autoinflammation** over infection as the mechanism. Absolute **eosinophil count** correlated with **ADHD**, consistent with shared IgE-mediated pathways. The inverse relationship between inflammation and erythropoiesis markers was consistent with **Anemia of Chronic Disease**.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians, this study provides evidence that **routine blood tests**—specifically CBC with differential—can detect immune signatures associated with the overall **p-factor** of psychopathology in children and adolescents. The **neutrophil-predominant inflammation** linked to internalizing problems, combined with low HDL, aligns with growing evidence for **autoimmune/inflammatory pathways** in depression and anxiety. The association between eosinophils and ADHD adds to evidence for **allergic-immune pathways** in attentional difficulties. While these effect sizes are modest (β = 0.07–0.20), they represent **robust, independent associations** in a large population-based cohort. Clinicians might consider CBC results as part of a broader biopsychosocial assessment, particularly when children present with comorbid somatic complaints or treatment-resistant symptoms. The study supports the **Sigma-factor (Σ-factor) model** of psychopathology as multi-organ systemic derangement.

## Methodology Note

This is a quantitative_experimental paper (secondary data analysis).

__Statistical Power__: The large sample (N = 1,229) provides adequate power for detecting the modest effect sizes reported.
However, the moderate β values (0.07–0.20) explain small proportions of variance, consistent with the polygenic and multifactorial nature of psychopathology in the ABCD cohort.

__Preregistration__: Not mentioned for this specific analysis.

__Effect Sizes__: Inflammation Factor: β = 0.081, p = .005, 95% CI [0.022, 0.140].
Anemia Factor: β = -0.070, p = .018, 95% CI [-0.128, -0.013].
Male sex: β = 0.199, p = .001, 95% CI [0.086, 0.315].
Correlation matrices used Spearman ρ with 95% bootstrapped confidence intervals (10,000 rounds).
Internalizing-externalizing correlation: ρ = .57, 95% CI [.53, .61].

__Statistical Rigor__: **PCA** was used for dimension reduction to address multicollinearity before OLS regression.
Correlation matrices were ordered by **hierarchical clustering** (hclust) to display clustering patterns.
Bootstrapped CIs for Spearman correlations provide appropriate non-parametric inference.
The ACS Raked Propensity Score was included as a covariate to address sampling bias.
Race was deliberately excluded from models per current recommendations to reduce racial bias reinforcement.
9.2% of the sample exceeded clinical significance on internalizing and 4.5% on externalizing scales.

__Measurement Validity__: **CBCL 6-18** is a well-validated parent-report measure with good internal consistency in this sample (syndromic scales α ≈ .89; DSM-5 scales α ≈ .86; broadband scales α ≈ .73).
CBC parameters are objective laboratory measures with established clinical utility.
HDL and ferritin were included as additional biological markers.
A limitation is that blood tests were drawn at different time points (month 24, 36, or 48) for different participants, though age at test time was not a significant predictor.

__Open Science__: This is a secondary analysis of de-identified ABCD Study data obtained through NIMH Data Archive (NDA) under an approved Data Use Certification.
The ABCD Study itself has extensive open data resources.
No specific analysis code or materials are mentioned for this paper.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 1,229 from the ABCD Study, aged 10–15 years at measurement.
44.3% female, 54.6% male.
Wide racial and household income distribution.
The study population differed from the full ABCD sample on ACS Raked Propensity Score (p = .0001), which was controlled for.
22% of the sample was tested at month 24, 26.4% at month 36, 18% at month 48.
