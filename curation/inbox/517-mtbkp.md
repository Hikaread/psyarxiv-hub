## Summary
This study used **ecological momentary assessment (EMA)** to examine **affective reactivity** to **racial discrimination** and **general stress** among 493 African American adults (M age = 65.8, SD = 5.5, range 55-75; 72% female) from The Heart of Detroit Study. Participants completed prompts up to **four times daily** during a seven-day EMA protocol (average 20.3 of 28 prompts completed). **Residual dynamic structural equation modeling (RDSEM)** with Bayesian estimation was used to analyze concurrent and lagged associations.

As hypothesized (H1), momentary **general stress** was concurrently associated with elevated **negative affect (NA)** and decreased **positive affect (PA)**. Experiences of racial discrimination were also concurrently associated with elevated NA and lower PA in discrimination-only models. However, when both stressors were entered simultaneously in **combined models**, only general stress — not racial discrimination — independently predicted concurrent **affective reactivity** (H3 and H4 were not supported). Stress and discrimination were moderately correlated at the concurrent level.

A novel **cyclical pathway** emerged from cross-lagged analyses: elevated NA predicted higher intraindividual stress four hours later, and elevated PA predicted lower intraindividual stress four hours later. However, stress did **not prospectively predict** future NA or PA. This pattern was not observed for racial discrimination, which showed only concurrent associations. **Depressive symptoms** (CES-D) and **neuroticism** (BFI-2) were associated with higher mean general stress, higher mean NA, lower mean PA, and greater **intraindividual variability in PA** (depressive symptoms beta = 0.18; neuroticism beta = 0.22), but were not associated with affective reactivity to stress.

## Clinical Insight
The finding that **general stress subsumes** the affective impact of racial discrimination when both are modeled simultaneously challenges the assumption that racial discrimination has unique acute affective effects beyond general daily stressors in older African American adults. However, this may reflect methodological limitations (binary discrimination measure vs. continuous stress severity, low discrimination incidence) rather than a true absence of unique effects. The discovery of a **cyclical affect-stress pathway** — where NA amplifies future stress and PA buffers it — has direct implications for intervention. Rather than targeting stress reactivity alone, interventions that **sustain positive affect** may interrupt a vicious cycle that perpetuates chronic stress. The association of **depressive symptoms and neuroticism** with greater PA variability, but not with NA reactivity, suggests that trait-level vulnerability manifests in PA dysregulation rather than exaggerated NA responses, informing personalized treatment approaches for this population.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 493 for stress-only models provides adequate power for RDSEM.
Discrimination-only and combined models had substantially reduced sample sizes due to exclusion of participants lacking intraindividual variability.
The reduced samples limited the ability to test between-person heterogeneity in discrimination models.

__Preregistration__: Hypotheses and analytic plan were preregistered on OSF after data collection but before analyses.
This is a post-hoc preregistration, which is weaker than pre-data-collection registration.

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes are reported as standardized beta coefficients with 95% credible intervals.
Depressive symptoms showed a strong association with mean NA (beta = 0.51) and moderate association with mean stress (beta = 0.16).
Neuroticism showed strong association with mean NA (beta = 0.48) and stress inertia (beta = 0.49).

__Statistical Rigor__: RDSEM with Bayesian estimation is an appropriate and sophisticated approach for intensive longitudinal data.
The four-hour lag structure may have missed shorter affective responses in older adults.
Single-item measures for stress and discrimination limit construct validity.

__Measurement Validity__: NA and PA assessed with well-established multi-item scales showing strong reliability (Rkr = 0.98 for PA, 0.97 for NA).
CES-D (polychoric alpha = 0.84) and BFI-2 neuroticism (polychoric alpha = 0.81) demonstrated acceptable internal consistency.
Single-item measures for momentary racial discrimination and general stress are a significant limitation.

__Open Science__: Preregistration available on OSF.
Data available upon reasonable request (not fully open).

__Participant Transparency__: Well-described community sample of middle-aged to older African American adults from metropolitan Detroit.
Exclusion criteria (cardiovascular disease, HIV, neurodegenerative diseases, psychiatric disorders, cognitive impairment) are clearly stated.
EMA compliance is reported (average 20.3/28 prompts).
