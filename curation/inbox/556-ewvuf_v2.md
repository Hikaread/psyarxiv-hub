## Summary
Introduces the **Living SEB Skills Project**, a continuously updated research infrastructure for cumulative science on **social, emotional, and behavioral (SEB) skills** — defined as functional capacities distinct from personality traits that individuals can draw upon to build relationships, pursue goals, think creatively, and regulate emotions. Grounded in the framework by Soto et al. (2021), SEB skills are organized into five domains paralleling the **Big Five**: **Innovation skills** (linked to openness), **Self-management skills** (conscientiousness), **Social engagement skills** (extraversion), **Cooperation skills** (agreeableness), and **Emotional resilience skills** (emotional stability). The project integrates ongoing literature searches, an open repository of coded studies (364 publications identified, 30 meeting inclusion criteria, 26 samples in quantitative synthesis), and an interactive web application enabling customizable **meta-analyses**. Demonstrating the database's utility, **multilevel meta-analyses** tested the predictive validity of SEB domains for academic achievement, finding significant associations particularly for Self-management (r = 0.26) and Cooperation (r = 0.21). **Meta-analytic structural equation models** (MASEM) demonstrated incremental validity of SEB skills beyond personality traits: each SEB domain predicted academic achievement above and beyond the corresponding Big Five trait. Notably, **skills–traits mismatches** (e.g., high extraversion but low social engagement skills) predicted unique outcomes, suggesting that having the capacity (skill) without the typical tendency (trait) produces different real-world outcomes than trait-skill alignment.

## Clinical Insight
The SEB skills framework offers clinicians a complementary lens to traditional personality assessment that focuses on what patients **can do** rather than what they **typically do**. This distinction is clinically valuable: a patient with **social anxiety** may have low social engagement as a trait tendency but retain the underlying capacity for social interaction — a skill that can be activated and strengthened through therapy. The finding that **skills–traits mismatches** have unique predictive value suggests that assessing both dimensions can identify patients whose functional capacities exceed their typical behavioral patterns (suggesting good prognosis with targeted activation) versus those whose trait tendencies outstrip their actual skills (suggesting a need for skill-building interventions). For **DBT** skills training, the SEB framework provides a validated organizational structure for understanding which skill domains are being targeted (e.g., Emotional Resilience skills map onto distress tolerance and emotion regulation modules). The living review infrastructure ensures that evidence on SEB skills remains current, supporting evidence-based decisions about which skills to prioritize in treatment planning. The open-access interactive application allows clinicians and researchers to conduct customized meta-analyses without statistical expertise.

## Relevant For
- DBT and CBT clinicians incorporating skills training into treatment
- Clinical psychologists assessing functional capacity vs typical behavior
- Researchers studying social-emotional skill development and intervention
- Assessment developers working on competency-based clinical measures
