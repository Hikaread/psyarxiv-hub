## Summary
This study developed and validated a **subdomain-based logistic regression model** for the **Addenbrooke's Cognitive Examination-III (ACE-III)**, moving beyond traditional total-score cut-offs to leverage domain-specific cognitive profiles for **dementia risk estimation**. The development cohort comprised 431 patients with dementia (spanning **Alzheimer's disease**, **behavioural variant FTD**, **semantic variant PPA**, **nonfluent variant PPA**, **corticobasal syndrome**, **PSP**, and other subtypes) and 107 non-dementia controls from the FRONTIER Research Group, yielding 1,068 ACE-III assessments. The best model incorporated all five **cognitive subdomains** (attention, memory, fluency, language, visuospatial ability) and achieved **Tjur's R-squared = 0.819**, correctly classifying **91.95%** of cases at a 95% risk threshold — significantly outperforming total-score models (p < 0.05). Validation in an independent cohort from the **Sydney Memory and Ageing Study** (64 dementia, 273 controls) yielded an **AUC = 0.937** and **accuracy = 88.7%**. An interactive **online screening tool** was implemented and is publicly available.

## Clinical Insight
Traditional ACE-III interpretation relies on total-score thresholds (88/100; 82/100), which assume uniform decline and may miss diagnostically informative patterns. A patient scoring above 88 can still harbor significant **fluency deficits** suggestive of frontotemporal dementia, while a patient below 88 may not show clinically meaningful impairment in any domain. The subdomain model addresses this by computing a **personalised probability estimate** from the configuration of scores across attention, memory, fluency, language, and visuospatial ability. This approach is particularly valuable for **differentiating dementia subtypes** that present with distinct cognitive profiles — for instance, the prominence of **language deficits** in primary progressive aphasia versus **visuospatial impairment** in posterior cortical atrophy. For neuropsychologists and geriatricians, the publicly available online tool offers a practical way to supplement bedside screening with quantitative, profile-based risk estimation. The study's large, multi-subtype sample and external validation strengthen clinical confidence. A limitation is that the cohort was predominantly English-speaking with a minimum of six years of education, restricting applicability to linguistically and educationally diverse populations.
