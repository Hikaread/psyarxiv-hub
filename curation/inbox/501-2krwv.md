## Summary
This experimental study (N=796; 278 community members, 518 university students) used a **randomized block design** with **Linear Mixed Models** to examine how exposure to TikTok videos about **ME/CFS and Long COVID** influenced **illness perceptions**, **prejudice**, and **stigma**. Participants completed baseline measures then were randomly assigned to view up to four blocks of paired videos (one ME/CFS, one Long COVID per block), completing post-video measures after each. Exposure to TikTok content was generally associated with statistically significant **increases in perceived illness severity** for both conditions. Scores related to **illness prejudice and stigma typically decreased or remained comparable to baseline**, suggesting that personal narrative content on TikTok may reduce stigmatizing attitudes. The study addresses a critical gap: while previous research has examined TikTok health *content*, this is among the first to experimentally test how that content *shapes viewers' beliefs*. Both ME/CFS (affecting ~1% of the population) and Long COVID (~35%) are **medically invisible conditions** prone to dismissal, trivialization, and misdiagnosis, making public understanding particularly important for clinical outcomes.

## Clinical Insight
Clinicians working with ME/CFS and Long COVID patients should be aware that **social media exposure significantly shapes illness perceptions** — including perceptions of severity. Increased severity perceptions after viewing TikTok content could be either beneficial (validating patients' experiences) or harmful (amplifying nocebo effects or health anxiety), depending on the content and the viewer. The finding that stigma and prejudice **decreased** after exposure is encouraging and suggests that patient-generated TikTok content may serve as a **destigmatizing force** — particularly valuable for conditions where medical dismissal is common. Clinicians should consider asking patients about their social media consumption related to their condition, as it may influence their **illness identity, help-seeking behavior, and treatment expectations**. The study also highlights the **algorithmic amplification** risk: TikTok's engagement-based algorithm may create "echo chambers" that repeatedly expose users to similar content, potentially reinforcing either adaptive or maladaptive illness beliefs. For both conditions, where symptoms are non-specific and diagnosis relies on self-report, **public understanding directly affects clinical encounters** — patients who have encountered validating content may be better advocates for their own care.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper (randomized experimental design).

__Statistical Rigor__: Linear Mixed Models with pre/post contrasts within and between conditions are appropriate for the repeated-measures design.
Estimated marginal means were derived for effect estimation.
The study used four different video blocks, allowing some assessment of content variation effects.

__Measurement Validity__: The study used established illness perception constructs but does not specify standardized measures in the preprint.
Video content was curated by the researchers rather than algorithmically selected, which may not reflect typical TikTok viewing.
Participants could choose how many blocks to complete (up to 4), introducing self-selection bias.

__Participant Transparency__: 796 participants (278 community, 518 university students) from Aotearoa New Zealand.
Demographic breakdowns are reported in the study but not in the preprint abstract.
The mixed community/student sample improves generalizability but the New Zealand context may limit international applicability.

__Open Science__: Available as a preprint on OSF.
Raw data available from corresponding author upon request with a data use agreement (not fully open).
Funded by a University of Otago Research Grant.
