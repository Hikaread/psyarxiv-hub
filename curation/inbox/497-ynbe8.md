## Summary
This observational study examined the effects of **one-to-one Trauma Center Trauma-Sensitive Yoga (TCTSY)** on psychological symptoms and interoception over six months in 10 clients across four countries (8 women, mean age 44.8, 90% with PTSD diagnoses). TCTSY is a protocolized, evidence-based adjunctive trauma treatment guided by five core principles: **choice-making, effective action, present-moment experience, non-coercion, and shared authentic experience**. Using **linear mixed models** with multiple imputation for missing data, the study found statistically significant improvements over time in **anxiety** (F(21,97.81)=9.76, p<.001), **depression** (F(21,101.94)=8.45, p<.001), **posttraumatic stress symptoms** (F(21,114.74)=4.99, p<.001), and three interoceptive awareness domains: **attention regulation** (F=6.03, p<.001), **self-regulation** (F=4.51, p<.001), and **body listening** (F=7.44, p<.001). Symptom reductions showed initial decreases at early time points with continued improvement by post-study. **Sleep** and **substance use** (measured by CAGE-AID) did not change significantly. Measures included the **DASS-21** (3-item short forms for session surveys, alpha=.71-.83), **PCL-5** (alpha=.95), **MAIA-2** subscales (alpha=.75-.95), **B-PSQI** (alpha=.63), and **PC-PTSD-5** (alpha=.71). This is the first study to examine TCTSY in a 1-1 format; previous RCTs only examined group-based delivery.

## Clinical Insight
This study extends the TCTSY evidence base to **individual therapy contexts**, finding that the bottom-up, body-based approach produces significant improvements in PTSD, anxiety, and depression comparable to group-delivered TCTSY (where RCTs show large effect sizes, d=1.07-1.18). For clinicians, 1-1 TCTSY offers a **flexible treatment option** for clients who may not tolerate group settings — particularly relevant given that trauma survivors with negative emotions related to physiological sensations are more likely to discontinue **top-down cognitive trauma therapies** like CPT. The significant improvements in **interoceptive awareness** (attention regulation, self-regulation, body listening) are clinically meaningful because interoceptive deficits are a core feature of PTSD and dissociation. Clinicians should note that TCTSY's emphasis on **choice and non-coercion** directly counters the loss of agency central to trauma, making it particularly suitable for complex trauma survivors. The lack of significant change in sleep and substance use may reflect the small sample or the specific mechanisms of TCTSY, which primarily targets interoceptive awareness rather than behavioral sleep or substance use patterns.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper (observational longitudinal).

__Statistical Power__: The sample of N=10 is extremely small, limiting statistical power and generalizability.
The study acknowledges this limitation and used multiple imputation (5 datasets) and winsorization to handle missing data.
Effect sizes were not reported, making it difficult to compare with group-based TCTSY RCTs (d=1.07-1.18).

__Preregistration__: The protocol was preregistered with OSF, though the preregistration ID was masked in the manuscript.
Several deviations from protocol are transparently reported: ongoing enrollment, use of multiple imputation, and no between-group comparison.

__Statistical Rigor__: Linear mixed models with autoregressive covariance structures are appropriate for the repeated-measures design with unequal time points.
Five multiply imputed datasets were used with pooled estimates, appropriate for the 10% missing data rate.
No correction for the six primary outcome tests was reported.

__Measurement Validity__: Established measures were used (DASS-21, PCL-5, MAIA-2, B-PSQI, CAGE-AID, LEC-5).
Micro-longitudinal 3-item short forms were used for session surveys to reduce burden, which may reduce reliability.
B-PSQI had low internal consistency (alpha=.63) in this sample.

__Participant Transparency__: Sample was 80% women, predominantly White, from 4 countries (US, Australia, Greece, Israel).
90% had PTSD diagnoses; participants experienced an average of 5.60 trauma types.
The study was observational with no control group, limiting causal inference.

__Open Science__: Preregistered on OSF with transparent deviation reporting.
Deidentified data available upon request with community partner approval (not fully open due to community partnership agreements).
Published in the International Journal of Yoga Therapy (peer-reviewed).
