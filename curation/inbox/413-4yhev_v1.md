## Summary
This cross-sectional study applied **network analysis** to examine the item-level structure of the **Alcohol Use Disorder Identification Test (AUDIT)** and the interconnections among established AUD risk factors in a combined sample of N = 933 alcohol users. Two Gaussian Graphical Models were estimated using the **ggmModSelect** algorithm with EBIC model selection: an AUDIT item network (9 nodes, morning drinking excluded due to near-zero variance) and a broader risk factor network (11 nodes including **AUDIT-C**, **AUDIT-P**, **DSM-5 Cross-Cutting Symptom Measure**, **Impulsive Sensation Seeking Scale**, executive functioning, age of onset, craving, tobacco use, and coping/enhancement/social drinking motives via the **DMQ-R**). Networks were compared between adolescents (16-25 years, n = 421) and adults (35-65 years, n = 252) using Network Comparison Tests with 1,000 permutations and Holm-corrected edge comparisons. In the AUDIT network, **binge drinking** was the most central node (expected influence = 1.42) and served as the key bridge connecting consumption to use-related problems including loss of control, guilt, and memory loss. The risk factor network showed **AUDIT-C** as the most central node (strength = 1.42), with **enhancement motives** showing the highest expected influence (1.15), and a strong association between **DSM-5 cross-cutting symptoms and executive functioning** (0.47). Coping motives were specifically linked to AUDIT-P and mental health symptoms. Despite adolescents scoring significantly higher on alcohol use, use-related problems, impulsivity, and mental health symptoms (all p < .001), **no significant differences** in network structure, global strength, or centrality emerged between age groups (all p > .10). Centrality stability was acceptable to good (CS = 0.44-0.75 across networks).

## Clinical Insight
The finding that the **relational architecture** of alcohol use and AUD risk factors is stable across adolescence and adulthood, despite significant differences in severity, has direct implications for clinical assessment and intervention planning. Clinicians working with younger patients can use the same **network-informed conceptualization** — targeting binge drinking as the central bridge behaviour between consumption escalation and loss of control, while addressing enhancement motives as the highest expected influence point for driving consumption. The strong association between **coping motives, mental health symptoms, and use-related problems** reinforces the need for integrated treatment approaches that simultaneously address affect regulation and substance use, particularly since craving was connected to both consumption and coping motives across age groups. The slightly denser interconnectivity observed in adolescent networks (19/55 vs 14/55 edges) tentatively suggests that interventions targeting a single risk factor in younger patients may produce more cascading effects than in adults, though this did not reach statistical significance. The AUDIT item-level network also supports using individual AUDIT items rather than total scores alone for case formulation, as items like inability to stop, memory loss, and guilt formed a distinct loss-of-control cluster that was highly central but independent of pure consumption frequency.

## Methodology Note
__Statistical Power__: The sample of N = 933 (421 adolescents, 252 adults in comparison groups) provides reasonable power for network estimation, though the unbalanced groups and 260 excluded mid-range participants may have reduced sensitivity to detect age differences. No a priori power analysis was reported. __Effect Sizes__: Edge weights are reported as partial correlations with bootstrap CIs, and centrality stability coefficients (CS = 0.44-0.75) are provided, enabling assessment of estimate reliability. __Statistical Rigor__: The use of ggmModSelect with EBIC (γ = 0.5) rather than LASSO regularisation was appropriately motivated by violated sparsity assumptions. Spearman correlations were correctly chosen for ordinal AUDIT items. NCT comparisons used Holm correction for multiple edge tests. However, all measures were self-report, and the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference. __Measurement Validity__: All measures are well-established (AUDIT, DMQ-R, DSM-5 CCSM, ImpSS, Webexec, TLFB). The DSM-5 CCSM provides broad psychopathology screening rather than disorder-specific assessment, which may limit precision. __Open Science__: The preprint is open access; no mention of data, code, or preregistration availability. __Participant Transparency__: The sample was predominantly highly educated (87-88%), female (52-59%), and non-Dutch, recruited via Prolific, social media, and flyers — limiting generalisability beyond Western, educated, non-clinical alcohol users.
