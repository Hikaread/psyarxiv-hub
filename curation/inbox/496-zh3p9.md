## Summary
This cross-sectional study (N=261 asexual-spectrum individuals) provides the first test of **Hatzenbuehler's Integrative/Psychological Mediation Framework (IPMF)** in an asexual population, examining pathways from minority stressors to **psychological distress** (anxiety/depression). Using **PROCESS mediation analysis** with 5,000 percentile bootstraps, the study tested nine indirect pathways. Four were significant: outness predicted distress via **internalized acephobia** (ab=-.05, 95% CI [-.10, -.01]) and via **expressive suppression** (ab=-.05, 95% CI [-.09, -.01]); acephobic discrimination predicted distress via internalized acephobia (ab=.06, 95% CI [.02, .11]) and via expressive suppression (ab=.03, 95% CI [.005, .07]). All significant paths held in a combined **path analysis model** controlling for gender and age. Non-significant paths included rumination and cognitive reappraisal as mediators, though the authors note these may reflect insufficient power rather than true null effects. The study also introduces novel asexual-specific measures: the **Acephobic Harassment, Rejection and Discrimination Scale (AHRDS)** (alpha=.92), the **Internalized Acephobia Scale (IAS)**, and an outness-as-asexual measure.

## Clinical Insight
Clinicians working with asexual clients should be aware that **internalized acephobia** and **expressive suppression** are key mechanisms linking minority stress to psychological distress. The **DSM-5 exemption for asexuality** within Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder (added in 2013) reflects progress, but clinicians may still inadvertently **pathologize asexuality** without affirmative practice. The IPMF offers a structured framework for developing asexual-specific interventions targeting these mediators. Notably, outness had complex effects: while it associated with *lower* distress via reduced internalized acephobia, it also associated with *higher* distress via increased discrimination exposure — mirroring the **concealment dilemma** well-documented in LGB populations. Therapists should explore both the **protective value of identity disclosure** and the **risks of increased prejudice exposure** with each client individually.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper (cross-sectional mediation).

__Measurement Validity__: The study adapted established LGB minority stress measures for asexual populations, including the AHRDS (adapted from the HRDS, alpha=.92) and the IAS (adapted from the Internalized Homophobia Scale).
Novel asexual-specific measures showed acceptable reliability but require further validation.
Outness-as-asexual was reduced to two items (family, allosexual friends) due to high missingness (39.5% on the full scale), which limits the construct's representation.

__Statistical Rigor__: Mediation analyses used 5,000 bootstrap samples with 95% CIs, appropriate for testing indirect effects.
No multiple comparisons correction was reported for the nine tested indirect paths, inflating Type I error risk.
The cross-sectional design precludes causal inference, and the direction of mediation cannot be confirmed.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample was predominantly from the Global North (50% US, 15% UK, 8% Canada), limiting generalizability.
Asexual-spectrum labels were diverse (186 asexual, 27 gray-asexual, 30 demisexual), and 38% identified as transgender/non-binary.
Exclusion of non-identified asexual individuals (who may experience the same stigma without the label) may understate the impact of acephobia.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a preprint on OSF.
Measures are stated to be available at the lead author's website, though the URL was masked for review.
No data, analysis code, or preregistration is mentioned.
