## Summary

This transdiagnostic study examined physiological responses during threat anticipation across 163 participants with specific phobia, social anxiety disorder (SAD), OCD, and unaffected controls using the NPU-threat test. Startle reflex, skin conductance responses (SCR), and event-related potentials (N1, P3) were measured during predictable and unpredictable threat conditions. Patients with OCD showed enhanced autonomic responding (SCR) during unpredictable threat anticipation. Dimensionally, anxious arousal was associated with reduced SCR during unpredictable threat and lower P3 amplitudes. Intolerance of uncertainty modulated startle primarily in controls. A latent profile analysis identified three physiological response profiles; a threat-reactive profile (increased N1, SCR, and startle to unpredictable threat) was particularly clinically enriched, with higher severity and greater representation of SAD and OCD.

## Clinical Insight

This study advances the understanding of how different anxiety disorders share and diverge in their underlying threat physiology, with direct implications for case formulation and treatment planning. The finding that a specific multisystem threat-reactive profile — rather than any single physiological marker — characterizes the most clinically severe presentations supports a personalized approach to anxiety treatment. For therapists, this suggests that asking clients about their subjective experience of unpredictability and threat anticipation (e.g., "Do you feel on edge when you don't know what's coming next?") can provide clinically meaningful information that goes beyond diagnostic labels. The distinct physiological profile of OCD patients during unpredictable threat aligns with clinical observations that intolerance of uncertainty drives much of the suffering in OCD, and supports prioritizing interventions that target uncertainty tolerance, such as exposure-based approaches that deliberately introduce unpredictability. The finding that intolerance of uncertainty modulated startle primarily in controls, not patients, suggests that once a disorder has developed, the relationship between uncertainty and physiological reactivity may have become dysregulated in ways that standard psychoeducation alone cannot address.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with anxiety disorders and OCD, particularly those using transdiagnostic approaches or exposure-based therapies that target intolerance of uncertainty
