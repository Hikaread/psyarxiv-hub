## Summary
This study provides the first formal **psychometric validation of the Polish version of Leichsenring's Borderline Personality Inventory (BPI)**, a self-report measure grounded in **Kernberg's object relations theory** of personality pathology. Using a **gender- and age-matched community sample of 600 participants** (aged 18-36), the study examined internal structure via **confirmatory factor analysis (CFA)** with DWLS estimation, reliability using **Cronbach's alpha**, **McDonald's omega**, and **composite reliability**, measurement precision via **multidimensional IRT (2PL model)**, and **gender-related differential item functioning (DIF)** via the Mantel-Haenszel method. CFA supported the theoretical **four-factor model** (Identity Diffusion, Primitive Defences, Impaired Reality Testing, and Fear of Fusion) with good fit after minor modifications. Total scale reliability was excellent (omega = .89, CR = .84), with subscale alphas ranging from .63 (Fear of Fusion) to .82 (Cut-20). **mIRT analysis** revealed that the BPI provides **greater measurement precision at higher levels** of borderline personality organization — consistent with its intended use as a screening tool for more severe pathology. After **Holm correction**, no item showed statistically significant **gender-related DIF**, supporting the comparability of scores across women and men.

## Clinical Insight
The BPI offers clinicians a **theory-grounded, brief self-report screening tool** for assessing the severity of **borderline personality organization** — a broader construct than the BPD diagnosis that captures structural-level personality pathology including **identity diffusion**, **primitive defenses**, and **reality testing impairments**. The finding that the BPI is most precise at **higher levels of pathology** makes it particularly suitable for clinical settings where identifying more severe personality disturbances is the priority. The absence of **gender-related DIF** after correction supports the instrument's fairness across genders, addressing a common concern in personality disorder assessment. Clinicians using the **ICD-11 or DSM-5 Alternative Model** for personality disorders may find the BPI's four dimensions conceptually aligned with the emphasis on **personality functioning severity** and **self/interpersonal impairment**, though the study notes that direct equivalence with these diagnostic systems was not tested. The BPI should be used as a **complement to clinical interviews** (such as the STIPO-R) rather than a standalone diagnostic tool, consistent with best practices in multimethod personality assessment.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric study (quantitative_experimental).

__Statistical Power__: The final matched sample of N = 600 provides adequate power for CFA and DIF analyses.
The gender-matched design (300 women, 300 men) ensures sufficient sample size per group for DIF detection.
However, the community sample limits the number of participants with elevated scores, particularly on the Impaired Reality Testing subscale.

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes are not the primary focus of this psychometric study.
Fit indices (CFI, TLI, RMSEA, SRMR) are reported with conventional threshold criteria.
Inter-factor correlations (0.46 to 0.88) are reported, revealing substantial overlap between BPI dimensions.

__Statistical Rigor__: CFA used DWLS estimation, appropriate for dichotomous (true/false) item data.
Multiple comparisons correction (Holm method) was applied for DIF analyses, preventing Type I error inflation.
Both AVE and MSV were examined for convergent and discriminant validity, with transparent reporting of limitations.

__Measurement Validity__: The four-factor CFA model showed good fit, supporting the theoretical structure.
However, three of four factors had AVE values below 0.50, indicating limited convergent validity at the factor level.
No external criterion (e.g., clinical diagnosis, interview-based assessment) was used to establish criterion validity.
Test-retest reliability was not assessed, representing a significant gap for a screening instrument.

__Open Science__: The data are openly available on OSF.
The study materials and BPI version used are described in the methods section.
This is a positive example of open data practices in psychometric research.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample consists of young adults (aged 18-36, M = 23.8 years) from a community setting.
Data were pooled from seven independent studies collected between 2012 and 2022.
The sample is predominantly young, educated, and non-clinical, limiting generalizability to clinical populations.
Participants identifying as nonbinary or other genders (n = 9) were excluded due to insufficient group size.

## Notes
Community sample only — clinical validation is the critical next step. The high inter-factor correlations (up to .88) raise questions about the distinctness of the four BPI dimensions in practice.
