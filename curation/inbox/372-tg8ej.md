## Summary

This 17-year longitudinal study of 1,106 Mexican-origin adults examines whether modifiable Lancet Commission dementia risk factors (hypertension, depression symptoms, sleep problems, etc.) mediate the relationship between Big Five personality traits and midlife cognitive function. The research uses latent growth curve modeling to analyze personality trajectories and their association with cognitive outcomes, testing whether addressing modifiable risk factors could buffer the cognitive impact of personality-related vulnerabilities.

## Clinical Insight

This study is directly relevant for therapists working with midlife and older adult clients, particularly those concerned about cognitive decline. It identifies personality traits as early markers of cognitive risk and shows that modifiable factors like depression symptoms and sleep quality serve as mediators. For therapists, this means that treating depression, improving sleep hygiene, and managing cardiovascular risk factors in midlife clients may have downstream benefits for cognitive health. The findings support an integrative approach where mental health treatment is framed as part of a broader cognitive health strategy, giving therapists additional motivation-based language for engaging clients in lifestyle and psychological interventions.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with midlife and older adults, neuropsychologists, clinicians integrating lifestyle medicine with psychotherapy, therapists treating late-onset depression.

## Notes

Mexican-origin sample provides valuable diversity in dementia research. 17-year longitudinal design is a major strength. Personality-cognition link has implications for early identification of at-risk individuals.
