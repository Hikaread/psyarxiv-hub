## Summary

This study applied a **value-based decision-making (VBDM) framework** to resolve the longstanding disconnect between laboratory evidence that people drink to regulate stress and the failure of **ecological momentary assessment (EMA)** studies to detect within-person stress-drinking associations in daily life. **250 at-risk adult drinkers** (median **AUDIT** score = 9) completed a 14-day EMA protocol with two daily scheduled prompts plus optional **event-contingent** self-initiation following stressors, yielding **5,978 completed EMA assessments** (99.5% choice trial completion). At each prompt, participants self-reported affective and contextual states and completed 160 trials of a cognitive choice task pairing personalized alcoholic and non-alcoholic drinks. **Drift diffusion modeling** decomposed choice and response time data into decision parameters; **mixed-effects LASSO** and **random forest models** identified moderators of affect-choice associations. **Stressful events increased alcohol choice by approximately 10 percentage points** and selectively biased evidence accumulation toward alcohol. However, contextual factors (social setting, time of day, availability) gated whether stress translated into actual drinking behaviour, explaining why previous EMA studies with limited contextual assessment failed to detect the association.

## Clinical Insight

This study has significant implications for **addiction treatment** and **relapse prevention**. The resolution of the laboratory-EMA paradox — stress does bias alcohol choice, but context gates its expression — provides a more precise model for understanding **urge-drinking** pathways in daily life. For clinicians delivering **CBT for substance use** or **motivational interviewing (MI)**, the findings support helping clients identify not just their emotional triggers but the specific **contextual gatekeepers** that allow or prevent stress from translating into drinking. The **drift diffusion modeling** approach reveals that stress biases the **evidence accumulation process** toward alcohol rather than simply increasing drinking motivation — a distinction that maps onto the clinical observation that clients often describe drinking as the "only option that made sense" in the moment. This framing supports **cognitive restructuring** interventions that target the perceived inevitability of stress-drinking sequences. The **event-contingent EMA design** — allowing participants to self-initiate entries after stressors — is itself a clinical intervention, as it prompts **momentary awareness** of the stress-drinking connection. The AUDIT median of 9 indicates a sample at the hazardous drinking threshold, making findings directly relevant to clients in **early addiction treatment** or **brief interventions** in primary care.

## Relevant For
- Addiction therapists using CBT, MI, or relapse prevention approaches
- Clinicians treating hazardous or harmful alcohol use
- Researchers studying stress-substance use pathways using EMA
- Digital health developers creating just-in-time intervention systems for alcohol use

## Notes

PDF was not available; curation based on the full abstract description. The sample was recruited via Prolific (online), which may not represent treatment-seeking populations. The 14-day EMA protocol is relatively short for understanding stable patterns. The cognitive choice task is a novel methodological contribution that bridges laboratory precision with ecological validity. The use of drift diffusion modeling to decompose decision processes is methodologically sophisticated and provides richer data than simple choice outcomes. The contextual gating finding is the key clinical contribution.
