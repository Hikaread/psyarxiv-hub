## Summary
This **measurement-burst study** (N = 743 young adults aged 16–39, 70% female, four bursts over ~1.5 years, each with 21 days of up to 4 daily beeps) examined how **researcher degrees of freedom** in study design and analysis affect conclusions about the longitudinal link between **stress reactivity** and **self-reported health**. Eighteen **multilevel SEMs** were compared, varying three key decisions: (1) daily vs. **momentary stress reactivity**, (2) **stationary vs. time-varying** cross-lagged effects, and (3) different ways of controlling for **mean negative affect**. Across models, **reciprocal associations** between stress reactivity and health were found, and stress reactivity showed **incremental predictive validity** beyond mean negative affect levels. However, the pattern of results **differed substantively** when cross-lagged links were constrained to be stable vs. allowed to vary over time, demonstrating that analytical choices can alter clinical conclusions.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians and researchers interested in **stress-health pathways**, this study serves as a methodological caution with direct clinical implications. The core message is that whether you conclude "stress reactivity predicts worsening health" or "the relationship is more complex" may depend on **how you measure and model** stress reactivity, not just on the underlying phenomenon. For practitioners using **EMA or experience sampling** in clinical research, the finding that daily vs. momentary operationalizations yield different conclusions suggests that **measurement frequency matters** for clinical decision-making. The evidence for **reciprocal causation** (stress reactivity worsens health, and poor health amplifies stress reactivity) supports a **vicious cycle model** that clinicians can communicate to patients. The study also underscores that **mean affect levels and affective reactivity are distinct constructs** — a patient's average stress level does not fully capture their stress dynamics, and reactivity may be the more clinically relevant parameter for predicting health trajectories.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 743 with intensive longitudinal data (up to 4 beeps/day for 21 days across 4 bursts) provides exceptional statistical power for multilevel models.
The measurement-burst design with 4 waves allows robust examination of time-varying effects.
The large sample also enables comparison of 18 different model specifications.

__Preregistration__: The paper references a DFG grant (NE 2480/1-1) but no explicit OSF preregistration was mentioned.
The transparent comparison of 18 models serves a similar function to multiverse analysis.

__Effect Sizes__: Cross-lagged path coefficients were reported across models but no standardized effect sizes or CIs were extracted.
The key finding is qualitative: the **direction and significance** of paths changed depending on model specification.
Stress reactivity showed incremental predictive validity beyond mean negative affect, but effect magnitudes were not extracted.

__Statistical Rigor__: The comparison of 18 multilevel SEMs representing systematic variations in researcher degrees of freedom is methodologically excellent and functions as a **multiverse analysis**.
The measurement-burst design (4 waves over 1.5 years) is state-of-the-art for distinguishing within-person from between-person effects.
Limitations include limited **ethnic and socioeconomic diversity** (acknowledged by authors), which restricts generalizability.

__Measurement Validity__: Stress reactivity was operationalized as the within-person coupling of stressor occurrence and negative affect.
Multiple operationalizations (daily, momentary) were compared, which is a strength.
Self-reported health was used as the outcome, which captures subjective health but not objective health indicators.
A single EMA stressor item was used (common but limiting).

__Open Science__: **All data and research materials** are available at osf.io/nep7b.
**Analysis code** is available at osf.io/7sqkv.
This represents exemplary open science practices.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 743, aged 16–39, 70% female, from Germany.
Participants were young adults **transitioning out of upper secondary education**.
The authors acknowledge limited **ethnic and socioeconomic diversity**.
The sample is specific to a developmental transition period, which may limit generalizability to clinical or older populations.
