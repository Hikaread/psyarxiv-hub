## Summary
This UK-based **mixed-methods study** explored **sensory processing sensitivity (SPS)**, **decision-making**, and **self-regulation** in **highly sensitive persons (HSP)**. Using the revised **HSP Scale** (Pluess et al., 2024) and semi-structured interviews analyzed via **reflexive thematic analysis** (Braun and Clarke, 2022), 30 participants completed the scale (20 classified as HSP) and 16 were interviewed. Two distinct **HSP subtypes** emerged: **Type One** (80%, elevated on 1–2 sensitivity dimensions) and **Type Two** (20%, elevated on 3–4 dimensions, higher overall scores). Three themes were generated: (a) **Negative Experiences and SPS Changeability**, (b) **Factors acting as both facilitators and barriers** (seven factors identified: negative experiences, surrounding people, depth of processing, emotional states, physical overstimulation, time, and information), and (c) **Binary Tendency in Self-Regulation and Decision Control**. Self-regulation was primarily employed **pre- and post-decision** rather than during execution.

## Clinical Insight
With up to **50% of psychotherapy clients** estimated to be HSP (Aron, 2010), this study has direct clinical relevance. The identification of **two HSP subtypes** is particularly useful for case formulation: Type One clients may present with challenges in specific sensitivity domains (e.g., primarily emotional reactivity), while **Type Two clients** with broad, high sensitivity across multiple domains may experience more pervasive difficulties and require more comprehensive therapeutic adaptations. The seven factors influencing HSP decision-making provide a **concrete framework** for therapists to explore with HSP clients. The finding that self-regulation is used **before and after decisions but not during execution** suggests that HSP clients may struggle with **in-the-moment regulation** — therapists could focus on building **mid-decision checking strategies**. The study also questions the **HSP Scale's comprehensiveness** due to its lack of negative-experience items, cautioning clinicians against over-reliance on the scale for clinical assessment.

## Methodology Note
This is a mixed_methods-type paper.

__Statistical Rigor__: The quantitative component (HSP Scale, N = 30) is descriptive and appropriately presented as such.
The qualitative component used **reflexive thematic analysis** following Braun and Clarke (2022), which is the current gold standard.
A **deductive approach** guided by established SPS and decision-making frameworks was used, which is appropriate for theory-building.
The small interview sample (N = 16) is typical for in-depth qualitative work but limits transferability.

__Measurement Validity__: The revised **HSP Scale** (Pluess et al., 2024) was used, which is the most recent version.
The authors critically note the scale's **lack of negative-experience items** and limited capacity to identify heterogeneity among HSP — a significant validity concern.
The finding that HSP scores are **broadly continuous with no clear boundary** separating groups challenges the clinical utility of the scale's cut-off.

__Open Science__: The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv**.
The study has an OSF project page (osf.io/pdfwj/).
No interview transcripts or codebook were mentioned as available.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 30 completed the HSP Scale (17 female, 12 male, 1 unspecified).
16 participants were interviewed after scoring at or above the HSP threshold.
Recruitment was via social media and community groups, with only **5 psychology-trained individuals** included to reduce professional bias.
Participants ranged across age and ethnicity but most held **undergraduate or postgraduate qualifications**, limiting socioeconomic diversity.
