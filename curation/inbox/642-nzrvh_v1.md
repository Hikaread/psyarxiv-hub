## Summary

This paper presents the **Decision Regulation Inventory (DRI)**, a 24-item self-report instrument that measures decision regulation as a **temporally extended process** — integrating anticipation, real-time control, and post-decision reevaluation within a single framework. Grounded in dual-process theory and the distinction between **approach-related dysregulation under positive arousal** and **stress-related control loss under negative affect**, the DRI proposes five dimensions: **Empathic Deliberation** and **Principled Deliberation** (pre-decision), **Control Failure under Excitation** (CF-Excitation) and **Control Failure under Pressure** (CF-Pressure; during decision), and **Post-decision Reevaluation**.
In a sample of **557 French-speaking adults** (377 healthy reference, 180 with self-reported psychiatric/neurological diagnoses), **exploratory factor analysis** (parallel analysis + oblimin rotation) recovered the predicted five-factor structure. **Confirmatory factor analysis** in a split-half replication achieved acceptable fit (CFI/TLI weighted more heavily than RMSEA per WLSMV guidelines). **Configural and metric measurement invariance** across sex was supported. Convergent and divergent correlations with six external instrument families (REI, ERQ, BRIEF-A, SRIS, RRS-SF, SD4, SDT-14) broadly followed the predicted **nomological pattern**. In exploratory clinical contrasts, **CF-Pressure showed transdiagnostic elevation** across six of seven diagnostic categories (anxiety, neurodevelopmental, neurological, mood, stress-related, and sleep disorders), with FDR-corrected significance.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians, the DRI offers a **process-level assessment tool** that could bridge a significant gap in both assessment and case formulation. Existing instruments measure fragments of self-regulation — emotion regulation (ERQ, DERS), impulsivity (UPPS-P), executive function (BRIEF-A) — but none integrate these into a temporal framework spanning the full decision episode. In clinical practice, the DRI's five dimensions map directly onto common therapy presentations: clients who describe **impulsive decisions under excitement** (CF-Excitation; relevant to mania, addiction, compulsive buying) vs. those who **freeze or capitulate under stress** (CF-Pressure; relevant to depression, anxiety, trauma). The **two deliberation dimensions** (empathic vs. principled) could inform case formulation about whether a client's decision-making difficulties stem from **over-empathising** (difficulty prioritising self in interpersonal contexts) or from **rigid rule-following** (inflexible adherence to internalised principles).
The finding that **CF-Pressure is transdiagnostically elevated** is clinically important: it suggests that stress-induced control failure is a shared mechanism across conditions, potentially making it a valuable **treatment target** for transdiagnostic interventions like the Unified Protocol or dialectical behaviour therapy skills. However, the authors appropriately note that discriminant validity against **negative affectivity** remains to be tested — the transdiagnostic signal may partially reflect general distress rather than specific regulatory dysfunction.

## Methodology Note

Paper type: psychometric.

__Statistical Power__: N = 557 total (377 healthy reference, 180 with diagnoses).
EFA used Half A (n = 188), CFA used Half B (n = 189).
Clinical contrasts: diagnostic categories ranged from n = 12 (eating disorders) to n = 65 (anxiety disorders).
The small eating disorder subsample limits the reliability of that specific contrast.

__Preregistration__: Not stated in the available text.

__Effect Sizes__: Convergent/divergent correlations were reported as age-partial Spearman coefficients.
FDR-corrected group comparisons for clinical contrasts were reported.
Clinical group differences for CF-Pressure were significant across 6/7 categories but specific effect sizes for each comparison are detailed in the full paper.

__Statistical Rigor__: The split-half EFA/CFA replication design is methodologically strong for initial validation.
Parallel analysis was used to determine factor count rather than arbitrary criteria.
WLSMV estimation with ordinal data is appropriate for the 6-point Likert scale.
The authors acknowledge that item reduction was performed on the full healthy sample (not blind to Half B), so the CFA tests structural replicability rather than out-of-sample item selection.
Model fit was evaluated with appropriate thresholds, weighting CFI/TLI over RMSEA for WLSMV.

__Measurement Validity__: The 24-item DRI demonstrates a clear five-dimensional structure consistent with the theoretical framework.
Reliability was estimated as omega total from the CFA model (specific values in the full paper).
Nomological validation against six external instrument families broadly conformed to predictions.
Configural and metric invariance across sex was supported; scalar invariance was deferred.
Cross-country invariance (Canada/France) was supported but noted as preliminary.

__Open Science__: The full 24-item DRI (French and English versions) is provided in supplementary materials.
Ethics approval from Laval University (no. 2025-072).
No mention of data sharing or OSF project page.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 557 French-speaking adults (age 18–86, M = 37.0, SD = 15.0).
377 healthy reference (no diagnosis), 180 with self-reported psychiatric/neurological diagnoses.
Diagnostic categories were not mutually exclusive (28.9% reported multiple diagnoses).
Participants from Canada, France, Switzerland, Belgium, and other French-speaking regions.
The self-reported (non-clinically verified) diagnostic status is a limitation for clinical validation.
