## Summary

Two studies (total **N = 645**, recruited via Prolific, UK-based) investigated whether public perceptions of homeless men and women differ in terms of **stereotypes** (warmth and competence per the **Stereotype Content Model**), **threat appraisals** (violence, pathogen, and economic), and attributed characteristics. In Study 1 (**N = 291**), 92% of participants pictured a typical homeless person as a man. The most common associations for both sexes were **alcohol addiction** (75%), **drug addiction** (66%), and **mental illness** (54%) — rates substantially higher than actual UK prevalence estimates of 5-7% for substance dependency. Study 2 (**N = 354**) used random assignment to homeless-man or homeless-woman conditions. Contrary to hypotheses derived from gender stereotype literature, homeless men were rated as both **warmer** (d = 0.36, p < .001) and **more competent** (d = 0.24, p = .024) than homeless women. **Economic threat** partially mediated warmth ratings and fully mediated competence ratings, with homeless women perceived as a greater **burden on the economy** (d = -0.28, p = .008). No gender differences emerged for violence threat, pathogen threat, or emotional reactions (anger, fear, disgust, compassion). Domestic violence was associated with homeless women far more than men (66% vs 19%, chi-squared = 80.1, p < .001).

## Clinical Insight

These findings carry important implications for **clinical work with homeless populations** and for **stigma reduction** in mental health settings. The finding that homeless women are perceived as **less warm, less competent, and more economically threatening** than homeless men suggests that women experiencing homelessness face a compounded layer of **gendered stigmatization** that may impede access to support and housing services. Clinicians working in outreach, crisis intervention, or social work should be aware that homeless women may encounter unique barriers rooted in these biased perceptions, including being viewed as less deserving of help. The strong public association of homelessness with **substance addiction and mental illness** — vastly overestimating actual prevalence — perpetuates harmful stereotypes that can affect how homeless individuals are treated in healthcare encounters. For therapists, these findings underscore the importance of examining their own implicit biases regarding homeless clients and recognizing that **system justification theory** may drive harsher appraisals of homeless women whose circumstances are perceived as violating gendered norms of behavior. The discrepancy between public perception (92% male imagery) and the reality that women now constitute a growing share of the homeless population in the UK also highlights the phenomenon of **hidden homelessness** — a form of under-detection that clinical screening practices should address.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians working with homeless or housing-insecure populations
- Social workers and case managers in community mental health
- Outreach and crisis intervention teams
- Psychologists studying stigma, stereotyping, and social cognition
- Professionals involved in housing-first or supported housing programs

## Notes

Master's thesis by single author (Doroty Donzello) from VU Amsterdam, supervised by Joshua M. Tybur. Study 1 was limited by a highly unbalanced sample (only 23 participants imagined a homeless woman), which Study 2 addressed through random assignment. No significant gender differences in emotional reactions (anger, fear, disgust, compassion) were found. Mediation analyses for warmth were only partial, suggesting additional unexplored mechanisms drive the lower warmth ratings for homeless women.
