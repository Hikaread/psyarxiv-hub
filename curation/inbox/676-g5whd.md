## Summary
This study proposes a **brain-based measure of emotional introspection** — the trial-wise correlation between a **neural signature of negative affect** (the Picture Induced Negative Emotion or **PINE** brain signature) and self-reported affect ratings during an emotion rating task. In a within-subject crossover design (N = 25), participants completed the task before and after two 2-week meditation blocks (**body scan** vs. **self-enquiry meditation**). The neural-self-report correlation was **strong (r = 0.58)** and showed **good test-retest reliability (r = 0.64)** across sessions regardless of intervention order. However, the measure was **not differentially affected** by body scan versus self-enquiry meditation, and the overall **emotion introspection score did not change** with either intervention. An exploratory analysis found some evidence that the neural measure correlated with self-reported introspection ability.

## Clinical Insight
The development of a **neural metric for emotional introspection** addresses a key measurement gap: current assessments rely on questionnaires vulnerable to self-report bias. For therapists, this is relevant because **emotional introspection** is theorized as a mechanism of change across multiple modalities including **psychotherapy** and **mindfulness-based interventions**. The finding that the measure is **stable over time** (r = 0.64) suggests it captures a trait-like capacity rather than a state. However, the null finding regarding meditation training is important — it suggests that **brief (2-week) meditation interventions may not alter the neural substrates of emotional introspection**, even when subjective reports suggest benefit. Clinicians should be cautious about interpreting improved self-reported emotional awareness after short mindfulness courses as reflecting genuine changes in interoceptive accuracy. This measure could eventually serve as a **biomarker** for tracking therapeutic progress in treatments targeting emotional awareness.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample of **N = 25** is very small for a within-subject fMRI design, limiting statistical power particularly for the meditation comparison.
The strong neural-self-report correlation (r = 0.58) provides evidence for the measure's validity despite the small N.
The study was likely underpowered to detect small training effects, which may explain the null meditation finding.

__Preregistration__: The study is described as having pre-registered within-subject studies.
The focus on null findings (no differential meditation effect) suggests adherence to preregistered hypotheses rather than post-hoc fishing.

__Effect Sizes__: The primary metric was the **correlation between neural and self-reported affect** (r = 0.58).
**Test-retest reliability** was r = 0.64.
No CIs were reported for these correlations in the available abstract.

__Statistical Rigor__: The crossover design with counterbalancing is methodologically sound for controlling order effects.
The meditation comparison was appropriately framed as null, but the small sample limits confidence in this conclusion.
An exploratory analysis of the relationship with self-reported introspection was clearly labeled as such.

__Measurement Validity__: The **PINE brain signature** is a previously established and validated neural measure of negative affect.
Applying it as a trial-wise measure correlated with self-report to create an introspection index is novel but methodologically reasonable.
The absence of a validated behavioral measure of emotional introspection as a convergent validity benchmark is a limitation.

__Open Science__: The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv**.
The PINE brain signature is a previously published and open tool.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 25 participants.
No demographic details were available in the abstract.
The sample size is a significant limitation for generalizability.
