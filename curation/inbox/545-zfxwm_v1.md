## Summary
The **FEARFALL study** (N = 160 community-dwelling older adults aged 70+, randomized to exercise + CBT intervention or sham control) investigated long-term effects of a 4-month **multi-component program** on concerns about falling (CaF), stress physiology, and inflammation. Using the **Falls Efficacy Scale-International (FES-I)**, FES-I Avoidance Behavior (FES-IAB), and Updated Perceived Control over Falling (UP-CoF), CaF decreased significantly over time (FES-I estimate = 3.85, p < .001; FES-IAB estimate = 2.12, p = .001; UP-CoF estimate = -1.68, p < .001) but with **no between-group differences** — both intervention and sham control improved equally. Total **diurnal cortisol output** decreased (estimate = 0.66, p = .017) and **alpha-amylase** increased (estimate = -10.07, p = .013). Inflammatory markers showed a mixed pattern: **IL-6** (p = .002) and **IL-1beta** (p = .004) increased while **TNF-alpha** decreased (p < .001); CRP and IL-10 remained stable. Results were robust to both per-protocol and intention-to-treat analyses.

## Clinical Insight
The most striking clinical finding is that both the **active intervention** (exercise + CBT based on "A Matter of Balance") and the **sham control** produced equivalent reductions in fall-related concerns, anxiety, and perceived stress. This suggests substantial **non-specific treatment effects** — possibly including the therapeutic benefit of structured social contact, attention from research staff, and positive expectancy. For clinicians, this underscores that the mechanism of improvement in fall anxiety interventions may not be the specific CBT or exercise components but rather the **broader therapeutic context**. The physiological changes (cortisol decrease, amylase increase) and the mixed inflammatory picture complicate simple stress-reduction interpretations. Clinicians should be cautious about attributing treatment gains to specific therapeutic components in this population and may consider that **structured group activities with social engagement** could achieve similar outcomes to more resource-intensive multi-component programs.

## Relevant For
- Geriatric clinicians and physical therapists working with fall anxiety
- CBT practitioners in older adult populations
- Researchers studying non-specific treatment effects
- Those interested in psychoneuroimmunology in aging

## Notes
The sham control group received structured attention, complicating interpretation of the null between-group finding. The sample was relatively high-functioning (mean age ~80, mostly community-dwelling). Inflammatory marker changes are difficult to interpret directionally.
