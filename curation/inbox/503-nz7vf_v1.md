## Summary
This study examined whether emotion regulation strategies can be detected in the content and dynamics of spontaneous thought, using a Think-Aloud paradigm across two studies. In Study 1, participants (N=233) completed nearly 5,000 Think-Aloud sessions over three weeks, including baseline sessions and daily 5-minute sessions, with a subset preceded by negative mood induction. After each session, participants reported their use of emotion regulation strategies, enabling the training of logistic regression models to identify linguistic signatures of strategy use from patterns of thought content and thought dynamics. In Study 2, an independent sample (N=780) completed a single 10-minute Think-Aloud session following positive or negative mood induction to test generalizability.

Models successfully identified six distinct emotion regulation strategies from spontaneous thought patterns: acceptance, rumination, savoring, expressive amplification, reappraisal, and distraction. The strongest features aligned with theoretical expectations: rumination was characterized by negative, repetitive thought patterns with constrained dynamics; reappraisal was associated with self-referential content and fewer positive-to-negative transitions; savoring involved positive, present-focused content with expansive thought transitions. Individual differences in strategy expression predicted both momentary emotional changes and trait-level mental health outcomes. Acceptance, reappraisal, and savoring were associated with greater emotional improvement following the task and lower state and trait anxiety, whereas rumination showed the opposite pattern. Crucially, these associations held in the independent Study 2 sample and in held-out data from Study 1, demonstrating generalizability.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides a novel methodological advance: rather than relying on retrospective self-report (which is vulnerable to bias and limited insight), emotion regulation strategies can be identified from the natural flow of a person's thoughts. This has significant implications for both assessment and treatment. Clinicians could use Think-Aloud or text-based analysis to identify a patient's habitual regulation strategies in a more ecologically valid way than questionnaires like the ERQ, potentially revealing patterns that self-report misses.

The finding that rumination in spontaneous thought predicts worse anxiety outcomes, while acceptance and reappraisal predict improvement, reinforces the clinical importance of helping patients shift away from repetitive negative thinking. However, the study also reveals that these strategies operate continuously in everyday cognition, not just during deliberate regulation attempts. This suggests that therapeutic approaches targeting habitual thought patterns (like CBT, mindfulness-based therapies, and behavioral activation) may work in part by reshaping the ongoing regulatory dynamics of spontaneous thought, not just by teaching deliberate strategies.

## Relevant For
Researchers studying emotion regulation, clinicians interested in novel assessment methods for regulation strategies, and those working with anxiety disorders where regulation patterns are central to pathology.

## Notes
Innovative use of computational linguistics to detect regulation in natural thought. Two large samples with independent validation. From Princeton (Tamir lab). Long-term clinical applications could include passive monitoring of regulation through text/chat data.
