## Summary

This protocol describes a **qualitative systematic review** employing **thematic synthesis** to consolidate qualitative evidence on how mental distress and wellbeing are conceptualised and responded to across **African contexts**, interpreted through a **decolonial lens**. The review addresses a significant gap: while diverse qualitative studies across Africa have documented culturally grounded understandings of mental health, this evidence remains fragmented and has never been synthesised using an explicitly decolonial interpretive framework.

The review will follow established guidance including **PRISMA-P**, **ENTREQ**, **PRISMA-S**, and the **JBI Manual for Evidence Synthesis**. Eligibility is guided by the **SPIDER framework** (Sample, Phenomenon of Interest, Design, Evaluation, Research type). The review will include primary qualitative studies and mixed-methods studies with extractable qualitative findings conducted in African settings, searching across **11 databases** including PubMed, PsycINFO, Scopus, CINAHL, Africa-Wide Information, and African Journals Online (AJOL), plus grey literature and supplementary sources.

Quality appraisal will use the **Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) Qualitative Checklist**, and confidence in findings will be assessed using **GRADE-CERQual**. The synthesis follows **Thomas and Harden's three-stage thematic synthesis**: line-by-line coding, descriptive theme development, and generation of higher-order analytical themes. The interpretive framework draws on decolonial scholarship (Quijano, Mignolo, Santos, Ndlovu-Gatsheni) and **Indigenous knowledge systems** to critically examine how coloniality, power, and epistemic justice shape mental health conceptualisations. The review asks how African knowledge systems converge with, diverge from, or complement dominant **biomedical paradigms**.

## Methodology Note

__Paper Type__: qualitative (protocol for qualitative systematic review)

__Statistical Power__: Not applicable. This is a review protocol, not an empirical study. No primary data collection or statistical analysis is planned.

__Preregistration__: Adequate. The protocol was prospectively registered on OSF before commencement of study selection, which is a methodological strength for a systematic review protocol.

__Effect Sizes__: Not applicable. Qualitative synthesis does not produce effect sizes; findings will be assessed for confidence using GRADE-CERQual.

__Statistical Rigor__: Not applicable. The protocol describes a rigorous qualitative methodology following multiple established reporting guidelines (PRISMA-P, ENTREQ, PRISMA-S). The use of SPIDER rather than PICO is appropriate for qualitative evidence synthesis. A limitation acknowledged by the authors is restriction to English-language studies, which may omit relevant evidence published in other languages.

__Measurement Validity__: Not applicable for a protocol. However, the planned use of CASP for quality appraisal and GRADE-CERQual for confidence assessment represents best practice in qualitative evidence synthesis.

__Open Science__: Adequate. Published as an OSF preprint with prospective registration. The protocol itself promotes transparency in planned methods.

__Participant Transparency__: Not applicable at the protocol stage. The review will include diverse participants across African contexts (community members, people with lived experience, traditional healers, healthcare professionals, policymakers). Heterogeneity across included studies is anticipated as a limitation.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working in **multicultural settings** or with **African diaspora populations**, this upcoming review has direct relevance. The decolonial lens is not merely academic — it addresses a practical clinical reality: many patients hold conceptualisations of distress that blend **biomedical, spiritual, communal, and moral frameworks**, yet clinical training predominantly equips therapists to work within a single biomedical paradigm. The review's focus on how African knowledge systems "converge with, diverge from, or complement" biomedical approaches directly mirrors the **cultural formulation** process in clinical practice. Understanding that a patient's explanatory model may involve spiritual causation, communal responsibility, or moral violation — rather than purely individual pathology — is essential for **therapeutic alliance**, **treatment adherence**, and **culturally responsive care**. Clinicians working in global mental health, **cross-cultural therapy**, or with refugee and migrant populations should watch for the completed review, which may provide an evidence base for integrating non-biomedical understandings into assessment and treatment planning.
