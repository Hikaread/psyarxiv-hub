## Summary
This study adapted and validated the Italian version of the Cardiac Distress Inventory (CDI) in 293 patients who experienced a cardiac event (myocardial infarction, bypass surgery, valve surgery, or arrhythmia) within the previous 12 months, recruited from two Italian centers (Bologna and San Donato Milanese). The CDI is a 55-item instrument developed by Jackson et al. (2022) that comprehensively assesses the cardiac distress construct: a persistent negative emotional state involving multiple psychosocial domains that challenges coping capacity following cardiac events. The study followed a two-phase design: translation/cross-cultural adaptation with preliminary validation, followed by comprehensive psychometric testing.

The translation followed established guidelines (Beaton et al., 2000) with forward translation, back-translation, face validity evaluation with five patients, and content validity assessment by 11 cardiac healthcare professionals (CVI-S/Ave = .72, considered suboptimal). Exploratory factor analysis (principal axis factoring with oblimin rotation) revealed a five-factor structure explaining 37.05% of variance, comprising Role Change and Adaptation to Disease, Social Isolation, Cognitive Challenges, Emotional Overwhelm, and Doctor-Patient Communication. After removing 11 items for insufficient loadings and 2 for reliability issues, the final 42-item version showed good internal consistency across factors (omega = .746 to .879). Criterion validity was supported through correlations with Emotion Thermometers and the Kessler-6. ROC analysis demonstrated good discriminative accuracy (AUC = .83) with an optimal cut-off score of 26, yielding sensitivity of 81% and specificity of 74% for identifying clinically significant distress.

## Clinical Insight
The Italian CDI fills a significant gap in cardiovascular psychology assessment in Italy, where current psychological evaluation of cardiac patients is fragmented and relies on non-specific instruments like the SCL-90-R or BDI-II. The 2025 European Society of Cardiology Clinical Consensus Statement now recommends cardiac distress screening as part of routine care for cardiovascular patients, making validated instruments like the Italian CDI timely and clinically relevant.

The five-factor structure provides clinically meaningful domains that can guide targeted interventions: clinicians can identify whether a patient's distress is primarily driven by role changes, social isolation, cognitive challenges, emotional overwhelm, or communication difficulties with their medical team. The cut-off of 26 provides a practical threshold for identifying patients who may benefit from psychological intervention. However, the suboptimal content validity (CVI-S/Ave = .72) suggests some items may not fully capture the Italian clinical context, and the original eight-factor structure was not replicated.

## Relevant For
Health psychologists working in cardiology, cardiac rehabilitation teams, and researchers studying psychological responses to acute cardiac events in Italian or cross-cultural contexts.

## Notes
Good psychometric foundation for the Italian CDI. Five-factor structure differs from original eight-factor model, suggesting cultural differences in how cardiac distress is experienced. Suboptimal content validity is a noted limitation.
