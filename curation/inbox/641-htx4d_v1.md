## Summary

This **cross-sectional study** investigates whether **Non-Suicidal Self-Injury (NSSI)** differs between women with **Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)** (n = 113; WPCOS) and women without PCOS (n = 305; WoPCOS). PCOS is an endocrine condition associated with physical (acne, hair loss, weight gain) and mental health complications (depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation), which combined with **atypical emotional regulation** may increase NSSI risk. Participants completed demographic questions and a measure of NSSI characteristics (type, onset age, duration, severity).
Comparative analyses revealed that WPCOS women reported a **greater propensity to cut and scratch** compared to WoPCOS women, but did not differ on other NSSI types. Among women who engaged in NSSI, PCOS status neither impacted **onset age, duration, nor severity** of self-injury. A **cluster analysis** identified three distinct typologies of NSSI engagement: **"Scar-deriving"** (engaging in NSSI for the resulting scars), **"Nil-to-low engagers"**, and **"Intensive engagers"** (high frequency and severity across multiple NSSI methods).

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with women who have PCOS or who present with NSSI, this study has several clinically relevant findings. The identification of a **"Scar-deriving" cluster** is particularly noteworthy — it suggests a distinct NSSI subtype where self-injury serves an identity or self-expression function rather than (or in addition to) emotion regulation. Clinicians should assess not only *why* clients engage in NSSI but also *what function the scars themselves serve*, as this may guide different intervention approaches (e.g., alternative self-expression strategies for the scar-deriving group vs. emotion regulation skills for others). The finding that PCOS is associated with **cutting and scratching specifically** (but not other NSSI forms) may relate to the **visible physical changes** of PCOS (acne, hair loss) that alter body image and skin appearance, potentially making skin-directed self-injury more salient for this population.
The study underscores the need for **integrated care** that addresses both the endocrine and psychological aspects of PCOS. Mental health screening for NSSI should be routine in PCOS clinics, and conversely, clinicians working with women who self-injure should assess for undiagnosed PCOS. The authors advocate for **international-scale education** on NSSI and emotion regulation for healthcare providers working with PCOS patients.

## Methodology Note

Paper type: quantitative_experimental.

__Statistical Power__: Total N = 418 (113 WPCOS, 305 WoPCOS).
This provides adequate power for the comparative analyses and cluster analysis.
However, the cross-sectional design limits causal inference about whether PCOS contributes to NSSI onset.

__Preregistration__: Not stated in the abstract.

__Effect Sizes__: The abstract reports comparative results (greater propensity to cut/scratch in WPCOS) but does not provide specific effect sizes, odds ratios, or confidence intervals in the available text.
No PDF was available for full-text review.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of **cluster analysis** to identify NSSI typologies is an appropriate person-centred approach that goes beyond simple group comparisons.
The cross-sectional design is a significant limitation for establishing directionality.
No PDF was available to assess control variables, confounders, or sensitivity analyses.

__Measurement Validity__: A dedicated NSSI measure was used to assess type, onset age, duration, and severity.
The specific instrument could not be verified without the full paper.
PCOS status was based on self-report, which may introduce misclassification bias compared to clinical diagnosis.

__Open Science__: Information limited to the abstract — no PDF was available for review.
Open science practices could not be assessed.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 418 women (113 with self-reported PCOS, 305 without).
Demographic details and recruitment methods are not available from the abstract alone.
The self-reported PCOS status is a limitation — clinical verification would strengthen the diagnostic classification.
