## Summary
This study (N = 160, data collected 2023-2024) investigated how listeners' engagement behaviors influence speakers' emotions during dyadic emotional self-disclosure, comparing face-to-face and video-mediated interactions with full attention versus attentional distraction. Engagement behaviors (nodding, gaze, smiling) and speakers' autonomic arousal were tracked in real time. Attentional disengagement substantially reduced nodding and smiling while increasing gaze diversion. At the whole-encounter level, listeners' nodding during disengagement reduced speakers' autonomic arousal. At the moment-to-moment level, nodding, gaze diversion, and smiling all transiently increased speakers' arousal. The gaze-arousal link was weaker in video calls, and the smiling-arousal link was stronger under full attention. The findings demonstrate that specific listener behaviors drive moment-to-moment fluctuations in speakers' emotional states, and these dynamics are sensitive to both attentional disruptions and technology-mediated communication.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have direct implications for psychotherapy, where emotional self-disclosure and listener (therapist) engagement are central. The study demonstrates that therapist nonverbal behaviors — nodding, gaze, and smiling — are not merely social niceties but actively regulate clients' autonomic arousal moment by moment. For telehealth practitioners, the weakened gaze-arousal link in video calls suggests that the therapeutic impact of eye contact is attenuated remotely, potentially affecting emotional processing during sessions. Clinicians conducting teletherapy should be aware that compensatory behaviors like more explicit verbal engagement or intentional nodding may be needed to maintain the emotional dynamics present in face-to-face work. The finding that smiling has a stronger arousal effect under full attention underscores the importance of sustained therapist presence — even brief attentional lapses may diminish the impact of positive facial feedback on client emotional processing.

## Relevant For
Psychotherapists working with emotional self-disclosure; telehealth practitioners; supervisors training therapists in nonverbal communication; researchers studying therapeutic process and the therapeutic alliance; those investigating the impact of teletherapy on treatment effectiveness.

## Notes
Used a non-clinical sample in a laboratory self-disclosure task rather than actual therapy sessions. Autonomic arousal (not subjective emotional experience) was the primary outcome. The self-disclosure task may not fully capture the dynamics of ongoing therapeutic relationships.
