## Summary
This study investigated individual differences in **frustration triggers** and their associations with psychopathology symptoms in **536 young adults** (ages 18-25; 61.7% female; 72.2% White; 23.1% Hispanic/Latino).
Participants completed symptom rating scales measuring **anxiety, depression, irritability, anger, oppositionality**, and **inattention/hyperactivity**, and described three situations that cause them frustration.
**Latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA)** — a natural language processing method — identified four latent topics of frustration triggers:
- **"Other People"** (48.6% of triggers, reported by 79.1% of participants)
- **"Effort and Performance"** (27.2%, 54.5%)
- **"Unmet Expectations"** (15.2%, 33.0%)
- **"Friends and Plans"** (9.0%, 21.3%)
**"Other People"** triggers predicted **irritability** and **oppositionality** (externalizing spectrum).
**"Unmet Expectations"** triggers predicted **anxiety** and **depression** (internalizing spectrum).
Unexpectedly, **"Effort and Performance"** triggers predicted **lower** levels of irritability.
No significant associations were found for **anger**, **inattention/hyperactivity**, or the **"Friends and Plans"** topic.
The findings support a **transdiagnostic framework** where external frustrations (other people) map onto externalizing symptoms, while internal frustrations (unmet expectations) map onto internalizing symptoms.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides a novel, **idiographic approach** to understanding frustration — a central yet understudied construct in irritability and psychopathology.
For clinicians working with **irritable patients**, assessing *what* triggers a client's frustration (rather than just how intensely they experience it) could improve case formulation and treatment targeting.
The differential mapping of trigger types to symptom dimensions has direct clinical implications: clients whose frustration is primarily driven by **other people's behavior** may benefit from **interpersonal therapy** or **anger management** approaches, while those frustrated by **unmet expectations** may respond better to **cognitive restructuring** targeting perfectionism or maladaptive expectations.
The finding that **effort/performance frustration predicted lower irritability** is counterintuitive but clinically meaningful — it suggests that frustration arising from one's own efforts may be adaptive and associated with engagement rather than dysregulation.
The **LDA methodology** demonstrates a scalable approach to extracting clinically meaningful themes from open-ended responses, which could be integrated into **intake assessments** or **routine outcome monitoring**.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample of **N = 536** is adequate for the LDA topic modeling and regression analyses.
However, the study is cross-sectional, limiting causal inference about whether frustration triggers cause or result from psychopathology.
No **a priori power analysis** was reported.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of **latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA)** for identifying frustration trigger themes is an innovative application of NLP methods in clinical psychology.
LDA provides a data-driven approach to categorizing open-ended responses, reducing researcher bias inherent in content analysis coding.
The **linear and logistic regression models** testing associations between trigger topics and symptom dimensions are appropriate for the research questions.
Multiple testing across six symptom outcomes was not explicitly corrected for, which increases the risk of **Type I error**.

__Effect Sizes__: Regression coefficients and odds ratios are reported for associations between trigger topics and symptoms.
The paper provides specific effect estimates that allow clinicians to gauge the practical significance of the associations.
**Confidence intervals** would strengthen the reporting.

__Measurement Validity__: Psychopathology symptoms were measured using established rating scales.
A strength is the use of **idiographic open-ended responses** (participants described their own frustration triggers) rather than predetermined categories.
A limitation is that the frustration triggers are based on **self-report** and may be influenced by social desirability or recall biases.
The sample is predominantly **White (72.2%)** and recruited from a university setting, limiting generalizability.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint**.
The manuscript notes supplementary materials containing 7 additional tables.
No mention was made of whether data, code, or the LDA model are publicly available.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample included **536 young adults** (ages 18-25; 61.7% female; 72.2% White; 23.1% Hispanic/Latino).
The demographic composition is reported transparently.
The university-based young adult sample limits generalizability to clinical or older populations.
