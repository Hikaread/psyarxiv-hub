## Summary
This exploratory data analysis characterized **polysubstance use patterns** at time of death in 73 fatal overdose decedents from the **UTHealth Houston Brain Collection** (2016-2025). Using toxicology reports from the Harris County Institute of Forensic Sciences, drugs were attributed to parent compounds and collapsed into 25 classes. Decedents had a mean of **3.5 parent drugs** detected (SD=2.2) and **2.5 drugs implicated as cause of death** (M=2.2 drug classes). At least four drugs were detected in **43.8%** of cases. The **Gini-Simpson diversity index** was .96, indicating that 86.3% of the 51 observed drug-class combinations in cause-of-death determinations were unique to single cases. **Fentanyl** was present in the majority of cases, consistent with the "fourth wave" of the U.S. opioid crisis defined by fentanyl-stimulant co-involvement. The sample was 72.6% male, 71.2% White, with a mean age of 41.1 years (SD=14). The study argues that the extreme heterogeneity of polysubstance combinations poses fundamental challenges for epidemiological modeling and pharmacogenomic research, where simplifying assumptions about drug involvement may involve substantial tradeoffs between parsimony and granularity.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians working in addiction medicine and harm reduction, this study underscores that **polysubstance use is the norm, not the exception** in fatal overdoses — with nearly half of decedents having four or more drugs detected. The extreme **heterogeneity of drug combinations** (GSI=.96) means that clinicians cannot rely on typical "drug profiles" when assessing overdose risk. Each patient's polysubstance pattern may be essentially unique, making individualized assessment critical. The findings have direct implications for **naloxone distribution** and **overdose prevention education**: the presence of multiple CNS depressants (fentanyl, benzodiazepines, alcohol) and stimulants complicates both the acute presentation and the reversal of overdose. Clinicians should also be aware that the **pharmacogenomic implications** are profound — genetic variants affecting metabolism of one drug (e.g., CYP3A4 substrates like fentanyl) may have cascading effects on the toxicity of co-involved substances, making personalized medicine approaches to overdose risk particularly challenging.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper (exploratory data analysis of postmortem data).

__Statistical Rigor__: The study is explicitly exploratory, using descriptive statistics and the Gini-Simpson Index to quantify combinatorial diversity.
No inferential statistics or hypothesis tests are reported, appropriate for an EDA approach.
The mathematical argument about exponential growth of possible combinations (2^k - 1 for k drugs) provides a compelling theoretical framework.

__Measurement Validity__: Toxicology data from a medical examiner's office provides objective, high-quality substance detection.
Drugs were attributed to parent compounds using established metabolic pathways, though some attribution decisions involve clinical judgement.
The 25-class categorization system was developed for this study and may not align with other classification systems.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample is a convenience sample from the UTHealth Brain Collection (N=73), limiting generalizability.
The sample is 72.6% male, 71.2% White, 8.2% Hispanic, 1.4% Asian from Harris County, Texas.
9.6% of deaths were classified as fatal suicide, providing information about intent.
Deaths span 2016-2025, capturing the transition across opioid crisis "waves."

__Open Science__: Available as a preprint on OSF.
The UTHealth Houston Brain Collection is an established resource with documented protocols.
No individual-level data or analysis code is shared in the preprint.
