## Summary
Across two preregistered studies (N = 8,488 and N = 2,547 US adults), the authors tested whether "intervention ensembles"—coordinated combinations of distinct psychological modules—outperform single-mechanism interventions in shaping behavior and attitudes. Three design principles were evaluated: outcome-optimizing, mechanism-coverage, and multi-level synergy ensembles. In Study 1 (climate policy support and real donations), the strongest multi-level synergy ensemble increased policy support by 41.5% and donations by 43.0% relative to an informed control. In Study 2, an ensemble constructed from top-performing anti-animosity interventions significantly outperformed its individual components in reducing partisan animosity and strengthening democratic attitudes. LLM-assisted analysis of open-ended responses revealed that effective ensembles resolve complementary psychological barriers rather than simply providing more arguments.

## Clinical Insight
The ensemble approach—strategically combining interventions that target different psychological mechanisms (individual, collective, and systemic levels)—offers a transferable design framework for clinicians constructing multi-component treatment packages. Rather than seeking the single most effective technique, clinicians may achieve larger effects by identifying and addressing the specific constellation of barriers each patient faces through coordinated intervention modules.

## Relevant For
Behavioral intervention designers and clinical researchers developing multi-component treatments

## Notes
Both studies targeted non-clinical societal outcomes (climate policy, partisan animosity) in online samples of US adults; generalizability to clinical populations and in-person therapeutic settings requires direct testing. Effects are from single-session interventions with unknown durability.
