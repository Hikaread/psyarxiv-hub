## Summary

This study addresses the **factor structure** problems that have plagued **Dickman's Impulsivity Inventory (DII)** since its publication 36 years ago. Despite widespread international use and translation into multiple languages, the DII's factor structure has remained inconsistent, with different language adaptations removing different items without theoretical or semantic rationale. In Study 1, the authors assessed existing item models using **confirmatory factor analysis (CFA)** and employed **exploratory factor analysis (EFA)** to propose a new 8-item model. In Study 2, this **8-item DII (8-DII)** was tested alongside the best-fitting existing models, demonstrating **superior fit indices** while maintaining adequate **internal reliability**. The DII differentiates between **functional impulsivity** (rapid, accurate decisions in situations where this is beneficial) and **dysfunctional impulsivity** (rapid, error-prone decisions where this is harmful), a distinction with direct relevance to clinical assessment of impulsivity across disorders including **ADHD**, **borderline personality disorder (BPD)**, and **substance use disorders**. The shortened 8-item version provides a more practical measure for both research and clinical practice.

## Clinical Insight

Impulsivity is a transdiagnostic construct central to the presentation and maintenance of numerous clinical conditions, from **BPD** and **antisocial personality disorder** to **ADHD**, **substance use disorders**, and **gambling disorder**. The DII's unique distinction between **functional and dysfunctional impulsivity** offers clinicians a more nuanced assessment than unidimensional measures like the Barratt Impulsiveness Scale (BIS-11). In therapy, understanding whether a client's impulsivity is functional (enabling quick, effective action) or dysfunctional (causing harmful consequences) can inform **case formulation** and intervention selection. For example, a client with **BPD** whose impulsivity is primarily dysfunctional may benefit from **DBT skills training** targeting distress tolerance and emotion regulation, while a client whose impulsivity has functional elements may need help distinguishing contexts where rapid responding is adaptive versus maladaptive. The 8-DII's brevity makes it feasible for **routine screening** in outpatient, emergency, and primary care settings, and the improved factor structure means clinicians can have greater confidence that scores reflect the intended constructs. For researchers, the resolution of the long-standing factor structure problem enables more reliable cross-study comparisons and meta-analyses of impulsivity as a treatment predictor or outcome.

## Relevant For
- Clinical psychologists assessing impulsivity in personality and substance use disorders
- BPD and ADHD clinicians using impulsivity measures in case formulation
- Researchers studying impulsivity as a transdiagnostic construct
- Assessment developers working on brief personality measures

## Notes

PDF was not available; curation based on the full abstract description. The paper includes two studies with separate samples, strengthening the validation. The international translation problem described is a real barrier to cross-cultural research that the shortened scale may partially address. Adrian Furnham is a well-established personality assessment researcher. The paper does not report clinical sample validation — the shortened scale was tested in non-clinical populations. Norms for clinical groups are needed. The functional/dysfunctional distinction, while theoretically valuable, may blur in practice as many impulsive behaviours have both adaptive and maladaptive aspects.
