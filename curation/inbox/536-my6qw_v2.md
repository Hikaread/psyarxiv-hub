## Summary
This ecological momentary intervention study (N = 260, 5,902 observations) tested whether training individuals to use wise empathy on Instagram could improve their well-being. Half the sample received training to wisely regulate empathy for both positive and negative posts — cultivating appreciative joy for others' positive experiences and compassionate responding to negative experiences without personal distress. Using experience sampling methodology, the intervention increased wise empathy online: boosting appreciative joy, positive emotion sharing, and compassion for negative emotions while reducing negative emotion sharing, upward social comparisons, and personal distress. When participants had recently browsed Instagram, those in the wise empathy condition reported higher well-being than controls, an effect mediated by changes in wise empathy. The study positions wise empathy as a scalable, trainable skill that can enhance well-being during social media use.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians addressing the mental health impact of social media on their clients, this study offers a novel, skills-based approach that goes beyond screen-time restriction. Rather than asking patients to reduce social media use, the wise empathy intervention teaches them to transform how they engage with content — replacing upward social comparison and personal distress with appreciative joy and compassionate responding. The mediated effect through wise empathy suggests the mechanism is genuinely about changing empathic engagement patterns rather than a placebo. This approach could be integrated into treatment for conditions where social media exacerbates symptoms, including depression, social anxiety, and body image concerns. The ecological momentary design and real-world Instagram use enhance ecological validity over lab-based studies.

## Relevant For
Clinicians addressing social media-related distress; therapists working with depression, anxiety, or body image concerns where social comparison plays a role; those interested in digital well-being interventions; researchers studying empathy regulation in daily life.
