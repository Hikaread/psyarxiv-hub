## Summary
This protocol paper outlines a **qualitative systematic review** employing **thematic synthesis** to consolidate qualitative evidence on African conceptualisations of mental distress and wellbeing, culturally grounded responses, and their relationship with dominant **biomedical paradigms**. The review follows PRISMA-P, ENTREQ, PRISMA-S, and JBI guidance. Qualitative and mixed-methods studies conducted in African contexts will be identified through systematic searches of bibliographic databases, grey literature, and supplementary methods. The **SPIDER framework** guides eligibility. Methodological quality will be appraised using the **CASP Qualitative Checklist**, and confidence in findings assessed using **GRADE-CERQual**. Findings will be synthesized using Thomas and Harden's thematic synthesis approach and interpreted through a **decolonial lens** informed by Indigenous knowledge systems. The review aims to generate higher-order insights relevant to **culturally responsive** global mental health research, policy, and practice.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians practicing in multicultural settings or working with **African diaspora populations**, this protocol highlights the critical gap between Western biomedical frameworks and **African epistemologies** of mental health. African mental health knowledge systems encompass **relational, communal, spiritual, ecological, moral, and biomedical understandings** that frequently coexist and interact. Clinicians should recognize that a patient's conceptualization of their distress may draw on these **non-biomedical frameworks**, and that culturally responsive practice requires **engaging with rather than overriding** these understandings. The review's decolonial approach challenges the assumption that Western diagnostic categories and treatment models are universally applicable, supporting the need for **cultural formulation** in clinical assessment. Even before the review's findings are published, this protocol provides a framework for clinicians to reflect on how their own conceptualizations of mental health may be culturally situated.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians working with African or African diaspora populations
- Those interested in cultural competence and decolonial approaches to mental health
- Global mental health researchers and practitioners
- Therapists providing culturally responsive assessment and formulation

## Notes
This is a protocol paper, not a completed review. No findings are yet available. The review's actual results, when published, will be the key resource for clinical practice.
