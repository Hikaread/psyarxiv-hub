## Summary
This validation study introduces the **Negative Mind-Wandering Inventory (NMWI)**, a 6-item self-report measure designed to assess intrusive, repetitive, negatively valenced self-generated thought that is difficult to control and emotionally distressing. The measure was evaluated in a sample of 77 adults assessed **weekly over four weeks**, providing both between-person and within-person evidence. The NMWI demonstrated high internal consistency (**Cronbach's alpha = .89**; **omega total = .91**) and a predominantly **unidimensional factor structure**. Convergent validity was established through moderate-to-strong correlations with established rumination measures. Critically, the NMWI explained **additional variance in depressive symptoms and trait anxiety** beyond established rumination measures, demonstrating medium to large **incremental validity effects**. **Repeated-measures correlations** indicated that within-person fluctuations in NMWI scores covaried significantly with weekly changes in both depressive symptoms and trait anxiety, supporting its sensitivity to state-like fluctuations and suitability for intensive longitudinal designs.

## Clinical Insight
The NMWI fills an important gap in the assessment toolkit for conditions where **negative self-generated thought** is a central maintaining factor, particularly **depression and generalized anxiety**. Its brevity (6 items) makes it highly practical for repeated measurement in clinical settings, including **session-by-session monitoring** and **experience sampling protocols**. The demonstration that negative mind-wandering predicts depressive symptoms and anxiety **above and beyond established rumination measures** suggests it captures a distinct facet of maladaptive self-focus — one that is specifically characterised by intrusiveness, difficulty of control, and emotional distress rather than the reflective brooding measured by instruments like the **RRS**. This distinction is clinically relevant because it maps more directly onto the **worry and intrusive thought** presentations that patients often describe in session but that traditional rumination scales may not fully capture. Clinicians working with depression and anxiety could use the NMWI as a brief progress monitor to track whether interventions (such as mindfulness-based cognitive therapy, metacognitive therapy, or focused attention techniques) are reducing the frequency and distress of negative mind-wandering episodes over time.

## Methodology Note
Paper type: psychometric. __Statistical Power__: The sample of N = 77 assessed over 4 weekly waves provides adequate data for basic psychometric evaluation (reliability, factor structure) but is underpowered for precise estimation of incremental validity effects and within-person associations. No a priori power analysis was reported. __Effect Sizes__: Incremental validity effects are reported qualitatively as medium to large but specific R-squared change values are not provided in the available text, making it difficult to evaluate clinical significance. __Statistical Rigor__: The use of repeated-measures correlations to separate within-person from between-person effects is methodologically appropriate and adds value beyond standard cross-sectional validation. However, the small sample limits the precision of these within-person estimates. __Measurement Validity__: The NMWI shows strong internal consistency and convergent validity with established rumination measures. The unidimensional factor structure was supported, though the small sample limits the robustness of factor analytic evidence. No test-retest reliability or measurement invariance data were reported. __Open Science__: The preprint is open access; data and code availability are not mentioned. __Participant Transparency__: The sample of 77 adults was recruited via convenience methods; limited demographic information is available in the abstract, restricting generalisability assessments.
