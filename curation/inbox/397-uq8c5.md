## Summary
This study investigated whether emotionally salient moments measured in a naturalistic virtual reality (VR) environment correspond to those identified in actual everyday life, addressing the longstanding question of whether laboratory emotion findings generalize to real-world contexts. Participants experienced ethnographically informed VR scenarios depicting everyday activities (commuting, socializing, working, relaxing) while their subjective emotional responses and physiological signals (EDA, heart rate, respiration) were continuously recorded.

The study compared subjective emotional experience and physiological responses across VR scenarios, examining convergence between self-reported emotional salience and physiological markers. The VR environment was designed based on time-use surveys and ethnographic observation to maximize ecological validity. Results demonstrated that subjective and physiological measures captured emotionally salient moments in VR, though the correspondence between subjective ratings and physiological activation was not always straightforward — some emotionally significant moments showed physiological responses that differed from traditional laboratory findings.

## Clinical Insight
The ecological validity gap between controlled laboratory paradigms and real-world emotional experience is a fundamental challenge for clinical emotion research. This study shows that VR can serve as a bridge, offering experimental control while maintaining ecological validity. For clinical assessment, this suggests that VR-based emotion probes could eventually complement or enhance traditional clinical interviews and self-report measures, particularly for conditions where emotional reactivity is central (e.g., anxiety disorders, PTSD, emotion regulation difficulties). The finding that subjective and physiological measures do not always align in naturalistic contexts also has clinical implications: therapists should not assume that physiological arousal always corresponds to subjective distress, and multi-method assessment remains essential.

## Relevant For
Clinical researchers studying emotion, therapists working with emotion regulation difficulties, developers of clinical assessment tools, researchers interested in ecological momentary assessment and VR-based interventions.
