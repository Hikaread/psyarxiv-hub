## Summary
This study examined **differential item functioning (DIF)** in the **Eysenck Personality Questionnaire-Revised Neuroticism (EPQ-RN) scale**, investigating whether well-documented **gender differences in neuroticism scores** reflect genuine personality differences or **measurement bias** in the instrument. Using DIF analysis methods, the study identified specific items that functioned differently for males and females, and critically examined the implications of this potential bias for the **predictive validity** of neuroticism. Given the central role of neuroticism in **predicting depression, anxiety, and overall mental health outcomes**, gender-biased items could lead to **systematic misestimation** of risk, particularly in epidemiological and clinical research where the EPQ-RN is widely used. The study provides evidence-based recommendations for **item revision or removal** to improve measurement equity.

## Clinical Insight
The EPQ-RN is one of the most widely used brief personality measures in both research and clinical practice. If specific items show **gender DIF**, this means the same underlying level of neuroticism can produce different total scores for men and women — potentially leading to **over- or under-estimation** of personality risk factors in clinical assessments. For clinicians using the EPQ-RN for **case formulation** or **treatment planning**, understanding which items are biased is essential for accurate interpretation. The study's focus on **predictive validity implications** goes beyond mere psychometric documentation — it asks whether biased items actually affect the scale's ability to predict mental health outcomes, which is the clinical bottom line. This work is part of a broader movement toward **bias-aware assessment** in clinical psychology.
