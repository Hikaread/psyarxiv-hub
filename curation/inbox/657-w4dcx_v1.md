## Summary
This longitudinal study tested whether the **mental health and wellbeing** of parents of **transgender youth** declined following the **2025 U.S. presidential inauguration**, which was accompanied by executive orders targeting transgender youth.
The design spanned **three years pre-inauguration (2022-2024)** and **two months surrounding the inauguration**, including parents of transgender youth (N youth = 160, ages 12-17) and a comparison group of parents of **cisgender youth**.
Across **preregistered analyses**, the study found **significant declines in mental health and wellbeing** among parents of transgender youth relative to parents of cisgender youth.
Interestingly, **fewer differences over time** were observed between transgender and cisgender youth themselves, suggesting the political climate affected **parents** more acutely than youth in this sample.
The sample was drawn from a population that, on the whole, **did not support the new administration**, suggesting the effects reflect the specific policy targeting rather than general political preferences.
The findings identify an **immediate mental health cost** of the current political environment for supportive parents of transgender youth.

## Clinical Insight
This study documents the **psychological impact of anti-transgender policy signals** on a vulnerable population — parents of transgender youth — who play a critical role in their children's wellbeing.
For clinicians working with **LGBTQ+ families**, these findings highlight that **parental mental health** may be an underrecognized casualty of the political climate, even when parents are supportive and affirming.
The finding that parents were more affected than youth themselves suggests that **parental burden** — including anticipatory anxiety, vigilance, and grief — may be a hidden treatment need in family therapy with transgender youth.
Clinicians should assess **parental stress and mental health** as part of working with transgender youth, particularly during periods of political threat.
The study underscores the importance of **systemic and advocacy perspectives** in clinical practice: clinicians working with gender-diverse populations cannot ignore the sociopolitical context.
These findings are relevant to the **minority stress framework** and suggest that policy changes targeting a family member can produce **secondary minority stress** in caregivers.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample included **N = 160 transgender youth** (ages 12-17) and **N = 167 parents**, which is adequate for detecting moderate effect sizes in longitudinal analyses.
The comparison group of cisgender youth and their parents strengthens causal inference.
The longitudinal pre-post design spanning three years provides a strong baseline for detecting change.

__Preregistration__: The study used **preregistered analyses**, strengthening confidence that the findings were not the result of exploratory data analysis.
Preregistration is particularly important in politically charged research where confirmatory bias is a concern.

__Statistical Rigor__: The **longitudinal design** with multiple pre-inauguration time points (2022-2024) allows each participant to serve as their own control, strengthening causal inference about the inauguration's effect.
The inclusion of a **cisgender comparison group** controls for general temporal trends in mental health.
A limitation is that the two-month post-inauguration window captures only **immediate effects** — longer-term impacts remain unknown.

__Effect Sizes__: The study reports significant between-group differences in mental health trajectories.
Specific effect size estimates (Cohen's d or similar) for the magnitude of parental mental health decline were not available in the abstract.
The finding of significant effects despite the relatively short post-inauguration window suggests clinically meaningful impact.

__Measurement Validity__: The study does not specify which mental health and wellbeing measures were used in the abstract.
The use of **longitudinal repeated measures** provides more reliable estimates than cross-sectional assessment.
A limitation is that the measures may not capture all relevant domains of parental stress specific to having a transgender child (e.g., vigilance for discrimination, healthcare access concerns).

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint** and uses **preregistered analyses**.
No mention was made of whether data or analysis code are publicly available.
The preregistration of hypotheses and analysis plan is a major strength for this politically sensitive topic.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample included **160 transgender youth** (ages 12-17) and **167 parents** of transgender youth, plus a cisgender comparison group.
The sample was recruited from a population that **did not support the new administration**, which is both a strength (reduces confounding by political alignment) and a limitation (findings may not generalize to politically conservative parents of trans youth).
Geographic and racial/ethnic diversity of the sample was not detailed in the abstract.
