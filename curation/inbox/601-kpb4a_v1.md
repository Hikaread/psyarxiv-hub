## Summary
This qualitative study (N=31 semi-structured interviews) examined how **media serve as resources** for **LGBTQ+ self-acceptance** and **other-acceptance** (acceptance by close others such as friends and family). Through **thematic analysis**, the study identified distinct pathways through which media contribute to acceptance processes. For **self-acceptance**, media provided **representation and visibility** (seeing oneself reflected), **narrative frameworks** for understanding identity, and **community connection** beyond physical proximity. For **other-acceptance**, media served as **educational tools** for close others, **conversation starters** that facilitated dialogue about LGBTQ+ identity, and **empathy-building resources** through exposure to diverse stories. The study also examined how different media types (fiction, documentary, social media, news) function differently in these processes and identified **boundary conditions** where media can be counterproductive.

## Clinical Insight
With **LGBTQ+ discrimination increasing** and acceptance declining in many contexts, understanding what facilitates acceptance is clinically urgent. For therapists working with LGBTQ+ clients, this study provides an evidence base for a practical intervention: **prescribing specific media content** as a therapeutic adjunct. Media can serve as a **low-threshold, destigmatizing bridge** for family members struggling to understand a loved one's identity — a common clinical scenario in family therapy. The study also illuminates how LGBTQ+ individuals actively use media for **self-acceptance work**, which clinicians can support and validate. The distinction between **self-acceptance** and **other-acceptance** pathways is clinically useful: a therapist working with an LGBTQ+ client might recommend very different media depending on whether the goal is supporting the client's own identity integration or facilitating a family member's acceptance.

## Relevant For
- Therapists working with LGBTQ+ clients and their families
- Family therapists supporting acceptance processes
- Clinicians interested in media-based therapeutic interventions
- Cultural competence trainers
