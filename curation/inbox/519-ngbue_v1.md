## Summary

This **randomised controlled trial** (N = 135) examined how the one sec **digital friction** app affects **social media use**, **momentary affect**, and broader **well-being** during a 7-day **ecological momentary assessment** period. Participants self-selected a target application (most commonly Instagram) and were randomly assigned to one of three conditions: affect-only, added friction, or timed friction. Across 18,796 **app interactions** and 4,209 valid **mood reports**, total target app interactions decreased significantly over time (b₁ = −1.06, p = .002), corresponding to approximately 5% reduction per day. **Valence** shifted significantly towards the positive end over the study period (b₁ = 0.025, p < .001), whereas **arousal** showed no reliable change. In pre-post surveys (n = 44), **stress** decreased significantly (dz = −0.66, p(FDR) = .001), and perceived impact of phone use on **focus**, **productivity**, and **mood** all improved. Participants dismissed 29% of target app-opening attempts, with descriptively higher dismissal rates in friction conditions. The affect-only group showed 18.9% dismissal rates, suggesting that **mood reporting** itself introduced measurement reactivity.

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: The multi-method design combining **objective behavioural logs** with event-contingent **EMA** and surveys addresses a major gap in digital well-being research. The use of real-world data from participants' own devices on their self-selected target apps provides high **ecological validity**. The preregistered analytical approach using **linear mixed-effects models** with random intercepts is appropriate for repeated-measures EMA data. The **FDR correction** for multiple comparisons in pre-post analyses is commendable. The three-condition design isolates effects of mood reporting from friction.

__Limitation__: The 34% mean **skip rate** for mood reports is concerning and may bias valence estimates if participants were more likely to skip during negative states. EMA prompts were present in all conditions, making it impossible to disentangle **measurement reactivity** from intervention effects. Only 44 of 135 participants completed the post-survey due to attrition and technical issues, making pre-post findings tentative. The sample was unpaid, recruited via in-app banner, and predominantly female — limiting generalisability. The 7-day duration is insufficient to detect changes in slower-moving outcomes like **life satisfaction**. No objective **screen-time** data were collected, so substitution effects to non-target apps cannot be ruled out.

## Clinical Insight

This study provides preliminary evidence that simple **digital friction** interventions can reduce unwanted social media use and improve momentary **valence** and self-reported **stress**. The finding that mood reporting itself acts as a form of friction has clinical implications: asking patients to briefly reflect on their emotional state before engaging with digital habits may be a low-threshold self-regulation strategy. The significant reduction in perceived negative impact of phone use on **focus** and **productivity** (dz = 0.59 and 0.38 respectively) suggests that digital friction tools may be useful adjuncts for patients presenting with **attention problems** or **work-related stress** exacerbated by social media. However, clinicians should note the short intervention duration and that **life satisfaction** did not change significantly, indicating that friction-based tools may address acute **digital self-regulation** difficulties without producing broader well-being shifts in one week.

## Relevant For

- Clinicians treating patients with problematic social media use or digital addiction
- Mental health professionals working on attention and productivity concerns
- Researchers studying digital well-being interventions
- Practitioners interested in low-threshold behavioral self-regulation tools
