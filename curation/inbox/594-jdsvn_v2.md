## Summary
This **quasi-experimental pilot study** (N=25) investigated the effects of **Process- and Embodiment-Focused Psychology (PEP)** treatment on depressive symptoms, anxiety symptoms, and generalized self-efficacy. PEP is an integrative approach combining **cognitive techniques** with **embodiment-focused procedures** including **sensorimotor self-stimulation** (acupoint tapping), structured work with **self-referential cognitions**, and **self-esteem activation**. Participants received either therapist-guided PEP (n=12, 14–35 sessions) or a structured self-study condition using a PEP-based manual (n=13, ~6 weeks). Both groups improved significantly, but the therapist-guided condition showed dramatically larger effects with **large within-group effect sizes**: BDI-II d=3.05, BAI d=2.31, GSWE d=1.93 versus control d=1.24, d=1.24, d=0.77. Significant **time × group interactions** were found for all three outcomes (BDI-II: F(1,23)=27.23, p<.001, np2=.54; BAI: F(1,13)=323.00, p<.001, np2=.96; GSWE: F(1,23)=5.51, p=.028, np2=.19). By post-treatment, baseline group differences in symptom severity and self-efficacy had largely resolved.

## Clinical Insight
PEP represents a novel integration of **cognitive, embodied, and resource-oriented** approaches that is deliberately designed to be **low-threshold and scalable** — deliverable not only by licensed psychotherapists but also by GPs and complementary practitioners with PEP certification. The extremely large effect sizes in the therapist-guided condition (d=3.05 for depression) are striking, though the **quasi-experimental design**, **baseline differences** (EG was more severe), and **small sample** (N=12 per group) necessitate extreme caution in interpretation. The inclusion of **generalized self-efficacy** as an outcome is theoretically grounded in **Bandura's social-cognitive theory** and distinguishes PEP from purely symptom-focused approaches. The finding that both therapist-guided and self-study conditions produced improvements suggests that PEP techniques have merit even as **psychoeducational self-help**, though therapist guidance adds substantially. Clinicians should view this as promising preliminary evidence for a novel modality, not as efficacy confirmation.

## Relevant For
- Therapists interested in integrative and embodied approaches
- Clinicians seeking low-threshold intervention options
- Practitioners working with comorbid depression and anxiety
- Service designers looking for scalable treatment models

Full PDF: 14 pages. Quasi-experimental (not randomized). Single therapist delivered all PEP treatment. All participants in EG had same practitioner — cannot separate therapist effects from treatment effects. BDI-II and BAI used as primary outcomes. 60% of sample had comorbid anxiety. PEP includes tapping/acupoint stimulation elements similar to EFT (Emotional Freedom Techniques). German language study published in English.
