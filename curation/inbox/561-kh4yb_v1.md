## Summary

Using a **classical grounded theory** methodology, this study explores how employed people maintain **meaningful working lives** in the context of **emotionally charged work** — defined as work involving high **emotional demands** and **emotional labour**. The research began with purposeful sampling of **eight employees and managers from psychiatric hospitals** and expanded through theoretical sampling to include **15 additional participants** across diverse occupations including ministers of religion, military personnel, social care workers, educators, and IT managers. Through **open coding** and **constant comparison**, the authors developed a grounded theory of **collective capacity building** for emotionally charged work. The core finding is that the main concern of participants was maintaining meaningful work, and the central strategy for achieving this in emotionally charged contexts was building **collective capacity** — shared resources, mutual support, and organizational structures that distribute the emotional burden. The theory addresses the prevention of negative consequences previously associated with emotionally charged work, including **ill health** and **alienation**, and offers a framework for the sustainable organization of work in high-emotion occupations. The study provides both theoretical and practical guidance for **preventing compassion fatigue** in settings marked by sustained emotional labour.

## Clinical Insight

For mental health professionals working in high-stress clinical environments — particularly **psychiatric hospitals**, **crisis teams**, and **trauma-focused services** — this grounded theory offers a preventive framework for **compassion fatigue** and **burnout** that shifts the focus from individual resilience strategies to **collective, organizational-level capacity building**. The finding that the primary concern of workers in emotionally charged roles is maintaining meaning in their work aligns with extensive research on **moral injury** and **vicarious traumatization** in clinical populations of therapists themselves. The theory of **collective capacity building** suggests that interventions targeting individual clinicians — such as mindfulness training or self-care plans — while valuable, are insufficient without concurrent investment in shared team-level processes: peer consultation groups, reflective practice spaces, shared caseload management, and organizational cultures that normalize emotional processing of difficult clinical work. The inclusion of psychiatric hospital staff as the initial purposive sample grounds the theory directly in mental health settings, making it particularly relevant for clinical supervisors designing **staff wellbeing programs**. Clinicians can draw on the theory's emphasis on collective structures to advocate for institutional changes — such as regular team debriefs, peer support systems, and workload distribution policies — that sustain the emotional resources necessary for effective therapeutic work over time.

## Relevant For
- Clinical supervisors and managers in psychiatric or mental health settings
- Therapists at risk of compassion fatigue or vicarious traumatization
- Organizational psychologists designing staff wellbeing interventions
- Mental health service leaders developing sustainable workforce strategies
- Social care professionals working in emotionally demanding roles
