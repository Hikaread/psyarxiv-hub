## Summary
This cross-sectional observational study (N = 81 adolescents aged 14–17; 34 cannabis users, 47 controls) examined the interrelation of cannabis use (CU), psychotic-like experiences (PLEs), and dopamine-related neurobehavioral markers. A significant CU × PLEs interaction predicted reduced reward motivation (OR = 2.41, 95% CI 1.10–5.27, p = .028), such that cannabis-using adolescents with high PLEs showed less willingness to exert effort for rewards at low-to-medium effort levels. CU quantity and PLEs were positively associated at high levels of subcortical 1/nT2* (a tissue iron MRI marker linked to dopamine neurophysiology): daily concentrate hits interacted with 1/nT2* in the left putamen (β = 51.58, p = .01) and right thalamus (β = 70.93, p = .02), and daily cannabis grams in the left putamen (β = 73.34, p = .02), right putamen (β = 101.69, p = .001), and right thalamus (β = 102.38, p = .01).

## Clinical Insight
Adolescent cannabis users presenting with PLEs may exhibit distinct reward processing deficits—reduced motivation and less strategic effort allocation—that could serve as early behavioral markers of psychosis-spectrum risk. Screening for motivational changes alongside PLEs in adolescent cannabis users may help identify those at elevated risk who could benefit from early intervention targeting reward system dysfunction.

## Relevant For
Clinicians working with adolescents using cannabis, especially those presenting with subclinical psychotic symptoms

## Notes
Cross-sectional design precludes causal inferences about the directionality of CU, PLEs, and reward processing alterations. Small sample (N = 81), hypotheses were formulated during data collection rather than a priori, and medication use (32% of cannabis group on antidepressants) may confound dopaminergic and motivational measures.
