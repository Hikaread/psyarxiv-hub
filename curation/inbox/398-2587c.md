## Summary
This study conducted a conceptual replication and extension of the Blind Implantation paradigm (Otgaar et al., 2022), a scalable method for inducing false autobiographical memories without human interaction. The sample (N=348) was three times larger than the original, using a multi-target event design with three critical events and a non-suggestive control group. The procedure involved a personalized profiling phase where participants reported real autobiographical events, followed by an AI-assisted suggestive feedback phase claiming that certain events had been independently verified.

Results confirmed that the paradigm reliably induces false beliefs and false memories, supporting the reconstructive nature of memory. However, induction rates were substantially lower than originally reported: 12-15% for false memories and approximately 30% for false beliefs across events, compared to the original study's ~40% and ~60% respectively. The multi-target design revealed variability across events, suggesting that event characteristics influence suggestibility. The control group showed minimal false endorsement (<2%), confirming that the suggestive feedback — not demand characteristics — drives the effect. Memory quality ratings for false memories were significantly lower than for true memories, suggesting that even induced false memories are experienced as less vivid and detailed.

## Clinical Insight
False memory research has direct relevance to clinical practice, particularly in forensic psychology, trauma therapy, and any context where memory accuracy matters. The lower-than-originally-reported induction rates are reassuring — while false memories can be created through suggestive techniques, a majority of participants resisted the manipulation even under optimized conditions. For trauma-focused therapists, this study underscores the importance of using non-suggestive memory recovery techniques. The finding that false memories are experienced as less vivid than true memories provides a potential metacognitive cue that clinicians and clients can use to evaluate memory accuracy. The study also demonstrates that AI-assisted methods can create convincing but false personal narratives, with implications for understanding how suggestive influences operate in therapeutic, legal, and digital contexts.

## Relevant For
Forensic psychologists, trauma therapists, clinical psychologists working with memory, legal mental health professionals, researchers studying suggestibility and memory distortion.

## Notes
Replication with 3x the original sample size. Multi-target design is more ecologically valid than single-target paradigms. Uses AI-generated feedback rather than human experimenters. Henry Otgaar is a prominent false memory researcher. The 12-15% false memory rate still represents a meaningful minority vulnerable to suggestion.
