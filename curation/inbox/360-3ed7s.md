## Summary

This large-scale study examined youth resilience across three phases of the COVID-19 pandemic using Dutch population-based samples (pre-pandemic N=12,937; during N=8,934; post-pandemic N=37,797; ages 12-25). Higher optimism, lower loneliness, and better self-rated health were consistently associated with resilient functioning (better-than-expected mental health given stress exposure) across all phases. However, moderation analyses showed inconsistent evidence that these characteristics buffered the impact of stress on mental health. The findings support resilience as a complex, context-dependent process rather than the result of single protective factors.

## Clinical Insight

This study challenges a common therapeutic assumption that building protective factors like optimism or social connection will directly buffer clients against stress. For therapists working with young people, the message is nuanced: fostering psychosocial resources is associated with better resilient functioning, but these resources do not reliably moderate the effect of stress itself. Clinicians should set realistic expectations when building resilience—it helps young people function better overall but may not make them immune to stress impacts. The large sample and multi-wave design make these findings particularly robust for clinical guidance.

## Relevant For

- Child and adolescent therapists
- School counselors and youth mental health workers
- Clinicians working on resilience-building interventions with young people
- Therapists treating pandemic-related mental health impacts in youth
