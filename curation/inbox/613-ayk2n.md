## Summary

This cross-sectional study (N = 1,197 mothers) examined the association between exposure to the **Rx Kids program** — the first universal and unconditional prenatal and infant cash transfer program in the United States, launched in Flint, Michigan in January 2024 — and **maternal anxiety** measured by the **GAD-7**. The program provides a $1,500 prenatal payment followed by $500 monthly through the infant's first year (total ~$7,500 per child). Using a **multivariable difference-in-differences model** with four study groups, the study found that exposed Flint mothers reported significantly less moderate-to-severe anxiety compared to pre-program Flint mothers (−9.5 percentage points, p < .05), lower mean **GAD-7** scores (−1.5 percentage points, p < .05), decreased frequency of **nervousness** (−13.5 percentage points, p < .01), and decreased **worrying too much** (−9.8 percentage points, p < .05). The adjusted DiD analysis estimated a 10-percentage-point reduction in clinically elevated anxiety — a **34% relative decrease** from the 2023 Flint baseline. Fear ("something awful might happen") showed a **46% relative reduction** (p < .05 in adjusted analysis). Notably, exposed Flint mothers achieved anxiety outcomes comparable to or better than substantially more advantaged non-Flint comparison groups. The **GAD-7** demonstrated excellent internal consistency in this sample (Cronbach's alpha = 0.92). The study followed **STROBE** guidelines and was conducted at Hurley Medical Center, where approximately 70% of Flint mothers give birth.

## Clinical Insight

This study provides some of the first real-world evidence that **unconditional economic support** during the perinatal period is associated with clinically meaningful reductions in **maternal anxiety** — a finding with direct implications for perinatal mental health practice. The 34% relative reduction in moderate-to-severe anxiety is substantial, particularly given that perinatal anxiety affects approximately 15–20% of mothers and is associated with adverse infant outcomes including preterm birth and impaired attachment. For clinicians working in perinatal mental health, these results reinforce the **family stress model** (Masarik and Conger, 2017): reductions in material hardship alleviate stress processes contributing to psychological distress. The finding that socioeconomically disadvantaged mothers achieved anxiety outcomes comparable to more advantaged comparison groups carries important implications for **health equity** — suggesting that simple, dignity-preserving economic interventions can narrow mental health disparities. Clinicians should note that some anxiety symptoms (trouble relaxing, restlessness, irritability) did not show significant reductions, which the authors interpret as possibly reflecting **adaptive vigilance** in the context of chronic adversity rather than psychopathology per se. The program's design features — universal eligibility, unconditional disbursement, and messaging emphasising dignity and agency — align with evidence that autonomy-supportive interventions are more effective than conditional or prescriptive approaches. For therapeutic practice, this suggests that advocating for upstream economic policies may be as important as providing individual-level treatment.

## Relevant For

- Perinatal and maternal mental health clinicians
- Clinical psychologists working with low-income populations
- Policy advocates for economic interventions in mental health
- Researchers studying social determinants of anxiety disorders

## Notes

Cross-sectional design limits causal inference; parallel trends assumption could not be formally tested. No confidence intervals reported — only standard errors and p-values. Infant age differed between pre-program (17–24 months) and during-program (0–12 months) groups. Affiliations: Michigan State University and University of Michigan. Reported per STROBE guidelines.
