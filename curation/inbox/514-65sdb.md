## Summary

This instrument development paper documents the creation of the **DESI-18** (Dyadic Emotional Safety & Inclusion), a brief **partner-rated measure** for assessing **felt safety** in first-meeting dyads. Felt safety is defined as "the degree to which a person experiences the relational space with another as safe enough to be authentic." The instrument was developed across three failed vantage points: (1) **physiological measurement** (HRV, EDA) was abandoned when sensors **disrupted the encounter** they were meant to measure, particularly for socially anxious participants; (2) a **behavioral observation sheet** (NSI), fielded across 15 dyads, revealed a **masking paradox**—observable behavior can appear warm while felt experience is absent; (3) the surviving approach was **partner-report** (180-degree), where each person rates the other.

The current DESI-18 comprises **16 scored items** (plus 1 attention check) organized into a **4 × 4 structure** with four factors: **Engagement** (eye contact, listening cues, warmth, undivided attention), **Responsiveness** (feeling understood, valued, partner responded rather than waited), **Relational Attunement & Perceived Safety**, and **We-ness** (shared identity, belonging beyond the dyad). The four-factor structure is a **theory-led hypothesis** (not yet factor-analyzed). The construct was reframed from a safety-and-withdrawal account to a **social-cure, We-ness** account drawing on Haslam et al.'s work on shared social identity and belonging. **No reliability or factor-analytic evidence** exists yet; a dedicated validation study is planned.

## Clinical Insight

For couples therapists and clinicians working with relational difficulties, the DESI-18 addresses a crucial but elusive construct: the moment-by-moment sense of **safety to be authentic** in another's presence. The authors' observation that physiological measurement can destroy the very state it aims to capture is an important methodological lesson for therapists interested in biofeedback or physiological monitoring of therapeutic relationships. The **masking paradox**—where warm behavior coexists with absent felt safety—directly mirrors clinical situations where patients present as engaged while feeling unsafe. The choice of **partner-report** over self-report is theoretically motivated: self-report of a state one is actively managing may invite defensive self-presentation. For couples therapy, this 180-degree approach (rating the other) could complement existing measures like the **WAI** or **PERQ**, particularly for assessing initial sessions where safety foundations are being established.

## Methodology Note

This is a psychometric paper.

__Statistical Rigor__: The paper is transparently honest about the **absence of empirical validation data** at this stage.
No reliability estimates (α, ω, ICC) exist for any version of the instrument.
The four-factor structure is explicitly described as a **theory-led hypothesis to be tested**, not a data-derived solution.
The NSI behavioral sheet was fielded across 15 dyads with coding fidelity completed, but by a **single coder** (no inter-rater reliability).
The physiological approach was abandoned at pilot (3 dyads) due to the measurement reactivity problem.

__Measurement Validity__: The DESI-18 is in **early development** with no psychometric evidence reported.
All items are positively keyed (no reverse items) to avoid the confusion that mixed-keying created in earlier versions.
The 4 × 4 balanced structure (4 items per factor) is a design strength.
The theoretical grounding draws on multiple validated constructs: **psychological safety** (Edmondson, 1999), **perceived partner responsiveness** (Reis et al., 2018), **polyvagal theory** (Porges, 2007, 2011), **Inclusion of Other in the Self** (Aron et al., 1992), and **social cure theory** (Haslam et al., 2018).
The **masking paradox** is an important theoretical contribution to the measurement of felt states.
Social anxiety was screened using the **SIAS-6** in the parent study.

__Open Science__: As a preprint development paper, no validation data are presented.
The paper explicitly states what remains to be validated.
The parent study (FRIEND) received ethical approval (UEL2026AGAF) with an ethics amendment for removing the physiological protocol.

__Participant Transparency__: The NSI was fielded across 15 dyads (no demographic details available from the reviewed text).
DESI-12 was tested on a single dyad (proof-of-concept only).
DESI-14 was design-only (not fielded).
DESI-18 (current form) has no validation data.
The parent study used the **Fast Friends protocol** (Aron et al., 1997) to structure first meetings.
The paper does not describe the demographic characteristics of dyad participants in detail.

## Notes

This is explicitly a development paper, not a validation paper. The authors are admirably transparent about the instrument's current evidentiary status. The shift from a "safety-and-withdrawal" to a "social-cure/We-ness" framework represents a meaningful theoretical evolution. The ethical decision to abandon the physiological protocol when it distressed participants is noteworthy. The companion validation study will be critical for assessing whether the theory-led four-factor structure holds empirically.
