## Summary

This paper introduces the **Significant Other AI (SO-AI) Measurement Framework**, a multidisciplinary conceptual framework that asks whether AI systems can occupy the role of a **psychologically significant other** in people's lives — analogous to how human partners, mentors, or therapists function. Building on theories from **clinical psychology, family therapy, sociology, and education**, the framework synthesises multiple relational functions (emotional support, identity formation, guidance, companionship) into a unified conceptual model. The authors developed the **SO-AI Questionnaire** through a systematic, theory-driven process: multidisciplinary literature synthesis, exploratory qualitative investigations, iterative **expert panel review** (N = 17 experts across 9 disciplines), and systematic item refinement. The resulting questionnaire uses a **parallel assessment strategy** that evaluates both human significant others and AI systems using corresponding relational dimensions, and incorporates **situation-based behavioural scenarios** to examine consultation preferences across everyday contexts. The paper explicitly positions itself as providing the conceptual and methodological foundation for future validation studies rather than presenting final psychometric properties.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists and clinical psychologists, this paper is relevant for several reasons. First, as **AI companions and therapeutic chatbots** become increasingly prevalent (e.g., Wysa, Woebot, Replika), clinicians need frameworks for understanding how clients relate to these systems and whether such relationships support or undermine therapeutic goals. The SO-AI framework provides a structured way to assess whether an AI is functioning as a **significant relational figure** — which has implications for transference dynamics, attachment patterns, and therapeutic boundary management. Second, the **parallel assessment paradigm** (evaluating human and AI significant others on the same dimensions) offers a clinical tool for exploring whether a client's AI relationship is **supplementing, substituting for, or competing with** human relationships — a distinction that matters for treatment planning. Third, the framework's integration of **family therapy** and **clinical psychology** theories (Bowlby's attachment, Andersen's relational schemas) means it speaks directly to clinicians' existing conceptual vocabularies. Fourth, as digital mental health tools increasingly incorporate **relational AI features**, understanding how clients perceive and engage with these systems becomes a necessary component of clinical assessment, particularly for younger clients who may normalise AI relationships. The paper cautions that empirical validation is still needed before clinical application.

## Methodology Note

This is an other-type paper (conceptual framework and questionnaire development, pre-validation), so Statistical Power, Preregistration, and Effect Sizes are not applicable.

__Statistical Rigor__: The development process followed best practices for instrument development: literature synthesis, qualitative exploration, expert panel review (N = 17 across 9 disciplines including clinical psychology and family therapy), and iterative item refinement. However, this paper does not present quantitative psychometric data (factor structure, reliability, validity) — these are deferred to a subsequent validation study. The framework is grounded in established theories from multiple disciplines.

__Measurement Validity__: The SO-AI Questionnaire is in the **initial development phase**.
Content validity was addressed through the expert panel review process and iterative refinement.
The parallel assessment paradigm (same items for human and AI targets) is a novel methodological innovation for reducing method variance.
No reliability or construct validity data are reported yet.

__Open Science__: The paper is a preprint on PsyArXiv.
No mention of preregistration, data sharing, or OSF project page for the development phase.

__Participant Transparency__: The qualitative investigations involved participants exploring their AI relationships (specific sample details not provided in the available text).
The expert panel included 17 experts across psychology, sociology, education, family therapy, and HCI.
No demographic details of qualitative participants are available in the preprint.
