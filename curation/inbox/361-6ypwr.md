## Summary

Using longitudinal data from four large aging cohorts (HRS, SHARE, CHARLS, KLoSA) across the US, Europe, China, and South Korea, this study examined depressive symptom trajectories around the retirement transition. Results showed that changes in depression around retirement were generally modest at the group level, but patterns varied by country and historical period. The US showed pre-retirement increases and an immediate rise at retirement, followed by post-retirement decline. Europe showed a small immediate decrease, China showed steady increases, and South Korea showed an immediate increase followed by decline. Period-specific analyses revealed further variation.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with older adults, this study provides valuable context for understanding how retirement may affect mental health. Rather than assuming a uniform effect, clinicians should assess individual factors including cultural context, pre-retirement mental health, and the specific circumstances of the transition. The finding that changes are generally modest at the group level suggests that retirement itself is not inherently depressive or protective—it is the personal meaning, preparation, and social context that matter. Therapists can use this evidence to normalize clients' experiences and provide psychoeducation that retirement-related mood changes vary widely and are often manageable.

## Relevant For

- Therapists working with older adults and retirees
- Clinicians addressing late-life depression
- Counselors helping clients navigate life transitions
- Practitioners in culturally diverse settings serving aging populations
