## Summary
This development paper documents the creation of the Dyadic Emotional Safety and Inclusion (DESI-18) instrument, a brief 18-item partner-rated measure designed to capture felt safety in first-meeting dyads. Felt safety is defined as the degree to which a person experiences the relational space with another as safe enough to be authentic. The instrument emerged from two failed measurement attempts: a physiological approach (HRV and EDA) was abandoned when sensors disrupted the encounter, and a behavioral coding sheet (NSI) revealed a masking paradox — observable warmth can coexist with felt unsafety. These failures led to the partner-report vantage point where each person rates the other. The current four-factor structure (Engagement, Responsiveness, Rapport, We-ness) is theory-led and reframes felt safety through a social-cure lens, emphasizing shared identity rather than mere absence of threat. The authors are transparent about current limitations: only a single-dyad proof-of-concept exists, with no reliability or factor-analytic evidence yet.

## Clinical Insight
Felt safety is a foundational yet under-measured construct in psychotherapy — it is the affective substrate of the therapeutic alliance and the prerequisite for vulnerable self-disclosure. The DESI-18's development story is itself clinically instructive: the masking paradox (behavior can look warm while felt experience is not) mirrors the therapeutic challenge of distinguishing genuine alliance from compliance or performance. For therapists, this underscores that relational safety cannot be read from overt behavior alone and must be explicitly explored in the therapeutic relationship. The social-cure reframing — connecting felt safety to shared identity and belonging — offers a theoretical bridge between attachment-based and group-based approaches to therapeutic change. While the instrument requires validation before clinical use, the construct clarity and honest documentation of measurement challenges provide a valuable foundation for future research on therapeutic safety and alliance formation.

## Relevant For
Therapapists interested in therapeutic alliance measurement; researchers studying the role of felt safety in clinical change; those developing or validating relationship measures; clinicians working with populations where safety is a primary concern (trauma, personality disorders).

## Notes
This is a development paper with no validation data beyond a single-dyad proof-of-concept. The instrument should not be used clinically until dedicated validation studies are completed. The four-factor structure is hypothesis-driven, not data-derived.
