## Summary

This study investigated whether mentalization (the ability to infer others' mental states) is better characterized through vector-based response representations than classical aggregate scores. 164 university participants completed the IRI, RMET, and Triangles task. Classical score correlations showed limited convergence across tasks. Representational similarity analyses showed vector-based representations reliably converged with classical scores within tasks, but cross-task analyses revealed that score-based and vector-based representations produced partly different prediction patterns. The findings suggest that how mentalization is measured (aggregate scores vs. full response configurations) organizes individual differences differently.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists who use mentalization-based treatment (MBT), this research highlights that standard mentalization assessments may miss important nuances in how clients process social information. Two clients with the same total score on a mentalization measure may have very different response patterns, reflecting different underlying cognitive strategies. This supports clinical approaches that examine not just whether a client can mentalize, but how they mentalize—the specific patterns of their social-cognitive processing. Clinicians should consider going beyond total scores to understand qualitative differences in clients' mentalization profiles.

## Relevant For

- Therapists practicing mentalization-based treatment (MBT)
- Clinicians assessing social cognition and theory of mind
- Practitioners working with personality disorders where mentalization deficits are prominent
- Clinical psychologists developing or refining social-cognitive assessments
