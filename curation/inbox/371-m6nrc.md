## Summary

This study investigates the relationship between brain age gap (the difference between predicted and chronological brain age), cortisol reactivity, and the developmental timing of childhood sexual abuse in women with major depressive disorder (MDD). Across two independent samples (N=71 and N=85), the authors examined women with current MDD, remitted MDD, and healthy controls, all with histories of childhood sexual abuse. The study explores how childhood trauma timing interacts with depression status and physiological stress responsivity to produce accelerated biological aging in the brain.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with trauma and depression, this study provides biological evidence supporting the link between early adverse experiences and long-term physiological dysregulation. The finding that developmental timing of abuse matters for brain aging trajectories reinforces the clinical importance of detailed trauma history-taking, particularly regarding when maltreatment occurred. Therapists can use this knowledge to psychoeducate clients about the mind-body connection in trauma recovery and to validate that the physiological effects of childhood abuse are real and measurable, not just psychological. The cortisol reactivity findings may also inform discussions about stress management and nervous system regulation in trauma-focused therapy.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with adult survivors of childhood sexual abuse, trauma-focused therapists, clinicians treating treatment-resistant depression, therapists integrating psychoeducation about biological stress responses into their practice.

## Notes

Two-sample design strengthens findings. Focus on women with CSA histories limits generalizability. Brain age gap is an emerging biomarker with clinical potential for tracking treatment progress.
