## Summary
This **pilot study** examined the effects of **transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS)** on psychological health, sleep, and strength performance in **10 healthy adult powerlifters** over a **four-week intervention** period.
Participants self-administered taVNS **five times per week** for **30 minutes** after strength training or before bedtime, with measurements at **pre, post, and one-month follow-up**.
Outcomes spanned **subjective psychological domains** (recovery, stress, perceived stress, affect, memory, attention, anxiety, well-being), **objective sleep metrics** (nocturnal HRV, sleep score, sleep duration, subjective sleep quality), and **submaximal strength performance** (squat, bench press, deadlift).
Results showed **statistically significant pre-post improvements in positive affect and attention** with **large effect sizes**, while remaining psychological and sleep outcomes did not reach significance.
A **responder analysis** revealed **mixed individual response patterns**, with some participants showing clinically meaningful improvements across multiple domains while others showed negligible change.
**No distinct beneficial effect on strength performance** was observed, and gains did not differ from expected training progression.

## Clinical Insight
While conducted in a non-clinical athletic population, this study has **direct implications for clinical practice** because **taVNS** is increasingly investigated as an **adjunctive treatment** for depression, anxiety, and PTSD.
The **large effect sizes for positive affect and attention** are consistent with prior taVNS studies in clinical populations and suggest the mechanism (vagal afferent activation modulating noradrenergic and serotonergic pathways) may operate similarly in healthy and clinical groups.
The **highly variable individual responses** (responder analysis) are clinically important: they mirror the **heterogeneity seen in clinical taVNS trials** and highlight the need for **predictor-based personalization** rather than one-size-fits-all application.
The **lack of sleep improvements** contrasts with some clinical taVNS findings and may reflect the already-optimized sleep of trained athletes versus the disrupted sleep of clinical populations, suggesting taVNS may be most beneficial where **baseline functioning is impaired**.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample of **N = 10** is extremely small, even for a pilot study.
No **a priori power analysis** was conducted.
Non-parametric tests were appropriately chosen given the small sample, but statistical power was severely limited, increasing the risk of both **Type II errors** (failing to detect real effects) and **inflated effect sizes** in significant results.
The authors acknowledge this limitation and frame the study as exploratory.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of **non-parametric testing** was appropriate for the small sample size and likely non-normal distributions.
A **responder analysis** was a methodological strength, going beyond group-level averages to examine **individual-level change patterns**.
However, the lack of a **control or sham condition** means that observed changes cannot be attributed to taVNS specifically rather than to training progression, expectancy effects, or repeated testing.
The **three time-point design** (pre, post, follow-up) is adequate for detecting short-term maintenance of effects.

__Effect Sizes__: **Large effect sizes** were reported for positive affect and attention improvements.
However, given the **tiny sample**, these effect size estimates are highly **unstable** and likely overestimated.
No **confidence intervals** for effect sizes were reported, which would have helped convey the uncertainty around these estimates.
The **responder analysis** provided a more nuanced picture of effect heterogeneity than group-level statistics alone.

__Measurement Validity__: The study used a **broad battery of subjective and objective measures** spanning psychological, sleep, and performance domains.
Subjective measures included validated scales for recovery, stress, affect, anxiety, and well-being.
Objective sleep metrics (nocturnal HRV, sleep score via wearable) add ecological validity beyond self-report.
The **absence of blinding** (participants self-administered taVNS) introduces potential **expectancy bias** in subjective outcomes.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint**, supporting open access.
No mention was made of data, code, or materials being publicly available.
The **preregistration status** is unclear from the abstract.

__Participant Transparency__: Participants were **10 healthy adult powerlifters** — a highly specific, homogeneous non-clinical population.
Demographic details (age range, sex distribution, training experience) were not provided in the abstract.
**Exclusion criteria** (e.g., history of psychiatric conditions, medication use) were not specified.
The sample is **not representative** of clinical populations, limiting generalizability to therapy contexts.
