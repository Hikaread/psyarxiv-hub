## Summary

This study (N=120 healthy volunteers) investigated how pre-existing anxiety affects the formation of nocebo hyperalgesia through classical conditioning. Participants were randomized to three groups: anxiety induction plus nocebo conditioning (ANC), nocebo conditioning only (NC), and control. Contrary to the theoretical prediction that anxiety would enhance nocebo effects, pretreatment anxiety actually disrupted the formation of nocebo hyperalgesia. The NC group showed expected nocebo effects and elevated pain expectancies, but the ANC group did not develop nocebo hyperalgesia despite the same conditioning procedure. Neither pain expectancies nor post-treatment anxiety mediated the effect.

## Clinical Insight

This finding has a counterintuitive but clinically important implication for pain therapists: patients who are highly anxious before treatment may not develop the negative pain expectations that typically impair recovery. This challenges the common assumption that anxiety always worsens pain outcomes. However, the anxiety used here was anticipatory shock anxiety rather than pain-related anxiety, so the application requires careful thought. Therapists should consider that the relationship between anxiety and pain response is more complex than a simple linear model, and that a patient's emotional state at the time of initial treatment experiences may shape subsequent pain expectations in non-obvious ways.

## Relevant For

- Pain psychologists and therapists working with chronic pain
- Clinicians using psychoeducation about pain expectations
- Therapists interested in nocebo effects and treatment responses
- Health psychologists working in medical settings
