## Summary

This study estimated the prevalence of social disconnection, depression, and anxiety among 410 Slovak mothers on parental leave (mean age 33.4) using standardized self-report measures. The prevalence of moderately severe to severe depression (PHQ-9 >= 10) was 38.3% and anxiety (GAD-7 >= 10) was 28.3%, with 23.2% experiencing both. Elevated loneliness was reported by 78.3% (UCLA-3 >= 6) and social isolation by 26.8% (LSNS-6 < 12). Regression analysis showed that anxiety and loneliness were the strongest predictors of depressive symptoms, with models explaining 61-62% of variance. Psychological factors were more important than demographic variables, including length of parental leave.

## Clinical Insight

The 38.3% rate of moderately severe to severe depression among mothers on parental leave is strikingly high — more than triple general population prevalence — and underscores that parental leave is a high-risk period for mental health deterioration, not a protective one. For therapists, the strong predictive role of loneliness (rather than demographic factors like leave duration) means that assessing social connection should be a routine part of perinatal mental health work. The finding that 78.3% reported elevated loneliness suggests that social isolation during parental leave is the norm rather than the exception, and therapists should normalize this experience while helping mothers find ways to maintain social connections. The study's context in Slovakia, where parental leave can extend to three years, is relevant: longer leave does not protect against depression and may actually increase isolation. Therapists should be aware that in cultures with extended leave policies, mothers may need proactive outreach rather than assuming the system is providing adequate support.

## Relevant For

Perinatal mental health therapists, those working with postpartum depression and anxiety, and practitioners in healthcare systems with extended parental leave policies
