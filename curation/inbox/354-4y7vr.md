## Summary

Cross-sectional online survey of 410 Slovak biological mothers on maternity/parental leave (M age=33.4, SD=4.61; 88.3% living with a partner; 83.7% university-educated; mean leave duration=34.9 months, SD=28.0, median=27.5, range=1-176 months), recruited October 2025-January 2026 via social media, pediatricians, and maternity centers. Depression was assessed with PHQ-9 and EPDS, anxiety with GAD-7, loneliness with UCLA-3 (3-item), and social isolation with LSNS-6. Prevalence of moderately severe-to-severe depression was 38.3% (PHQ-9 >=10) and 36.6% (EPDS >=13), with strong agreement between measures (McNemar p=.419). Anxiety prevalence was 28.3% (GAD-7 >=10), with 23.2% meeting criteria for both depression and anxiety. Elevated loneliness affected 78.3% (UCLA-3 >=6), while 26.8% were at risk of social isolation (LSNS-6 <12), and 23.6% met criteria for both. Direct Likert measures showed 39.3% felt lonely "sometimes" and 24.1% "most of the time," while only 8.8% reported never feeling lonely. Two multiple linear regression models (controlling for age, SES, education, number of children, and leave length) found anxiety was the strongest predictor of depressive symptoms (PHQ-9: beta=.701, p<.001; EPDS: beta=.647, p<.001), followed by loneliness (PHQ-9: beta=.135, p<.001; EPDS: beta=.173, p<.001). Social isolation showed a small negative association with EPDS (beta=-.111, p<.001). Models explained 61.8% (PHQ-9) and 60.5% (EPDS) of variance. Length of parental leave was not a significant predictor in either model (PHQ-9: beta=-.036; EPDS: beta=.039), nor were number of children or SES.

## Clinical Insight

The 38.3% rate of moderate-to-severe depression among Slovak mothers on parental leave is more than triple the typical postpartum prevalence (~12%) and substantially higher than early postpartum rates previously reported in Slovakia (6.1% shortly after childbirth, 11.6% at 6-8 weeks; Skodova et al., 2021), suggesting that risk accumulates over the course of extended leave rather than being confined to the early postpartum period. For clinicians, several specific implications follow. First, the finding that 78.3% reported elevated loneliness — nearly four in five mothers — indicates that social disconnection during parental leave is normative, not exceptional, and should be proactively assessed rather than waiting for clients to report it. Second, anxiety (beta=.701) was by far the strongest predictor of depression, suggesting that treating comorbid anxiety may be the most efficient route to reducing depressive symptoms in this population. Third, the irrelevance of leave duration to depression (beta=-.036, nonsignificant) directly challenges the assumption that extended leave is protective — in Slovakia, where leave can extend to three years, longer leave does not reduce and may increase isolation risk. Therapists working in systems with extended leave policies should screen for depression and loneliness throughout the entire leave period, not just in the first few months. Fourth, the negative association between social isolation (LSNS-6) and EPDS scores (beta=-.111), alongside the positive association of subjective loneliness (UCLA-3), indicates that perceived loneliness is the more clinically relevant target than objective social network size — mothers can have adequate social networks and still feel lonely, and it is the subjective experience that drives depression.

## Relevant For

Perinatal mental health therapists, those working with postpartum depression and anxiety, practitioners in healthcare systems with extended parental leave policies (especially Central/Eastern European contexts), maternal-child health professionals conducting routine screening

## Notes

Cross-sectional design precludes causal inference; online recruitment may have attracted distressed mothers (selection bias); self-report screening measures may overestimate prevalence relative to clinical interviews; sample is highly educated (83.7% university) and predominantly partnered (88.3%), limiting generalizability to lower-SES or single-mother populations; the mean leave duration of 34.9 months (nearly 3 years) reflects the Slovak policy context and may not apply to countries with shorter leave.
