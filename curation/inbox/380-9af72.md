## Summary

Cross-sectional network analysis of 936 alcohol users (ages 16-81, M=32.1, SD=13.9; 52.6% female) drawn from two merged studies, examining unique associations between social cognition measures and alcohol use severity (AUDIT; M=8.16, SD=6.25, 40.5% at-risk). The 10-node LASSO-regularized Gaussian Graphical Model included the Drinking Motives Questionnaire-Revised (social and conformity subscales), the Social Reward Questionnaire (admiration, negative social potency, passivity, sociability, prosocial interactions), the Social Attunement Questionnaire (cognition and behavior subscales), and AUDIT scores. In the partial correlation network (CS stability=0.75, 20 of 45 edges non-zero), only social drinking motives and SRQ sociability were directly connected to alcohol use severity. SAQ behavior (adjusting behavior to match the social environment) emerged as the most central node across centrality indices. An age-moderated network found that all associations were stable across age except the sociability-AUDIT edge, which weakened in older participants (interaction weight=-0.076, retained in 65% of bootstrap samples). A network comparison test between male (n≈443) and female (n≈488) networks found no differences in global strength (S=0.015, p=0.972), structure (M=0.121, p=0.680), or edge weights, despite males scoring higher on AUDIT, negative social potency, and conformity motives, and females scoring higher on SAQ cognition, sociability, and prosocial interactions. Bivariate Spearman correlations showed positive associations between nearly all social measures and AUDIT, but most did not survive network partialing.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians treating alcohol use disorder, this network study clarifies that broad social engagement (sociability) and social drinking motives are the primary social drivers of alcohol use severity, while more specific social-cognitive factors like social attunement and prosocial tendencies operate indirectly through these pathways. This means that interventions targeting social contexts of drinking (e.g., reducing alcohol-centered social activities, building non-drinking social outlets) may be more impactful than interventions targeting specific social-cognitive deficits like prosocial orientation. The age finding has a direct practical implication: the link between sociability and drinking weakens with age, consistent with the "maturing out" phenomenon, suggesting that younger clients may benefit most from interventions addressing the social reward value of drinking contexts. The absence of sex differences in network structure, despite mean-level differences in scores, implies that therapeutic approaches addressing social drivers of alcohol use need not differ substantially by gender — what differs is prevalence, not mechanism. Clinicians should be cautious about over-interpreting bivariate associations between specific social-cognitive measures and drinking, as the network shows many of these are indirect.

## Relevant For

Addiction therapists, clinicians treating alcohol use disorder, therapists incorporating social cognition into addiction treatment, those working with younger adults whose drinking is socially driven

## Notes

Cross-sectional design; age moderation may reflect cohort effects rather than developmental change; bootstrap stability of the age moderation effect was only moderate; sample overrepresents younger, higher-educated, cisgender individuals; data from two studies with different designs were pooled, including an extreme-groups sample; single author for the manuscript despite multi-author list; network approach adds value over bivariate approaches by isolating unique associations.
