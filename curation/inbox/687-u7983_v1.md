## Summary
This **mixed-methods survey** examined accessibility barriers, participation, and perceived wellbeing impacts among **149 autistic adults** and **97 parents/caregivers** of autistic people across seven life domains in Australia: healthcare, employment, employment support, transport, retail, education and training, and community/leisure activities. Barriers were **widespread**, often experienced across multiple domains, and commonly perceived to have **moderate-to-large impacts on wellbeing**. **Sensory environments** were the most frequently endorsed barrier type. **Reflexive thematic analysis** of 998 open-text responses generated four themes: environments were actively hostile to autistic needs; services were available but not always accessible; access depended on whether people understood autistic needs; and repeated inaccessibility **narrowed everyday life**. The study frames accessibility as a **systemic responsibility** rather than an individual adaptation problem.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians working with autistic individuals, this study reframes accessibility barriers not as occasional inconveniences but as **chronic, compounding stressors** that directly impact mental health. The finding that inaccessibility **narrows everyday life** — reducing participation, increasing avoidance, and depleting energy — connects directly to the concept of **autistic burnout**, a debilitating state of exhaustion and reduced functioning. Clinicians should assess not only internal symptom presentations but also the **accessibility burden** their autistic clients carry: the extra work of planning, explaining needs, recovering from overwhelming environments, and navigating inflexible systems. The neurodiversity-affirming framing shifts clinical responsibility from "helping autistic people cope" with inaccessible environments toward advocating for **environmental and systemic change**. Sensory environments emerged as the most common barrier, suggesting that sensory-focused clinical interventions should be paired with environmental advocacy.

## Methodology Note
This is a cross-sectional mixed-methods survey study.

__Design__: Mixed-methods online survey with quantitative barrier ratings and qualitative open-text responses.
149 autistic adults (self-reporting) and 97 parents/caregivers (reporting on behalf of autistic person).
Seven life domains assessed with both structured and open-text items.
998 open-text responses analyzed via reflexive thematic analysis.

__Measurement Validity__: Survey covered seven practical life domains, enhancing ecological validity.
Quantitative barrier impact ratings provided structured data alongside qualitative themes.
Reflexive thematic analysis is appropriate for the exploratory qualitative component.
No validated accessibility measure was used; domain-specific items appear to have been developed for this study.

__Limitations__: Cross-sectional design; cannot establish causal relationships between barriers and wellbeing.
Parent/caregiver proxy reports may not accurately reflect autistic individuals' experiences.
Convenience sampling through autism organizations may not capture the most isolated individuals.
Australian-specific context (National Autism Strategy 2025–2031) may limit international generalizability.
Autism diagnosis not independently verified.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
Ethics approval obtained from Griffith University (2025/950).
Data not publicly available due to sensitive open-text responses and identification risk.

## Relevant For

## Notes
The concept of "access burden" — the cumulative work of navigating inaccessible environments — is a clinically useful framework. The finding that barriers compound across domains rather than being isolated incidents has direct implications for understanding why autistic individuals experience elevated mental health difficulties.
