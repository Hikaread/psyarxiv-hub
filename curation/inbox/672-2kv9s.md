## Summary
This paper presents **evidence-informed, co-produced clinical guidelines** for adapting **eating disorder focused family therapy (FT-ED)** for **Autistic children and young people** with **anorexia nervosa (AN)**. The guidelines were developed through a series of workshops involving Autistic individuals with lived experience of eating disorders, parents and carers, clinicians, researchers, and third-sector representatives. Autistic young people receiving FT-ED are more likely than non-Autistic peers to require **longer and more intensive treatment**, and qualitative evidence indicates that both families and clinicians struggle with the perceived **inflexibility of standard FT-ED delivery**, which may be mis-attuned to autistic needs. Clinicians report a **lack of confidence** in adapting FT-ED for this population, and prior to these guidelines, **limited structured guidance** existed. The resulting recommendations aim to support clinicians in delivering more **autism-affirming** and effective care, and may also help young people and families advocate for appropriate adaptations within FT-ED.

## Clinical Insight
These guidelines are directly actionable for clinicians working in **eating disorder services** who treat Autistic young people — a population that is overrepresented in ED settings yet frequently poorly served by standard protocols. The co-production model, centering Autistic lived experience alongside clinical and research expertise, provides a template for how **neurodiversity-affirming adaptations** can be systematically developed rather than left to ad hoc individual clinician decisions. Key clinical implications include the need to **flexibly adapt session structure**, accommodate **sensory sensitivities** in the therapeutic environment, use more **concrete and literal communication**, and recognize that **family dynamics** may require different framing when autism is part of the clinical picture. The guidelines also implicitly support **psychoeducation** for families about the intersection of autism and eating disorders, which may reduce caregiver frustration and improve engagement.
