## Summary
This study validated the **English translation** of the 11-item **Social Attunement Questionnaire (SAQ)** and examined its associations with **substance use**, **impulsivity**, and **sensation seeking** across the adult lifespan (N = 449, 54.5% female, age range 16-61, M = 33.86, SD = 12.04). The SAQ measures the tendency to **harmonize with and adapt to the social environment** to find social reward in the absence of explicit group pressure, consistent with the **social plasticity hypothesis** (Cousijn et al., 2018).

**Confirmatory factor analysis** supported the original two-factor structure (Cognition subscale, 5 items; Behavior subscale, 6 items) with acceptable to good fit (RMSEA = .068, CFI = .959, TLI = .947). Internal consistency was good for the full scale (alpha = .82, omega = .83), Cognition (alpha = .75, omega = .75), and Behavior (alpha = .78, omega = .78). The SAQ demonstrated good **measurement invariance to sex** across configural, metric, scalar, and strict levels. No **sex differences** were found in SAQ scores. Younger age was associated with higher SAQ scores.

SAQ full-scale scores — guided by the **Behavior subscale** — were positively associated with **AUDIT** scores (driven by alcohol-related problems, AUDIT-P), **CUDIT-R** scores, and **IMPSS impulsivity**. The Cognition subscale showed no significant associations with substance use or impulsivity outcomes. **Age moderated** the association between SAQ and alcohol consumption (B = -.003, p = .036) and between SAQ and sensation seeking (B = -.003, p = .013): associations were positive in younger individuals but became more negative with increasing age. The alcohol moderation effect would not survive Bonferroni correction (p = .108). Age did not moderate SAQ-cannabis associations. Data were analyzed using **Spearman correlations** and **multiple linear regression** with moderation in JASP 0.19.3.

## Clinical Insight
The SAQ captures a construct — **social attunement** — that is distinct from traditional measures of **peer pressure** and **conformity**, and its age-dependent associations with substance use have direct clinical implications. The finding that high social attunement is associated with greater **alcohol use** and **sensation seeking** in younger individuals, but these associations reverse with age, supports the **social plasticity hypothesis**: social attunement may facilitate both the escalation and de-escalation of substance use across development. Clinically, this suggests that high social attunement in adolescence could be a **risk factor** for substance use escalation, but the same trait may become a **protective factor** in adulthood when social norms shift away from heavy use. The SAQ could help clinicians identify adolescents whose substance use is socially driven and who may benefit from **social skills interventions**. The distinction between the Cognition and Behavior subscales is clinically relevant: the Behavior subscale appears more consequential for substance use than the Cognition subscale.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric paper.

__Measurement Validity__: The two-factor CFA model showed acceptable to good fit (RMSEA = .068, CFI = .959, TLI = .947).
The one-factor model showed substantially worse fit (RMSEA = .094, CFI = .920), supporting the two-subscale structure.
Good measurement invariance across sex (all levels including strict).
Internal consistency is adequate to good across all scales.
The SAQ Cognition subscale showed no associations with most outcomes, raising questions about its incremental validity in this sample.

__Open Science__: Data available on OSF (https://osf.io/mdpgb/overview).
Analyses conducted in JASP 0.19.3, which is open-source.
Translation procedure (forward translation, team review, native speaker check) is clearly described but informal.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 449 after exclusion of 108 participants (17 failed catch questions, 91 inconsistent responding).
Highly educated sample (92.8% high education).
96.2% reported lifetime alcohol use, 67.9% lifetime cannabis use, reflecting recruitment targeting substance users.
Multiple nationalities represented, though predominantly European.
Two participants with non-binary sex at birth were excluded from sex-based analyses.
