## Summary
**Quasi-experimental study** (N=249 first-year psychology students) examining whether **online vs in-person briefing** for **Experience Sampling Method** (ESM/EMA) protocols affects data quantity (**compliance**), data quality (**careless responding**), and participant experiences (**burden**, **perceived briefing sufficiency**). Participants completed a 7-day ESM protocol with 10 daily prompts assessing affect, thoughts, and context. Results showed that briefing modality did not significantly affect mean levels of compliance, careless responding, participant burden, or perceived briefing sufficiency. However, a significant **time-by-modality interaction** emerged for compliance: the online briefing group started with significantly higher compliance that declined steeply over the study period, while the in-person group started lower but maintained more stable compliance across days. Both groups converged to similar compliance levels by day 7. **Careless responding** showed temporal increases across both conditions (based on even-odd item correlations and response time variability), while momentary burden actually decreased over time — suggesting participants adapted their response strategies rather than experiencing increasing fatigue. No significant modality effects were found for careless responding, burden, or briefing sufficiency trajectories.

## Clinical Insight
For clinical researchers using **EMA** to study daily-life processes in depression, anxiety, psychosis, or suicidality, these findings support **online briefing** as a viable and practical alternative to in-person briefing, particularly for short (7-day) protocols with relatively straightforward questionnaires. This is practically significant because online briefing reduces researcher workload, travel costs, and scheduling barriers, while potentially enabling recruitment of geographically distributed or clinically complex populations who cannot attend in-person sessions. However, the steeper compliance decline in the online group suggests that for longer ESM protocols — which are increasingly common in **precision psychiatry** and personalized modeling (average 12.4 days across 482 ESM studies) — in-person briefing may better sustain engagement. Clinical EMA studies with **depressed**, **anxious**, or **psychotic** patients (who may have lower motivation, cognitive difficulties, or digital literacy) should consider in-person briefing given the higher demands on these populations. Regardless of briefing modality, the temporal increases in careless responding underscore the need for ongoing engagement strategies (reminders, gamification, incremental incentives) beyond the initial briefing.

## Relevant For
- Clinical researchers designing EMA protocols for mood, anxiety, and psychosis
- Precision psychiatry researchers using intensive longitudinal data
- Clinical trialists incorporating experience sampling as outcome measures
- Research coordinators managing participant onboarding for EMA studies

## Notes
Quasi-experimental design (modality determined by recruitment phase, not random assignment). Sample limited to Belgian psychology students (predominantly female). Key limitation: power analysis targeted overall compliance, not time-by-modality interactions. Average ESM study is 12.4 days — much longer than the 7-day protocol tested here.
