## Summary
This study investigates continuous traumatic stress (CTS) in Ukrainian civilians affected by the Russo-Ukrainian War, comparing those displaced to Austria with those remaining in Ukraine. With 113 participants, the study found that CTS is a strong predictor of multiple psychopathological outcomes including depression, anxiety, PTSD, and disturbances in self-organization. Emotion dysregulation showed small but present moderation effects on the CTS-psychopathology relationship. Surprisingly, participants in Austria reported higher symptom levels than those in Ukraine, and questionnaire-derived diagnostic rates were elevated throughout the sample.

## Clinical Insight
The CTS framework is particularly valuable for therapists because it captures the reality of clients from ongoing conflict zones where traditional PTSD models (which assume a past event) may not fully apply. Therapists should consider that the act of displacement may introduce additional psychological burden beyond the war exposure itself, possibly through loss of identity, social network disruption, and the ambiguity of temporary refuge status. Treatment planning should integrate emotion regulation strategies alongside trauma processing, and clinicians should not assume that physical safety alone resolves traumatic stress responses.

## Relevant For
Trauma therapists, refugee mental health services, clinicians working with displaced populations, supervisors in cross-cultural therapy contexts.

## Notes
Cross-sectional design limits causal inference. Sample was predominantly female (87.6%). Single-author study. Small sample size for the moderation analysis.
