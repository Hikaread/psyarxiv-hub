## Summary
This master's thesis empirically validates the construct of continuous traumatic stress (CTS) in 113 Ukrainian civilians affected by the Russo-Ukrainian War, comparing displaced individuals in Austria (n=67) with those remaining in Ukraine (n=46). Using ICD-11-aligned measures (ITQ, IDQ, IAQ) and the CTSR scale developed for Ukrainian war contexts, the study tested three hypotheses: (1) CTS predicts depression, anxiety, PTSD, and DSO symptoms; (2) emotion dysregulation moderates these associations; (3) displaced civilians report fewer symptoms than those in Ukraine. Hypothesis 1 was strongly supported, with CTS explaining 28-47% of variance across outcomes, substantially exceeding the variance added by geographical exposure (0.1-1%). Hypothesis 2 was partially supported: ED significantly interacted with CTS for anxiety, PTSD, and DSO outcomes, but the interactions were small (1-4% additional variance) and in a negative direction — meaning higher ED actually weakened, rather than strengthened, the CTS-psychopathology association. Johnson-Neyman analyses showed that CTS effects on these outcomes became non-significant above specific ED thresholds. Hypothesis 3 was rejected: participants in Austria reported significantly higher PTSD and DSO symptoms and more traumatic exposures than those in Ukraine. Exploratory analyses revealed that 66.4% of the sample met criteria for at least one ICD-11 disorder, with CPTSD prevalence significantly higher among displaced participants (37.3% vs. 13.0%).

## Clinical Insight
The finding that CTS explains large unique variance in psychopathological outcomes beyond standard ICD-11 diagnostic measures is the most clinically significant result of this study. It suggests that the ongoing, present-tense quality of traumatic stress — as opposed to the past-tense focus of PTSD — captures a dimension of suffering that standard trauma assessments miss. Practically, therapists working with war-affected clients should consider adding CTS assessment (using the Ukrainian-validated CTSR) to their clinical formulation. The counterintuitive finding that displaced participants fared worse psychologically than those remaining in the conflict zone challenges the common assumption that physical safety automatically reduces traumatic stress. For therapists, this means that treatment should address the unique stressors of displacement: loss of social roles and professional identity, uncertainty about legal status and duration of stay, language barriers, and the guilt of being "safe" while others remain in danger. The ED moderation finding requires nuanced clinical interpretation: rather than ED amplifying CTS effects as expected, high ED appeared to saturate distress levels, creating a ceiling effect where ongoing threat adds relatively little. This may indicate that for highly dysregulated clients, trauma processing approaches that assume a linear relationship between stressor exposure and symptom severity need adjustment.

## Relevant For
Trauma therapists working with refugees and displaced persons, clinicians using ICD-11 diagnostic frameworks, supervisors in cross-cultural trauma therapy, mental health professionals in organizations serving displaced Ukrainians.

## Notes
Master's thesis (single author, University of Vienna, 2026). Cross-sectional design limits causal inference. Sample predominantly female (87.6%) and highly educated (80.5% university education). Preregistered on OSF. DERS-Awareness subscale excluded per established practice. No formal psychiatric diagnoses — only questionnaire-derived. The Ukrainian CTSR showed a two-factor structure (Exhaustion and Rage; Fear and Betrayal) differing from the original English three-factor structure.
