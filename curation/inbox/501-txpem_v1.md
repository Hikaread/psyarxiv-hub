## Summary
Adults and youth are increasingly confiding in generative AI chatbots about suicidal thoughts, yet no standardized guidance exists for how these systems should respond to suicide risk disclosures. This study developed a consensus statement to address this critical gap, employing a structured expert consensus process. The resulting taxonomy catalogs potential harms and benefits of AI chatbot responses to suicidal users, providing a framework for responsible design and deployment of conversational AI in mental health contexts.

The consensus process engaged experts in clinical psychology, suicidology, and AI safety to identify key principles for chatbot responses. The taxonomy distinguishes between responses that may reduce risk (e.g., providing crisis resources, validating emotional distress, encouraging connection with support) and those that may increase harm (e.g., providing harmful suggestions, minimizing distress, creating false therapeutic expectations). The statement addresses the unique challenge that AI chatbots operate without human clinical judgment while being accessible 24/7 to individuals in crisis.

## Clinical Insight
As AI chatbots become a first point of contact for individuals in suicidal crisis, this consensus statement provides a timely and much-needed framework for responsible design. For clinicians, the taxonomy offers a reference for evaluating which AI tools are safe to recommend to patients and what safeguards should be in place. The statement also highlights the importance of ensuring AI systems provide clear pathways to human support rather than attempting to provide therapeutic interventions themselves.

The gap between existing AI capabilities and safe clinical practice is significant. Clinicians should be aware that patients may already be using AI chatbots for crisis support and should proactively discuss the limitations and risks of these tools. The consensus statement can inform organizational policies about AI tool adoption and patient education about safe technology use during mental health crises.

## Relevant For
Suicidologists, clinical psychologists working with high-risk patients, AI developers in mental health, and healthcare organizations developing AI use policies.
