## Summary
This study examined whether **within-session emotion coherence** — the alignment between self-reported mood and automated facial expression coding — predicts **subsequent depression scores** in psychotherapy. Using **multilevel Response Surface Analysis (ML-RSA)** in 65 patients with **major depressive disorder** (205 sessions), the study tested whether congruence between subjective and expressed negative affect forecasted symptom change. Greater coherence predicted **lower subsequent depressive symptoms**, while increasing discrepancies were associated with **worse outcomes** (curvature along incongruence, a4). Crucially, the effect was **directional**: sessions where patients were "feeling more than showing" (higher self-reported than facially expressed negative affect) were **particularly predictive of worsening depression** (slope a3). These effects were **moderated by expressive suppression**: among high suppressors, the coherence-outcome link remained pronounced, and coherence became more informative overall — jointly elevated reported and expressed negative affect predicted lower depression, while jointly low affect predicted higher depression.

## Clinical Insight
This study establishes **emotional coherence** as a measurable **process variable** in psychotherapy that carries prognostic significance. For therapists, the finding that "feeling more than showing" negative emotion predicts worsening depression is particularly clinically meaningful — it may reflect **alexithymic tendencies**, **emotional inhibition**, or a breakdown in **communicative signaling** that impedes the therapeutic work. The moderation by **expressive suppression** suggests that for patients who habitually suppress emotion, the relationship between inner experience and outward expression becomes a more critical marker of therapeutic progress. Clinicians could theoretically use automated facial coding (though not yet practical in routine settings) or simply attend to **incongruence between verbal report and observed affect** as a clinical signal. The ML-RSA approach is methodologically sophisticated, capturing both the degree and direction of coherence-incoherence simultaneously rather than treating them as a single dimension.
