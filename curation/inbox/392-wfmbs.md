## Summary
This comprehensive systematic review synthesizes 219 resting-state fMRI studies of traumatic brain injury, covering the full spectrum from civilian mild TBI to severe injury and sports-related impacts. The review identifies methodological heterogeneity in data processing pipelines as the primary barrier to reaching consensus about network-level changes after TBI. The authors provide a structured analysis of how different approaches to data cleaning, denoising, and brain parcellation affect findings, offering guidance for future research and clinical application.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists working with TBI survivors, understanding the neural network level of injury helps explain the constellation of cognitive, emotional, and behavioural symptoms that often persist long after the acute injury phase. The review's finding of significant methodological variability across studies helps clinicians understand why neuroimaging results may not always align with observed functional impairments, and why treatment planning should rely on comprehensive clinical assessment rather than imaging alone. This is particularly relevant when working with clients who have mild TBI where conventional imaging often appears normal despite significant functional complaints.

## Relevant For
Therapists in brain injury rehabilitation, neuropsychologists, clinicians assessing post-concussion syndrome.

## Notes
Review covers 219 studies but does not provide a meta-analytic effect size. The focus is on methodology rather than treatment. Large multi-author collaboration suggesting this is a major effort in the field.
