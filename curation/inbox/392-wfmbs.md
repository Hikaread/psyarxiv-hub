Key patterns: the default mode network (DMN) showed the most heterogeneous findings (acute hyperconnectivity in sports, hypoconnectivity in civilian mTBI), the frontoparietal network showed the most uniform pattern (hypoconnectivity across civilian, moderate-severe, and military cohorts), and the salience network was strongly context-dependent. clinical_insight: For therapists working with TBI survivors, this review clarifies why neuroimaging results from different patients can appear contradictory — the same brain region may show hyperconnectivity in one study and hypoconnectivity in another because of differences in time since injury, injury severity, and analytic method, not because the science is unreliable. More practically, the review identifies rsfMRI as a promising future tool for prognosis and treatment stratification: if a patient's network connectivity profile matches patterns associated with good recovery, they may need less intensive intervention; if it shows patterns linked to persistent symptoms, earlier and more targeted treatment may be warranted. The finding that DMN changes are the most variable and population-dependent aligns with clinical observations that memory, self-referential thinking, and internal narrative processing are often the most disrupted aspects of post-TBI life. Therapists can use this knowledge to explain to clients why their cognitive symptoms may differ from others with similar injuries, and to normalize that variability as reflecting the brain's dynamic recovery process rather than a fixed deficit.

## Summary
This large-scale systematic review synthesizes 219 resting-state fMRI studies of traumatic brain injury across six distinct populations (civilian mTBI, moderate-severe TBI, sports-related, military, pediatric, and IPV-related), remapping all findings onto a common 13-network cortical atlas to enable cross-study comparison for the first time. The review addresses the field's central challenge — apparent inconsistency in findings — by identifying three key moderators: time since injury (the single most important), population and injury severity, and analytical methodology (ICA vs. seed-based vs. graph theory approaches, which produce systematically different results from the same data). The review documents coherent network-level patterns: the default mode network is the most heterogeneous and population-sensitive, the frontoparietal network is the most consistently hypoconnected, and the salience network varies most with clinical context. A non-monotonic recovery trajectory is described for moderate-to-severe TBI, progressing from acute disconnection through compensatory hyperconnectivity to either normalization or persistent disconnection. The review includes AI-assisted data extraction using NotebookLM and Claude (verified against primary sources) and proposes specific recommendations for the field including adoption of shared processing pipelines, longitudinal designs with multimodal measures, and data sharing through existing repositories.

## Clinical Insight
This review's most clinically actionable finding is that the apparent chaos in the rsfMRI-TBI literature resolves into coherent patterns when organized properly. For therapists, this means that a client's neuroimaging results should not be interpreted in isolation but in the context of their injury-to-assessment interval and population characteristics. A patient scanned at 2 months post-injury with DMN hypoconnectivity may be on a very different trajectory than one scanned at 18 months. The review's emphasis on time-since-injury as the dominant moderator has a direct formulation implication: therapists should obtain the date of injury and map the assessment timeline when interpreting neuroimaging reports, as the clinical meaning of connectivity changes shifts substantially over the first 1-2 years. The identification of consistent frontoparietal hypoconnectivity across populations suggests that executive function deficits may be the most robust neurological marker of TBI across diverse injury types, which can guide cognitive rehabilitation planning. The practical barriers to clinical translation — methodological variability and small sample sizes — are also important for therapists to understand when evaluating the reliability of neuroimaging referrals.

## Relevant For
Neuropsychologists, therapists in brain injury rehabilitation, clinical researchers, clinicians interpreting neuroimaging reports for TBI clients, rehabilitation specialists.

## Notes
Systematic review — no primary data collected. 23-author collaboration. The review covers studies up to the search date but acknowledges rapid publication growth may have missed recent work. IPV-related TBI had only 2 studies, representing a major gap. The common-network remapping approach enables comparison but loses some spatial specificity available in the original studies. No meta-analysis of effect sizes was conducted due to methodological heterogeneity preventing meaningful quantitative synthesis.
