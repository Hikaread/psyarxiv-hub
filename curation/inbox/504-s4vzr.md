## Summary

This **systematic review** examined how **sustained and skill-developed meditation practices** shape well-being, interpersonal relationships, and emotion regulation, following **PRISMA guidelines**. A systematic search of **PubMed, MEDLINE, PsycINFO, and Embase** (April 5, 2026) identified **1,229 articles**, of which **52 met inclusion criteria**. The review synthesizes both qualitative and quantitative findings.

The evidence suggests that **long-term and advanced meditators** experience greater well-being outcomes than those observed in more basic practice, including deeper **relational attunement** and more refined **emotional capacities**. Critically, these outcomes appear associated with **skill-based proficiency** rather than practice duration alone. However, the field's continued reliance on **temporal criteria** for defining advanced practice, combined with **measurement tools poorly suited** to more experienced practitioners, may obscure these deeper benefits. The authors argue that realizing meditation's full transformative potential requires reorienting research toward investigating **meditative skills, stages, and attainments** in relation to well-being, rather than simply counting hours of practice.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians who incorporate **mindfulness or meditation** into their work, this review highlights a significant gap between how meditation research is typically conducted and how experienced practitioners actually describe their practice. The finding that **skill proficiency matters more than practice duration** suggests that clinicians should focus on the **quality and depth** of meditation engagement rather than simply prescribing more practice time. The review also points to the limitations of standard **self-report measures** (e.g., FFMQ, MAAS) which may fail to capture the more subtle, advanced capacities that long-term practitioners develop—particularly in the domains of **interpersonal attunement** and refined **emotion regulation**. Clinicians working with meditation-informed therapies should be aware that the evidence base for long-term practice outcomes remains methodologically limited.

## Methodology Note

This is a meta_analysis paper (systematic review).

__Statistical Power__: Not applicable for a systematic review without meta-analytic effect size pooling.

__Preregistration__: The review followed **PRISMA guidelines** but no explicit preregistration protocol (e.g., PROSPERO) is mentioned in the available abstract.

__Effect Sizes__: No meta-analytic effect sizes are reported; findings are synthesized qualitatively and quantitatively in narrative form.

__Statistical Rigor__: The review followed established systematic review methodology.
Searches were conducted across four major databases (PubMed, MEDLINE, PsycINFO, Embase).
From 1,229 initial records, 52 studies met inclusion criteria—a focused selection that suggests specific inclusion criteria.
The abstract does not report whether risk of bias was formally assessed (e.g., using Cochrane ROBINS, AXIS, or CASP tools).
Without access to the full text, the specific analytic approach (narrative synthesis vs. meta-analysis) and quality assessment methods cannot be fully evaluated.

__Measurement Validity__: A key finding of the review itself is that existing **measurement tools are poorly suited** to capture the outcomes of advanced meditation practice.
Standard mindfulness measures (e.g., FFMQ, MAAS) were designed for novice populations and may have ceiling effects or construct irrelevance for long-term practitioners.
This measurement limitation is both a finding and a methodological concern for the field.

__Open Science__: As a preprint systematic review, data availability depends on the individual included studies.
The review itself does not appear to have a published protocol or analysis plan available.

__Participant Transparency__: The 52 included studies cover diverse meditation traditions and populations.
The abstract notes that the field relies heavily on **temporal criteria** (hours/years of practice) to define "advanced" meditation, which the authors critique as potentially inadequate.
Specific demographic characteristics of the aggregated participant pool cannot be assessed from the abstract alone.
