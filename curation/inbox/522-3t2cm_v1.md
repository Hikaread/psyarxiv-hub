## Summary

This study, accepted for publication in **Nature Mental Health**, applies **Bayesian change point models** to four waves of **ABCD Study** data (N = 11,868 youths aged 9-10 at baseline) to examine whether covariation among higher-order **psychopathology dimensions** reflects comorbidity. The authors introduce the concept of **nomorbidity** — shared absence of symptoms — and demonstrate that it, not comorbidity, drives the observed correlations. At Wave 1, a 1-point increase in **internalizing** at asymptomatic levels was associated with a 32% increase in **externalizing** (B = .28), but only a 7% increase at higher symptom levels (B = .07), a 4.6-fold difference. **One-change point negative binomial models** consistently outperformed linear models across all six pairwise dimension comparisons (externalizing, internalizing, **attention problems**, **thought problems**) and all four waves. The change point consistently fell at 2-3 endorsed symptoms, well below clinical thresholds. These findings challenge the interpretation of the **p-factor** and covariance-based structural models of psychopathology as capturing meaningful comorbidity.

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: The use of **Bayesian change point models** with **negative binomial link functions** represents a methodologically sophisticated approach that directly tests whether relationships between psychopathology dimensions are constant across the symptom severity spectrum. The enormous sample size (N = 11,868) across four developmental waves provides exceptional statistical power. The replication of findings across all wave-by-dimension pairs, across reversed predictor-criterion orderings, and in a supplementary analysis of caregiver **Adult Self-Report** data greatly strengthens conclusions. The inclusion of a **simulation study** demonstrating bias in linear models under nonlinearity is a valuable methodological contribution. The paper includes thorough robustness checks including **generalised additive multilevel models** as an alternative estimation approach.

__Limitation__: The study relies on **caregiver-reported CBCL** symptom counts, which may not capture the full clinical picture and could introduce reporter bias. The **ABCD Study** is a community-based sample with predominantly low symptom levels (81% nonclinical), and the authors acknowledge limited power to examine associations at the most severe levels. The analyses focus on **higher-order dimensions** (sum scores) rather than conducting factor analyses, though subscale-level analyses in the appendix show consistent patterns. The change point models identify where slopes shift but do not explain why they shift. The clinical implications are inferred rather than directly tested in clinical populations.

## Clinical Insight

This study has profound implications for how clinicians interpret research on the **structure of psychopathology**. The **p-factor**, often cited as evidence for a general liability to mental disorder, may primarily reflect a statistical artifact arising from the inclusion of predominantly **asymptomatic** individuals in community samples. At clinically relevant symptom levels, psychopathology dimensions become **more differentiated** rather than more overlapping — a patient with severe depression does not necessarily have elevated externalizing or thought disorder symptoms. This supports the clinical utility of **disorder-focused assessments** and treatments rather than purely transdiagnostic approaches. For clinicians, the key takeaway is that **covariance-based research findings** — including factor structures derived from community samples — may not accurately describe the relationships between disorders at the severity levels encountered in clinical practice. The study reinforces the importance of **individualized case formulation** over categorical or purely dimensional classification.
