## Summary
This **qualitative case study** examines the **intergenerational transmission of trauma** within **LGBTQ+ communities** through structured storytelling encounters between three dyads of LGBTQ+ elders and youth. Drawing on Robyn Fivush's theoretical framework of intergenerational narrative and trauma narration, the study analyses how storytelling can function as a **developmental resource** promoting healing, connection, and **collective continuity**, or alternatively how it can **impede healing, perpetuate harm**, and silence participants. Three cases illustrate distinct dynamics: Case 1 ("shared voice") showed **mutual vulnerability and generativity** where both elder and youth shared trauma and wisdom, producing a sense of collective strength; Case 2 ("self-voice, other-silence") demonstrated how an elder's unprocessed trauma dominated the conversation, **burdening and silencing the youth's** own story; Case 3 ("other-voice") revealed the most harmful pattern, where an elder transmitted **internalized homophobia and transphobia**, misgendered the youth, and advocated for closeted identity — yet even here, the youth engaged in **"eldering"** by offering wisdom back to the elder. The authors discuss how **cultural and internalized anti-LGBTQ+ stigma**, HIV/AIDS trauma, and the queering of the concept of "elderhood" (where youth may hold more identity integration than recently-out elders) shape these intergenerational dynamics.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists working with LGBTQ+ clients, this study illuminates the complex role that **intergenerational trauma narratives** play in identity development and mental health. Several clinical implications emerge. First, the three-case framework provides a useful schema for understanding how trauma sharing unfolds in therapeutic or community settings: **shared voice** (mutual, generative), **self-voice/other-silence** (one party dominates, burdening the other), and **other-voice** (unprocessed trauma imposed on the listener). Clinicians can use this to assess whether a client's intergenerational relationships are serving healing or harm functions. Second, the concept of **"eldering" from youth to elder** — where younger LGBTQ+ individuals who have grown up in more accepting contexts offer wisdom about identity integration to older individuals carrying internalized stigma — is particularly relevant for therapists working across generational lines within LGBTQ+ communities. Third, the study highlights how **internalized stigma** can be transmitted intergenerationally through well-meaning advice (e.g., "you don't have to tell anybody you're gay"), which may appear protective but actually reinforces shame and avoidance. Clinicians should be attuned to whether clients have received such messages and help them differentiate protective wisdom from internalized oppression. Fourth, the finding that some elders took up too much conversational space mirrors dynamics that can occur in therapy, reminding clinicians of the importance of **creating space for mutual meaning-making** rather than unidirectional transmission.

## Methodology Note
Paper type: qualitative.

__Statistical Rigor__: SKIP for qualitative.

__Measurement Validity__: The study used a **dyadic qualitative design** with three LGBTQ+ elder-youth dyads who engaged in a lab-based storytelling encounter, followed by individual interviews. Data analysis used Fivush's (2004) framework of self-voice, other-voice, self-silence, and other-silence as an analytic lens, combined with thematic narrative analysis. The case study approach provides rich, contextualized data but limits generalisability. The authors acknowledge the limitation of interpreting stories as traumatic when participants may not agree with that framing.

__Open Science__: Materials and preregistration for the larger project are available on OSF (osf.io/b7qhm/). However, the qualitative data cannot be shared due to confidentiality concerns (not possible to fully de-identify).

__Participant Transparency__: Three dyads of LGBTQ+ elders and youth were recruited for the larger project. Specific demographic details (ages, racial/ethnic identities, sexual orientations, gender identities) are provided for each case within the paper. The cases represent diverse intersections of identity — including race (Black participants), gender identity (trans, agender), and cultural background (Southeast Asian immigrant). The small sample (three dyads) is appropriate for the in-depth case study methodology but limits broader generalisability.
