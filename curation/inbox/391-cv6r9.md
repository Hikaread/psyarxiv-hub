## Summary
This study compared menstrual health experiences between 153 South Asian and 153 White British women living in the UK, examining menstrual experiences, menstrual literacy, stigma-related factors, and ethnic microaggressions in healthcare contexts. The research addresses a gap in understanding how cultural norms shape women's health experiences and their interactions with healthcare systems. Findings have implications for how therapists and healthcare providers approach menstrual health discussions with women from different cultural backgrounds.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists, this study highlights that menstrual health is not merely a medical issue but a culturally embedded experience that can affect psychological wellbeing, help-seeking behaviour, and therapeutic rapport. Cultural stigma around menstruation may interact with other presenting concerns such as anxiety, depression, or sexual dysfunction. Therapists should be mindful that clients from South Asian backgrounds may have experienced microaggressions in healthcare settings, which could affect their willingness to disclose menstrual or sexual health concerns in therapy.

## Relevant For
Therapists working with diverse women, sexual health therapists, trainees in multicultural counselling.

## Notes
Equal-sized samples of South Asian and White British women in the UK. The study focuses on menstrual health attitudes rather than clinical disorder, but has direct relevance to therapeutic assessment and cultural competence.
