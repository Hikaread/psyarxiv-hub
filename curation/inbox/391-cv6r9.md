## Summary
This preregistered study (N=306, equal South Asian and White British women) provides a comprehensive comparison of menstrual health across four domains: experiences, literacy, stigma, and healthcare microaggressions. The most striking finding was the large effect for avoidance behaviors: South Asian women reported significantly greater avoidance in 8 of 11 domains assessed, including working outside the home, exercise, cooking, housekeeping, talking to men about menstruation, seeking support from both male and female health professionals, engaging in prayers, and sexual intimacy (partial eta-squared = .319). This avoidance was largely mediated by menstrual secrecy (not by internalized loathing) and by lower menstrual health literacy. South Asian women also reported higher pain-related menstrual distress, lower menstrual health literacy (less likely to attribute menstruation to physiological causes), and more frequent ethnic microaggressions in healthcare settings. Contrary to simplistic expectations, South Asian women reported more positive emotions toward menstruation than White British women, suggesting their cultural framing of menstruation may be more accepting in principle even while behavioral avoidance remains high. Mediation analyses confirmed that menstrual literacy and secrecy are key mechanistic pathways between cultural background and stigma outcomes.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists, this study reframes menstrual stigma in South Asian women as fundamentally a behavioral avoidance problem driven by secrecy and literacy gaps, rather than an attitudinal problem of disgust or shame. This distinction matters for treatment: psychoeducation that normalizes menstruation physiologically may be more effective than approaches targeting emotional attitudes alone. The healthcare microaggression findings are particularly concerning — if South Asian women are already avoiding male healthcare providers and experiencing microaggressions in medical settings, this creates a compounding barrier where both the cultural secrecy and the healthcare system itself reinforce avoidance of necessary medical care. Therapists should be aware that South Asian women may need support specifically for navigating gynaecological appointments, and that asking about menstrual health should be approached with cultural sensitivity. The large effect size for avoidance behaviors (partial eta-squared = .319) indicates this is not a minor cultural difference but a substantial clinical concern affecting multiple domains of daily functioning.

## Relevant For
Therapists working with women from South Asian backgrounds, sexual health therapists, couple therapists, gynaecological counsellors, clinicians working on cultural competence.

## Notes
Preregistered study but self-report only (social desirability bias possible). The "South Asian" category is heterogeneous — national, religious, and linguistic diversity within this group was not assessed. Online recruitment via Prolific may underrepresent women with very traditional views who avoid online surveys about menstruation. Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference. Some menstrual distress subscales showed low internal validity in factor analyses. No assessment of gynecological conditions that could explain pain differences.
