## Summary
This study employed a **computational model of momentary mood** within a gambling task to dissociate the roles of **reward prediction error (RPE)** in depression and anxiety across **2,043 participants** in five experiments.
Using a **bifactor model** to separate anxiety-specific and depression-specific traits from a shared general factor, the study tested how each dimension relates to RPE-based mood fluctuations.
**Depression** was associated with **dampened mood fluctuations** due to **mood hyposensitivity to RPE** — depressed individuals showed blunted emotional responses to unexpected rewards and losses.
This pattern was replicated in **patients with affective disorders**, confirming clinical relevance.
**Anxiety** was associated with **heightened mood fluctuations** stemming from **mood hypersensitivity to RPE** in non-clinical participants — anxious individuals showed amplified emotional reactions to unexpected outcomes.
The **shared depression/anxiety component** was linked to **lower affective baseline** (chronically lower mood) and **greater risk aversion**.
The study presents **multi-dimensional computational signatures** that dissociate depression from anxiety using RPE-based mood modeling.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides a **computational dissociation** of depression and anxiety that has direct implications for clinical assessment and treatment.
The finding that depression involves **mood hyposensitivity to RPE** (blunted responses to outcomes) helps explain the **emotional flatness and anhedonia** that characterize depression, and suggests that treatment should focus on **restoring sensitivity to positive outcomes**.
The finding that anxiety involves **mood hypersensitivity to RPE** (amplified reactions to outcomes) helps explain the **emotional volatility and unpredictability** that characterize anxiety disorders.
For clinicians, these computational signatures could eventually serve as **objective biomarkers** distinguishing depression-predominant from anxiety-predominant presentations, particularly in mixed presentations where comorbidity makes differential diagnosis challenging.
The **risk aversion** finding for the shared depression/anxiety component suggests that interventions targeting **behavioral avoidance** (e.g., behavioral activation, exposure therapy) may address a transdiagnostic mechanism common to both conditions.
The bifactor approach — separating shared from disorder-specific variance — provides a model for how computational psychiatry can move beyond categorical diagnoses toward **dimensional, mechanistic understanding** of psychopathology.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: The total sample of **N = 2,043** across five experiments provides exceptional power for detecting individual differences in computational parameters.
The inclusion of both **non-clinical and clinical samples** strengthens the translational relevance.
The **bifactor modeling** approach requires large samples to reliably estimate shared and specific factors, which this study meets.

__Statistical Rigor__: The use of a **formal computational model** (mood update rule based on RPE) provides a principled, theory-driven framework rather than post-hoc statistical patterns.
The **bifactor model** separating shared, depression-specific, and anxiety-specific variance is methodologically rigorous and addresses the well-known problem of **comorbidity** between depression and anxiety.
The **five-experiment design** allows for internal replication and increases confidence in the generalizability of findings.
The finding that depression results were replicated in a clinical sample is a major strength.

__Effect Sizes__: Computational model parameters (learning rates, mood sensitivity) serve as the primary effect sizes, providing theoretically meaningful indices.
The paper reports specific parameter estimates for depression, anxiety, and the shared factor.
**Confidence intervals** for parameter estimates would strengthen the reporting.

__Measurement Validity__: Mood was measured using a computational model applied to **momentary mood ratings** during a gambling task, providing an ecologically valid measure of mood fluctuations.
Depression and anxiety traits were measured using established questionnaires and decomposed using **bifactor modeling**, which is more psychometrically appropriate than using total scale scores.
The gambling task paradigm is well-validated in computational psychiatry but may not fully capture the complexity of real-world mood dynamics.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint**.
No explicit mention was made of whether data, code, or computational models are publicly available.
The large multi-experiment design supports reproducibility.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample comprised **2,043 participants** across five experiments, including both non-clinical and clinical (affective disorder) samples.
Detailed sample characteristics for each experiment were not available in the abstract.
The inclusion of a **clinical replication sample** with affective disorders is a significant strength.
