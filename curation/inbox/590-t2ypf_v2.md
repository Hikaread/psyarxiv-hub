## Summary
This multicentre PRONIA study (N=177: 40 recent-onset psychosis, 50 recent-onset depression, 33 clinical high-risk for psychosis, 54 healthy controls) evaluated whether **62 NLP-derived features** from a brief **verbal fluency task (VFT)** could classify early-stage psychotic and affective disorders as accurately as the full **MATRICS Consensus Cognitive Battery (MCCB)**. Using **nested cross-validated elastic net classifiers** with 25 iterations, five hierarchical models were compared: MCCB alone, MCCB+VF, MCCB+VF+NLP, VF+NLP, and NLP-only. The key finding was that **NLP-only models achieved classification accuracy statistically comparable to the full four-domain MCCB** across all six pairwise diagnostic comparisons (DeLong's test, all p > 0.54). **Cross-group transfer analysis** showed that classifiers trained on one diagnostic comparison generalized to others, indicating **transdiagnostic** rather than diagnosis-specific linguistic markers. **Dimensional staging analysis** confirmed the predicted severity gradient: HC < ROD = CHR < ROP in model-predicted patient probability. Group differences were driven by **verbal productivity** and **search organization** (clustering/switching) rather than semantic coherence. Features were computed using **FastText embeddings** and the **SIGMA speech feature library V17.2.0**.

## Clinical Insight
A two-minute **verbal fluency assessment** with automated NLP analysis could match a comprehensive cognitive battery for **differential diagnosis** of early psychotic versus affective disorders — a striking finding with immediate clinical implications. The transdiagnostic nature of the markers suggests they capture a general psychopathology severity dimension rather than diagnosis-specific patterns. For clinicians, this means a brief, easily administered verbal task could serve as a **screening or monitoring tool** in early intervention services, potentially reducing the need for lengthy neuropsychological assessments. The finding that **productivity and organization** (not coherence) drive group differences is clinically intuitive — it aligns with clinical observations of **poverty of speech** in psychosis and **psychomotor retardation** in depression. The study's use of **multicentre PRONIA data** strengthens generalizability, though replication in larger and more diverse samples is needed before clinical deployment.

## Notes
PRONIA study, German sites (Cologne, Munich), all native German speakers. NLP features extracted from semantic (animal) and phonetic (S-words) VFT using FastText embeddings pretrained on German Wikipedia. 62 features total including coherence metrics, cluster/switch counts, word frequency, and SIGMA library features. Key limitation: relatively small sample per group, single-language validation. Open science: data available through PRONIA consortium.
