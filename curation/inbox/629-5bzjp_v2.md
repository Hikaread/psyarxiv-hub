## Summary
This perspective paper addresses four methodological problems that limit the informational value of **many-analysts studies** — an approach where multiple independent analysis teams analyse the same dataset and research question, revealing the extent of **analytic variability** in research conclusions. Problem 1: **mismatches between study design and research goals** — the authors recommend designing many-analysts projects with explicit objectives (capturing diversity vs. testing robustness vs. parameter estimation) and adapting scope to available resources. Problem 2: **loss of analysts' expertise and interpretive insights** — the solution is creating structured opportunities for analyst reflection through **systematic commentary protocols**. Problem 3: **insufficient differentiation between analytic contributions of differing plausibility or quality** — addressed by **integrating analytic quality indicators** into meta-regressions (Solution 3a) or using **Bayesian priors** to weight contributions by a priori plausibility (Solution 3b), which shrinks outlier estimates toward a common mean. Problem 4: **limited understanding of which analytic decisions drive variability** — addressed through **sparse meta-regression models** (Solution 4a), **complementary methodologies** including machine learning and global sensitivity analysis (Solution 4b), and **hierarchical Bayesian models** that integrate multiple uncertainty sources (Solution 4c). All solutions are illustrated with data from the **Many-Analysts Religion Project** and the **OSSC19 Crowdsourced Replication Initiative**.

## Clinical Insight
Although this is a methodology paper, its recommendations have direct implications for **evidence-based clinical practice**. Many-analysts studies are increasingly used to evaluate the robustness of clinical research findings — for example, whether a particular therapeutic effect holds across different analytic approaches. The proposed solutions help ensure that when clinicians and guideline developers review many-analysts evidence, they receive **nuanced, weighted syntheses** rather than an undifferentiated forest plot that treats all analyses as equally credible. The recommendation to **weight analyses by quality and plausibility** (Solutions 3a/3b) is directly relevant to **systematic reviews and meta-analyses** in clinical psychology, where the choice of analytic approach can substantially alter conclusions about treatment efficacy. The **hierarchical Bayesian approach** (Solution 4c) for integrating sampling error and analytic variability into a single uncertainty estimate offers a more honest characterisation of the evidence base than traditional meta-analysis. For clinical researchers conducting **crowdsourced replications** of psychotherapy outcome studies, these guidelines provide a practical framework for maximising the informative value of such projects.
