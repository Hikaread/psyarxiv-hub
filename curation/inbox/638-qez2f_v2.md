## Summary

This **Community Perspective** paper examines whether the autism diagnostic label can be reimagined within a **neurodiversity-affirming framework** — shifting from a deficit-based disorder model to one that conceptualises autism as a **neurotype** (a value-neutral profile of experiencing and engaging with the world). The author grounds the analysis in the **disease-illness-sickness triad** (Hofmann, 2002), evaluating the current label from **clinical, personal, and societal perspectives**. Drawing on synthesised qualitative findings from autistic individuals and their relatives, the paper documents the label's **ambivalent nature**: it provides recognition, self-understanding, and access to services, while simultaneously reproducing ableist practices, triggering **diagnostic upgrading**, and exposing individuals to behavioural normalisation interventions.
Three alternatives to the current label are assessed: **elimination** of classificatory diagnoses, replacement with individual **clinical case formulation**, and **self-identification** as the standard. Each addresses certain shortcomings but fails to preserve the full range of the label's functional value. The author then advances a **two-part proposal** for a revised diagnostic description: (1) a co-produced, pluralistic reformulation of behavioural diagnostic criteria that integrates **first-person perspectives** and reduces deficit-based language; and (2) replacement of the **impairment criterion** with a contextualised assessment of **person-environment mismatch**.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians involved in **autism assessment and diagnosis**, this paper offers a nuanced roadmap for navigating the tension between the neurodiversity paradigm and the practical functions of the diagnostic label. The key clinical insight is that the diagnosis serves multiple stakeholders simultaneously — the autistic person (identity, self-understanding), the clinician (classification, treatment planning), and society (service access, policy) — and reforms must account for all three. The proposal to replace the **impairment criterion** (criterion D) with an assessment of **person-environment mismatch** is particularly relevant: it aligns the diagnostic process with the **social model of disability** without abandoning the clinical utility of the label, and it shifts the clinician's role from documenting individual deficits to evaluating the fit between a person's neurotype and their environmental demands.
The paper also highlights the risk of **over-diagnosis** driven by austerity politics and **diagnostic upgrading** (assigning the label when not all criteria are met to justify service access), which clinicians should be aware of when conducting assessments. For **therapists working with autistic clients**, the emphasis on integrating first-person perspectives into the diagnostic description underscores the importance of moving beyond third-party observation toward understanding the cognitive, emotional, motivational, and existential dimensions of autistic experience. The discussion of how behavioural normalisation interventions can undermine **autistic authenticity** and foster **camouflaging** has direct implications for therapeutic goals — clinicians should question whether intervention aims to help the client thrive as an autistic person or to appear less autistic.

## Methodology Note

This is an other-type paper (Community Perspective / conceptual argument), so Statistical Power, Preregistration, and Effect Sizes are not applicable.

__Statistical Rigor__: This is an opinionated but evidence-grounded conceptual analysis.
It synthesises findings from multiple qualitative interview studies and metasyntheses of autistic people's experiences with the diagnostic label (DePape & Lindsay, 2015/2016; Gray et al., 2024; Kiehl et al., 2024; Legg & Tickle, 2019; Nayyar et al., 2025).
The disease-illness-sickness triad provides a structured analytical framework.

__Measurement Validity__: Not applicable — this is not an empirical study.
The author clearly defines "neurotype" and distinguishes it from existing constructs.

__Open Science__: Published open access in the journal *Neurodiversity* under CC BY-NC 4.0 license.

__Participant Transparency__: Not applicable — this is a conceptual paper drawing on published literature rather than primary data collection.
The cited qualitative studies include autistic adolescents, adults, parents, and relatives across multiple countries.
