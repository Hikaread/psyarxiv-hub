## Summary
This **preregistered cross-sectional study** examined **within-person differences** in **reflective functioning** and **personality functioning** across two adult attachment relationships: **romantic partner** versus **primary parental attachment figure**. In a sample of **100 German-speaking adults** in romantic relationships, participants completed **context-specific adaptations** of the Parental Reflective Functioning Questionnaire (PRFQ) and the OPD-SQ personality functioning measure for both relationship contexts. **Linear mixed-effects models** revealed significantly higher **Interest and Curiosity** and **Certainty about Mental States** in the partner context compared to the parental context. Participants also reported **lower relationship-specific stress** and **fewer impairments in self-regulation and affect experiencing** with partners. **Relationship-specific self-efficacy** emerged as the strongest, most consistent correlate of both reflective functioning and personality functioning, particularly in the **parental attachment context**. Conversely, **attachment insecurity**, **mentalizing deficits**, and **relational stress** predicted regulatory difficulties primarily in the **partner context**. Exploratory analyses showed small associations between **perceived parental relationship quality** and participants' romantic functioning, suggesting intergenerational transmission patterns.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists using **mentalization-based treatment (MBT)** or **attachment-informed approaches**, this study provides evidence that **mentalizing is not a fixed trait** but varies across relational contexts. The finding that **relationship-specific self-efficacy** is the strongest predictor of reflective functioning — particularly with parents — suggests that **strengthening perceived relational competence** may be a viable therapeutic target. In MBT, this could mean explicitly addressing a patient's confidence in managing difficult interactions with family members, rather than focusing solely on mentalizing capacity. The finding that **attachment insecurity and stress** are stronger predictors of mentalizing difficulties in the partner context suggests that **romantic relationships may be more vulnerable** to mentalizing breakdowns under stress, which is clinically relevant for couples therapy. The moderate effect sizes indicate that while context matters, **core mentalizing capacity shows some consistency** across relationships. The exploratory **intergenerational findings** — linking perceived parental relationship quality to romantic functioning — support the use of **genogram work** and **intergenerational exploration** in therapy. The study also validates the clinical practice of asking patients to describe their mentalizing in relation to **specific relationships** rather than assessing it as a global capacity.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental study (cross-sectional).

__Statistical Power__: An a priori power analysis indicated N = 98 for detecting medium effects (f-squared = .15) with six predictors.
The final sample of N = 100 meets this threshold for the regression analyses.
However, the sample may be underpowered for detecting small context effects in the mixed models.

__Preregistration__: The study was preregistered on AsPredicted (#240883).
This supports transparency and distinguishes confirmatory from exploratory analyses.
The preregistration is a notable strength for this type of novel research question.

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes for context differences are reported as statistically significant but moderate.
Specific standardized effect sizes (Cohen's d) for the mixed model contrasts would aid interpretation.
The multiple regression results report association strengths that can be evaluated for clinical significance.

__Statistical Rigor__: Linear mixed-effects models appropriately account for the within-person design.
The number of statistical tests conducted is acknowledged as a limitation for Type I error.
Internal consistency was acceptable to excellent (alpha = .59-.93), though the partner-context PRFQ IC subscale was modest.

__Measurement Validity__: Context-specific adaptations of the PRFQ and OPD-SQ were used but not formally validated.
This is a significant limitation, as the adapted measures' psychometric properties in adult attachment contexts are unknown.
The original PRFQ and OPD-SQ are established instruments with good psychometric support.

__Open Science__: The study is published as a preprint on PsyArXiv.
Preregistration on AsPredicted supports transparency.
No mention of data or materials being publicly available.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample is 75% female, with a mean age of 30.62 years.
37 participants scored in the moderate-to-severe depressive range (PHQ-8 >= 10).
The predominantly female, well-educated, community sample with high relationship satisfaction limits generalizability.
Participants were in relationships averaging 7.63 years, representing established partnerships.

## Notes
Important proof-of-concept for relationship-specific mentalizing. The unvalidated context-adapted measures are the main limitation. The self-efficacy finding has direct therapeutic implications.
