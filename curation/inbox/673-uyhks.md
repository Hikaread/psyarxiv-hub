## Summary
This comprehensive overview introduces **Neuroscience-Informed Psychoeducation (NIPE)**, an approach that extends conventional psychoeducation by centering on **brain-based explanations** of psychiatric and neurological disorder mechanisms, symptoms, and treatment responses. NIPE is distinguished from standard psychoeducation by two core domains: **content** (neurochemical, neurocircuit, and cognitive/neurofunctional explanations) and **structure** (brain-adaptive delivery features including **salience**, **trustworthiness**, **repetition**, **multimedia delivery**, and **interactive elements**). The paper describes three content pillars — **neuroscience factual information** (e.g., explaining depression via **VMPFC hyperactivity** and **DLPFC hypoactivity**), **brain strategies** (metacognitive approaches leveraging neurocognitive knowledge like the **method of loci**), and **brain activities and games** (cognitive exercises paired with neuroeducational content). NIPE is positioned as relevant across disorders including **depression**, **schizophrenia**, **TBI**, **substance use disorders**, **eating disorders**, and **OCD**, with particular promise for addressing **impaired self-awareness** (anosognosia) and reducing **shame and stigma** through mechanistic normalization.

## Clinical Insight
NIPE offers a structured framework that clinicians can integrate into existing **CBT**, **motivational interviewing**, or **psychotherapy** modules. For therapists working with clients who have **poor insight** or **anosognosia** — common in **psychosis**, **substance use**, and **eating disorders** — NIPE provides brain-based explanations that may bypass psychological defensiveness by externalizing the problem to neurobiology. The emphasis on **brain-adaptive learning principles** (spaced repetition, multimedia, interactive delivery) aligns with how clinicians already try to optimize psychoeducation but provides a more systematic rationale. The framework also addresses the risk of the **"seductive allure of neuroscience"** — clinicians should avoid oversimplified or overly localized brain explanations. NIPE may be particularly useful in **neurodiversity-informed** contexts where mechanistic understanding can coexist with identity-affirming approaches, and in **psychoeducation for TBI** recovery where brain-based framing feels naturally congruent.

## Methodology Note
This is an other-type paper (narrative review / conceptual framework), so Statistical Power, Preregistration, and Effect Sizes are not applicable.

__Measurement Validity__: The paper presents a **conceptual framework** rather than empirical findings.
The NIPE model synthesizes evidence from multiple domains (neurochemical, neurocircuit, cognitive) and is grounded in established models such as the **Allostatic Load Model**.
The framework's content domains are well-specified but have not yet been subjected to formal validation as a treatment protocol.

__Open Science__: The preprint is openly available on **PsyArXiv**.
The multi-author, multi-institutional collaboration (31 authors across institutions in the US, UK, Iran, Germany, Australia, UAE, Canada, Poland, and Austria) suggests broad engagement but no formal consensus method (e.g., Delphi) was reported.

__Participant Transparency__: This is a review and position paper, not an empirical study with participants.
The author team includes clinicians and researchers from **psychiatry**, **neuropsychology**, and **neuroscience**, lending multidisciplinary credibility.
No patient or participant data is involved.
