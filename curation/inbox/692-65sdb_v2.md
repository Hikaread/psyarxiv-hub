## Summary
This paper documents the development of the **Dyadic Emotional Safety and Inclusion instrument (DESI-18)**, a brief **partner-rated measure** designed to capture **felt safety** — the subjective sense that one is safe enough to be oneself with another person — in **first-meeting dyads**. The development process involved three vantage points: physiological measurement (HRV, electrodermal activity) was abandoned when sensors disrupted the encounter; behavioral observation (NSI coding sheet) revealed a **masking paradox** where warm behavior did not reflect felt safety; and finally, **partner-report** was adopted as the only viable approach. The current DESI-18 proposes a **four-factor structure** (Engagement, Responsiveness, Rapport, We-ness) with 16 scored items, all positively keyed. The paper is notably transparent about its limitations: the instrument has only a single-dyad proof-of-concept and **no reliability or factor-analytic evidence yet**.

## Clinical Insight
Felt safety is the **affective foundation** of therapeutic alliance, yet it is rarely measured directly. The DESI-18's partner-report approach — where each person rates the other's safety-creating behavior — has potential applications beyond first meetings. For **couples therapists**, assessing felt safety from each partner's perspective could reveal **asymmetric experiences** of the relationship that self-report measures miss (one partner may feel safe while the other does not). The concept reframes felt safety through a **social-cure lens**: it is not merely the absence of threat but the first stirring of shared identity ("we-ness"). For therapists conducting **intake assessments** or **relationship evaluations**, this framework offers a structured way to assess the relational substrate on which therapy depends. The paper's honest reporting of **two measurement failures** is itself clinically instructive — it demonstrates that the most observable signals (physiology, behavior) can be the most deceptive.

## Methodology Note
This is an instrument development paper (no validation data yet).

__Design__: Instrument development across three methodological generations (physiological, behavioral, partner-report).
The current DESI-18 is a 16-item partner-rated measure with 4 theory-led factors.
Single-dyad proof-of-concept only.
No reliability (α, ω, test-retest), factor analysis, or validation data reported.

__Strengths__: Exceptionally transparent reporting of development failures and current evidentiary limitations.
Theoretical grounding in psychological safety, perceived partner responsiveness, and social cure literatures.
The "masking paradox" finding has implications for all observational research on relational constructs.

__Limitations__: No psychometric data whatsoever at this stage — this is a development paper, not a validation study.
Single-dyad proof-of-concept means the instrument has barely been tested.
The authors explicitly acknowledge that the four-factor structure is a theory-led hypothesis, not data-derived.
The first-meeting context may not generalize to established relationships or therapeutic settings.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
Authors are commendably explicit about the current state of evidence and what remains to be validated.

## Notes
Important conceptual contribution with an honest assessment of its own limitations. The "masking paradox" — that observable warmth can coexist with absent felt safety — is a crucial insight for both research and clinical practice. The instrument's utility will depend entirely on the forthcoming validation study.
