## Summary
This mixed-methods survey examined accessibility barriers, participation, and perceived wellbeing impacts among 149 autistic adults and 97 parents/caregivers of autistic people in Australia, spanning seven life domains: healthcare, employment, employment support, transport, retail, education/training, and community/leisure. Barriers were widespread, often experienced across multiple domains, with more than half of respondents across all domains reporting moderate-to-large wellbeing impacts. Sensory environments were the most frequently endorsed barrier type. Reflexive thematic analysis of 998 open-text responses generated four themes: (1) environments were actively hostile to autistic needs; (2) services were available but not always accessible; (3) access depended on whether people understood autistic needs; and (4) repeated inaccessibility narrowed everyday life. The study introduces the concept of access burden — the ongoing, resource-intensive process of navigating inaccessible environments, including planning, sensory management, recovery, and negotiating whether needs would be understood or dismissed.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians working with autistic clients, this study highlights that accessibility barriers are not confined to specific settings but represent a pervasive, cross-domain burden that directly impacts wellbeing. The concept of access burden is particularly clinically relevant: autistic clients may present with exhaustion, reduced functioning, or withdrawal that reflects the cumulative toll of navigating inaccessible environments rather than an intrinsic feature of their condition. Clinicians should assess the extent to which everyday accessibility demands contribute to presenting problems, including potential autistic burnout. The finding that access often depends on whether people understand autistic needs underscores the role of psychoeducation and self-advocacy support in treatment. The study also supports systemic advocacy as a therapeutic intervention — improving wellbeing requires not just helping autistic people cope, but pushing for environmental and systemic change.

## Relevant For
Clinicians working with autistic adults; therapists assessing wellbeing and burnout in neurodivergent populations; those providing advocacy or systemic interventions; researchers studying environmental barriers to mental health in autism.

## Notes
Australian sample limits cross-cultural generalizability. Parent/caregiver reports for autistic people they support may not fully capture the autistic person's subjective experience. The study did not explicitly measure autistic burnout though accounts aligned with existing burnout literature.
