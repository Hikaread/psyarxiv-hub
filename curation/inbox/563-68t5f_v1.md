## Summary

This qualitative study examined the **perceived utility and scope** of **Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA)** and **Ecological Momentary Interventions (EMIs)** for **Non-Suicidal Self-Injury (NSSI)** from the dual perspectives of treatment-seeking individuals and **mental health professionals**. Semi-structured interviews were conducted with **30 treatment-seeking individuals** engaging in NSSI, following a four-week EMA period, and with **14 professionals** who used EMA in clinical treatment. Using **reflexive thematic analysis**, three themes were identified. First, EMA and EMIs were perceived as extending **therapeutic support** by enhancing awareness, reflection, and skill use in daily life. Second, participants emphasized that EMIs must be **tailored to changing needs** in function of **dynamic risk levels** — informing the development of a **Needs-Based Dynamic Risk Perspective**. Third, both groups identified practical and ethical considerations around real-time monitoring of self-injury urges. The study bridges the gap between retrospective, static assessment approaches and the rapid within-person fluctuations that characterize NSSI, proposing a framework for personalized, just-in-time digital support.

## Clinical Insight

This study has direct implications for how clinicians assess and intervene with **NSSI**, particularly given the well-documented limitation that retrospective self-report of self-injury urges and behaviours is often inaccurate. The **Needs-Based Dynamic Risk Perspective** offers a clinically actionable framework: rather than relying on session-by-session check-ins, clinicians could integrate **EMA data** to track real-time fluctuations in NSSI urges, identify high-risk windows, and deploy **just-in-time interventions** such as grounding exercises, distress tolerance skills from **DBT**, or encouraging contact with supports. The finding that EMA itself was perceived as therapeutic — enhancing self-awareness and prompting skill use — suggests that the assessment process can serve a dual function, aligning with therapeutic models like **Behavioral Chain Analysis** that emphasize understanding the moment-to-moment antecedents of self-harm. For clinicians implementing **DBT** or **CBT** for NSSI, the study supports incorporating digital EMA as an adjunctive tool between sessions. The ethical considerations raised by participants — including concerns about surveillance, data privacy, and the potential for EMA to become punitive rather than supportive — are critical for clinicians to address when proposing digital monitoring tools to clients.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians treating NSSI using DBT, CBT, or integrated approaches
- Mental health professionals interested in digital mental health tools
- Researchers studying dynamic risk assessment for self-harm
- Clinical supervisors developing EMA-integrated treatment protocols
- Crisis intervention teams working with self-harm
