## Summary
This systematic review, following PRISMA guidelines, examined the effects of long-term and advanced meditation practices on well-being, emotion regulation, and interpersonal relationships. A search of PubMed, MEDLINE, PsycINFO, and Embase (April 2026) identified 1,229 articles, of which 52 met inclusion criteria. The review synthesizes qualitative and quantitative findings demonstrating that long-term and advanced meditators experience greater well-being outcomes, including deeper relational attunement and more refined emotional capacities, compared to outcomes observed in novice practitioners. Critically, these enhanced outcomes appear associated with skill-based proficiency rather than practice duration alone. The review identifies a significant measurement limitation: existing tools are poorly suited to capture the subtler benefits experienced by advanced practitioners. The authors argue that the field's continued reliance on temporal criteria (hours of practice) combined with these measurement inadequacies may obscure the deeper transformative benefits of sustained meditative practice.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians integrating meditation-based interventions into therapy, this review challenges the common assumption that more practice hours automatically yield better outcomes. Instead, the quality and sophistication of meditative skills — what the authors term proficiency — appears more important than sheer duration. This aligns with clinical observations that some patients develop greater benefit from focused, skillful practice than from prolonged but unfocused engagement. The review's emphasis on measurement limitations is particularly relevant for clinicians assessing meditation's impact: standard well-being scales may fail to capture meaningful changes in advanced practitioners. Clinicians should consider whether their assessment tools adequately capture the relational and emotional refinements that meditation can produce, and may need to supplement standard measures with qualitative or specialized assessments when working with experienced meditators.

## Relevant For
Clinicians incorporating mindfulness and meditation into therapeutic work; researchers developing meditation-based interventions; those interested in the dose-response relationship in contemplative practices; therapists working with clients who have extensive meditation experience.
