## Summary
This exploratory neuroimaging study (N = 131 adults from **71 romantic couples**) used a novel paradigm presenting **positive or negative partner-directed feedback** either alone or alongside conflicting **self-directed feedback**. Partner-alone feedback engaged classic **empathy regions** (**dACC**, **anterior insula**, **dmPFC**, **TPJ**). During incongruent feedback (simultaneous conflicting self- and partner-directed feedback), participants showed diminished affective responses but recruited **frontoparietal cognitive control regions** (**dlPFC**, **supramarginal gyrus**). Critically, when receiving **positive partner feedback** in the presence of **negative self-feedback**, enhanced **dmPFC activity** was associated with **lower self-reported emotion regulation difficulties** and **more partner-rated support** in everyday life, linking neural responses during this interpersonal paradigm to real-world relationship functioning.

## Clinical Insight
This study has direct implications for **couples therapy** and work with clients at risk for **suicidality**. The finding that self-focused information **dampens neural and affective responses** to a partner's experience helps explain a common clinical presentation: a depressed or suicidal client who becomes so self-absorbed that they cannot attune to their partner's needs. For couples therapists, the paradigm suggests that **cognitive control resources** (dlPFC, supramarginal gyrus) are recruited when partners must simultaneously process conflicting emotional information — a frequent real-world scenario during conflict. The link between **dmPFC activity during positive partner feedback amid negative self-feedback** and real-world support provision suggests that **protective interpersonal processes** can be measured neurally. In suicide risk assessment, the finding that trait **emotion regulation difficulties** relate to how the brain processes interpersonal feedback underscores the importance of assessing **interpersonal emotion regulation** as part of suicide risk formulation, consistent with interpersonal theories of suicide.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 131 (from 71 couples) is a moderate sample for an fMRI study.
The within-couple design increases ecological validity but introduces non-independence that may affect power estimates.
The exploratory nature of the study means some analyses may be underpowered for small effects.

__Preregistration__: The study is explicitly described as **exploratory**, suggesting limited or no preregistration of specific neural hypotheses.
The authors appropriately note that further validation and replication are warranted.

__Effect Sizes__: No standardized effect sizes were reported in the available abstract.
The reporting focuses on neural activation patterns and correlations with behavioral measures.
The link between dmPFC activity and real-world partner-rated support is reported as an association but without effect magnitude.

__Statistical Rigor__: The novel paradigm is a strength methodologically.
The exploratory framing limits concerns about multiple comparisons, but also means findings require replication.
The use of both self-report and partner-report measures of real-world behavior strengthens the ecological validity of the neural-behavioral links.

__Measurement Validity__: The neuroimaging paradigm is novel with no prior validation, which is both a limitation and a contribution.
Partner-rated support provides a **multi-informant** validity check.
Trait emotion regulation was assessed via self-report, which is standard but subject to bias.

__Open Science__: The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv** in its 8th version, indicating ongoing revision.
No data or code availability statement was found in the abstract.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 131 adults from 71 romantic couples.
No further demographic details were available in the abstract.
The couples-based design means findings may not generalize to non-partnered individuals.
