## Summary
This systematic literature review, following **PRISMA 2020 guidelines**, synthesizes evidence from 52 studies (of 486 identified through Scopus and Google Scholar) to examine how **Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT)** can address **career decision regret** in Muslim university students. Using the **Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT)** for quality appraisal, the review identifies five thematic areas: (1) psychological determinants of career decision regret (counterfactual thinking, rumination, self-blame, reduced career adaptability); (2) effectiveness of REBT and **cognitive restructuring** in career counselling; (3) contributions of **Islamic psychology** and religious coping (tafakkur, muhasabah, hikmah); (4) the role of **hifz al-aql** (preservation and development of human intellect) as a philosophical foundation for rational thinking; and (5) contextual considerations in career development among Muslim students studying away from home communities. The review proposes a novel **Hifz al-Aql-Based REBT Framework** that integrates REBT's **ABCDE cognitive disputation** techniques with Islamic principles of reflective thinking (tafakkur), self-evaluation (muhasabah), and wise judgement (hikmah).

## Clinical Insight
This review offers a practical framework for clinicians working with Muslim university students experiencing **career decision regret** — a construct associated with **reduced career adaptability, emotional exhaustion, depressive symptoms, and poorer psychological well-being**. The proposed framework integrates Ellis's **ABCDE model** (Activating event, Beliefs, Consequences, Disputation, Effective new philosophy) with Islamic cognitive practices, making it a culturally responsive adaptation of CBT. For therapists in university counselling settings, the key insight is that career regret is sustained through **irrational beliefs and counterfactual reasoning** rather than simply poor information — requiring cognitive restructuring rather than additional career guidance. The integration of **tafakkur** (reflective thinking) as an Islamic parallel to cognitive reappraisal, and **muhasabah** (self-evaluation) as parallel to behavioral self-monitoring, provides culturally meaningful language for Muslim clients. Clinicians should note that Muslim students studying away from home communities may face additional cultural, familial, and religious pressures compounding career regret.

## Methodology Note
This is a meta_analysis-type paper (systematic literature review).

__Statistical Rigor__: Follows PRISMA 2020 reporting guidelines with a clear flow diagram.
Searches were conducted in Scopus and Google Scholar, which may miss databases relevant to counselling psychology (e.g., PsycINFO, Web of Science).
The MMAT was used for quality appraisal, appropriate for reviews including diverse study designs.

__Measurement Validity__: No meta-analysis or effect size synthesis was conducted; findings are presented thematically.
The proposed Hifz al-Aql-Based REBT Framework is theoretical and has not been empirically validated.
The review acknowledges that evidence on Muslim university students' career regret remains limited.

__Participant Transparency__: The reviewed studies span diverse educational and cultural contexts.
The review highlights a gap: most career regret research has been conducted in Western, non-Muslim populations.
Specific demographic characteristics of participants across the 52 studies are not systematically reported.

__Open Science__: The review is available as a preprint on OSF.
No protocol was preregistered (noted as a limitation).
No data extraction sheet or analysis code is shared.
