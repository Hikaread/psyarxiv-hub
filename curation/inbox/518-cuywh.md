## Summary
This study piloted a **novel experiential uncertainty task** designed to move beyond retrospective self-report measures of **intolerance of uncertainty (IU)**. Participants were asked to insert their hands into an **opaque box** and feel an **unknown object**, while the study assessed both **subjective affective reactions** and **physiological responses** including **skin conductance** and **heart rate**. The task aimed to capture real-time behavioral and physiological responses to uncertainty in a controlled but ecologically valid paradigm.

The study addresses a critical gap in the IU literature, where most measures rely on **retrospective self-report** questionnaires that may not accurately capture how individuals actually respond to uncertain situations in the moment. By combining **behavioral approach** (willingness to engage with the unknown object), **subjective affect** (self-reported emotional reactions), and **peripheral physiology** (skin conductance and heart rate), the paradigm offers a multi-method assessment of uncertainty tolerance that could better predict mental health outcomes. The pilot nature of the work suggests initial feasibility testing and validation of the task parameters.

## Clinical Insight
The development of an **experiential uncertainty task** has direct relevance for clinical assessment of anxiety-related disorders where IU is a transdiagnostic maintaining factor, including **generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)**, **obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)**, and **social anxiety disorder**. If validated, this paradigm could provide a more ecologically valid behavioral measure of IU than existing questionnaires like the IUS, potentially improving **case conceptualization** and **treatment planning**. The inclusion of **physiological measures** (skin conductance, heart rate) adds objectivity to the assessment of uncertainty responses, which could be particularly valuable for tracking treatment progress in **exposure-based therapies** that target intolerance of uncertainty. The opaque box paradigm is simple enough for potential clinical deployment while maintaining experimental control.
