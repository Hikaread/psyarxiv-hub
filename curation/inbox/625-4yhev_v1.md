## Summary
This study applied **network analysis** to examine (1) the item-level structure of the **AUDIT** (Alcohol Use Disorder Identification Test) and (2) the interconnections among established **AUD risk factors** including age of onset, craving, drinking motives, mental health symptoms, impulsivity, and tobacco use in a combined sample of N = 933 participants. Networks were compared between adolescents (16–25 years, n = 421) and adults (35–65 years, n = 252). In the **AUDIT item network**, consumption measures clustered strongly together, with **binge drinking** emerging as the central node connecting consumption to use-related problems including **loss of control**. This structure was consistent across age groups with no significant differences in network structure or edge weights detected via Network Comparison Tests. In the **AUD risk factor network**, alcohol consumption showed the highest **strength centrality** and was strongly associated with use-related problems. **Drinking motives** were densely interconnected — **enhancement motives** showed the highest expected influence, strongly linked to social motives and consumption, while **coping motives** were more strongly connected to AUDIT-P and **mental health symptoms**. **Craving** was strongly associated with both alcohol consumption and coping motives across both age groups, suggesting a connection between craving and negatively reinforced drinking regardless of age. Although adolescents scored significantly higher on AUDIT total, use-related problems, enhancement and social motives, mental health symptoms, and impulsivity, the underlying relational network architecture was stable across development.

## Clinical Insight
The finding that **binge drinking** is the central bridge between consumption and problems in the AUDIT network has direct implications for screening and early intervention — clinicians should pay particular attention to binge episodes as a key indicator of escalating risk. The strong interconnection between **coping motives**, **craving**, and **mental health symptoms** across both age groups underscores the need for integrated treatment approaches that address **comorbid psychopathology** alongside alcohol use rather than treating AUD in isolation. The observation that adolescents show a more densely connected risk factor network (19/55 edges vs. 14/55 in adults) suggests that interventions targeting a single risk factor (e.g., coping skills training) may have broader cascading effects in younger populations. The consistency of the **drinking motives** architecture across development implies that motive-based interventions — particularly targeting enhancement and coping motives — may be applicable across the lifespan. The strong **craving–consumption–coping** pathway reinforces the clinical utility of craving assessment (e.g., Alcohol-VAS) as a transdiagnostic marker for treatment planning, while the cross-sectional design limits causal inference and the non-clinical sample restricts generalisability to treatment-seeking AUD populations.

## Methodology Note
__Statistical Power__: The final sample of N = 933 (421 adolescents, 252 adults after excluding 260 participants outside age ranges) is adequate for the Gaussian Graphical Model estimation; however, no formal power analysis was reported, and the adult subgroup (n = 252) yielded lower centrality stability in some bootstrap estimates. __Preregistration__: The study was not preregistered. __Effect Sizes__: Edge weights (partial correlations) were reported with bootstrap CIs; however, no clinical significance thresholds or effect size benchmarks were applied to the network parameters. __Statistical Rigor__: Network Comparison Tests with 1,000 permutations were used for group comparisons with Holm-corrected p-values for edge-specific tests; the ggmModSelect algorithm with EBIC (γ = 0.5) was appropriately chosen over LASSO after preliminary analyses indicated sparsity assumption violations; centrality stability was assessed via case-dropping bootstrap (CS coefficients ≥ .25 reported). __Measurement Validity__: Established measures were used throughout — the AUDIT, DMQ-R, DSM-5 CCSM, Webexec, ImpSS, and Alcohol-VAS — though all were self-report and the Webexec is a brief screening measure rather than a comprehensive executive functioning assessment. __Open Science__: The paper is open access on PsyArXiv but no data, code, or preregistration materials are publicly available. __Participant Transparency__: Demographics were reported (54.7% non-Dutch nationality; 88.4% highly educated in both groups; predominantly female); the sample was a convenience cohort combining online (Prolific) and MRI-study participants, which limits generalisability; exclusion criteria (never drinkers, missing onset data) were clearly stated; the unbalanced age groups and nationality differences between groups were acknowledged as limitations.
