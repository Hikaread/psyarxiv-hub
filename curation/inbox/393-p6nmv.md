## Summary
This large-scale online study (N=166: 101 autistic adults, 65 non-autistic adults) investigated whether autistic adults integrate beat gestures (simple up-and-down hand movements that accompany speech) with spoken prosody to the same extent as non-autistic adults. Participants judged the perceived stress pattern of disyllabic words (e.g., CONtent vs. conTENT) presented in three audiovisual conditions: beat gesture on the first syllable (BeatOn1), beat gesture on the second syllable (BeatOn2), or no beat gesture (NoBeat). Using a visual analog scale, participants rated stress patterns on a Strong-Weak to Weak-Strong continuum.

Results showed that beat gestures significantly biased stress perception in both groups: BeatOn1 increased Strong-Weak ratings compared to BeatOn2 and NoBeat (all p<.001), and BeatOn2 decreased SW ratings compared to NoBeat (p=.003). Critically, there was no significant group difference (GroupNon-Autistic contrast: p=.461), no group-by-condition interaction (p=.598), and no relationship between audiovisual integration effects and autistic traits measured by the Autism Quotient-28 or the Action and Feelings Questionnaire. Bayesian analysis provided moderate evidence for the null hypothesis of no group difference (BF10 = 0.22). The study was pre-registered and used a large sample compared to prior work in this area.

## Clinical Insight
These findings challenge the long-standing assumption that autistic individuals have attenuated multisensory integration. For therapists working with autistic adults, this suggests that visual communicative cues such as hand gestures are processed effectively and can be leveraged in therapy without special accommodation. The results align with a growing body of evidence showing that audiovisual integration differences in autism may be developmental rather than persistent into adulthood. Clinicians should be cautious about applying child-based research findings to adult autistic clients, and should not assume that multimodal communication approaches need modification for autistic adults. The study also found no relationship between autistic traits and integration ability, suggesting this is not a trait-dependent phenomenon.

## Relevant For
Therapists working with autistic adults, neurodivergence-affirming practitioners, speech-language therapists, clinical psychologists updating their understanding of autism across the lifespan.

## Notes
