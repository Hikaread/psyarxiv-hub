## Summary

This paper critically reanalyzes two highly influential studies—**Foody et al. (2013)** and its replication **Foody et al. (2015)**—that are frequently cited as evidence that **hierarchical deictic relations** in ACT exercises (seeing thoughts as contained by a larger self) outperform **distinction relations** (seeing thoughts as separate from self) in reducing distress. The reanalysis used a **multiverse analysis** approach, extracting summary statistics and computing **Hedges' g** across all combinations of analytic choices: four outcome measures (anxiety, discomfort, stress, pooled), with or without **Morris (2008) baseline correction**, and with or without **Bonferroni familywise correction**—yielding 14 specification variants.

Across **all analytic specifications** in both the original study and the replication, **no confidence interval excluded zero** for the hierarchy-versus-distinction comparison. The original study's significant RM-ANOVA interaction (p = .04 for stress only) did not survive even minimal correction for multiple comparisons. The author further documents that **Foody et al. (2013)** was not a true replication of **Luciano et al. (2011)**, differing in sample, design, number of follow-ups, outcome measures, and analytic strategy. The paper catalogs how these results have been **uncritically cited** in textbooks (Oxford Handbook of ACT, Behavior Therapy) and influential articles, often misrepresenting the failed replication as supportive evidence. The author declares a conflict of interest as a former student of Prof. Dermot Barnes-Holmes (a co-author of the original studies).

## Clinical Insight

For ACT trainers and clinicians, this reanalysis has significant implications. The claim that **hierarchical framing** of self-as-context produces superior therapeutic outcomes compared to **distinction framing**—a claim that has influenced ACT training curricula and RFT-informed practice—appears **unsupported by the original data**. This does not mean ACT is ineffective or that self-as-context is invalid, but it does mean that one key evidentiary link between **Relational Frame Theory** and ACT practice is substantially weaker than the literature suggests. Clinicians and training programs should exercise caution when citing Foody et al. (2013) and should be aware that the failed replication by the same group (Foody et al., 2015) has been consistently obscured or mischaracterized. The broader lesson is about the importance of **critical engagement with cited evidence** in evidence-based practice.

## Methodology Note

This is an other-type paper (critical reanalysis).

__Statistical Rigor__: The **multiverse analysis** is an appropriate and rigorous approach for evaluating the robustness of findings across analytic choices.
**Morris (2008) corrections** were appropriately applied to control for baseline differences.
**Hedges' g** was calculated as the effect size metric.
The 14 specification variants comprehensively cover the reasonable analytic decisions.
The limitation of working from summary statistics rather than raw data is acknowledged but unavoidable given data access constraints.
The critique that Foody et al.'s (2013) RM-ANOVA interaction tested only one of three outcomes and did not include between-group post-hoc tests is methodologically sound.

__Measurement Validity__: The original studies used three **single-item visual analogue scales (VAS)** for discomfort, anxiety, and stress.
These are extremely brief measures (one item each) with unknown reliability and validity for the specific experimental context.
The AAQ-II was administered only at baseline, limiting its utility.

__Open Science__: The reanalysis itself follows transparent, reproducible methods.
Data and code for the reanalysis are not explicitly stated as available.
The author publishes under an open-science-friendly framework (Perspectives on Behavior Science).

__Participant Transparency__: Original study: 18 per condition (36 total), non-clinical sample.
Replication: 12 per condition (48 total across four conditions).
Both studies used small, non-clinical, likely student samples.
The very small sample sizes are a fundamental limitation of the original studies.

## Notes

This paper exemplifies how uncritical citation practices can propagate unsupported claims through influential textbooks and training materials. The author's disclosed relationship with a co-author of the original studies adds transparency but may also be relevant for readers evaluating potential advocacy bias. The broader pattern—where RFT-ACT bridge studies have failed to replicate (also Sierra et al., 2016; Pendrous et al., 2020)—warrants systematic attention.
