## Summary
This **theoretical paper** argues that **computational psychiatry's** focus on domain-general learning biases (e.g., threat overgeneralization, altered precision weighting) has produced **small, inconsistent effects** because it targets the wrong level of analysis.
The authors distinguish between **task models** (the structure of a specific experimental paradigm) and **world models** (an individual's causal beliefs about threat, control, and the self across life domains).
They argue that **trait anxiety** is generated not by how individuals learn within a novel task, but by **person-specific world models** that have been shaped through domain-specific learning over development.
The paper reviews evidence that computational findings in anxiety are **inconsistent at the task level** but become **theory-consistent when conditioned on context and domain** — supporting the world model framework.
The authors propose that the central task for the next generation of computational anxiety models is **inferring idiographic world models** using developmental, phenomenological, and **language-model methods**.
The paper calls for a shift from **nomothetic task-based parameters** to **idiographic, domain-specific models** of anxiety, drawing on tools from the **idionomics** framework (Hayes et al., 2026).

## Clinical Insight
This paper has **direct implications for clinical assessment and treatment of anxiety disorders**.
If trait anxiety is driven by **person-specific world models** (beliefs about threat, control, and the self) rather than domain-general learning biases, then **assessment should focus on mapping an individual's idiosyncratic threat beliefs** across life domains rather than administering standardized threat-learning tasks.
This aligns closely with **formulation-based approaches** in CBT and **process-based therapy (PBT)**, where the therapist identifies the specific cognitive and behavioral processes maintaining anxiety for each individual.
The paper provides a **computational rationale** for why standardized anxiety protocols may have **modest effect sizes** — they target general processes that may not map onto any individual's specific world model.
For practitioners, the key takeaway is that understanding a client's **personal narrative about threat and safety** — their world model — may be more clinically useful than measuring their performance on a probabilistic learning task.
The proposal to use **language-model methods** (e.g., analyzing session transcripts or journal entries with NLP) to infer world models is particularly promising for **clinical practice**, as it could provide a systematic way to extract belief structures from naturally occurring clinical material.

## Methodology Note
This is an other-type paper (theoretical).

__Statistical Rigor__: As a theoretical review and position paper, no original statistical analyses are presented.
The argument is supported by a **systematic review of existing computational anxiety literature**, with careful attention to effect sizes and consistency of findings across studies.
The **task model vs. world model** distinction is clearly defined and provides a useful theoretical framework for interpreting the mixed findings in the computational psychiatry of anxiety literature.

__Measurement Validity__: The paper critically evaluates the **measurement approach** of computational psychiatry — specifically, the reliance on **task-based paradigms** (e.g., fear conditioning, approach-avoidance) that measure domain-general parameters.
The authors argue that these tasks have **limited construct validity** for capturing the idiographic world models that actually generate trait anxiety.
The paper proposes **language-model methods** and **phenomenological approaches** as alternative measurement strategies that may better capture individual world models.

__Open Science__: The paper is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint**.
No original data or code is associated with this theoretical piece.
The authors build on the open-source **idionomics** framework (Hayes et al., 2026) and reference open tools for idiographic analysis.

__Participant Transparency__: Not applicable to this theoretical paper.
The argument draws on evidence from multiple existing studies of anxiety using computational methods, with participants ranging from non-clinical to clinical populations.
The paper does not systematically review participant characteristics across cited studies.
