## Summary
This **qualitative study** explored men's **perceptions, expectations, and satisfaction** with mental health services for depression, focusing on how these dimensions **interacted over time** throughout the help-seeking process. Using **semi-structured interviews**, the study traced men's journeys from initial recognition of depression through engagement with services, examining how expectations were formed (often through media, family, and GP referral), how they were met or violated during treatment, and how satisfaction was negotiated across different phases of care. The study illuminates the **dynamic interplay** between pre-treatment expectations and in-treatment experiences, revealing that men's engagement cannot be understood at a single time point. Findings identify specific **barrier patterns** (misalignment of expectations with service reality, perceived therapeutic invalidation, and mismatched communication styles) and **facilitator patterns** (collaborative treatment planning, perceived therapist competence, and flexible approach) that influenced men's continued engagement.

## Clinical Insight
Men's **high dropout rates** from mental health services are a persistent clinical problem. This study provides a **longitudinal, process-level understanding** of why men disengage — not as a static barrier but as a dynamic mismatch that evolves across the treatment trajectory. For clinicians, the key insight is that **expectation management** is critical from the first point of contact: when men's expectations (often shaped by media portrayals or brief GP conversations) collide with the reality of weekly therapy sessions focused on emotional exploration, the mismatch can trigger disengagement. The study suggests that **early collaborative treatment planning**, explicit discussion of what therapy involves, and a **flexible, non-prescriptive stance** may improve retention. The qualitative design captures **nuanced subjective experiences** that quantitative studies of help-seeking barriers miss, particularly the temporal dimension of how perceptions shift during treatment.

## Relevant For
- Therapists working with male-identifying patients
- Service managers seeking to reduce male dropout rates
- GPs and primary care practitioners as first-contact points
- Researchers in men's mental health and help-seeking
