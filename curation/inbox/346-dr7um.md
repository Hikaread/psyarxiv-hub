## Summary

This community-based study documented the first Indigenous TCTSY-Facilitator Training Cohort's perspectives on Trauma Center Trauma-Sensitive Yoga (TCTSY). Twenty interviews were conducted with 14 program participants (13 women) who completed the 20-hour training and 1 program mentor, drawn primarily from Great Plains Tribal Nations. Using consensual qualitative research methods, the study found that participants reported positive impacts on their approach to trauma care. Key takeaways included learning about the neurobiological effects of trauma and the value of invitational language (offering choices without direction). Participants described integrating TCTSY with Indigenous healing practices including smudging, land-based practice, and intentional movement within ceremony spaces. Suggested improvements included greater emphasis on yoga history and philosophy, collaboration with Elders, and acknowledgment of ongoing community trauma.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with trauma survivors from Indigenous or other culturally distinct communities, this study illustrates both the potential and the complexity of adapting body-based interventions across cultural contexts. The participants' natural integration of TCTSY with existing Indigenous healing practices, such as smudging and land-based ceremony, demonstrates that trauma-sensitive yoga does not need to replace traditional healing but can complement it. The emphasis on invitational language, a core TCTSY principle, resonates with Indigenous values of autonomy and self-determination, and therapists working cross-culturally will find this alignment useful. The finding that participants valued learning about the neurobiological effects of trauma suggests that psychoeducation about trauma's somatic impact can be a powerful bridge between Western and Indigenous healing frameworks. Therapists should be aware that delivering trauma interventions in communities experiencing ongoing collective trauma requires additional sensitivity and that acknowledging current trauma, rather than treating it as purely historical, is essential for ethical practice.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with trauma in Indigenous, refugee, or other culturally distinct communities, and those interested in integrating body-based and somatic approaches with existing cultural healing practices
