## Summary

This study provides the first evidence that **empathic disequilibrium**—the imbalance between **cognitive empathy** and **emotional empathy**—is modifiable through intervention. A total of **157 undergraduate psychology students** (n = 89 experimental, n = 68 control; 80% female; M age = 23.7 years) at Ben-Gurion University completed ten days of daily **10-minute guided mindfulness meditation** or an active control condition involving **emotion regulation psychoeducation** and daily **POMS mood ratings**. Empathic disequilibrium was calculated as the difference between **standardized IRI** cognitive and emotional empathy subscale scores.

The **mindfulness training significantly reduced the magnitude of empathic disequilibrium** compared to controls (time × group interaction: F(1, 157.16) = 10.64, p < .01, **ηp² = .06**). Within the experimental group, empathic disequilibrium decreased significantly from T1 to T2 (p = .002, **d = .34**), while the control group showed no significant change (p = .10, d = -.19). The reduction reflected a **narrowing of the gap** between cognitive and emotional empathy rather than a directional shift toward one component. However, the hypothesized mediation through reduced **emotional reactivity** (measured by the **ERS**, α = .86) was not supported (BF₀₁ = 25.98, providing strong evidence for the null). **Propensity score weighting**, outlier exclusion, and controlling for **autistic traits** (AQ) all confirmed the robustness of the primary finding.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with clients who experience empathy-related difficulties, these findings suggest that **empathic disequilibrium**—whether emotional dominance (feeling overwhelmed by others' emotions) or cognitive dominance (feeling detached)—may be a **malleable interpersonal target**. The fact that a brief, low-intensity intervention (10 minutes daily for 10 days) produced measurable change in a non-clinical sample is noteworthy. The non-significant mediation by emotional reactivity suggests the mechanism of change may operate through **interoceptive awareness** or **oxytocinergic pathways** rather than simple arousal reduction, as the authors discuss.

This is particularly relevant for populations where empathic disequilibrium has been linked to clinical outcomes, including **anxiety disorders**, **eating disorders**, **depression**, **schizophrenia**, and **self-injurious behaviors in autistic individuals**. Clinicians could consider incorporating brief mindfulness components when working with clients who report feeling either emotionally flooded or disconnected in relationships.

## Methodology Note

This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: An a priori **Monte Carlo simulation** (5,000 resampling) was conducted assuming |r| = .5 between empathic disequilibrium and emotional reactivity and r = -.3 for the intervention effect.
This yielded a target sample of **N = 150** for β > .80 at α < .05 to detect medium mediation effects.
The achieved sample of 157 met this target.
The study was not powered for the omnibus ANOVA approach; mixed-effects models were used instead.

__Preregistration__: The study was **not preregistered**, which the authors acknowledge as a limitation.
Analyses were conducted in R v4.3.0 using lmerTest, lme4, BayesFactor, and ggplot2.

__Effect Sizes__: The primary effect (empathic disequilibrium magnitude) showed ηp² = .06 and Cohen's d = .34 (within-group, experimental).
The mediation indirect effect was small (b = -.002, β = -.001) with zero in the 95% CI.
Emotional reactivity itself decreased significantly in the experimental group (d = -.18).
Cognitive empathy increased in the experimental group (d = .18).
Bayes Factor for the null mediation: BF₀₁ = 25.98 (strong evidence).

__Statistical Rigor__: Appropriate use of **linear mixed-effects models** with random intercepts for the within-subject design.
The dependent variable was **square-root transformed** to address non-normal residuals.
VIFs were all below 4.35, below the threshold of 5.
Complementary **Bayesian analysis** provided evidence for or against the null.
**Propensity score weighting** (stabilized inverse-probability-of-treatment weights, trimmed at the 99th percentile) addressed the quasi-randomized design.
Sensitivity analyses included outlier exclusion and controlling for **AQ scores**.
The non-randomized design (participants self-selected into conditions through the university pool) is a meaningful limitation.
Emotional empathy at T2 showed relatively low internal consistency (α = 0.58), which may have affected the precision of follow-up empathic disequilibrium estimates.

__Measurement Validity__: Empathy was measured using a **state-adapted IRI** (28 items), asking participants to report on the last week rather than trait-level responses.
Cognitive empathy (Perspective Taking + Fantasy) showed α = .74 (T1), .72 (T2).
Emotional empathy (Empathic Concern + Personal Distress) showed α = .71 (T1), .58 (T2)—the low T2 reliability is concerning.
Emotional reactivity was measured using the **ERS** (21 items, α = .86 at both time points), translated into Hebrew via **back-translation**.
The IRI adaptation from trait to state has been used previously (Shalev et al., 2023b) but is not a standard measure.

__Open Science__: Data are described as "de-identified and made openly available."
No preregistration.
Analysis code was written in R; packages are specified.
Study followed **JARS** reporting standards.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 157 (89 experimental, 68 control) from Ben-Gurion University participant pool.
92% Jewish-Israeli, majority Hebrew-speaking.
79.6% female.
Exclusion criteria: ADHD diagnosis, significant prior meditation experience.
22% of experimental and 12% of control participants disclosed a clinical diagnosis.
The homogeneous sample (Israeli university students, predominantly female) limits generalizability.
Five participants were excluded (cancellations, missing data, misassignment, failed attention checks).

## Notes

The finding that empathic disequilibrium is modifiable but not through emotional reactivity mediation opens important theoretical questions. The authors propose **interoceptive awareness** and **oxytocin pathways** as candidate mechanisms for future research. The authors importantly note that empathic disequilibrium should not be treated as inherently pathological—it is normally distributed and its clinical significance depends on context and intensity.
