## Summary

This study presents the **POWERED (Personalized Optimization of Weight Recovery in Eating Disorders)** interview, a novel **semi-structured clinical interview** developed to support **individualized expected body weight (EBW)** determination in **eating disorder treatment**. Currently, no reproducible, individualized method exists to guide EBW determination — a critical component of treatment planning for restrictive-spectrum eating disorders. The POWERED interview was designed to capture **medical history**, **psychiatric history**, and **eating disorder behaviour history** for use alongside automated growth chart-based tools that support individualized EBW determination. A preliminary evaluation with **N = 17 youth and young adults** (ages 14-20) with restrictive-spectrum EDs assessed **feasibility** and **acceptability**. Participants reported high confidence in their ability to provide accurate responses across interview domains, supporting feasibility. Qualitative feedback and quantitative ratings indicated strong acceptability. The study also examined preferences regarding EBW disclosure to patients, providing initial guidance on this ethically complex aspect of ED treatment communication.

## Clinical Insight

For **eating disorder clinicians**, EBW determination is one of the most consequential and contentious aspects of treatment planning, yet it has lacked a standardised, individualized approach. The POWERED interview fills this gap by providing a structured method for gathering the biographical and medical data needed to calculate patient-specific weight targets rather than relying on generic population norms. This is particularly important because eating disorder patients often have complex **growth trajectories** disrupted by illness, making standard BMI-based targets inappropriate. The interview's semi-structured format allows clinical judgment while ensuring **systematic data collection** across domains. For **dietitians** working in ED treatment teams, the POWERED interview provides a framework for contributing to treatment planning in a way that is both evidence-informed and patient-centred. The study's attention to patient preferences around EBW disclosure addresses a real clinical dilemma: whether, when, and how to share weight targets with patients who may fixate on numbers. The preliminary sample of 17 is small but appropriate for a feasibility study; the tool now requires larger-scale validation and testing of its impact on **treatment outcomes** such as weight restoration rate, rehospitalisation, and psychological recovery.

## Relevant For
- Eating disorder clinicians determining expected body weight targets
- Dietitians in ED treatment settings
- ED treatment programme directors developing standardised protocols
- Clinical researchers studying weight restoration in eating disorders
