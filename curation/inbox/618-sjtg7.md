## Summary

Two experiments (total N = 187 healthy adults, Experiment 2 pre-registered with >95% power) used a **two-arm bandit reward learning task** with sporadic regret probes ("Do you think you should have chosen the other option?") to decompose the cognitive architecture of **subjective regret**. Computational modelling using a **Rescorla-Wagner model with softmax decision rule** generated trial-by-trial **expected value (EV)** trajectories for each participant. **Logistic regression** per participant revealed that regret was predicted by two independent processes: (1) reward outcomes (Exp 1: t(83) = −10.61, p < .001; Exp 2: t(95) = −12.33, p < .001) and (2) EV difference between chosen and unchosen options (Exp 1: t(83) = −10.86, p < .001; Exp 2: t(95) = −11.64, p < .001). Rewards contributed significantly more than EV (Exp 1: t(83) = 5.91, p < .001), but both made independent contributions. A 2 x 2 ANOVA found large effects of reward on regret (Exp 1: **eta_p² = 0.750**; Exp 2: **eta_p² = 0.647**) and significant effects of ground-truth arm quality (Exp 1: **eta_p² = 0.130**; Exp 2: **eta_p² = 0.282**). Average task accuracy was M = 0.71 (SD = 0.06). The task design — withholding unchosen outcomes — mirrors real-world conditions where missed opportunities must be inferred rather than observed, increasing ecological validity.

## Clinical Insight

The mechanistic decomposition of regret into two independent pathways — **outcome reactivity** and **belief-based inference** — has direct implications for understanding **pathological regret** across psychiatric conditions. The paper documents that **pathological regret** is a hallmark of **depression** (Broomhall et al., 2017 meta-analysis), **anxiety** (Roese et al., 2009), while **attenuated regret** characterises **psychopathy** (Arielle et al., 2016) and high **impulsivity** (Sijtsema et al., 2022). This decomposition suggests that a depressed patient's excessive regret could arise from either heightened outcome reactivity (reacting too strongly to losses), maladaptive belief-tracking (persistently inferring that unchosen options would have been better), or both — each requiring different therapeutic approaches. For **CBT**, this framework suggests distinguishing between interventions targeting outcome interpretation (e.g., cognitive reframe of losses) versus those targeting belief calibration (e.g., reducing counterfactual inflation). For patients with **psychopathy or impulsivity**, the attenuated regret finding suggests these individuals may fail to update their behaviour from counterfactual inference, pointing to potential targets for **decision-making rehabilitation**. The task itself — a simple bandit paradigm with regret probes — could be adapted as a clinical assessment tool for quantifying which regret pathway is dysregulated in individual patients.
