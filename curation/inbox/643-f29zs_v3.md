## Summary

This study uses **intensive longitudinal data** from the **CHILL Study** — a smartphone intervention targeting **neuroticism** reduction — to examine how intervention **strategy content**, **timing**, and **situational context** shape proximal outcomes (response, compliance, satisfaction, and momentary positive affect). The sample included N = 399 participants (85% female, M_age = 25.48, 47% in Switzerland) generating **38,275 observations** across 12 habit-level and state-level intervention strategies.
**State-level strategies** (emotional support, behavioural activation, physical activity) outperformed **habit-level strategies** (cognitive restructuring, goal setting, skill building via modelling) on proximal outcomes. **Emotional support** and **behavioural activation** achieved the best overall profiles — above-average response rates, satisfaction, and positive affect changes. In contrast, **cognitive restructuring** and **skill building via modelling** showed below-average response, satisfaction, and affect despite higher compliance rates. For timing, response and compliance **decreased across the intervention period** while positive affect increased. For context, higher noise levels, proximity to a distressing event, and being surrounded by strangers reduced response rates — supporting the hypothesis that **less taxing environments** facilitate engagement.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists and intervention designers, this study provides actionable evidence for **optimising the delivery of therapeutic strategies**. The finding that **state-level strategies** outperform habit-level strategies at the proximal level challenges the common assumption that cognitive restructuring and goal setting — staples of CBT — are the most effective change mechanisms. While habit-level strategies may drive long-term trait change, they generate less immediate engagement and positive affect, which could undermine adherence. Clinicians designing **digital interventions** or structuring **between-session homework** should consider prioritising **emotional support and behavioural activation** as engagement anchors, then layering in cognitive strategies once therapeutic alliance and momentum are established.
The context findings have direct implications for **just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAIs)**: prompts delivered in quiet, low-stimulation environments (at home, alone, not recently distressed) yield better engagement. This suggests that mobile therapy apps should incorporate **passive sensing** to detect receptiveness and delay delivery during high-noise or high-stress moments. The decrease in adherence over time (despite increasing positive affect) highlights the **adherence-efficacy tension** familiar to therapists: clients may be benefiting but still disengaging. Strategies like **contingency management**, periodic strategy rotation, and progress feedback could help sustain engagement.

## Methodology Note

Paper type: quantitative_experimental.

__Statistical Power__: N = 399 with 38,275 ESM observations provides exceptional power for the multilevel models.
The intensive longitudinal design captures within-person processes with high temporal resolution.
However, the sample is predominantly female (85%) and young (M = 25.48), limiting generalisability.

__Preregistration__: The CHILL Study design was preregistered at osf.io/mc7v3.
The analysis plan was preregistered at osf.io/7mpvb.

__Effect Sizes__: Odds ratios for logistic multilevel models (response, compliance) and standardised fixed effects for linear multilevel models (satisfaction, positive affect) are reported in Table 2.
Specific values are detailed with 98.75% confidence intervals (Bonferroni-corrected for 4 outcomes).
The correction notice indicates data preparation pipeline errors were discovered and corrected.

__Statistical Rigor__: Multilevel modelling appropriately accounts for the nested data structure (observations within persons).
Bonferroni correction (alpha = .0125) was applied for multiple proximal outcomes.
The analysis plan was preregistered, though most analyses are described as exploratory.
A correction notice was issued for data preparation pipeline errors, though general conclusions were unchanged.

__Measurement Validity__: Proximal outcomes were measured via ESM: response (click), compliance (task completion), satisfaction (liking rating), and momentary positive affect.
Situational context was assessed through both self-report (ESM) and passive mobile sensing (GPS, Bluetooth, noise).
The combination of self-report and sensing data is a methodological strength for ecological validity.

__Open Science__: Strong — study design and analysis plan preregistered on OSF.
Dataset, codebooks, and analysis scripts available at osf.io/zb85f.
Published as a corrected postprint following data pipeline error discovery and correction.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 399 (85% female, M_age = 25.48, 47% living in Switzerland).
The gender imbalance is a significant limitation.
Participants were recruited for the CHILL Study, a community-based personality intervention.
Exclusion criteria and recruitment methods are detailed in the full paper and the preregistration.
