## Summary
This study compared **linear models** with **binomial** and **beta-binomial models** for analyzing **discrete, bounded scores** from neuropsychological tests, using data from **165 cognitively healthy individuals** on a **verbal memory test**. The **beta-binomial model** demonstrated substantially better fit than the linear model (**BIC = 463.6 vs. 583.4**). The linear model produced **predicted scores outside the real range** and showed poor model adequacy, with **32.73% of points outside the simulated envelope** compared to only **1.82%** for the beta-binomial model. The beta-binomial model naturally respects the **bounded, discrete nature** of neuropsychological test scores and provides **appropriate confidence intervals** that do not extend beyond possible score ranges. **R scripts** are provided for calculating **randomized quantile residuals** from beta-binomial models, enabling clinicians and researchers to assess model fit for their own data.

## Clinical Insight
For **neuropsychologists** and clinicians interpreting test scores, this study demonstrates that the standard practice of using **linear models** for bounded test scores can produce **misleading results** — including impossible predicted values and inappropriate confidence intervals. When clinicians use normative data to determine whether a patient's score falls within normal limits, the **statistical model underlying those norms matters**. A linear model may suggest that scores below zero or above the maximum are "within normal limits," which is clinically nonsensical. The **beta-binomial model** is particularly important for **tests with small score ranges** (e.g., 0-10 or 0-20 points), where the bounded nature of scores most severely violates linear model assumptions. The provided **R scripts** make this approach accessible to clinicians and researchers without advanced statistical training. For **clinical psychologists** constructing normative data for new assessments or cultural adaptations, adopting beta-binomial modeling would improve the accuracy of **confidence intervals** and **clinical cutoffs**. This is especially relevant for **brief screening instruments** commonly used in clinical practice where scores are discrete and range-restricted.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental study (methodological comparison).

__Statistical Power__: The sample of N = 165 cognitively healthy individuals is adequate for comparing model fit.
Four illustrative examples demonstrate the advantages of the beta-binomial approach across different data patterns.
The study focuses on model comparison rather than hypothesis testing, so traditional power considerations are less directly applicable.

__Effect Sizes__: Model fit is compared using BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion), AIC (Akaike Information Criterion), and half-normal plots with simulated envelopes.
These are appropriate metrics for comparing non-nested statistical models.
The BIC difference of ~120 points represents very strong evidence favoring the beta-binomial model.

__Statistical Rigor__: Multiple model fit criteria (BIC, AIC, half-normal plots, randomized quantile residuals) provide converging evidence.
The half-normal plots with simulated envelopes offer a visual diagnostic that clinicians can interpret intuitively.
The comparison includes both theoretical arguments and empirical demonstration.

__Measurement Validity__: The verbal memory test used is not specified in the abstract, which limits evaluation.
The study's focus is on the statistical model rather than the psychometric properties of a specific test.
The approach is generalizable to any discrete, bounded neuropsychological measure.

__Open Science__: R scripts are provided for researchers and clinicians to implement the beta-binomial approach.
Published as an open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
The provision of practical tools alongside the methodological argument is commendable.

__Participant Transparency__: Data from 165 cognitively healthy individuals are used.
The age range, education level, and other demographic characteristics of this normative sample are important for evaluating generalizability but are not detailed in the abstract.
The sample is described as cognitively healthy, which is appropriate for normative comparison purposes.
