## Summary

This study introduces the **Mental Health Digital Shadows (MHDS)** benchmark — a dataset of **75,000 outputs** from 15 large language models (LLMs) including models from Grok, DeepSeek, Mistral, Qwen, Microsoft, IBM, and OpenAI. The benchmark employs two conditions: a **human-shadow mode** (56,250 records) where LLMs are conditioned on sociodemographic and **Big Five** personality profiles alongside **DASS-21** depression, anxiety, and stress severity levels, and an **LLM-assistant mode** (18,750 records) without persona conditioning. Each record includes DASS-21 item-by-item explanations, an **Emotional Recall Task** (10 emotional words), and six topic-oriented open-ended responses addressing mental health themes including stigma, support, and AI psychologists. Validation against a human reference sample (N = 28,473 from OpenPsychometrics) showed that human-shadow LLMs produced DASS-21 distributions with appropriate rank ordering (**stress > depression > anxiety**) approximating human populations. **OLS regression** on human-shadow outputs (N = 56,250) confirmed **neuroticism** as the strongest predictor across all three DASS-21 subscales (depression b = 0.745, p < .001; anxiety b = 0.960, p < .001; stress b = 1.587, p < .001), consistent with the Lovibond and Lovibond (1995) validation. However, anxiety was systematically underestimated compared to human norms. Critically, **14 of 15 models** passed validation checks, while **Granite-4H-T** (IBM, 7B) showed intrinsic distress bias and complete inability to modulate responses by role. LLM assistants exhibited a systematic **positivity bias** with elevated **trust** and **joy** emotions, which could create false reassurance in clinical contexts.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians navigating the rapidly growing use of **AI-assisted mental health tools**, this benchmark provides essential evidence about both the promise and peril of LLMs in clinical contexts. The finding that LLMs without persona conditioning default to near-zero **DASS-21** scores — effectively an inherent "wellness baseline" — suggests they may inappropriately normalise distress when used as mental health assistants, potentially leading to under-identification of anxious or depressed users. The systematic **positivity bias** observed across LLM assistants (elevated **trust** and **joy** in emotion detection analyses) mirrors the **sycophancy** problem identified in AI safety research and raises concerns about false reassurance in therapeutic or support contexts. Clinicians should be aware that **22% of U.S. adults** now obtain health information from AI chatbots (Pew Research, 2026) and approximately 1 in 8 U.S. adolescents use AI for mental health advice (RAND). The **Granite-4H-T** failure case serves as a cautionary example: a model with intrinsic distress bias that cannot modulate its emotional output could amplify or misrepresent a user's mental state. The **Textual Forma Mentis Network (TFMN)** analysis revealed that the word **"struggle"** consistently emerged as a high-distress marker across models and DASS-21 subscales, while **"respect"** and **"discuss"** characterised low-distress responses — lexical patterns that clinicians might recognise in their own patients' language. The benchmark is released under **CC0 public-domain dedication** with an open pooling system, enabling the community to test new models and clinical prompting strategies.
