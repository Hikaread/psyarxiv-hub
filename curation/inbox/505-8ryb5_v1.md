## Summary
This experimental study examined the influence of a trauma-analogue stressor on updating of danger-related expectations and its association with analogue intrusive memories and resilience factors, grounded in predictive processing accounts of PTSD. Healthy participants (N=129) were randomly assigned to watch either a trauma film or a neutral control film. Belief updating was assessed the following day using a trauma-adapted Bias Against Disconfirmatory Evidence task (TR-BADE), where participants rated the plausibility of danger-related and neutral interpretations of ambiguous scenarios that unfolded toward safe or dangerous endings. Analogue PTSD symptoms were measured via a 7-day intrusion diary.

Contrary to the preregistered hypothesis, trauma and neutral film groups did not differ in overall TR-BADE scores, Negative Interpretation Bias (NIB), or Inflexible Interpretation Updating (IIU). This suggests that a single trauma analogue exposure may be insufficient to produce measurable changes in belief updating at the group level. However, within the trauma-film group, higher TR-BADE scores (reflecting greater difficulty updating beliefs) were positively associated with more diary-recorded intrusions.

Exploratory decomposition of the TR-BADE score revealed that Negative Interpretation Bias (the initial tendency to favor danger-related interpretations) predicted intrusive memories, while Inflexible Interpretation Updating (failure to revise interpretations given disconfirmatory evidence) did not. NIB was also associated with lower Sense of Coherence (SoC), and mediation analyses showed a significant indirect effect of SoC on diary intrusions via NIB. This suggests that individuals with weaker coherent worldview frameworks may be more prone to forming negative danger-related expectations after trauma, which in turn drive intrusive symptom development.

## Clinical Insight
The findings refine predictive processing models of PTSD in an important way: it is not the belief updating process per se (how rigidly beliefs are held in the face of disconfirmatory evidence) that drives post-traumatic intrusions, but rather the initial tendency to form negative, danger-related interpretations in the first place. This shifts the clinical emphasis from targeting belief rigidity to addressing the formation of negative appraisals, which aligns more closely with established trauma-focused CBT approaches like cognitive therapy for PTSD (Ehlers and Clark model).

The mediation finding linking Sense of Coherence to intrusions via Negative Interpretation Bias is clinically significant. It suggests that strengthening a person's overall sense of comprehensibility, manageability, and meaningfulness (the three components of SoC) may reduce the formation of threat-related expectations after trauma, thereby reducing intrusive symptoms. This provides a theoretical basis for interventions that target global cognitive frameworks (like narrative exposure therapy or meaning-focused approaches) alongside traditional trauma processing.

## Relevant For
Trauma-focused CBT practitioners, researchers studying cognitive mechanisms in PTSD, and clinicians interested in predictive processing approaches to psychopathology.

## Notes
Preregistered hypotheses not supported, but exploratory analyses yielded important findings. Analogue design limits direct clinical generalizability. Pre-registered on AsPredicted. From Saarland University.
