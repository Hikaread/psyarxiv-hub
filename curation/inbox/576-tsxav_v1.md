## Summary

This study investigated the immediate effects of a single **10-minute mindfulness intervention** on **food cravings** in adults (**N = 56**) from the UK and Australia, comparing a **virtual reality (VR)** mindfulness group with a control group viewing the same nature scene on a standard computer screen. **Food cravings** were assessed using a **food rating picture task** and the **Food Cravings Questionnaire-State (FCQ-S)**, alongside **state mindfulness** measures. **Mixed ANOVAs** indicated that the mindfulness intervention significantly reduced food cravings and increased mindfulness scores across both groups, with **no significant differences between VR and control conditions**. This suggests that the active ingredient was the mindfulness exercise itself rather than the delivery modality. The study addresses accessibility barriers to in-person mindfulness programmes, including limited capacity and mobility impairment. The finding that even a single brief session can reduce cravings has implications for **eating disorder treatment** and **food addiction** management. Authors include Sophie Hascher and Halley Pontes, who also developed the Sophie Sugar Addiction Scale (paper #562 in this collection).

## Clinical Insight

The finding that a brief mindfulness exercise reduces food cravings regardless of delivery modality (VR vs. screen) has practical implications for **eating disorder treatment** and **weight management** interventions. For clinicians integrating **mindfulness-based eating awareness training (MB-EAT)** or **mindful eating** approaches, this study supports the use of brief, accessible digital formats for clients who cannot attend in-person sessions. The absence of a VR advantage over a simple screen delivery is actually a positive finding for scalability — it means the intervention can be delivered through widely available devices (phones, computers) without requiring expensive VR equipment. For therapists using **DBT** skills with clients who experience **emotional eating** or **food cravings**, the study suggests that even very brief **mindful pausing** before eating can reduce craving intensity. The FCQ-S and food rating task used here could serve as session-by-session progress measures. The study's limitation — measuring only immediate effects — means that the durability of craving reduction remains unknown. However, as a proof-of-concept for accessible delivery, it supports the development of **just-in-time digital interventions** for eating-related distress that clients could access at the moment of craving in their daily lives.

## Relevant For
- Eating disorder therapists using mindfulness-based approaches
- Clinicians treating food addiction or emotional eating
- Digital health developers creating accessible mindfulness interventions
- Binge eating treatment providers

## Notes

PDF was not available; curation based on the full abstract description. The sample size of 56 is relatively small, limiting statistical power for detecting the VR vs. control difference. The single-session design cannot assess treatment durability or cumulative effects. The mindfulness intervention content is not described in detail in the available materials. The connection to the Sophie Sugar Addiction Scale (same first and last authors) suggests a programme of research on addictive eating behaviours. No clinical eating disorder sample was tested; findings are in a general adult population.
