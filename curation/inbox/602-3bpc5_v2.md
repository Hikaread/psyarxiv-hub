## Summary
This study reports the cultural adaptation and **psychometric validation** of the **Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire** for Brazilian Portuguese (**CAT-Q-BR**), a 25-item self-report measure assessing compensatory behaviors used by autistic individuals to mask or adapt to social expectations. The translation followed **ITC-TARES** standards with independent forward translations, expert judge evaluation (including five autistic adults), and CVI-based item refinement. A total of **1,394 participants** (923 autistic adults, 470 non-autistic adults) from across Brazil completed the CAT-Q-BR and the **AQ-10** screening tool. **Confirmatory factor analysis** supported the original three-factor structure (Compensation, Masking, and Assimilation) with acceptable fit in the autistic group (**CFI = 0.964**, **TLI = 0.96**, **RMSEA = 0.076**, **SRMR = 0.066**). **Internal consistency** was good (**alpha = 0.88** in the autistic sample). Higher **camouflaging** scores were significantly associated with self-reported **anxiety** (d = 0.49) and **depression** (d = 0.72), but showed no significant correlation with the **AQ-10**. **Gender-diverse** participants reported significantly higher camouflaging than cisgender men and women.

## Clinical Insight
The CAT-Q-BR provides the first validated instrument for measuring **social camouflaging** in Brazilian autistic adults, filling a critical gap in Latin American clinical assessment. The dissociation between camouflaging scores and autistic trait severity (AQ-10) is clinically important: camouflaging reflects a distinct process from trait expression, meaning clinicians should not rely on trait measures alone to identify individuals who may be masking. The robust association between higher camouflaging and both **anxiety** and **depression** (with a medium-to-large **effect size of d = 0.72** for depression) reinforces that sustained camouflaging is a **mental health risk factor** warranting clinical attention. The elevated scores among **gender-diverse** autistic individuals highlight a particularly vulnerable subgroup who may benefit from targeted support. Two items showed poor factor loadings (items 12 and 18), suggesting cultural specificity in how certain camouflaging behaviors (e.g., forced eye contact, impression management) are interpreted in Brazil, which clinicians should consider when interpreting individual item responses.
