## Summary
This quasi-experimental study examined whether a **dog-assisted intervention** could modulate **physiological stress responses** in 95 university students across two Spanish universities, using **diurnal salivary cortisol** secretion, the **cortisol awakening response (CAR)**, and **area under the curve (AUC)** as primary indicators. Participants were assigned to experimental (dog-assisted intervention) and control groups, with cortisol samples collected at six time points on both a regular academic day and the day preceding an examination. Both groups showed the expected circadian cortisol pattern with peak levels 30 minutes post-awakening. Significant CAR increases were observed on the pre-examination day in both groups (p < .001). However, the **control group exhibited a greater CAR increase**, while the experimental group showed a broader distribution of positive delta-AUC responses (p = .003), suggesting a **differential HPA axis activation pattern**. Psychological variables were assessed using the **DASS-21**.

## Clinical Insight
Animal-assisted interventions (AAIs) are increasingly deployed in therapeutic and educational settings, yet rigorous evidence for their **physiological effects** remains limited. This study contributes by using objective **HPA axis biomarkers** rather than self-report alone. The finding that the dog-assisted group did not show reduced cortisol but rather a **broader distribution of positive AUC responses** is clinically nuanced: it suggests the intervention may have promoted **physiological flexibility** or **stress buffering** rather than simple stress reduction. This aligns with emerging views that resilience involves adaptive regulation of the stress response rather than suppression. The anticipatory CAR increase before examinations is consistent with well-established literature on the **cortisol awakening response** as an index of anticipated demand. For clinicians considering AAIs, the study underscores the importance of measuring physiological outcomes and recognizing that therapeutic effects may manifest as **regulatory flexibility** rather than straightforward decreases in stress markers. The quasi-experimental design and lack of randomization are notable limitations, and the sample of university students may not generalise to clinical populations with diagnosed stress disorders.
