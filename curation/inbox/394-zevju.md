## Summary
This study examined whether day-to-day fluctuations in cognitive performance ("off-days") predict real-world behavior in 160 older adults (mean age ~72, many at elevated risk for cognitive decline) who completed in-home cognitive testing and had GPS-tracked driving behavior over approximately 3 years (19,628 individual trips). Participants completed brief cognitive assessments via the Digit Symbol Substitution Test (DSST) on a tablet up to twice daily. Driving outcomes included adverse events (hard braking, speeding, lane deviations) and the proportion of trips avoided at night.

Using mixed-effects models with AICc-based model selection, the study found that within-person deviations in DSST scores from each individual's personal mean were significantly associated with adverse driving events on the same day — even after controlling for global cognitive ability, age, and sex. A model including within-person cognitive deviations was 3 times more supported than a base model and over 8 times more likely than a model using only global cognition. Crucially, it was not the absolute level of cognitive performance that predicted unsafe driving, but deviations from one's own baseline. Higher cognitive variability was also associated with a greater tendency to avoid driving at night, suggesting partial awareness of off-days.

## Clinical Insight
This study has direct implications for assessing capacity and safety in older adults and clinical populations where cognitive fluctuation is relevant. The key finding — that within-person deviation, not absolute performance, predicts functional outcomes — suggests that clinicians should track cognitive performance over time rather than relying on single time-point assessments. For therapists working with older adults, dementia risk, or post-concussion populations, this supports incorporating ambulatory cognitive monitoring into treatment planning. The "off-day" concept may also apply to psychiatric conditions where cognitive function fluctuates (e.g., depression, bipolar disorder, chronic fatigue). Clients who report having a "bad day" cognitively may indeed be at elevated risk for functional impairment, and this should be validated rather than dismissed.

## Relevant For
Clinicians working with older adults, neuropsychologists, occupational therapists assessing driving fitness, therapists working with conditions involving cognitive fluctuation.

## Notes
Longitudinal naturalistic study with GPS-tracked driving. The DSST is a processing speed measure. The sample was drawn from studies of preclinical Alzheimer's disease, so generalization to healthy populations requires caution. Night-driving avoidance may represent adaptive self-regulation rather than impairment per se.
