## Summary
This meta-analysis synthesised evidence from 35 randomised controlled studies (total N = 6,834) examining the efficacy of stress optimization interventions, which aim to help people appraise stress as enhancing and growth-supportive rather than debilitating. Random-effects models revealed that stress reappraisal and mindset interventions produced meaningful changes across multiple physiological outcomes: increased cardiac output (CO; d = 0.41), decreased total peripheral resistance (TPR; d = 0.47), and reduced salivary cortisol (d = 0.20) in acute stress contexts. For subjective outcomes, interventions reduced recent distress (d = 0.30) and improved affective responses (d = 0.15), but showed no significant effect on reported stress arousal (d = 0.07). No effects emerged for pre-ejection period (PEP; d = 0.11) or heart rate (HR; d = -0.03). Heterogeneity varied by outcome (I-squared from 0% to 67%). The findings demonstrate that brief psychological interventions targeting stress appraisals can shift both physiological and subjective stress responses, with the pattern of increased CO and decreased TPR suggesting a challenge-type cardiovascular response associated with approach-oriented coping.

## Clinical Insight
These findings provide robust evidence that stress reappraisal interventions can serve as a practical, low-cost adjunct to therapy for stress-related presentations. The dual impact on both physiological (cortisol, cardiovascular) and subjective (distress, affect) outcomes supports a genuinely biopsychological mechanism rather than a purely self-report effect. For clinicians working with anxiety, trauma, and stressor-related disorders, these interventions could be integrated into psychoeducation phases of treatment or used as standalone brief interventions. The challenge-pattern cardiovascular response (increased CO, decreased TPR) is particularly noteworthy, as it mirrors the physiological profile associated with active coping and resilience. The minimal effect on stress arousal despite improvements in affect and distress suggests that these interventions work by changing the relationship to stress rather than eliminating the stress experience itself. Clinicians should note the moderate heterogeneity, indicating that individual differences moderate treatment response.

## Relevant For
Clinicians treating stress-related and anxiety disorders, therapists integrating CBT and third-wave approaches, health psychologists working with stress management, and researchers studying psychophysiological stress mechanisms.
