## Summary
This **mixed-methods** online survey study investigated **algorithmic exposure** to body image content on **TikTok** among 335 international users (99 male, 231 female, 5 non-binary; M age = 28.34 years, SD = 9.32) recruited via Prolific. Participants averaged **8.4 hours per week** on the platform, with 95.2% using it for more than one year. The study examined four categories of body image content: the **thin ideal**, **fit ideal**, **body purity**, and **body acceptance**. Nearly all participants (>99%) identified TikTok as reinforcing specific body ideals, with body acceptance content being the most frequently identified (34%), followed by muscularity (31.6%), body purity (26.9%), and thinness (20%).

**Regression analyses** revealed that exposure to thin ideal content (β = .141, p = .02) and fit ideal content (β = .198, p = .002) significantly predicted greater **pressure to conform** to societal appearance standards, as measured by the **SATAQ-SM**. In contrast, exposure to body acceptance content was associated with reduced pressure (β = -.118, p = .036). Over one-third of participants (36%) reported that exposure had directly affected their own **body perception**.

Participants were almost evenly divided between those who **actively searched** for body image content (49.3%) and those who encountered it **passively through algorithmic curation** (51.7%). Most concerning, **one-third of all users (33%)** had been exposed to **pro-eating disorder (pro-ED) content**, despite only 6% intentionally seeking such material. Those exposed to pro-ED content scored significantly higher for pressure to conform (p = .007, d = .29) and reported stronger impact on body perception (p < .001, d = .37). Participants expressed frustration that the **algorithm surfaced pro-ED content** following engagement with benign material such as recipes, underscoring the involuntary nature of harmful exposure.

Qualitative findings revealed that users demonstrated **awareness of algorithmic influence** but felt limited agency. Coping strategies included engaging with alternative content to "retrain" the algorithm and "**mindful scrolling**" — preparing mentally before opening the app to rapidly scroll past unwanted content. Notably, very few participants reported using **platform-provided moderation tools**, and none mentioned the "reset algorithm" function. A **24-month longitudinal follow-up** is currently underway.

## Clinical Insight
The finding that **passive algorithmic exposure** — not just intentional content-seeking — constitutes a meaningful pathway to psychological harm has direct implications for eating disorder assessment and treatment. Clinicians should routinely assess **social media exposure patterns** in body image and eating disorder evaluations, distinguishing between active content-seeking and passive algorithmic exposure. The low uptake of **platform moderation tools** among users highlights a critical gap between technological safeguards and real-world user behavior, suggesting that psychoeducation about algorithmic awareness may be more effective than relying on platform tools alone. The protective effect of **body acceptance content** (β = -.118) offers an evidence-based rationale for recommending engagement with positive content communities as an adjunctive coping strategy. The planned longitudinal follow-up will be valuable for establishing temporal directionality between algorithmic exposure and body image outcomes.

## Methodology Note
This is a mixed_methods paper.

__Measurement Validity__: The Sociocultural Attitudes Towards Appearance Questionnaire for Social Media (SATAQ-SM) is a well-established measure for assessing internalization of appearance ideals.
Single-item assessments of exposure types and psychological impact limit construct validity.
Qualitative thematic analysis was conducted on open-ended responses without reported inter-rater reliability.

__Open Science__: Data and materials availability not stated in the preprint.
Preregistration status is not reported.
The longitudinal follow-up study currently underway may address some methodological limitations.

__Participant Transparency__: Demographics are well-reported including age range, gender distribution, and platform usage statistics.
The sample is international but recruited through Prolific, which may limit generalizability.
Mean age of 28.34 years (SD = 9.32) means findings may not extend to adolescent populations who are particularly vulnerable.
