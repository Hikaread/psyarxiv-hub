## Summary
This study tested whether **empathic disequilibrium** — the imbalance between cognitive and emotional empathy — can be modified through a **brief mindfulness intervention**. A total of **157 psychology students** were randomly assigned to ten days of daily 10-minute guided mindfulness training (n = 89) or an active control condition involving emotion regulation lessons and daily mood ratings (n = 68). Empathic disequilibrium and emotional reactivity were assessed at baseline and post-intervention using validated self-report measures. The mindfulness group showed **significant reductions in both forms of empathic disequilibrium** (emotional dominance and cognitive dominance) compared to controls. However, contrary to the hypothesis, this reduction was **not mediated by changes in emotional reactivity**, even though reactivity itself decreased. Complementary **Bayesian and propensity-score sensitivity analyses** supported the robustness of these effects.

## Clinical Insight
Empathic disequilibrium has been consistently linked to psychopathology across multiple conditions — **emotional empathy dominance** is associated with anxiety, eating disorders, schizophrenia, and self-injurious behavior in autism, while **cognitive empathy dominance** is linked to depression and psychopathic traits. This study provides the first evidence that this imbalance is **malleable** through a very brief (10 minutes/day for 10 days) mindfulness intervention. For clinicians, this opens a potential adjunctive pathway for patients whose interpersonal difficulties stem from empathy imbalance — whether they feel **overwhelmed by others' emotions** or **detached from people they want to feel close to**. The finding that emotional reactivity did not mediate the effect suggests mindfulness works on empathic disequilibrium through **mechanisms beyond simple arousal reduction**, possibly through enhanced present-moment awareness of one's own and others' emotional states.

## Methodology Note
This is a randomized controlled trial with an active control group.

__Design__: Randomized controlled trial comparing 10-day mindfulness intervention (n = 89) to active control (emotion regulation lessons + mood ratings, n = 68).
Pre-post design with two measurement points.
A priori power analysis based on Monte Carlo simulations (N = 150 for β > .8, α < .05).
Final N = 157 after 5 exclusions from initial 162.

__Measurement Validity__: Empathic disequilibrium computed from the Interpersonal Reactivity Index (IRI) using established residual-based and absolute-difference approaches.
Emotional reactivity measured with validated self-report instruments.
Manipulation check confirmed mindfulness group engagement.
Both Bayesian analyses and propensity-score sensitivity analyses complemented frequentist results, strengthening evidence quality.

__Limitations__: Student sample (psychology undergraduates, 79.6% female, 92% Jewish Israeli), limiting generalizability.
Brief intervention duration (10 days) — durability of effects unknown.
Self-report measures only; no behavioral or physiological empathy assessment.
Excluded individuals with ADHD and significant prior meditation experience.
No follow-up assessment to examine maintenance of effects.

__Open Science__: Open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
Multiple analysis strategies (frequentist, Bayesian, propensity-score) enhance transparency and robustness.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians working with patients who experience empathy-related interpersonal difficulties
- Mindfulness-based intervention practitioners and researchers
- Therapists treating anxiety, depression, or personality disorders where empathy imbalance is prominent
- Researchers studying empathy as a transdiagnostic clinical target

## Notes
First demonstration that empathic disequilibrium is modifiable. The non-mediated finding regarding emotional reactivity is theoretically important — it suggests mindfulness reduces empathy imbalance through mechanisms distinct from general arousal reduction. The very brief intervention format (10 min/day) makes this potentially scalable as an adjunctive technique.
