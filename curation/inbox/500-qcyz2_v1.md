## Summary
This systematic review followed PRISMA guidelines to investigate whether and how dissociative symptoms manifest in chronic illness populations. Searching major psychological and medical databases, the authors identified eight articles meeting inclusion criteria. The analysis revealed several thematic domains: navigating chronic illness, coping, predisposition, experiential aspects, somatization, and ways of relating to self and others. The review found that dissociative symptoms are not a prominent or defining feature of chronic illness per se, but they appear in a pre-symptom fashion that can intensify into a distressing experience depending on the individual's mindset and coping strategies. The phenomenological analysis draws on embodiment theory to bridge clinical and lived-experience perspectives on dissociation, particularly in conditions where chronic pain or bodily changes alter one's sense of self and agency. The authors propose that dissociative experiences in chronic illness may represent a maladaptive extension of normally adaptive cognitive flexibility needed to cope with persistent bodily disruption.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians working at the intersection of physical health and psychological wellbeing, this review highlights an under-recognised phenomenon: dissociative experiences in chronic illness that may be overlooked or misattributed. Patients presenting with chronic pain, autoimmune conditions, or other long-term physical health conditions may describe episodes of depersonalisation, derealisation, or dissociative absorption that are functionally related to their illness experience rather than reflecting a primary dissociative disorder. Understanding these experiences through the lens of embodied cognition and adaptive coping (rather than purely psychopathological frameworks) can inform more nuanced case formulation. The emphasis on mindset and coping strategies as modulators of dissociative severity suggests that therapeutic approaches targeting psychological flexibility, acceptance, and present-moment awareness (such as ACT or mindfulness-based interventions) may be particularly appropriate. Clinicians should routinely screen for dissociative experiences in chronic illness populations and consider their functional role in the patient's overall coping repertoire.

## Relevant For
Clinical psychologists working in health psychology or chronic pain settings, therapists treating somatic and functional disorders, and researchers studying the interface between dissociation, embodiment, and chronic illness.
