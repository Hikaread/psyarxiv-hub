## Summary

This longitudinal qualitative study explored barriers and facilitators to wellbeing in 26 care-experienced children and 25 caregivers during the transition from primary to secondary school in England and Wales. Semi-structured interviews were conducted before and after the transition, one year apart, and analyzed using framework-based thematic analysis. Three overarching themes were identified: children's sense of belonging, relationships and emotional support; the extent to which systemic support met individual needs; and children's personal resources including coping strategies and self-perceptions. While some experiences mirrored those of the general population, distinct challenges were identified requiring targeted responses, particularly around relational stability, the impact of placement changes during the transition period, and the need for support systems that account for maltreatment history.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with looked-after children or those with maltreatment histories, this study highlights that school transitions represent a period of heightened vulnerability that extends well beyond the typical challenges of changing schools. The identification of belonging, relational support, and personal coping resources as the three core domains shaping wellbeing provides a clear framework for clinical assessment during transition periods. Therapists should proactively ask about upcoming school transitions during sessions with care-experienced children and consider whether the child's support network is stable enough to buffer the transition. The finding that systemic support often failed to meet individual needs suggests that therapists who work within or alongside social care systems should advocate for transition planning that is tailored to the child's specific relational and emotional needs rather than following generic protocols. The longitudinal design revealing that some children's wellbeing trajectories diverged over the transition year underscores the importance of ongoing monitoring rather than one-off assessments.

## Relevant For

Therapists working with looked-after children, foster carers, and adoptive families, particularly around educational transitions and school-based support
