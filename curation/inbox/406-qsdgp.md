## Summary
This secondary exploratory meta-regression analyzed 12 placebo-controlled fluoxetine trials in pediatric depression (N = 2,788; 1,325 fluoxetine, 1,463 placebo), investigating whether number of study sites (NOSS), participant age, baseline severity, or added psychological interventions explain the drug's declining efficacy estimates. NOSS increased over time but was not linearly associated with outcomes; log-transformed NOSS showed associations with decreased efficacy (B = 1.57, 95% CI 0.24–2.90, p = .02) and greater placebo response (B = −1.90, 95% CI −3.41 to −0.40, p = .01), but these were driven entirely by the outlying single-center Emslie et al. (1997) trial (Cook's distance = 3.92) and did not survive Benjamini–Hochberg adjustment. Age, baseline severity, and psychological interventions showed no significant associations with efficacy or placebo response.

## Clinical Insight
Fluoxetine's diminished meta-analytic efficacy in pediatric depression cannot be attributed to changes in trial design characteristics such as multi-site recruitment, participant age, symptom severity, or concurrent psychotherapy. Clinicians should be aware that the evidence base for fluoxetine as first-line pharmacotherapy in youth depression is weaker than historically assumed, and the decline likely reflects sponsorship bias and publication issues rather than methodological artifacts of modern trials.

## Relevant For
Child and adolescent psychiatrists, psychopharmacologists, and researchers interpreting antidepressant trial evidence.

## Notes
With only 12 trials, statistical power was limited. Age and baseline severity analyses used trial-level means, introducing ecological fallacy risk. The analysis was exploratory without a pre-registered protocol, and the CDRS-R outcome is clinician-rated and susceptible to unblinding effects.
