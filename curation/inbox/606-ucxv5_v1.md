## Summary
This cross-sectional survey study (N = 2,002 demographically representative single US adults) investigated associations between **GLP-1 receptor agonist** use (e.g., Ozempic, Wegovy, Zepbound) for weight loss and **dating outcomes**, **sexual experiences**, and **perceived stigma**. Overall, **7.7%** of participants reported GLP-1 use in the past year. Among users, **28.4%** reported improved dating lives and **32.9%** reported improved sex lives, while **13.5%** and **11.6%** reported worsening, respectively. **Men** were more likely than women to report increased dating confidence (45.5% vs. 29.5%), improvements in **sexual functioning** (22.1% vs. 9.0%), and concern about being judged for GLP-1 use (59.8% vs. 34.6%). **Women** were more likely to report no sexual changes (60.3% vs. 35.1%) and no concern about stigma. The most common reaction to GLP-1 disclosure was that partners **did not care** (13.5%) or were **supportive** (18.7%), though 16.1% reported critical reactions.

## Clinical Insight
As GLP-1 medications become increasingly prevalent, clinicians -- particularly those in **sex therapy**, **couples counseling**, and **eating disorder treatment** -- should proactively assess how these medications may be affecting patients relational and sexual well-being. The study demonstrates **bidirectional variability** in outcomes: some patients experience increased confidence, desire, and sexual functioning while others report the opposite, suggesting that **body image changes** interact with pre-existing psychological factors in complex ways. The gender differences are clinically noteworthy: men appear more vulnerable to **stigma concerns** around medicated weight loss, potentially reflecting conflicts with masculine ideals of effortful body transformation, while women may be more likely to **withhold disclosure** (51.3% had not discussed GLP-1 use with a partner). Clinicians should create a non-judgmental space for patients to discuss the psychosocial and relational dimensions of GLP-1 use, going beyond physical side effects to address **body confidence**, **sexual desire changes**, and **relational self-presentation**.

## Relevant For
- Sex therapists addressing body image and sexual functioning
- Couples counselors working with clients using weight loss medications
- Clinicians in eating disorder or bariatric psychology
- Mental health providers addressing psychosocial aspects of medical weight loss
