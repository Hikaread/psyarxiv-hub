## Summary
This study examined trait apathy and fatigue in 332 university students aged 30-50, measuring their relationships with multiple academic functioning indicators including autonomous and controlled motivation, study engagement, effort, stress, dropout intentions, performance, and satisfaction. Both apathy and fatigue were broadly associated with academic outcomes, but apathy emerged as the stronger and more consistent predictor, particularly for autonomous motivation, engagement, and satisfaction. The study controlled for family, household, and work-related factors, strengthening the case for trait-level motivational differences as meaningful predictors of functioning.

## Clinical Insight
The distinction between apathy and fatigue has direct clinical implications. Apathy reflects a deficit in goal-directed behaviour that may respond to interventions targeting values clarification, behavioural activation, and structured goal-setting, while fatigue may require energy management, sleep interventions, and medical evaluation. Therapists working with adult clients who present with stagnation at work or study should consider whether trait apathy is a contributing factor, as it may require a different therapeutic approach than mood-focused interventions. The mature student context also highlights that these motivational challenges persist across the lifespan and are not limited to younger populations.

## Relevant For
CBT therapists, counsellors working with mature students, therapists addressing motivational deficits in adult populations.

## Notes
Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality. Self-report measures only. The age range (30-50) captures mature students who may differ from traditional students in important ways not fully controlled for.
