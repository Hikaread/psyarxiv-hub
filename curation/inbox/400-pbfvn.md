## Summary

This study examined the intercorrelation structure of paraphilic interests in a US-representative sample (N = 1409; 50% women, 48.5% men, 1.5% nonbinary; ages 18-84, M = 46.34, SD = 15.99) recruited via Prolific. Fourteen paraphilic interests were assessed using DSM-5-aligned items (1-4 scale), along with hypersexuality via the HBI-19. Network analysis (Gaussian graphical model, GLASSO) revealed three clusters in the full sample: (1) Potentially consensual (transvesticism, masochism, asphyxiation, sadism, urophilia, fetishism), (2) Nonconsensual (exhibitionism, voyeurism, frotteurism, scatologia, pedophilia), and (3) Rare (coprophilia, necrophilia, zoophilia). Pedophilic interest was connected to other paraphilias in the full sample, contrary to predictions.

Latent class analysis identified three classes: Minimal interest (n = 1011, 71.8%), Moderate interest (n = 366, 26.0%), and High interest (n = 32, 2.3%). HBI-19 scores differed significantly across all classes (Minimal M = 9.55 vs. Moderate M = 23.3 vs. High M = 38.7, all p < .001).

In the pedophilic subsample (n = 142, those scoring 2+ on the pedophilia item; 92 men, 53 women), the network structure changed markedly: pedophilic interest became almost completely disconnected from other paraphilias, forming its own isolated cluster. Latent class analysis in this subsample yielded two classes: Primarily pedophilic (n = 109, lower HBI-19 M = 22.3) and Multi-paraphilic (n = 36, higher HBI-19 M = 40.0, p < .001). Sensitivity analyses using subsamples defined by other paraphilic interests (asphyxiation, voyeurism, zoophilia) and by hypersexuality levels did not reproduce this disconnection pattern, suggesting it is specific to pedophilic interest rather than a methodological artifact.

The network structure from a previous CSAM-user sample (Latt et al., 2026b) fit poorly in both the general population (CFI = .63, RMSEA = .133) and the pedophilic subsample (CFI = .73, RMSEA = .141), indicating meaningfully different correlation structures across populations. The authors speculate that hypersexuality may act as a third variable explaining why pedophilia correlates with other paraphilias in the general population but not in the pedophilic subsample.

## Clinical Insight

The finding that pedophilic interest disconnects from other paraphilias in the pedophilic subsample has important implications for clinical assessment and case formulation in sexology. In the general population, a person endorsing pedophilic interests is likely to also endorse other paraphilic interests, potentially reflecting a general hypersexuality factor. However, in clinical or forensic samples where pedophilic interest is more pronounced, this co-occurrence diminishes substantially. This suggests that clinicians should not assume that pedophilic interest implies broader paraphilic psychopathology. The three-class latent structure (minimal, moderate, high paraphilic interest) with corresponding hypersexuality levels provides a useful heuristic for triage: the High interest class (2.3% of the population) showed mean HBI-19 scores (M = 38.7) well above clinical thresholds, suggesting that comprehensive paraphilia assessment may be most efficiently targeted at individuals presenting with high hypersexuality. The consent-based clustering (consensual vs. nonconsensual) aligns with clinical distinctions between paraphilic interests and paraphilic disorders, supporting the DSM-5 framework that pathologizes only nonconsensual or distressing expressions.

## Relevant For

Sexologists, forensic psychologists, clinicians assessing paraphilic interests, therapists working with hypersexuality

## Notes

Cross-sectional survey data. Self-report measures of paraphilic interests may be subject to social desirability bias despite anonymity. The pedophilic subsample was relatively small (n = 142). Causation cannot be inferred from network models. Pre-registered on OSF.
