## Summary
This protocol describes a **21-day micro-randomized trial (MRT)** evaluating a **Just-in-Time Adaptive Intervention (JITAI)** designed to mobilize **social support resources** for adults with **elevated depressive symptoms** (BDI-II > 13) who are seeking outpatient psychotherapy. The "Support Now Study" targets N = 120 participants in Switzerland, delivering the intervention via smartphone app during the critical pre-therapy waiting period. The JITAI uses five daily **Ecological Momentary Assessments (EMAs)** to assess vulnerability (negative affect, stress, loneliness, rumination) and receptivity, then triggers support-seeking prompts based on one of three decision rules: (1) **vulnerability-triggered** (fixed cutoff ≥ 5 on 7-point scales), (2) **vulnerability + receptivity-triggered**, or (3) **support-need-triggered** (participant self-report of needing support), with a (4) no-intervention control condition. Primary outcomes are daily **depressive symptoms** and **received social support**; secondary outcomes include **loneliness** and **perceived social support**. Building on a prior feasibility study, this protocol also addresses a critical gap in the JITAI literature by empirically comparing triggering strategies to determine the most effective timing for intervention delivery. The study is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT07471529) and funded by the Swiss National Science Foundation.

## Clinical Insight
The pre-therapy **waiting period** represents a clinically significant gap: individuals with elevated depressive symptoms may wait weeks to months for psychotherapy to begin, during which symptoms can deteriorate. This **digital health intervention** offers a low-threshold bridge by activating patients' existing **social support networks** — a well-established protective factor that is often underutilized due to barriers in seeking help during moments of distress. For clinicians, this protocol is relevant in several ways: first, it models how **EMA-informed digital tools** can supplement clinical care between referral and first session; second, by comparing three **triggering strategies**, it directly informs the design of future JITAIs by testing when interventions are most effective — a question the field has acknowledged lacking empirical answers to ("we lack an empirical basis on when to intervene," Smyth & Ebner-Priemer). The finding from the feasibility study that **self-reported support need** outperformed fixed and personalized vulnerability thresholds for perceived timing and helpfulness challenges the assumption that researcher-defined cutoffs are optimal, suggesting that giving patients **agency** in the support-seeking process may improve engagement. If effective, this approach could be integrated into stepped-care models as a bridging intervention, and the triggering strategy findings would generalize to other JITAIs targeting depression and social functioning.

## Methodology Note
This is an other-type paper (clinical trial protocol), so Statistical Power, Preregistration, and Effect Sizes dimensions are not applicable in the traditional sense.

__Statistical Rigor__: The study uses a **micro-randomized trial (MRT)** design — a gold-standard approach for evaluating JITAIs that randomizes at the daily/occasion level within participants (1:1:1:1 allocation), enabling causal inference about intervention timing effects. Power analysis was conducted to target N = 120 based on the feasibility study data; the MRT design provides higher statistical power than between-subjects approaches by leveraging within-person comparisons across ~105 EMA occasions per participant. Decision rules were developed through a **participatory design** process involving the target population.

__Measurement Validity__: The primary measures — BDI-II (depressive symptoms) and daily EMA-based assessments — are well-validated instruments. The support-need triggering condition directly assesses participants' self-reported need rather than inferring it from proxy variables, representing a content-valid approach to measuring the construct of interest. The study also assesses **enacted support** (received support) separately from **perceived support**, a theoretically important distinction.

__Open Science__: Strong — ClinicalTrials.gov preregistration (NCT07471529), ethics approval documented, protocol published as SPIRIT-compliant structured summary, data sharing planned via OSF, and OSF project page (osf.io/4e8gz) available.

__Participant Transparency__: Inclusion: adults 18–70 seeking outpatient psychotherapy with BDI-II > 13, German-speaking, owning a smartphone. Exclusion: therapy starting within 4 weeks, suicidal ideation (BDI item 9 > 2), manic symptoms (MDQ > 7), shift work. Recruited in Switzerland. The sample is limited to German speakers and excludes acutely suicidal individuals.
