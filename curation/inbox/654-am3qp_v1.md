## Summary
This **Registered Report** (Stage 1) proposes a **meta-analytic mean and covariance structure analysis (MASEM)** of the **Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS)**, one of the most widely used **clinician-rated instruments** for assessing depressive symptom severity.
Despite its widespread use in pharmacological trials and clinical practice, evidence on the **dimensional structure** of the MADRS remains inconsistent, with studies supporting unidimensional, two-factor, and three-factor solutions.
The proposed MASEM will examine three aims: (a) which **latent structure** best represents MADRS responses and how reliably its factors are measured, (b) whether items or symptom dimensions function differently across **clinical moderators** (diagnostic group, pharmacological treatment, assessment time point) and **measurement moderators** (language version), and (c) whether diagnostic groups, treatment regimens, and treatment stages are associated with distinct **latent symptom profiles**.
The study is part of the **DFG Priority Program META-REP** and will use AI-assisted tools (**ASReview** for screening, **MASEMiner** for data extraction).
Upon completion, all coded data and analysis scripts will be made publicly available on **OSF**, and the review will be registered in **PROSPERO**.

## Clinical Insight
The **MADRS** is among the most commonly used clinician-rated depression scales worldwide, particularly in **pharmacological trials** where it serves as a primary outcome measure.
Understanding its **factor structure** is clinically important because if the MADRS captures multiple distinct dimensions (e.g., cognitive-affective vs. somatic symptoms), total scores may obscure differential treatment response across symptom domains.
For clinicians, this study will clarify whether interpreting **subscale scores** in addition to total scores is justified, which could improve **treatment monitoring** and **case formulation**.
The examination of **measurement invariance** across diagnostic groups and treatment stages will determine whether the MADRS measures depression the same way across different clinical populations — essential for valid comparison of treatment outcomes across trials.
The focus on **language versions** as a moderator addresses a critical issue in international clinical research and practice.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric paper (Stage 1 Registered Report — protocol only, no results yet).

__Measurement Validity__: The **MASEM approach** is the gold standard for examining measurement structure across studies, as it jointly estimates the factor model and its between-study variability.
The plan to examine **measurement invariance** across clinical and measurement moderators is methodologically rigorous and directly addresses the limitations of single-study factor analyses.
The use of **ASReview** for systematic screening and **MASEMiner** for statistical extraction introduces both efficiency gains and potential AI-related measurement error that will need to be managed.

__Statistical Rigor__: As a **Registered Report** with Stage 1 acceptance, the analysis plan is peer-reviewed and pre-specified before data collection, maximizing the credibility of eventual findings.
The **meta-analytic approach** pools evidence across many studies, providing much more stable estimates of factor structure than any single study.
The planned moderator analyses (diagnostic group, treatment, time point, language) are comprehensive.

__Open Science__: The study follows the highest open science standards.
It is a **Registered Report** with Stage 1 acceptance.
Data, code, and protocol will be available on **OSF**, and the review will be registered in **PROSPERO**.
Funded by the **DFG Priority Program META-REP**, which focuses on optimizing replicability.

__Participant Transparency__: As a meta-analysis synthesizing existing study data, participant characteristics will depend on the included primary studies.
The protocol describes the planned inclusion criteria and moderator analyses, which will capture heterogeneity in clinical populations across studies.
