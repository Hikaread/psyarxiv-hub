## Summary
This study examined whether the **verifiability** of victim statements — the extent to which details can in principle be checked against external sources — differs across three memory types in delayed **sexual abuse** reports from archival police files (N = 140). Statements classified as **continuous memories** (always accessible) contained more **verifiable details** than those classified as **recovered memories following an active search** (confirmatory analysis). Exploratory analyses further indicated that details reported following an active search were **more often disconfirmed** by other witness statements or evidence compared to those in continuous or spontaneously recovered memories. The **verifiability approach**, originally developed in deception detection research, was applied here to capture systematic differences arising from memory retrieval and reconstruction processes rather than intentional deception. **Interrater reliability** for memory type classification was established.

## Clinical Insight
The recovered memory debate has profound implications for clinical practice, particularly for therapists working with trauma survivors who report delayed recall of abuse. This study provides an empirically grounded middle ground: it does not question the reality of traumatic experiences but demonstrates that the **retrieval context** (continuous, spontaneous, or active search) systematically influences the quality and verifiability of reported details. For therapists, this reinforces the importance of understanding how memories are retrieved during therapy, particularly when clients engage in active searching for possible past abuse. The finding that **actively searched memories** contain fewer verifiable details and more disconfirmed details does not mean they are necessarily false, but it does suggest a greater role for **inference and reconstruction** in their formation — consistent with **reality monitoring theory** and the **fuzzy-trace theory** of memory. Clinicians conducting forensic evaluations should be aware that **verifiability** can serve as an additional indicator of statement quality beyond traditional credibility assessment methods. The study also highlights the complexity of distinguishing between genuinely recovered memories and those that may incorporate confabulated elements, a distinction critical in both therapeutic and legal contexts.
