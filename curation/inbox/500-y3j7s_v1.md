## Summary
This narrative review, incorporating a realist rapid review phase, provides a comprehensive examination and categorization of measurement instruments for Gambling Disorder (GD) in adults across screening, diagnostic, and treatment outcome evaluation purposes. The review searched PubMed, PsycInfo, Scopus, Medline, and Google Scholar, focusing on studies evaluating psychometric properties, diagnostic accuracy, and practical application of GD instruments. The authors identify a critical problem: instruments are frequently used interchangeably and inappropriately across clinical stages, with the same measures sometimes applied for screening, diagnosis, and treatment monitoring despite being validated for only one purpose.

The review distinguishes between brief screening tools (e.g., Lie/Bet Questionnaire, NODS-CLiP) for general population use, longer screening instruments (e.g., PGSI, SOGS) for enriched settings, and structured diagnostic interviews for formal diagnosis. A key finding is that sensitivity and specificity vary substantially across settings: the SOGS, for example, tends to over-identify cases in general populations (higher false positive rates), while the PGSI may not adequately differentiate milder but clinically important manifestations. For treatment outcome evaluation, the review identifies measures of symptom severity, processes of change, and recovery, noting a relative scarcity of validated process measures.

The review provides concrete, evidence-based recommendations for optimal instrument choice in specific clinical contexts including prevalence estimation in general populations, screening in enriched clinical settings, formal diagnosis, and treatment response monitoring. It identifies critical gaps including limited validation across diverse cultural populations and language groups, lack of gold-standard comparison studies, insufficient differentiation between in-person and online gambling, and the need for brief process measures for tracking mechanisms of change during treatment.

## Clinical Insight
This review provides an essential practical guide for clinicians and services working with gambling problems. The key clinical message is that no single instrument serves all purposes, and using the wrong instrument for the wrong task can lead to misidentification and inappropriate treatment decisions. For example, using a screening tool like the PGSI to make a formal diagnosis can result in both false positives and missed cases, while using a diagnostic instrument for routine outcome monitoring may be impractical and insensitive to change.

Clinicians setting up gambling services should use brief screening tools (e.g., Lie/Bet, NODS-CLiP) for initial case-finding, progress to structured diagnostic interviews for confirmation, and use treatment-specific outcome measures for monitoring progress. The review also highlights that existing measures have been validated primarily in Western, English-speaking populations, raising concerns about their applicability to diverse cultural groups. Services working with non-Western populations should exercise particular caution.

## Relevant For
Clinicians and service managers in gambling disorder treatment, researchers developing gambling assessments, and commissioners designing gambling treatment pathways.

## Notes
Comprehensive review from a strong UK-based consortium including Cambridge and Southampton. Practical recommendations are a key strength. Adult focus only; does not cover adolescent measures.
