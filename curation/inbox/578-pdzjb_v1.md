## Summary

Two studies investigated whether simulating future activities through different modalities could improve **prospective evaluations of behavioural engagement** in **dysphoria** and **depression**. Across both studies, participants completed **mental imagery**, **verbal reasoning**, or (Study 2) **virtual reality (VR)** tasks while evaluating future activities. In Study 1, both mental imagery and verbal reasoning improved **perceived likelihood of engagement**, **importance**, **vividness**, **anticipatory pleasure**, and **mood**. Mental imagery showed selective advantages for **vividness** and **anticipatory pleasure**, although verbal reasoning produced broadly comparable effects. Study 2 introduced a VR condition to test whether greater immersion would strengthen outcomes. Contrary to predictions, **VR did not produce additional benefits** over mental imagery or verbal reasoning. All three modalities improved engagement predictions relative to baseline, with effects observed across varying levels of dysphoric symptom severity. The findings suggest that the active ingredient is **prospective simulation** regardless of modality, and that even simple verbal reasoning about future activities can counteract the **anticipatory anhedonia** characteristic of depression.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists working with **depressed** or **dysphoric** clients who report difficulty anticipating pleasure from future activities — a core feature of **anticipatory anhedonia** central to **behavioural activation (BA)** — these findings have direct clinical implications. The key message is that helping clients mentally simulate or verbally reason about future activities can improve their predicted engagement, potentially increasing the likelihood that they will actually attempt those activities. In BA, the initial barrier is often not the activity itself but the client's belief that it will not be enjoyable or worthwhile. This study provides experimental evidence that brief simulation exercises can shift those beliefs. Importantly, the finding that **verbal reasoning** was as effective as mental imagery is clinically practical — not all clients can generate vivid mental images (some have **aphantasia** or trauma-related avoidance of imagery), and verbal reasoning offers an accessible alternative. The failure of VR to add benefits suggests that expensive technology is unnecessary; therapists can use simple guided exercises within sessions. For **CBT** practitioners, these techniques could be integrated into **activity scheduling** as a preparatory step: before assigning an activity, guide the client through a brief verbal simulation to boost their predicted enjoyment and likelihood of follow-through.

## Relevant For
- CBT and behavioural activation therapists treating depression
- Clinicians working with anticipatory anhedonia
- Therapists incorporating imaginal techniques into depression treatment
