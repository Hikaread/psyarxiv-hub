## Summary
This systematic review (PROSPERO registered, CRD42023493626) explored training provision, needs, and barriers affecting Link Workers (LWs) supporting individuals with mental health and wellbeing needs through social prescribing. Following PRISMA guidance, systematic searches across Embase, OVID MEDLINE, APA PsycINFO, and Social Policy and Practice identified 551 initial records, with an updated search adding 238 more. After screening, 17 sources (both peer-reviewed and grey literature) were synthesized thematically. The review had global scope but UK studies were most represented given the maturity of the UK social prescribing system.

The synthesis identified multi-layered training needs and barriers across five themes. Three training need themes emerged: (1) experiential learning for relationally intensive, emotionally demanding practice, including the need for supervised practical experience with complex cases; (2) core capability development in communication, boundary-setting, self-care, mental health literacy, risk navigation, and safeguarding/escalation knowledge; and (3) contextual competence in social determinants of health, inequalities, and population-specific needs. Two barrier themes were identified: (1) role ambiguity and referral mismatch, where LWs receive complex mental health referrals that exceed their role description and training; and (2) fragmented, under-resourced training systems characterized by inconsistent provision and reliance on prior experience that creates workforce inequities. Supervision, mentoring, shadowing, reflective spaces, and face-to-face learning were consistently valued, while generic or purely procedural training formats were often insufficient.

## Clinical Insight
Social prescribing Link Workers are increasingly functioning as frontline mental health workers, yet their training has not kept pace with the complexity of their role. This review identifies a dangerous mismatch: LWs are receiving referrals for individuals with complex mental health needs while being trained primarily as community navigators and coordinators. For mental health professionals, this review highlights the importance of understanding the social prescribing workforce and ensuring appropriate referral pathways, clear role boundaries, and adequate handoff protocols.

The finding that reliance on prior experience creates workforce inequities is particularly concerning. LWs from social work or peer support backgrounds may be better prepared than those from administrative roles, but this variability means service quality depends on the individual worker rather than on systematic training. Organizations employing or referring to LWs should advocate for standardized mental health training requirements and clear escalation pathways to ensure patient safety.

## Relevant For
Social prescribing program managers, primary care mental health teams, policymakers developing social prescribing standards, and mental health professionals who refer patients to social prescribing services.
