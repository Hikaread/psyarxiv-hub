## Summary

This study employed **classical grounded theory** to explore how employed people sustain meaningful working lives in the context of **emotionally charged work** — work that encompasses high emotional demands and **emotional labour**. The research addresses an important gap: while the negative consequences of emotionally charged work (burnout, secondary trauma, alienation) have been extensively theorized, the **prevention of these consequences** and the active strategies people use to maintain wellbeing have received far less attention.

The study began with **purposeful sampling** of eight employees and managers from **psychiatric hospitals**, then expanded through **theoretical sampling** to include 15 additional participants across diverse occupations: ministers of religion, military personnel, **social care workers**, educators, IT managers, and others (total N = 23). Data were collected through interviews and analyzed using **open coding** and **constant comparison** — the hallmark methods of grounded theory methodology.

The core finding is that participants' **main concern** was maintaining meaningful working lives despite the emotional toll of their work. Participants addressed this concern through a process the authors term **collective capacity building** — a grounded theory describing how people develop shared strategies, social structures, and relational resources to sustain themselves in emotionally demanding roles. The theory offers both theoretical and practical guidance for **sustainably organizing work** in occupations marked by high levels of emotional charge, with particular relevance for preventing **compassion fatigue** and **burnout**.

## Methodology Note

__Paper Type__: qualitative (classical grounded theory)

__Statistical Power__: Not applicable. Grounded theory is a qualitative methodology that does not rely on statistical power. The sample size of N = 23 is typical for grounded theory studies, where sampling continues until theoretical saturation is reached.

__Preregistration__: Not applicable for grounded theory, which is an inductive methodology. Preregistration is not expected for this research design.

__Effect Sizes__: Not applicable. Qualitative research does not produce effect sizes. Findings are presented as a theoretical model with illustrative data.

__Statistical Rigor__: Not applicable. The use of classical grounded theory (Glaser and Strauss) with open coding and constant comparison follows established methodological conventions. The combination of purposeful and theoretical sampling to reach diverse occupational contexts strengthens the transferability of findings. A potential limitation is that the initial sample was drawn exclusively from psychiatric hospital settings, which may have shaped early theoretical development toward clinically-influenced constructs.

__Measurement Validity__: Not applicable in the traditional psychometric sense. The trustworthiness of grounded theory findings depends on theoretical saturation, constant comparison, and member checking. Whether these were applied should be assessed when reading the full paper.

__Open Science__: Partial. Published as an OSF preprint, which provides open access. No mention of data sharing or supplementary materials was available in the screening description.

__Participant Transparency__: Adequate for a qualitative study. The researchers described their sampling strategy, initial and expanded participant pools, and the range of occupational contexts included. The sample spans psychiatric hospitals, military, clergy, social care, education, and IT management, providing diverse perspectives on emotionally charged work.

## Clinical Insight

This study speaks directly to a central concern in clinical practice: **burnout and compassion fatigue** among mental health workers. The finding that the primary strategy for sustaining meaningful work is **collective capacity building** — rather than individual resilience or self-care — has important implications for how organizations and clinical teams approach staff wellbeing. It shifts the focus from "take care of yourself" (which often places the burden on individual clinicians) toward **shared structural strategies** for managing the emotional demands of clinical work.

For therapists working in **psychiatric settings**, **social work**, or any role involving sustained empathic engagement with distress, the grounded theory of collective capacity building suggests that **peer support**, **shared meaning-making**, and **organizational structures** that acknowledge emotional labour are more protective than individual coping strategies alone. This aligns with growing evidence that **team-based interventions** and **organizational culture change** are more effective for preventing burnout than individual-level self-care programs. The diverse occupational sample also means these findings extend beyond clinical settings to any workplace where emotional labour is central, including **pastoral care**, **military mental health**, and **education**.

## Relevant For

- Clinical supervisors and team leaders in psychiatric settings
- Occupational psychologists working with emotional labour
- Organizational consultants focused on staff wellbeing
- Social care workers and therapists in high-emotion roles
