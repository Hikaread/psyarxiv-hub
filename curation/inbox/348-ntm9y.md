## Summary

This study investigated the clinical, behavioral, and neurophysiological effects of EMDR psychotherapy in 18 patients with PTSD and 24 matched healthy controls using a discriminative response task during EEG recording at baseline and follow-up. At baseline, PTSD patients showed greater symptom severity, slower and more variable responses, higher error rates, and reduced Bereitschaftspotential (BP) and prefrontal Negativity (pN) amplitudes, which index anticipatory motor preparation and proactive cognitive control. Following EMDR, patients exhibited significant reductions in anxiety, depressive, and dissociative symptoms, with faster, more stable, and more accurate task performance. Critically, BP and pN amplitudes increased after treatment to levels indistinguishable from healthy controls. No changes were observed in the control group.

## Clinical Insight

This study provides neurophysiological evidence that EMDR does more than reduce subjective distress — it appears to restore anticipatory cognitive control processes that are impaired in PTSD. For EMDR practitioners, this supports explaining to clients that the therapy is not simply about processing traumatic memories but may also be restoring their brain's capacity for proactive planning and response preparation. Clinically, this may help normalize patients' experiences of difficulty with decision-making and anticipatory anxiety, framing these as treatable consequences of PTSD rather than characterological deficits. The finding that neural normalization accompanied clinical improvement also supports the use of EMDR as a first-line treatment where anticipatory impairment is prominent, such as in clients who describe persistent difficulties with planning, initiating tasks, or feeling prepared for daily challenges. The small sample size (N=18) means these neural findings should be considered preliminary but clinically plausible.

## Relevant For

EMDR practitioners and trauma therapists working with PTSD clients who present with cognitive control difficulties, anticipatory anxiety, or impairments in daily functioning beyond intrusive symptoms
