## Summary

This cross-sectional validation study evaluated Sinhala and Tamil translations of three suicide-related measures -- the **Suicide Behaviors Questionnaire-Revised (SBQ-R)**, the **Interpersonal Needs Questionnaire-10 (INQ-10)**, and the **Acquired Capability for Suicide Scale-FAD (ACSS-FAD)** -- in a combined sample of N=649 (551 university students and 98 patients presenting with self-harm). The **SBQ-R** demonstrated a **unidimensional structure** with excellent model fit (CFI=1.00), good **internal consistency** (ordinal α=0.86 Sinhala, 0.92 Tamil), full **measurement invariance** across language and gender, and acceptable discrimination between community and clinical groups (**AUC=0.716** for all self-harm patients, rising to **AUC=0.876** when restricted to patients reporting suicidal intent). The original 7-item **ACSS-FAD** showed poor fit and suboptimal reliability, but a reduced **4-item version** (items FAD2, FAD3, FAD5, FAD7) demonstrated substantially improved psychometric performance across both languages. The **INQ-10** failed to support the hypothesized **two-factor structure**; a bifactor model revealed a dominant general interpersonal distress factor (ECV=0.56) with limited unique variance attributable to the **perceived burdensomeness** and **thwarted belongingness** subscales, leading the authors to recommend cautious interpretation of INQ-10 subscale scores in Sri Lankan populations.

## Methodology Note

__Sampling and Sample Size__: The study recruited 551 university students (409 Sinhala-speaking, 142 Tamil-speaking) from two universities and 98 Sinhala-speaking self-harm patients from a single tertiary care hospital. The sample size met the minimum of 10 participants per item criterion, but several limitations constrain generalizability: the student sample was predominantly female (~74% Sinhala, ~67% Tamil), no Tamil-speaking clinical group was included, and the clinical sample came from a single hospital. The absence of a community general-population sample means findings may not extend beyond young, educated adults or older clinical populations.

__Analytic Approach__: Analytic methods were generally well-chosen for a psychometric study. The authors used DWLS estimation with polychoric correlations for CFA, which is appropriate for ordinal Likert items. Model fit was evaluated with multiple indices (CFI, TLI, RMSEA, SRMR), and measurement invariance was tested sequentially using ΔCFI ≤ 0.01 criteria. Reliability was estimated using ordinal alpha (the primary estimate, appropriate for ordinal data), McDonald's omega, and Cronbach's alpha for comparison. ROC analysis with AUC, sensitivity, and specificity was used for discriminative validity. Known-group comparisons adjusted for demographic confounders using multiple linear regression. One notable limitation is the reliance on Pearson correlations for convergent validity with ordinal data, where polychoric or Spearman correlations would have been more appropriate.

__Validity and Measurement__: Structural validity was rigorously examined through CFA across multiple models (one-factor, two-factor, bifactor). The SBQ-R results are compelling with near-perfect fit indices. The ACSS-FAD item reduction from 7 to 4 items was data-driven but cross-validated against the Bangla adaptation, which removed the same three items -- strengthening the argument for a culturally consistent short form. For the INQ-10, the bifactor analysis was thorough (reporting ECV and omega hierarchical), and the honest conclusion that subscale scores should be interpreted cautiously is appropriate given the dominance of the general factor. Known-group validity was well-designed with demographic adjustment, though the unexpected finding of lower ACSS-FAD scores in the clinical group (contrary to IPTS predictions) raises questions about the construct's applicability in this cultural context. The use of the clinician-administered **Beck Suicidal Intent Scale (BSIS)** as a convergent validity criterion in the clinical sample was a strength, yielding a strong SBQ-R-BSIS correlation (r=0.61).

__Reproducibility and Generalizability__: Data and analysis code are available on OSF (doi:10.17605/OSF.IO/PQK7S), which is a notable strength. The follow-up study for predictive validity was largely unsuccessful (50% response rate, only 3 repeat self-harm events and 1 death), preventing meaningful evaluation of longitudinal prediction. Test-retest reliability was not assessed, limiting conclusions about temporal stability. The finding that the same three ACSS-FAD items performed poorly in both Bangla and Sri Lankan contexts raises important questions about the cross-cultural transportability of Western-developed measures of **fearlessness about death**, particularly in societies where Buddhist and Hindu concepts of death, impermanence, and rebirth may fundamentally alter how items about death are interpreted.

## Clinical Insight

The **SBQ-R** emerges as the most clinically ready instrument from this study, with its brief 4-item format, established **cut-off of ≥7** supported by ROC analysis, and demonstrated **measurement invariance** across both of Sri Lanka's major languages -- a critical feature given that Tamil-speaking populations in the Northern and Eastern provinces have historically been underrepresented in research despite experiencing some of the country's highest suicide rates. The strong correlation between SBQ-R and clinician-rated **suicidal intent** (r=0.61 with the BSIS) supports its use as a screening tool in emergency and psychiatric settings where brief, validated instruments are needed. However, clinicians should be aware that the moderate specificity (72%) at the ≥7 cut-off means approximately 1 in 4 positive screens may be false positives, and the relatively low sensitivity (63%) when all self-harm presentations are included suggests the SBQ-R may miss patients whose self-harm was not driven by suicidal intent. The mixed performance of the **INQ-10** and the need to substantially shorten the **ACSS-FAD** highlight a broader challenge: Western-derived theoretical constructs from the **Interpersonal Theory of Suicide** may not fully map onto the interpersonal dynamics and death attitudes prevalent in Sri Lankan **collectivist** and **religiously pluralistic** contexts, where belongingness may operate through family and community structures quite differently than in individualistic Western societies.

## Relevant For
- Suicide researchers conducting cross-cultural validation studies in South Asia
- Clinicians and researchers seeking validated screening tools for suicide risk in Sinhala and Tamil
- Investigators applying the Interpersonal Theory of Suicide in non-Western populations
- Public health researchers studying suicide epidemiology in multilingual, multi-ethnic settings

## Notes

Data and materials are openly available on OSF. The clinical sample was limited to Sinhala-speaking patients from a single hospital, and predictive validity could not be assessed due to low follow-up rates (50%) and few repeat events. The paper notes that GPT-5.5 was used for language editing only, not for analysis or interpretation.
