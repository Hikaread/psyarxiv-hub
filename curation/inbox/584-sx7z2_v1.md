## Summary
This paper introduces a **Cognitive Framework** designed to improve the design, mechanistic specificity, and evaluative interpretability of **Music-Based Interventions (MBIs)** targeting cognition. The framework addresses a critical limitation in current MBI research: the tendency to treat MBIs as a uniform category despite vast heterogeneity in musical content, delivery format, dosage, and hypothesised mechanisms. The authors argue that both music and cognition must be defined at a **granular subcomponent level** rather than as broad constructs. For example, **auditory perception** encompasses spectral processing, temporal processing, and auditory scene analysis — each differentially engaged by different musical activities. Similarly, **executive functions** must be decomposed into **working memory**, **inhibitory control**, and **cognitive flexibility**. The framework provides a structured model for explicitly linking specific **musical features** (rhythm, melody, harmony, timbre) to hypothesised **cognitive processes**, enabling mechanism-informed hypotheses and more precise outcome measurement. It builds on and extends existing efforts including the **NIH MBI Toolkit** and the **Experimental Medicine Framework**, but goes further by requiring process-level specificity in both the intervention content and the outcome measures.

## Clinical Insight
For therapists and clinical researchers using or studying **music therapy** and music-based cognitive rehabilitation, this framework offers a much-needed methodological foundation. Current MBI studies often fail to specify which musical elements are hypothesised to engage which cognitive processes, making it difficult to interpret why an intervention succeeded or failed, and nearly impossible to replicate findings. This is particularly problematic in clinical populations such as those with **dementia**, **Parkinson's disease**, or **traumatic brain injury**, where MBIs are increasingly used for **cognitive rehabilitation** but evidence remains mixed. The framework's emphasis on subcomponent-level specification supports a shift toward **precision music-based interventions** — analogous to precision medicine — where the musical content is deliberately matched to the individual's specific cognitive profile and therapeutic goals. Clinicians can use this framework to better articulate the theoretical rationale for their musical choices and to select outcome measures that directly map onto the targeted cognitive processes. The paper is conceptual rather than empirical, so it provides guidance for future research rather than direct evidence of efficacy.

## Relevant For
- Music therapists designing cognitive-focused interventions
- Clinical researchers developing or evaluating MBIs
- Neuropsychologists working with cognitive rehabilitation
- Therapists using creative arts modalities in clinical practice
