## Summary
Cross-sectional **validation study** evaluating the **psychometric properties** of the Sinhala and Tamil versions of three **suicide risk measures**: the **Suicide Behaviors Questionnaire–Revised** (SBQ-R), the **Interpersonal Needs Questionnaire–10** (INQ-10), and the **Acquired Capability for Suicide Scale–Fearlessness About Death** (ACSS-FAD). The study included **551 university students** (409 Sinhala-speaking, 142 Tamil-speaking) and **98 patients** presenting with self-harm at a Sri Lankan hospital. Structural validity was assessed via CFA, with measurement invariance tested across language and gender. The **SBQ-R** demonstrated a stable unidimensional structure with good internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha > 0.70), measurement invariance across language and gender, and acceptable discrimination between students and clinical participants (AUC = 0.716 overall, rising to AUC = 0.876 when restricted to patients reporting suicidal intent). The established cut-off of ≥7 was supported. The **INQ-10** showed poor fit for the hypothesized two-factor structure (perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness); a bifactor model suggested a dominant general factor with mixed construct validity evidence. The **ACSS-FAD** required item reduction from 7 to 4 items — the same 3 items removed as in the Bangla adaptation — with the shortened version demonstrating satisfactory validity and reliability. Cultural factors, particularly Buddhist and Hindu concepts of death, appeared to influence responses to abstract death-related items.

## Clinical Insight
The **SBQ-R** emerges as a viable brief **suicide screening tool** for use in both Sinhala and Tamil-speaking Sri Lankan populations, supporting its deployment in primary care, emergency, and community settings where formal mental health assessment is unavailable. The ≥7 cut-off performs well for identifying clinically at-risk individuals. However, the mixed performance of the **INQ-10** and shortened **ACSS-FAD** indicates that the **Interpersonal Theory of Suicide** (IPTS) constructs — particularly thwarted belongingness and fearlessness about death — may not transfer straightforwardly to South Asian collectivist contexts where belonging is configured through family and community rather than individual interpersonal relationships, and where religious concepts of death (Buddhist contemplation of impermanence, Hindu beliefs in rebirth) alter the meaning of death-related items. Clinicians working with South Asian diaspora or in multicultural settings should be aware that standard IPTS-based assessment may require cultural adaptation, and that perceived burdensomeness appears to be a more robust cross-cultural marker than thwarted belongingness.

## Relevant For
- Suicide prevention clinicians working in South Asian or multicultural settings
- Researchers conducting cross-cultural psychometric validation of suicide risk measures
- Clinicians using the Interpersonal Theory of Suicide framework in non-Western populations
- Assessment developers adapting Western measures for collectivist cultural contexts

## Notes
Key finding: SBQ-R validated for Sri Lanka with cut-off ≥7; INQ-10 and ACSS-FAD require cultural adaptation. Study limited by student sample, small Tamil clinical group, and low follow-up rates for predictive validity.
