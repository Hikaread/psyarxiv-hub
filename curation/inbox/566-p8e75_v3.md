## Summary

This theoretical paper addresses a phenomenon termed the **mindfulness ceiling** — the observation that some experienced practitioners of **mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs)** attain high scores on standard mindfulness measures while showing limited corresponding change in **self-referential processing**. Drawing on the **Yogacara eight-consciousness architecture**, the paper locates the ceiling at the stratum of **klista-manas** — a persistent background process of **self-appropriation** beneath ordinary introspective access. The author diagnoses an **awareness trap**: a three-layer progressive entrapment in which identification shifts from mental content (Layer 1), to the observer-position (Layer 2, the primary ceiling), to awareness of awareness itself (Layer 3, commonly mistaken for liberation). The paper argues that standard **MBCT** and **MBSR** programs, which cultivate decentering and meta-awareness, may inadvertently strengthen the very self-grasping patterns they aim to dissolve when operating at Layer 2. The proposed solution involves practices that specifically target the background self-appropriation process, drawing on advanced Yogacara contemplative methods. ## Clinical Insight

For **MBSR and MBCT instructors** and therapists integrating mindfulness into clinical practice, this paper articulates a structural mechanism that may explain why some clients plateau in treatment despite diligent practice. The **mindfulness ceiling** concept is clinically relevant: therapists may observe clients who develop strong observational skills and can report their internal experiences with precision, yet continue to struggle with the same self-critical patterns, perfectionism, or identity-based suffering that brought them to therapy. The paper's analysis suggests this is not a failure of technique adherence but a structural limitation — the **decentering** cultivated by standard MBIs operates at a different level than the **self-grasping** that maintains certain forms of psychological distress. For **ACT practitioners**, this analysis resonates with the distinction between **cognitive defusion** and **self-as-context**, suggesting that some clients may achieve defusion without accessing the more fundamental shift in self-experience that characterizes psychological flexibility. The three-layer model provides a heuristic for case formulation: clients stuck at Layer 2 may benefit from approaches that explicitly address self-identification patterns, such as **schema therapy** work on modes, **compassion-focused therapy (CFT)** targeting shame-based self-attachment, or more advanced contemplative practices. The paper does not advocate abandoning MBIs but rather supplementing them with practices targeting deeper self-structural transformation.

## Notes

Single-author theoretical/philosophical paper from a Buddhist studies rather than clinical psychology perspective. The full 42-page PDF was extracted. The paper employs textual reconstruction and comparative conceptual analysis rather than empirical methods. The "mindfulness ceiling" phenomenon is well-documented in the contemplative science literature (Lindahl et al., 2017; Vago & Silbersweig, 2012) but the Yogacara framing is novel. Clinical implications are inferred rather than empirically tested. A companion paper (dnex7_v3) addresses the same topic from a different angle.
