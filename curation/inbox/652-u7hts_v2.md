## Summary
This study developed and validated the **Korean Self-Conscious Emotion Dictionary (KSED)**, a computational linguistics tool for measuring four **self-conscious emotions**: shame, guilt, **authentic pride**, and **hubristic pride** in Korean text.
The dictionary was validated using **three independent datasets**.
The first two validations demonstrated that **KSED word frequencies** corresponded with emotions expressed in **essays** and **movie comments** — shame-related words were more frequent in shame-related contexts, etc.
The third validation showed that KSED word frequencies **predicted human-coded emotion ratings** of online comments, demonstrating convergent validity with human judgment.
The study also examined **associations between self-conscious emotion word usage and mental health indicators** to illustrate the dictionary's applied value.
**English translations** of all KSED entries are provided to enable use by non-Korean-speaking researchers.
The KSED addresses a significant gap: most computational emotion analysis tools focus on **basic emotions** (happiness, anger, sadness) and **English-language** text, neglecting self-conscious emotions and non-English languages.

## Clinical Insight
Self-conscious emotions (shame, guilt, pride) are **central to clinical psychology** — they play key roles in depression, social anxiety, personality disorders, and trauma.
The KSED provides a **novel methodological tool** for clinicians and researchers interested in measuring these emotions from **natural language data** (e.g., therapy session transcripts, journal entries, online forum posts, social media).
For therapists using **narrative-based or emotion-focused approaches**, the KSED could enable systematic tracking of how clients' self-conscious emotion language changes over the course of treatment.
The distinction between **authentic pride** (achievement-based, socially adaptive) and **hubristic pride** (dominance-based, potentially maladaptive) is clinically important — hubristic pride has been linked to **narcissism, aggression, and interpersonal difficulties**, and the KSED allows these to be measured separately in text.
The demonstration that KSED word frequencies predict **mental health indicators** suggests potential applications in **screening and monitoring** — for example, tracking shame proneness in clients with depression or social anxiety through their written language.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric paper.

__Statistical Power__: The study used three validation datasets, though the **sample sizes** for each are not detailed in the abstract.
For a dictionary validation study, the critical question is whether the word lists reliably discriminate between emotion categories, which was demonstrated across multiple datasets and modalities (essays, movie comments, online comments).
A limitation is that the generalizability of the dictionary depends on the **breadth and representativeness** of the source corpora used to construct it.

__Statistical Rigor__: The **three-dataset validation approach** is methodologically strong, providing evidence across different text types and validation criteria (self-reported vs. human-coded).
Demonstrating convergence between **computational dictionary scores and human ratings** is the gold standard for dictionary validation.
The inclusion of **English translations** facilitates cross-linguistic research and enables non-Korean-speaking researchers to evaluate the word choices.

__Measurement Validity__: The KSED was validated for **content validity** (theoretically driven word selection for four emotion categories), **convergent validity** (dictionary scores predicting human-coded ratings), and **discriminant validity** (shame words more frequent in shame contexts than other contexts).
A limitation is that self-report emotion measures were used as the ground truth in some validations, which themselves have known biases.
The dictionary captures **explicit emotion word usage** but may miss implicit or metaphorical expressions of self-conscious emotions.

__Open Science__: The study is available as a **PsyArXiv preprint**.
Providing **English translations** alongside the Korean dictionary supports open science and cross-linguistic research.
It is not clear whether the **full word lists, validation data, or analysis code** are publicly available beyond what is reported in the paper.

__Participant Transparency__: The three validation datasets drew from different populations (essay writers, movie commenters, online commenters).
Specific demographic details about the participants in each dataset were not provided in the abstract.
The **online comment** validation dataset likely includes a broad range of users, but selection biases (e.g., more active users contributing more data) are possible.
The Korean-language focus is both a strength (filling a gap) and a limitation (findings may not generalize to other languages).
