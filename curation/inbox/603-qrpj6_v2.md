## Summary
This **qualitative study** explored factors affecting **resilience** among autistic higher education students during the transition back to in-person classes following COVID-19 lockdowns in the United Kingdom. **Semi-structured interviews** were conducted with nine autistic participants (aged 20-36) across UK colleges and universities. **Inductive thematic analysis** identified six main factors organized into three **enablers** of resilience (support systems including family, institutional, and mental health professional support; coping strategies such as structured planning and music-based techniques; and positive reappraisal of the transition) and three **barriers** (sensory overwhelm in campus environments; social anxiety and masking demands; and lack of institutional accommodations). The study highlights that the return to in-person learning was experienced as a significant **life transition** requiring active adaptation, with resilience emerging as a dynamic, context-dependent process rather than a fixed trait.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians working with autistic adults, this study underscores the importance of framing **resilience** as a contextual process shaped by environmental factors rather than an individual deficit. The identification of **sensory overwhelm** and **masking demands** as primary barriers suggests that clinical interventions should prioritize environmental modification and self-advocacy skills alongside traditional coping strategies. The finding that **structured planning** and **music-based coping** were effective enablers offers concrete, low-cost intervention strategies that therapists can incorporate. Notably, the role of **institutional accommodations** (or their absence) as a barrier highlights the systemic dimension of autistic distress in academic settings, pointing to the value of **advocacy-focused therapeutic work**. The positive role of professional mental health support as an enabler reinforces the importance of accessible **autism-informed counseling** services in higher education.

## Relevant For
- Therapists supporting autistic university students
- Clinicians working on transition and resilience in autism
- Disability services professionals in higher education
- Autistic adults navigating major life transitions
