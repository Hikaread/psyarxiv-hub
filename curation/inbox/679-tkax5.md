## Summary
Two preregistered within-subject studies (Study 1: N = 24; Study 2: N = 20) used **AI-generated deepfakes** and **immersive VR** to examine how **self-recognition** and **self-identification** with a virtual child influence **self-compassion** during a comforting exercise. Participants verbally comforted an emotionally responsive virtual child in VR. Across both studies, **self-compassion increased and self-criticism decreased** from pre- to post-intervention regardless of condition. In Study 1, self-recognition (own childhood face vs. unfamiliar child) was associated with greater **physical and emotional closeness**, increased **self-connectedness**, less distinct **body boundaries**, and heightened **negative affect**. **Heart rate variability (RMSSD)** increased post-intervention. Neither self-recognition nor self-identification (synchronous vs. asynchronous visuotactile stimulation in Study 2) produced robust differences in self-compassion outcomes.

## Clinical Insight
This study contributes to the growing evidence on **self-externalization techniques** for cultivating self-compassion, alongside established methods like **chairwork**, **imagery rescripting**, and **compassion-focused therapy** exercises. The consistent finding that comforting a virtual child self increased self-compassion across all conditions — regardless of the degree of self-recognition or identification — suggests that the **act of externalizing and comforting itself** may be the active ingredient. For therapists, this implies that even simple externalization exercises (e.g., writing a letter to one's younger self) may be effective without requiring high-tech VR or deepfake technology. The increased **negative affect** with self-recognition is noteworthy: clinicians using childhood-based exercises should be prepared for clients to experience **emotional activation** before self-compassion benefits emerge. The HRV increase (RMSSD) provides physiological evidence of **parasympathetic engagement**, supporting the biological plausibility of the intervention.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper.

__Statistical Power__: Study 1 (N = 24) and Study 2 (N = 20) are **very small samples** for within-subject designs, severely limiting power to detect interaction effects.
The null findings for the experimental manipulations may reflect genuine absence of effects or may be due to **Type II error**.
The studies were explicitly framed as pilot/exploratory despite being preregistered.

__Preregistration__: Both studies were **preregistered**, which is commendable.
The distinction between confirmatory (null) and exploratory findings was maintained.

__Effect Sizes__: Self-compassion changes from pre- to post-intervention were reported as significant but no standardized effect sizes were provided in the abstract.
HRV (RMSSD) changes were reported as significant in Study 1 but effect magnitude was not specified.

__Statistical Rigor__: The within-subject design is appropriate for the research question.
The very small N limits the interpretability of null manipulation effects.
Using **AI-generated deepfakes** of participants' childhood faces is methodologically innovative but the reliability of the self-recognition manipulation was not reported.

__Measurement Validity__: Self-compassion and self-criticism were assessed via questionnaires — likely the **Self-Compassion Scale (SCS)** and a self-criticism measure.
**HRV (RMSSD)** provides an objective physiological index of parasympathetic activity.
The use of **acoustic vocal features** as an additional physiological proxy is novel but its validity for measuring compassion is unestablished.

__Open Science__: Both studies were **preregistered**.
The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv** (version 3).
No data or code availability was mentioned.

__Participant Transparency__: Study 1: N = 24.
Study 2: N = 20.
No demographic information was available in the abstract.
The samples are too small and underdescribed for generalizability claims.
