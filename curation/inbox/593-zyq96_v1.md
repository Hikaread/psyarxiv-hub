## Summary
This study developed an **ultra-brief parent-report measure** derived from the **Revised Multidimensional Assessment of Parenting Scale (MAPS V2)**, using **item response theory (IRT) graded response models** to maximize psychometric information while minimizing item count. In a national sample of parents of children aged 3–17 (Sample 1; N=1,699), items were selected for high **discrimination** and optimal coverage across the latent trait continuum. The resulting brief measure was then validated in a second sample (Sample 2), demonstrating strong **reliability**, **factorial validity**, and **measurement invariance** across key demographic groups including **race/ethnicity**, **income**, and **family structure**. The ultra-brief format addresses a critical barrier in clinical practice and research: existing parenting measures are often lengthy and burdensome, leading to respondent fatigue and incomplete data, particularly among diverse and high-stress families who stand to benefit most from assessment.

## Clinical Insight
Parenting behavior is one of the strongest predictors of both **child mental health outcomes** and **parental wellbeing**, yet assessment in clinical settings is hampered by the length of existing instruments. An ultra-brief, psychometrically sound measure that performs **equivalently across diverse family structures** (single-parent, multigenerational, blended families) is a genuinely useful clinical tool. The use of **IRT** rather than classical test theory for item selection is methodologically rigorous — it ensures the brief version retains maximum information at the item level. For therapists working in family-based interventions, this measure could serve as a quick **session-by-session monitoring tool** or intake screener. The demonstrated **measurement invariance** across race/ethnicity and income is particularly important for equitable clinical practice, as it means scores can be meaningfully compared across diverse populations without bias from the instrument itself.

## Relevant For

- Clinicians working with diverse family structures
- Child and adolescent mental health services
- Researchers needing brief, validated parenting assessment
