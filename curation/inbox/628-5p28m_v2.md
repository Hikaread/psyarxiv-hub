## Summary
Two large-scale surveys of **American knowledge workers** (Study 1: N = 32,245 across six waves; Study 2: N = 5,216 longitudinal) conducted during the **COVID-19 pandemic** examined the relationship between **remote work** and both **racial discrimination** experiences and **well-being outcomes**. First, workers of color reported significantly **less racial discrimination** when working remotely compared to in-person (within-person analyses holding constant individual differences), with Study 2 revealing that this effect was **more pronounced for women of color** than men of color (β interaction effect). Second, greater **remote work frequency** was associated with better well-being across multiple outcomes including **work-life balance** (β = .21, 95% CI [.19, .22] for White workers), **job satisfaction**, **psychological safety**, **affective commitment**, **sense of belonging**, and **reduced stress**. For most well-being outcomes, the benefits of remote work were **equivalent across racial groups**; however, for work-life balance, flexibility, and focus, White workers benefited slightly more than workers of color. Results held using **propensity score matching** and after controlling for demographic and organizational characteristics.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides the first systematic evidence that **remote work reduces experiences of racial discrimination** for workers of color, with implications for **occupational mental health** and **workplace wellbeing interventions**. The finding that even small, subtle acts of racial discrimination can have significant downstream consequences on performance and mental health makes this reduction clinically meaningful. For **therapists and counsellors** working with clients from marginalised racial backgrounds, remote work may represent an underutilised **environmental coping strategy** worth discussing in treatment planning, particularly for clients experiencing workplace-based racial stress. The **intersectional finding** — that women of color experienced the greatest reduction in discrimination when remote — highlights the importance of considering intersecting identities in case formulation. The consistency of well-being benefits across racial groups suggests that **remote work accommodations** could be framed as both an equity intervention and a universal well-being support. However, the COVID-19 context may limit generalisability, and the cross-sectional survey design cannot establish causation. Clinicians should note that for some outcomes (turnover intentions), remote work frequency showed no association, suggesting its benefits are not uniform across all work-related psychological domains.

## Methodology Note
__Statistical Power__: Both samples were very large (N = 32,245; N = 5,216), providing excellent power for detecting even small effects; however, the studies were not preregistered, and no formal power analysis was conducted. __Preregistration__: No aspects of either study were preregistered. __Effect Sizes__: Standardised regression coefficients with 95% confidence intervals were consistently reported for all key analyses, enabling evaluation of both statistical and practical significance; within-person effect sizes for the discrimination reduction were reported. __Statistical Rigor__: Within-person analyses were used to control for between-person confounds; propensity score matching was employed as a robustness check; multiple controls (demographic, organizational) were included; however, the exploratory nature of the analyses and the large number of well-being outcomes tested without explicit multiplicity correction are limitations. __Measurement Validity__: Study 1 used single-item measures for some well-being outcomes, while Study 2 used more robust validated measures (affective commitment, psychological safety); discrimination was measured via self-report rather than behavioural indicators. __Open Science__: All materials, data, and analysis code are available on OSF, representing strong open science practices. __Participant Transparency__: Detailed demographics were reported including race, gender, age, organisational tenure, company size, and seniority; participants were American knowledge workers recruited via Qualtrics panel services; attention checks and minimum completion time criteria were used for data quality.
