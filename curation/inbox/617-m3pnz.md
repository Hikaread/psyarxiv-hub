## Summary

Two pre-registered experiments using **Bayesian sequential designs** (total N = 360) tested key predictions of the **Dual Representation Account (DRA)** of intrusive trauma memories (Brewin et al., 2010). Experiment 1 (N = 240, 2 x 2 x 2 between-participants design) tested whether a **visuospatial interference intervention** (Tetris) would selectively decrease **involuntary** but not **voluntary** memory for a trauma film analogue. Experiment 2 (N = 120) focused specifically on replicating the intervention effect on involuntary memories. Contrary to DRA predictions: (1) the visuospatial intervention did **not** decrease involuntary memories (Experiment 2: **BF₀₁ = 7.69**, exceeding the stopping criterion for the null); (2) involuntary and voluntary memory were **positively correlated** (r = 0.54 for memory count, r = 0.51 for unique details, both with extreme Bayesian evidence, BF₁₀ > 10⁶), directly contradicting the DRA's functional independence prediction; and (3) trauma content did not decrease voluntary recall relative to neutral content (**BF₀₁ = 10.53**). The study used a trauma film paradigm validated with pilot participants (n = 19), with trauma clips rated significantly more negative, arousing, and impactful than neutral clips (all BF₁₀ > 10⁴). Memory was assessed via a **Vigilance Retrieval Task (VRT)** scored using the **Autobiographical Interview** coding system (ICC = 0.96–0.99). The authors conclude the findings provide evidence **against** the DRA as a viable account of intrusive memories, at least in laboratory analogue settings, and support unitary accounts of episodic memory.

## Clinical Insight

These findings challenge a theory that has significantly influenced PTSD treatment, particularly the use of **visuospatial tasks** (e.g., Tetris) as early interventions to prevent intrusive memories. Clinicians should note several important nuances: first, the DRA was originally designed to explain clinical PTSD, and the authors acknowledge these laboratory analogue findings do not directly address whether the DRA accounts for clinical **dissociative flashbacks**, which may rely on distinct, extra-hippocampal memory representations (Kearney and Lanius, 2024). Second, the positive correlation between voluntary and involuntary memory (r ≈ 0.5) supports **unitary memory models** — suggesting both forms of retrieval derive from the same underlying trace, which aligns with clinical observations that patients who can narrate their trauma in therapy also tend to report more intrusions. Third, the authors raise the possibility that the **unconstrained text entry** in the VRT may have inadvertently caused **memory elaboration** that attenuated intervention effects — a concern for any research methodology that asks patients to describe their intrusive memories in detail. The recommendation to return to **real-life diary methods** for measuring intrusions, while methodologically challenging, may better capture the clinical phenomenon. Recent clinical trials (Ramineni et al., 2023; Beckenstrom et al., 2026) that avoided prompting memory descriptions in daily diaries found the intervention reduced diary intrusions, suggesting the clinical approach may work through mechanisms not captured by lab-based VRT measures.

## Relevant For

- Clinical psychologists treating PTSD and trauma-related conditions
- Researchers studying intrusive memories and trauma memory mechanisms
- Clinicians using or recommending visuospatial interventions for trauma
- Trauma-focused therapists interested in memory science
