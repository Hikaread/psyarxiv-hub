## Summary
This **conceptual paper** provides a comprehensive introduction to **personalizing Just-in-Time Adaptive Interventions (pJITAIs)**, which extend standard JITAIs by incorporating **online learning and decision-making algorithms** that update **decision rules** during deployment using streaming data. The paper unifies two dominant theoretical perspectives: **online reinforcement learning (RL)** and **adaptive control engineering**, which have progressed in parallel but share substantial conceptual overlap.

Standard JITAIs deliver adaptive support via four components: **decision points** (times at which intervention is initiated), **intervention options** (available support types), **decision rules** (if-then logic mapping tailoring variables to options), and **tailoring variables** (internal/external factors guiding decisions). The critical limitation of standard JITAIs is that **static decision rules** informed by pre-deployment data can become **outdated** due to societal shifts or differences between deployment cohorts. pJITAIs address this by enabling **within-deployment modification** of decision rules based on real-time data.

The paper provides detailed terminology mapping between RL/control engineering and JITAI components. In RL: time steps = decision points; actions = intervention options; state variables = tailoring variables plus predictors; policy = decision rules; reward = proximal/distal outcome. The paper distinguishes between **learning** (updating parameter estimates in the algorithm's model) and **decision-making** (using updated rules to select intervention options). A key concern addressed is **intervention fidelity**: the paper emphasizes that online learning modifies the pJITAI's decision rules (which are prespecified as modifiable) but does not change the fundamental pJITAI components (outcomes, tailoring variables, intervention options, decision points), preserving the intervention's theoretical grounding.

## Clinical Insight
pJITAIs represent the next generation of **digital mental health interventions**, with direct relevance to conditions requiring **real-time adaptive support** such as substance use (craving management), depression (activity scheduling), anxiety (exposure reminders), and chronic disease management (medication adherence). The ability to **update decision rules** during deployment addresses a fundamental limitation of current digital interventions: the assumption that relationships observed in development data remain stable across contexts and over time. For clinical implementation, the **intervention fidelity** discussion is crucial — clinicians need assurance that algorithmic adaptation is bounded and theoretically constrained rather than unconstrained optimization. The unification of **RL and control engineering** perspectives will help interdisciplinary teams communicate more effectively when designing personalized digital interventions. However, the **nascent evidence base** for pJITAIs means that controlled comparisons against standard JITAIs are still needed before widespread clinical adoption.
