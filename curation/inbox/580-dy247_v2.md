## Summary

This study used **network analysis** to examine the interplay between **disordered eating behaviours (DEB)**, **emotion regulation (ER)**, and **anxious and depressive symptomatology** in **N = 126 individuals** with high BMI (M = 36.95, SD = 8.08; mean age = 40.29, SD = 8.91). A **regularized partial correlation network** was estimated, and **centrality** and **bridge symptoms** were examined. The three symptoms with the highest **Expected Influence (EI)** were **Lack of ER Strategies** (EI = 1.22), **Eating Concerns** (EI = 1.05), and **Impulse/NonGoals** (EI = 0.95). The most central **bridge node** — connecting the emotion regulation and disordered eating clusters — was **Depression** (Bridge EI = 0.42). The network demonstrated **excellent stability** for centrality metrics. These findings suggest that **emotion regulation deficits** are the most influential nodes in the disordered eating-emotion psychopathology network among individuals with high BMI, and that **depression** serves as the key pathway linking emotional difficulties to eating pathology. The network approach reveals specific targets for intervention that differ from traditional categorical or dimensional approaches.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians treating **binge eating disorder**, **bulimia nervosa**, or **disordered eating** in clients with higher body weight, this network analysis provides a precision-medicine framework for treatment planning. The finding that **Lack of ER Strategies** is the most central symptom suggests that targeting emotion regulation should be the primary treatment focus — consistent with **dialectical behaviour therapy (DBT)** for eating disorders, which prioritises distress tolerance and emotion regulation skills. The centrality of **Eating Concerns** and **Impulse/NonGoals** (reflecting impulsive eating behaviour) suggests that addressing both cognitive preoccupation with eating and behavioural impulsivity is important. The identification of **Depression** as the key bridge symptom means that treating depressive symptoms may have downstream effects on both emotion regulation and disordered eating — supporting an integrated treatment approach rather than targeting eating symptoms in isolation. For **CBT-E** practitioners, the network structure supports Fairburn's transdiagnostic model by empirically demonstrating the interconnection between mood, emotion regulation, and eating pathology. The sample's mean age of 40 and BMI of 37 suggest findings are most applicable to adults with longstanding weight concerns, differentiating this from studies of younger or lower-BMI populations.

## Relevant For
- Eating disorder clinicians treating higher-BMI populations
- DBT practitioners working with binge eating or emotional eating
- CBT-E therapists targeting transdiagnostic maintaining mechanisms
- Clinical researchers using network analysis in eating disorders
