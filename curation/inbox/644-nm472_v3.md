## Summary
This study developed and **psychometrically validated** the **Japanese version of the Sensory Experience Questionnaire 3.0 (SEQ-J)**, a caregiver-report measure of sensory features in autistic children.
The sample included **154 children with ASD** (44 with comorbid **ADHD**, 66 with other neurodevelopmental conditions) and **156 typically developing children**, aged 6-12.
**Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA)** supported a bifactor model with **four content factors** (sensory hyper- and hyporesponsiveness, sensory interests, repetitive and seeking behaviors, and enhanced perception) and **six method factors** (five sensory modalities plus social context), showing acceptable fit in both ASD and non-ASD groups.
**ROC analyses** demonstrated that most SEQ factors had **good classification accuracy** distinguishing ASD from typically developing groups, though the **enhanced perception** factor did not differentiate groups.
Importantly, ROC analyses comparing children with ASD to those with **comorbid neurodevelopmental conditions** showed **unsatisfactory discrimination**, indicating the SEQ captures general neurodevelopmental sensory features rather than ASD-specific ones.
**Representational similarity analyses (RSAs)** revealed that the overall configuration of sensory scores differed significantly between diagnostic groups, while within-ASD comparisons (ASD only vs. ASD + ADHD) showed no differences in score configuration.

## Clinical Insight
The **SEQ-J** provides clinicians and researchers in Japan with a **culturally validated, multidimensional tool** for assessing sensory features in autistic children, filling an important gap since no gold-standard sensory measure existed for this population.
The **bifactor structure** (content + method factors) is clinically useful because it allows practitioners to distinguish between **what** sensory domain is affected (content) and **which sensory modality** is involved (method), enabling more targeted intervention planning.
The **poor discrimination between ASD and comorbid neurodevelopmental conditions** is a critical caveat for clinicians: the SEQ should not be used as a **diagnostic differentiator** but rather as a **sensory profiling tool** to guide **occupational therapy** and environmental accommodations.
The finding that **enhanced perception** did not differentiate ASD from TD groups suggests this factor may reflect a **broader neurodivergent trait** rather than an ASD-specific sensory feature, which aligns with growing evidence that sensory differences transcend diagnostic boundaries.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric paper.

__Measurement Validity__: The **SEQ-J** was validated using a rigorous multi-step approach.
CFA confirmed the bifactor model with acceptable fit indices in both ASD and non-ASD groups.
ROC analyses provided classification accuracy evidence, though the cross-validation sample for comorbid conditions was limited.
The **representational similarity analysis (RSA)** was a novel methodological addition that examined the overall configuration of sensory profiles rather than individual factors alone.
A limitation is the reliance on **caregiver-report** rather than direct behavioral assessment, which may introduce informant bias.

__Statistical Power__: The total sample of **310 children** (154 ASD, 156 TD) is adequate for CFA with the specified factor structure.
However, when splitting the ASD group into subgroups (ASD only, ASD + ADHD, ASD + other), cell sizes become smaller, potentially limiting the reliability of subgroup comparisons.
No explicit **a priori power analysis** was reported, which is a limitation for a validation study.

__Open Science__: The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv** as an open-access document.
Data and analysis code availability were not mentioned in the abstract, so it is unclear whether these materials are publicly accessible.
The use of an established measure (SEQ 3.0) with published factor structure supports **replicability** of the validation approach.

__Participant Transparency__: The sample included children aged **6-12 years** across two groups: 154 with ASD (with detailed comorbidity breakdown: 44 ADHD, 66 other conditions, 44 ASD-only) and 156 typically developing children.
Exclusion criteria and recruitment methods were not detailed in the abstract.
The geographic and cultural context (**Japan**) is a strength, as it extends the SEQ's validation beyond Western populations.
