## Summary
In a pre-post experimental design, 119 participants were randomly assigned to view either a climate disaster video or a neutral weather forecast. SPS moderated the effect of condition on negative affect, with high-SPS individuals showing significantly greater increases in negative affect when exposed to climate content. Positive affect and rumination were not significantly affected by condition or SPS. Nature connectedness emerged as an independent predictor of negative affect. The results demonstrate that SPS is a relevant individual difference for understanding emotional responses to environmental media content.

## Clinical Insight
Highly sensitive persons (HSPs) frequently present in therapy reporting overwhelm, emotional reactivity, and difficulty coping with news and media. This study offers an evidence base for normalising these experiences and distinguishing between heightened emotional responding (which is SPS-related) and rumination (which is not). In treatment, this distinction matters: interventions for emotional reactivity may focus on sensory regulation, grounding, and controlled exposure, while rumination-focused interventions target cognitive processes. Therapists should assess for SPS as part of case formulation and consider how media consumption patterns may be maintaining distress in sensitive clients.

## Relevant For
Therapists working with HSP clients, practitioners treating anxiety and emotional dysregulation, therapists addressing eco-anxiety or media-related distress.

## Notes
Used a non-clinical sample of 119 participants. The climate-change-specific context may limit generalisability to other forms of distressing media. The study does not address clinical thresholds of SPS or its overlap with other traits like neuroticism.
