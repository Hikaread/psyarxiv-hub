## Summary

This integrative analysis combined 11 experience-sampling datasets (N=1,720 individuals, 134,470 observations) to examine how emotion regulation strategy use varies with three contextual features: emotional intensity, perceived controllability, and social context (presence and closeness of others). Against theoretical expectations, the study found limited evidence that context meaningfully drives daily strategy selection. The well-powered design allowed detection of even small effects, suggesting that the strategy-situation fit framework, while theoretically appealing, may not operate as strongly in daily life as laboratory studies suggest.

## Clinical Insight

This large-scale finding challenges a core assumption underlying much emotion regulation therapy: that teaching clients to match strategies to situations is the primary path to better outcomes. If context drives strategy use less than assumed, therapists may need to reconsider the emphasis on strategy-situation matching in protocols like DBT and CBT-E. Instead, the findings suggest that individual differences in strategy habits and preferences may be more important than contextual fit. For therapists, this means that helping clients identify and practice a personal repertoire of go-to strategies — rather than teaching complex decision rules about which strategy fits which situation — may be more aligned with how regulation actually works in daily life. This does not invalidate the value of teaching multiple strategies, but it does suggest that the therapeutic rationale should focus on building automaticity and personal fit rather than situational analysis. The study also implies that when a client repeatedly uses the same strategy across situations, this may not be a deficit requiring correction but a normal and potentially adaptive pattern.

## Relevant For
Therapists teaching emotion regulation skills across modalities (CBT, DBT, ACT), particularly those working with clients who struggle to apply different strategies in different contexts
