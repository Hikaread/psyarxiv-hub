## Summary
This perspective paper proposes a framework extending **affective computing** from static emotion recognition to continuous modeling of **emotional dynamics** in daily life. Physiological data from wearable devices are used to infer emotional trajectories in **arousal-valence space**, represented as movement across individualized **effective potential landscapes**. These landscapes may provide quantitative proxies for **emotional temperament**, with stable regions (attractor basins) shaping emotional transitions, recovery, and vulnerability to maladaptive states. The framework outlines a staged deployment path: **population-level models** trained with high-temporal-resolution affect labels, refined through ecologically valid real-world data, and adapted through **user-specific personalization**. Conceptual simulations illustrate how emotional landscapes might change gradually through life experience or rapidly after **adverse events**. The authors discuss applications for emotional self-awareness, psychotherapy, mental health research, and temperament study, while highlighting ethical challenges around privacy, reification, unequal performance, and psychological effects of **sustained monitoring**.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians, this framework offers a future-oriented vision of how **continuous emotional monitoring** could transform psychotherapy. Rather than relying on retrospective self-report, therapists could access real-time or accumulated data on a patient's **emotional dynamics** — including how quickly they recover from distress, what triggers emotional transitions, and whether their emotional landscape is becoming more rigid or unstable over the course of treatment. This could enable truly **personalized treatment** by identifying individual-specific vulnerability patterns and tracking change at a granularity impossible with session-by-session assessment. However, the authors appropriately caution against **reification**: model outputs are not emotional truth, and clinicians must guard against over-interpreting algorithmic outputs. The framework also raises important considerations about **therapeutic boundaries** when patient emotional data is continuously monitored, and the potential for such monitoring to itself become a source of anxiety or self-surveillance pathology.

## Notes
This is a perspective/theoretical paper rather than an empirical study. The proposed framework is conceptual with simulations, not yet validated with real patient data.
