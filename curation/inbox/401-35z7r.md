## Summary

This pre-registered lab experiment (N = 111, 70.3% female, M age = 25.29, SD = 8.58) investigated whether reward expectation drives the "brain drain" effect of smartphone presence on sustained attention. Participants were randomly assigned to three conditions (n = 37 each): (1) no smartphone present (control), (2) smartphone present face-down on silent (mere presence), (3) smartphone present with induced reward-message expectation (1/4 chance to win 40 euros via text). All completed a Sustained Attention to Response Task (SART; 336 trials, digits 1-9, withhold response to "3") before and after the manipulation.

The mere presence condition showed significantly smaller RT improvement from pre to post compared to control (Time x Condition interaction: β = 7.69, SE = 1.93, t(65948) = 3.98, p < .001), supporting the brain drain hypothesis. However, the expectation condition did not differ from control (β = 1.19, SE = 1.93, t(65948) = 0.62, p = .54), failing to support reward-based attentional capture as the mechanism. Accuracy on No-Go trials showed no significant effects across conditions.

The exploratory moderation analysis revealed that Online Vigilance (OV; measured by the 12-item OVS, M = 2.95, SD = 0.65) significantly moderated the effect. High-OV participants showed reduced RT improvements in both the mere presence condition (Time x Condition x OV: β = 32.87, SE = 2.91, t(65649) = 11.28, p < .001) and the expectation condition (β = 20.56, SE = 3.25, t(65645) = 6.32, p < .001). Adding smartphone addiction (SAS), OV, and BAS reward responsiveness as covariates did not change the pattern. The reward expectation manipulation was credible (M = 73.46/100 on a VAS), and a sensitivity analysis excluding participants below 50/100 (n = 5) yielded the same results. Data and R scripts are available on OSF.

## Clinical Insight

These findings are relevant to clinical work with clients presenting with attention difficulties, problematic smartphone use, or anxiety disorders where digital connectivity perpetuates hypervigilance. The key clinical takeaway is that the mere physical presence of a smartphone impairs sustained attention (effect size d derived from the RT interaction), and this effect is substantially amplified in individuals with high Online Vigilance, a trait-like cognitive orientation toward online communication. This suggests that for patients who score high on the OVS, even having their phone nearby during therapy sessions or while engaging in homework tasks (e.g., exposure exercises, mindfulness) may subtly undermine cognitive engagement. The finding that momentary reward expectation did not add to the mere presence effect implies that the attentional cost is driven by learned associations rather than acute anticipation. Clinicians working with digital wellbeing or ADHD-like presentations should consider the OVS as a screening tool and discuss phone placement strategies. The 91.9% White, predominantly female, young Belgian student sample limits generalizability.

## Relevant For

Clinical psychologists working with attention difficulties, digital wellbeing practitioners, therapists incorporating behavioral experiments, clinicians assessing problematic smartphone use

## Notes

Pre-registered on OSF. Sample predominantly young, female, White, and highly educated. Lab setting may not capture real-world smartphone interference. SART accuracy showed ceiling effects. NASA-TLX was administered between tasks and excluded as a covariate.
