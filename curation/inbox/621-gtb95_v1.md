## Summary

This study examines the convergent validity of coding **psychological inflexibility** in **written emotional disclosure (WED)** narratives and its associations with **posttraumatic stress (PTS)** symptomology and functional impairment in a community sample (N = 415). The authors developed a coding system to assess psychological inflexibility — a core process in **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)** — directly from the language participants used during expressive writing exercises, rather than relying solely on self-report measures.

Results demonstrated strong convergent validity: coded written psychological inflexibility was **negatively associated with self-report psychological flexibility** (measured via the **Comprehensive Assessment of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy Processes**, CAACP) and **positively associated with PTS symptoms**, anxiety-related distress, and impairment. Notably, written psychological inflexibility predicted PTS-related outcomes **over and above** self-report psychological flexibility, cumulative exposure to potentially traumatic events (measured by the **Life Events Checklist for DSM-5**), and narrative word counts.

The study used the **PCL-5** for PTSD symptom assessment and the **Overall Anxiety Severity and Impairment Scale (OASIS)** for anxiety-related distress. Regression analyses showed that the language-based measure captured variance in PTS outcomes that self-report alone missed, suggesting that how people write about their experiences may reveal cognitive and emotional patterns not fully accessible through self-reflection questionnaires. These findings extend the growing literature on the validity of evaluating psychological inflexibility in naturalistic language contexts.

## Methodology Note

__Paper Type__: quantitative_experimental

__Statistical Power__: Adequate. With N = 415, the study was well-powered for the regression and correlational analyses conducted. Sensitivity analyses were not reported but the sample size supports detection of small-to-medium effects.

__Preregistration__: Not mentioned. No statement about preregistration or an analysis plan was found, which limits confidence in the confirmatory status of the findings.

__Effect Sizes__: Reported as correlations and regression coefficients. The incremental variance explained by written psychological inflexibility over self-report measures was statistically significant but specific R-squared change values would strengthen interpretation.

__Statistical Rigor__: Adequate. The use of hierarchical regression controlling for known predictors (cumulative trauma exposure, word count, self-report flexibility) is methodologically sound. However, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference about whether written inflexibility contributes to PTS or reflects existing symptomatology.

__Measurement Validity__: Mixed strength. The PCL-5 and OASIS are well-validated clinical measures. The CAACP is an established process measure. However, the novel coding system for written psychological inflexibility is the key instrument, and while inter-rater reliability would be expected for such coding, it was not explicitly discussed in the available materials.

__Open Science__: Partial. The study was posted as a preprint on OSF, but no data, code, or supplementary materials were referenced for open access.

__Participant Transparency__: Adequate. A community sample of N = 415 was used. Demographic characteristics and trauma exposure history were described, though recruitment specifics (e.g., geographic region, online vs. in-person) would further contextualize the findings.

## Clinical Insight

This study offers a promising avenue for clinicians who use **expressive writing** or **narrative-based interventions** in trauma work. The finding that written psychological inflexibility predicts PTS outcomes beyond self-report suggests that **language-based markers** may capture clinically meaningful information that patients cannot or do not report through standard questionnaires. For ACT practitioners, this validates the theoretical model: psychological inflexibility manifests not just in reported behavior but in the **structure and content of how people narrate their experiences**. Clinicians might pay closer attention to clients' written or spoken language for patterns of **experiential avoidance**, **cognitive fusion**, and **self-as-content** — processes that may be observable before a client can articulate them. The WED paradigm itself has therapeutic value and is relatively easy to implement as a **low-intensity intervention** or adjunct to trauma-focused therapy.
