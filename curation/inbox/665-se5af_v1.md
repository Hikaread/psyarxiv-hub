## Summary
This study investigated the **Cone of Gaze (CoG)** — the range of gaze angles perceived as looking at oneself — as a potential **biobehavioural marker for social anxiety disorder**. The research combined **meta-analysis** with **well-powered experimental replications**. Meta-analyses revealed significantly **wider CoG** for socially anxious vs. non-anxious individuals (10 studies, N = 403) and **wider CoG for angry vs. neutral expressions** (9 studies, N = 523). However, the existing evidence was **sparse, highly heterogeneous**, and often based on small samples. The authors' own **preregistered experiments** (N = 514) did **not replicate** either the effect of social anxiety on CoG or the moderation by emotional expression. The integrated approach of combining meta-analytic evidence with large-scale replication provides a **rigorous framework** for assessing the robustness and **clinical translational potential** of proposed cognitive markers. The authors highlight the need for **larger, more transparent datasets** to accurately estimate CoG effects and other putative markers of psychiatric conditions.

## Clinical Insight
This study delivers an important **cautionary message** for clinicians and researchers interested in **cognitive biomarkers** of social anxiety. The **Cone of Gaze** had been proposed as a promising objective marker of social anxiety — the idea being that socially anxious individuals perceive a wider range of gaze directions as "looking at them," potentially maintaining **self-focused attention** and **threat perception** in social situations. However, the **failed replication** in a well-powered sample (N = 514) suggests that this marker is **not robust enough** for clinical use. For clinicians, this underscores the importance of relying on **well-validated assessment tools** rather than experimental cognitive measures that have not survived large-scale replication. The broader methodological contribution — combining **meta-analysis with preregistered replication** — provides a model for how the field should evaluate other proposed markers (e.g., attentional biases, interpretation biases) before incorporating them into clinical practice or using them as **treatment targets** in bias modification programs. The finding that the published literature showed significant effects (with high heterogeneity) that did not replicate raises concerns about **publication bias** in the social anxiety cognition literature.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental study (meta-analysis + experimental replication).

__Statistical Power__: The meta-analyses were based on small, heterogeneous samples (10 and 9 studies).
The replication experiment (N = 514) was well-powered, representing a methodological strength.
A priori power analysis was presumably conducted for the experimental component.

__Preregistration__: The experimental replications were preregistered.
This is a significant strength, allowing clear distinction between confirmatory and exploratory analyses.
The meta-analysis component does not appear to have a separate preregistered protocol.

__Effect Sizes__: The meta-analyses report summary effect sizes with confidence intervals and heterogeneity statistics.
The experimental replication reports effect sizes that can be directly compared to the meta-analytic estimates.
This side-by-side comparison of meta-analytic vs. replication effect sizes is a key contribution.

__Statistical Rigor__: The study uses a rigorous integrated design combining meta-analysis with direct replication.
Heterogeneity in the meta-analyses was high, which the authors appropriately highlight as a concern.
The replication study was well-designed with adequate statistical power to detect the meta-analytic effect sizes.

__Measurement Validity__: The CoG is measured using established gaze perception paradigms with psychometric support.
Social anxiety was assessed using standardized measures, though the specific instruments vary across meta-analyzed studies.
The experimental study used consistent measurement protocols across conditions.

__Open Science__: Published as a preprint, enhancing accessibility.
The replication was preregistered, supporting transparency.
Data and materials availability for the replication study would strengthen the contribution.

__Participant Transparency__: The meta-analysis included participants from 10 studies with varying characteristics.
The replication sample of N = 514 is well-described in terms of recruitment and inclusion criteria.
The specific demographic composition of the replication sample should be examined for generalizability.
