## Summary

This narrative review scopes research on sensitive periods during which children are particularly vulnerable to abuse and its long-term consequences. Three themes emerged: (1) earlier adversity tends to produce worse outcomes, (2) two sensitive periods correspond to major developmental transitions—early childhood and early adolescence, and (3) sensitive periods are domain-specific, meaning the timing of abuse affects different aspects of psychosocial functioning differently. The authors caution that findings are largely study-specific, and more prospective longitudinal research is needed. They emphasize that timing of child abuse is clinically relevant for optimizing and personalizing treatment.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists conducting trauma-informed assessments and case formulations, understanding when adversity occurred can meaningfully inform treatment planning. Abuse during early childhood may disrupt attachment and emotional regulation foundations, while adversity during early adolescence may impact identity formation and social-cognitive development. This review supports the clinical practice of carefully mapping the timing and nature of adverse experiences when formulating cases. Therapists can use this knowledge to tailor interventions—for example, prioritizing attachment-focused work for early childhood trauma versus identity and social-processing interventions for early adolescent trauma.

## Relevant For

- Trauma therapists and counselors working with adult survivors of childhood abuse
- Clinicians conducting trauma-informed assessments and case formulations
- Therapists working with developmental trauma and complex PTSD
- Practitioners interested in the developmental timing of psychological interventions
