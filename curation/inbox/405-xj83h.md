## Summary
In this pre-registered RCT, 299 adolescents (M age = 16.34; 55% female) were randomly assigned to a six-session online need crafting intervention (based on Self-Determination Theory) or a passive control, with assessments at baseline, post-intervention, and two-week follow-up. The intervention improved general need crafting and need satisfaction, with effects maintained at follow-up; however, effects were driven almost entirely by the 58% of participants classified as highly engaged. Serial mediation in the high-engagement group confirmed significant indirect effects on well-being via need crafting → need satisfaction (β = .22, SE = .06, p < .001) and on ill-being via need crafting → decreased need frustration (β = −.08, SE = .03, p = .003). Domain-specific effects replicated in school (β = .19, p = .009) and leisure (β = .19, p = .015) but were absent for social media use.

## Clinical Insight
Need crafting—the proactive skill of seeking out need-fulfilling experiences—can be trained in adolescents and operates through a clear SDT-consistent mechanism. However, engagement is critical: low-engaged participants showed no benefits and in some domain-specific outcomes fared worse than controls, suggesting that passive or minimal exposure may be counterproductive without active participation.

## Relevant For
Adolescent mental health clinicians, school counselors, and prevention program developers working with youth aged 15–18.

## Notes
The follow-up period was only two weeks and overlapped with exam season, potentially masking well-being effects. The study used a passive control rather than an active comparator, and effects did not extend to the social media domain, limiting conclusions about digital context applicability.
