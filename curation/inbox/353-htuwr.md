## Summary

This narrative review (UCLouvain) proposes an integrated neurocognitive framework explaining why physical exercise can reduce negative symptoms of schizophrenia while the symptoms themselves impede exercise engagement. The review synthesizes evidence across four domains: (1) exercise's effects on negative symptoms — meta-analyses report small effects for aerobic interventions (Sabe et al., SMD=-0.20, 95% CI -0.37 to -0.03), medium effects across broader exercise modalities (Kim et al., g=-0.51, 95% CI -0.72 to -0.31), and medium-to-large effects via multilevel meta-analysis (Gallardo-Gomez et al., g=0.65, 95% CI 0.53-0.78); (2) evidence that negative symptoms reduce physical activity engagement, including digital phenotyping data (Yang et al., 2025) showing avolition and anhedonia strongly associated with reduced step counts and GPS-derived distance traveled in 99 recently discharged patients; (3) subjective exercise experience in schizophrenia, including findings from Duncan et al. (2016) that moderate-intensity walking increased perceived exertion during the task while pleasure improved only after completion, and Décombe et al. (2020) showing individuals with schizophrenia report higher perceived effort overall than healthy controls even at low physical demands; and (4) a novel learning-based framework in which exercise generates prediction errors — discrepancies between anticipated and experienced effort/pleasure — that can update maladaptive internal models. The framework draws on evidence of preserved consummatory pleasure but impaired anticipatory pleasure, effort-based decision-making deficits (transdiagnostic meta-analysis g=-0.40; Pillny et al., 2026), altered reinforcement learning, and blunted prediction-error signaling in frontostriatal circuits in schizophrenia.

## Clinical Insight

This review offers a specific, mechanism-based approach to prescribing exercise for patients with schizophrenia that goes well beyond the standard advice to "exercise more." The key clinical protocol derived from the framework has several concrete steps: (1) Before each exercise session, ask the patient to explicitly predict how effortful, unpleasant, or tiring the activity will be on a rating scale — this makes the prediction error salient and encodeable. (2) Start with activities that are genuinely expected to be easy (generating positive prediction errors where exercise feels less effortful than expected), drawing on the finding that lower-than-expected exertion increases pleasure (Brevers et al., 2024). (3) After each session, compare predictions with actual experience and explicitly discuss the discrepancy. This is critical because prediction error processing is altered in schizophrenia (attenuated neural responses in ventral striatum during reward anticipation), so positive experiences will not automatically update beliefs. (4) When exercise is harder than expected (negative prediction errors), reframe the experience as evidence of mastery and tolerance ("this was difficult, but I managed it") rather than confirming that exercise is aversive. (5) Focus on immediate rewards (calm, accomplishment, social connection) rather than delayed health benefits, given that individuals with schizophrenia show steeper delay discounting. (6) Consider aerobic over resistance training initially, as Korman et al. (2026) found that fatigue decreased after aerobic exercise but increased after resistance training in patients with psychotic disorders. This approach can be integrated into existing CBT for psychosis or recovery-oriented practice, and mirrors behavioral activation techniques used for anhedonia in depression.

## Relevant For

Psychologists and mental health workers supporting people with schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, psychiatric rehabilitation specialists, CBT for psychosis practitioners, exercise physiologists working in mental health settings

## Notes

Narrative review rather than systematic review or meta-analysis; the proposed framework is theoretical and has not been empirically tested; evidence on in-exercise pleasure and exertion in schizophrenia is very limited (few studies, small samples); BMI and metabolic factors (highly prevalent in schizophrenia) may confound affective responses to exercise; the analogy to exposure therapy (prediction error updating) is promising but untested in this population.
