## Summary

This narrative review investigates the bidirectional relationship between negative symptoms of schizophrenia (particularly avolition and anhedonia) and physical activity engagement. The authors propose that negative symptoms impair engagement in exercise by altering cost-benefit analysis, exacerbated by deficits in anticipatory pleasure and reward learning underpinned by dopaminergic and glutamatergic dysfunction. They hypothesize that physical exercise can rewire these deficits through enhanced dopamine transmission, modulation of glutamatergic and GABAergic systems, and reduced inflammation. Grounded in learning theory and a cognitive-behavioural perspective, the review argues that exercise must be experienced as less effortful and more pleasurable than anticipated to generate positive prediction errors that re-associate exercise with positive outcomes, creating a virtuous cycle.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with schizophrenia, this review reframes physical exercise from a lifestyle recommendation into a therapeutic intervention with a clear mechanism of action. The key clinical insight is that the standard advice to "exercise more" will fail for most patients with negative symptoms because their anticipatory pleasure deficits mean they cannot imagine exercise feeling good. Instead, therapists should apply a behavioral activation approach to exercise: start with activities that are genuinely experienced as less effortful than expected (generating positive prediction errors), use graded task assignment, and explicitly address anticipatory beliefs about effort and pleasure before the activity begins. This mirrors CBT techniques for anhedonia more broadly. The cognitive-behavioural framework also suggests that simply prescribing exercise is insufficient; therapists need to work on the patient's expectations and beliefs about physical activity as much as on the activity itself. This approach can be integrated into existing CBT for psychosis or recovery-oriented practice.

## Relevant For

Therapists and mental health workers supporting people with schizophrenia, particularly those targeting negative symptoms, avolition, or anhedonia through behavioral activation or recovery-oriented practice
