## Summary

This study evaluated the **factor structure** and **psychometric properties** of the **Body Appreciation Scale-2 for Autistic adults (BAS-2A)** — a measure of **positive body image** — in a sample of **N = 191 Autistic transgender and gender diverse (TGD)** adults aged 18 to 85 years across four Western countries (Australia, Canada, UK, US). Participants completed the BAS-2A alongside measures of physical well-being and **eating disorder symptomatology**. **Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA)** supported a 12-item, **unidimensional structure** in this population. The scale demonstrated **excellent composite reliability** and evidence of **convergent**, **concurrent**, **discriminant**, and **incremental validity**. Body appreciation was associated with both physical and psychological well-being among Autistic TGD individuals, extending previous findings from neurotypical and general Autistic samples. This is the first validation of any body image measure specifically within the Autistic TGD population, a group at elevated risk for **body dissatisfaction** and **eating pathology** due to the intersection of autism-related sensory and social differences with gender-related body concerns.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with **Autistic adults**, particularly those who are also **transgender or gender diverse**, this study provides a much-needed validated tool for assessing a protective factor — **body appreciation** — that may buffer against **eating disorder development**. Autistic TGD individuals face compounded risk: autism is associated with sensory sensitivities that can affect body experience, while gender dysphoria introduces additional body-related distress. The BAS-2A's unidimensional structure in this population means clinicians can use it as a brief, reliable **screening tool** in eating disorder, autism, and gender identity clinics. The 12-item format makes it practical for **routine clinical assessment** and **treatment outcome monitoring**. Clinicians should note that body appreciation is not merely the absence of dissatisfaction but a distinct construct that may support **protective factors** such as intuitive eating and adaptive body care. In therapeutic work integrating **CBT-E**, **ACT**, or **gender-affirming approaches**, tracking changes in body appreciation alongside symptom reduction could provide a more complete picture of recovery. The cross-national sample (four countries) supports the measure's applicability across Western English-speaking contexts, though further validation is needed for non-Western and non-English-speaking Autistic TGD populations.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians assessing body image in Autistic and/or TGD adult populations
- Eating disorder therapists working with neurodivergent clients
- Gender identity clinic practitioners
- Researchers studying body image at the intersection of autism and gender diversity
