## Summary
This **case study** presents proof-of-concept for **intracranial accelerated iTBS** (intermittent theta-burst stimulation) delivered through stereoelectroencephalography (sEEG) depth electrodes implanted in the left **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** of a 35-year-old patient with **treatment-resistant major depressive disorder (MDD)** comorbid with refractory epilepsy. The protocol consisted of 20 iTBS sessions delivered over approximately 10 hours in a single day, using a bipolar configuration at 4 mA with 20-30 minute inter-session intervals. **Depression remitted** within 24-36 hours post-stimulation and persisted for at least 9 weeks, as measured by the **PHQ-9**, **MADRS-SR**, **NDDI-E**, **GAD-7**, and **CAT-MH** depression and suicidality modules. The patient's passive suicidal ideation and intrusive thoughts resolved entirely. Electrophysiological indices of **target engagement** included enhanced **theta oscillations** in the DLPFC, increased **single-unit firing rates** in bilateral hippocampi, and changes in **cortico-cortical evoked potentials (CCEPs)** with dissociable early and late component effects. By 18 weeks, the patient relapsed to prior depression levels. No seizure activity was triggered and no manic/hypomanic symptoms emerged.

## Clinical Insight
This report bridges the gap between non-invasive **accelerated TMS protocols** and implantable neuromodulation approaches for treatment-resistant depression. The single-day accelerated protocol achieved remission far more rapidly than standard pharmacological or conventional TMS timelines, which typically require weeks. The intracranial recording provided unprecedented mechanistic data: the progressive enhancement of **DLPFC theta power** across stimulation sessions suggests **metaplasticity** — a gradual reshaping of neural excitability that may underlie the durable clinical effect. The bilateral hippocampal firing rate increases are clinically notable given the well-established role of hippocampal dysfunction in **depressive pathology** and **episodic memory** deficits. For clinicians working with epilepsy patients who have comorbid depression, these findings suggest that intracranial stimulation channels positioned near DLPFC could be leveraged therapeutically during monitoring periods. However, the single-case design and the patient's complex comorbidity profile (including **cannabis use disorder** and **PTSD**) limit generalizability. The 18-week relapse despite no medication changes highlights the need for maintenance protocols.
