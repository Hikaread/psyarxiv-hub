## Summary

This paper introduces **Design Psychology**, an interdisciplinary framework that conceptualises **digital design features** and **affordances** as **modifiable influences** on core psychological processes including **attention**, **reinforcement learning**, **social comparison**, and **perceived control**. The authors argue that research has been constrained by focusing on coarse exposure measures like **screen time**, yielding inconsistent associations with mental health. Drawing on insights from **human-computer interaction**, **behavioural science**, and **digital mental health**, the framework elucidates **causal pathways** linking specific design elements to mental health outcomes. Rather than framing the problem as use versus non-use, Design Psychology shifts focus to **optimising design elements** to promote wellbeing. The framework supports targeted, scalable interventions and informs the development of **evidence-based design standards**. The paper proposes a mechanistic and actionable foundation for creating digital environments that support rather than undermine mental health. Full text extracted from a 19-page PDF. Notable author team includes leading digital mental health researchers (John Torous, Joseph Firth, Jake Linardon).

## Clinical Insight

For therapists whose clients struggle with **digital wellbeing** — including excessive social media use, problematic gaming, or smartphone dependence — this framework offers a more precise way to understand and address the problem than simply recommending reduced screen time. The Design Psychology perspective suggests that clinicians should help clients identify which specific **design features** (e.g., infinite scroll, push notifications, variable-ratio reinforcement schedules, social comparison cues) drive their maladaptive use patterns, and then develop targeted strategies that modify their interaction with those features rather than pursuing unrealistic abstinence goals. This maps directly onto **CBT** and **ACT** approaches: identifying specific triggers (design features), examining the beliefs and emotional responses they elicit, and developing alternative responses. For **digital mental health** developers, the framework provides a theoretical foundation for designing therapeutic tools whose own design principles align with their clinical goals — avoiding the irony of mental health apps that employ the same engagement-maximising design patterns that contribute to the problems they aim to solve. The inclusion of **perceived control** as a core psychological process directly connects to clinical models of **learned helplessness**, **locus of control**, and **psychological flexibility** that are central to CBT and ACT.
