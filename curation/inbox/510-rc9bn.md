## Summary

This **preregistered qualitative study** explored culturally situated meanings of listening through 20 **semi-structured interviews** with Israeli participants (aged 19–87; M = 36.95, SD = 17.48), primarily reflecting **Jewish-Israeli Hebrew-speaking** communicative contexts. Using **reflexive thematic analysis** (Braun & Clarke, 2021) in **Atlas.ti**, five interrelated themes were generated: (1) relational conditions enabling listening, (2) motivational pull to listen, (3) how listening is expressed and experienced, (4) how listening transforms individuals and relationships, and (5) how listening operates under strain.

Participants described listening as an **episodic, effortful, and relationally contingent process** rather than a stable interpersonal skill. Listening was most consequential during **disagreement, emotional intensity, and power asymmetry**, where relational stakes are heightened. A key finding is that listening emerges from a specific **interactional tension** where **expressive involvement** coexists with **heightened social monitoring**. A culturally embedded **relational model of listening** is proposed, distinguishing between **core relational mechanisms** and their culturally structured enactment. The authors argue that listening training should move beyond technique-based models to address **cultural norms, emotional regulation, and power dynamics**.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists and counselors, this study's finding that listening is **episodic and effortful** rather than a trait-like skill has direct implications for therapeutic practice. The emphasis on the **interactional tension** between expressive involvement and social monitoring resonates with the therapeutic challenge of simultaneously being present and tracking the therapeutic process. The finding that listening is most critical during **disagreement and power asymmetry** aligns with moments of therapeutic **rupture and repair**. The culturally specific dynamics described—particularly around **directness, verbal immediacy, and collective norms** in Israeli communication—remind clinicians that listening is culturally mediated and that techniques developed in Western individualistic contexts may not transfer directly. The proposal that listening training should address **power dynamics** and **cultural norms** rather than just technique aligns with calls for more culturally responsive clinical training.

## Methodology Note

This is a qualitative paper.

__Statistical Rigor__: The study used **reflexive thematic analysis** with a clear epistemological stance (social constructionist, experiential orientation).
Coding was inductive, including both semantic and latent levels.
The lead author is an Israeli-Jewish researcher who conducted and analyzed interviews in Hebrew, with international co-authors providing reflexive perspective.
Interpretive claims were retained only when supported by patterned meaning across multiple participants.
The authors explicitly state they did not seek saturation but rather rich, conceptually coherent themes.
No inter-coder reliability is reported, consistent with the reflexive TA tradition but limiting claims about coding stability.

__Measurement Validity__: Data were collected via a standardized semi-structured protocol from the **Many Labs Listening Project**.
Interviews were conducted in Hebrew for linguistic and cultural accessibility.
The protocol included open-ended prompts about specific listening experiences, follow-up questions, and probes about difficulty, emotional strain, and power differences.
Interviews lasted 45–60 minutes.

__Open Science__: The study was **preregistered on OSF** (https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/47ZKJ) with research questions, methodology, and analytic strategy specified before analysis.
This is notable for a qualitative study and enhances credibility.

__Participant Transparency__: 20 participants purposively sampled for diversity in age (19–87), gender, region, ethnicity, life stage, and professional background.
10 married, 9 single, 1 widowed.
9 young adults (18–30), 7 midlife (31–50), 4 older adults (51–87).
18 Jewish, 2 Arab Israeli.
11 from central Israel, 9 from the north.
The small, purposive sample is appropriate for qualitative inquiry but limits transferability.
The Israeli-Jewish cultural focus is both a strength and a boundary condition.

## Notes

This paper is part of the **Many Labs Listening Project**, an international collaboration. The distinction between "core relational mechanisms" and "culturally structured enactment" provides a useful framework for thinking about which aspects of listening are universal versus culture-specific. The finding that listening quality depends on the listener's capacity for **emotional regulation** during high-stakes interactions has implications for therapist training and self-care.
