## Summary
This **retrospective observational study** evaluated the effectiveness of **ECT in reducing anhedonia** among **67 patients with depression** at the University Psychiatric Clinics Basel. Anhedonia was measured using the **Snaith-Hamilton Pleasure Scale (SHAPS)** and depression severity using the **MADRS** and **BDI-II** across four time points: pre-ECT, after the sixth session, post-acute treatment, and at 2-3 month follow-up during maintenance ECT. Results demonstrated **clinically meaningful improvements in anhedonia** after six sessions (Cohen's **d = -0.73**, 95% CI [-1.00, -0.46]), peaking post-treatment (**d = -1.07**, 95% CI [-1.36, -0.78]), and largely sustained at follow-up (**d = -0.84**, 95% CI [-1.15, -0.54]). **Anhedonia response rates** (50% SHAPS reduction) reached **44.9%** by session six and **63.9%** at follow-up, substantially exceeding **MADRS response rates** (35.1% to 41.5%). Notably, **baseline anhedonia did not predict ECT outcomes** for general depression (interaction p = .518), unlike standard treatments where anhedonia compromises response. **Cross-lagged panel models** suggested that early anhedonia improvements may **precede and predict** broader depressive symptom relief, pointing to a potential **catalytic role for positive affect restoration** during ECT.

## Clinical Insight
These findings have immediate implications for **treatment sequencing in depression**. For patients with **pronounced anhedonia** who have not responded to standard antidepressants or psychotherapy, **ECT should be considered earlier** rather than as a last resort. The SHAPS response rates of **~64% at follow-up** substantially exceed what standard pharmacotherapy typically achieves for anhedonic symptoms. Crucially, unlike first-line treatments where **higher baseline anhedonia predicts poorer outcomes**, ECT effectiveness was **unaffected by anhedonia severity** — this removes a key clinical barrier to considering ECT for anhedonia-dominant presentations. The finding that **anhedonia improvement may catalyze broader depression recovery** suggests that monitoring SHAPS scores early during ECT (by session six) could serve as an **early treatment response indicator**. Clinicians should also note that while **rTMS** and **ketamine** show comparable antianhedonic effects, ECT produced more rapid improvements, making it preferable in **urgent or severe cases**. The study used **bifrontal electrode placement** as standard, which is relevant for clinicians concerned about cognitive side effects.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental study (retrospective observational with longitudinal design).

__Statistical Power__: The sample of N = 67 patients is relatively large for a clinical ECT study, though the cross-lagged panel model typically requires N >= 100.
The authors acknowledge this limitation and emphasize the exploratory nature of the temporal analysis.
The multilevel models had adequate power for the primary hypotheses with Šidák-corrected significance levels.

__Preregistration__: The hypotheses and analysis plan were preregistered on OSF prior to data analysis.
This strengthens the interpretability of the results and reduces concerns about analytic flexibility.
Deviations from the preregistered plan are not prominently discussed, which would have been informative.

__Effect Sizes__: Effect sizes are reported with 95% confidence intervals throughout, following best practices.
Cohen's d values are interpreted using standard conventions and demonstrate large effects on anhedonia.
The clinical significance is further supported by response and remission rate analyses at each time point.

__Statistical Rigor__: Multilevel models accounted for the nested data structure (repeated measures within individuals).
Šidák correction was applied for multiple comparisons across the four time point tests.
The cross-lagged panel model showed excellent fit (CFI = 1.00, RMSEA = .00) but sensitivity analyses with outlier removal are noted.

__Measurement Validity__: The SHAPS is a well-validated measure of anhedonia, though it primarily captures consummatory anhedonia rather than anticipatory anhedonia.
The MADRS was designed specifically to capture treatment-related changes in depression.
Both are established instruments with strong psychometric properties in depression populations.

__Open Science__: The study protocol and analysis plan were preregistered on OSF.
Data are not publicly available due to the sensitive nature of clinical data and ethical restrictions.
Analytic code is available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.

__Participant Transparency__: Participants were patients receiving ECT for depressive episodes (ICD-10: F31.3-31.5, F32, F33).
Data collection spanned August 2021 to September 2025 at a single Swiss site.
The sample is naturalistic, enhancing external validity but limiting internal validity due to the absence of a control group.

## Notes
Strong clinical study with preregistration. Key limitation is retrospective design without control group. The anhedonia-as-catalyst finding is exploratory and needs intensive longitudinal replication.
