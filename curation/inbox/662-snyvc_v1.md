## Summary
This **scoping review** synthesizes human evidence on the **habenula's role in addiction**, following **PRISMA-ScR guidelines**. Nineteen studies were included, organized by measure category: **volume**, **structural connectivity**, **functional connectivity**, **activity**, and **genetic measures**. Key findings include **reduced fronto-habenula white-matter integrity** in individuals with addiction, **elevated habenula-striatal resting-state functional connectivity**, and **withdrawal-related habenula-frontal connectivity changes**. Volumetric studies did not find consistent evidence linking **habenula volume** to addiction status. Activity findings were mixed: increased **habenula blood flow during withdrawal**, but **reduced differentiation between positive and negative feedback** in task-based fMRI, alongside heightened reward responses. **Genetic findings** most consistently implicated **nicotine-related habenula signaling**. The review found that human evidence converged most clearly with preclinical models for **withdrawal mechanisms** and **nicotine addiction**, but no domain showed direct one-to-one human-preclinical alignment. Several domains were either **one-sided** (findings in only one species), based on **non-equivalent measures**, or entirely **absent** in both literatures.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians working in addiction treatment, the **habenula** is emerging as a potential **treatment target** and **biomarker** for addiction, though clinical applications remain premature. The most translationally relevant finding is the consistent evidence for **habenula involvement in withdrawal states** — the habenula-frontal connectivity changes during withdrawal suggest a neural mechanism for the **negative affect and anhedonia** that drive relapse. This reinforces the clinical importance of addressing **withdrawal-related distress** proactively in treatment planning. The **nicotine-specific genetic findings** are particularly relevant for smoking cessation efforts, suggesting that individuals with certain **habenula-related genetic profiles** may have differential responses to nicotine replacement or varenicline. Clinicians should be aware that the habenula's role in **reward processing** (specifically, signaling negative reward prediction errors) may partially explain why **contingency management** and other reward-based interventions show variable efficacy across patients. The review's emphasis on the **lack of direct human-preclinical alignment** serves as an important caution against over-interpreting animal model findings for clinical practice.

## Methodology Note
This is a qualitative study (scoping review).

__Measurement Validity__: The review synthesizes evidence from diverse measurement modalities (MRI, fMRI, DTI, genetics), each with different validity considerations.
The PRISMA-ScR guidelines were followed, providing a structured approach to evidence synthesis.
However, scoping reviews do not systematically assess study quality, which limits conclusions about the reliability of individual findings.
The heterogeneity of measurement approaches across studies makes cross-study comparisons challenging.

__Open Science__: The review is published as an open-access preprint on PsyArXiv.
No protocol registration number is mentioned, though PRISMA-ScR was followed.
The review does not appear to have a registered or published protocol prior to the search.

__Participant Transparency__: The 19 included studies cover diverse addiction populations, though the specific substances, sample sizes, and demographics are summarized in the review tables.
The review highlights that most human studies have small sample sizes, limiting the precision of estimates.
Specific demographic characteristics of the aggregate sample are not systematically reported.

## Notes
Scoping review with no quality assessment of included studies. The finding that nicotine evidence is most developed reflects the disproportionate research focus on smoking. Clinical applications are speculative at this stage.
