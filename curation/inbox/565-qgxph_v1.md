## Summary

This study examined the effects of **human-animal interaction** and **animal touch** on soldiers' **stress** during active wartime duty. In a sample of **N = 31 soldiers** on active military deployment, participants completed online questionnaires rating their comfort from an **animal companion**, the frequency and quality of human-animal interactions, and levels of **trait anxiety** and **state anxiety**. Contrary to the initial hypothesis that positive animal interaction would uniformly reduce stress, results revealed a more nuanced pattern: soldiers with higher **trait anxiety** (rather than state anxiety) experienced greater comfort from animal interactions. A clear subgroup divide emerged — soldiers who grew up with pets, **female soldiers**, and **non-combat soldiers** engaged in more frequent human-animal interactions, derived greater comfort, and exhibited higher levels of both trait and state anxiety. In contrast, **combat soldiers** and **male soldiers** showed markedly different patterns. The study contributes to understanding **animal-assisted interventions (AAI)** in extreme trauma-exposed populations and highlights the importance of individual differences in response to animal-based stress relief.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians working with **trauma-exposed populations**, particularly military personnel and veterans, this study offers evidence for the potential role of **animal-assisted interventions (AAI)** as a complementary approach to trauma-focused therapy. The finding that soldiers with higher **trait anxiety** benefited most from animal contact suggests that AAI may be especially valuable for individuals with a dispositional vulnerability to anxiety — a known risk factor for **PTSD** development. The differential responses by combat versus non-combat roles and by gender underscore the need for **personalized treatment planning** when integrating AAI into clinical work. Combat soldiers' reduced engagement with animals may reflect emotional numbing, hyperarousal-related avoidance, or cultural norms around masculinity that clinicians should explore in therapy. For therapists using **prolonged exposure (PE)**, **cognitive processing therapy (CPT)**, or **EMDR** with military clients, the study suggests that incorporating animal interaction — where available and desired by the client — might support **affect regulation** between sessions and provide a non-threatening avenue for **somatic grounding**. The small sample size (N = 31) limits statistical power, but the wartime context provides rare ecological validity for studying real-time stress physiology and intervention responses in active combat zones.

## Relevant For
- Trauma therapists working with military personnel and veterans
- Clinicians incorporating animal-assisted interventions into practice
- Mental health professionals in military and defence settings
- Researchers studying AAI in extreme stress contexts

## Notes

PDF was not available; curation based on the full abstract description. The sample of 31 soldiers is very small, limiting statistical power and generalisability. The cross-sectional design cannot establish causal direction — it is unclear whether animal interaction reduces stress or whether less distressed soldiers seek out animals more. The wartime context is both a strength (ecological validity) and limitation (potential confounding from active threat). The distinction between trait and state anxiety as moderators is a valuable contribution to understanding individual differences in AAI response.
