## Summary
This study leveraged population-wide Norwegian registry data (N=6,242,506 residents) to compute Pearson-Aitken Family Genetic Risk Scores (PA-FGRSs) for 10 major psychiatric and neurodevelopmental diagnoses, including schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, major depression, anxiety disorders, ADHD, ASD, substance use disorders, and others. PA-FGRSs quantify genetic liability based on the diagnostic status of first- and second-degree relatives, weighted by genetic relatedness. The study represents the largest application of family-based genetic risk scoring to date.

PA-FGRSs for depression and anxiety showed low specificity, being elevated across nearly all diagnostic categories. This suggests substantial shared genetic vulnerability across internalizing disorders. In contrast, PA-FGRSs for conditions like schizophrenia and ASD showed more specific patterns. Higher PA-FGRSs were generally associated with earlier age of onset and higher recurrence rates, supporting their utility as prognostic indicators in clinical settings.

Partner correlations for psychiatric diagnoses ranged from .04 to .22, indicating assortative mating contributes modestly to familial clustering. A within-family design using cousin and sibling comparisons generally supported the assumptions of the PA-FGRS approach, though the Dissimilarity in Unshared Diagnoses (DUD) metric for ADHD and substance use disorders may capture non-genetic environmental effects rather than purely genetic transmission, suggesting caution in interpreting these specific scores.

## Clinical Insight
PA-FGRSs offer a practical, registry-based approach to quantifying familial genetic risk that does not require genetic testing. For clinicians, these scores could serve as a rapid screening tool to identify individuals at elevated genetic risk, particularly for informing early intervention strategies. The low specificity of depression and anxiety PA-FGRSs underscores that genetic risk for these conditions is highly pleiotropic, meaning a family history of depression should raise suspicion for a broad range of psychiatric vulnerabilities, not just mood disorders.

The association between higher PA-FGRSs and earlier onset plus higher recurrence has direct clinical implications: patients with strong family histories may benefit from more aggressive or earlier treatment, closer monitoring, and psychoeducation about recurrence risk. However, the finding that DUD may capture non-genetic effects for some disorders (particularly ADHD and SUD) reminds clinicians that family history reflects both genetic and shared environmental influences.

## Relevant For
Clinical psychologists, psychiatrists, and genetic counselors working in settings where family history assessment is part of routine care, and researchers interested in transdiagnostic genetic risk architectures.

## Notes
Large-scale population registry study from Norway. PA-FGRS provides a practical alternative to polygenic risk scores when genetic data is unavailable. Key limitation: Scandinavian population limits generalizability.
