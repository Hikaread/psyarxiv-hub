## Summary

This observational study used **baseline data from a randomized controlled trial** of digital wellbeing trainings to estimate the prevalence of **anxiety**, **depression**, and **occupational burnout** among **school system employees (SSEs)** post-pandemic, and to identify correlates of distress symptoms. The sample comprised **N = 827** school system employees (teacher n = 449). Results showed that after schools returned to pre-pandemic routines, SSEs reported significantly higher average levels of **anxiety** (0.90 SD above general population) and **depression** (0.61 SD above general population), with elevated odds of clinically meaningful levels (ORs = 1.63-2.01). **Multivariable linear and logistic regressions** revealed that perceptions of **administrator appreciation** and **satisfaction with students** — but not COVID-specific anxiety — explained unique variance in **occupational burnout**, controlling for anxiety and depression. Lower earning, female, and White SSEs showed distinct patterns of distress correlates. The findings suggest that occupational rather than pandemic-specific factors drive ongoing distress in this workforce.

## Clinical Insight

For **occupational psychologists** and **therapists** working with education sector employees, this study provides valuable data on the persistent mental health burden carried by teachers and school staff. The effect sizes — nearly one standard deviation elevation in anxiety relative to the general population — indicate a workforce experiencing clinically significant distress that warrants **preventive intervention**. The finding that **administrator appreciation** and **student satisfaction** predicted burnout over and above anxiety and depression points to organizational-level factors as key intervention targets, consistent with the **Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model**. Clinicians consulting to school systems should consider recommending organizational interventions (improving leadership support, recognition practices) alongside individual-level approaches (CBT-based stress management, mindfulness programs). The demographic differences in distress correlates suggest that **tailored approaches** are needed — for example, lower-earning staff may face additional economic stressors that compound occupational demands. The study also demonstrates the utility of **digital wellbeing interventions** as a scalable approach for large school systems, with implications for **workplace mental health programs** in other high-stress helping professions.

- Therapists treating burnout and occupational stress in helping professions
- School psychologists and counsellors

- Researchers studying post-pandemic occupational mental health
