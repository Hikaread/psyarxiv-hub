## Summary
This **preregistered longitudinal EMA study** (N = 607 diagnosed with **Alcohol Use Disorder**) examined whether **working memory capacity (WMC)** moderates the within-person association between **stress** and **alcohol consumption** over one year. Participants completed a gamified smartphone-based **WMC task every 4 weeks** and EMA assessments of stress and drinking **every two days**. As preregistered, the key finding was a significant **stress x WMC interaction** (b = -0.67, p = .009): participants with **low WMC** consumed more alcohol in stressful months (b = 1.03, p = .005), while those with **high WMC** showed no stress-drinking association (b = -0.32, p = .384). Exploratory analyses revealed that **within-person fluctuations in WMC** also moderated the stress-drinking link (b = -1.94, p = .005), and the effect was robust to Bayesian hurdle modeling and pattern mixture models. **dlPFC volume** showed a non-significant trend in the expected direction.

## Clinical Insight
This study provides the first **real-world evidence** that WMC acts as a **protective buffer** against stress-induced drinking in AUD. For clinicians, this suggests that **cognitive functioning assessments** — particularly WMC — could help identify patients most vulnerable to stress-relapse pathways. The finding that **within-person WMC fluctuations** also matter is clinically significant: a patient whose cognitive resources are temporarily depleted (e.g., due to poor sleep, intoxication, or comorbid conditions) may be more susceptible to stress-driven drinking in that period. This supports the development of **just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAI)** that could deliver cognitive support or coping prompts when WMC is likely to be low. From a dual-process perspective, higher WMC may help patients **shield long-term abstinence goals** from stress-triggered automatic approach tendencies, suggesting that **cognitive remediation** or **WMC training** could be a valuable adjunct to AUD treatment.

## Methodology Note
This is a quantitative_experimental-type paper.

__Statistical Power__: The sample of **N = 607** with AUD provides strong statistical power, particularly important as the authors note that translational behavioral task effects are often small.
Power was adequate to detect the preregistered stress x WMC interaction (p = .009).
The study duration of one year with 13 WMC assessments per participant provides substantial within-person data points.

__Preregistration__: The study was **preregistered** on OSF (osf.io/9ze2u/).
The primary hypothesis (H6) was confirmed as preregistered.
Exploratory analyses (within-person WMC fluctuations, daily frequency analysis) were clearly labeled as such.
One deviation: initial analyses used a more complex model; the simplified model (reported) yielded consistent findings.

__Effect Sizes__: The key interaction effect was reported as **b = -0.67** with 95% CIs implicitly available through the t-statistic.
Simple slopes were provided: low WMC group b = 1.03 (p = .005), high WMC group b = -0.32 (p = .384).
A Bayesian hurdle model confirmed the direction with **99.4% probability of direction**.
No standardized effect sizes (e.g., Cohen's d) or clinical significance thresholds were reported for the interaction.

__Statistical Rigor__: Robustness checks included testing interactions with age, gender, AUD criteria, baseline consumption, and session number — none were significant.
Multiple analysis approaches (frequentist mixed model, Bayesian hurdle model, pattern mixture models) converged on the same conclusion.
Limitations include substantial dropout, with the WMC x stress effect being **stronger in completers** (b = -0.26, p = .020), raising concerns about attrition bias.
A single-item stress measure was used (common in EMA but limiting construct validity).

__Measurement Validity__: WMC was measured using a **gamified smartphone task** — innovative for ecological validity but with unknown convergent validity against standard WMC assessments.
The within-person WMC increase over time (training effect) suggests the task may have been insufficiently difficult, potentially producing ceiling effects.
Stress was assessed via a single EMA item correlated with a validated questionnaire, but this limits the specificity of stress measurement.
AUD diagnosis was based on clinical criteria, which is appropriate.

__Open Science__: The study was **preregistered** on OSF.
The preprint is available on **PsyArXiv**.
Supplementary materials including robustness checks and additional analyses were referenced.
Data and code availability were not explicitly stated in the available text.

__Participant Transparency__: N = 607 participants diagnosed with AUD.
The sample included more men than women, limiting **gender generalizability**.
Age range was not specified in the extracted text.
The sample was drawn from a clinical AUD population, which is a strength for external validity to treatment-seeking populations.
