## Summary
This methods paper unifies two dominant approaches — **reinforcement learning (RL)** and **control engineering** — for building personalizing **just-in-time adaptive interventions (pJITAIs)**. JITAIs deliver digital health support tailored to rapidly changing internal and external factors via **decision rules**. Standard JITAIs use fixed decision rules designed from historical data, which can become stale across deployments. pJITAIs address this by using **online learning algorithms** that modify decision rules within a deployment based on streaming data. The paper provides a comprehensive **terminology mapping** between pJITAI concepts, RL, and control engineering (e.g., tailoring variables = state variables, intervention options = actions, decision rules = policy, outcomes = reward/cost). A concrete example illustrates a pJITAI for **physical activity promotion** with three intervention options (walking reminder, stand-and-stretch reminder, or no message), three daily decision points, and weekly model updates. The **pJITAI Toolbox** is referenced as a no-code platform for implementing these algorithms.

## Clinical Insight
For clinicians developing or using **digital mental health interventions**, this paper provides the conceptual foundation for the next generation of **adaptive treatment tools**. pJITAIs represent a significant advance over static digital interventions (e.g., fixed-schedule psychoeducation push notifications) because they **learn in real-time** which intervention components work for which patients, adapting as patterns change across deployment contexts. This is particularly relevant for mental health where **treatment response is highly individual** and may shift over time. Clinicians should understand that the decision rules in their digital tools need not remain static, and that online learning approaches can continuously optimize intervention delivery. However, the paper also raises implicit clinical cautions: **algorithmic optimization** of intervention delivery must remain aligned with clinical judgment, and the risk of **algorithmic drift** away from therapeutically meaningful content requires oversight.

## Notes
This is a methods/conceptual paper unifying RL and control engineering for pJITAIs. It does not present empirical treatment outcomes but provides the algorithmic framework that could be applied in future clinical trials.
