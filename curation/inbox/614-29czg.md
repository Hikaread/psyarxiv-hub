## Summary

This scoping review, following the **PRISMA-ScR** framework, synthesised 85 documents published between 2000 and 2025 examining **executive functions (EF)** in individuals with genetically confirmed **Williams syndrome (WS)** — a genetic neurodevelopmental disorder associated with mild-to-moderate intellectual disability, relative language strengths, visuospatial difficulties, and marked hypersociability with social disinhibition. Four databases were searched: ProQuest, Scopus, Web of Science, and EBSCOhost. The review found **severe and generalised executive dysfunction** across all EF domains examined, including **working memory**, **inhibition**, **cognitive flexibility**, and **planning**. Critically, EF impairments were significantly associated with emotional, behavioural, social, and adaptive outcomes, positioning executive dysfunction as a central transdiagnostic factor in WS that bridges cognitive deficits and real-world functional impairment. However, the review identified considerable methodological heterogeneity across studies, which predominantly used cross-sectional comparative designs with small sample sizes. A key gap identified is the scarcity of longitudinal data, limiting understanding of executive developmental trajectories across the lifespan. Limited intervention evidence was found, highlighting the need for studies clarifying which specific executive components are most amenable to improvement through targeted training.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians assessing and working with neurodivergent populations, this review provides a comprehensive map of the **EF profile in Williams syndrome** and its downstream impact on everyday functioning. The finding that EF impairments serve as a **transdiagnostic mediator** between cognitive deficits and adaptive outcomes is clinically significant: it suggests that targeting executive functions in intervention could yield broad improvements across emotional regulation, behavioural control, social functioning, and adaptive skills — consistent with EF-focused approaches used in other neurodivergent populations such as ADHD and autism. The methodological heterogeneity identified across 85 studies is itself a clinical concern, as it means there is no standardised **EF assessment protocol** validated specifically for WS populations. Clinicians should be cautious about generalising findings from one assessment tool to the WS cognitive profile, given the disorder's distinctive pattern of relative language strength coexisting with visuospatial deficits and hypersociability — a profile that may interact with standard EF tasks in atypical ways. The hypersociability and **social disinhibition** characteristic of WS, combined with generalised EF deficits, creates particular vulnerability in social decision-making contexts that clinicians should assess proactively. The near-absence of intervention evidence represents a significant gap: while EF training programs exist for ADHD and autism, there are none tailored to the unique cognitive profile of WS.
