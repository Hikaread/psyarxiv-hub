## Summary

Two studies examined **sensory discomfort** across multiple modalities in individuals with **migraine**. Study 1 (N = 60 migraine; N = 59 headache-free) used standardised questionnaires during an interictal (headache-free) period, finding greater **visual discomfort** (d = 0.40), **odor-related discomfort** (d = 0.46), and **tactile pain** measured by kinesiophobia (d = 0.40) in the migraine group, but no significant differences in **auditory discomfort** or **motion sickness susceptibility**. When subdivided, the **migraine with aura** group showed greater visual discomfort, while the **migraine without aura** group showed greater odor discomfort. Study 2 was a **30-day prospective diary** (N = 18 with migraine; 10 with aura, 8 without aura) using daily modified questionnaires. **Auditory discomfort** increased significantly the day before a migraine compared to regular days (p = .046) and peaked on the migraine day (p < .001). For the **migraine with aura** group specifically, **visual discomfort** also increased the day before migraine pain (p < .05). **Motion sickness** increased substantially on the migraine day.

## Methodology Note

__Strengths__: The combination of a cross-sectional comparison with a **prospective daily diary** design provides both between-group and within-person evidence. The use of **standardised questionnaires** across multiple sensory modalities (visual, auditory, odor, tactile, motion) is a strength. The z-scoring approach in Study 1 allows valid cross-modality comparisons. The a priori separation of **migraine with aura** and **migraine without aura** subgroups is clinically important.

__Limitation__: Study 2 had a very small final sample (N = 18, 10 MA, 8 MO) after substantial attrition from 54 initial recruits, limiting statistical power for subgroup analyses. Both studies were conducted online during the **COVID-19 pandemic**, which likely increased **screen time** and may have inflated visual discomfort scores. Few Study 2 participants reported any **odor discomfort** or **tactile pain** on a daily basis, preventing analysis of these modalities across the migraine cycle. The **allodynia** measure in Study 1 could not be collected from headache-free participants due to a technical oversight. No correction for multiple comparisons was applied in Study 1. The studies relied entirely on **self-report** with no physiological or behavioural validation.

## Clinical Insight

The finding that **auditory discomfort** increases the day before migraine pain — and that **visual discomfort** shows the same prodromal pattern in migraine with aura — supports the clinical use of **sensory tracking** as an early warning system for approaching attacks. For patients with **migraine with aura**, daily monitoring of visual and auditory discomfort could enable earlier administration of **acute medication** or **prophylactic interventions** before the headache phase begins. The study highlights that sensory symptom profiles differ meaningfully between **migraine with aura** and **migraine without aura**, with aura patients showing stronger cross-modal correlations suggesting a more **domain-general sensory hypersensitivity**. Clinicians should assess sensory discomfort across multiple modalities rather than relying solely on the IHS-required **photophobia** and **phonophobia** criteria, as odor and tactile discomfort were elevated interictally even when auditory discomfort was not.

## Relevant For

- Neurologists managing migraine with and without aura
- Headache specialists interested in prodromal symptom monitoring
- Clinical psychologists working with chronic pain populations
- Researchers studying sensory processing in primary headache disorders
