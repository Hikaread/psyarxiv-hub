## Summary

This study integrated the transdiagnostic cognitive-behavioral and cognitive-interpersonal models of eating disorders using network analysis in 493 Australian participants reporting ED symptoms, of whom 271 scored above the clinical cutoff on the EDE-QS and 108 reported a lifetime eating disorder diagnosis. A partial correlation network was estimated, with bootstrapped stability and centrality difference tests. Concern Over Mistakes emerged as the most central node overall (expected influence = 1.48) and the strongest bridge node connecting cognitive-behavioral and interpersonal clusters (bridge expected influence = 0.86). The edge between Overvaluation of Weight and Shape and Weight and Shape Rigidity was among the strongest in the network (0.45). The network demonstrated excellent stability.

## Clinical Insight

The prominence of Concern Over Mistakes as both the most central symptom and the primary bridge between cognitive-behavioral and interpersonal dimensions suggests that perfectionism, rather than body dissatisfaction alone, may be the engine that connects disordered eating cognition with relational dysfunction. For therapists, this supports directly assessing and targeting perfectionism early in treatment, even when a client presents primarily with shape concerns or interpersonal difficulties. The strong connection between Overvaluation of Weight and Shape and Rigidity indicates that rigid beliefs about weight, rather than the content of those beliefs alone, may be a key maintaining factor that deserves explicit attention in cognitive restructuring. These findings are cross-sectional and cannot confirm causal pathways, but they provide a empirically grounded map for prioritizing treatment targets within the transdiagnostic framework.

## Relevant For

Clinicians treating eating disorders using CBT-E or integrative approaches, particularly when clients present with comorbid perfectionism and interpersonal difficulties
