## Summary

This **seven-day experience sampling study** (n = 174; **4,625 observations**; 91.49% compliance) tested **Lee and Hancock's (2024) social media mindsets framework** in young South African adults (M age = 20.92). The study investigated within-person associations between momentary **agency evaluations** (perceived control over use) and **valence evaluations** (perceived positive/negative effects), **passive social media use** (mindless scrolling), and **negative affect**.

More positive **valence evaluations** were consistently associated with lower negative affect (b = -0.188 to -0.208, p < .001 across models). Greater perceived **agency** was also associated with lower negative affect (b = -0.069 to -0.084, p = .001–.004). **Mindless scrolling** was associated with small increases in negative affect (b = 0.004, p < .001) but did **not reliably mediate** the relationship between evaluations and well-being (indirect effects not significantly different from zero). **Valence evaluations moderated** the SMU–negative affect association: increases in use were less strongly associated with negative affect when use was experienced as more beneficial (H7B supported). The **appraisal pathway** received support, while the **behavioral pathway** (mediation through scrolling) did not.

## Clinical Insight

For clinicians addressing social media-related distress in young adults, this study provides evidence that **how clients interpret their social media use** matters more than the behavior itself. The **appraisal pathway**—where negative evaluations of use directly increase distress—suggests that **cognitive restructuring** of social media beliefs could be a therapeutic target, paralleling CBT approaches to other behavioral concerns. The finding that **passive use (mindless scrolling)** had only a small direct effect and did not mediate the evaluation-wellbeing relationship suggests that interventions focused solely on reducing screen time or scrolling may miss the more impactful target of **use interpretations**. The moderation finding—that positive evaluations buffer the negative affect associated with increased use—supports helping clients develop more balanced, less catastrophizing appraisals of their digital habits.

## Methodology Note

This is a quantitative_experimental paper.

__Statistical Power__: N = 174 with 4,625 observations provides excellent power for within-person analyses.
Compliance rate of 91.49% is very high.
The study was adequately powered for the within-person effect sizes detected.
Between-person effects (n = 174) have less power but the focus was on within-person processes.

__Preregistration__: **Preregistered on OSF** (https://osf.io/rq4mu/?view_only=a6b02aedd3d247c4aee94843a06e2841).
Hypotheses and analysis plan were specified before data collection.

__Effect Sizes__: Valence evaluations: b = -0.188 to -0.208, p < .001.
Agency evaluations: b = -0.069 to -0.084, p = .001–.004.
Mindless scrolling: b = 0.004, p < .001 (small effect).
Moderation of SMU-negative affect link by valence evaluations was confirmed.
Mediation (behavioral pathway): indirect effects included zero in confidence intervals.

__Statistical Rigor__: Appropriate multilevel modeling for the ESM data structure (observations nested within persons).
Lagged affect was controlled for (b = 0.222, p < .001), strengthening causal inference.
Sensitivity analyses included adjusting for time of day and prior affect.
The distinction between within-person and between-person effects is properly maintained.
FDR correction was likely applied to multiple tests.
The study followed Lee and Hancock's (2024) theoretical framework with clear, pre-registered hypotheses.

__Measurement Validity__: All measures were state-level, referring to the hour preceding each survey.
SMU was measured as time spent on social media (self-report).
Passive use was measured as mindless scrolling (single item or brief scale).
Valence and agency evaluations were measured as momentary state appraisals.
Negative affect was measured as a state-level composite.
All items were in English; the South African context may introduce linguistic and cultural measurement concerns for some participants.
No donated log data are reported (only self-report SMU).

__Open Science__: Preregistered on OSF.
Compliance rate (91.49%) and observation count (4,625) are reported transparently.

__Participant Transparency__: n = 174 (66.09% female, 32.18% male, 1.72% other).
M age = 20.92 (SD = 3.25; median = 20).
South African university students, recruited from a larger survey study.
Two batches recruited July–August 2024.
Average 26.58 completed surveys per participant (SD = 2.56, min = 16).
The young, student sample limits generalizability to broader populations.

## Relevant For

## Notes

The study was conducted as the second phase of a larger project on SMU among young adults in South Africa. The finding that the behavioral pathway (mindless scrolling mediation) was not supported while the appraisal pathway was, suggests that the growing "screen time" narrative may be less clinically relevant than the "how you think about your use" narrative. The South African context adds cultural diversity to a literature dominated by US and European samples.
