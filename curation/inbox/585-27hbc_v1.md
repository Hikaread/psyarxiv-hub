## Summary
This study investigated whether **prosodic features** (fundamental frequency, intensity, duration) and **voice quality measures** (jitter, shimmer, harmonic-to-noise ratio) differ between individuals with **latent aphasia** (WAB Aphasia Quotient > 93.8, i.e. within "normal limits") and matched neurotypical controls during spontaneous connected speech. Ten speakers with latent aphasia post-stroke and ten controls were recorded retelling the Cinderella story from **AphasiaBank**. Using **linear mixed-effects models**, the study found significant group-by-position interactions for **f0** and **intensity**: both groups showed higher f0 and lower intensity at utterance-final versus utterance-initial positions, but these patterns were reduced in the latent aphasia group. The latent aphasia group also showed consistently **longer word duration** than controls. Voice quality measures showed **minimal group differences**, consistent with the clinical classification of these individuals as having "normal" speech. Linguistic features including **syllable number**, **part of speech**, and **word type** (content vs. function) were controlled.

## Clinical Insight
**Latent aphasia** represents a diagnostically challenging presentation where patients score within normal limits on standardised tests like the **Western Aphasia Battery (WAB)** yet exhibit subtle but measurable language deficits detectable through instrumental analysis. This has significant implications for clinical practice: patients who appear to have recovered from aphasia may still experience underlying linguistic processing difficulties that affect daily communication. The finding that **prosodic features** — particularly reduced f0 modulation and longer word duration — can reliably distinguish latent aphasia from neurotypical speech supports the development of **acoustic biomarkers** for post-stroke language impairment. For speech-language pathologists, these results suggest that perceptually normal speech may still harbour subtle deficits requiring instrumental detection. The use of **connected speech** rather than reading tasks enhances ecological validity, as spontaneous speech places greater demands on linguistic planning. Voice quality measures (jitter, shimmer, HNR) were less informative for group differentiation but provide novel baseline data for this population using spontaneous speech rather than the sustained vowel tasks typically used in voice assessment.

## Relevant For
- Speech-language pathologists assessing post-stroke aphasia
- Neuropsychologists evaluating subtle language deficits
- Researchers developing acoustic biomarkers for language disorders
- Clinicians working with patients who appear recovered from aphasia
