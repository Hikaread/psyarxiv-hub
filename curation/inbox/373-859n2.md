## Summary

This paper proposes a dual-engine framework for scaling evidence-based mental health interventions, addressing the tension between maintaining fidelity to proven mechanisms and adapting to new contexts. Drawing on implementation science and the Shamiri Institute model, it argues that organizations need two simultaneous processes: one optimizing the core intervention components and another learning from real-world adaptations. The framework aims to prevent voltage drop, the silent erosion of active ingredients when interventions scale across different settings, workforces, and populations.

## Clinical Insight

For therapists and clinical leaders involved in program development, training, or implementing manualized treatments at scale, this framework offers a practical governance model. Many therapists encounter the challenge of adapting evidence-based protocols for their specific client populations while worrying about fidelity. This paper provides a structured way to think about which adaptations are safe and which risk undermining the interventions mechanisms of change. Clinical supervisors and training directors will find the dual-engine concept useful for balancing adaptation guidance with fidelity requirements in their training programs.

## Relevant For

Clinical supervisors, training directors, therapists implementing manualized treatments, program managers in community mental health, clinicians involved in dissemination and implementation.
