## Summary
This critical reanalysis examines two influential studies (Foody et al., 2013; Foody et al., 2015) that tested the theoretical link between Relational Frame Theory (RFT) and Acceptance and Commitment Therapy's (ACT) concept of Self-as-Context. The original 2013 study, which has 134 Google Scholar citations placing it in the top 1% of psychology articles, is frequently cited in the ACT literature (including the Oxford Handbook of ACT) as evidence that hierarchical framing of private events reduces distress more effectively than distinction framing. Hussey identified several critical issues: the central claim did not correspond with the reported analyses, and the failed 2015 replication was routinely omitted from citations. A multiverse analysis mapping onto the original claim found null results under every set of conditions tested. The paper documents how these studies continue to be cited at face value in major handbooks and textbooks, despite the failed replication and the mismatch between claims and analyses.

## Clinical Insight
This paper has direct implications for evidence-based practice in ACT. The hierarchical-versus-distinction framing exercises derived from Foody et al. (2013) are described in the Oxford Handbook of ACT as evidence-based techniques, yet this reanalysis shows the underlying evidence does not support the claimed differential efficacy. For ACT practitioners, this means the theoretical justification for preferring one type of self-based exercise over another should be reconsidered. More broadly, the paper illustrates how uncritical citation practices and weak research methodology can propagate unsupported claims into clinical training materials and handbooks. Clinicians should be aware that even highly-cited studies in major reference works may have significant methodological flaws that undermine their conclusions. This supports the case for practitioners engaging with the primary evidence base rather than relying solely on secondary summaries.

## Relevant For
ACT practitioners and trainers; researchers in contextual behavioral science; clinicians interested in evidence-based evaluation of therapeutic techniques; those concerned with replication and citation practices in clinical psychology.

## Notes
This is a reanalysis paper rather than an empirical study. It focuses specifically on the RFT-ACT link regarding Self-as-Context exercises and does not evaluate ACT as a whole. The multiverse analysis used summary statistics from the original studies.
