## Summary
The Repeatable Social Stress Test (ReSST) is a novel experimental paradigm designed to overcome limitations of existing stress induction tasks, which typically cannot be repeated within or across sessions. By combining adapted versions of the Trier Social Stress Test and the Iowa Singing Social Stress Test with video-based stress reminders, the ReSST enables researchers to elicit and study recurring stress responses in a controlled setting. Validation with 66 healthy adults demonstrated robust increases in subjective stress, heart rate, and cortisol compared to matched no-stress control conditions. The paradigm successfully reactivated stress responses within sessions using video reminders of the social-evaluative component.

## Clinical Insight
Repeated stress exposure is a hallmark of many clinical presentations, from workplace burnout to chronic PTSD. The ReSST provides an empirically validated tool for clinical researchers investigating how stress accumulates and whether interventions can buffer against repeated activation. For practicing therapists, the paradigm's finding that video reminders alone can reactivate stress responses suggests that environmental cues play a powerful role in maintaining stress cycles, reinforcing the importance of cue-exposure techniques in CBT and other trauma-focused therapies. The sex differences in subjective responding warrant attention in clinical assessment.

## Relevant For
Clinical researchers investigating stress reactivity, therapists working with trauma and stress-related disorders, health psychologists studying psychophysiological stress responses.

## Notes
Validated with healthy volunteers aged 19-58; results may differ in clinical populations. The hybrid format combining two established stress tests with novel video-reminder components provides a methodological advance for within-subject stress research designs.
