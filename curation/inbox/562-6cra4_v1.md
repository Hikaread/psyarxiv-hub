## Summary

This study developed and validated the **Sophie Sugar Addiction Scale (SSAS)**, a novel **psychometric instrument** assessing **sugar addiction** as a distinct **behavioural addiction**, grounded in **Griffiths' (2005) components model** of addiction. An initial 18-item pool covering six dimensions — **salience**, **mood modification**, **tolerance**, **withdrawal**, **conflict**, and **relapse** — was administered to **N = 1,017 adults** (M_age = 28.7, SD = 8.8; 41% female). **Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)** guided refinement to a final six-item unidimensional scale demonstrating high **internal consistency** (alpha = .87, omega = .86) and strong **convergent validity** with the **Yale Food Addiction Scale 2.0 (YFAS 2.0)**. The SSAS also showed meaningful associations with **ADHD**, **OCD**, **depression**, **anxiety**, and **stress**. A critical finding: **28.9% of participants met criteria for sugar addiction without meeting food addiction thresholds**, supporting the construct's uniqueness as distinct from broader food addiction. The SSAS provides a brief, reliable, and valid tool for identifying sugar addictive behaviours in both research and clinical contexts.

## Clinical Insight

The SSAS fills a significant gap in the **eating disorder and addiction assessment** toolkit by operationalizing sugar addiction separately from general food addiction. Clinicians working with **binge eating**, **emotional eating**, or **substance use** presentations should note the high comorbidity the study documents between sugar addiction and **ADHD**, **OCD**, and mood symptoms — suggesting that sugar-addictive behaviours may serve as a form of **affect regulation** or **impulse control** difficulty that cuts across traditional diagnostic categories. The finding that over a quarter of participants met criteria for sugar addiction without qualifying for food addiction challenges the common assumption that sugar addiction is simply a sub-type of food addiction, supporting more **nuanced case formulation**. In treatment settings, the brief six-item format makes the SSAS practical for **screening** in primary care, eating disorder clinics, and addiction services. Clinicians using **CBT-E**, **dialectical behaviour therapy (DBT)**, or **acceptance-based approaches** for disordered eating could use the SSAS to track treatment-related changes in addictive eating patterns alongside broader symptom measures. The association with ADHD and OCD also raises the question of whether sugar addiction reflects an underlying **executive dysfunction** that might respond to **impulsivity-targeted interventions**.

## Relevant For
- Clinicians assessing and treating eating disorders and addictive eating behaviours
- Researchers studying behavioural addictions and food addiction
- Practitioners working with comorbid ADHD, OCD, and disordered eating
- Dietitians and nutrition therapists in clinical settings

## Notes

The sample was primarily young adults (M = 28.7) recruited online, which may limit generalisability to clinical eating disorder populations. The unidimensional factor structure is a strength for brevity but may not capture the multi-component nature of addiction experiences. Validation in clinical treatment-seeking samples would strengthen the scale's utility.
