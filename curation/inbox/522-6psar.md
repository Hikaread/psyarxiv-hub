## Summary
This paper introduces the **SOcial experiences daiLy Occurrence (SOLO) scale**, a brief **end-of-day diary** measure designed to capture daily enjoyment of both **social interactions** and **solitude experiences** within a single instrument. Two validation studies were conducted: a single-day assessment (Sample 1, N = 203, recruited via Prolific, mean age 28.96) and a 14-day diary assessment (Sample 2, N = 75).

Item development began with 28 items covering social interaction quality, duration, superficial and meaningful encounters, and solitude enjoyment. Through **exploratory factor analysis** (promax rotation, eigenvalues greater than 0.3 criterion), items were reduced to 13 forming two scales: the **SOLO Social Interaction subscale** (10 items, alpha = .751, omega = .797) and the **SOLO Solitude subscale** (3 items, alpha = .611, omega = .634). The two subscales were empirically **independent** of each other, supporting the conceptualization of social enjoyment and solitude preference as **distinct dimensions**. The SOLO was initially developed for use in a clinical trial on changes in daily social interactions following **bariatric surgery**.

In Sample 1, the Social Interaction subscale correlated positively with the **Social Network Index** diversity score and the **Social Connectedness Scale-Revised**, and negatively with the **UCLA Loneliness Scale** and **Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale**, demonstrating good convergent and discriminant validity. The Solitude subscale was largely independent of these measures, confirming it captures a unique construct. In the 14-day Sample 2, the Social Interaction subscale showed meaningful associations with daily **well-being** and **positive affect**. A priori power analysis recommended N = 197 for detecting correlations of rho = 0.25 at 95% power, which was met by Sample 1.

## Clinical Insight
The SOLO scale fills a genuine measurement gap by capturing both **social engagement** and **solitude** in a single brief diary, which is valuable for clinical populations where both dimensions are relevant. In **eating disorder** treatment (the scale was originally developed for bariatric surgery patients), assessing how patients experience daily social interactions versus solitude could inform interventions targeting **social avoidance** or **social eating**. The finding that the two subscales are independent supports clinical work that treats social engagement and solitude comfort as separate therapeutic targets. The **end-of-day diary format** offers a practical compromise between intensive EMA and retrospective questionnaires, making it feasible for clinical trial settings. The inclusion of **brief encounters with weak ties** addresses an underappreciated aspect of daily social experience relevant for socially anxious or isolated patients.

## Methodology Note
This is a psychometric paper.

__Measurement Validity__: The SOLO demonstrates adequate convergent validity through correlations with established measures (SNI, UCLA Loneliness, LSAS, SCS-R, SOBI).
Discriminant validity is supported by the independence of Social Interaction and Solitude subscales.
The Solitude subscale has only 3 items with moderate reliability (alpha = .611), which is a limitation.
The 14-day test-retest data in Sample 2 provides evidence for temporal stability of the factor structure.

__Open Science__: Analysis code and scale materials available on OSF (view-only link provided for peer review).
AI-assisted writing (Claude 4.6 by Anthropic) was used for improving clarity, transparently declared.

__Participant Transparency__: Sample 1 recruited via Prolific (N = 203, 104 women, 99 men, mean age 28.96, SD = 10.42).
Participants were asked to refrain if suffering from untreated depressive or psychotic states.
One participant excluded for completing the survey in less than 4 minutes.
Both samples are predominantly young adults, limiting generalizability.

## Relevant For

- Health psychologists studying social isolation and well-being
- Clinicians treating conditions characterized by social withdrawal or avoidance

## Notes
The SOLO was initially developed for bariatric surgery research (anonymized in the paper). The 3-item Solitude subscale is notably short, which enables brief administration but limits reliability. The authors acknowledge Claude 4.6 was used to improve clarity, which is transparently reported.
