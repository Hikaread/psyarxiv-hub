## Summary
This **perspective paper** proposes a comprehensive framework for extending **affective computing** from static **emotion recognition** to continuous modeling of **emotional dynamics** in everyday life. The authors argue that current affective computing research is limited by **tightly controlled laboratory paradigms**, short recordings, **static emotion classification**, and insufficient **personalization**. The proposed framework conceptualizes emotional life as a **complex adaptive system**, where continuous emotional trajectories in **arousal-valence space** can be modeled using **wearable physiological data** (e.g., PPG, EDA, skin temperature, acceleration).

The central innovation is the concept of **personalized effective potential landscapes** — individualized topological representations where stable regions correspond to emotional **attractor basins** that shape emotional transitions, recovery speed, and vulnerability to **maladaptive states**. The authors outline a **staged deployment roadmap**: (1) develop a population-level model using high-temporal-resolution affect labels from controlled experiments (including a proposed dual-pass paradigm where participants first experience stimuli naturally, then re-view them while continuously rating emotions); (2) deploy the model in naturalistic settings via smartphone-wearable systems for **continual learning**; and (3) adapt through **user-specific personalization** using EMA-informed prompts triggered by model-detected emotional changes.

**Conceptual simulations** illustrate how emotional landscapes might change gradually through cumulative life experience or rapidly after **major adverse events**. The paper discusses applications for **emotional self-awareness**, **psychotherapy support**, mental health research, and the study of **emotional temperament**, while highlighting ethical challenges related to **privacy**, **reification** (treating model outputs as emotional truth), **unequal performance** across populations, and the psychological effects of sustained emotion monitoring.

## Clinical Insight
This framework has substantial potential for clinical application in **mental health monitoring** and **psychotherapy augmentation**. The vision of passively tracking emotional dynamics via wearables could enable **early detection** of mood episodes in conditions like bipolar disorder, major depressive disorder, and anxiety disorders. The concept of **emotional landscapes** provides a quantitative language for discussing emotional **temperament** and **vulnerability** that could enhance case formulation. For therapists, having access to temporal patterns of emotional regulation (recovery speed, attractor stability, transition patterns) could inform treatment planning and progress monitoring. The emphasis that the system should **scaffold introspection** rather than replace it is clinically appropriate. However, the significant **ethical concerns** raised — particularly reification, where users might defer their own emotional understanding to algorithmic outputs — warrant careful consideration before clinical deployment.
