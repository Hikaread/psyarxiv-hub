    11→Abstract. This study investigates the effects of three variants of Restricted
    12→Environmental Stimulation Techniques (REST)—guided meditation, multimodal
    13→Ganzfeld (MMGF), and a control resting condition—on subjective experiences related
    14→to altered states of consciousness (ASC). Specifically, the study examines emotional
    15→responses, time perception, and perceived body boundaries, while also assessing
    16→the role of trait absorption as an individual difference variable. In a within-subjects
    17→design, 48 healthy participants without prior meditation experience completed three
    18→counterbalanced 25-minute sessions across the three conditions. Measures included
    19→the Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM), Subjective Time, Self, and Space Scales
    20→(STSS), Perceived Body Boundaries Scale (PBBS), and the Tellegen Absorption
    21→Scale (TAS). Results revealed that guided meditation significantly reduced arousal
    22→and time-related thoughts compared to both the control and Ganzfeld conditions,
    23→indicating decreased mind wandering and enhanced relaxation. Correlational
    24→analyses demonstrated that positive affect and low arousal were associated with
    25→fewer time-related thoughts and faster perceived time in the control condition. The
    26→Ganzfeld condition linked reduced body boundary salience to positive emotional
    27→states as well as positive affect with a faster passage of time. In the meditation
    28→condition lower arousal was associated with reduced sense of body boundaries.
    29→Regression analyses further indicated that higher trait absorption predicted
    30→diminished body boundary salience in both the control and Ganzfeld conditions,
    31→suggesting that individual differences in absorptive capacity modulate susceptibility to
    32→ASC-like experiences. Overall, the findings highlight both shared and distinct effects
    33→of REST variants on experiences and underscore the importance of personality traits
    34→in shaping subjective responses to reduced environmental stimulation.
    35→Keywords: meditation; Ganzfeld; REST; relaxation; body boundaries; time perception

    40→1. Introduction
    41→Altered states of consciousness (ASC) refer to temporary changes in one's mental
    42→state or awareness that deviate from the normal waking state. These alterations can
    43→manifest in various forms, such as changes in perception, cognition, emotions, and
    44→sense of self (Fort et al., 2025). Unlike pathological changes seen in neurology and
    45→psychiatry, these altered states usually persist for short periods ranging from minutes
    46→to hours (Dittrich, 1985; 1998). ASC can be induced through different induction
    47→methods including meditation, hypnosis, sensory deprivation, and psychedelic
    48→substances (Kjellgren et al., 2008; Berkovich-Ohana et al., 2013; Cardeña &
    49→Terhune, 2019; Smigielski et al., 2019; Hruby et al., 2024).
    50→Restricted environmental stimulation techniques (REST; Suedfeld, 1980) entail a
    51→marked reduction of external stimuli (perceptual deprivation) and solitude in a dark,
    52→sound-attenuated environment. By minimizing external input, REST encourages
    53→individuals to shift their attention inward in a meditative manner and thus offer a
    54→controlled means of examining meditation-like states even among individuals without
    55→prior meditation experience (Soláriková & Bartolen, 2025). One common denominator
    56→in REST studies is the experience of silence; even brief periods of exposure can be
    57→highly effective for relaxation and emotional self-regulation (Ben-Soussan et al.,
    58→2021). In different experimental settings silence was judged to significantly increase
    59→relaxation, improve mood states, and alter the perception of time as well as direct
    60→orientation towards the present moment (Pfeifer et al., 2020; Pfeifer & Wittmann,
    61→2020).
    62→With the method of Floatation-REST, individuals float almost weightlessly on the
    63→surface of supersaturated water maintained at skin temperature. This typically takes
    64→place in a quiet, dark tank environment, which promotes profound physical and
    65→mental relaxation (Kjellgren et al., 2008; Schiffer et al., 2025). Participants report
    66→diminished awareness of bodily boundaries and a distortion of time, phenomena that
    67→have been systematically documented (Hruby et al., 2024; Pantazis & Wittmann,
    68→2025).
    69→In Bed-REST, individuals lie supine and relaxed on a bed in a dark, quiet room and,
    70→similar to Floatation-REST, experience reductions in arousal, anxiety, tension, and
    71→stress. In Chamber-REST, individuals are not physically restricted on a bed and can
    72→move freely within a completely darkened room, which may be furnished with a chair
    73→and a bed. Exposure to this form of REST can last for hours or even days and
    74→induces a relaxation response as well as reduces stress and anxiety (Vytykáčová et
    75→al., 2022; Soláriková & Bartolen, 2025).
    76→Silent sitting meditation can be understood as a form of REST, in which practitioners
    77→disengage from reacting to sensory stimuli. Experienced meditators report that
    78→silence and stillness are closely linked to the absence of concepts, mental activity,
    79→noise, thoughts, and distractions (Woods et al., 2020). Both anecdotal reports and
    80→systematic research with experienced meditators indicate that this practice is linked
    81→to alterations in self- and time-perception, often described as experiences of ego
    82→dissolution and a diminished sense of time (Wittmann, 2015, 2018; Droit-Volet &
    83→Dambrun, 2019). In a controlled laboratory study, experienced meditators reported

    89→weakening of bodily boundaries while meditating (Linares Gutiérrez et al., 2022).
    90→Interestingly, similar reductions in body boundary perception have also been
    91→observed in individuals who underwent a body scan meditation with no prior
    92→meditation experience (Dambrun, 2016).
    93→Another REST method is the Ganzfeld technique or multimodal Ganzfeld (MMGF;
    94→Schmidt & Prein, 2019). Wolfgang Metzger had introduced the Ganzfeld (in German
    95→meaning ‘whole field’) into experimental psychology (Metzger, 1930). In MMGF,
    96→participants are exposed to homogeneous, unstructured auditory and visual
    97→stimulation, in essence a perceptual deprivation technique (Gibson & Waddell, 1952;
    98→Dittrich, 1985). Unlike sensory deprivation techniques, which consist of cancelling out
    99→any external sensory information (in Floatation-REST), perceptual deprivation entails
   100→exposure to homogenous sensory stimulation, i.e. uniformly coloured light. Auditory
   101→homogeneity is generally achieved by using broadband sounds, such as white or
   102→brown noise (Lloyd et al., 2012; Wackermann et al., 2008; Schmidt & Prein, 2019;
   103→Schmidt et al., 2020).
   104→Prolonged exposure to MMGF can give rise to illusory perceptions, contributing to the
   105→subjective experience of altered states of consciousness (Pütz et al., 2006;
   106→Wackermann et al., 2008). In one study utilizing a specialized Ganzfeld setup—an
   107→egg-shaped, whole-body perceptual deprivation chamber—participants exhibited
   108→pronounced distortions in time perception (Glicksohn et al., 2017). Subjective reports
   109→ranged widely, from a complete loss of temporal awareness to the sensation of
   110→markedly slowed time. Regarding effects of colour, it has been suggested that
   111→colours with longer wavelengths, like red and yellow, are more stimulating. Empirical
   112→findings have shown that exposure to green versus red light MMGF combined with
   113→brown noise produced distinct effects (Kübel et al., 2021): green light was associated
   114→with relatively lower arousal, more positive emotions, the perception of a faster
   115→passage of time. These results are further supported by physiological and
   116→behavioural evidence. Red light which consists of longer wavelengths has been
   117→shown to elevate arousal, reflected in increased skin conductance levels and
   118→responses (Walters et al., 1982; Rajae-Joordens & Hanique, 2012), while light with
   119→shorter wavelengths, such as green and blue, is perceived as calming (Buechner &
   120→Maier, 2016). Both human and nonhuman primate studies indicate that red and
   121→yellow stimuli elicit more aggressive, restless, and fidgety behaviours (Humphrey,
   122→2006, pp. 19ff).
   123→Individuals vary in how easily altered states are reached, with absorption as a
   124→personality trait being a key factor in determining this ease (Hölzel & Ott, 2006).
   125→Tellegen and Atkinson introduced the concept of trait absorption in 1974 and created
   126→the Tellegen Absorption Scale (TAS), which reflects an individual's openness to self-
   127→altering experiences. More specifically, absorption refers to the tendency to undergo
   128→intense episodes where one's attention is so deeply focused on an activity or
   129→experience that they lose awareness of their surroundings (Tellegen & Atkinson,
   130→1974). A study involving 251 meditators demonstrated that absorption had a more
   131→significant effect on meditation depth than the amount of meditation practice (Hölzel
   132→& Ott, 2006). This finding suggests that the attainment of deep meditative states is
   133→influenced not only by the amount of practice but also by an individual's

   139→importance of personality factors. In investigating ASC in Floatation-REST, a stronger
   140→absorption capacity as measured with the TAS led to a stronger dissolution of body
   141→boundaries (Hruby et al., 2024).
   142→1.1. Research aim
   143→Some previous research has examined the effects of meditation on perceived
   144→passage of time, sense of bodily self, and emotions. Similarly, a few studies have
   145→explored the impact of multimodal Ganzfeld (MMGF) exposure on these variables.
   146→However, to our knowledge, no direct comparison has been undertaken to directly
   147→investigate effects and differences between meditation and Ganzfeld on the same
   148→sample of subjects within a single study design. Moreover, we included a REST
   149→control condition where individuals lie on a mat with reduced light in the room. In
   150→essence, the three interventions could all be literally termed variants of REST
   151→(Reduced Environmental Stimulation Technique) conditions since natural
   152→environmental stimulation is reduced. In all three conditions of our study design,
   153→participants lay comfortably on a gymnastics mat in order to provide a constant body
   154→position across the three interventions that are relaxation inducing.
   155→Since we investigated a typical convenience sample of university students with no
   156→extensive meditation experience and no prior exposure to the Ganzfeld we did not
   157→expect to induce strong ASC but minor, still detectable, differences to the control
   158→resting condition. We explicitly wanted to exclude participants with extensive
   159→meditation experience in order to compare meditation with a Ganzfeld setup, the
   160→latter being a novel experience for almost everyone. This decision, however,
   161→precluded us to use meditation as a typical REST method. Since participants were
   162→not versed in meditation techniques, we had to play a pre-recorded and calm
   163→meditation instruction. Thus, what the three induction methods have in common is a
   164→potential relaxation response.
   165→Since a convenience sample of participants will be characterized by a considerable
   166→amount of individual differences, we employed the Tellegen Absorption Scale (TAS)
   167→in order to assess how the propensity of absorption would affect the dependent
   168→variables, expecting that higher scores on the TAS would lead to larger effects on
   169→these measures.
   170→2. Methods
   171→2.1. Participants
   172→Forty-nine participants took part in the study, one of whom aborted the Ganzfeld
   173→session prematurely due not feeling well (22 males, 26 females). Their ages ranged
   174→from 18 to 40 years, with a sample average of 28.85 years (SD: 4.02). The
   175→educational attainment of the participants varied as follows: middle school diploma
   176→(n=2), high school diploma (n=30), and university degree (n=16). They were recruited
   177→by advertisement on an online platform of the University of Freiburg, by recruitment
   178→flyers, and by word of mouth. All participants underwent a brief telephone screening
   179→for eligibility. Potential participants were excluded if they reported any psychiatric or

   184→neurological disorders, or the regular use of psycho-pharmaceutical drugs. Moreover,
   185→individuals were excluded if they declared meditating regularly or if they had taken
   186→part in official meditation courses or retreats. Participants received a financial
   187→compensation of 25 euros at the end of the third session. Each participant gave
   188→written, informed consent. The study was approved by the local ethics committee of
   189→the Institute for Frontier Areas of Psychology and Mental Health (IGPP-2023-03).
   190→2.2. Study design
   191→The study used a within-subjects repeated-measures design. Each participant
   192→underwent three 25-minute sessions, one employing a green Ganzfeld visual
   193→stimulation with brown noise, one while listening to a guided meditation audio, and
   194→one control resting condition. To avoid confounding sequence patterns, a
   195→counterbalanced order of the three conditions was implemented to allow for every
   196→possible combination of six sequences across the 48 subjects, using a Latin square
   197→design, the three sequences plus their reverses. Therefore, six condition sequences
   198→were repeated eight times. The sessions had to be separated by at least one day,
   199→and maximally 14 days. Further, in order for the measurements to take place during
   200→participants’ same circadian phase, the sessions were scheduled for approximately
   201→the same time of the day (± 3 hr). Before the first session, participants filled out the
   202→Tellegen Absorption Scale. Furthermore, after each session, the Self-Assessment-
   203→Manikin scales, the Subjective Time, Self, and Space Scales, as well as the
   204→Perceived Body Boundaries Scale were used to assess emotional reaction,
   205→subjective time perception, and perceived body boundaries.
   206→2.3. Materials
   207→2.3.1. Trait measure: Absorption
   208→The German version of the Tellegen Absorption Scale (TAS) was used to measure
   209→absorption (Ritz & Dahme, 1995). Absorption is defined as the tendency to become
   210→mentally absorbed in sensory or imaginative everyday experiences. Tellegen and
   211→Atkinson (1974; p. 268) described absorption as ‘‘a disposition for having episodes of
   212→‘total’ attention that fully engage one’s representational (i.e., perceptual, enactive,
   213→imaginative, and ideational) resources’’. The TAS comprises 34 items with answer
   214→categories on a 5-point Likert scale of 0-4 (not applicable/slightly/partly/
   215→mostly/absolutely applicable). Composite scores range from 0 to 136 and higher
   216→scores indicate higher levels of absorption. The internal consistency value is a
   217→Cronbach's alpha of 0.89 (Ritz & Dahme, 1995).
   218→2.3.2. State measures
   219→2.3.2.1. Emotional reaction
   220→The Self-Assessment-Manikin Scales (SAM¸ Bradley & Lang, 1994) were used to
   221→measure participants’ emotional reaction. The SAM scales are nonverbal, pictorial
   222→representations of valence (pleasure) and arousal. Above the pictorial scales, the
   223→questions “How positive/negative do you feel right now?”, and “How aroused/calm do
   224→you feel right now?” appear. 5-point versions of the scales were used in this study.
   225→Valence is coded between -2 (maximum negative affect) and +2 (maximum positive
   226→affect) and arousal between 0 (minimum arousal) and 4 (maximum arousal).

   231→2.3.2.2. Time perception
   232→Subjective time perception was assessed by two 100-mm visual analogue scales
   233→from the Inventory on Subjective Time, Self, Space (STSS) (Jokic et al., 2018). The
   234→following two questions were used: “How often did you think about time?” (anchor
   235→points: not at all–extremely intensively), “How fast did time pass for you?” (anchor
   236→point: extremely slowly–extremely fast).
   237→2.3.2.3. Perceived body boundaries
   238→Perceived body boundaries were assessed by means of the Perceived Body
   239→Boundaries Scale (PBBS) (Dambrun, 2016). This instrument measures the extent to
   240→which one’s bodily self feels separated from the rest of the world. The PBBS depicts
   241→seven drawings of human bodies with different body boundaries, drawn as
   242→permeable envelopes. The following question appears above the drawings: “How
   243→intensively did you perceive your body boundaries?”. Underneath this question, an
   244→explanation of body boundaries is given as follows: “Below you can see different
   245→bodies with decreasing levels of visibility. A body that is clearly visible (shown on the
   246→right) is one in which one feels enclosed, that is distinctly different from the bodies of
   247→others, and that sets a clear boundary between yourself and the rest of the world. In
   248→contrast, a body with low visibility (shown on the left) is strongly connected to its
   249→environment, is a kind of permeable shell without distinctive boundaries.” Participants
   250→are asked to choose the drawing that most accurately describes the body boundaries
   251→they perceived during the session. The salience of the drawings’ body boundaries
   252→increases from the left pole (nearly unnoticeable boundaries) to the right pole (highly
   253→salient). Scores can range from 1 to 7, 1 indicating a weak body boundary, and 7, a
   254→strong body boundary.
   255→2.4. Procedures
   256→Upon their first arrival at the institute, participants were led to the experimentation
   257→room. Once seated, they were asked to read the participation information and sign
   258→the consent form. Subsequently, they filled out the TAS-questionnaire, as well as
   259→questions about demographic information. Then, participants were instructed
   260→according to the subsequent experimental condition (see below). They were informed
   261→that they could withdraw from the experiment at any time. In all three conditions,
   262→participants lay on a 190 x 100 x 1.3 cm gymnastics mat.
   263→2.5. Experimental Conditions
   264→2.5.1. Control REST condition
   265→In the control condition, an active control condition, as it has REST properties,
   266→participants were asked to lie down on the mat and relax for 25 minutes, without
   267→falling asleep. The room lights were turned off and a small lamp was turned on at the
   268→other end of the room, the room thus being not in complete darkness. When
   269→participants were comfortably laid, the experimenter left the room. After 25 minutes,
   270→the experimenter came back and participants filled out the questionnaires.
   271→2.5.2. Ganzfeld Setup

   277→1.4 (Kasina DeepVision™) were used in the Ganzfeld condition. A homogeneous
   278→field of vision is generated by arranging coloured LED lights beneath a transparent

   291→2.5.3. Guided Meditation Audio
   292→Using the above described Mp3 player with headphones, a guided 25-minute body-
   293→scan meditation was played to the participants. The body scan is an exercise that
   294→mindfully directs attention to different body parts. With the guided meditation,
   295→participants were asked to follow instructions leading them to sense every part of
   296→their body – from the feet to the head. The guided meditation was spoken and
   297→recorded in German by Geha Haas, Freiburg, an experienced psychotherapist and
   298→mindfulness teacher.
   299→2.6. Statistics
   300→The primary aim of this study was to compare the experiences associated with the
   301→three REST induction methods, body-scan meditation, the Ganzfeld condition, and
   302→control REST. The parametric distribution was assessed using the Kolmogorov–
   303→Smirnov test, which indicated that all state measures as well as the trait measure
   304→(absorption) were non-parametrically distributed. Therefore, the Friedman test for
   305→repeated measures was employed to analyse the differences among the three REST
   306→conditions.
   307→The impact of the TAS score on various variables was evaluated using a regression
   308→analysis with the bootstrapping method, suitable for non-normally distributed data.
   309→The Spearman correlation coefficient was calculated to examine the relationship
   310→among state measures. False Discovery Rate (FDR) calculations were applied to
   311→adjust the significance level for multiple comparisons.

   429→3.3. Regression between Trait Measure Absorption and State Measures within
   430→conditions
   431→The relationship between participants' trait absorption levels, as measured by the
   432→TAS, and the state measures was analysed using regression analysis. The TAS
   433→score was the independent variable, while the dependent variables were the five
   434→state measures in the three conditions (n = 15 regressions).
   435→In the control-REST condition, there is a significant association between higher TAS
   436→scores and a decrease in the salience of body boundaries (non-standardized
   437→regression coefficient: -.029, p = .002; significant after FDR-adjustment).
   438→In the Ganzfeld condition, a higher TAS score is significantly associated with a
   439→decrease in the salience of body boundaries (non-standardized regression
   440→coefficient: -.037, p < .001; significant after FDR-adjustment) and with fewer thoughts
   441→about time (non-standardized regression coefficient: -.365; p = .025; not significant
   442→after FDR-adjustment).
   443→In the body scan meditation, a higher TAS score associates with an increase in
   444→positive valence (non-standardized regression coefficient: .010; p = .016; not
   445→significant after FDR adjustment) and with fewer thoughts about time (non-
   446→standardized regression coefficient: -.202; p = .047; not significant after FDR-
   447→adjustment).
   448→4. Discussion
   449→In this study we disclosed three patterns of results, (1) related to differences in
   450→experienced states between the three REST conditions, (2) correlations of different
   451→experiences within the REST conditions, and (3) regression analyses between trait
   452→absorption and experiences within the REST conditions.
   453→(1) Arousal varied significantly between conditions. Post-hoc comparisons revealed
   454→that the meditation condition resulted in significantly lower arousal levels compared to
   455→both the control REST condition and the Ganzfeld condition. This relaxation response
   456→is one of the core outcomes of contemplative techniques (Pfeifer et al., 2016; Linares
   457→Gutiérrez et al., 2022). Thoughts about time also differed significantly across
   458→conditions. Participants reported significantly fewer time-related thoughts during
   459→10
   460→
   461→Wittmann et al. (2026). Trait absorption as predictor of altered states of consciousness across three relaxation induction
   462→methods. PsyArXiv
   463→meditation compared to control REST and Ganzfeld. The aim and technique of
   464→meditation is the reduction of mind wandering during a given contemplative session
   465→but also in daily life (Linares Gutiérrez et al., 2019). There is a methodological reason
   466→as to why thoughts about time were decreased during the meditation session. The
   467→recorded session guided individuals through the meditation, leaving less time for
   468→ruminations about time passage, similar to how mantra meditation, when one repeats
   469→a holy word over and over again, reduces rumination (Bringmann et al., 2021). The
   470→downregulation of self-ruminations is one key to alleviate symptoms of psychiatric
   471→disorders (Wittmann, 2022, 2025). The perception of time passage showed a trend
   472→toward significance: Time was perceived as passing fastest during meditation. No
   473→significant differences were observed in perceived body boundaries across
   474→conditions.
   475→(2) Correlation analyses revealed consistent patterns across conditions. In the control
   476→REST condition, higher positive valence was associated with lower arousal, fewer
   477→time-related thoughts, and a faster perceived passage of time. Additionally, higher
   478→arousal was linked to more time-related thoughts and a slower perception of time.
   479→These correlations align with prior empirical findings and theoretical frameworks,
   480→often summarized by the adage “time flies when we are having fun.” In other words,
   481→time tends to pass more quickly when individuals feel relaxed and in a positive mood
   482→(Rutrecht et al., 2021), whereas it appears to slow down when they experience
   483→heightened negative arousal or low mood (Senyk et al., 2026). Affective states in
   484→relation to underlying bodily states serve as signals for variations in felt time (Teghil &
   485→Wittmann, 2025; Dombrowski & Trepte, 2026).
   486→A different correlational pattern emerged in the Ganzfeld condition, where positive
   487→valence was associated with reduced salience of body boundaries. Notably, body
   488→boundary perception showed stronger associations with other variables in the
   489→Ganzfeld condition as well: increased arousal and more time-related thoughts were
   490→linked to a stronger sense of body boundaries, whereas more positive and relaxed
   491→states were associated with diminished body boundary perception. In the body scan
   492→meditation condition, only one significant correlation was observed: higher arousal
   493→was associated with stronger perceived body boundaries. These patterns relating to
   494→a reduction of body boundaries with more relaxation and positive affect is in
   495→accordance with the positive moods and states of relaxation during Floatation-REST,
   496→which leads to reduced body boundaries (Hruby et al., 2024; Pantazis & Wittmann.
   497→2025).
   498→(3) Regression analyses examining the relationship between trait absorption and
   499→state measures revealed that higher TAS scores were significantly associated with
   500→reduced salience of body boundaries in both the control REST and Ganzfeld
   501→conditions, significant after FDR correction. Additional associations—such as fewer
   502→time-related thoughts in the Ganzfeld condition and increased positive valence and
   503→fewer time-related thoughts in the meditation condition—did not remain significant
   504→after FDR correction. The effect of trait absorption on the openness to experience
   505→ASC (a reduction in the salience of body boundaries is a sign of ASC; Fort et al.,
   506→2025) has been shown in relation to meditation (Hölzel & Ott, 2006) and experience
   507→in Floatation-REST (Hruby et al., 2024). Here we show for the first time that the
   508→11

   511→methods. PsyArXiv
   512→propensity for getting absorbed also changes states of consciousness in the
   513→Ganzfeld and a simple relaxation condition.
   514→Naturally, our study has several limitations. For one, the goal of having three REST
   515→conditions was not completely achieved. Since we relied on a convenience sample,
   516→in the meditation session we had to present individuals with a guided body scan
   517→meditation instruction. Thus, the presented speech countered the idea of restricted
   518→environmental stimulation. The multimodal Ganzfeld, a perceptual deprivation
   519→technique, may have been a too unusual induction method for a first experience.
   520→Participants may have had to cope considerably with the unusual situation. In a
   521→future study, one could have participants experience a familiarization session, a
   522→typical strategy used in Floatation-REST (Hruby et al., 2024).
   523→Summary
   524→This study revealed three key findings across REST conditions. First, meditation led
   525→to lower arousal and fewer time-related thoughts than control REST and Ganzfeld,
   526→consistent with reduced mind wandering. Second, correlations showed that in control
   527→REST, positive mood and low arousal were linked to fewer time-related thoughts and
   528→faster time passage, whereas the Ganzfeld condition highlighted body boundary
   529→changes, with relaxation associated with diminished bodily self-perception. Third, trait
   530→absorption predicted reduced salience of body boundaries in control REST and
   531→Ganzfeld, indicating that individual differences in absorptive capacity influence
   532→altered states of consciousness across conditions.
