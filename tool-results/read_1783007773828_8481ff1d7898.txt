    13→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 2
    14→Abstract
    15→The chapter “Developing a measure of the need for sex” presents results from a research
    16→program that aimed at developing a picture-story measure of the need for sex (n Sex) through an
    17→experimental-manipulation, measure, indicator, and criterion (EMMIC) validation approach. The
    18→resulting coding system, which also includes a measure of sexual conflict, is comprised of
    19→categories that were reliably sensitive to motive arousal across 6 studies with more than 800
    20→participants. Findings demonstrating the measure’s validity in terms of associations with
    21→affective processes accompanying experimental motive arousal and in terms of predicting a
    22→behavioral outcome are presented. Results documenting n Sex’s convergent and discriminant
    23→validity with other implicit and explicit motive measures and with participant gender are also
    24→reported.
    25→Keywords: thematic apperception; assessment; validation; libido; sexual motivation; behavior;
    26→need for sex; n Sex; sexual conflict

    28→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 3
    29→Developing a measure for the need for sex
    30→Sex is central for the survival of sexually reproducing species like ours. Without sex, no
    31→babies. Without babies, no future generations. So at the collective level of the species, sex is a
    32→necessity. But for the individual, engaging in sexual behavior is often a luxury compared to other
    33→needs that are more immediately relevant for one’s immediate survival, such as the need for food
    34→or the avoidance of impending harm. One ignores these latter motives at the risk of one’s death,
    35→whereas no one has ever died from ignoring the sexual urge. In fact, there are many examples of
    36→individuals who for cultural, religious, or spiritual reasons deliberately forsake the need for sex
    37→and not only survive, but may lead content lives (see discussion in Ågmo, 2007).
    38→But the sexual urge makes sure that it is not forgotten or sidelined. It compensates its
    39→irrelevance for the individual’s survival with the promise of minutes (extending, in rare cases, to
    40→hours) of pleasant activity, culminating in some seconds of orgasmic bliss. Such affective
    41→rewards are addictive and fuel all kinds of learning that in turn charge stimuli and behaviors with
    42→the lure of more orgasmic bliss, making them irresistible. This is how sex becomes dynamite,
    43→socially speaking – it becomes such a powerful interpersonal motivational force that it has the
    44→potential to corrupt reason and fray social relationships. Therefore, cultures have various rules
    45→for sexual engagement and execute more or less tight control of what the sexual urge is allowed
    46→to do and what is prohibited.
    47→The tension between the individual’s sexual urges and societal prohibitions against them
    48→was central for Freud’s psychoanalytic theory (Freud, 1953). One key hypothesis from that
    49→theory is the idea that sexual urges are socially so unacceptable that they cannot show up raw in a
    50→person’s thoughts and behavior, but instead need to be transformed into less threatening images
    51→or sublimated into nonsexual activities. We suspect that this specific Freudian notion of how the
    52→
    53→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 4
    54→tug-of-war between individual sexual urges and societal norms plays out cast a long shadow on
    55→attempts to measure sexual motivation through thematic apperceptive methods, leading
    56→researchers astray for quite some time.
    57→In the following, we first briefly chronicle previous attempts to assess the need for sex (n
    58→Sex) in picture stories and discuss why they were eventually abandoned. We will then describe
    59→our own approach to measuring sexual motivation and provide empirical and conceptual
    60→arguments for the validity and utility of our new measure.
    61→Previous attempts to assess n Sex
    62→After McClelland and colleagues had demonstrated that it is possible to devise valid
    63→thematic-apperceptive measures of motivation through the experimental arousal of motivational
    64→states, researchers quickly went beyond the motives originally studied by these scholars (i.e.,
    65→hunger and achievement; Atkinson & McClelland, 1948; McClelland et al., 1953) and applied
    66→the validation approach to various other needs (see Atkinson, 1958), including sex.
    67→Clark (1952)
    68→Clark (1952) conducted three studies in which he used pictures taken mainly from
    69→Murray’s (1943) Thematic Apperception Test (TAT) and examined the effects of experimental
    70→arousal of sexual motivation on story content. All three studies tested young men. The first study
    71→(N = 78) and the third (N = 62) were similar in that pictures of nude women (arousal group) or
    72→landscape scenes (control group) were presented to manipulate motivational arousal. They
    73→differed in that participants were sober in the first study and slightly drunk in the third. In the
    74→second study (N = 59), participants either interacted, in an informal setting, with a female
    75→(arousal) or a male (control) experimenter before writing TAT stories. Clark coded TAT stories
    76→
    77→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 5
    78→for indications of sexual behavior (sexual intercourse; instrumental activity en route to sex;
    79→affiliative imagery) and guilt over sexual behavior (shame and nervousness; [self-]punishment;
    80→guilt) based on Murray’s (1938) conceptualization of n Sex. In addition, he also scored stories
    81→for the presence of affect (passion and lust; love and tenderness), but did not report findings in
    82→the paper, except for stating in a footnote that they paralleled those for sexual imagery. In studies
    83→1 and 2, Clark consistently found fewer instances of sexual imagery (Study 1: φ = -.36; Study 2:
    84→φ = -.35; calculated from reported χ², with sign for effect size direction added) and sex-related
    85→guilt (Study 1: φ = -.26; Study 2: φ = -.37) in the arousal than in the control group. Only the
    86→inebriated participants of Study 3 delivered the hypothesized arousal effect for sexual imagery (φ
    87→= .31) and sexual guilt (φ = .30). Clark interpreted these findings as evidence that under normal
    88→conditions, sexual arousal through external cues leads to internal inhibition of sexual motivation.
    89→Clark and Sensibar (1955)
    90→Clark and Sensibar (1955) reanalyzed the stories collected by Clark (1952) based on the
    91→Freudian notion that under normal circumstances (i.e., without the disinhibiting effects of
    92→alcohol), aroused sexual motivation is kept from becoming expressed directly and instead shifted
    93→towards more symbolic manifestations. In doing so, they also took the nature of the TAT pictures
    94→into account and coded as latent sexual imagery, for instance, if someone described the person in
    95→TAT picture 14 (man silhouetted in a window) as entering a house, because entering a building
    96→was interpreted as a symbol for penile penetration of a vagina. Clark and Sensibar reported weak
    97→evidence in favor of the idea that participants wrote stories with more sexually symbolic imagery
    98→of this kind in the arousal than in the control conditions of studies 1 and 2. A major blemish for
    99→their hypothesis, however, was the observation that participants who wrote the most manifest
   100→sexual imagery were also the ones with the highest sexual-symbolism scores. This casts doubt on
   101→
   102→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 6
   103→the idea that symbolization occurs to the degree that sexual urges are kept from being directly
   104→expressed.
   105→Mussen and Scodel (1955)
   106→Mussen and Scodel (1955) procedurally replicated the sexual arousal procedure of
   107→Clark’s (1952) Study 1 (nude pictures), but varied only whether the experimenter was a “stern
   108→father figure” (p. 90; formal group, n = 33) or a young, “permissive” graduate student (informal
   109→group, n = 38). The informal group had higher manifest and symbolic sex scores than the formal
   110→group, d = 0.51. However, because no suitable control condition was included, it is impossible to
   111→say which group showed an arousal effect, an inhibition effect, or neither.
   112→Beardslee and Fogelson (1958)
   113→Beardslee and Fogelson (1958) took Freudian notions of repressed libido further by
   114→packing sexual symbolism both into the arousal procedure itself and into the story coding. The
   115→authors exposed 71 women and 84 men either to arousing music (i.e., music that was rhythmic
   116→and had “a gradual buildup to a climax”; p. 133) or neutral music (i.e., music with little rhythmic
   117→content). The musical excerpts were from movies and featured only instruments, no voices. The
   118→authors coded all stories, which they obtained with TAT pictures, with (a) Clark’s (1952) coding
   119→system for manifest sexual imagery, (b) a newly devised Symbolic Sex Activity coding system
   120→based on the Freudian concept of sex as rhythmic self-stimulation, with the content categories
   121→motion (e.g., “he walked”), rhythm (e.g., “swinging”), peak (e.g., “he climbed up on the
   122→mountain”), and penetration (e.g., “he dove into the water”; all examples taken from Beardslee &
   123→Fogelson, 1958), and (c) a Sex Symbolism scoring based on the authors’ intuitions, clinical
   124→experience, and familiarity with Freudian theory. The authors did not find much evidence for
   125→
   126→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 7
   127→arousal effects with the Clark manifest-sex coding system, d = 0.14 (based on averaged t values).
   128→However, arousal effects were more substantial for Symbolic Sex Activity, d = 0.59, and Sex
   129→Symbolism, d = 0.79.
   130→Strizver (1961)
   131→Strizver (1961) conducted an experimental arousal study with 120 young, unmarried
   132→men. He varied whether they were exposed to pictures of fully clothed, partially clothed (“pin-
   133→up”), or nude women, representing three increasing levels of sexual stimulation, and whether the
   134→experimental atmosphere was inhibiting or permissive by having a middle-aged woman,
   135→behaving very formally, or a young adult man, behaving permissively, serve in the role of
   136→experimenter. This resulted in a 3 (sexual stimulation) x 2 (inhibition/permissiveness) between-
   137→subjects design, with 20 participants assigned to each of the resulting conditions. Following the
   138→arousal procedure, all participants wrote stories about six newly devised pictures that varied on a
   139→gradient of stimulus relevance for sex (e.g., a picture of a lone man on a park bench at the low
   140→end of stimulation; a picture of a man and woman kissing at the high end), which represented a
   141→within-subjects factor. All picture stories were coded with a system that, like Clark’s (1952),
   142→drew on Murray’s (1938) description of the need for sex. Sexual imagery was rated per story,
   143→with weights in ascending order of the following: (a) any reference to marriage or romance (e.g.,
   144→“They were in love”, “husband and wife”), (b) instrumental behavior preceding sexual
   145→intercourse (e.g., kissing, petting), and (c) actual or implied sexual intercourse (e.g., “They have
   146→sex”; “She was pregnant”). In addition, sexual guilt was rated as internal (e.g., feeling guilty,
   147→anxious, or ashamed over sexual activity), external (e.g., criticism or punishment for sexual
   148→activity), or indirect (e.g., misfortune after sexual activity), again with three weighting levels of
   149→severity for each type. Strizver found sexual stimulation to have an effect on sexual imagery in
   150→
   151→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 8
   152→picture stories, partial η² = .09 (converted from F statistics provided in the dissertation), such that
   153→individuals in the high-stimulation condition had considerably higher scores than participants in
   154→either the low- or medium-stimulation conditions. He also found that participants in the high-
   155→inhibition condition injected less sexual imagery into their stories than participants in the low-
   156→inhibition condition, d = 0.62 (converted from F statistic). The sexual stimulation x inhibition
   157→interaction did not become significant. Importantly, picture relevance had a direct positive effect
   158→on sexual imagery that was not moderated by stimulation or inhibition (an effect size measure for
   159→the main effect could not be derived from the reported results): strongly suggestive pictures like
   160→one showing a man and woman kissing elicited much more sexual imagery than weakly
   161→suggestive pictures like one of a man on a park bench. Finally, Strizver failed to observe a
   162→hypothesized substantial effect of experimental inhibition on sexual guilt scores, d = 0.14.
   163→Some conclusions
   164→Looking back, it seems like a lost opportunity that the best study of the original efforts to
   165→assess n Sex through thematic apperception – Strizver’s (1961) – was never published. The high
   166→quality of this study is evident in the creation of new picture cues that systematically varied in
   167→their incentive value for sexual motivation. Anyone familiar with the original TAT has an
   168→appreciation that its picture cues are unsuitable to elicit sexually themed stories (except if you are
   169→a die-hard Freudian and look for symbolism in the stories). And if the picture cues do not elicit
   170→even a modicum of the kind of imagery a researcher is interested in, all other efforts to obtain
   171→such imagery, particularly through experimental variations of sexual arousal, are bound to fail.
   172→We suggest that this issue is mainly responsible for the failure of the studies by Clark (1952;
   173→including Clark & Sensibar, 1955) and Beardslee and Fogelson (1958) to find a straightforward
   174→effect of experimental manipulations on sexual motivation. The findings of Strizver’s study also
   175→
   176→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 9
   177→suggest that the combination of direct sexual arousal (nude pictures) and sufficiently suggestive
   178→picture cues for the elicitation of stories is best suited to detect effects of experimental
   179→motivational arousal.
   180→An issue that was problematic in all studies, including Strizver’s, is the reliance on a
   181→coding system for manifest sexual motivation based on Murray’s (1943) suggestion that, apart
   182→from clear-cut descriptions of sexual activity, references to a romantic bond between two people
   183→suffices as evidence of sexual motivation. However, as a large body of research shows, this type
   184→of imagery represents the need for affiliation, a motive that has in at least one instance been
   185→linked to less, rather than more, sexual activity (Schultheiss et al., 2003) and that, as we will state
   186→below, does not increase in response to experimentally induced sexual arousal. Current
   187→biopsychologically based models of motivation, like Panksepp’s (e.g., 1998), stress that
   188→neurobiological systems supporting reproductive behavior are distinct from systems that mediate
   189→attachment, thus casting further doubt on the conflation of imagery related to social closeness
   190→and imagery related to sexual activity into one measure. Thus, the validity of n Sex scores
   191→derived in the manner described by Clark (1952) and Strizver (1961) may be weakened because
   192→they are contaminated with affiliative imagery. Finally, although the pioneers of thematic
   193→apperceptive methods for measuring sexual motivation have attempted to capture effects of
   194→situational inhibition on n Sex and aspects of avoidance motivation such as guilt and fear of
   195→consequences, results were weak and inconsistent for story-content measures of inhibition.
   196→Nevertheless, both Mussen and Scodel (1955) as well as Strizver (1961) observed that inhibiting
   197→situational factors elicit a reduction in manifest n Sex scores, if not an increase in guilt scores.
   198→Toward a new n Sex measure
   199→
   200→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 10
   201→Sixty years later, we gave the assessment of n Sex via thematic apperception another try.
   202→In doing so, we aimed to benefit from lessons learned in past research by using straightforward
   203→procedures for arousing sexual motivation, employing picture cues that are clearly suggestive of,
   204→although not prescriptive for, sexual themes, and identifying those aspects of narrative imagery
   205→that are consistently sensitive to arousal of sexual motivation.
   206→We adopted an EMMIC validation approach in our work, with the acronym standing for
   207→experimental manipulation, measure, indicator, and criterion (Schultheiss et al., submitted). This
   208→approach is based on McClelland’s (1958, 1987) concept of validity as sensitivity of a measure
   209→to variations in the thing it measures. Such a view of validity as causal validity has also re-
   210→emerged in modern validity theory (Borsboom et al., 2004; Bornstein, 2011), which puts a
   211→premium on the demonstration of a causal link between variations of an attribute or process and
   212→its measure. This understanding of validity as sensitivity to experimental manipulation was the
   213→basis and cardinal feature of all research on implicit motives, starting with Atkinson and
   214→McClelland’s development of a thematic apperceptive measure of hunger motivation based on
   215→variations in food deprivation in research participants. However, Schultheiss et al (submitted)
   216→also suggested that for such a sensitive measure to be valid as a measure of motivation, it must be
   217→demonstrated that experimentally elicited changes in thematic imagery are mediated by an
   218→independent indicator of motivation. Based on a broad consensus in modern theories of
   219→motivation, Schultheiss et al. argued that changes in affect may represent such a core indicator of
   220→motivation. Finally, Schultheiss et al also suggested that for a measure to be useful, it should
   221→predict relevant outcomes or criteria, and that the variance portion of the measure that is
   222→sensitive to experimental arousal is also the one that is associated with the criterion.
   223→
   224→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 11
   225→Thus, in our development of a valid n Sex measure, we intended to demonstrate not only
   226→that the measure is sensitive to experimental arousal of sexual motivation and therefore has
   227→causal validity, but that this arousal effect is mediated by changes in subjective (self-reported
   228→arousal) and objective, physiological measures of affect (corrugator and zygomatic activation;
   229→pupil dilation) and in turn mediates effects of experimental variations on a relevant outcome
   230→measure, key pressing for extended viewing of erotic pictures. In addition, we took the following
   231→issues into consideration in our research:
   232→Coding system and n Sex score derivation
   233→Inductive approach. As suggested by Boyatzis (1998), we used an inductive, bottom-up
   234→approach to identify a broad array of narrative themes from the picture stories we collected. We
   235→aimed at devising a comprehensive, specific, and differentiated coding system that captured a
   236→broad range of themes in participants’ stories that could be relevant for sexual motivation and
   237→thus represent potential “signal carriers” for causal effects of motivational arousal on thematic
   238→apperception. In doing so, we considered both positive, approach-oriented and negative,
   239→avoidance-oriented or conflictual imagery in participants’ stories, because we were interested in
   240→if and how experimental manipulation of approach versus avoidance aspects of sexual motivation
   241→would differentially impact story themes.
   242→Complementing deductive approach. We complemented this inductive approach with a
   243→deductive, top-down approach in the form of the sequence model of motivated action employed
   244→in the development of many other motive coding systems. The sequence model posits a person
   245→harboring a need, positive and negative expectations, and responding with positive or negative
   246→affect to the process and outcome of goal pursuit. The person engages in instrumental activity
   247→
   248→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 12
   249→aimed towards (or away from) the goal, which in the end is reached (or not). On the way to the
   250→goal, the person may encounter obstacles within her- or himself or in the environment. The
   251→model helped us to classify and systematize the narrative themes we identified through the
   252→inductive, bottom-up approach in the stories. However, in keeping with the inductive approach,
   253→we kept an open mind for identifying and describing themes that did not fit the sequence model
   254→and added those to the coding system, too.
   255→Intercoder reliability as prerequisite for testing effects of experimental manipulation.
   256→Boyatzis (1998) emphasized that high intercoder reliability is a critical prerequisite for a
   257→meaningful test of whether a given coding category is also sensitive to arousal. We therefore first
   258→ensured that coders could identify all types of imagery with sufficient intercoder reliability
   259→before testing individual imagery categories for experimental arousal effects.
   260→Sensitivity criteria for detecting positive and negative arousal effects. We classified
   261→coding categories as sensitive to experimental arousal if they (a) increased at least 1 percentage
   262→point in the sexual-arousal condition and (b) this increase was at least 2 percentage points greater
   263→than in the control condition (Hinzmann et al., 2023). In studies that featured a negative arousal
   264→condition, we added the stipulation that the score should not decrease more than 1 percentage
   265→point in the negative arousal group (Schultheiss et al., 2023). Thus, we based our classification
   266→not on an inferential-statistics criterion, which would vary with sample size and face challenges
   267→due to the infrequency of many types of imagery, but on the absolute criterion of a minimum
   268→increase. The second criterion of a contrast with the control group was aimed to ensure that we
   269→did not capture an unspecific effect (e.g., scores rising in both the arousal and control groups). At
   270→the same time, we did not capitalize on scores decreasing in the control group, because
   271→demonstrating an experimental manipulation effect on a measure primarily by exploiting a score
   272→
   273→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 13
   274→reduction in the control condition would be a nonsensical and misleading criterion for measure
   275→validation.
   276→Emphasis on within-person changes. For the same reason, we focused on within-subject
   277→changes in the sexual-motivation arousal groups as our primary estimate of the sensitivity of the
   278→n Sex measure to experimental arousal of sexual motivation and did not put emphasis on a post-
   279→manipulation difference between arousal and control groups or its effect size. The magnitude of
   280→the latter difference may be due to other factors besides an experimental arousal effect – for
   281→instance, it may reflect preexisting group differences in motivation even before the experimental
   282→manipulation took place. Exploiting such differences by focusing on post-manipulation
   283→differences between experimental and control groups could thus be misleading with regard to the
   284→measure’s validity as conceived as its within-person sensitivity to motivational arousal.
   285→Picture cue selection
   286→Devising sets of high-relevance cues. We compiled two sets of 8 pictures each (X and Y; 16
   287→pictures in total). All pictures were chosen so that a woman and a man were depicted in an
   288→intimate situation whose outcome could be sex, but also something other than sex. All depicted
   289→persons were clothed, albeit some in underwear. For instance, one picture showed a couple
   290→engaging in a pillow fight in bed. Another showed a man with his hand at the zipper of the back
   291→of a woman’s dress. Thus, the selected pictures correspond to what Strizver (1961) would have
   292→classified as high-relevance cues. Still, we made sure that they were merely suggestive of sexual
   293→activity, but not prescriptive for such a story theme. Each set also included at least one “anchor”
   294→picture that had been used in previous research on motives and that was deemed to be suitable
   295→for the assessment of n Sex (e.g., nightclub scene; trapeze artists; see Schultheiss & Pang, 2007).
   296→
   297→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 14
   298→Scores from these anchor pictures derived for other motivational domains, such as power and
   299→affiliation, served as a gauge with regard to how comparable in terms of general motivation the
   300→samples we tested were to populations studied in earlier research.
   301→Decomposing picture and arousal effects. Each picture set was used once with each of
   302→three arousal techniques (see below), and each arousal method was tested in two studies, one for
   303→each picture set. This resulted in a cross-study 3 (arousal method) x 2 (picture set) meta-design
   304→that allowed us to decompose arousal effects that are due to method and arousal effects that are
   305→due to picture suitability. The design also allowed us to test whether imagery changes found with
   306→on picture set would robustly replicate for changes found with a different set, thus helping us to
   307→ensure that whatever tell-tale changes in thematic apperception we identified as a function of
   308→motivational arousal would not be specific to a particular set of picture cues.
   309→Identification of highly sensitive picture cues. Although this remains work yet to be done,
   310→our pool of 16 pictures divided into two sets will allow us to identify those pictures that are most
   311→sensitive for the assessment of n Sex. We define picture sensitivity as a picture’s ability to pick
   312→up motivational arousal effects (i.e., eliciting low-imagery stories under neutral and high-
   313→imagery stories under arousal conditions). We assume that a picture that is more sensitive to
   314→experimentally induced variations in sexual motivation will also be more sensitive in detecting
   315→interindividual differences in dispositional n Sex and hence contribute, in combination with a
   316→valid coding system, to the overall validity of n Sex assessment. A secondary, auxiliary criterion
   317→for picture selection is the overall mean of n Sex scores it elicits, with a higher mean being
   318→preferable. The ultimate goal is the identification and selection of the three to five most suitable
   319→pictures into a picture story exercise (PSE) that measures n Sex with exquisite sensitivity to the
   320→
   321→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 15
   322→motivational state of the story narrator and sufficient differentiation between individuals to be
   323→suitable as a measure of a person’s current motive state and predictor of relevant outcomes.
   324→Experimental design
   325→Mixed-model design. In all studies, we used an experimental design in which effects of
   326→arousal versus control conditions, varied between subjects, are measured as changes in sexual
   327→imagery in picture stories from before (T1) to after the manipulation (T2) within each subject
   328→(see Figure 1). This mixed-model design, first introduced into motive research by Uleman (1972)
   329→and frequently employed in research since then (e.g., Schultheiss et al., 2004; Wiemers et al.,
   330→2015; Wirth & Schultheiss, 2006), guards against capitalizing on initial differences in the
   331→experimental groups (because they are measured and thus controlled) and allows for the
   332→estimation of magnitude and direction of motivational changes separately in experimental and
   333→control groups.
   334→Picture-set balancing. If changes in motivation are measured within-subjects, one picture-
   335→story test needs to be administered before and one after the manipulation. Administering the
   336→same set of pictures twice may incur problems of boredom or unnatural responses (e.g., Lundy,
   337→1985), and administering one set of pictures always before and another set of pictures always
   338→after the manipulation is bound to yield pre-post differences that are simply due to between-set
   339→differences in cue properties. Both issues would make a detection and/or meaningful
   340→interpretation of differential T1-T2 changes in experimental and control groups difficult or even
   341→impossible. We therefore split the set of 8 pictures we used in each study into 2 four-picture
   342→subsets, A and B, and presented one before and one after the manipulation in balanced order, AB
   343→or BA, and orthogonally to the experimental condition. This ensured that whatever differential
   344→
   345→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 16
   346→changes in sexual imagery we observed in the arousal versus control conditions could not be
   347→attributed to systematic picture set effects.
   348→Validity indicators. Our focal dependent variable was the change that arousal of sexual
   349→motivation elicited in various types of imagery in participants’ picture stories. Conversely, we
   350→considered as valid measures of sexual motivation those imagery categories that showed the
   351→requisite changes according to the criterion described above. However, this manner of
   352→conceptualizing validity as sensitivity (McClelland, 1958) or causal validity (Borsboom et al.,
   353→2004; Schultheiss et al., submitted) is vulnerable to overfitting and circularity. Overfitting refers
   354→to the danger of selecting those imagery categories that respond sensitively to the arousal
   355→conditions of a given experiment with its specific arousal method and sample of participants. It
   356→may therefore not generalize to other arousal methods or samples. We countered this validity
   357→threat by aiming to identify those content categories that turned out to be sensitive to
   358→motivational arousal across multiple samples and arousal procedures. This is the main focus of
   359→the present chapter. Circularity refers to the problem that the effect of the experimental
   360→manipulation (changes in sexual imagery) is taken as proof that the manipulation had the desired
   361→effect (arousing sexual motivation). However, such an approach to validity cannot rule out that
   362→imagery changes are due to other effects of the experimental manipulation, such as the arousal of
   363→another than the targeted motive, a change in cognitive set, an effect of language used in the
   364→experimental manipulation priming similar language in picture stories, but without really altering
   365→motivation, and so on.
   366→To evade the circularity problem, we therefore followed the EMMIC approach and assessed
   367→in all studies an independent indicator of sexual motivation: changes in (sexual) affect. We did
   368→this using both self-report measures of general affect (energization, tension, hedonic
   369→
   370→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 17
   371→tone/valence) and specific affect (sexual arousal) as well as, in some studies, physiological
   372→measures of hedonic responses (positive: zygomatic muscle changes; negative: corrugator
   373→muscle changes) and arousal (pupil dilation). We thus also aimed for indicator validity; that is,
   374→for the demonstration that our experimental manipulations impacted affect and that changes in
   375→affect mediated the effect of the manipulation on changes in sexual imagery expressed in picture
   376→stories.
   377→We complemented the requirements for causal and indicator validity with a requirement for
   378→criterion validity, such that changes in the n Sex measure mediate effects of the experimental
   379→manipulation on a relevant criterion. The EMMIC concept of criterion validity differs from the
   380→classical concept of the same name in that a mere correlation between measure and criterion is
   381→not enough. It must be demonstrated through mediation analysis that the variance in the measure
   382→that is sensitive to motivational arousal is the same that also predicts the outcome. In our
   383→research program, we administered a key-press task (KPT) as a criterion measure. This task
   384→assessed how frequently participants pressed a key to increase (or decrease) exposure to erotic
   385→pictures, relative to key pressing for neutral pictures alternating with erotic pictures. Overall
   386→viewing time across all picture presentations was held constant at 4 min. Thus, only the
   387→proportion of erotic picture exposure relative to non-erotic picture exposure could vary, not the
   388→absolute duration.
   389→Systematic variation of experimental manipulation methods. Because a coding system for
   390→the assessment of n Sex should be broadly representative of variations in sexual motivation and
   391→not reflect more or less idiosyncratic responses to only one arousal method, we aimed at
   392→systematic variation in our means of manipulating sexual motivation experimentally. In the first
   393→set of studies, reported by Hinzmann et al. (2023), we used a priming procedure that employed

   501→Causal validity
   502→As shown in Table 3, the final n Sex score was sensitive to positive sexual arousal in all 6
   503→studies and in response to all arousal methods, with the within-subject change d ranging from .27
   504→to .61 and the confidence interval excluding zero in all studies except for the first. Our meta-
   505→analytical aggregation indicated a medium overall effect size of d = .43. In contrast, the n Sex
   506→score did not increase in either the negative-arousal or control groups – confidence intervals of
   507→meta-analytical effects included zero in both instances, as did confidence intervals for every
   508→single study both for negative arousal and the control condition (with the exception of Study 2,
   509→which yielded a negative control-group effect on n Sex). Thus, our n Sex measure is causally
   510→valid for experimental manipulation of positive sexual motivation.

   522→Indicator validity
   523→We have reported strong effects of motivational arousal on subjective and physiological
   524→measures of affect in all original publications on the derivation of the n Sex measure and thus
   525→documented that our arousal methods were consistently successful in changing motivational
   526→states. For our present analyses for the n Sex score, we focused on measures of self-reported
   527→sexual arousal, either in response to specific stimuli used for arousal or as a change in subjective
   528→state from before to after the experimental manipulation. Unlike physiological measures, which
   529→we obtained only in three of the six studies, measures of subjective arousal were included in 5 of
   530→all 6 studies and thus provided us with a comparable yardstick to evaluate the degree to which
   531→changes in affect mediated the effect of experimental manipulations on changes in n Sex – our
   532→definition of indicator validity. We compared subjective arousal with subjective hedonic
   533→responses to stimuli or conditions, because we had obtained those consistently, too, and because
   534→an a-priori argument can be made that sexual motivation may represent a capacity for greater
   535→enjoyment of sexual incentives (paralleling other motives; see Schultheiss & Köllner, 2021).
   536→As shown in Table 5, increases in self-reported arousal in response to positive sexual
   537→arousal conditions mediated the effect of experimental manipulations on n Sex in all studies,
   538→
   539→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 24
   540→with the confidence interval of only one study including zero. Meta-analytically, the mediation
   541→effect was robust. Thus, we have obtained consistent evidence for n Sex to be indicator-valid
   542→specifically for feelings of sexual arousal.
   543→Valence (or hedonic) responses to arousal manipulations, on the other hand, did not
   544→mediate the effect of experimental manipulations on changes in n Sex. This was true both at the
   545→level of every individual study and in meta-analytical aggregation (Table 5). Thus, whether
   546→individuals increased in n Sex in response to experimental manipulations did not depend on the
   547→degree to which they found these manipulations pleasant or unpleasant.
   548→Our search for valid indicators for the SC score was fruitless. We failed to obtain any
   549→evidence for a systematic mediation of negative arousal on SC via arousal, hedonic tone/valence,
   550→or self-reported tension or energization. Neither did we find evidence for a systematic mediation
   551→via physiological measures. The general lack of indicator validity casts serious doubt on whether
   552→SC measures anything motivational at all, despite its sensitivity to experimental manipulation.
   553→Criterion validity

   559→arousal in every single study and at the meta-analytical level. Thus, the n Sex score is criterion

   588→Other validity indicators
   589→For four studies, we had obtained codings for the needs for achievement, affiliation, and
   590→power based on Winter’s (1994) running-text system. In five studies, we had administered the
   591→German version of the sexual excitation/inhibition scales (SES/SIS; Turner et al., 2013), a self-
   592→report measure of sexual motivation (SES) and avoidance (SIS1: fear of performance failure;
   593→SIS2: fear of negative consequences of sex), and the revised version of the sociosexuality
   594→inventory (SOI-R; Penke & Asendorpf, 2008), a measure of the motivation to engage in
   595→uncommitted sexual relationships. In all studies, we assessed participants’ self-assigned gender
   596→(female, male) and sexual orientation (5-point Kinsey scale ranging from exclusively
   597→heterosexual [1] to exclusively homosexual [5]). However, because we targeted heterosexual
   598→individuals through our recruitment and pre-screening, there was too little variation in the latter
   599→variable to allow for meaningful analyses in most studies, and we therefore did not analyze the
   600→association between content coding scores and sexual orientation.
   601→As shown in Table 8, n Sex assessed at baseline (T1) did not differ between women and
   602→men meta-analytically or in any individual study. This is remarkable, because there is a robust
   603→tendency of men to report more, and of women to report less, sexual motivation and activity in
   604→their everyday lives (Frankenbach et al., 2022) -- a tendency that we also observed for our own
   605→samples when we looked at self-reported sexual motivation (SES) and inclination for
   606→promiscuous sex (SOI-R) (e.g., Schultheiss et al., 2023). In interpreting this finding, we suggest
   607→that the n Sex measure reflects a situationally elicited response to the specific picture cues
   608→

   610→presented (see Schultheiss & Schultheiss, 2014) and does not require testees to make or endorse
   611→declarative statements about themselves. It thus differs fundamentally from retrospective self-
   612→report measures of motivation which, as Dawson and Chivers (2014) have argued, are more
   613→prone to represent reporting biases along the lines of gender roles and expectations.
   614→In terms of overlap with other motives assessed via content-coding these same picture
   615→stories from which the n Sex score was derived, we observed a small negative overlap with n
   616→Achievement, a small positive overlap for n Power (both < 1% shared variance), and small-to-
   617→medium overlap with n Affiliation (about 4% shared variance). While the latter association was
   618→evident in 3 out of four individual studies, too, the former two associations only emerged at the
   619→meta-analytic level. Thus, individuals who inject their stories with n Sex imagery tend to think a
   620→bit less than others about achievement and a bit more about issues of having impact on others
   621→and particularly about having close, harmonious relationships with others. At first blush, these
   622→findings are testament to the essentially social nature of sexual motivation: sexual motivation
   623→goes along with concerns for influencing and bonding with other people. And they may suggest
   624→that although sex and social closeness may be functionally and neurobiologically separable
   625→systems, they may also covary to some extent (like other motivational system; see Panksepp,
   626→1998). However, due to unresolved validity issues of the running-text n Affiliation measure (see
   627→Schultheiss, this volume), future research needs to re-examine the association between n Sex and
   628→n Affiliation with more valid measures for the latter. Even if it turns out that the covariation
   629→between n Affiliation and n Sex is real, the two types of motivation are not the same, are likely to
   630→predict different outcomes, and should be kept separate at the measurement level

   668→Conclusions
   669→Overall, the evidence for n Sex as a measure of sexual motivation is strong and
   670→consistent. Our n Sex measure, which we constructed according to sensitivity criteria that were in
   671→each case fulfilled across several studies and across all experimental methods (picture priming,
   672→movie clips, audio stories), consistently increased after positive sexual arousal (i.e., positively
   673→toned portrayals of sexual activity), but not after negative sexual arousal or in control conditions,
   674→with a medium effect size. The causal validity of this measure was flanked and supported by its
   675→indicator and criterion validity. Experimentally elicited changes in n Sex were mediated by
   676→changes in affective responses to the arousal conditions. Changes in n Sex in turn transmitted the
   677→effect of experimental motivational arousal on a behavioral criterion (key-pressing for relatively
   678→more exposure to erotic than non-erotic pictures). Thus, n Sex fulfills all criteria for a valid and
   679→useful measure of sexual motivation as specified by the EMMIC framework of measure
   680→validation.
   681→
   682→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 30
   683→Moreover, we found n Sex as a dispositional baseline measure to be unbiased with regard
   684→to testee gender and to be associated with slightly less achievement imagery, but slightly more
   685→power and more affiliation imagery, which is consistent with the social nature of sexual
   686→motivation and activity. n Sex also converges with self-report measures of sexual excitation and
   687→sociosexuality, but not with measures of sexual inhibition.
   688→In contrast, our efforts to derive a motivational measure that is sensitive to negative
   689→sexual arousal (i.e., sexual activity that is followed by negative consequences) did not come to
   690→full fruition. Although we were able to construct a measure of sexual conflict by focusing on
   691→those content categories that were specifically and consistently sensitive to negative arousal
   692→conditions, the causal validity effect was small and not backed up by either indicator validity
   693→(i.e., there was no evidence for affective mediation of variations in SC) or criterion validity (i.e.,
   694→SC did not mediate effects of experimental arousal on the key-press criterion measure). We
   695→therefore refrain from recommending SC as a measure of avoidant sexual motivation – it is
   696→simply uncertain whether the type of imagery constituting the SC measure captures anything
   697→motivational at all. Given its pattern of overlap with n Affiliation (negative) and n Power
   698→(positive), perhaps SC reflects sexual cynicism or self-distancing with regard to the sexual
   699→aspects of social relationships. Thus, the SC measure may be more attitudinal than motivational,
   700→more about judgment than about “drive”. It remains an open question for future research, though,
   701→if and how SC may impact the expression of sexual motivation, as assessed by n Sex, on relevant
   702→outcome measures.
   703→Recommendations and future directions
   704→
   705→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 31
   706→We believe that the n Sex measure is ready for use in research addressing substantive
   707→conceptual and applied issues. However, we urge potential users to be mindful of the information
   708→we have accumulated here and keep an eye on scorer precision. As Table 2 suggests, some
   709→content categories, although plausible from an a priori point of view, did not turn out to be valid
   710→according to the positive-arousal sensitivity criterion and should therefore be expressly omitted
   711→when coding stories. Others were specifically sensitive to the negative-arousal criterion, and
   712→should therefore also be omitted during coding. Scorer drift is an issue in content coding, and if
   713→the definitional boundaries of a valid category like, say, seduction soften and the coder starts to
   714→subsume all instances of flirting behavior (which we did not find to be comprehensively valid),
   715→too, then the overall measure may lose some of its validity. So being as cognizant about what
   716→kind of imagery differentiates an aroused from a non-aroused state and what imagery does not
   717→provides coders with helpful guidance for valid coding. At the same time, more research on the
   718→foundations of n Sex measurement using additional arousal procedures and other indicator
   719→measurements (e.g., physiological assessment of genital arousal) may still to some extent change
   720→our understanding of which categories are valid and which are not and thus the composition of
   721→the n Sex score. More generally, our present view of the validity of the n Sex measure depends
   722→on the specific arousal methods, picture cues, coding categories, and indicator and criterion
   723→measures we employed in our research. While we exercised great care to avoid the mistakes of
   724→past research and tried to make all procedural details as reasonable and straightforward as
   725→possible in terms of their meaningfulness for the assessment of sexual motivation, all our
   726→decisions can and should be subject to scrutiny and criticism. It is our hope that future
   727→researchers will do exactly that – go to the trouble of doing again what we did, and better the
   728→next time, in a continuing effort to make the thematic apperceptive measurement of sexual
   729→
   730→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 32
   731→motivation as meaningful and valid as possible. The development of good measures is an
   732→iterative process. We may think we are coming full circle, but are really ascending a spiral
   733→staircase toward better measurement.
   734→Apart from the neverending quest for better measurement, some further validation issues
   735→are on our agenda that will contribute to a differentiated understanding of our newly developed
   736→instrument. One issue is its predictive validity for actual sexual behavior. Do individuals high in
   737→n Sex engage in sexual activity more frequently than others? Do they enjoy it more; does it
   738→contribute more to their overall emotional well-being in daily life, similar to what has been found
   739→for other motives (e.g., Schultheiss, 2021)? With regard to the first question, we have obtained
   740→preliminary evidence in a longitudinal study that n Sex does indeed positively predict the
   741→frequency of sexual activity with a partner (Pekarek et al., in preparation). How stable or variable
   742→is the n Sex score? Again, preliminary data from the same study suggests that n Sex has a similar
   743→sum score stability as other motives, and additional analyses will address whether it also shows
   744→ipsative stability (i.e., the correlation of picture-story profiles across repeated assessments; see
   745→Schultheiss et al., 2008).
   746→Another central issue is the suitability of the n Sex measure for other sexual orientations
   747→than heterosexual. We expect the story content of individuals with homosexual or bisexual
   748→orientations to picture cues to be similar to that of individuals with heterosexual orientation,
   749→provided that the picture cues are suitable for their sexual orientation and show, for instance,
   750→same-sex pairs of individuals in suggestive situations. Ideally, the issue of whether the validity of
   751→n Sex is the same for all sexual orientations, provided that the PSE stimuli match one’s
   752→orientation, remains to be empirically tested, and ideally with suitable experimental arousal
   753→studies, too.
   754→

   756→The same may apply to the question whether the n Sex measure, which we validated with
   757→German-language picture stories written by individuals living in Germany, will retain its validity
   758→if applied to other languages and cultural backgrounds (see Hofer, this volume). The issue of
   759→cross-cultural and cross-language equivalence concerns not only the language content that is
   760→coded as relevant imagery and that may differ between languages. It also concerns the suitability
   761→of the picture cues employed. What may be a sexually suggestive picture for individuals from
   762→one culture may be suggestive of non-sexual themes for individuals from another or be
   763→considered too suggestive for individuals from yet another one. Cultural learning may play a
   764→crucial role in which types of situations acquire sexual incentive value and which do not.
   765→Finally, we think it may be worth exploring how valid n Sex remains as a measure of
   766→sexual motivation if applied (a) to archival TAT stories that were not written with picture
   767→material geared towards the assessment of sexual motivation and (b) to texts other than picture
   768→stories. One of the great benefits of thematic apperceptive methods is that the stories collected
   769→with them can be reanalyzed with new research questions in mind as new, valid coding systems
   770→become available. Provided that the picture stimuli originally used are minimally sufficient for
   771→eliciting themes related to sexuality, this may enable researchers to study, for instance,
   772→differences in sexual motivation in individuals with a variety of clinical diagnoses and control-
   773→group individuals based on already collected data. One could also extend the measurement of n
   774→Sex beyond thematic apperception and apply it to other documents, such as diaries, literature,
   775→historical documents, or political speeches, as has been successfully demonstrated by
   776→McClelland (1961) and Winter (this volume; 2018). Thus, we hope that the development of a
   777→valid measure of n Sex may have added a tool for measuring personality at a distance, that is, in

   781→left suitable linguistic traces to allow for an assessment of their sexual motivation.

   784→Open questions
   785→Beyond the issues we identified in the last section of this chapter, we believe that the
   786→following open questions are particularly worthy of attention:
   787→• What is the relationship between the n Sex measure and self-report measures of
   788→sexual motivation? How do they interact in shaping sexual thoughts, feelings,
   789→and behaviors? Our finding of a modest meta-analytical convergence between
   790→our new measure and established questionnaire measures of sexual motivation
   791→does not preclude that some individuals respond with lots of sexual imagery to
   792→the n Sex PSE, but portray themselves as lacking sexual motivation on self-
   793→report measures. Likewise, some people may attribute high sexual motivation to
   794→themselves on questionnaire measures, but show little evidence of sexual
   795→themes on the PSE. How do such mismatches affect sexual behavior and the
   796→degree of satisfaction that people derive from it?
   797→• What contributions can the n Sex measure make to a better understanding of
   798→clinical aspects of sexual behavior? Is there something that suitably adapted n
   799→Sex PSEs can tell us about arousal disorders or various paraphilias that existing
   800→measures of such conditions can not?
   801→• The PSE has been described as an if…then contingency measure that reflects
   802→the types of situations – represented in the picture cues -- an individual has
   803→learned to associate with motivational gratification (Schultheiss & Schultheiss,
   804→2014). How can this aspect of content-coding measurement technology be
   805→harnessed to learn more about typical and atypical objects and situations that
   806→have become associated with sexual arousal for a given individual?
