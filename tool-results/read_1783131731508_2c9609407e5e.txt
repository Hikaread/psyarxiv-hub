    48вҶ’    48вҶ’Abstract
    49вҶ’    49вҶ’The unsolicited reception of genital images is a prevalent phenomenon, yet the
    50вҶ’    50вҶ’influence of online social norms and victim behavior on judgments surrounding these
    51вҶ’    51вҶ’incidents remains underexplored. In two studies with social media users from the United
    52вҶ’    52вҶ’Kingdom, we examined how victim behavior and acceptance of myths about cyber-
    53вҶ’    53вҶ’sexual violence influence victim-blaming and perceptions of incident severity. Study 1
    54вҶ’    54вҶ’(N = 131) found that participants attributed more blame to the victim and perceived the
    55вҶ’    55вҶ’incident as less severe when the victim had previously chatted (vs. not chatted) with the
    56вҶ’    56вҶ’perpetrator. This effect was only present among participants with higher acceptance of
    57вҶ’    57вҶ’cyber-sexual violence myths. In Study 2 (N = 202), a similar pattern emerged, with the
    58вҶ’    58вҶ’victim perceived as more blameworthy and the incident as less severe when she had
    59вҶ’    59вҶ’shown sexual interest (vs. no interest) toward the perpetrator before the incident,
    60вҶ’    60вҶ’particularly among participants with higher acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual
    61вҶ’    61вҶ’violence. Moreover, we found an indirect effect of perceived sexual consent on this
    62вҶ’    62вҶ’relationship. Our findings highlight the influence of online social norms on judgments
    63вҶ’    63вҶ’of cyber-sexual violence and emphasize the importance of education on online sexual
    64вҶ’    64вҶ’interactions.
    65вҶ’    65вҶ’Keywords: Myths about cyber-sexual violence, dick pic, unsolicited genital image,
    66вҶ’    66вҶ’victim blaming, social perception

    71вҶ’    71вҶ’We live in a rape culture, that is, [вҖҰ] it is necessary to profoundly transform a culture
    72вҶ’    72вҶ’that makes sex indistinguishable from sexual violence (Serra, 2024)
    73вҶ’    73вҶ’Similar to offline socialization, online sexual interactions exist on a continuum,
    74вҶ’    74вҶ’ranging from consensual flirting and playful behavior to one-sided, non-consensual,
    75вҶ’    75вҶ’intimidating, and violating actions (WГ©ry & Billieux, 2016). Among the different forms
    76вҶ’    76вҶ’of online sexual interactions, the exchange of sexually explicit materials is one of the
    77вҶ’    77вҶ’most common practices among various age groups (Setty, 2019; WГ©ry & Billieux,
    78вҶ’    78вҶ’2016). However, not all exchanges of sexual images in online settings are consensual;
    79вҶ’    79вҶ’they can be conceptualized as sexting or as a form of cyber-sexual violence depending
    80вҶ’    80вҶ’on prior agreement to the exchange (Henry & Powell, 2015).
    81вҶ’    81вҶ’Unsolicited sexual imagesвҖ”particularly genital onesвҖ”are among the most
    82вҶ’    82вҶ’common forms of cyber-sexual violence, especially affecting women and sexual
    83вҶ’    83вҶ’minorities (Anonymous, 2025; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024). Beyond their frequency,
    84вҶ’    84вҶ’these experiences have significant consequences, including feelings of humiliation,
    85вҶ’    85вҶ’sadness, and embarrassment (Marcotte et al., 2021; Powell & Flynn, 2023). However,
    86вҶ’    86вҶ’these incidents are frequently minimized, and victims may be blamedвҖ”particularly
    87вҶ’    87вҶ’when preexisting beliefs held by perceivers or contextual factors (e.g., prior
    88вҶ’    88вҶ’relationships or sexual interest) shape interpretations of the event (Bohner et al., 2023;
    89вҶ’    89вҶ’Romero-SГЎnchez et al., 2012). Understanding how these perceptions form is crucial, as
    90вҶ’    90вҶ’they can shape social responses and institutional practices. Thus, the present research
    91вҶ’    91вҶ’aims to experimentally explore how individual (i.e., acceptance of myths about cyber-
    92вҶ’    92вҶ’sexual violence) and situational (i.e., victim behavior) factors influence the social
    93вҶ’    93вҶ’perceptions of unwanted genital image incidents.

    96вҶ’    96вҶ’Emerging Forms of Sexual Violence: The Sending of вҖҳDick PicsвҖҷ or Cyberflashing
    97вҶ’    97вҶ’With the advent of new technologies, sexual interactions have changed
    98вҶ’    98вҶ’significantly in recent years. One notable example of this shift is the increasing
    99вҶ’    99вҶ’exchange of sexual images via social networking platforms, particularly the sending of
   100вҶ’   100вҶ’genital images or вҖҳdick picsвҖҷ (Amundsen, 2021; Anonymous, 2025; Waling & Pym,
   101вҶ’   101вҶ’2019). However, whereas requested and desired genital images may smoothly facilitate
   102вҶ’   102вҶ’intimate exchanges and accelerate the echoes of sexual desire in various contexts, the
   103вҶ’   103вҶ’unsolicited and/or unwanted sending of genital images can be a violent and humiliating
   104вҶ’   104вҶ’experience (Paasonen et al., 2019).
   105вҶ’   105вҶ’The unsolicited reception of genital images, also known as cyberflashing, is a
   106вҶ’   106вҶ’form of unsolicited sexting that occurs when a person (often a man) sends sexually
   107вҶ’   107вҶ’suggestive or explicit material (i.e., a picture of their genitals) to someone else (often a
   108вҶ’   108вҶ’woman) without being asked to do so (Marcotte et al., 2021). In the context of male
   109вҶ’   109вҶ’perpetration, this phenomenon is present in the everyday use of hookup apps by men
   110вҶ’   110вҶ’who have sex with menвҖ”between 5% and 48% of non-heterosexual men report having
   111вҶ’   111вҶ’received unsolicited genital images (Oswald et al., 2020; Smith, 2018)вҖ”but it is
   112вҶ’   112вҶ’especially prevalent among women (Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024; Jeacock et al., 2024).
   113вҶ’   113вҶ’For example, two in five women in the United Kingdom have reported receiving
   114вҶ’   114вҶ’unsolicited genital images from men (Smith, 2018), and data from the United States
   115вҶ’   115вҶ’indicate that 53% of women have received such images (Pew Research Center, 2017).
   116вҶ’   116вҶ’Research shows that common self-reported reasons for sending this type of
   117вҶ’   117вҶ’image include seeking sexual reciprocation, initiating in-person encounters, or signaling
   118вҶ’   118вҶ’sexual interest (MartГӯnez-Bacaicoa et al., 2023; Oswald et al., 2019). However, while
   119вҶ’   119вҶ’the unsolicited sending of such images in non-heterosexual contexts has been associated
   120вҶ’   120вҶ’with motivations such as excitement or boredom on the part of the sender (Dietzel,
   121вҶ’   121вҶ’
   122вҶ’   122вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 6
   123вҶ’   123вҶ’2022), women who receive them in heterosexual contexts often experience them as acts
   124вҶ’   124вҶ’of misogyny disconnected from sexual desire (Jeacock et al., 2024). In this context, a
   125вҶ’   125вҶ’self-produced вҖҳdick picвҖҷ functions as a symbol of phallic male power that diminishes
   126вҶ’   126вҶ’womenвҖҷs sense of sexual agency (Paasonen et al., 2019; Waling & Pym, 2019).
   127вҶ’   127вҶ’Nevertheless, such images operate as a digital form of вҖҳcatcallingвҖҷ: in both scenarios,
   128вҶ’   128вҶ’men may present their actions as compliments or expressions of sexual interest, yet they
   129вҶ’   129вҶ’are more accurately used as tactics of control and dominance over women (Amundsen,
   130вҶ’   130вҶ’2021; Mandau, 2020; Paasonen et al., 2019). In fact, several studies have indicated that
   131вҶ’   131вҶ’the unsolicited sending of these images to women is associated with harassment,
   132вҶ’   132вҶ’appearing alongside negative social media comments, revenge pornography, and
   133вҶ’   133вҶ’gender-based hate speech (Ringrose et al., 2019; Vitis & Gilmour, 2017).
   134вҶ’   134вҶ’Moreover, women experience unsolicited genital images as humiliating,
   135вҶ’   135вҶ’unpleasant, and invasive (Mandau, 2020; Waling & Pym, 2019). Research indicates that
   136вҶ’   136вҶ’women were generally bothered by their inability to consent to receiving the image, as
   137вҶ’   137вҶ’well as by a loss of sexual agency (Amundsen, 2021), leading to feelings of anger, fear,
   138вҶ’   138вҶ’sadness, and annoyance (RodrГӯguez-DomГӯnguez et al., 2025; Salerno-Ferraro et al.,
   139вҶ’   139вҶ’2021). However, unsolicited genital images are perceived as вҖңjust something to laugh
   140вҶ’   140вҶ’atвҖқ by some bystanders (Amundsen, 2021; Ringrose et al., 2019). As a result, many
   141вҶ’   141вҶ’women choose not to report these incidentsвҖ”not only due to the stigma and shame
   142вҶ’   142вҶ’associated with receiving such images, but also due to fear of being met with mocking
   143вҶ’   143вҶ’and victim-blaming reactions when they do speak out (Flynn et al., 2022; Ringrose et
   144вҶ’   144вҶ’al., 2019; VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024).
   145вҶ’   145вҶ’In this context, previous qualitative studies suggest that perceptions and
   146вҶ’   146вҶ’responses to unsolicited genital images are shaped by broader cultural frameworksвҖ”
   147вҶ’   147вҶ’such as rape cultureвҖ”that serve to normalize and legitimize such behaviors (Dietzel,
   148вҶ’   148вҶ’
   149вҶ’   149вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 7
   150вҶ’   150вҶ’2022; VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Related studies in offline contexts have shown that
   151вҶ’   151вҶ’these reactions are often underpinned by sexist beliefs and interpretive schemas
   152вҶ’   152вҶ’commonly referred to as myths (Bohner et al., 2023).
   153вҶ’   153вҶ’Myths about Cyber-Sexual Violence Against Women
   154вҶ’   154вҶ’In recent years, a distinct framework of rules governing online behavior has
   155вҶ’   155вҶ’emerged within digital environments, which includes social scripts that shape how,
   156вҶ’   156вҶ’where, and with whom users interact across various contexts (Henry & Powell, 2015).
   157вҶ’   157вҶ’These scripts also extend to online sexual behavior and differ based on gender
   158вҶ’   158вҶ’(Ringrose et al., 2022). For instance, studies on online flirting behavior suggest that, for
   159вҶ’   159вҶ’some users, it is expected that women upload photos to social media, while men engage
   160вҶ’   160вҶ’by reacting or commenting on those images (Gill, 2023; VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2025).
   161вҶ’   161вҶ’Other qualitative research has shown that women who share semi-nude content (e.g.,
   162вҶ’   162вҶ’bikini photos) are more likely to be perceived as provocative or overtly sexual, whereas
   163вҶ’   163вҶ’men posting similar content are often viewed as jokingвҖ”even when their images are
   164вҶ’   164вҶ’unsolicited (McKinlay & Lavis, 2020; Ringrose et al., 2022). As a result of these
   165вҶ’   165вҶ’gendered scripts about online sexual behaviors, many individuals may interpret
   166вҶ’   166вҶ’unsolicited sexual interactions or instances of cyber-sexual violence as consensual or
   167вҶ’   167вҶ’normal expressions of sexuality.
   168вҶ’   168вҶ’In this sense, traditional literature on offline settings has emphasized that
   169вҶ’   169вҶ’judgments and interpretations of sexual interactions are influenced by the attitudes of
   170вҶ’   170вҶ’perceivers, such as sexist beliefs or offline rape myths (Bohner et al., 2023). For
   171вҶ’   171вҶ’instance, in rape cases, sexist attitudes have been associated with victim blaming and
   172вҶ’   172вҶ’the mitigation of perpetrator responsibility (Bareket & Fiske, 2023). Prior research in
   173вҶ’   173вҶ’offline contexts has also shown that individuals who endorse rape myths are more likely
   174вҶ’   174вҶ’to blame victims of sexual violence (van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014), perceive such
   175вҶ’   175вҶ’
   176вҶ’   176вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 8
   177вҶ’   177вҶ’incidents as less severe (Romero-SГЎnchez et al., 2012), and attribute greater sexual
   178вҶ’   178вҶ’intent to victim behavior (Lofgreen et al., 2017). However, despite its relevance in the
   179вҶ’   179вҶ’study of sexual violence, these beliefs do not account for the social context of online
   180вҶ’   180вҶ’environments.
   181вҶ’   181вҶ’Recently, certain attitudes toward cyber-sexual violence against women have
   182вҶ’   182вҶ’been identified within online spaces, including statements such as: вҖңMuch of the sexual
   183вҶ’   183вҶ’innuendo directed at women online is a result of the websites they engage withвҖқ or
   184вҶ’   184вҶ’вҖңInstead of focusing on women who receive a вҖҳdick picвҖҷ, society should pay more
   185вҶ’   185вҶ’attention to the real victims of sexual assaultвҖқ (VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024). These
   186вҶ’   186вҶ’attitudes have been conceptualized as myths about cyber-sexual violence, encompassing
   187вҶ’   187вҶ’вҖңa set of descriptive or prescriptive beliefs about the conception, causes, consequences,
   188вҶ’   188вҶ’context, victims, and perpetrators, which serve to justify, minimize, or deny cyber-
   189вҶ’   189вҶ’sexual violence against women by menвҖқ (VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2025, p. 4).
   190вҶ’   190вҶ’According to Attribution Theory (Heider, 1958), such beliefs may serve as
   191вҶ’   191вҶ’frameworks for evaluating social interactionsвҖ”particularly when these interactions are
   192вҶ’   192вҶ’ambiguous (e.g., during an online conversation) or when specific factors (e.g., the
   193вҶ’   193вҶ’victimвҖҷs behavior) must be considered. In particular, when such incidents appear as
   194вҶ’   194вҶ’socially accepted sexual scriptsвҖ”such as the sending of sexual imagesвҖ”they are more
   195вҶ’   195вҶ’likely to be misperceived as consensual. This can lead to greater victim-blaming or to
   196вҶ’   196вҶ’interpreting the situation as less serious (McCaughey & Cermele, 2022).
   197вҶ’   197вҶ’The Role of VictimвҖҷs Behavior on the Judgement of (Cyber)Sexual Violence
   198вҶ’   198вҶ’There is a certain consensus about the need to analyze how people perceive and
   199вҶ’   199вҶ’judge the different forms of violence against womenвҖ”particularly those that have
   200вҶ’   200вҶ’emerged in todayвҖҷs main socialization spaces, such as cyber-sexual violence (Bohner et
   201вҶ’   201вҶ’al., 2023; Flood & Pease, 2009; Henry & Powell, 2015). It is important to recognize that
   202вҶ’   202вҶ’
   203вҶ’   203вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 9
   204вҶ’   204вҶ’sexual violence against womenвҖ”whether offline or onlineвҖ”not only involves the
   205вҶ’   205вҶ’actions of the perpetrator but also unfolds within a broader social context that may
   206вҶ’   206вҶ’either condemn or tolerate such behavior under certain circumstances (Flood & Pease,
   207вҶ’   207вҶ’2009). Traditionally, some individuals have justified sexual assault by suggesting that
   208вҶ’   208вҶ’the victim behaved inappropriately, thereby shifting the focus from the aggressor to the
   209вҶ’   209вҶ’victimвҖ”a process known as victim blaming (Bohner et al., 2023; MegГӯas et al., 2018).
   210вҶ’   210вҶ’This tendency has been observed across both offline and online contexts (Romero-
   211вҶ’   211вҶ’SГЎnchez et al., 2012; VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024). However, in the digital sphere,
   212вҶ’   212вҶ’minimization has emerged as another frequent societal reactionвҖ”characterized by the
   213вҶ’   213вҶ’denial, trivialization, or dismissal of cyber-sexual violence reported by women (Flynn et
   214вҶ’   214вҶ’al., 2022; VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Both victim blaming and minimization reflect
   215вҶ’   215вҶ’broader cultural norms that shift responsibility away from the perpetrator and are shaped
   216вҶ’   216вҶ’by social expectations regarding how victims should behave and what kinds of acts are
   217вҶ’   217вҶ’recognized as violence (Temkin & KrahГ©, 2008).
   218вҶ’   218вҶ’In this sense, prior research has highlighted that women who did not fit the
   219вҶ’   219вҶ’вҖҳvictim stereotypeвҖҷ were more likely to receive judgments of blame in rape cases, and
   220вҶ’   220вҶ’the incidents were perceived as less severe (Bohner et al., 2023; Paganini et al., 2023).
   221вҶ’   221вҶ’Specifically, victims of offline sexual violence are often questioned and blamed due to
   222вҶ’   222вҶ’behaviors such as having a prior relationship with the perpetrator (Mainwaring et al.,
   223вҶ’   223вҶ’2023; van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014), having previously consumed alcohol (Romero-
   224вҶ’   224вҶ’SГЎnchez et al., 2012; Temkin & KrahГ©, 2008), wearing provocative clothing (Bohner et
   225вҶ’   225вҶ’al., 2023; van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014), or having previously shown sexual desire
   226вҶ’   226вҶ’toward the perpetrator (Hills et al., 2020; Romero-SГЎnchez et al., 2012). Overall, these
   227вҶ’   227вҶ’judgments may be explained by the perceived sexual consent of the victims (Bouffard &
   228вҶ’   228вҶ’Miller, 2023; Hills et al., 2020), which increases the attribution of responsibility to them
   229вҶ’   229вҶ’
   230вҶ’   230вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 10
   231вҶ’   231вҶ’and diminishes the perceived gravity of the incidentвҖ”on the assumption that they were
   232вҶ’   232вҶ’seeking out or exposing themselves to such situations (Bohner et al., 2023).
   233вҶ’   233вҶ’In contrast to the study of judgements associated with offline sexual violence,
   234вҶ’   234вҶ’research on cyber-sexual violence is more recent but growing. In online interactions,
   235вҶ’   235вҶ’women frequently report experiencing cyber-sexual violence, such as receiving
   236вҶ’   236вҶ’unsolicited genital imagesвҖ”often during the very first conversation in a chat or shortly
   237вҶ’   237вҶ’after exchanging social media profiles (Gill, 2023; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024;
   238вҶ’   238вҶ’VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Research has also shown that a shared intention of
   239вҶ’   239вҶ’hooking up is positively associated with experiencing this form of cyber-sexual violence
   240вҶ’   240вҶ’on dating apps (Centelles et al., 2021; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024), suggesting that
   241вҶ’   241вҶ’such behavior may be mistakenly perceived as a normalized or expected part of the
   242вҶ’   242вҶ’consent process in online settings.
   243вҶ’   243вҶ’As a result, in cases of cyber-sexual violence, victim behavior, such as chatting
   244вҶ’   244вҶ’with or previously showing sexual interest toward the perpetrator, can become factors
   245вҶ’   245вҶ’that lead to victim-blaming and the trivialization of the incident. However, similar to
   246вҶ’   246вҶ’offline sexual violence, such judgments could be more likely to occur among
   247вҶ’   247вҶ’individuals who endorse stereotypical beliefs about cyber-sexual violence, its victims,
   248вҶ’   248вҶ’and its perpetratorsвҖ”that is, among those who support more myths about cyber-sexual
   249вҶ’   249вҶ’violence (e.g., Bohner et al., 2023).

   770вҶ’   770вҶ’General Discussion
   771вҶ’   771вҶ’The unsolicited genital image is a widespread form of cyber-sexual violence
   772вҶ’   772вҶ’against women, and its occurrence is often linked toвҖ”and justified byвҖ”the victimвҖҷs
   773вҶ’   773вҶ’prior behavior (Centelles et al., 2021; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024). This suggests the
   774вҶ’   774вҶ’existence of online social norms that may serve to misinterpret victim behavior as
   775вҶ’   775вҶ’implying sexual consent, leading to the justification and perpetration of unsolicited
   776вҶ’   776вҶ’sexual interactions. Across two experimental studies, we examined how myths about
   777вҶ’   777вҶ’cyber-sexual violence and the victimвҖҷs behavior affect the interpretation of unsolicited
   778вҶ’   778вҶ’genital images as normalized and consensual expressions of sexuality. Specifically, we
   779вҶ’   779вҶ’assessed levels of victim-blaming and perceptions of the incidentвҖҷs severity, and further
   780вҶ’   780вҶ’explored the indirect role of perceived sexual consent in these relationships.
   781вҶ’   781вҶ’Study 1 showed that the victim who had previously chatted with the perpetrator
   782вҶ’   782вҶ’was blamed more, and the incident was perceived as less severe compared to a victim
   783вҶ’   783вҶ’who had not chatted with him. However, this effect was only observed among men and
   784вҶ’   784вҶ’women with higher (vs. lower) acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual violence. In
   785вҶ’   785вҶ’Study 2, we manipulated the victimвҖҷs sexual interest and replicated prior patterns where,
   786вҶ’   786вҶ’again, the victim who had shown prior sexual interest toward the perpetrator was
   787вҶ’   787вҶ’blamed more, and the incident was perceived as less severeвҖ”again, particularly among
   788вҶ’   788вҶ’participants with higher acceptance of such myths. Notably, in this second study, we
   789вҶ’   789вҶ’also examined the indirect effect of perceived sexual consent, assessed through
   790вҶ’   790вҶ’participantsвҖҷ qualitative responses. Results revealed that participants with higher (vs.
   791вҶ’   791вҶ’lower) acceptance of cyber-sexual violence myths perceived greater sexual consent
   792вҶ’   792вҶ’
   793вҶ’   793вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 31
   794вҶ’   794вҶ’when the victim had shown prior sexual interest (vs. no interest), which in turn was
   795вҶ’   795вҶ’associated with increased victim blaming and decreased perceptions of incident severity.
   796вҶ’   796вҶ’Women and men are socialized into different gender roles, such that, in the
   797вҶ’   797вҶ’social imagination, men are expected to take an active role in sexual interactions,
   798вҶ’   798вҶ’whereas women are expected to take a passive role (Newstrom et al., 2021). This leads
   799вҶ’   799вҶ’to the interpretation of the indirect communication associated with womenвҖҷs traditional
   800вҶ’   800вҶ’roles as signals of sexual consent (Hills et al., 2020). Supporting this, previous studies
   801вҶ’   801вҶ’in offline settings have shown that rape victims are more likely to be blamed if they had
   802вҶ’   802вҶ’prior contact with the perpetrator (Mainwaring et al., 2023; van der Bruggen & Grubb,
   803вҶ’   803вҶ’2014), accepted a drink from him (Romero-SГЎnchez et al., 2012; Temkin & KrahГ©,
   804вҶ’   804вҶ’2008), or expressed sexual interest (Hills et al., 2020; Romero-SГЎnchez et al., 2012).
   805вҶ’   805вҶ’Similarly, a recent study by Pacilli et al. (2024) found that female victims of image-
   806вҶ’   806вҶ’based sexual abuse were blamed more when they displayed greater sexual agency and
   807вҶ’   807вҶ’had a transient relationship with the perpetrator. However, to date, no studies have
   808вҶ’   808вҶ’explored the online dynamics in the unsolicited reception of genital images, a behavior
   809вҶ’   809вҶ’that has often been trivialized or ridiculed on social networking platforms (VizcaГӯno-
   810вҶ’   810вҶ’Cuenca et al., 2024). In this sense, our results showed that women victims of cyber-
   811вҶ’   811вҶ’sexual violence can be blamed and the incident (i.e., to receive a dick pic) trivialized by
   812вҶ’   812вҶ’their behavior in social mediaвҖ”such as having chatted with or shown sexual interest
   813вҶ’   813вҶ’toward the perpetrator.
   814вҶ’   814вҶ’Nevertheless, according to our findings, not all individuals judge and perceive
   815вҶ’   815вҶ’the unsolicited genital image incidents in the same way, but only when specific
   816вҶ’   816вҶ’individual factors are presents. In line with prior non-experimental research (VizcaГӯno-
   817вҶ’   817вҶ’Cuenca et al., 2025), stronger acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual violence was
   818вҶ’   818вҶ’associated with greater victim-blaming and the minimization of incidents of cyber-
   819вҶ’   819вҶ’
   820вҶ’   820вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 32
   821вҶ’   821вҶ’sexual violence (i.e., receiving sexual comments on social media). Our study extends
   822вҶ’   822вҶ’these findings to cases involving unsolicited genital images, showing that higher
   823вҶ’   823вҶ’acceptance of these myths was positively related to victim-blaming and negatively
   824вҶ’   824вҶ’associated with the perceived severity of the incident.
   825вҶ’   825вҶ’These patterns are similar to those found in offline settings, in which offline rape
   826вҶ’   826вҶ’myths (Romero-SГЎnchez et al., 2012) or offline myths about intimate partner violence
   827вҶ’   827вҶ’(MegГӯas et al., 2018) were positively associated to victim blaming. However, despite
   828вҶ’   828вҶ’these similarities, it is essential to explore the specific dynamics that emerge in online
   829вҶ’   829вҶ’contexts, which sustain and perpetuate sexist ideology and violence against women in
   830вҶ’   830вҶ’these spaces (Henry & Powell, 2015). Among the potential interactions of women in
   831вҶ’   831вҶ’online settings, their behavior in chats has been one of the most questioned in reports of
   832вҶ’   832вҶ’cyber-sexual violence (e.g., Flynn et al., 2022; Gill, 2020). Moreover, unlike in-person
   833вҶ’   833вҶ’sexual violence, women who reported these incidents often received reactions of
   834вҶ’   834вҶ’trivialization (Henry & Powell, 2015; VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2024).
   835вҶ’   835вҶ’The literature on offline sexual violence has pointed out that, in cases of rape,
   836вҶ’   836вҶ’individuals often use their prior beliefs to shift responsibility from male perpetrators to
   837вҶ’   837вҶ’female victims (MegГӯas et al., 2018; Temkin & KrahГ©, 2008), suggesting that sexist
   838вҶ’   838вҶ’beliefs interact with situational factors in these interactions to justify such incidents
   839вҶ’   839вҶ’(Bohner et al., 2023). In this sense, our results showed the moderating role of myths
   840вҶ’   840вҶ’about cyber-sexual violence on perceptions of victim behavior in a reported incident of
   841вҶ’   841вҶ’an unsolicited sexual image. Specifically, the victim was blamed more and the incident
   842вҶ’   842вҶ’was perceived as less severe; however, our results highlighted that these judgements
   843вҶ’   843вҶ’may be explained by the perceived sexual consent of the victim.
   844вҶ’   844вҶ’Supporting our results, prior research has indicated that, in cases of rape,
   845вҶ’   845вҶ’individuals with higher levels of sexist ideology (e.g., rape myths and sexist attitudes)
   846вҶ’   846вҶ’
   847вҶ’   847вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 33
   848вҶ’   848вҶ’were more likely to attribute greater sexual consent to a victim who had prior contact
   849вҶ’   849вҶ’with the perpetrator (Lofgreen et al., 2017), to underrecognize the incident as a form of
   850вҶ’   850вҶ’violence in ambiguous interactions (Deming et al., 2013) and to use perceived pleasure
   851вҶ’   851вҶ’as a justification for sexual violence against women (Hills et al., 2020; Reling et al.,
   852вҶ’   852вҶ’2018). Ryan (2011) argues that rape myths shape sexual scripts, reframing acts of sexual
   853вҶ’   853вҶ’violence as desired and consensual encounters. In a similar vein, our research shows
   854вҶ’   854вҶ’that myths about cyber-sexual violence operate as a cognitive framework through which
   855вҶ’   855вҶ’womenвҖҷs prior online behaviorвҖ”such as chatting or engaging in a hookupвҖ”are
   856вҶ’   856вҶ’interpreted as signals of sexual consent, thus legitimizing cyber-sexual violence against
   857вҶ’   857вҶ’them. Thus, considering the role of online settings in our daily lives (Kemp, 2025), it is
   858вҶ’   858вҶ’essential to challenge and transform existing myths about cyber-sexual violence against
   859вҶ’   859вҶ’women.
   860вҶ’   860вҶ’Theoretical and Practical Implications
   861вҶ’   861вҶ’This research offers important theoretical implications. Two studies showed how
   862вҶ’   862вҶ’situational factorsвҖ”specifically, a victimвҖҷs prior behaviorвҖ”can serve as justification for
   863вҶ’   863вҶ’cyber-sexual violence when observers hold certain beliefs, such as myths about cyber-
   864вҶ’   864вҶ’sexual violence. Our findings suggest that these myths function as sexual scripts,
   865вҶ’   865вҶ’shaping how online sexual interactions are interpreted. Sexual Script Theory (Simon,
   866вҶ’   866вҶ’2017) posits that individuals interpret sexual behavior through culturally shared
   867вҶ’   867вҶ’narratives and social interactions. Within this framework, myths about cyber-sexual
   868вҶ’   868вҶ’violence help shape how such behaviors are understood and how victims are perceived
   869вҶ’   869вҶ’(Hassebrauck & Aron, 2001), influencing judgments regarding cyber-sexual violence.
   870вҶ’   870вҶ’From a practical perspective, these findings can inform prevention and
   871вҶ’   871вҶ’awareness programs addressing cyber-sexual violence against women (e.g.,
   872вҶ’   872вҶ’https://www.endviolenceagainstwomen.org.uk/campaign/online-abuse/). These findings
   873вҶ’   873вҶ’
   874вҶ’   874вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 34
   875вҶ’   875вҶ’highlight the pervasive influence of (cyber)rape culture, and provides a framework for
   876вҶ’   876вҶ’developing interventions that modify myths about cyber-sexual violence, challenge
   877вҶ’   877вҶ’established sexual scripts of consensual interactions, and help prevent revictimization.
   878вҶ’   878вҶ’Many women refrain from reporting such incidents due to the negative reactions they
   879вҶ’   879вҶ’receiveвҖ”such as victim-blaming and the trivialization of their experiencesвҖ”after being
   880вҶ’   880вҶ’exposed to unsolicited genital images, which often leads to further revictimization.
   881вҶ’   881вҶ’Therefore, targeted programs should be developed to address self-blame and support
   882вҶ’   882вҶ’women in coping with these negative responses.
   883вҶ’   883вҶ’Limitations and Future Research
   884вҶ’   884вҶ’This work is not without limitations. First, the unsolicited reception of genital
   885вҶ’   885вҶ’images was examined solely within a heteronormative and binary framework. However,
   886вҶ’   886вҶ’research indicates that sexual minorities are also targets of various forms of cyber-
   887вҶ’   887вҶ’sexual violence, and that sexist ideologies may play a role in the justification and
   888вҶ’   888вҶ’perpetration of these experiences (Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024). Future studies should
   889вҶ’   889вҶ’therefore explore how the victimвҖҷs gender identity and/or sexual orientation influence
   890вҶ’   890вҶ’perceptions of unsolicited genital image incidents. Additionally, this research focused
   891вҶ’   891вҶ’exclusively on the victimвҖҷs behavior; however, qualitative studies suggest that
   892вҶ’   892вҶ’contextual factorsвҖ”such as whether the incident occurs on hookup apps (e.g., Tinder,
   893вҶ’   893вҶ’Hinge)вҖ”can also be used to justify cyber-sexual violence (Gill, 2023; Ringrose et al.,
   894вҶ’   894вҶ’2022). Second, the sample was drawn exclusively from the United Kingdom, which
   895вҶ’   895вҶ’may limit the generalizability of the findings. Future research should replicate these
   896вҶ’   896вҶ’results in other cultural contexts, although prior work suggests that these beliefs are
   897вҶ’   897вҶ’relatively consistent across cultures (e.g., VizcaГӯno-Cuenca et al., 2025). Third, Study 1
   898вҶ’   898вҶ’showed potential floor and ceiling effects in the measurement of victim-blaming and
   899вҶ’   899вҶ’perceived severity, which may have limited the sensitivity to detect some hypothesized
   900вҶ’   900вҶ’
   901вҶ’   901вҶ’RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 35
   902вҶ’   902вҶ’effects in Study 1. While Study 2 adapted measures to address these issues, the
   903вҶ’   903вҶ’moderated mediation analyses conducted remain exploratory. Therefore, replication in
   904вҶ’   904вҶ’future studies is necessary to confirm these effects and to deepen the understanding of
   905вҶ’   905вҶ’the mechanisms involved, particularly regarding the interpretation of perceived sexual
   906вҶ’   906вҶ’consent. Lastly, while our results indicate an indirect effect of perceived sexual consent,
   907вҶ’   907вҶ’future studies should use quantitative measures to assess this construct to further
   908вҶ’   908вҶ’explore and clarify its role in shaping judgements toward cyber-sexual violence.
   909вҶ’   909вҶ’Conclusions
   910вҶ’   910вҶ’This research, consisting of two experimental studies, examined how myths
   911вҶ’   911вҶ’about cyber-sexual violence and victim behavior impact judgments of unsolicited
   912вҶ’   912вҶ’genital images. Results showed that when the victim had prior interactions (e.g.,
   913вҶ’   913вҶ’chatting or showing sexual interest) with the perpetrator, victim-blaming increased and
   914вҶ’   914вҶ’the incident was perceived as less severe. However, this effect was only present among
   915вҶ’   915вҶ’participants who accepted myths about cyber-sexual violence. Study 2 further revealed
   916вҶ’   916вҶ’that perceived sexual consent played a roleвҖ”participants who saw the victim as
   917вҶ’   917вҶ’showing sexual interest were more likely to interpret her behavior as consent, leading to
   918вҶ’   918вҶ’greater victim-blaming and reduced severity perceptions, especially among those
   919вҶ’   919вҶ’endorsing these myths. These findings highlight the importance of addressing myths
   920вҶ’   920вҶ’about cyber-sexual violence to prevent its normalization.
