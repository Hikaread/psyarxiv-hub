    40→Abstract
    41→Multivariate time series models are commonly used in psychology to investigate
    42→person-specific associations between multiple variables. They are often represented and
    43→interpreted as dynamic network models, where features such as the centrality of nodes can
    44→potentially guide treatment selection and outcome prediction. Researchers typically rely on
    45→point estimates of specific network features while ignoring estimation uncertainty, which can
    46→lead to wrong inferences and over-optimistic claims. We introduce a one-step Bayesian
    47→approach for estimating multilevel vector autoregressive models (BmlVAR), which enables
    48→uncertainty quantification for person-specific network features and the regression of external
    49→outcomes on such features. In a preregistered simulation study, we compare the new model
    50→with several popular methods for network estimation. We also apply all methods to
    51→empirical data to highlight their differences. Our simulation results indicate that all methods
    52→perform mediocrely in estimating different centrality measures in practically relevant settings.
    53→BmlVAR still outperforms the other methods in many simulation conditions, especially
    54→regarding the statistical power to detect associations with an outcome. However, estimating
    55→the model properly can be challenging with limited data. Overall, all methods require a lot
    56→of data or very large effects to produce reasonably accurate results. Thus, although centrality
    57→measures based on dynamic networks have been widely used, our simulation study suggests
    58→they are unlikely to work well for achieving major goals of interest, such as guiding treatment
    59→selection and outcome prediction. We provide a new model that incorporates estimation
    60→uncertainty into the modeling process, thereby protecting against premature conclusions.
    61→Keywords: Time Series; Network Analysis; Uncertainty; Centrality; Bayesian

    65→Introduction
    66→The widespread use of intensive longitudinal data in psychology has allowed the study
    67→of the dynamics and interrelations of psychological processes over time (Hamaker & Wichers,
    68→2017). Commonly, vector autoregressive (VAR) models and variants thereof are used to
    69→investigate the temporal interplay of multiple variables (Jordan et al., 2020; Lütkepohl,
    70→2005). Researchers often choose to represent these models as networks of mutually
    71→influencing variables, aligning them with network theoretical approaches in psychology
    72→(Borsboom et al., 2021; Bringmann et al., 2022). Because these models commonly contain a
    73→large number of variables and parameters, their results are not always easy to interpret. As a
    74→remedy, researchers commonly focus on summary measures that reflect certain features of a
    75→network considered to be important (Bringmann et al., 2022). This includes centrality
    76→measures (e.g., the strength of all connections from a given variable to others in the network)
    77→and network density or connectivity measures, which quantify the overall strength of
    78→associations in a network.1 Hereafter, we will refer to these measures as network features. In
    79→this paper, we investigate the potential of common time series models to estimate these
    80→features reliably at the person level, and highlight possible pitfalls in their application.
    81→Given the growing interest in network approaches in psychology in general (Borsboom
    82→et al., 2021), connectivity and centrality measures have been the subject of much scientific
    83→debate. Much of this discourse has revolved around network features in cross-sectional data.
    84→After initial enthusiasm for their use and some promising empirical evidence (Elliott et al.,
    85→2020), network features have been criticized for their questionable assumptions (Bringmann
    86→et al., 2019; Neal & Neal, 2023), psychometric properties (Hallquist et al., 2021), poor
    87→mapping to causal structures (Dablander & Hinne, 2019), and discouraging empirical results
    88→(Spiller et al., 2020). However, some of these problems may be addressed by so-called
    89→“dynamic networks” using longitudinal data, where network features represent properties of
    90→1 With density, we refer to the sum of all absolute edge weights, sometimes also referred to as “global
    91→strength.”
    92→
    93→CENTRALITY UNCERTAINTY 5
    94→within-person processes with a clear temporal structure.2
    95→Researchers typically use person-specific properties of longitudinal networks in two
    96→main ways. First, centrality measures can be used for treatment selection by identifying
    97→variables (termed nodes in networks) with a high centrality (Hofmann et al., 2024; Levinson
    98→et al., 2021, 2023). Such variables are considered to have a strong influence on the system
    99→and, therefore, serve as potential treatment targets. Second, network features can be used for
   100→outcome prediction or estimating correlations with variables of interest, such as treatment
   101→success (Lutz et al., 2018; Schumacher et al., 2024), psychopathology (Dotterer et al., 2020;
   102→Fisher et al., 2024; Kelley et al., 2023; Palermo et al., 2024), or personality traits
   103→(Bringmann et al., 2016). In the following, we call such a variable a “distal outcome.” The
   104→empirical success of such lines of research is crucial for assessing the overall utility of the
   105→network approach for (clinical) psychology. Therefore, Bringmann et al. (2022) recently
   106→concluded that “the predictive value of network connections and centrality on the course of
   107→mental health conditions needs to be studied systematically.”
   108→However, there is a critical and so far underappreciated problem in using network
   109→features as predictors: Researchers typically estimate individual-level dynamic networks and
   110→use the resulting point estimates of the estimated associations (termed edges of the network)
   111→to calculate person-specific network features. This approach ignores the sampling error of the
   112→estimates, which impedes proper statistical inference in the following analysis steps. In the
   113→econometric literature, this is known as the problem of “generated regressors” (Pagan, 1984)
   114→and can lead to a strong standard error bias in the form of underestimated standard errors
   115→when using the generated regressors in subsequent analyses (W. Chen et al., 2023).
   116→Conceptually, the problem with generated regressors is similar to using predictors with
   117→measurement error (Schuurman & Hamaker, 2019). In recent years, this topic has received
   118→increased attention in the literature on affect dynamics, where model-based estimates of
   119→2 Although there are good theoretical reasons to be skeptical of network connectivity in longitudinal
   120→networks as well (Hoekstra et al., 2024).
   121→

   123→certain autoregressive or cross-lagged effects are often used as predictors of outcomes of
   124→interest (see, for example, Dejonckheere et al., 2019). Statistical theory and multiple
   125→simulation studies (Jobst et al., 2020; Koslowski & Holtmann, 2023; Liu et al., 2021;
   126→Neubauer et al., 2020; Wenzel & Brose, 2023) have shown that the simple approach of using
   127→point estimates as predictors in a regression while disregarding their estimation uncertainty
   128→can severely distort the estimation of regression coefficients.
   129→This topic is relevant to network features for three main reasons. First, multivariate
   130→network (VAR) models are highly parameterized and more complex than simpler
   131→longitudinal models (e.g., univariate autoregressive models), and thus contain a substantial
   132→amount of uncertainty in their estimates given the often limited amount of data (see, e.g.,
   133→Hoekstra et al., 2022; Mansueto et al., 2023; Siepe, Kloft, & Heck, 2024, for evidence of
   134→considerable sampling variability). Second, the properties of dynamic network features and
   135→the performance of different network estimation methods in recovering features of interest
   136→have received limited attention so far. The results of Hoekstra et al. (2022) on centrality
   137→estimation with idiographic networks indicate that a commonly used method for idiographic
   138→network estimation often fails to accurately recover centrality indices. Moreover, Cusack
   139→et al. (2024) demonstrated that centrality estimates can be sensitive to item selection. Johal
   140→and Rhemtulla (2024) related various network-derived metrics to a distal outcome in four
   141→empirical data sets and found that using the time series mean of the most central nodes, but
   142→not the centrality measures themselves, improved predictive accuracy. Third, the
   143→investigation of between-person heterogeneity is a key aim of dynamic network analyses
   144→(Hoekstra et al., 2022). While the degree of heterogeneity likely has a large impact on the
   145→ability to identify the most central nodes and general model performance, it has not yet been
   146→systematically investigated. Thus, both a systematic investigation of different estimation
   147→approaches for dynamic network features, the degree of uncertainty in their estimation, and
   148→potential solutions to the issue of generated regressors are still lacking.
   149→In this article, we address this gap in the literature by evaluating whether dynamic
   150→
   151→CENTRALITY UNCERTAINTY 7
   152→network estimation techniques can reliably identify central nodes and whether they can aid
   153→in outcome prediction, which both are prerequisites for the proposed use of dynamic
   154→networks in treatment selection. Specifically, we first investigate the performance of several
   155→popular estimation methods in recovering person-specific network features. We focus on
   156→centrality measures, but our results extend to other network features such as network density,
   157→which is a simple sum of all node centralities. In the second part, we investigate the use of
   158→these dynamic network features to predict a distal outcome by first extracting network
   159→summary features and then using them as predictors in a linear regression. As an alternative
   160→to this two-step approach of using point estimates in a subsequent regression, we propose a
   161→Bayesian one-step approach where the within-person network models and the between-person
   162→regression are modeled simultaneously. This both addresses the generated regressors problem
   163→and yields a full, joint posterior distribution over all network features, which enables direct
   164→uncertainty quantification. Both parts of the investigation are part of a preregistered
   165→simulation study. Following this simulation, we apply all methods to empirical data to
   166→investigate their differences in an applied context. We additionally provide code to implement
   167→these models and conclude the paper with recommendations for applied researchers.

   186→Aims
   187→We aimed to evaluate different methods of obtaining dynamic network features and
   188→predicting a distal outcome with those features. Specifically, we were interested in how well
   189→different methods can recover (point) estimates for individual edge weights, centrality
   190→measures based on these edge weights, and the subsequent linear regression using these
   191→centrality estimates as predictors.
   192→Data-Generating Mechanisms
   193→We generated data by simulating from a 6-variable graphical vector autoregressive
   194→(GVAR) model for each individual (see below for more details on GVAR models). We chose
   195→these parameter values using realistic values for the auto-regressive effects, cross-lagged
   196→effects (Siepe, Kloft, & Heck, 2024), and innovations with a unit variance (as chosen, e.g., in
   197→Haslbeck et al., 2023) and innovation covariances similar to previous studies (Haslbeck et al.,
   198→2023; Siepe, Kloft, & Heck, 2024). We chose edge weights for both temporal and innovation
   199→associations such that, at the group level, the first node always had the largest fixed effect of
   200→strength centrality among all nodes (outstrength and instrength for the temporal network,
   201→and strength for the contemporaneous network). The associations between innovations are
   202→often referred to as contemporaneous associations, but this term is controversial as various
   203→influences can lead to correlated innovations (Bringmann et al., 2024). The matrices for data
   204→generation are presented in the Appendix.

   344→(2) GIMME
   345→“Group iterative multiple model estimation” (GIMME) is a data-driven iterative
   346→model search algorithm based on unified structural equation modeling as implemented in the
   347→gimme R package (Lane et al., 2024). The approach estimates separate idiographic models
   348→with temporal and contemporaneous associations per person but also searches for
   349→associations between variables shared by a majority of the full sample or subgroups thereof.
   350→A more detailed description of the algorithm is provided in Beltz and Gates (2017). We
   351→estimated the model by beginning the model search with autoregressive paths estimated per
   352→default and (sub-)group cutoffs of .75, which defines the number of individual networks
   353→having a certain path for it to be considered a (sub-)group effect. We used the default
   354→“Walktrap” method to cluster individuals into subgroups. We did not allow multiple
   355→solutions. We set the VAR option to true, meaning that covariances between innovations
   356→instead of directed associations were modeled. We chose this setting as we can convert the
   357→resulting covariances to partial correlations to mimic the results of the other methods.
   358→(3) mlVAR
   359→A frequentist multilevel VAR model initially introduced by Bringmann et al. (2013) is
   360→implemented in the R package mlVAR (Epskamp et al., 2024). The approach uses sequential
   361→univariate multilevel models using all lagged variables as predictors to obtain temporal
   362→
   363→CENTRALITY UNCERTAINTY 14
   364→effects (Epskamp et al., 2018). Contemporaneous networks are obtained in a second step by
   365→modeling the residuals. See Epskamp et al. (2018) for more details. We estimated the model
   366→with correlated random effects.
   367→(4) BmlVAR

   369→(Stan Development Team, 2023), interfaced via the rstan package (Stan Development Team,

   698→Empirical Example
   699→We now turn to an empirical example to illustrate the differences between the
   700→methods used above when applied to real data. We used data from Kuppens et al. (2012)
   701→analyzed in Bringmann et al. (2016) who illustrated a multilevel vector autoregressive
   702→approach on two data sets. It is an early and highly-cited (> 250 citations at the time of
   703→writing) example of the use of network features in psychology.
   704→Methods
   705→We focused on the first, larger data set, which includes 95 undergraduate psychology
   706→students who answered 10 daily questionnaires on their mood and social context across 7
   707→days. As in Bringmann et al. (2016), we used the six emotion variables “relaxed”, “happy”,
   708→“dysphoric”, “anxious”, “sad”, and “angry”, all assessed on a 1-100 Likert scale. Additionally,
   709→neuroticism was assessed as a time-invariant variable with the Ten Item Personality
   710→Inventory in its Dutch version (Hofmans et al., 2008) before or after the ESM phase
   711→(Thompson et al., 2015). Bringmann et al. (2016) estimated separate univariate multilevel
   712→VAR models per node using restricted maximum likelihood estimation, which conceptually
   713→corresponds to the frequentist mlVAR approach used in our study. They obtained different
   714→person-specific network feature estimates, including network density and node outstrength,
   715→and used them to predict the neuroticism score of an individual. They found that network
   716→density and the outstrength centrality of multiple variables were significant predictors of
   717→neuroticism. More information on the sample and procedures of the original study can be
   718→found in Bringmann et al. (2016).
   719→We re-analyzed the data with all four methods presented above to illustrate the
   720→different conclusions one can arrive at when using different methods to a) estimate individual
   721→networks and b) relate network features to a distal outcome. In that sense, our empirical
   722→example is akin to a small multiverse analysis (Siepe & Heck, 2023; Steegen et al., 2016). We
   723→used the same settings and computational environment described in the Methods section of
   724→our simulation study above. For ease of comparison, we focused on the temporal outstrength
   725→
   726→CENTRALITY UNCERTAINTY 27
   727→of a single variable (“angry”), as its outstrength had the highest correlation to the outcome
   728→in the original study. As in the original study, we did not account for unequal time intervals
   729→due to overnight effects here, so overnight lags are considered equivalent to same-day lags in
   730→the analysis. To enable LASSO regularization in graphicalVAR, we standardized all variables
   731→using the person-specific mean and standard deviation. We had to deal with missing values
   732→and unequal predictor variances to enable the use of all four methods. We excluded three
   733→individuals with a variance of < 1 on any of the predictor variables, leaving us with a final
   734→sample size of n = 92. The remaining participants had an average of 9.6% of missing
   735→observations and around 60 non-missing observations on average. We then imputed all
   736→missing values using a person- and variable-specific Kalman filter approach as implemented
   737→in the imputeTS R package (Moritz & Bartz-Beielstein, 2017), leading to a mean of 67
   738→observations per individual. For BmlVAR, we used four chains with 500 warmup iterations
   739→and 2,000 sampling iterations, each resulting in 8,000 samples for inference. We additionally
   740→set adapt_delta to 0.99, as we encountered slight convergence issues with a lower setting.
   741→Additional descriptive statistics can be found in the online supplement.
   742→Results
   743→All models converged. We present the point estimates and the lower and upper
   744→bounds of the 95% confidence or credible interval of the network feature regression in
   745→Table 2. Estimation of the BmlVAR model took around 4.5 hours when parallelized across

   832→Discussion
   833→The ability to accurately estimate features of dynamic, individualized network models
   834→is crucial for the proposed utility of network approaches in psychology. In this study, we
   835→evaluated the performance of different methods of using person-specific centrality measures
   836→for (1) treatment selection and (2) outcome prediction. First, the recovery of the most
   837→central node per person only worked effectively when using multilevel models in conditions
   838→with strong homogeneity (i.e., when the same node was the most central one for all
   839→individuals). However, in more realistic scenarios with heterogeneity, all methods performed
   840→mediocrely in selecting the most central node indices, though multilevel methods, which pool
   841→information across individuals, had a slight advantage. Second, predicting a distal outcome
   842→with centrality measures was mostly underpowered in realistic scenarios. While our newly
   843→proposed Bayesian one-step approach showed a performance advantage in terms of bias and
   844→power, it still required 200 individuals and 120 time points to detect a correlation of r = .40
   845→with a probability above 80%. In our empirical example, two out of four network estimation
   846→methods found an association between network density with the distal outcome, which only
   847→partially replicated the results of the original study by Bringmann et al. (2016). The size of
   848→the estimated association varied considerably between methods. In the following, we
   849→elaborate on these points in more detail and discuss implications for applied and
   850→methodological researchers.

   853→Treatment Selection
   854→The assumption that centrality estimates can be used to guide treatment selection
   855→comes with strong theoretical assumptions (Bringmann, 2021). As we have shown, even
   856→under moderate assumptions of heterogeneity, identifying the most central node to guide
   857→personalized interventions may often be severely hindered by suboptimal estimation accuracy.
   858→Finding the “most central” node as a potential treatment target is thus hard from a
   859→statistical point of view and may not necessarily be helpful from a substantive one. As
   860→expected, longer time series lead to higher accuracy, while a larger number of individuals only
   861→slightly improves the estimation performance of person-specific features in multilevel models.
   862→In our setup, multilevel methods performed best in identifying the node with the
   863→highest temporal outstrength, especially under homogeneity. This is consistent with other
   864→simulation studies (Lafit et al., 2022) showing that multilevel VAR models regularly
   865→outperform person-specific VAR models. The effect can be explained by shrinkage: if the
   866→individual coefficients cannot be reliably estimated, they will be strongly affected by the
   867→coefficients at the group level. If the ordering of coefficients is identical for everybody, this
   868→will result in a performance benefit for multilevel models. However, assuming that one node
   869→has the highest centrality for everybody is a very strong assumption that is often implausible
   870→in practice. More importantly, it contradicts the core assumption of heterogeneity between
   871→individuals, which motivated dynamic network analyses and applications in the first place.
   872→Our heterogeneous condition thus seems more relevant for real-world settings.
   873→Beyond heterogeneity between individuals, it is difficult to reason about realistic,
   874→practically relevant true differences between the most central and other nodes when
   875→specifying a data-generating process for a simulation, and the magnitude of differences is
   876→context-dependent. We considered the difference of approximately 25% in our main
   877→simulation to be quite large and larger than, for example, differences between centralities in
   878→the mlVAR study using time series data with the largest sample size that we could find
   879→
   880→CENTRALITY UNCERTAINTY 33
   881→(Ebrahimi et al., 2021).7 The lack of established empirical phenomena and solid theoretical
   882→foundations about realistic network configurations underlines the importance of uncertainty
   883→quantification in applied research. The results of our empirical example further illustrated
   884→the differences between various approaches to network estimation, resulting in substantially
   885→different centrality estimates for some individuals. We believe that increasing the reuse of
   886→openly available experience sampling data could help to establish more knowledge about
   887→realistic data-generating processes, as well as enabling the empirical testing of differences in
   888→conclusions between different network estimation methods (Siepe et al., 2025).
   889→Outcome Prediction
   890→All methods showed low false-positive rates for predicting a time-invariant outcome.
   891→However, the power to detect a true, nonzero effect was unsatisfactory in the heterogeneous
   892→conditions and only reached the commonly used threshold of 80% in a highly idealized
   893→condition of a medium-to-large true correlation of .40 in a sample of 200 individuals with 120
   894→time points. Performance was considerably worse in the condition with a small-to-medium
   895→true correlation of .20. Even the size of this correlation between centrality and a distal
   896→outcome, which assumes perfect reliability, could be considered unrealistically high in
   897→practical applications.
   898→The lack of power to detect a true association was accompanied by a systematic
   899→underestimation of the regression coefficient by all two-step methods, as shown in Figure 2.
   900→Using contemporaneous centrality measures as predictors also did not work well for all
   901→methods across all conditions. The contemporaneous network consists of the associations
   902→between time series innovations, which may be difficult to estimate in typical time series
   903→data. The relatively low sparsity of our data-generating process for the pairwise associations
   904→may also have led to the poorer performance of contemporaneous centrality. However, the
   905→focus in practical applications typically lies on temporal centrality. Bearing in mind the
   906→7 We found one study with a larger sample size in the review by Blanchard et al. (2023), but this study used
   907→less than 10 observations per individual, which we consider to be insufficient for individualized network

   911→limited generalizability of our simulation study, we conclude that centrality indices may often
   912→not be suited as predictors, even under relatively ideal conditions.
   913→The poor performance of mlVAR was surprising. While it was on par with or even
   914→better than other methods for the purpose of treatment selection, where the focus is on
   915→within-person differences in centrality, it failed for outcome prediction, where between-person
   916→differences are estimated. We have experimented with multiple mechanisms to understand
   917→and address this issue in our supplementary materials, but we have not found a conclusive
   918→explanation. The results of the empirical example may appear to contradict the simulation
   919→results, as mlVAR showed a larger regression coefficient for network density than BmlVAR.
   920→To understand this empirical finding, we inspected the results of a simulation condition with
   921→a similar number of time points (120) and individuals (75) with a realistic effect size
   922→(correlation of .20) in Figure 3. Here, we found that mlVAR – although performing less well
   923→with respect to bias on average – still had a higher regression point estimate than BmlVAR
   924→in 20% of the iterations.8 The large variability of the point estimates may thus explain why
   925→methods that perform less well overall sometimes still show large associations.
   926→As a more general caveat, we used the term “prediction” loosely here to denote that
   927→the time-invariant variable served as a regression outcome, which is a common use of the
   928→term in psychology (Yarkoni & Westfall, 2017). However, we did not perform “actual”
   929→prediction in the sense of predicting out-of-sample data or future observations as it is
   930→commonly understood in machine learning (Yarkoni & Westfall, 2017). Below, we
   931→recommend predictive modelling procedures for the use of network features in psychology.
   932→BmlVAR
   933→Alongside our main goals of investigating treatment selection and outcome prediction
   934→with dynamic network features, we developed a novel Bayesian one-step mlVAR approach
   935→and evaluated its performance. Our implementation performed relatively well in most
   936→conditions for estimating the temporal network. As expected, the one-step approach

   940→improved the estimation of the network feature regression. We further illustrated how
   941→Bayesian estimation allows researchers to quantify the uncertainty for parameters of interest,
   942→such as the (posterior) probability of a specific node having the highest outstrength. This
   943→logic of generating new quantities based on all posterior samples can be extended to a broad
   944→range of applications. For instance, it could be used to generate a posterior distribution for
   945→the difference between the centrality values of two individuals.
   946→There are three major downsides to BmlVAR, which are typical of complex Bayesian
   947→hierarchical models: First, the model takes multiple hours to estimate. Second, ensuring
   948→model convergence is not easy, and we observed divergent transitions both in the simulation
   949→study and in the empirical example. While this is undesirable, convergence issues may point
   950→the user to possible statistical issues due to properties of the model or the data (e.g., a lack
   951→of empirical identifiability of certain parameters). Such issues may remain hidden in other,
   952→less transparent modeling approaches. More generally, the specific performance results we
   953→find here are linked to our simulation setup, where we simulated from a multilevel model
   954→with a certain type and degree of heterogeneity. Results may be different with other
   955→quantitative or qualitative forms of heterogeneity, such as multiple distinct subgroups or
   956→latent classes within a sample (Ernst & Haslbeck, 2025). Third, while all methods performed
   957→suboptimally in estimating the contemporaneous centrality indices, BmlVAR was
   958→considerably worse. The higher MSE, despite the bias being acceptable for estimating
   959→individual edges, suggests that high estimation variance could explain these results. This
   960→implies that choosing stronger regularization priors could improve estimation performance
   961→for the contemporaneous network.
   962→In the empirical example, our prior setup for BmlVAR induced a relatively strong
   963→shrinkage of outstrength toward the fixed effect (i.e., the average outstrength across persons).
   964→We did not explicitly set a prior on the outstrength parameter; rather, the prior on the
   965→outstrength is implicitly induced by independent prior distributions on the temporal
   966→coefficients included in the sum. We thus recommend performing prior predictive checks for

   972→Implications for Practice
   973→Our results build on and extend previous simulation studies indicating
   974→underappreciated difficulties in estimating and using person-specific network models for
   975→practical applications in psychology (Hoekstra et al., 2022, 2024; Mansueto et al., 2023;
   976→Siepe & Heck, 2023; Siepe, Kloft, & Heck, 2024). All of these studies dampened the
   977→enthusiasm with which person-specific network models have been received, but there are
   978→nevertheless many open questions about their utility in applied research. What should
   979→researchers do to maximize the potential usefulness of network summaries? Figure 6 provides
   980→an overview of our suggestions.
   981→When planning a study, it should be clarified if and why a particular network feature
   982→is considered relevant for treatment selection or the prediction of a certain outcome.
   983→Additionally, intensive longitudinal studies in psychology are often designed to obtain a large
   984→number of time points per person at the cost of including fewer participants. This reduces
   985→the power and precision for estimating associations with a distal outcome across individuals.
   986→Researchers should therefore consider the minimal sample size needed to detect plausible and
   987→practically relevant effect sizes.
   988→The choice of network estimation method can have a large impact on the estimated
   989→network features. This was illustrated by both our simulation and the empirical example.
   990→Besides the estimation method, choices of preprocessing can impact network features and
   991→their use in outcome prediction. If all variables are standardized for each person before
   992→estimation, a common practice in time series models, differences in variability between
   993→variables within an individual and between individuals are removed. Such an approach is
   994→useful to interpret and compare within-person effects (Schuurman et al., 2016). However, the
   995→variability of a time series can contain crucial information about an individual and should

  1004→out-of-sample validation should be used to properly assess predictive performance (Yarkoni

  1006→Estimation uncertainty should be taken into account when interpreting the results of

  1018→Limitations and Future Research
  1019→The main limitation of our study is the use of a single data-generating process for the
  1020→simulation. Although this choice was unavoidable due to the considerable computational
  1021→overhead of model estimation, especially in the Bayesian approach, it strongly limits the
  1022→generalizability of our results. In our main simulation study, we did not vary the sparsity of
  1023→the data-generating network and the type of between-person heterogeneity, both of which are
  1024→likely to influence the relative performance of different methods. For example, heterogeneity
  1025→in the form of distinct subgroups and more sparsity would likely favor GVAR and GIMME.
  1026→However, we already changed a key GVAR hyperparameter (γ) to increase sensitivity, and
  1027→standard settings would have likely led to even poorer results. Additional targeted
  1028→simulations reported in the Sensitivity Analyses subsection above indicate that the overall
  1029→pattern of results remains similar under sparser data-generating conditions.
  1030→As stated in the introduction, we did not explicitly investigate network density as a
  1031→predictive marker, but this feature can be calculated simply by adding the centrality
  1032→estimates of all nodes. It is therefore conceptually similar to the centrality indices we
  1033→investigated. Future research could also investigate other network features, such as density or
  1034→measures from control theory, such as controllability (Henry et al., 2022). While these
  1035→alternative measures are conceptually appealing, they are unlikely to solve the general
  1036→statistical difficulty of accurately estimating features of multivariate time series models with
  1037→a limited amount of data.
  1038→As an alternative to Bayesian modeling, other approaches may be used to assess and
  1039→incorporate the uncertainty of dynamic network features. For example, errors-in-variable
  1040→regression approaches are commonly used to cope with measurement error in predictors and
  1041→can be estimated using maximum likelihood (Fuller, 1987). Similarly, the reliability of
  1042→estimated network features could be assessed as the reliability of a sum of parameters by
  1043→assuming that they follow a multivariate normal distribution (see Neubauer et al., 2020, for
  1044→reliability estimation of single parameters). However, due to the regularization
  1045→
  1046→CENTRALITY UNCERTAINTY 39
  1047→(graphicalVAR), heuristic edge selection (mlVAR), and iterative model search process
  1048→(GIMME), the sampling distributions of edges may diverge too strongly from the default
  1049→assumption of a normal distribution to be useful for errors-in-variables approaches. In this
  1050→case, bootstrapping approaches (W. Chen et al., 2023) may be more appropriate. Overall,
  1051→more work on uncertainty quantification in these models is needed.
  1052→Multilevel VAR models can also be estimated in other modeling frameworks, such as
  1053→discrete-time dynamic structural equation modeling (Asparouhov et al., 2018) or
  1054→continuous-time structural equation modeling (Driver & Voelkle, 2018). In a recent paper,
  1055→Jongerling et al. (2024) also implemented a Bayesian multilevel VAR model in Stan and
  1056→evaluated its performance in a simulation study. Their model differs from ours in several
  1057→details, such as the choice of priors, but could nevertheless be extended to include a
  1058→regression on centrality features. Currently, all these implementations lack a principled
  1059→approach to variable selection of edges and model averaging across alternative network
  1060→structures. Implementing these methods, which have been developed for cross-sectional
  1061→networks (Marsman et al., 2025), for dynamic network models would be a useful addition to
  1062→the statistical toolbox in psychology.
  1063→Conclusions
  1064→Our results suggest many statistical hurdles in using network features such as
  1065→centrality to guide treatment selection and outcome prediction. We believe that awareness of
  1066→these issues and potential mitigation strategies, as presented in Figure 6, will move the field
  1067→forward and provide greater clarity on the usefulness of dynamic network models.
