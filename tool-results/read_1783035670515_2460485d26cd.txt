    13→Abstract
    14→Behavioral science tends to seek the single most predictive explanation for behavioral phenomena and
    15→subsequently the most effective intervention for behavior change. Yet many consequential outcomes, from
    16→climate action to democratic attitudes, result from multiple psychological processes, raising the question of
    17→whether combining interventions that leverage different processes would prove more effective. Across a
    18→13-intervention megastudy and a second, megastudy-informed experiment (Ns = 8,488 and 2,547 US adults;
    19→both quota-matched and preregistered) we test whether intervention ensembles—deliberate combinations of
    20→distinct psychological modules delivered together—can more effectively shape target behaviors and attitudes.
    21→We test three principles for constructing such ensembles: 1) outcome optimizing ensembles (combining
    22→top-performingindividualinterventions),2)mechanismcoverageensembles(coveringkeypsychologicaldrivers
    23→of the outcome), and 3) multi-level synergy ensembles (combining individual-, collective-, and system-level
    24→processes). Ensemblesconsistentlyoutperformedtheirconstituentsingle-mechanisminterventions. InStudy1,
    25→relativetoaninformedcontrol,thestrongestensembleincreasedclimatepolicysupportby41.5%anddonation
    26→by 43.0%. And in Study 2, the ensemble was significantly more effective than its components in reducing
    27→partisan animosity and strengthening democratic attitudes. The largest gains were achieved by multi-level

    31→analysesofparticipantreasoningrevealthateffectiveensemblesworkbyresolvingcomplementarypsychological
    32→barriers,notbysimplyprovidingmorearguments.Forcomplexsocietalchallengesshapedbymultiplebarriers,
    33→interventions can achieve behavior change at greater rate and scale by designing coordinated intervention

    37→Significance Statement. Behavioral science typically searches for the single most effective intervention to
    38→change behavior, yet such interventions often produce small effects. This may be because consequential
    39→behaviors—from supporting costly climate policy to reducing partisan animosity—are shaped by many
    40→psychological forces at once, so no single approach reaches everyone. Our studies show that strategically
    41→combining psychological interventions into coordinated “ensembles” produces larger effects than relying on
    42→any one component alone. The most effective ensembles were not the longest ones, but those that combined
    43→processesoperatingatdifferentlevels: personalrelevance,socialinfluence,andconfidenceininstitutions. These
    44→findingsshiftbehavioralinterventiondesignfromidentifyingthesinglemostpowerfulmechanismtocoordinating
    45→complementarymechanismsthataddressthebarrierssustainingsocietalinaction.

    96→been to compare interventions against one another in order to identify the single most effective mechanism18.
    97→Beyond the field’s theoretically-motivated desire for greater mechanistic clarity, the reluctance to combine
    98→interventions may also reflect longstanding concerns within psychological theory about how people process,
    99→respond to, and resist influence attempts. First, combinations of interventions may overtax recipients’ cognitive
   100→and attentional capacity19. Cognitive load theory, for example, holds that working memory is sharply limited
   101→in its ability to process novel information simultaneously20. Exposing recipients to multiple distinct persuasive
   102→mechanisms may therefore saturate these finite resources, causing each component to be processed less deeply.
   103→Interventions that engage multiple psychological processes may also dampen motivation by inadvertently
   104→activating multiple and potentially competing goals, which, according to goal systems theory, can weaken
   105→commitment to each21. Another class of apprehensions about combining interventions stems from classic
   106→concerns in persuasion research. More overt and multifaceted influence attempts can heighten perceptions of
   107→threat to autonomy, thereby causing reactance22. And even in the absence of motivated resistance to influence,
   108→adding weaker arguments may dilute the impact of a stronger message23. From this perspective, behavioral
   109→science’s emphasis on identifying the single best intervention may reflect a theoretically grounded expectation
   110→thatcombininginterventionsismorelikelytoyielddiminishingreturnsthanstrongereffects.

   564→4 Discussion
   565→Behavioral science has long sought to identify which interventions work best at motivating behaviors like
   566→exercising53, supporting governmental policies18, or risky choice preferences54,55, often by pitting mechanisms
   567→and framings against one another in tournament-style comparisons in search of the single most effective lever.
   568→The present findings suggest that this approach may be incomplete for most behaviors shaped by multiple
   569→psychological processes and barriers at once— a category that includes most, if not all, behaviors needed to
   570→tackle contemporary societal challenges. Across two megastudy-informed experiments, the central result was
   571→clear: strategicallydesignedensemblesdidmorethanthesingleinterventionsfromwhichtheywerebuilt. They
   572→produced stronger shifts in consequential outcomes spanning both action and attitude: increasing support for
   573→personally costly climate policy and real donations to climate advocacy in Study 1, and reducing entrenched
   574→outpartisanhostilityinStudy2,withthemostconsistentevidenceforloweringsupportforundemocraticpractices
   575→also emerging in the ensemble condition. However, simply adding more intervention content via the “kitchen
   576→sink”conditiondidnotoutperformstrategicallydesignedsmallerensembles,despitecontainingmorepersuasive
   577→material. The findings point to a more complex conclusion than “more is better”: ensembles can be effective,
   578→but their benefits depend on how components are combined. Specifically, combining interventions that target
   579→19
   580→
   581→psychologicalprocessesatmultiplelevelsofanalysisprovidedthelargestgains.
   582→The ensemble advantage was consistent and robust. Every ensemble tested outperformed the informed
   583→control as well as its constituent single-mechanism interventions on policy support (when this comparison was
   584→possible). Wefoundthispatternacrossthethreedesignprinciples(outcomeoptimization, mechanismcoverage
   585→and multi-level synergy) and for attitudinal (policy support and partisan animosity) and behavioral (donation)
   586→outcomes across domains. This cross-domain pattern empirically counters the skepticism around the potential
   587→effectiveness of ensembles, stemming from psychological theories about bounded cognitive resources20, split
   588→motivation between competing goals21, and reactance to persuasion22. The present findings show that these
   589→concerns do not apply uniformly: strategically coordinated combinations can outperform single interventions,
   590→whereas indiscriminate bundling may still produce diminishing returns. The kitchen-sink ensemble—which
   591→added components indiscriminately rather than strategically—did show diminishing returns relative to the best
   592→smaller ensembles, consistent with concerns about cognitive overload and signal dilution. As such, the key
   593→differentiator of the intervention success of ensembles seems to be whether their combination is designed to
   594→addresscomplementarybarrierswithoutredundancyorincoherence. Thisdistinctionalsomattersforscalability.
   595→Because large-scale interventions are time- and resource-intensive, and real-world audiences may devote far
   596→less attention than paid study participants, identifying efficient combinations is crucial. The three-component
   597→multi-level synergistic ensembles may represent a useful sweet spot: they produced substantial gains over
   598→single-mechanisminterventionswhileremainingmoreefficientandscalablethanthekitchen-sinkapproach.
   599→The effectiveness of multi-level synergistic ensembles also presents an empirical counter to popular and
   600→academic discourse that often contrasts individual-focused (i-frame) and system-focused (s-frame) approaches
   601→to addressing societal challenges and driving behavior change56. Here, we find that it is the integration of
   602→interventions at these two levels that reliably shifts attitudes and behaviors. This pattern aligns with the
   603→core logic of the socio-ecological model, which holds that behavior is shaped by the interaction between
   604→individual characteristics, community dynamics, and broader environmental and systemic forces32. Just as the
   605→socio-ecologicalmodelandemergentcalls33,34inthefieldtheorizethatnosinglelevelofinterventionissufficient
   606→toaccountforbehavioraloutcomes,ourfindingssuggestthatinterventionsanchoredatonlyonelevelfallshort.
   607→Specifically, when citizens experience personal stakes in the problem (reduced psychological distance), view
   608→salientgroupmembersasbeinginvolved(socialnorms/futuregenerations),andreceiveevidenceofgovernment
   609→success in implementing solutions, they are most likely to act on collective challenges such as climate change,
   610→even if this poses a short-term personal cost. Our synergistic ensemble therefore translates this multilevel logic
   611→intoascalablepsychologicalinterventionforshiftingpublicattitudesandbehavior.
   612→Whymightensembles,especiallysynergisticones,outperformsingle-mechanismapproaches?Ourqualitative
   613→findings suggest that their advantage does not stem from simply giving people more reasons to support a
   614→policy. Instead,effectiveensemblesappeartoshiftwhichconsiderationsbecomesalient. Intheclimatedomain,
   615→the multi-level synergistic ensemble increased references to highly effective supportive considerations such as
   616→20
   617→
   618→environmental concern, collective responsibility, health benefits, and intergenerational concern, while reducing
   619→effectiveopposition-generatingreasoning,includingclimateskepticismandconcernsaboutpolicyeffectiveness.
   620→Bycontrast,thekitchen-sinkcondition,despitecontainingallofthiscontentandmore,didnotproducethesame
   621→more effective reasoning profile. This pattern echoes prior work showing that pro-environmental choices can
   622→increase when information is presented through multiple complementary frames—for example, when the fuel
   623→efficiency of cars is expressed in terms of miles per gallon, annual fuel cost, and greenhouse gas ratings—not
   624→because people simply count up more reasons, but because multiple alternative translations of efficiency can
   625→activate different latent goals and values, making it easier for decision-makers to act on concerns they already
   626→hold57.Effectiveensemblesappeartooperatesimilarly:ratherthanofferinganindiscriminatemenuofarguments,
   627→theyappeartocoordinatecomplementaryreasonsinwaysthatstrengthensupportandmotivatebehavior.
   628→In our studies, ensembles emerged effective across two distinct classes of societal challenge: costly climate
   629→action,wherepeoplemustacceptimmediatepersonalcostsfordiffusecollectivebenefits,anddemocraticattitudes,
   630→where interventions must shift entrenched partisan hostility and tolerance for democratic norm violations. That
   631→ensembles outperformed their components across both contexts suggests that their advantage is not tied to one
   632→policy, one outcome, or one intervention repertoire. Rather, when societal problems are sustained by multiple
   633→barriers,coordinatedcombinationsofinterventionscansucceedwhereevenstrongsingle-mechanismapproaches
   634→fallshort.
   635→Whatdoesthesuccessofsynergisticinterventionensemblesimplyforthetraditionofinterventiontournaments
   636→and related comparative approaches in behavioral science? It suggests that such efforts remain essential, but
   637→incomplete on their own. Identifying which single mechanisms, framings, or interventions perform best is a
   638→necessary foundation for theory building. Yet for outcomes constrained by multiple barriers at once, the field
   639→shouldnotstopatrankingandpromotingisolatedprocessesandtheirassociatedinterventions. Instead, insights
   640→from these comparative approaches should be used to design ensembles that combine effective elements in
   641→theoretically informed ways across levels of analysis. This means asking not only whether a given intervention
   642→works, but also where it operates: at the individual, collective, or system level, or across multiple levels
   643→simultaneously. Such mapping would enable a science of intervention design that can identify the magnitude
   644→andreasonsforthesuccessofindividualinterventions,andthecontextsinwhichdifferentcombinationsofthem
   645→mightpresentsynergisticboosts.

   655→Conclusion
   656→The question of which intervention works best to motivate specific socially-important behaviors has driven a
   657→productive era of behavioral science. But for outcomes constrained by multiple barriers at once, we find that
   658→identifying “best” single interventions leaves proverbial money on the table. Putting it into theory terms, it
   659→means that researchers are falling prey to the single-action bias, identified in a range of different contexts,
   660→where individuals take only one action (a single preventive measure) to reduce a perceived threat, even when
   661→multipleactionsareneeded58,59. Weshowthatthestrategiccombinationofinterventionscanoutperformrelying
   662→onanysinglemechanism,eventhoughtheindiscriminatebundlingofpsychologicalinterventionshasdiminishing
