     1‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚Üí189 reciprocated by promoting proximity-seeking, courtship, sex, and caregiving. Obsessive thinking
     2‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üí190 about a loved one also means that stimuli about the loved one are regularly present, in turn activating
     3‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚Üí191 neurobiological responses to the loved one, reinforcing romantic love. Empirical support for the
     4‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üí5
     5‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üí
     6‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚Üí192 proximity-seeking function comes from Valshtein et al. (2020), who found that obsessive thinking
     7‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚Üí193 predicted prospective proximity-seeking behaviour (see Behaviour, below). This is consistent with
     8‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚Üí194 the proposal that obsessive thinking promotes reproductively relevant approach behaviour toward the
     9‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üí195 loved one.
    10‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üí196 Gonzaga et al. (2008) have shown that the experience of love in romantic relationships, but not
    11‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üí197 sexual desire, facilitates the suppression of thoughts about attractive alternatives and reduces memory
    12‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üí198 for their attractiveness-related features. It is plausible that obsessive thinking about the beloved
    13‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üí199 contributes to this effect by occupying cognitive resources that would otherwise be available for
    14‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üí200 attending to rival stimuli. However, participants in that study had been in relationships for an average
    15‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚Üí201 of nearly three years, meaning many were unlikely to still be experiencing romantic love and its
    16‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚Üí202 characteristic obsessive thinking. Whether this suppression effect is stronger during the early,
    17‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚Üí203 obsessive-thinking-intensive phase of romantic love remains untested.
    18‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üí204 4 Development Across the Lifespan
    19‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üí205 Romantic love expresses in juveniles, with it being documented in children as young as 4 years old
    20‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üí206 (Hatfield et al., 1988). Indications are that obsessive thinking about a loved one is present in these
    21‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚Üí207 children experiencing romantic love. The Juvenile Love Scale; (Greenwell, 1983; Hatfield & Young,
    22‚Üí   222‚Üí   222‚Üí208 2009) includes two items assessing obsessive thinking: ‚ÄúDid you ever keep thinking about ___when
    23‚Üí   223‚Üí   223‚Üí209 you wanted to stop and couldn‚Äôt?‚Äù and ‚ÄúI am always thinking about .‚Äù However, there is some
    24‚Üí   224‚Üí   224‚Üí210 uncertainty about this as the studies validating the JLS were never peer reviewed and we are not
    25‚Üí   225‚Üí   225‚Üí211 privy to child participants‚Äô scores on those two specific items. Considerations of love in early
    26‚Üí   226‚Üí   226‚Üí212 adolescence have been published (e.g., Montgomery & Sorell, 1998) but no documentation of
    27‚Üí   227‚Üí   227‚Üí213 obsessive thinking appears to be present that we are aware of. Romantic love expresses with all its
    28‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚Üí214 features following puberty (Bode & Kushnick, 2021a). Understanding the age at which obsessive
    29‚Üí   229‚Üí   229‚Üí215 thinking about a loved one expresses in romantic love is an area for targeted research.
    30‚Üí   230‚Üí   230‚Üí216 Although obsessive thinking about a loved one is most often studied in prototypical romantic love
    31‚Üí   231‚Üí   231‚Üí217 (i.e., in the first two years of a romantic relationship), obsessive thinking about a loved one also
    32‚Üí   232‚Üí   232‚Üí218 persists in long-term romantic love. Among married individuals reporting being 'very intensely in
    33‚Üí   233‚Üí   233‚Üí219 love' after more than ten years, thinking about the partner when apart was a significant correlate of
    34‚Üí   234‚Üí   234‚Üí220 love intensity in both U.S. (O‚ÄôLeary et al., 2011) and Spanish (Cuenca-Montesino et al., 2015)
    35‚Üí   235‚Üí   235‚Üí221 samples. The frequency and content of obsessive thinking about a loved one across the duration of a
    36‚Üí   236‚Üí   236‚Üí222 relationship has not been systematically investigated and represents a priority for future research.
    37‚Üí   237‚Üí   237‚Üí223 5 Biological Mechanisms
    38‚Üí   238‚Üí   238‚Üí224 Almost nothing is known about the biological mechanisms that cause obsessive thinking about a
    39‚Üí   239‚Üí   239‚Üí225 loved one in romantic love. One fMRI study (Fujisaki et al., 2026) suggests that the anterior insula
    40‚Üí   240‚Üí   240‚Üí226 (aINS) may play a role: in males in the early stages of a romantic relationship, the distinctiveness of
    41‚Üí   241‚Üí   241‚Üí227 partner-specific neural representations in the aINS was positively associated with the frequency of
    42‚Üí   242‚Üí   242‚Üí228 intrusive thoughts about a partner measured via experience sampling. This finding awaits replication
    43‚Üí   243‚Üí   243‚Üí229 and extension to female samples.
    44‚Üí   244‚Üí   244‚Üí230 In the late 1990s and early 2000s, Helen Fisher and others (Fisher, 2000; Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al.,
    45‚Üí   245‚Üí   245‚Üí231 2002) confidently asserted that obsessive thinking was mechanistically similar to intrusive thinking
    46‚Üí   246‚Üí   246‚Üí232 found in OCD. They attributed it to lower levels of serotonin during romantic love. Claimed evidence
    47‚Üí   247‚Üí   247‚Üí233 for this included the first biological study of romantic love which measured circulating serotonin
    48‚Üí   248‚Üí   248‚Üí6
    49‚Üí   249‚Üí   249‚Üí
    50‚Üí   250‚Üí   250‚Üí234 transporters in blood platelets (Marazziti et al., 1999). Since that time, however, it has become
    51‚Üí   251‚Üí   251‚Üí235 apparent that this mechanistic explanation is almost certainly wrong.
    52‚Üí   252‚Üí   252‚Üí236 The findings from the original study upon which this theory drew its initial support were
    53‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üí237 misinterpreted. Marazziti et al. (1999) found that the density of the serotonin transporter in blood
    54‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚Üí238 platelets was lower in people who has recently fallen in love and OCD patients than in control
    55‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üí239 participants. This was interpreted to indicate that individuals experiencing romantic love exhibited
    56‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üí240 higher circulating serotonin. Since that time, however, it has become known that lower serotonin
    57‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üí241 transporter levels are actually associated with higher levels of circulating serotonin (J√∏rgensen et al.,
    58‚Üí   258‚Üí   258‚Üí242 2014; Mercado & Kilic, 2010). Although a later study (Langeslag et al., 2012) found lower levels of
    59‚Üí   259‚Üí   259‚Üí243 serotonin in blood plasma and serum in males experiencing romantic love than male controls, it also
    60‚Üí   260‚Üí   260‚Üí244 found higher plasma and serum serotonin levels in females experiencing romantic love than female
    61‚Üí   261‚Üí   261‚Üí245 controls. Interestingly, in females experiencing romantic love, there was a significant positive
    62‚Üí   262‚Üí   262‚Üí246 correlation between serum serotonin levels and scores on obsessive thinking about a loved one items
    63‚Üí   263‚Üí   263‚Üí247 taken form the Passionate Love Scale (PLS; Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986). It should be noted though
    64‚Üí   264‚Üí   264‚Üí248 that the sample size of this study was small. The final death knell for this theory comes from a
    65‚Üí   265‚Üí   265‚Üí249 correlational study (Bode, Kowal, et al., 2025) finding that individuals experiencing romantic love do
    66‚Üí   266‚Üí   266‚Üí250 not differ in obsessive thinking regardless of whether they are taking selective serotonin reuptake
    67‚Üí   267‚Üí   267‚Üí251 inhibitors (SSRIs) or not. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors are a common treatment for OCD
    68‚Üí   268‚Üí   268‚Üí252 (Del Casale et al., 2019). If they alleviate intrusive thinking in OCD, and intrusive thinking in OCD
    69‚Üí   269‚Üí   269‚Üí253 and obsessive thinking in romantic love are mechanistically linked, it follows that SSRIs would
    70‚Üí   270‚Üí   270‚Üí254 reduce obsessive thinking in romantic love. Because they don‚Äôt, this is strong evidence that the
    71‚Üí   271‚Üí   271‚Üí255 mechanisms causing these two phenomena are different.
    72‚Üí   272‚Üí   272‚Üí256 6 Ecology
    73‚Üí   273‚Üí   273‚Üí257 There is some evidence that ecology (in the form of national-level indictors) can influence the
    74‚Üí   274‚Üí   274‚Üí258 expression of obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love. In a cross-sectional study
    75‚Üí   275‚Üí   275‚Üí259 investigating sex differences in romantic love using the Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode, Luoto, et
    76‚Üí   276‚Üí   276‚Üí260 al., 2025), The United Nations Gender Inequality Index was used as a control in a hierarchical linear
    77‚Üí   277‚Üí   277‚Üí261 regression predicting obsessive thinking about a loved one. In the final model, Gender Inequality
    78‚Üí   278‚Üí   278‚Üí262 Index was the second largest predictor of obsessive thinking (after biological sex), indicating that
    79‚Üí   279‚Üí   279‚Üí263 individuals in more gender equal countries experienced less obsessive thinking about a loved one.
    80‚Üí   280‚Üí   280‚Üí264 This is consistent with another study (Sorokowski et al., 2023) demonstrating associations between
    81‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚Üí265 gender equality, modernisation and collectivism and three components of love in romantic
    82‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚Üí266 relationships (i.e., intimacy, passion, and commitment). This indicates that the cultural environment
    83‚Üí   283‚Üí   283‚Üí267 may have a causal influence on how obsessive thinking out a loved one expresses. Future studies
    84‚Üí   284‚Üí   284‚Üí268 should investigate the association between modernisation and collectivism on obsessive thinking
    85‚Üí   285‚Üí   285‚Üí269 about a loved one.
    86‚Üí   286‚Üí   286‚Üí270 7 Cognitions
    87‚Üí   287‚Üí   287‚Üí271 According to Tennov (1979), obsessive thinking about the loved one (what she called the ‚Äúlimerent
    88‚Üí   288‚Üí   288‚Üí272 object;‚Äù i.e., the person one feels limerence for) is a main component of romantic love. She relates it
    89‚Üí   289‚Üí   289‚Üí273 to compulsive daydreaming about, preoccupation with, and uncontrollable fantasizing about the
    90‚Üí   290‚Üí   290‚Üí274 limerent object. Likewise, Fisher (Fisher, 1998) and Fisher et al. (Fisher et al., 2002) list obsessive
    91‚Üí   291‚Üí   291‚Üí275 thinking about a loved one as one of the characteristics of romantic love. Here, we review studies that
    92‚Üí   292‚Üí   292‚Üí276 have assessed this cognitive aspect of romantic love.
    93‚Üí   293‚Üí   293‚Üí7
    94‚Üí   294‚Üí   294‚Üí
    95‚Üí   295‚Üí   295‚Üí277 Several studies have assessed how much people think about their loved one. In a study of participants
    96‚Üí   296‚Üí   296‚Üí278 who had ever experienced a romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), thoughts about the ex-partner
    97‚Üí   297‚Üí   297‚Üí279 occurred several times per week on average and were fairly vivid, with nostalgia, loneliness, certain
    98‚Üí   298‚Üí   298‚Üí280 songs, sadness, celebrations, anxiety, and boredom endorsed as common triggers. Among
    99‚Üí   299‚Üí   299‚Üí281 participants currently in love, self-reported estimates of time spent thinking about the loved one have
   100‚Üí   300‚Üí   300‚Üí282 ranged widely: 65% of waking hours in participants in love for 9 months or less (Langeslag et al.,
   101‚Üí   301‚Üí   301‚Üí283 2012), 50‚Äì61% in participants in a relationship for 9 months or less (Nilakantan et al., 2014), 67% in
   102‚Üí   302‚Üí   302‚Üí284 participants who were in love (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), 50% in young adults in love for less than
   103‚Üí   303‚Üí   303‚Üí285 two years from 33 countries (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025), and 74% (Langeslag, Submitted) and 77%
   104‚Üí   304‚Üí   304‚Üí286 (Evans et al. 2026) in participants experiencing limerence . Lower estimates of 21% and 49% come
   105‚Üí   305‚Üí   305‚Üí287 from experience sampling studies in male participants in relationships of 7 months or less (Fujisaki et
   106‚Üí   306‚Üí   306‚Üí288 al., 2026) and participants experiencing limerence (Evans et al., 2026), respectively. These lower
   107‚Üí   307‚Üí   307‚Üí289 figures may reflect reduced memory bias from capturing experiences in real-time. In sum, average
   108‚Üí   308‚Üí   308‚Üí290 self-reported thinking about the loved one ranged from 21 to 77% of the time across studies, with
   109‚Üí   309‚Üí   309‚Üí291 individual responses ranging from 0 to 100% indicating that, for some individuals, obsessive thinking
   110‚Üí   310‚Üí   310‚Üí292 about a loved one can be all-consuming.
   111‚Üí   311‚Üí   311‚Üí293 Time spent thinking about the loved one has been positively associated with romantic love intensity
   112‚Üí   312‚Üí   312‚Üí294 (r = .25; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025) and limerence intensity (r = .37; Langeslag, Submitted and r =
   113‚Üí   313‚Üí   313‚Üí295 .39; Evans et al., 2026) but not with companionate love intensity (r = .003; Langeslag & Philippi,
   114‚Üí   314‚Üí   314‚Üí296 2025). This indicates that obsessive thinking about a loved one is associated with romantic love
   115‚Üí   315‚Üí   315‚Üí297 specifically rather than companionate love, although the correlations are far from perfect, and we
   116‚Üí   316‚Üí   316‚Üí298 caution against using obsessive thinking about a loved one as a proxy for love intensity as has been
   117‚Üí   317‚Üí   317‚Üí299 done in some studies (i.e., Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007). Obsessive
   118‚Üí   318‚Üí   318‚Üí300 thinking about a loved one has also been shown to increase following threat to relatedness (i.e., the
   119‚Üí   319‚Üí   319‚Üí301 basic psychological need to feel connected to, cared for by, and accepted by others) and negative
   120‚Üí   320‚Üí   320‚Üí302 fantasizing about the loved one across a series of experimental studies using relatedness need
   121‚Üí   321‚Üí   321‚Üí303 manipulations and directed fantasy tasks (Valshtein et al., 2020).
   122‚Üí   322‚Üí   322‚Üí304 People think about their loved one during cognitive tasks unrelated to the loved one. In one study
   123‚Üí   323‚Üí   323‚Üí305 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), participants who were in love reported thinking about their loved one
   124‚Üí   324‚Üí   324‚Üí306 on 42% of thought probes and 34% of free responses during a sustained attention response task.
   125‚Üí   325‚Üí   325‚Üí307 Interestingly, both measures were only weakly associated with obsessive thinking about a loved one
   126‚Üí   326‚Üí   326‚Üí308 in general (both rs < .22), suggesting these two phenomena are only somewhat coupled. In studies of
   127‚Üí   327‚Üí   327‚Üí309 participants who had experienced a break-up and were instructed to suppress thoughts of their ex-
   128‚Üí   328‚Üí   328‚Üí310 partner (Baird et al., 2013), participants still thought about their ex-partner 14‚Äì22% of the time. High
   129‚Üí   329‚Üí   329‚Üí311 cognitive load increased probe-caught (but not self-caught) thoughts about the ex-partner, and desire
   130‚Üí   330‚Üí   330‚Üí312 to reconcile was positively associated with probe-caught thinking, suggesting that people who want
   131‚Üí   331‚Üí   331‚Üí313 to reconcile think about their ex-partner more ‚Äî and are less aware of doing so. The behavioural
   132‚Üí   332‚Üí   332‚Üí314 consequences of this obsessive thinking about a loved one are addressed below.
   133‚Üí   333‚Üí   333‚Üí315 8 Emotions
   134‚Üí   334‚Üí   334‚Üí316 Some terms analogous with "obsessive thinking about a loved one" have negative connotation, (e.g.,
   135‚Üí   335‚Üí   335‚Üí317 "intrusive thinking", "preoccupation", and "pathological obsession" (Fisher et al., 2002; Nilakantan et
   136‚Üí   336‚Üí   336‚Üí318 al., 2014; Tennov, 1979). Intrusive thinking implies recurrent and persistent thoughts that are at least
   137‚Üí   337‚Üí   337‚Üí319 sometimes unwanted and distressing, and that the individual attempts to ignore or suppress
   138‚Üí   338‚Üí   338‚Üí320 (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Indeed, in the study of participants who had experienced a
   139‚Üí   339‚Üí   339‚Üí321 romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), participants endorsed frightened, guilty, remorseful,
   140‚Üí   340‚Üí   340‚Üí8
   141‚Üí   341‚Üí   341‚Üí
   142‚Üí   342‚Üí   342‚Üí322 ashamed, anxious, and grateful as common feelings when thinking about the ex-partner. However,
   143‚Üí   343‚Üí   343‚Üí323 thinking about a loved one is not always bothersome. In a study of participants experiencing
   144‚Üí   344‚Üí   344‚Üí324 limerence recruited through internet limerence communities (Langeslag, Submitted), the mean score
   145‚Üí   345‚Üí   345‚Üí325 on a slider from "it bothers me" to "I enjoy it" was 41 out of 100, significantly below the midpoint.
   146‚Üí   346‚Üí   346‚Üí326 Thinking about the loved one bothered them. In contrast, in a study of participants who were in love
   147‚Üí   347‚Üí   347‚Üí327 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), the mean on the same slider was 84 out of 100, significantly above the
   148‚Üí   348‚Üí   348‚Üí328 midpoint. These participants enjoyed thinking about their loved one.
   149‚Üí   349‚Üí   349‚Üí329 What might determine whether thinking about the loved one has positive or negative emotional
   150‚Üí   350‚Üí   350‚Üí330 effects? Across these two studies (Langeslag, Submitted; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), enjoyment of
   151‚Üí   351‚Üí   351‚Üí331 thinking about the loved one did not correlate positively with romantic love intensity but did
   152‚Üí   352‚Üí   352‚Üí332 correlate positively with companionate love intensity as measured with the IAS attachment scale
   153‚Üí   353‚Üí   353‚Üí333 (Langeslag et al., 2013). Notably, only 5% of the limerence participants were in a romantic
   154‚Üí   354‚Üí   354‚Üí334 relationship with their loved one, compared with 92% of the in-love participants, while time spent
   155‚Üí   355‚Üí   355‚Üí335 thinking about the loved one did not differ drastically between the two samples (74% vs. 67%). It
   156‚Üí   356‚Üí   356‚Üí336 therefore seems that whether people enjoy thinking about their loved one or whether it bothers them
   157‚Üí   357‚Üí   357‚Üí337 depends more on companionate love intensity and whether they are in a romantic relationship with
   158‚Üí   358‚Üí   358‚Üí338 the loved one than on how much they are thinking about them.
   159‚Üí   359‚Üí   359‚Üí339 The rewarding character of thinking about a loved one is also evident in its analgesic effects. In two
   160‚Üí   360‚Üí   360‚Üí340 studies (Nilakantan et al., 2014), participants experienced less pain from thermal stimuli when
   161‚Üí   361‚Üí   361‚Üí341 viewing pictures of their romantic partner than of an acquaintance, and this analgesia was positively
   162‚Üí   362‚Üí   362‚Üí342 correlated with the percentage of waking hours spent thinking about their partner. The mechanism
   163‚Üí   363‚Üí   363‚Üí343 appears to be activation of brain reward regions rather than distraction (Younger et al., 2010),
   164‚Üí   364‚Üí   364‚Üí344 consistent with the enjoyment many people report when thinking about their loved one.
   165‚Üí   365‚Üí   365‚Üí345 There have also been some studies linking obsessive thinking about a loved one with features
   166‚Üí   366‚Üí   366‚Üí346 analogous to symptoms of mood disorders. In one study (Bode & Kavanagh, 2025) using the
   167‚Üí   367‚Üí   367‚Üí347 Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022), membership of clusters associated with
   168‚Üí   368‚Üí   368‚Üí348 higher obsessive thinking about the loved one was positively associated with hypomanic-like features
   169‚Üí   369‚Üí   369‚Üí349 (which are closely related to elevated mood (Faurholt-Jepsen et al., 2020)). This is consistent with
   170‚Üí   370‚Üí   370‚Üí350 previous studies (Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007) that found a similar
   171‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚Üí351 association and two studies finding inconsistent associations with depression symptoms (Bajoghli et
   172‚Üí   372‚Üí   372‚Üí352 al., 2013; Bajoghli et al., 2014).
   173‚Üí   373‚Üí   373‚Üí353 9 Behaviour
   174‚Üí   374‚Üí   374‚Üí354 Behavioural consequences of obsessive thinking about a loved one may be that it manifests as
   175‚Üí   375‚Üí   375‚Üí355 reduced task performance and that it may lead to proximity-seeking behaviour. Several studies have
   176‚Üí   376‚Üí   376‚Üí356 provided evidence that love produces behavioural distraction. Love intensity as measured with the
   177‚Üí   377‚Üí   377‚Üí357 PLS was positively correlated with the interference effect in Stroop and flanker tasks (van
   178‚Üí   378‚Üí   378‚Üí358 Steenbergen et al., 2014), and more obsessive thinking about a loved one in general was associated
   179‚Üí   379‚Üí   379‚Üí359 with poorer and slower performance on a short-term memory task (Langeslag & van Strien, 2020)
   180‚Üí   380‚Üí   380‚Üí360 and with reduced ability to withhold responses in a sustained attention response task (Langeslag &
   181‚Üí   381‚Üí   381‚Üí361 Philippi, 2025). These studies show that love and thinking about a loved one in general (rather than
   182‚Üí   382‚Üí   382‚Üí362 during the task specifically) are associated with behavioural distraction. In addition, the more
   183‚Üí   383‚Üí   383‚Üí363 participants thought about their loved one during the sustained attention response task as rated by
   184‚Üí   384‚Üí   384‚Üí364 coders, the slower their responses were (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and probe-caught thinking
   185‚Üí   385‚Üí   385‚Üí365 about an ex-partner during reading was associated with reduced reading comprehension (Baird et al.,
   186‚Üí   386‚Üí   386‚Üí9
   187‚Üí   387‚Üí   387‚Üí
   188‚Üí   388‚Üí   388‚Üí366 2013). Interestingly, self-caught thinking about the ex-partner was not associated with reduced
   189‚Üí   389‚Üí   389‚Üí367 reading comprehension (Baird et al., 2013), which suggests that thoughts about the loved one that the
   190‚Üí   390‚Üí   390‚Üí368 person is unaware of are particularly disruptive to performance. These studies suggest that thinking
   191‚Üí   391‚Üí   391‚Üí369 about a loved one during an unrelated task specifically is distracting. Behavioural distraction has
   192‚Üí   392‚Üí   392‚Üí370 clear implications for workplace productivity and engagement with the non-romantic social
   193‚Üí   393‚Üí   393‚Üí371 environment.
   194‚Üí   394‚Üí   394‚Üí372 Romantic love is also associated with proximity-seeking behaviours that vary in severity, with
   195‚Üí   395‚Üí   395‚Üí373 stalking as an example of a severe proximity-seeking behaviour. Valshtein et al. (2020) assessed
   196‚Üí   396‚Üí   396‚Üí374 prospective proximity-seeking behaviour in participants in a romantic relationship using 53 items
   197‚Üí   397‚Üí   397‚Üí375 asking how likely they would be to engage in behaviours such as showing up to the loved one's house
   198‚Üí   398‚Üí   398‚Üí376 unannounced and sending desperate messages. Across both studies, the score on a 12-item obsessive
   199‚Üí   399‚Üí   399‚Üí377 thinking measure was positively associated with prospective proximity-seeking behaviour. We can‚Äôt
   200‚Üí   400‚Üí   400‚Üí378 assert a causal relationship because the study was cross-sectional, but this is an avenue for future
   201‚Üí   401‚Üí   401‚Üí379 research.
   202‚Üí   402‚Üí   402‚Üí380 10 Discussion
   203‚Üí   403‚Üí   403‚Üí381 Obsessive thinking about a loved one has been identified as a core feature of romantic love for more
   204‚Üí   404‚Üí   404‚Üí382 than four decades (Tennov, 1979), is embedded in the field's most widely used measure of romantic
   205‚Üí   405‚Üí   405‚Üí383 love (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986), and is regularly invoked in theoretical accounts of the phenomenon
   206‚Üí   406‚Üí   406‚Üí384 (Bode, 2023; Fisher, 1998). Yet it has never been systematically reviewed. This article provides the
   207‚Üí   407‚Üí   407‚Üí385 first such synthesis, organised using an Eight Perspectives approach (Bode & Kowal, 2026).
   208‚Üí   408‚Üí   408‚Üí386 Obsessive thinking about a loved one appears to be a cross-culturally present feature of romantic love
   209‚Üí   409‚Üí   409‚Üí387 that expresses across the lifespan, is associated with romantic love specifically rather than
   210‚Üí   410‚Üí   410‚Üí388 companionate love, and has identifiable cognitive, emotional, and behavioural signatures. What
   211‚Üí   411‚Üí   411‚Üí389 emerges, however, is that our understanding of this fundamental feature of romantic love remains
   212‚Üí   412‚Üí   412‚Üí390 remarkably partial. Table 1 presents a summary of the main findings of this review.

   266‚Üí   466‚Üí   466‚Üí414 A second observation is that the pathologising frame implied by terms such as "intrusive thinking"
   267‚Üí   467‚Üí   467‚Üí415 and "pathological obsession" is difficult to reconcile with the available evidence. Selective serotonin
   268‚Üí   468‚Üí   468‚Üí416 reuptake inhibitors do not reduce obsessive thinking about a loved one (Bode, Kowal, et al., 2025),

   270‚Üí   470‚Üí   470‚Üí418 thinking about their loved one (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and the phenomenon is associated with
   271‚Üí   471‚Üí   471‚Üí419 analgesia (Nilakantan et al., 2014) and proximity-seeking behaviour (Valshtein et al., 2020)‚Äî both
   272‚Üí   472‚Üí   472‚Üí420 consistent with adaptive function. These findings suggest obsessive thinking in romantic love is
   273‚Üí   473‚Üí   473‚Üí421 better understood as a functional component of a mating-related motivational state than as a symptom
   274‚Üí   474‚Üí   474‚Üí422 of dysfunction. We therefore favour the term "obsessive thinking about a loved one", which specifies
   275‚Üí   475‚Üí   475‚Üí423 the object of thought rather than pathologising the thinker, and hope this review marks the start of a
   276‚Üí   476‚Üí   476‚Üí424 broader shift toward this usage across the field.
   277‚Üí   477‚Üí   477‚Üí425 Several limitations of the available evidence warrant mention. Studies of obsessive thinking about a
   278‚Üí   478‚Üí   478‚Üí426 loved one in romantic love are dominated by Western, young-adult samples, limiting claims about
   279‚Üí   479‚Üí   479‚Üí427 universality. Several load-bearing findings rest on single studies awaiting replication (e.g., Baird et
   280‚Üí   480‚Üí   480‚Üí428 al., 2013; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025; Nilakantan et al., 2014; Valshtein et al., 2020), and many of
   281‚Üí   481‚Üí   481‚Üí429 these studies are based on small samples and cross-sectional designs that limit causal inference.
   282‚Üí   482‚Üí   482‚Üí430 Measurement is heavily reliant on self-report and, in particular, on three items from the Passionate
   283‚Üí   483‚Üí   483‚Üí431 Love Scale (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986) that were not developed to assess obsessive thinking about a

   285‚Üí   485‚Üí   485‚Üí433 behavioural measures, would strengthen the evidence base considerably. These limitations inform the

   324‚Üí   524‚Üí   524‚Üí449 11 Conclusions
   325‚Üí   525‚Üí   525‚Üí450 Obsessive thinking about a loved one is a defining feature of romantic love, yet research on it has
   326‚Üí   526‚Üí   526‚Üí451 accumulated in a piecemeal fashion across disciplines that rarely speak to one another. This review
   327‚Üí   527‚Üí   527‚Üí452 has used an Eight Perspectives approach to bring that scattered literature into a single structure,
   328‚Üí   528‚Üí   528‚Üí453 clarify what is known, and make visible what is not. The phenotypic expression of obsessive thinking
   329‚Üí   529‚Üí   529‚Üí454 is reasonably well characterised; its mechanisms, evolutionary functions, and ecology are not.
   330‚Üí   530‚Üí   530‚Üí455 Addressing these gaps will require contributions from the evolutionary social and behavioural
   331‚Üí   531‚Üí   531‚Üí456 sciences, neuroscience, genetics, and cross-cultural research ‚Äî disciplines that have, to date, largely
   332‚Üí   532‚Üí   532‚Üí457 overlooked this striking and ubiquitous feature of human mating psychology.
