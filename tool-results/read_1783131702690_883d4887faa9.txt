    47→Abstract
    48→Background. Indigenous mothers in Canada experience disproportionate adversity related to
    49→mental health with increased risk of anxiety and depression (Black et al., 2019; Owais et al.,
    50→2020). The mental health disparity between Indigenous and non-Indigenous mothers reveals the
    51→need to provide mental health support services that are culturally aligned and meet the specific
    52→needs of this community. Indigenous identifying individuals who participated in a family mental
    53→health program for mothers of young children were contacted and invited to participate in an
    54→individual interview to share their experiences and provide their perspectives on mental health
    55→support service seeking. Research Objective and Questions. The aim of this study was to build
    56→knowledge surrounding the experiences of Indigenous mothers with mental health support
    57→service seeking and participation to inform improvements in mental health programming. This
    58→objective was framed around two central research questions. 1. What are the experiences and
    59→motivations of Indigenous mothers seeking and participating in mental health programs starting
    60→in the perinatal period? 2. How can improvements be made to existing programs? Methods. To
    61→lead this study in a culturally appropriate and collaborative way, the first author hosted a meet
    62→and greet gathering aimed at developing relationships and fostering a sense of community. The
    63→research team conducted seven virtual interviews with individual participants. Data was analyzed
    64→using a narrative approach, situating results within social, cultural, kinship, and individual
    65→contexts. Results. Findings illustrated key storylines across sub-samples of Anishinaabe (n=3)
    66→and Métis (n=4) participants. Core storylines within reflections of Anishinaabe narrators
    67→included (1) Taking Care of Me to Take Care of Them (Cycle Breaking), (2) Honouring the
    68→Child Within (Stories of Courage and Perseverance), (3) Seeking Connection (Exploring
    69→Cultural Identity), (4) Looking at the Bigger Picture (Wholistic Wellness), and (5) Systemic
    70→3
    71→
    72→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
    73→Oppression (Stories of Barriers, Protection, and Survival). Core storylines from Métis narrators
    74→included (1) Indigenous Enough (Stories of Connection and Disconnection, (2) Wearing Many
    75→Hats (Navigating Roles and Identities within Motherhood), (3) Healing the Self (Stories of
    76→Coping and Maintaining Individuality), and (4) Systems Navigation (Stories of Perseverance and
    77→Exhaustion). Discussion. Results highlight the value of relationality in the wellbeing of
    78→Indigenous mothers, as well as the importance of culturally responsive and trauma informed care
    79→in program delivery. This research will offer guidance for future studies and service providers
    80→aiming to incorporate Indigenous perspectives into program development and implementation.
    81→Findings may be used to create novel, culturally appropriate interventions to support Indigenous
    82→women and families.

    97→The Issue
    98→Impacts of colonialism and systemic oppression including the Residential School System,
    99→the 60s Scoop, and harmful practices within the Child and Family Services system have been
   100→found to correlate with poorer wellness outcomes for Indigenous people (Gone et al., 2019;
   101→Matheson et al., 2022; Mitchell, 2019). This compounded historical trauma, coupled with
   102→ongoing oppression have resulted in significant mental health-specific wellness impacts to this
   103→population (McKinley et al., 2020). Suicide, depression, anxiety, and post-traumatic stress
   104→disorder, (PTSD) are highly prevalent in Indigenous communities within Canada and globally
   105→(Nelson & Wilson, 2017; Pollock et al., 2018). Researchers have directly linked poor mental
   106→health outcomes with internalized racism and oppression (Gale et al., 2020). When contrasting
   107→the mental health of Indigenous women compared with Indigenous men in Canada, some studies
   108→reveal that Indigenous women more-so than men have a greater likelihood of suffering from
   109→PTSD, suicidality, anxiety and depression and others report fewer gender-based differences in
   110→the mental health of Indigenous people (Bingham et al., 2019; Levesque & Quesnel-Vallee,
   111→2019). The experience of motherhood involves numerous joys as well as challenges and can
   112→result in mental health difficulties to which women are particularly vulnerable in the perinatal
   113→period (O’Brien et al., 2023; Miller et al., 2024). Perinatal mental health challenges
   114→disproportionately impact racialized mothers, particularly Indigenous mothers (Meredith et al.,
   115→2023; Miller et al., 2024). Considering Indigenous motherhood specifically, a systematic review
   116→conducted in Canada revealed that Indigenous women had 87% greater likelihood of developing
   117→postpartum depression compared to non-Indigenous women (Black et al., 2019). Related results
   118→6
   119→
   120→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   121→are evidenced internationally where Indigenous mothers are found to experience disproportionate
   122→adversity related to mental health starting in the perinatal period with increased risk of anxiety
   123→and depression according to a meta-analysis of Indigenous women across Canada, the United
   124→States, Australia and New Zealand compared to their non-Indigenous counterparts (Owais et al.,
   125→2020). These experiences, compounded by societal and systemic factors can result in
   126→intergeneration challenges for families (Slomian et al., 2019). Therefore, it appears that there is a
   127→mental health disparity between Indigenous and non-Indigenous mothers with repercussion
   128→spanning across generations. There is a clear need to provide opportunities for mental health
   129→support to Indigenous women. However, less is known about how this can be best addressed
   130→based on input from Indigenous mothers themselves related to priorities and values when seeking
   131→support services.
   132→The Current Response
   133→Historically, psychological and other health care services have been unethical, harmful,
   134→and inappropriate for use with Indigenous demographics (Truth and Reconciliation Commission,
   135→2015). In acknowledgment of this, the Canadian Psychological Association (CPA) assembled a
   136→committee comprised of Indigenous practitioners and scientists to develop action items that can
   137→be implemented throughout the field at the national level (CPA, 2018). The Task Force on
   138→Responding to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada’s Report states that mental
   139→health treatments for Indigenous people must be collaborative, culturally appropriate and
   140→relevant, (e.g., considerate of and specific to cultural context) and empower Indigenous
   141→community members (CPA, 2018). Moreover, activists point to movements such as Idle No
   142→More, Land Back, and Calls to Actions from the National Centre for Truth and Reconciliation to
   143→guide meaningful change and to improve the quality of life for Indigenous Peoples (Abele, 2006;
   144→7
   145→
   146→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   147→Hudson, 2014; Kirwan, 2015; Truth and Reconciliation Commission, 2012). Here, values of self-
   148→determination and sovereignty are emphasized, along with collectivism, whereas Canada’s focus
   149→on individuality is in conflict with traditional Indigenous values of connection and community
   150→(Russell, 2000).
   151→A Gap in the Literature
   152→Extant literature highlights resilience factors evidenced intergenerationally in Indigenous
   153→families as protective against personal health consequences associated with adverse experiences
   154→associated with the impacts of colonialism and systemic oppression (Fuller-Thompson et al.,
   155→2020; Kirmayer et al., 2011). Using strengths-based approaches to facilitate wellbeing through
   156→cultural and spiritual traditions have been shown to promote resilience and wellness among
   157→Indigenous people (Ahmed et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2016; Craven et al., 2016; Gone, 2019;
   158→Koster et al., 2012). Studies have demonstrated that mental health and wellness programs
   159→developed by Indigenous communities are more successful for the people within those
   160→communities in terms of program efficacy regarding improved mental health outcomes. This is
   161→due the inclusion of culturally specific components and acknowledgement of shared historical
   162→context (Nelson & Wilson, 2017). This exemplifies the value of self-determination and
   163→Indigenous people regaining control over their own affairs. Other proposed methods for restoring
   164→wellness among Indigenous people include engaging in culturally appropriate programs and
   165→services, and returning to traditional healing modalities (Burrage, 2020; Gone, 2013). Hence,
   166→there is a growing body of documentation regarding themes and values salient to the wellbeing
   167→of Indigenous people broadly. However little research has documented the specific needs and
   168→values of Indigenous mothers starting in the perinatal period. Some studies report values of
   169→social support, family, and Indigenous identity as central to wellbeing in the perinatal period.
   170→8
   171→
   172→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   173→Although, what is currently known includes predominantly perspectives of staff and healthcare
   174→workers on the mental health treatment of Indigenous women in the perinatal period with calls
   175→for additional Indigenous-led research on this topic in ways that emphasise Indigenous mothers’
   176→voices and detail their experiences with mental health interventions. (Meredith et al., 2023). The
   177→current thesis details a study that seeks to understand service use experiences including
   178→challenges and fruition, and strategies for improving mental health programming to meet the
   179→needs of Indigenous mothers around the perinatal period in Manitoba, Canada. This includes
   180→previously piloted programs Building Emotional Awareness and Mental Health (BEAM) and
   181→Building Regulation in Dual Generations (BRIDGE) which were developed to build emotional
   182→awareness in mothers and their children through group therapy intervention with online and app-
   183→based components (Joyce et al., 2023; Roos et al., 2023).
   184→Research Objective and Questions
   185→The aim of this thesis was to build knowledge surrounding the experiences of Indigenous
   186→mothers regarding mental health support service seeking and participation starting in the
   187→perinatal period, (from pregnancy until 12 months following childbirth) and inform
   188→improvements in future and current programming (Noonan et al., 2017) This objective was
   189→achieved through the inquiry into three key research questions:
   190→1. What are the experiences and motivations of Indigenous mothers regarding participation
   191→in Western mental health programs, including BEAM and BRIDGE?
   192→a. What does the journey of mental health support seeking look like as an
   193→Indigenous mother?
   194→2. How can improvements be made to existing programs?
   195→a. To what extent should these programs be adapted for Indigenous women?

   203→Methods
   204→Overview
   205→Constructivist and transformative paradigms represent the foundation of this exploratory
   206→and descriptive qualitative study aimed at gaining knowledge on participant experiences and
   207→using findings to advocate for change in current practices within the field of psychology related
   208→to Indigenous priorities and perspectives. Qualitative methods are most appropriately suited to
   209→working with Indigenous populations as quantitative approached are referenced by community
   210→members and Knowledge Keepers as being disrespectful (Lillie, personal communication 2023;
   211→Stone, personal communication, 2019). Data was collected through 46–60-minute semi-
   212→structured qualitative interviews and analyzed using a narrative approach (Reissman, 1993).
   213→Participants were also given an opportunity to review the results of the narrative analysis to
   214→provide their thoughts and feedback to serve as a final opportunity for data analysis and
   215→revisions. Readers of this work are encouraged to develop their own meaning making of stories
   216→presented herein, with the recognition that each may be understood differently based on personal
   217→experiences, biases and values (Archibald, 2008; Kovach, 2010).
   218→The BEAM and BRIDGE Programs
   219→The BEAM Program (Building Emotional Awareness and Mental Health for mothers
   220→with young children) was designed to offer accessible online mental health group supports to
   221→mothers of young children across Manitoba and Alberta (Joyce et al., 2023). Participants
   222→10
   223→
   224→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   225→included mothers with symptoms of depressions who had children between 18-36 months old.
   226→BEAM consisted of a 10-week mental health intervention with a social support forum,
   227→psychoeducation modules, and weekly telehealth therapy (Joyce et al., 2023). BEAM was piloted
   228→between July 2021 and September 2021. The sample was not entirely Indigenous, however a
   229→large subset of participants identified as Indigenous via demographic questionnaires.
   230→The BRIDGE pilot program (Building Regulation in Dual Generations) ran from July
   231→2019 to September 2019 and was designed to incorporate Dialectical Behavioural Therapy and
   232→parenting skills in a group format for mothers of young children to address symptoms of
   233→depression and anxiety (Roos et al, 2023). The program spanned 20 weeks, consisting of weekly
   234→sessions led by DBT clinicians with 60 minutes of DBT skills training and 50 minutes of
   235→parenting skills training. Skills were explored further through role play activities and small group
   236→discussion. Handouts and worksheets accompanied lessons taught each week.
   237→The Current Study
   238→Manitoba-residing participants who identify as Indigenous and participated in either
   239→BEAM or BRIDGE were contacted and invited to participate in interviews to share about their
   240→experiences with the programs and provide their overall perspectives on mental health support
   241→service seeking starting in the perinatal period. Both BEAM and BRIDGE studies were approved
   242→by the University of Manitoba’s Research Ethics Board and only those participants who
   243→consented to being contacted for future research studies and meet eligibility criteria were
   244→recruited for the current study.

   331→Data Analysis
   332→Data was analyzed using an inductive narrative approach (Delve, 2021). Audio files from
   333→interview recordings were transcribed using Otter and Zoom recording software. Following the
   334→transcription of audio recordings, research assistants compared the written documents with audio
   335→for accuracy. The first author then reviewed transcripts for familiarity. Importantly, pauses and
   336→filler words were left in the transcripts during the analytic stage in keeping with narrative
   337→methodology to understand how stories are shared along with their content (Delve, 2021).
   338→Narrative segments (individual stories) were identified in each interview. Segments were
   339→identified through examining exit and entrance talk to distinguish one narrative from another
   340→(Reissman, 1993). Here, underlying themes were identified within the narrations with particular
   341→consideration of personal, kinship, historical, and cultural contexts. Connections were made
   342→across all participant interviews to understand trends in experiences based on stories shared and
   343→overlapping themes within these narratives. This produced core narratives, with notable nuances
   344→based on unique experience (Crossley, 2002; Delve, 2021; Reissman, 1993; Weatherhead, 2011).
   345→Research team meetings were held following the individual transcript analysis of each participant
   346→and following the synthesis of core storylines to increase flexibility in interpretations of the data.
   347→As a final portion of the data analysis, participants were invited to provide a response to
   348→both results from narrative analysis. Participants were sent copies of transcripts and initial
   349→interview interpretations. They were asked to review these materials and were invited to provide
   350→feedback to the research team about how their stories have been understood and structured. This
   351→procedure was used in acknowledgement of multiple truths and realities based on narrator and
   352→15

   354→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   355→interviewer perspectives and experiences. Such an approach serves to increase the credibility of
   356→results and promote collaboration within this study in support of data sovereignty. Only one
   357→participant provided feedback at this stage, sharing her approval of the interpretations made and
   358→her appreciation for the insights offered through the analysis.

   388→Taking Care of Me to Take Care of Them (Cycle Breaking)
   389→A sentiment shared among narrators was the desire to take care of themselves well to best
   390→take care of their children. This was framed as a critical means of breaking cycles from within
   391→their families and ensuring that their children would experience better childhoods than they did.
   392→Breaking cycles in these contexts encapsulates several issues related to the impacts of colonial
   393→violence and systemic oppression including mental health, trauma, parenting styles, and CFS
   394→involvement. In this way, cycle breaking is connected to the pursuit of intergenerational thriving.
   395→Sheryl (pseudonym) experienced a portion of her childhood in foster care. She shared
   396→stories about her hope to be a positive role model for her son by unlearning behaviours of
   397→substance use and relationship disfunction that she witnessed growing up. She expressed the
   398→difficulty in this process of breaking cycles which can be laden with loneliness and isolation,
   399→particularly given its co-occurrence with the Covid-10 pandemic. Sheryl highlighted this cycle
   400→breaking goal as a motivating factor in pursuing support.
   401→…we grew up with my Mom, and then we ended up in foster care. … there's just lots of
   402→different things that happened, and it just got harder to manage. You can't really talk to
   403→17

   405→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   406→people and can't socialize, and you know, everybody lives far away from me. I think that
   407→just wanting to do better for my son as well, and I wasn't working too…

   409→Child and Family Services (CFS). She expressed her wish to disrupt this legacy within her
   410→family, to offer her children a stable and supportive family environment that she did not
   411→experience, as well as to prevent further involvement with the CFS system.
   412→I want to try to break the cycle of what I grew up with and stuff like that, and just do

   414→picnic stuff and movies. I lost years and things, trying to make it up.

   807→revisions. Further, meetings held with research assistants aimed at discussing storylines

   809→participants were provided with the opportunity to have a supplement audio file included
   810→alongside results of their interview to maintain the integrity of the oral storytelling. When
   811→presented with this option, two participants responded. One expressed indifference and the other
   812→indicated that she was opposed to this approach as she wanted her stories to remain as
   813→anonymous as possible. The lack of responses from the remaining participants may indicate that
   814→the maintenance of oral stories was not a priority.

   865→Discussion
   866→This study sought to generate knowledge of the experiences of Indigenous mothers
   867→seeking mental health support starting in the perinatal period to inform improvements in future
   868→and current programming. Elucidating these questions, results found five core storylines in the
   869→experiences of Anishinaabe mothers (1) Taking Care of Me to Take Care of Them (Cycle
   870→Breaking), (2) Honouring the Child Within (Stories of Courage and Perseverance), (3) Seeking
   871→Connection (Exploring Cultural Identity), (4) Looking at the Bigger Picture (Wholistic
   872→Wellness), and (5) Systemic Oppression (Stories of Barriers, Protection, and Survival) and four
   873→core storylines in the experiences of Métis mothers (1) Indigenous Enough (Stories of
   874→Connection and Disconnection, (2) Wearing Many Hats (Navigating Roles and Identities within
   875→Motherhood), (3) Healing the Self (Stories of Coping and Maintaining Individuality), and (4)
   876→Systems Navigation (Stories of Perseverance and Exhaustion). Dominant narratives of
   877→Anishinaabe and Métis sub-samples were summarized as Ancestral Strength Despite Colonial
   878→Harms as well as Striving for Belongingness and The Injustice of Resilience respectively.

   882→Narratives of both Métis and Anishinaabe sub-samples bore similarities, however, core storylines
   883→were identified based on story content emphasized most within each sub-sample.
   884→Overall, these findings highlight the perseverance within this demographic along with the
   885→imperative of developing and implementing culturally specific programs to best meet the unique
   886→wellness needs of Indigenous mothers. This work is critical to advancing mental health care
   887→toward equitable and responsive services. It provides a foundation from which program co-
   888→development can occur with Indigenous mothers, in promotion of self-determination practices.
   889→By co-creating novel opportunities for wellbeing support to serve a demographic that has
   890→experienced historic and current mistreatment, Indigenous mothers may find the optimal care
   891→that they deserve through both development and implementation processes.
   892→Taken together, results emphasize the strength and resilience of Indigenous women faced
   893→with histories of adversity and current challenges in navigating identity, family dynamics, and
   894→systemic oppression. Importantly, the injustice identified through participants’ reflections
   895→concerning the development of resilience is a tension captured in critical feminist and
   896→intersectional research led by Black and Indigenous scholars through an examination the
   897→conceptual pitfalls of resilience. These works propose to instead emphasize resistance when
   898→describing the psycho-social-political experience of change-driven empowerment in the wake of
   899→adversity (De Saxe & Trotter-Simons, 2012; Goodkind et al., 2020). Resilience can imply an
   900→acceptance of harmful actions toward an individual or group, whereas resistance better
   901→encapsulates the nuances of actively advocating for change and fighting against oppression, a
   902→theme expressed throughout the reflections of narrators in the current study. Resilience is further
   903→criticized as being limited to an individual, whereas resistance is conceptualized as applicable to
   904→a collective through justice-oriented action (De Saxe & Trotter-Simons, 2012; Goodkind et al.,
   905→37
   906→
   907→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   908→2020). The collectivistic value of mutual support is particularly aligned with an Indigenous
   909→worldview which prioritizes collective wellbeing over that of the individual (Russell, 2000).
   910→These perspectives of resistance and collectivism are paramount in accurately conveying the
   911→experiences of this sample framed within autonomy, agency, empowerment, and unity.
   912→Core storylines from Anishinaabe narrators tell of participants’ mutual desires to disrupt
   913→legacies of colonialism within their families, seek connections to culture and community,
   914→practice wholistic wellness, and persevere through adversity despite a systemic oppression.
   915→These finding can be considered within the framework of self-determination theory in its focus
   916→on social factors that serve as facilitators or barriers to wellbeing (Ryan & Deci, 2017). This is
   917→related to the expressions of relational and wholistic values observed across narrators’
   918→experiences. The Breath of Life theory is equally aligned with findings in its emphasis on
   919→interconnection, wholism, and traditional knowledge in the experience of thriving among
   920→Indigenous people (Blackstock, 2011). Findings are consistent with extant literature
   921→demonstrating the wellness needs of First Nations people are related to systems level impacts of
   922→colonization as well as intrinsically linked to relationality, and cultural connection (Gould et al.,
   923→2021; Kyoon-Achan et al., 2021; Thunderbird Partnership Foundation, 2015).
   924→Core storylines from Métis narrators speak to issues of identity and cultural belonging
   925→along with challenges related to systems navigation. Tajfel’s social identity theory can be applied
   926→to these findings in its conceptualization of the various identities people hold both socially and
   927→individually (Hogg, 2016). In this sub-sample, conflicts in identities were explored through
   928→participants’ experiences navigating motherhood, personal identity, and cultural identity.
   929→Participants expressed disconnection from their Métis identity, which has been cited as occurring
   930→due to Western practices of the racialization of cultural identity, wherein Métis people are
   931→38
   932→
   933→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   934→discredited in their Indigeneity based on the notion that their genetic claim to the identity is
   935→insufficient (Macdougall, 2017). From the perspective of social identity theory, this experience
   936→will facilitate the marginalization of Métis individuals from the Indigenous “in-group” based on
   937→non-Indigenous perception and will similar challenge other personal and social identities
   938→according to social interactions, personal beliefs, and cognitions. In this way, the identity
   939→challenges across roles of motherhood, Métis community membership, and the individual self
   940→may be intrinsically connected. Literature has demonstrated the common experience of identity
   941→loss and mourning connected to the transition into motherhood reflected in the stories shared in
   942→the current study (Babetin, 2020). Relatedly, belongingness has been found to be a central pillar
   943→of wellbeing across domestic and cultural settings for Métis women (Auger, 2021; Flaminio et
   944→al., 2020). Similarly, the challenge of systems navigation can be conceptualized within the issue
   945→of belongingness as participants in this sample articulated these hardships as an experience of
   946→being lost and alone. With this, there is emerging evidence in support of a systems navigation
   947→approach to facilitate the receipt of adequate care services for individuals seeking additional
   948→wellness engagement opportunities and social connection (Rankin et al., 2022; Teggart et al.,
   949→2023).
   950→Key program recommendations across both sub-samples include offering weekly hybrid
   951→program sessions, providing childcare onsite, including traditional teachings related to parenting
   952→and mental health from Elders, offering free programming, connecting participants to resources
   953→during program, providing systems navigation support, offering follow-up check ins post group
   954→(individual and group formats), continuing care post-program for tailored in-depth support,
   955→implementing mandatory cultural sensitivity training for facilitators, and increasing opportunities
   956→for relationship building. In addition to consistency with the Breath of Life Theory (Blackstock,
   957→39
   958→
   959→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
   960→2011) and self determination theory (Ryan & Deci, 2017) in the emphasis on relational wellbeing
   961→and wholism, social determinants of health are highlighted in explicit recommendations from
   962→participants. Social determinants of health have been recognized as a crucial point of
   963→understanding for service providers when working with Indigenous people given the history of
   964→colonial violence within this populations, and its impact on wellbeing across multiple domains
   965→(Kim, 2019).
   966→Health and wellness equity for Indigenous people is disrupted by systemic barriers and
   967→oppressive policies that target Indigenous families (Nguyen et al., 2020). Researchers
   968→recommend that a trauma informed approach be adopted among service providers to mitigate the
   969→perpetuation of further systemic harms (Kim, 2019; Substance Abuse and Mental Health
   970→Services Administration, 2023). Participants’ recommendations align with best practices in
   971→trauma informed care including developing trust, fostering empowerment, using a collaborative
   972→approach, ensuring safety, providing peer support with those who share lived experience, and
   973→developing an awareness of cultural or historical factors impacting clients or participants (Koury
   974→et al., 2022; Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, 2023; Pinderhughes et
   975→al., 2016). Structural factors impacting health and wellness equity for Indigenous have also been
   976→cited as including financial barriers, inaccessible services, and a lack of collaboration with
   977→Indigenous community members in policy development. Researchers recommend increasing the
   978→cultural relevance of programs and services in partnership with Indigenous community members
   979→(Nguyen et al., 2020). These barriers and corresponding recommendations are well aligned with
   980→recommendations from participants in the current study along with the approach of this body of
   981→work.

   985→Furthermore, participant feedback detailing the value of integrating cultural teachings
   986→into programs by engaging Elders and other community members in their development is
   987→highlighted by extant literature. Research details the positive impact of cultural connection on
   988→mental health outcomes for Indigenous community members, particularly when mental health
   989→support programs are developed with representatives of the community they are meant to serve
   990→(Nelson and Wilson, 2017). This appears to be connected to uniting factors of shared histories
   991→and experiences. Similarly, adopting traditional healing modalities to promote wellbeing has
   992→been found to be an effective means of facilitating wellness in Indigenous communities (Burrage,
   993→2020; Gone, 2013) With this, a traditional healing often involves ceremonial practices which
   994→involve teachings and social connection with other community members. These practices were
   995→identified by participants as critical in maintaining their wellbeing and of high priority to include
   996→in a wellness program. The Canadian Psychological Association’s task force recommendations
   997→in response to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission’s Calls to Action reflect similar
   998→priorities in mental health service delivery for Indigenous people. Responding to Calls to Actions
   999→detailing the need to support the self-determination of Indigenous People, the Canadian
  1000→Psychological Association’s task force calls for the collaborative development of culturally
  1001→specific support with and for Indigenous people (CPA, 2018; TRC, 2012). This recommendation
  1002→is made in acknowledgement of the harms that have occurred within healthcare and academic
  1003→systems wherein neither collaboration nor culturally specific approaches were centred (CPA,
  1004→2018). In this way, to facilitate individuals’ and families’ opportunities for wellness support,
  1005→systems level change is required to restructure approaches used for both the development and
  1006→delivery of services.

  1010→Notably, the Métis and Anishinaabe sub-samples diverged in recommendations regarding
  1011→the importance of Indigenous facilitators. A majority of Anishinaabe participants indicated that
  1012→this is critical, whereas most Métis participants perceived this as not vital to programming for
  1013→Indigenous women. This may be due to the identity disparity between sub-samples wherein
  1014→Métis participants felt less connected to their identity as an Indigenous person than the
  1015→Anishinaabe participants. In practice, this holds implications for programs delivered to
  1016→participants of a given Nation. Specifically, service providers might consider ensuring that
  1017→facilitators of tailored Anishinaabe programs are themselves Anishinaabe, while facilitators for
  1018→tailored Métis programs may not require such identity-matched leadership. Divergence was also
  1019→observed across and within subsamples regarding the level of structure preferred in mental health
  1020→programing. Some participants indicated that they perceived the BEAM program specifically to

  1022→the structure of the program as it taught strategies for implementing structure within their home
  1023→environments. Therefore, adopting a flexible approach when delivering programs may be
  1024→beneficial to ensuring that structure is available to those who seek it, and opportunities for
  1025→informality can be offered to others.

  1042→Limitations
  1043→Several limitations to this study must be noted. First, transcription and data management
  1044→software including Otter and Zoom are Western-developed tools that may not be congruent with
  1045→traditions of Indigenous knowledges. Specifically, despite the efforts of the research team in
  1046→mitigating these issues, the alteration of an oral story as shared during an interview into a written
  1047→transcript may impede upon the sanctity of the spoken words in their original form. Although
  1048→participants did not indicate that this approach was problematic or misaligned with their values,
  1049→it is unknown whether this approach was a deterrent for others to participating in this study.
  1050→Additionally, storytelling principles vary by Nation and emphasize not only their content, but
  1051→also their delivery. The design of this study does not allow for diversity in such practices due to
  1052→constraints of time, space, and resources. Furthermore, voices of Indigenous mothers not
  1053→captured in this work include those who participated in the BEAM and BRIDGE programs but
  1054→did not wish to participate in the current study, as well as those who did not participate in the
  1055→BEAM and BRIDGE program. The absence of these perspectives limits the generalizability of
  1056→findings to a broader population within the scope of Indigenous maternal mental health in
  1057→Manitoba.

  1061→Implications and Future Directions
  1062→Findings produced through this work will contribute to filling a gap in the literature
  1063→concerning Indigenous perspectives on mental health support and service seeking starting in the
  1064→perinatal period. These results contribute to a vital area of research aimed at challenging the
  1065→historical development of programs embedded in Western values systems that do not serve the
  1066→needs of Indigenous people. Mental health programs and services developed without consultation
  1067→or partnership with Indigenous community members can produce harm within this population
  1068→and further perpetuate health inequities for Indigenous people (CPA, 2018). Program and
  1069→systems level inaccessibility to health services equally contributes to inequities in wellness
  1070→outcomes for Indigenous people (Nguyen et al., 2020). This research offers insight into the
  1071→importance of programming that is both accessible and directly responsive to the needs and
  1072→values of the demographic that it is intended to serve. This information will be beneficial to
  1073→service providers, community organization representatives, researchers, and academics who seek
  1074→to implement transformative practice toward equity-driven and cultural relevant care into their
  1075→research, programs, and/or services. The study design and engagement processes can serve as an
  1076→example of collaborative and respectful research with Indigenous people and contribute to an
  1077→emerging best practice in this area of Two-Eyed Seeing research, integrating traditional
  1078→Indigenous ways of knowing and doing with Western research practices (Hatcher et al., 2009).
  1079→This work will inform future program development and culturally appropriate treatment models
  1080→by integrating the feedback solicited herein to create novel support opportunities for Indigenous
  1081→mothers in the province. Finally, relationships developed herein will be built upon for additional
  1082→research and partnerships in advisory and co-development roles for on-going related projects to
  1083→maintain connection and offer novel opportunities and resources to participants. The
  1084→44

  1086→TELLING STORIES OF RESISTANCE – CALLING TO ANCESTRAL STRENGTH
  1087→development of a novel program will involve the collaboration of Indigenous mothers,
  1088→Knowledge Keepers, Elders, and academics with experience in program development. Program
  1089→content and structure will be developed collectively using recommendations from the current
  1090→study, followed by a pilot trial to assess the initial feasible and acceptable of this support
  1091→opportunity. This program will be aimed at offering accessible, and culturally specific support to
  1092→Indigenous mothers to promote intergenerational family thriving.
