    15→Abstract
    16→Background and Objectives: Mentalization-Based Treatment (MBT), including brief psychoeducational
    17→modules, has shown promising effects in reducing aggression among adolescents and forensic populations.
    18→However, little is known about the mechanisms through which MBT exerts its effects. This preliminary
    19→pilot study aimed to explore changes in hostile attribution following participation in an MBT
    20→psychoeducational module using a multimodal assessment approach.
    21→Methods: The six sessions (three meetings) of the MBT-Introductory Group were delivered to a total of 58
    22→participants across four groups: inmates, juvenile delinquents, high school students, and young adults. Pre-
    23→and post-intervention assessments combined quantitative measures with qualitative analyses to examine
    24→changes in different facets of hostile attribution. Tasks included ambiguous facial expression recognition,
    25→interpretation of visual scenes, the Ambiguous Intentions and Hostility Questionnaire, and interpretation of
    26→self-recalled interpersonal situations.
    27→Results: Participation in the MBT module was associated with a reduction in hostile attributions among
    28→young adults and juvenile offenders; results among high school students were mixed depending on the task
    29→type, while among inmates, changes were observed only for specific stimulus formats.
    30→Conclusions: Despite several limitations, this preliminary study suggests that psychoeducational MBT
    31→modules may be associated with changes in hostile attribution bias. The findings suggest that mentalization-
    32→based frameworks may represent a potentially promising direction for preventive and rehabilitative
    33→interventions for at-risk youth, including brief psychoeducational formats.
    34→Keywords: hostile attributions, mentalization-based treatment, inmates, juvenile delinquents

    36→Introduction
    37→Although the social cues were ambiguous, a tendency toward hostile attribution (HA) emerged.
    38→Hostile attribution refers to the inclination to interpret others' actions as intentionally hostile and
    39→to assign blame for those actions (Dodge, 2006; Tuente et al., 2019; Combs et al., 2017; Bodecka-
    40→Zych et al., 2021). HA belongs to a broader category of hostility biases, which also include the
    41→tendency to interpret emotionally ambiguous facial expressions as angry—such as morphed faces
    42→blending emotions like anger and fear—known as the hostile perception (HP) (Smeijers, 2023).
    43→Both biases—a tendency to perceive hostility in emotional expressions and to attribute hostile
    44→intent in ambiguous interactions—contribute to constructing an inner image of a hostile and
    45→threatening social world.
    46→Hostile Attribution: Specific Perception of Facial Affect and Interpretation of Social Events
    47→The ability to accurately recognize emotional facial expressions is essential for successful social
    48→interactions. However, individuals who display a bias towards anger or hostility in processing
    49→facial expressions may be more prone to conflict and aggression, particularly those with conduct
    50→problems (Mellentin et al., 2015). In general, individuals with higher trait aggression are more
    51→likely to respond aggressively in ambiguous situations and intentional scenarios compared to
    52→unintentional ones (Tremblay & Belchevski, 2004). This propensity may stem from a tendency
    53→among those high in trait aggression to make hostile attributions (Tremblay & Belchevski, 2004;
    54→Combs et al., 2007) and a reduced inclination to consider contextual cues or integrate non-hostile
    55→information (Wilkowski et al., 2007). Notably, research has shown that violent offenders,
    56→compared to non-violent individuals, exhibit a stronger correlation between perceiving
    57→intentionality and assigning blame to the wrongdoer, known as intentionality/blame isomorphism
    58→(Zajenkowska et al., 2022).
    59→In the research literature, both perceptual and interpretive facets of hostile biases including HA
    60→and HP have been assessed using different methodological approaches, offering a
    61→multidimensional understanding (Smeijers, 2023; Zajenkowska et al., 2025). As studies suggest
    62→that certain interventions may impact specific facets of hostile attribution while leaving others
    63→unaffected (Zajenkowska et al., 2019; Zajenkowska et al., 2021), there is a growing need for
    64→research employing comprehensive, multimodal assessments of HA.
    65→Targeting Hostility and Aggression Through Mentalization-Based Interventions
    66→Deficits in emotional processing and in understanding the intentions of other people are closely
    67→associated with inadequate mentalizing (Bateman & Fonagy, 2016). This is a process by which
    68→people implicitly and explicitly interpret the actions of themselves and others, and it is strongly
    69→related to antisocial tendencies (Bateman & Fonagy, 2008; Jurist et al., 2025) or, more broadly, to
    70→lower levels of personality functioning (Jańczak et al., in press; Rishede et al., 2021; Zettl et al.,
    71→2020). The ability to mentalize plays an extremely important role in everyday life, as it enables the
    72→understanding of social events and the creation of interpersonal bonds.
    73→
    74→Bateman and Fonagy (2008) developed Mentalization-Based Treatment (MBT) to enhance
    75→individuals' capacity to mentalize, particularly under conditions of emotional arousal and
    76→demanding interpersonal context, thereby facilitating effective affect regulation and improved
    77→social functioning. Recent meta-analyses have confirmed the effectiveness of several therapeutic
    78→protocols in reducing anger symptoms, including cognitive bias modification (CBM; Ciesinski et
    79→al., 2023) and mindfulness-based interventions (O'Dean et al., 2025), both of which have also been
    80→shown to reduce hostile attribution biases (Dillon et al., 2020; Schmidt & Vereenooghe, 2021).
    81→A full MBT program shows promise in improving clinical outcomes for adolescents and adults,
    82→particularly those diagnosed with a personality disorder (Simonsen et al., 2025; Volkert et al.,
    83→2019). Typically, MBT involves a combination of individual and group sessions lasting
    84→approximately 1.5 years. However, the initial step usually involves a 12-session group-based
    85→psychoeducational module. Preliminary data have demonstrated several promising effects of the
    86→MBT approach in reducing antisocial features (Drozek et al., 2021; Kasper et al., 2024), including
    87→reduction in HA (Zajenkowska et al., 2021).
    88→Current Study
    89→In this preliminary study, we conducted MBT psychoeducational trainings among inmates,
    90→juvenile delinquents, young adults, and high school students to explore changes associated with
    91→participation in the program by investigating changes in hostile attribution related to emotion
    92→recognition and the interpretation of social events. We employed multiple assessment methods to
    93→capture different facets of HA.
    94→Our hypotheses were that following the MBT psychoeducational module: (1) the number of anger
    95→recognitions (hostile perception-HP) in response to ambiguous facial expressions would be lower;
    96→(2) hostile attributions would also be reduced, meaning that people would ascribe less
    97→intentionality and blame to harm-doers in ambiguous situations, and that the intentionality/blame
    98→isomorphism would also decrease. Considering that hostile attributions are related to antisocial
    99→behaviors, we expected that the change in hostile attributions would be less prominent in inmates
   100→compared to non-inmates.

   102→Method
   103→Participants
   104→Fifty-eight individuals participated in the study, including juveniles, young adults, inmates, and
   105→high school students. Groups did not differ in the number of participants (χ2 = 6.96, p = .073).
   106→Power analysis indicated that at least 52 participants should be included to achieve power = .80, α
   107→= .05, assuming a moderate effect size, f = .20. Juvenile offenders were charged with car theft,
   108→battery, drug possession, extortion and robbery. Adult inmates were convicted for offences
   109→including drunk driving, theft, burglary, domestic violence, robbery, murder and attempted
   110→murder. Table 1 presents detailed demographic information.

   124→Procedure
   125→Volunteers were reached out through institutions interested in cooperating with the researchers
   126→(prison, juvenile prison, high school). Young adults were recruited using the snowball sampling
   127→method. As gratitude for participating, attendees received a voucher worth approximately $15.
   128→Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, a significant part of the project was carried out online; only
   129→meetings with juvenile offenders were conducted entirely in person.
   130→The research procedure consisted of: (I) pre-assessment meeting with an experimenter during
   131→which participants completed questionnaires and computer tasks measuring hostile attributions;
   132→(II) the psychoeducational module delivered in three extended training meetings (each ~2–3
   133→hours), covering the content of the first six sessions of the MBT-I manual; (III) post-assessment
   134→meeting to measure HA after the training. The entire procedure took approximately one month for
   135→each group. The study received ethics committee approval and institutional approvals. Written
   136→parental/guardian consent was obtained for underage participants.
   137→
   138→Psychoeducational Mentalization Training
   139→The intervention was a manualized, short-term psychoeducational group workshop based on the
   140→MBT model, following the guidelines for the first six sessions of the MBT-Introductory Group
   141→(MBT-I; Bateman & Fonagy, 2016). The version used included six sessions delivered in small
   142→groups by a trained group leader. Core topics included: (1) understanding mentalization, (2)
   143→common failures in mentalizing, (3) emotional awareness and regulation, (4) recognizing emotions
   144→in self and others, (5) attachment theory and relationship patterns, and (6) the role of early
   145→relational environments in shaping mentalizing capacity.
   146→Outcome Measures
   147→Hostile Perception. Ambiguous facial expressions (Schönenberg & Jusyte, 2014; Schneidt et al.,
   148→2019): Participants interpreted ambiguous emotional expressions using pictures from the Radboud
   149→Faces Database. Three continuous affective dimensions were created for each model: angry-fearful
   150→(AF), angry-happy (AH), and fearful-happy (FH). The hostility bias index was created by summing
   151→anger recognitions in ambiguous facial expressions (30%, 50%, 70% anger) for male and female
   152→models separately.
   153→Hostile Attributions. Visual scenes (Wilkowski et al., 2007): 27 graphic pictures depicting
   154→ambiguous physically harmful scenes with two actors (harm-doer and harm-receiver). Participants
   155→rated intentionality and blame on 9-point Likert scales for scenes depicting peers and authority
   156→figures.
   157→AIHQ. The Ambiguous Intentions and Hostility Questionnaire (Combs et al., 2007): Five
   158→hypothetical, ambiguous situations involving diverse social relationships. Participants rated
   159→intentionality, anger, and blame using Likert scales, creating a Hostile Attribution Index. Open-
   160→ended questions were analyzed qualitatively. Only adult participants completed the AIHQ.
   161→Self-recalled situations: Participants wrote two situations in which someone behaved toward
   162→them in a way causing negative feelings, then answered the same three questions (intentionality,
   163→blame, anger) as in the AIHQ.
   164→Data Analysis
   165→The basic design was mixed (4 × 2): group (juveniles, inmates, young adults, high school students)
   166→× intervention (pre/post). For hostile perception, this was expanded to 4 × 2 × 3 (anger rate). For
   167→visual scenes: 4 × 2 × 2 (authority). For AIHQ: 3 × 2 × 2 (situation type). Generalized Estimated
   168→Equations (GEE) with Poisson log-linear model were used for count variables (hostile perception);
   169→general linear model with repeated measures was applied for linear variables.

   171→Results
   172→Groups differed substantially in age, gender, and education. The juvenile and inmate groups
   173→included only men. Supplementary analyses indicated that men had higher HA-AIHQ scores
   174→before and after MBT, and older participants had lower AIHQ scores and intentionality ascription.
   175→Hostile Perception
   176→For angry-fearful (AF) expressions, there was a significant group × intervention interaction.
   177→Hostile perception decreased following MBT in juveniles (p = .025, r = .12) and young adults (p
   178→= .022, r = .13), increased in high school students (p = .013, r = .13), and did not change in inmates
   179→(p = .253). Inmates showed the highest hostile perception at baseline. The MBT × Rate interaction
   180→indicated a decrease in anger recognition only for the 70% anger rate (p < .001, r = .19). These
   181→interactions were not significant for AH and FH expressions.

   240→Qualitative Analysis
   241→Qualitative analysis of open-ended AIHQ responses revealed that in scenario 2 (involving an
   242→authority figure), there was a significant shift in attribution categories from t1 to t2 (p = .04).
   243→
   244→Hostile attributions (a1) decreased from 12% to 7%, and fundamental attribution errors (a2)
   245→decreased from 15% to 3%, while external attributions (a3) and mixed motives (a5) increased. For
   246→scenario 4 (friend not showing up), significant changes in declared reactions were observed, with
   247→aggressive reactions (e1, e2) disappearing and non-aggressive, constructive responses (e5)
   248→increasing from 29% to 46%. For the first recalled situation, a significant shift toward non-
   249→aggressive reactions (e5: from 36% to 53%) and away from aggressive ones (e1: from 14% to 3%)
   250→was observed (p = .040).

   252→Discussion
   253→In this preliminary multi-method investigation, we aimed to explore changes associated with
   254→psychoeducational mentalization training across different aspects of hostile attribution. Our
   255→hypotheses were partially confirmed, as changes in HA depended on the type of emotions
   256→presented on the face as well as the type of situations judged by participants.
   257→Hostile perception in angry-fearful facial expressions was reduced following MBT among
   258→juveniles and young adults, whereas an increase was observed among high school students. Among
   259→inmates, no such change was observed, though they exhibited the highest hostile perception at
   260→baseline. The lack of change in hostile perception among inmates may be associated with the
   261→presence of more chronically accessible hostile schemas or difficulties in organizing visual
   262→scanning in the presence of conflicting information (Zajenkowska et al., submitted).
   263→The unexpected increase in hostile perception among high school students may reflect several
   264→factors: adolescent developmental dynamics around aggression, the online delivery format, lower
   265→motivation, or measurement-related artifacts such as regression to the mean. Importantly, the
   266→juvenile offender group of comparable age showed reductions, suggesting that age alone does not
   267→explain this pattern. The in-person delivery of the juvenile intervention versus the online format
   268→for high school students may be relevant.
   269→Regarding hostile attributions in visual scenes, all groups showed greater intentionality and blame
   270→attribution to peers than to authority figures at baseline, consistent with prior research
   271→(Zajenkowska et al., 2018, 2023). Reductions in hostile attributions were observed specifically for
   272→recalled, personally experienced situations—but not for standardized AIHQ scenarios—among
   273→young adults and inmates. This suggests that MBT training was associated with greater changes in
   274→the reappraisal of personal, emotionally charged situations, potentially facilitating better
   275→integration of personal memories with broader contextual information.
   276→Clinical Implications
   277→This study suggests that while inmates may require more intensive MBT programs (Fonagy et al.,
   278→2025), brief MBT-based psychoeducational modules were associated with HA reductions in young
   279→adults and juvenile offenders. These findings align with meta-analytic evidence that HA bias
   280→underlies many aggressive behaviors and that targeting this bias yields clinical benefits (Tuente et
   281→al., 2019). MBT-informed modules are relatively easy to implement, require minimal training, and
   282→can be adapted to diverse institutional settings—offering a potentially safer alternative to
   283→mindfulness-based programs in certain at-risk populations (Montero-Marin et al., 2022).
   284→Limitations
   285→Several methodological limitations should be acknowledged. First, small group sizes may have
   286→limited statistical power to detect subtle effects. Second, groups differed substantially in
   287→demographic variables confounded with group membership. Third, variation in delivery format
   288→(online vs. in-person) due to the COVID-19 pandemic may have influenced engagement and
   289→
   290→outcomes. Fourth, the absence of a control group limits causal inference—observed changes may
   291→reflect passage of time, repeated testing effects, or general social benefits of group participation.
   292→Fifth, systematic monitoring of session attendance was not conducted. Future studies should
   293→include randomized control conditions, larger and more homogeneous samples, consistent delivery
   294→formats, and follow-up assessments.
   295→Conclusion
   296→This preliminary multi-method investigation suggests that patterns of change following the MBT
   297→psychoeducational training differed across participant groups and may be associated with greater
   298→receptiveness to processing and integrating both hostile and non-hostile cues. Such changes may
   299→potentially contribute to a more nuanced interpretation of others' intentions and recognition of
   300→emotions conveyed through facial expressions. These findings suggest the potential value of
   301→tailoring MBT training to the specific characteristics and requirements of individuals with a history
   302→of violence. The multimodal assessment approach introduced here may contribute to more nuanced
   303→evaluations of psychoeducational training targeting hostile attribution as a mechanism of change.
