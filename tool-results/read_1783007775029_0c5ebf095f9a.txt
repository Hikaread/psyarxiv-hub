    23→Abstract
    24→It is becoming common to measure cognition repeatedly to capture dynamic fluctuations over
    25→time. It is currently unknown the extent to which daily deviations in cognitive performance have
    26→implications for real-world behaviors or merely reflect short-term change of limited scope, such
    27→as assessment artifacts. Daily driving behavior was captured in 160 older adults using GPS
    28→tracking across 19,628 individual “trips”, defined as ignition start to ignition off. Automated
    29→processing pipelines quantified various aspects of each trip including distance traveled and
    30→frequency of “adverse” events such as speeding or hard braking. Participants completed daily
    31→assessments of cognition using a remote testing platform. Multilevel negative binomial models
    32→were constructed to relate daily cognitive performance to adverse driving events and adaptive
    33→behavior such as avoidance of nighttime driving, controlling for global ability, participant age,
    34→and distance travelled. Rigorous multi-model inference using AIC-based model averaging
    35→indicated that daily cognitive deviations received substantial model support (79-98%) in
    36→predicting adverse events and nighttime driving, relative to models that did not include a daily
    37→parameter. In contrast, global cognitive ability did not substantially improve prediction of either
    38→outcome. These results show that deviations in cognitive performance are not merely “noise”, as
    39→they have measurable implications for real-world outcomes. Importantly, it was not global
    40→performance that was important but deviations from typical performance. Thus, single-shot
    41→assessments of cognition may not be sufficient to make determinations about driving safety.
    42→More generally, our findings highlight the utility of repeated cognitive assessments in
    43→understanding how cognitive fluctuations may impact real-world behaviors.
    44→Keywords: remote cognitive assessments; cognitive fluctuations, real-world outcomes
    45→Public Significance Statement
    46→This study suggests that momentary drops in cognitive performance may affect everyday
    47→activities, including driving safely. Tracking these moment-to-moment changes could help
    48→identify when someone is at higher risk for adverse outcomes and support safer environments,
    49→especially for individuals who are already at higher risk.

    52→Introduction
    53→Performance on standardized cognitive instruments is frequently used to assess aptitude
    54→for high-risk careers (Draheim et al., 2022). An often-cited example is an air traffic controller
    55→who must monitor multiple sources of input simultaneously and in real-time, which is accepted
    56→as a stressful occupation where failure to perform with high accuracy and consistency may result
    57→in dire consequences. Many other jobs also require sustained attention and stable cognitive
    58→ability to perform efficiently and safely (e.g., workplace safety in construction or manufacturing;
    59→drivers transporting toxic chemicals; decision-making in medical settings, etc.). Thus, a test of
    60→attention, for example, might be a useful screening instrument, and in such situations, the focus
    61→is typically on average performance, where an individual with “high” (better) attention ability
    62→will be more likely to succeed. However, it is increasingly recognized that cognition is not static
    63→and is subject to substantial fluctuations, i.e., deviations from average performance, over short
    64→intervals (Ram et al., 2005; Salthouse, 2007; Schmiedek et al., 2013). These deviations in
    65→cognitive ability (i.e., having a “bad cognitive day”) might contribute to the successful (or not)
    66→completion of a task, which may also have crucial implications for job performance and personal
    67→safety.
    68→While most people do not continuously operate in such overtly high-stakes environments
    69→as the air traffic controller, there are many instances in day-to-day circumstances where cognitive
    70→deviations might be an essential indicator. Older adult driving behavior is a particularly useful
    71→example as driving comprises a complex behavior that many adults engage in routinely, relies
    72→heavily on cognitive processes (including motor and sensory abilities), and is essential for older
    73→adults to maintain independence within their community (Dickerson et al., 2019). More
    74→practically, older adults are at increased risk of fatal crashes (Cox & Cicchino, 2021) and often
    75→
    76→DRIVING AND COGNITION 4
    77→must restrict their driving in various ways, such as avoiding night time driving or rush hour
    78→(Vivoda et al., 2022), to accommodate cognitive or sensory difficulties. Many variables that are
    79→known to affect cognition, including sleep, blood alcohol levels, and some prescription
    80→medications, similarly increase the risk of car crashes (Brubacher et al., 2021; Martiniuk et al.,
    81→2013; Peck et al., 2008). Average performance on standard neuropsychological tests, such as the
    82→Trail Making Test, is useful for assessing driving safety (Carr et al., 2011), but of course, if
    83→cognition naturally fluctuates from day-to-day, driving may be more feasible and safer on certain
    84→days over others. This study seeks to establish whether repeated assessments of cognition have
    85→any utility in determining a safe and successful driving experience on any given day.
    86→Due to recent technological and statistical advances, studies that repeatedly measure
    87→cognitive or psychological processes over short intervals (e.g., several times per day for several
    88→weeks) are becoming common (Aschenbrenner & Jackson, 2023; Crawford et al., 2022; Doherty
    89→et al., 2020; McNeish & Hamaker, 2019). Such study designs, which we refer to as “high-
    90→frequency cognitive assessment”, have many advantages over traditional assessments, including
    91→enhanced reliability of the mean, which is afforded by averaging noisy individual measurements
    92→over a specific epoch (Nicosia et al., 2022; Sliwinski et al., 2018). However, deviations from
    93→average performance may not simply be noise but rather contain meaningful information about
    94→the likelihood of functionally important events. For example, the magnitude of cognitive
    95→deviations across multiple timescales (e.g., moment-to-moment or day-to-day) in various
    96→cognitive tasks has repeatedly been shown to be a predictor of Alzheimer disease (AD) risk
    97→(Aschenbrenner, Hassenstab, et al., 2024; Cerino et al., 2021; Duchek et al., 2009; Hultsch et al.,
    98→2000), long-term cognitive change (Bielak et al., 2010; Lövdén et al., 2007), and even mortality
    99→(Kochan et al., 2017).
   100→
   101→DRIVING AND COGNITION 5
   102→While important, these prior studies are limited as they measure between-person
   103→variability (i.e., individual differences in amount of variability across a week of time) to predict
   104→risk of a specific event (e.g., dementia) which is also a between-persons outcome. Other studies
   105→have utilized high-frequency assessment of other psychological constructs to assess how within-
   106→person deviations in e.g., happiness or stress, influence cognition measured the same day. For
   107→example, experience (Sliwinski et al., 2006) or anticipation (Hyun et al., 2019) of a daily stressor
   108→slows responses on a working memory task, engaging in social interactions improves cognitive
   109→performance on the same day and across several additional days (Zhaoyang et al., 2021), the
   110→diversity of tasks accomplished in a particular day is associated with improved working memory
   111→performance that same day (Luo et al., 2023), and daily happiness ratings are associated with
   112→improved processing speed (Bomyea et al., 2021).
   113→These studies clearly show that momentary experiences (e.g., happiness) are associated
   114→with cognitive performance measured over a similar time interval. However, none of these
   115→studies directly show whether cognitive deviations themselves transfer and have measurable
   116→practical implications for daily function in the real world. Some evidence suggests that deviations
   117→in cognition are associated with real-world outcomes. For example, variability across individual
   118→trials in an attention task predicted the number of self-reported real-world cognitive failures (e.g.,
   119→forgetting an appointment or excessive distractedness) in a daily diary study (Unsworth, 2015).
   120→One study has shown that variation on standard cognitive instruments (e.g., list learning tests,
   121→digit symbol substitution) across 8 testing sessions over 3 weeks covaried with fluctuation in the
   122→Daily Everyday Cognition Assessment, a cognitive measure that uses real-world stimuli (e.g.,
   123→medication use, financial management, etc.) over the same period (Gamaldo & Allaire, 2016).
   124→
   125→DRIVING AND COGNITION 6
   126→These prior studies, while informative, are also limited as the “real-world” measures are
   127→based either on self-report (such as a daily diary study) or are similar to cognitive assessments in
   128→the first place (e.g., Daily Everyday Cognitive Assessment). None reflect objective real-world
   129→activities and currently it is unclear if deviations seen in repeated cognitive assessments reflect a
   130→momentary lapse in attention, mood or motivation, or reflect meaningful changes in how one
   131→engages with the environment. Put simply, it is unknown whether deviations from average
   132→cognitive performance on any given day are associated with real-world behaviors on that same
   133→day. To answer this question, what is needed is a demonstration that daily cognitive performance
   134→is associated with objectively measured outcomes, which possess direct implications for real-
   135→world behavior.
   136→The present study aims to combine a high-frequency cognitive assessment paradigm with
   137→daily measurements of real-world driving behaviors to determine if deviations from typical
   138→cognitive ability directly translate to impaired performance on a functionally important
   139→behavioral outcome. We ask two questions in the current study: 1) Does poor cognition on a
   140→given day (deviations from average cognition) predict risky driving performance that day
   141→(defined as the presence of adverse driving events)? 2) Does poor cognition on a given day
   142→(deviations from average cognition) predict a decrease in the individual’s driving space
   143→(frequency of driving at night) that day?

   160→Participants: Data for the present analyses were collated from two separate research studies,
   161→each of which have standardized data processing pipelines to ensure consistent data treatment
   162→across participants. Standard outcomes from each of these studies have been previously
   163→published (Aschenbrenner et al., 2022; Bayat et al., 2021; Nicosia et al., 2022). The two studies

   168→cognitive assessment study in healthy and very mildly impaired older adults using smartphone

   188→DRIVES data collection and outcomes: Upon enrollment in DRIVES, a commercial datalogger
   189→(Azuga G2 Tracking device) was installed in each participant’s personal vehicle. The datalogger
   190→continuously collects various driving metrics for each driving “trip”, where a trip is defined as
   191→ignition on to ignition off (Babulal, Addison, et al., 2016; Babulal, Traub, et al., 2016). In total,
   192→there were 19,628 individual trips examined in this study and on average each person took 123
   193→trips (SD = 74). Some variables captured on each trip include the average speed, locations visited
   194→(in latitude and longitude), and specific events such as hard braking or speeding. Although a
   195→large number of driving variables are available (Bayat et al., 2021), for the purposes of the
   196→present study, we were interested in two daily metrics: “driving space” and “driving
   197→performance”, which we have used in prior studies to define meaningful subcomponents of
   198→driving behavior (Aschenbrenner et al., 2022; Bayat et al., 2021). As not all variables that are
   199→

   201→typically used to summarize driving behaviors are available at the daily level due to
   202→heterogeneity of driving patterns across participants, for the current study we defined the
   203→“driving space” variable as “number of night trips”, and “driving performance” as any
   204→occurrence of adverse events such as hard braking (deceleration greater than 8 miles per hour in
   205→one second), sudden acceleration (acceleration greater than 8 miles per hour in one second), or
   206→speeding (a speed greater than 6 miles per hour above the posted speed limit, Aschenbrenner et
   207→al., 2022). These variables are linked to the risk of vehicle crashes (Simons-Morton et al., 2012)
   208→and are related to pathological change over time (Doherty et al., 2023), and thus are useful
   209→descriptors of how well and how safely an individual is able to drive. The three indicators of
   210→performance were summed to form a single “driving performance” variable. There were 10,025
   211→adverse events observed during the study with an average of 63 events per person (SD = 67) and
   212→an average of 2.3 adverse events per day (SD = 2.0).
   213→ARC data collection and outcomes: Upon enrollment in ARC, participants downloaded a
   214→custom-built smartphone application that administers three, very brief cognitive tests at pseudo-
   215→random intervals four times per day for one week. The three tests include “Symbols”, where
   216→participants are presented with sets of abstract shapes and must find the matching items (primary
   217→outcome = seconds to make a correct response), “Grids”, where participants view three common
   218→household objects in a 5x5 grid and must place them back in the appropriate location after a brief
   219→delay (primary outcome = Euclidean distance score that reflects the difference between the
   220→selected and correct locations), and “Prices” where participants view 10 shopping items and
   221→prices and must select the correct price on an immediate memory test (primary outcome =
   222→percent errors). Outcomes from each test are scored such that higher is worse and for the
   223→purposes of the present analyses were z-scored to the mean and standard deviation of the initial
   224→
   225→DRIVING AND COGNITION 10
   226→evaluation of a larger sample of ARC participants (Nicosia et al., 2022) and averaged together to
   227→form an ARC composite score (ICC = 0.54). The weekly assessment “bursts” are repeated at 6-
   228→month intervals for the duration of the study. As ARC is currently ongoing, we used a data freeze
   229→of the ARC database in June 2024. In total, we examined performance from 12,960 individual
   230→ARC assessments and each participant completed, on average, 81 assessments over the course of
   231→the study (SD = 41). The average number of weeks completed per person was 5.9 (SD = 1.7,
   232→range = 2-9). The average adherence (defined as the number of ARC assessments completed
   233→divided by the total number of ARC assessment possible [4 assessments per day for 7 days for
   234→however many weeks they continued in the study]) across the entire study duration was 80% (SD

   237→days, all 4 ARC assessments were completed, 28% of the days 3 assessments were completed,

   239→averages across individual participants, and thus within an individual the percentages will sum to
   240→100%, but the group averages will not.
   241→Data Matching: Driving performance and driving space metrics from the DRIVES study are
   242→automatically tabulated at the daily level, and the ARC composite scores were averaged to form
   243→a daily ARC score (comprised of up to 4 testing sessions per day) to keep the assessments over
   244→the same interval (i.e., at the daily level). The data were then matched by calendar date so that a
   245→daily ARC score was paired with the number of adverse driving events, the number of total trips
   246→taken, and the number of night trips that occurred that same day.
   247→Statistical Analysis: The core of our analysis utilizes multilevel, negative binomial regression
   248→modeling in which an outcome (count of adverse events or number of night trips; each in
   249→
   250→DRIVING AND COGNITION 11
   251→separate models) was predicted by one or more of the following variables: total distance traveled
   252→on that day, age at enrollment in the study (centered at age 65), sex (dummy coded with “female”
   253→as the reference group), global CDR rating, “time” (defined as years since enrollment in ARC),
   254→and “cognition” which was defined in two ways. First, we calculated a “global cognitive score”
   255→which was simply the average of the cognitive tests across all available assessments. This was
   256→intended to reflect individuals who are relatively high or relatively low performers overall. We
   257→also created a person-centered “daily deviation” score, defined as the cognitive composite score
   258→on a given day subtracted from the person level global cognition score. This latter variable
   259→reflects how well a given day’s performance was compared to the individual’s average
   260→performance and is the focal predictor of interest in the current report. The creation of person-
   261→centered variables in this fashion allows for separation of between person (some people have
   262→better cognition than others) and within-person (individuals perform better on certain days than
   263→others) variance (Hoffman & Stawski, 2009). Random effects included an intercept across
   264→participants and a random slope of time. The days on which no trips occurred (and hence no
   265→adverse events or night trips were recorded) were not included in any models. We confirmed the
   266→adequacy of our model by comparing different models of varying complexity. Specifically, we
   267→compared a negative binomial model, a zero-inflated negative binomial model, a Poisson model
   268→and a zero-inflated Poisson model. For all outcomes, the negative binomial model (with no zero-
   269→inflation) produced the smallest (and hence best) AICc, and thus we retained that model for all
   270→subsequent analyses.
   271→We were motivated by the desire to avoid reliance on null hypothesis significance testing
   272→and to simultaneously quantify the strength of evidence of a given effect (cognitive deviations)
   273→as well as to account for multiple levels of uncertainty. Therefore, we employed multimodel
   274→
   275→DRIVING AND COGNITION 12
   276→inference (Burnham & Anderson, 2004a) to incorporate uncertainty in model structure and
   277→selection and parameter estimation. Specifically, for each outcome (adverse events and number
   278→of night trips) we constructed a set of 5 plausible candidate models (see Table 1) that were each
   279→analyzed with a negative binomial model. These models were compared to one another using
   280→corrected Akaike Information Criteria and Akaike weights (Burnham & Anderson, 2004b) which
   281→provide a method to rank the relative plausibility of each model given the observed data.
   282→Regression coefficients from the different models were then averaged together with each
   283→coefficient weighted based on the relative plausibility of that model, thereby accounting for
   284→uncertainty in model structure. We use the model averaged result to report an effect size for our
   285→focal predictor (cognitive deviations) consisting of the incidence rate ratio (IRR), which
   286→represents the increase in the outcome associated with a 1 SD increase in the cognitive
   287→deviations. As they are not of central interest, non-focal predictors (e.g., age, sex) are
   288→summarized based on the best fitting model only (i.e., not based on model averaging to reduce
   289→computational complexity). Cognitive scores were scaled so that higher equals worse
   290→performance; hence a “positive” daily deviation indicates worse cognition than average and
   291→therefore a positive IRR would indicate a poor cognitive day is associated with worse driving
   292→outcomes. Negative binomial models were fit using the glmmTMB package version 1.1.11
   293→(McGillycuddy et al., 2025) and AIC comparisons with AICcmodavg version 2.3.3 (Mazerolle,
   294→2023) in R version 4.5.0 (R Core Team, 2021)

   335→Discussion
   336→This study aimed to demonstrate whether short-term deviations in scores on a sensitive
   337→cognitive outcome are associated with real-world behavior. In line with our research questions,
   338→there are three critical takeaways from these results. First, high-frequency cognitive testing can
   339→provide information about practically relevant daily events in the real-world, specifically adverse
   340→driving events and proclivity to avoid driving at night. Second, it was not global performance
   341→that was important in these analyses; it was deviations from that global performance. Again, this
   342→highlights the value of repeated testing at regular intervals that can capture deviations from
   343→typical levels, so-called “off days”. Finally, the fact that daily deviations is associated with night
   344→time driving suggests that participants are at least partially adapting their behaviors to match
   345→their cognitive ability at the daily level. More broadly, these findings continue to support the
   346→notion that short-term deviations from typical cognitive functioning are not noise in cognitive
   347→assessment such as mind wandering during the task or getting interrupted by someone while
   348→taking the test in a non-standardized environment, but rather reflect deviations from typical
   349→
   350→DRIVING AND COGNITION 15
   351→performance that translates outside of the test taking environment into a meaningful, objective,
   352→real-world outcome. We discuss each of these issues in turn.
   353→First, in terms of driving performance, overall cognitive ability did not predict adverse
   354→driving events such that lower-performing individuals were just as likely to experience adverse
   355→events during driving as higher-performing participants. However, during a “low-performing”
   356→day, when cognitive scores that day were worse than the participants’ average, the incidence of
   357→adverse events was increased. This conclusion was based on a rigorous multimodal inference
   358→approach to account for various levels of uncertainty. These within-person results confirm that
   359→daily deviations in cognitive performance have detectable implications for real-world driving
   360→behavior. Previous studies have shown that momentary variation in key psychological constructs
   361→(e.g., stress, mood, happiness) is associated with deviations in cognitive performance (Bomyea et
   362→al., 2021; Hyun et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023; Zhaoyang et al., 2021), but no studies have directly
   363→shown that these deviations in cognition can themselves have measurable and practical
   364→implications for real-world outcomes. Notably, because the cognitive testing and driving
   365→assessments were asynchronous (i.e., participants were not taking the testing at the exact same
   366→time as driving) the influence of poor cognition extends beyond momentary distractions such as
   367→brief inattentiveness while responding to a text message or checking phone notifications. Rather,
   368→poor performance on cognitive testing has implications for driving behavior that extend for at
   369→least an entire day.
   370→It was surprising to see that the global cognitive score was not associated with adverse
   371→events. Individuals were equally likely to experience adverse events regardless of their overall
   372→levels of cognitive ability. We believe these findings confirm other studies in the literature that
   373→show cognitively healthy older adults can readily adapt or self-regulate their driving behaviors to
   374→
   375→DRIVING AND COGNITION 16
   376→match their cognitive and / or sensory abilities (Meng & Siren, 2012). For example, we have
   377→shown that individuals with relatively low attentional control ability significantly reduce their
   378→“driving space” (e.g., number of trips taken, number of trips at night) over time compared to
   379→individuals with higher attention scores (Aschenbrenner et al., 2022). We argue that the current
   380→results are another reflection of this adaptive behavior. That is, a typically low-performing driver
   381→may choose to avoid certain driving situations that minimizes the likelihood of adverse events
   382→occurring, for example choosing to avoid driving on freeways during rush hour. However, when
   383→there is a short-term deviation in their performance (i.e., having a bad cognitive day), their
   384→typical adaptations may no longer be sufficient to prevent adverse events. Alternatively,
   385→adaptation of driving behavior likely takes time to implement. Avoidance of the freeway for
   386→example is not an immediate decision but rather a gradual adjustment over years. Thus, driving
   387→adaptations that are typically studied in the literature may not be sufficient to guard against the
   388→relatively rapid changes in cognition that occur from day-to-day.
   389→This interpretation is partially supported by the effects on the nighttime driving metric. If an
   390→individual is somewhat aware that they are not performing at their normal cognitive levels, they
   391→may adjust their behavior to avoid the highest risk driving scenarios, e.g., driving at night. There
   392→is of course a limit on how much behavior modification is possible at the daily level, as doctor’s
   393→visits, grocery trips, and grandchildren’s extracurricular activities all occur regardless of one’s
   394→cognitive performance and hence a bad cognitive day may not be sufficient to overcome these
   395→other societal factors and limit driving behaviors, which may be contributing to our relatively
   396→small effect sizes.
   397→There are many strengths to this study including the combination of two innovative, high-
   398→frequency research programs that objectively and remotely measure driving behaviors on the
   399→
   400→DRIVING AND COGNITION 17
   401→same day as measures of cognition are collected. Nevertheless, some limitations merit mention.
   402→First, our sample consisted of highly educated individuals who were predominately non-Hispanic
   403→White, and the generalizability of our findings to a broader segment of the population, who may
   404→have different driving habits, is uncertain. It is an ongoing endeavor of both DRIVES and ARC
   405→to increase the representation of the participants in our sample. Second, we examined only
   406→adverse events defined as hard braking, sudden acceleration or speeding. More fine-grained
   407→metrics of driving performance or longer follow-up may produce different findings. Third, we
   408→did not preregister this study hence there is an inherent risk of bias in the data. We have
   409→mitigated this by making our data and code fully accessible as well as only defining variables
   410→that have been operationalized in our prior publications (Aschenbrenner et al., 2022; Nicosia et
   411→al., 2022). Finally, it should be emphasized that the overall effect size of the cognitive variables
   412→was small. As noted, a one standard deviation change in cognition increases adverse events by
   413→about 4% and decreases nighttime driving by 16%. These small effect sizes could in part be due
   414→to our conservative approach to making inferences with these data. Specifically, we performed
   415→model averaging whereby coefficients were averaged across all examined models in Table 1
   416→weighted by the degree of evidence that each model contributed.
   417→Nevertheless, regardless of estimation technique, all returned IRRs can be considered small.
   418→On one hand, this may imply our results are trivial and unimportant, especially if the goal is to
   419→determine “fitness to drive” on any given day. However, when considered within the zeitgeist of
   420→repeated assessments of psychological phenomena (McNeish & Hamaker, 2019), these results
   421→could be considered quite important. Specifically, these results demonstrate that repeated
   422→assessments do not simply capture fluctuations of internal states (e.g., mood, affect, cognition)
   423→but that these variations translate into functional implications, in our case driving. We consider
   424→
   425→DRIVING AND COGNITION 18
   426→this a critical “proof of concept” study that demonstrates the functional importance of daily
   427→variations in cognition, and we hope this will spur additional research into other functional
   428→domains such as risk of falling, financial decision making, or medication management.
   429→Moreover, it is essential to point out that our sample primarily consisted of clinically healthy
   430→adults with no evidence of cognitive impairment. Our participants are also very conscientious,
   431→which have implications for driving performance and could in part contribute to the overall low
   432→rate of adverse events in our study (Aschenbrenner, Carr, et al., 2024). The results shown here
   433→could be significantly larger in “at-risk” groups (e.g., those with clinical impairment, lower
   434→conscientiousness, recovering from stroke, surgery, TBI or any number of other medical events)
   435→or over longer periods of time than the one-week assessments analyzed here.
   436→Conclusions
   437→The ability to drive independently is critical to older adults’ social mobility and mental
   438→functioning. Determinations of driving ability are typically made using standard cognitive
   439→instruments or standardized road tests on single occasions. Our study shows that cognition, and
   440→its ability to predict driving behavior, changes from day-to-day. Repeated, brief cognitive testing
   441→may ultimately provide helpful information on making a daily determination of driving safety.
