    50‚Üí    50‚Üí    50‚Üíand emotional memory, distinguishing carefully between what existing evidence demonstrates
    51‚Üí    51‚Üí    51‚Üíand what this paper proposes. Third, it positions identity continuity as a clinically meaningful
    52‚Üí    52‚Üí    52‚Üítarget and develops the communication-theoretic foundations that explain why familiar voice
    53‚Üí    53‚Üí    53‚Üídiffers from generic auditory stimulation. The VOCES Framework is then presented as an
    54‚Üí    54‚Üí    54‚Üíintegrated model, followed by an applied illustration, a discussion of practical applications across
    55‚Üí    55‚Üí    55‚Üícare contexts, and recommendations for empirical evaluation. Throughout, the framework is
    56‚Üí    56‚Üí    56‚Üíoffered as a conceptual synthesis grounded in existing evidence rather than as a validated clinical
    57‚Üí    57‚Üí    57‚Üíintervention.
    58‚Üí    58‚Üí    58‚ÜíThe Problem Landscape
    59‚Üí    59‚Üí    59‚Üí
    60‚Üí    60‚Üí    60‚ÜíDemographic Context
    61‚Üí    61‚Üí    61‚ÜíDemographic aging is a worldwide phenomenon, reflecting decades of gains in life
    62‚Üí    62‚Üí    62‚Üíexpectancy and a corresponding rise in age-related cognitive conditions. The World Health
    63‚Üí    63‚Üí    63‚ÜíOrganization (WHO, 2025) estimates that approximately 57 million people were living with
    64‚Üí    64‚Üí    64‚Üídementia worldwide in 2021, with nearly 10 million new cases each year and more than 60% of
    65‚Üí    65‚Üí    65‚Üíaffected individuals residing in low- and middle-income countries. Alzheimer disease is the most
    66‚Üí    66‚Üí    66‚Üícommon cause, contributing an estimated 60% to 70% of cases, and dementia now ranks among
    67‚Üí    67‚Üí    67‚Üíthe leading causes of death and of disability and dependency among older adults globally (WHO,
    68‚Üí    68‚Üí    68‚Üí2025). Mild cognitive impairment, which may precede dementia, further expands the population
    69‚Üí    69‚Üí    69‚Üíthat could benefit from early, identity-preserving support.
    70‚Üí    70‚Üí    70‚ÜíThese trends carry particular significance because the majority of early cognitive support
    71‚Üí    71‚Üí    71‚Üíoccurs outside formal clinical settings. The WHO (2025) estimates that informal caregivers, most
    72‚Üí    72‚Üí    72‚Üíoften family members, provide on average several hours of care per day and account for roughly
    73‚Üí    73‚Üí    73‚Üíhalf of the global economic cost of dementia. Increasingly, older adults remain in home
    74‚Üí    74‚Üí    74‚Üíenvironments, relying on caregivers who may lack structured psychosocial tools to support
    75‚Üí    75‚Üí    75‚Üíidentity continuity. The scale and distribution of this need motivate interest in approaches that
    76‚Üí    76‚Üí    76‚Üíare low in technological complexity, culturally adaptable, and feasible within everyday relational
    77‚Üí    77‚Üí    77‚Üícontexts.
    78‚Üí    78‚Üí    78‚ÜíEmotional and Relational Disruption
    79‚Üí    79‚Üí    79‚ÜíEarly cognitive decline affects narrative coherence and relational stability. As
    80‚Üí    80‚Üí    80‚Üíautobiographical memory becomes less accessible, identity markers tied to roles,
    81‚Üí    81‚Üí    81‚Üíaccomplishments, and shared experiences may weaken, and family members frequently report
    82‚Üí    82‚Üí    82‚Üíemotional strain as familiar communication patterns shift. The disruption is therefore not solely
    83‚Üí    83‚Üí    83‚Üí
    84‚Üí    84‚Üí    84‚Üíneurological; it is relational. Boss (1999, 2011) characterized this experience as ambiguous loss,
    85‚Üí    85‚Üí    85‚Üía form of loss without closure in which a loved one is simultaneously present and
    86‚Üí    86‚Üí    86‚Üípsychologically altered, and which is associated with sustained, unresolved grief among
    87‚Üí    87‚Üí    87‚Üícaregivers. Recognizing the relational dimension of decline reframes the goal of early support:
    88‚Üí    88‚Üí    88‚Üíalongside cognitive maintenance, interventions may aim to sustain connection, mutual
    89‚Üí    89‚Üí    89‚Üírecognition, and continuity of relationship.
    90‚Üí    90‚Üí    90‚ÜíLimitations of Current Interventions
    91‚Üí    91‚Üí    91‚ÜíExisting psychosocial interventions frequently prioritize cognition and orientation.
    92‚Üí    92‚Üí    92‚ÜíCognitive stimulation has demonstrated modest benefits for cognitive function (Aguirre et al.,
    93‚Üí    93‚Üí    93‚Üí2013), and reminiscence therapy, which uses tangible prompts to evoke memories and stimulate
    94‚Üí    94‚Üí    94‚Üíconversation, has shown small and somewhat inconsistent effects on cognition, mood,
    95‚Üí    95‚Üí    95‚Üícommunication, and quality of life that vary by modality and setting (Woods et al., 2018). These
    96‚Üí    96‚Üí    96‚Üíapproaches are valuable, yet they often emphasize performance outcomes rather than identity
    97‚Üí    97‚Üí    97‚Üípreservation, and many lack a structured mechanism for reinforcing continuity of self as decline
    98‚Üí    98‚Üí    98‚Üíprogresses. Emotionally meaningful auditory engagement through familiar voices remains
    99‚Üí    99‚Üí    99‚Üícomparatively underdeveloped as a deliberate intervention strategy. The framework advanced
   100‚Üí   100‚Üí   100‚Üíhere is intended to complement, rather than replace, existing models by foregrounding relational
   101‚Üí   101‚Üí   101‚Üívoice as an explicit, structured target.
   102‚Üí   102‚Üí   102‚ÜíThe Science of Voice and Memory
   103‚Üí   103‚Üí   103‚ÜíCognitive decline does not affect all neural and psychological systems uniformly. While
   104‚Üí   104‚Üí   104‚Üíepisodic and declarative recall often diminish early, several lines of evidence suggest that
   105‚Üí   105‚Üí   105‚Üíaffective and auditory processes associated with familiar people may remain comparatively
   106‚Üí   106‚Üí   106‚Üí
   107‚Üí   107‚Üí   107‚Üíaccessible. This section reviews that evidence across three areas, then carefully distinguishes the
   108‚Üí   108‚Üí   108‚Üíestablished findings from the conceptual proposition this paper advances.
   109‚Üí   109‚Üí   109‚ÜíAuditory Cognition and Familiar Voice Processing
   110‚Üí   110‚Üí   110‚ÜíThe human voice conveys a rich array of information beyond linguistic content, including
   111‚Üí   111‚Üí   111‚Üícues to a speaker's identity and emotional state, which listeners perceive with notable ease (Belin
   112‚Üí   112‚Üí   112‚Üíet al., 2000). Using functional neuroimaging, Belin and colleagues identified voice-selective
   113‚Üí   113‚Üí   113‚Üíregions along the superior temporal sulcus, sometimes termed the temporal voice areas, that
   114‚Üí   114‚Üí   114‚Üírespond preferentially to vocal sounds relative to other complex sounds. The voice of someone
   115‚Üí   115‚Üí   115‚Üíknown appears to hold a privileged status within this system: personally familiar voices elicit
   116‚Üí   116‚Üí   116‚Üímore finely resolved neural response patterns than voices learned briefly in a laboratory or never
   117‚Üí   117‚Üí   117‚Üíencountered before, consistent with the view that extensive and varied exposure yields richer
   118‚Üí   118‚Üí   118‚Üírepresentations of the people we know (Kanber et al., 2025).
   119‚Üí   119‚Üí   119‚ÜíAt the same time, voice processing is not uniformly spared in cognitive decline.
   120‚Üí   120‚Üí   120‚ÜíSystematic investigation of neurodegenerative populations indicates that components of voice
   121‚Üí   121‚Üí   121‚Üíperception and voice recognition can be differentially affected in dementia, including in
   122‚Üí   122‚Üí   122‚ÜíAlzheimer disease (Hailstone et al., 2011). This nuance is important for the present framework,
   123‚Üí   123‚Üí   123‚Üías it cautions against assuming that intact recognition of a familiar speaker is the operative
   124‚Üí   124‚Üí   124‚Üímechanism. The relevant implication of this section is therefore conceptual rather than
   125‚Üí   125‚Üí   125‚Üídiagnostic: the voice is not a generic auditory stimulus but a carrier of identity and affect, and the
   126‚Üí   126‚Üí   126‚Üíframework's emphasis falls on the affective and relational salience of familiar voice rather than
   127‚Üí   127‚Üí   127‚Üíon preserved voice-identity recognition as such. On this view, familiar relational voice may
   128‚Üí   128‚Üí   128‚Üíremain a meaningful channel for engagement even as specific voice-processing abilities change.
   129‚Üí   129‚Üí   129‚ÜíEmotional Encoding and Amygdala-Hippocampal Interactions
   130‚Üí   130‚Üí   130‚Üí
   131‚Üí   131‚Üí   131‚ÜíEmotional memory depends in part on interactions between the amygdala and
   132‚Üí   132‚Üí   132‚Üíhippocampus that contribute to the encoding and retrieval of affectively charged experience. A
   133‚Üí   133‚Üí   133‚Üísubstantial body of research indicates that the amygdala modulates the consolidation of
   134‚Üí   134‚Üí   134‚Üímemories for emotionally arousing experiences, strengthening the retention of affectively
   135‚Üí   135‚Üí   135‚Üísignificant information (McGaugh, 2004). Mechanistic work in animal models has begun to
   136‚Üí   136‚Üí   136‚Üíspecify how this may occur, suggesting that coordinated amygdalo-cortical activity, including
   137‚Üí   137‚Üí   137‚Üíduring sleep-dependent consolidation, enhances memory for emotionally associated material and
   138‚Üí   138‚Üí   138‚Üíunderscores the role of inter-regional dialogue in prioritizing emotional memories (Saito et al.,
   139‚Üí   139‚Üí   139‚Üí2025). In Alzheimer disease, the hippocampus is typically affected early, impairing declarative
   140‚Üí   140‚Üí   140‚Üímemory, whereas evidence suggests that amygdala-mediated affective processing may make
   141‚Üí   141‚Üí   141‚Üídistinct and partially dissociable contributions to emotion and memory (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al.,
   142‚Üí   142‚Üí   142‚Üí2016). This dissociation is theoretically important: it suggests that the systems supporting factual
   143‚Üí   143‚Üí   143‚Üírecall and those supporting emotional responsiveness may decline along different trajectories,
   144‚Üí   144‚Üí   144‚Üíleaving affective pathways relatively engaged even as declarative detail becomes less accessible.
   145‚Üí   145‚Üí   145‚ÜíRelative Preservation of Emotional Experience in Cognitive Decline
   146‚Üí   146‚Üí   146‚ÜíDirect evidence for the persistence of emotion beyond memory comes from studies of
   147‚Üí   147‚Üí   147‚Üípatients with hippocampal damage and with Alzheimer disease. In a study of individuals with
   148‚Üí   148‚Üí   148‚Üísevere amnesia, induced feelings of sadness and happiness persisted well after participants had
   149‚Üí   149‚Üí   149‚Üílost factual memory for the films that produced them (Feinstein et al., 2010). This phenomenon
   150‚Üí   150‚Üí   150‚Üíwas subsequently demonstrated in Alzheimer disease: patients continued to experience
   151‚Üí   151‚Üí   151‚Üíprolonged emotional states even after their declarative memory for the eliciting events had faded,
   152‚Üí   152‚Üí   152‚Üíand, strikingly, weaker memory was associated with longer-lasting emotion (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et
   153‚Üí   153‚Üí   153‚Üíal., 2014).
   154‚Üí   154‚Üí   154‚Üí
   155‚Üí   155‚Üí   155‚ÜíPersonally meaningful auditory stimuli appear especially capable of evoking such
   156‚Üí   156‚Üí   156‚Üíresponses, and music has served as an instructive model case. Familiar music can cue
   157‚Üí   157‚Üí   157‚Üíautobiographical memories that are engaged through a distributed neural network including
   158‚Üí   158‚Üí   158‚Üímedial prefrontal cortex (Janata, 2009), and in Alzheimer disease such music-evoked
   159‚Üí   159‚Üí   159‚Üíautobiographical memories may be retained and tend to carry a positive emotional tone, a pattern
   160‚Üí   160‚Üí   160‚Üídescribed as a positivity effect (Cuddy et al., 2017). Brief exposure to familiar music has
   161‚Üí   161‚Üí   161‚Üílikewise been shown to induce lingering emotion in individuals with Alzheimer disease
   162‚Üí   162‚Üí   162‚Üí(Reschke-Hern√°ndez et al., 2020). Although these findings concern music rather than voice, they
   163‚Üí   163‚Üí   163‚Üíillustrate a broader principle relevant to the present framework: personally meaningful auditory
   164‚Üí   164‚Üí   164‚Üístimuli can engage emotion and autobiographical content even when declarative recall is
   165‚Üí   165‚Üí   165‚Üícompromised. Relational voice is proposed here as a parallel and potentially complementary
   166‚Üí   166‚Üí   166‚Üíchannel, distinguished by its direct connection to attachment relationships, a proposition that
   167‚Üí   167‚Üí   167‚Üírequires direct empirical testing rather than extrapolation from the music literature alone.
   168‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚ÜíFrom Evidence to Proposition: Voice as a Cognitive Anchor
   169‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚ÜíIt is important to delineate precisely what the foregoing literature establishes and what
   170‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üíthis paper proposes. Existing research demonstrates, among other findings, that the brain
   171‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚Üícontains voice-selective processing regions (Belin et al., 2000); that affective and declarative
   172‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üísystems may decline along partially dissociable paths (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2016); and that
   173‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üíemotional experience can persist after the memory of its cause has faded, including in Alzheimer
   174‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚Üídisease (Feinstein et al., 2010; Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2014). What this paper proposes, as its
   175‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üíprimary conceptual contribution, is an integrative interpretation: that familiar relational voice
   176‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üímay function as a cognitive anchor, an emotionally salient, identity-linked cue capable of
   177‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üísupporting emotional continuity, relational recognition, and a sense of orientation even as
   178‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚Üí
   179‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚Üíepisodic memory declines. This proposition is a synthesis of existing evidence into a new
   180‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚Üíframework; it is hypothesized rather than demonstrated, and it does not assert that voice
   181‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚Üíengagement alters neuropathology or disease progression. The conceptual leap is identified
   182‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚Üíexplicitly so that the framework's empirical status is not overstated.
   183‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚ÜíIdentity Continuity as a Clinical Priority
   184‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚ÜíIf emotionally salient relational cues remain accessible in early decline, then identity
   185‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üícontinuity, rather than recall accuracy alone, becomes a meaningful target for intervention. This
   186‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üísection develops the theoretical basis for treating identity as a clinical priority, drawing on
   187‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚Üínarrative identity, personhood and person-centered care, attachment theory, and the stabilizing
   188‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üírole of ritual.
   189‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚ÜíNarrative Identity and Self-Continuity
   190‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚ÜíContemporary psychology conceptualizes identity in substantial part as a story. Narrative
   191‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üíidentity refers to a person's internalized and evolving life story, which integrates the
   192‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üíreconstructed past and the imagined future to provide a sense of unity and purpose (McAdams &
   193‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚ÜíMcLean, 2013). On this view, selfhood is sustained not by the perfect retention of facts but by
   194‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚Üíthe ongoing integration of experience into a coherent narrative. Early cognitive decline can
   195‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üífragment this autobiographical integration, destabilizing self-concept and relational roles. The
   196‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚Üíframework proposed here suggests that reinforcing the affective and relational threads of a
   197‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚Üíperson's narrative, through familiar voice that affirms roles, relationships, and personal history,
   198‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üímay help sustain a sense of continuity even when factual recall is impaired. Continuity, in this
   199‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üísense, is conceptualized as sustained recognition of who a person is, rather than as flawless recall
   200‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚Üíof what a person did.
   201‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚ÜíPersonhood, Dignity, and Person-Centered Care
   202‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üí
   203‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚ÜíKitwood's (1997) account of personhood reoriented dementia care away from a purely
   204‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üíbiomedical model toward the recognition of the person. Personhood, in Kitwood's formulation, is
   205‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üía status conferred on one human being by others within relationship and social being, and it is
   206‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚Üísustained or undermined through the quality of interpersonal interaction (Kitwood, 1997;
   207‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚ÜíKitwood & Bredin, 1992). Among the psychological needs Kitwood identified, comfort,
   208‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚Üíattachment, identity, occupation, and inclusion are bound together by the centrality of
   209‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üírelationship. Subsequent work has elaborated person-centered care as an approach that values the
   210‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üíperson, treats individuals as unique, adopts their perspective, and provides a supportive social
   211‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üíenvironment (Brooker, 2007), and has shown that self and identity can be actively maintained
   212‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üíthrough social interaction even amid significant impairment (Sabat & Harr√©, 1992). Within this
   213‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üítradition, sustaining identity is not peripheral to care; it is the core ethical aim. The present
   214‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üíframework aligns with and operationalizes this aim by treating relational voice as a structured
   215‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚Üímeans of affirming personhood.
   216‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚ÜíRelational Identity and Attachment
   217‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚ÜíIdentity in cognitive decline is profoundly relational, and attachment theory offers a lens
   218‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üífor understanding why familiar figures carry such weight. Bowlby's (1969) account of
   219‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üíattachment as an enduring affectional bond has been extended to later life and dementia, where
   220‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üíproximity-seeking and attachment behaviors can intensify under conditions of uncertainty and
   221‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚Üíperceived insecurity (Miesen, 1993; Nelis et al., 2014). From this perspective, the familiar voice
   222‚Üí   222‚Üí   222‚Üíof an attachment figure is not merely a recognizable sound but a signal of safety and connection,
   223‚Üí   223‚Üí   223‚Üícapable of providing a secure base during disorientation. The framework proposed here draws on
   224‚Üí   224‚Üí   224‚Üíthis insight to suggest that relational voice may stabilize emotional states in part through its
   225‚Üí   225‚Üí   225‚Üíattachment significance, a possibility that warrants empirical examination.
   226‚Üí   226‚Üí   226‚Üí
   227‚Üí   227‚Üí   227‚ÜíRitual, Predictability, and Stabilization
   228‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚ÜíRitualized engagement introduces predictability within the context of cognitive
   229‚Üí   229‚Üí   229‚Üíuncertainty. As the reliability of memory fluctuates, repeated and structured sensory experiences
   230‚Üí   230‚Üí   230‚Üímay provide psychological grounding, reframing memory support from performance assessment
   231‚Üí   231‚Üí   231‚Üítoward identity reinforcement. Ritual, in this sense, functions not as routine alone but as a
   232‚Üí   232‚Üí   232‚Üístabilizing container through which continuity is reaffirmed. Embedding relational voice within
   233‚Üí   233‚Üí   233‚Üípredictable, repeatable structures is therefore proposed to enhance its stabilizing potential, an
   234‚Üí   234‚Üí   234‚Üíidea consistent with broader observations that structure and familiarity can reduce distress in
   235‚Üí   235‚Üí   235‚Üídementia care.
   236‚Üí   236‚Üí   236‚ÜíCommunication-Theoretic Foundations
   237‚Üí   237‚Üí   237‚ÜíBecause the proposed mechanism is relational voice, the framework requires a
   238‚Üí   238‚Üí   238‚Üícommunication-theoretic foundation that explains why familiar voice operates differently from
   239‚Üí   239‚Üí   239‚Üígeneric auditory input. This section draws on interpersonal, relational, family systems, and
   240‚Üí   240‚Üí   240‚Üíorganizational communication to articulate that distinction.
   241‚Üí   241‚Üí   241‚ÜíWhy Familiar Voice Differs From Generic Auditory Stimuli
   242‚Üí   242‚Üí   242‚ÜíFrom a communication standpoint, the familiar voice is a relational message, not merely
   243‚Üí   243‚Üí   243‚Üían acoustic event. It carries relational history, identity attribution, and affective meaning that a
   244‚Üí   244‚Üí   244‚Üíneutral or unfamiliar voice does not. Where generic auditory stimulation may engage attention or
   245‚Üí   245‚Üí   245‚Üíarousal, familiar relational voice additionally signals who is speaking and what that person
   246‚Üí   246‚Üí   246‚Üímeans to the listener, activating the identity and emotional associations built across a shared
   247‚Üí   247‚Üí   247‚Üírelationship. This distinction is central to the framework: the proposed anchoring effect is
   248‚Üí   248‚Üí   248‚Üíhypothesized to derive from relational familiarity and affective salience rather than from auditory
   249‚Üí   249‚Üí   249‚Üíinput as such.
   250‚Üí   250‚Üí   250‚Üí
   251‚Üí   251‚Üí   251‚ÜíInterpersonal and Relational Communication
   252‚Üí   252‚Üí   252‚ÜíInterpersonal and relational communication scholarship emphasizes that meaning is co-
   253‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üíconstructed and that relationships are sustained through ongoing, mutually recognized exchange.
   254‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚ÜíIn dementia care, communication strategies that affirm the person, support face and dignity, and
   255‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üíinvite reciprocity have been associated with more person-centered interactions (Ryan et al.,
   256‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üí2005; Savundranayagam & Moore-Nielsen, 2015). Relational voice can be understood as a
   257‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üívehicle for these affirming functions, conveying recognition, warmth, and continuity of
   258‚Üí   258‚Üí   258‚Üírelationship through tone and cadence as much as through propositional content. Framing voice
   259‚Üí   259‚Üí   259‚Üíengagement in relational terms positions it within a well-established body of communication
   260‚Üí   260‚Üí   260‚Üípractice rather than treating it as a novel sensory technique.
   261‚Üí   261‚Üí   261‚ÜíFamily Systems and Caregiver Communication
   262‚Üí   262‚Üí   262‚ÜíFamilies operate as systems in which a change in one member reverberates throughout
   263‚Üí   263‚Üí   263‚Üíthe whole. As cognitive decline alters a person's communicative capacities, established patterns
   264‚Üí   264‚Üí   264‚Üíof interaction are disrupted, and roles, expectations, and emotional dynamics shift across the
   265‚Üí   265‚Üí   265‚Üífamily. The experience of ambiguous loss can generate uncertainty about how to remain in
   266‚Üí   266‚Üí   266‚Üírelationship with a loved one who is present yet changed (Boss, 1999, 2011). Structured
   267‚Üí   267‚Üí   267‚Üírelational voice engagement may offer families an intentional means of maintaining connection,
   268‚Üí   268‚Üí   268‚Üíproviding a shared practice through which identity and relationship can be affirmed despite
   269‚Üí   269‚Üí   269‚Üícommunicative variability. In this sense, the framework addresses not only the person living with
   270‚Üí   270‚Üí   270‚Üídecline but the relational system surrounding them.
   271‚Üí   271‚Üí   271‚ÜíOrganizational Communication and Cultures of Care
   272‚Üí   272‚Üí   272‚ÜíAt the level of care organizations, communication shapes culture, and culture shapes the
   273‚Üí   273‚Üí   273‚Üíexperience of those receiving care. Kitwood (1997) argued that the social and organizational
   274‚Üí   274‚Üí   274‚Üí
   275‚Üí   275‚Üí   275‚Üíenvironment can either sustain or diminish personhood, and person-centered communication
   276‚Üí   276‚Üí   276‚Üípractices have been linked to more dignity-affirming care interactions (Savundranayagam &
   277‚Üí   277‚Üí   277‚ÜíMoore-Nielsen, 2015). Considered through an organizational lens, embedding relational voice
   278‚Üí   278‚Üí   278‚Üíinto the routines, training, and shared practices of care teams may help align everyday
   279‚Üí   279‚Üí   279‚Üícommunication with identity-preserving values. The framework thus has implications not only
   280‚Üí   280‚Üí   280‚Üífor individual interactions but for how care environments structure communication at a systemic
   281‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚Üílevel.
   282‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚ÜíThe VOCES Framework
   283‚Üí   283‚Üí   283‚ÜíConceptual Overview
   284‚Üí   284‚Üí   284‚ÜíThe VOCES Framework (Voz Orientada a la Continuidad, Emoci√≥n y Sost√©n)
   285‚Üí   285‚Üí   285‚Üíconceptualizes relational voice as a culturally embedded stabilizing mechanism in early
   286‚Üí   286‚Üí   286‚Üícognitive decline. Building on evidence suggesting that affective and auditory responses to
   287‚Üí   287‚Üí   287‚Üífamiliar people may be comparatively preserved, the framework positions intentional voice
   288‚Üí   288‚Üí   288‚Üíengagement as a structured method for reinforcing identity continuity. Rather than treating voice
   289‚Üí   289‚Üí   289‚Üías incidental sensory input, the model integrates emotionally salient auditory cues with ritualized
   290‚Üí   290‚Üí   290‚Üísupport structures designed to sustain relational recognition across evolving cognitive states. The
   291‚Üí   291‚Üí   291‚Üínaming of the framework reflects relational plurality and cultural responsiveness: aging and
   292‚Üí   292‚Üí   292‚Üímemory are shaped by linguistic, familial, and intergenerational narratives, and intervention
   293‚Üí   293‚Üí   293‚Üímodels must therefore treat cultural context as integral to identity preservation rather than as an
   294‚Üí   294‚Üí   294‚Üíadjunct consideration.
   295‚Üí   295‚Üí   295‚ÜíPillar I: Voz (Relational Voice)
   296‚Üí   296‚Üí   296‚ÜíVoz positions relational voice as the primary emotional and cognitive anchor within the
   297‚Üí   297‚Üí   297‚Üíframework. Familiar voices, particularly those associated with attachment figures, carry affective
   298‚Üí   298‚Üí   298‚Üí
   299‚Üí   299‚Üí   299‚Üísalience that may remain accessible despite declining episodic recall (Belin et al., 2000; Kanber
   300‚Üí   300‚Üí   300‚Üíet al., 2025; Miesen, 1993). Within VOCES, voice is operationalized as an intentional
   301‚Üí   301‚Üí   301‚Üíintervention mechanism rather than passive background stimulus. Structured affirmations of
   302‚Üí   302‚Üí   302‚Üírelational roles, identity markers, and personal history are proposed to reinforce continuity of self
   303‚Üí   303‚Üí   303‚Üíacross fluctuating cognitive states. Voz thus serves as the anchoring mechanism on which the
   304‚Üí   304‚Üí   304‚Üíother pillars depend.
   305‚Üí   305‚Üí   305‚ÜíPillar II: Continuidad (Identity Continuity)
   306‚Üí   306‚Üí   306‚ÜíContinuidad emphasizes the preservation of narrative coherence and identity markers.
   307‚Üí   307‚Üí   307‚ÜíBecause early cognitive decline often fragments autobiographical integration, destabilizing
   308‚Üí   308‚Üí   308‚Üírelational roles and self-concept (McAdams & McLean, 2013), the framework prioritizes
   309‚Üí   309‚Üí   309‚Üíreinforcement of identity through repeated, emotionally grounded cues that affirm personal
   310‚Üí   310‚Üí   310‚Üíhistory and relational significance. Continuity is conceptualized not as perfect recall but as
   311‚Üí   311‚Üí   311‚Üísustained recognition of who the individual is within evolving cognitive capacity. Continuidad
   312‚Üí   312‚Üí   312‚Üítherefore defines the identity objective toward which voice engagement is directed.
   313‚Üí   313‚Üí   313‚ÜíPillar III: Emoci√≥n (Emotionally Encoded Memory)
   314‚Üí   314‚Üí   314‚ÜíEmoci√≥n centers the activation of emotionally encoded pathways as a stabilizing
   315‚Üí   315‚Üí   315‚Üímechanism. Evidence that emotional experience can persist beyond declarative memory,
   316‚Üí   316‚Üí   316‚Üíincluding in Alzheimer disease (Feinstein et al., 2010; Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2014), suggests that
   317‚Üí   317‚Üí   317‚Üíaffective salience embedded within familiar relational cues may facilitate recognition and
   318‚Üí   318‚Üí   318‚Üíengagement even when factual retrieval diminishes. Within VOCES, emotionally meaningful
   319‚Üí   319‚Üí   319‚Üívoice is intentionally structured to engage preserved affective responsiveness, reinforcing
   320‚Üí   320‚Üí   320‚Üíconnection through tone, cadence, and relational familiarity rather than informational content
   321‚Üí   321‚Üí   321‚Üí
   322‚Üí   322‚Üí   322‚Üíalone. Emoci√≥n reframes memory engagement from cognitive testing toward affective
   323‚Üí   323‚Üí   323‚Üícontinuity.
   324‚Üí   324‚Üí   324‚ÜíPillar IV: Sost√©n (Structured Relational Support)
   325‚Üí   325‚Üí   325‚ÜíSost√©n refers to the structured relational and tangible supports that stabilize engagement
   326‚Üí   326‚Üí   326‚Üíover time. As cognitive reliability fluctuates, predictable and repeatable interaction structures
   327‚Üí   327‚Üí   327‚Üímay reduce anxiety and enhance emotional grounding. Within the framework, voice-based
   328‚Üí   328‚Üí   328‚Üíengagement is embedded within ritualized formats and, when applicable, supported by physical
   329‚Üí   329‚Üí   329‚Üíartifacts that reinforce continuity. Sost√©n emphasizes that the value of the approach is not derived
   330‚Üí   330‚Üí   330‚Üísolely from auditory input but from the consistent relational container within which voice is
   331‚Üí   331‚Üí   331‚Üíexperienced; stabilization is proposed to emerge through repetition, predictability, and sustained
   332‚Üí   332‚Üí   332‚Üíaffirmation of identity.
   333‚Üí   333‚Üí   333‚ÜíIntegration of the Pillars
   334‚Üí   334‚Üí   334‚ÜíTogether, the four pillars function as an integrated model rather than as discrete
   335‚Üí   335‚Üí   335‚Üícomponents. Voz provides the anchoring mechanism, Continuidad defines the identity objective,
   336‚Üí   336‚Üí   336‚ÜíEmoci√≥n engages preserved affective pathways, and Sost√©n establishes the stabilizing structure
   337‚Üí   337‚Üí   337‚Üíthrough which engagement is sustained. The framework proposes that identity preservation in
   338‚Üí   338‚Üí   338‚Üíearly cognitive decline requires simultaneous attention to relational voice, emotional encoding,
   339‚Üí   339‚Üí   339‚Üínarrative continuity, and structured support, and that these elements are mutually reinforcing
   340‚Üí   340‚Üí   340‚Üírather than independently sufficient.
   341‚Üí   341‚Üí   341‚ÜíApplied Illustration: MemoriGems
   342‚Üí   342‚Üí   342‚ÜíMemoriGems serves as one applied illustration of the VOCES Framework. The platform
   343‚Üí   343‚Üí   343‚Üífacilitates structured relational voice recordings paired with optional sensory supports,
   344‚Üí   344‚Üí   344‚Üíoperationalizing identity-based reinforcement within repeatable engagement structures. In doing
   345‚Üí   345‚Üí   345‚Üí
   346‚Üí   346‚Üí   346‚Üíso, it translates the theoretical components of the framework, relational voice, emotional
   347‚Üí   347‚Üí   347‚Üíencoding, narrative continuity, and structured support, into a practical format accessible to
   348‚Üí   348‚Üí   348‚Üífamilies navigating early cognitive change. MemoriGems does not purport to alter disease
   349‚Üí   349‚Üí   349‚Üíprogression; rather, it functions as a relational support tool aligned with the framework's
   350‚Üí   350‚Üí   350‚Üíemphasis on dignity, continuity, and emotionally grounded engagement. It is presented here as a
   351‚Üí   351‚Üí   351‚Üísingle instantiation of the model and not as the focus of the paper, and its effectiveness, like that
   352‚Üí   352‚Üí   352‚Üíof the framework more broadly, remains a matter for empirical evaluation.
   353‚Üí   353‚Üí   353‚ÜíPractical Applications Across Care Contexts
   354‚Üí   354‚Üí   354‚ÜíBecause relational voice requires minimal technological complexity and draws on
   355‚Üí   355‚Üí   355‚Üíexisting relationships, the framework is adaptable across the settings in which early cognitive
   356‚Üí   356‚Üí   356‚Üísupport occurs. The following applications are offered as conceptual possibilities to be examined
   357‚Üí   357‚Üí   357‚Üíempirically rather than as established practices.
   358‚Üí   358‚Üí   358‚ÜíFamily Caregiving and Home Care
   359‚Üí   359‚Üí   359‚ÜíMost early cognitive support takes place at home, where informal caregivers provide the
   360‚Üí   360‚Üí   360‚Üímajority of care (WHO, 2025). In these contexts, structured relational voice engagement may
   361‚Üí   361‚Üí   361‚Üígive caregivers an intentional practice for maintaining connection as communication patterns
   362‚Üí   362‚Üí   362‚Üíshift, allowing participation in affirmations of identity and shared history rather than reliance on
   363‚Üí   363‚Üí   363‚Üíspontaneous recall. This may also offer families a shared ritual through which relationship is
   364‚Üí   364‚Üí   364‚Üísustained amid ambiguous loss (Boss, 1999).
   365‚Üí   365‚Üí   365‚ÜíMemory Care Communities and Adult Day Health
   366‚Üí   366‚Üí   366‚ÜíIn memory care communities and adult day health programs, where staff are often
   367‚Üí   367‚Üí   367‚Üínecessarily task-oriented and may have limited knowledge of residents' personal histories,
   368‚Üí   368‚Üí   368‚Üírelational voice may help bring identity-affirming content into daily routines. Embedding
   369‚Üí   369‚Üí   369‚Üí
   370‚Üí   370‚Üí   370‚Üífamiliar voice within predictable engagement structures could complement person-centered
   371‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚Üícommunication practices and support continuity across staff and shifts (Ryan et al., 2005).
   372‚Üí   372‚Üí   372‚ÜíHospitals and Acute Care
   373‚Üí   373‚Üí   373‚ÜíHospitalization can be especially disorienting for people living with cognitive
   374‚Üí   374‚Üí   374‚Üíimpairment, who may experience unfamiliar environments as threatening and exhibit heightened
   375‚Üí   375‚Üí   375‚Üíattachment-seeking behavior (Miesen, 1993). Familiar relational voice may offer a portable
   376‚Üí   376‚Üí   376‚Üísource of reassurance and orientation during acute care, potentially supporting emotional
   377‚Üí   377‚Üí   377‚Üíregulation in settings where familiar people cannot always be present.
   378‚Üí   378‚Üí   378‚ÜíTelehealth and Remote Support
   379‚Üí   379‚Üí   379‚ÜíBecause voice can be recorded, shared, and delivered remotely, the framework is well
   380‚Üí   380‚Üí   380‚Üísuited to telehealth and distributed caregiving arrangements. Remote delivery may extend
   381‚Üí   381‚Üí   381‚Üíidentity-affirming engagement to families separated by geography and to care models that rely
   382‚Üí   382‚Üí   382‚Üíon virtual contact, broadening access without requiring complex infrastructure.
   383‚Üí   383‚Üí   383‚ÜíAdvance Care Planning
   384‚Üí   384‚Üí   384‚ÜíRelational voice may also have a role in advance care planning, where capturing a
   385‚Üí   385‚Üí   385‚Üíperson's voice, values, and relational priorities earlier in the trajectory could preserve identity-
   386‚Üí   386‚Üí   386‚Üírelevant material for later stages. Such applications would need to be approached with attention
   387‚Üí   387‚Üí   387‚Üíto consent, autonomy, and the evolving preferences of the person.
   388‚Üí   388‚Üí   388‚ÜíCulturally Responsive Dementia Care
   389‚Üí   389‚Üí   389‚ÜíThe framework's emphasis on linguistic and cultural responsiveness is intended to make
   390‚Üí   390‚Üí   390‚Üíit adaptable across diverse communities, including multilingual families and intergenerational
   391‚Üí   391‚Üí   391‚Üíhouseholds. Because the voice carries cultural and familial meaning, voice-centered approaches
   392‚Üí   392‚Üí   392‚Üí
   393‚Üí   393‚Üí   393‚Üímay be tailored to the languages, narratives, and relational structures that are most meaningful to
   394‚Üí   394‚Üí   394‚Üía given person, supporting equitable and dignity-centered care.
   395‚Üí   395‚Üí   395‚ÜíBroader Clinical and Social Implications
   396‚Üí   396‚Üí   396‚ÜíCaregiver Impact
   397‚Üí   397‚Üí   397‚ÜíStructured voice engagement may provide caregivers with intentional mechanisms for
   398‚Üí   398‚Üí   398‚Üímaintaining relational stability as communication patterns shift, reducing reliance on
   399‚Üí   399‚Üí   399‚Üíspontaneous recall and offering a framework for continued connection despite cognitive
   400‚Üí   400‚Üí   400‚Üívariability. Given the documented emotional burden and ambiguous loss experienced by
   401‚Üí   401‚Üí   401‚Üídementia caregivers (Boss, 1999, 2011), approaches that support the relationship itself, and not
   402‚Üí   402‚Üí   402‚Üíonly the person's cognition, may have value for caregiver well-being, a possibility that merits
   403‚Üí   403‚Üí   403‚Üídirect study.
   404‚Üí   404‚Üí   404‚ÜíEmotional Regulation
   405‚Üí   405‚Üí   405‚ÜíRitualized auditory anchoring may contribute to emotional stabilization by engaging
   406‚Üí   406‚Üí   406‚Üípreserved affective responsiveness. Predictable and emotionally meaningful voice cues are
   407‚Üí   407‚Üí   407‚Üíhypothesized to reduce distress associated with disorientation, particularly when embedded
   408‚Üí   408‚Üí   408‚Üíwithin consistent engagement structures. While empirical validation is required, this proposal is
   409‚Üí   409‚Üí   409‚Üíconsistent with evidence that familiar, emotionally salient stimuli can elicit and sustain emotion
   410‚Üí   410‚Üí   410‚Üíin cognitive decline (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2014; Reschke-Hern√°ndez et al., 2020).
   411‚Üí   411‚Üí   411‚ÜíAging in Place
   412‚Üí   412‚Üí   412‚ÜíVoice-centered approaches are adaptable to the home and community contexts in which
   413‚Üí   413‚Üí   413‚Üíthe majority of early cognitive support occurs (WHO, 2025). Because relational voice requires
   414‚Üí   414‚Üí   414‚Üíminimal technological complexity, the framework may offer scalable support within aging-in-
   415‚Üí   415‚Üí   415‚Üí
   416‚Üí   416‚Üí   416‚Üíplace environments that lack formal clinical infrastructure, aligning with broader goals of
   417‚Üí   417‚Üí   417‚Üíenabling older adults to remain in familiar settings.
   418‚Üí   418‚Üí   418‚ÜíBroader Neurological Contexts
   419‚Üí   419‚Üí   419‚ÜíAlthough developed within the context of early cognitive decline, the conceptual
   420‚Üí   420‚Üí   420‚Üíprinciples underlying the VOCES Framework may have relevance in other conditions involving
   421‚Üí   421‚Üí   421‚Üímemory and identity disruption, such as traumatic brain injury and neurorehabilitation. The
   422‚Üí   422‚Üí   422‚Üípotential applicability of structured relational voice in these broader neurological contexts is
   423‚Üí   423‚Üí   423‚Üínoted as a direction for future inquiry rather than an established extension.
   424‚Üí   424‚Üí   424‚ÜíResearch and Future Directions
   425‚Üí   425‚Üí   425‚ÜíAs a conceptual framework, VOCES requires empirical evaluation to determine its
   426‚Üí   426‚Üí   426‚Üíclinical relevance, feasibility, and measurable impact. Several lines of research are
   427‚Üí   427‚Üí   427‚Üírecommended.
   428‚Üí   428‚Üí   428‚ÜíFirst, mixed-methods studies could examine perceived identity continuity, relational
   429‚Üí   429‚Üí   429‚Üíconnection, and emotional experience among people living with early decline and their families,

   434‚Üí   434‚Üí   434‚Üíscience approaches could examine how the framework can be integrated into existing care
   435‚Üí   435‚Üí   435‚Üíworkflows, caregiver routines, and organizational practices, attending to barriers, facilitators, and
   436‚Üí   436‚Üí   436‚Üícultural adaptation.
   437‚Üí   437‚Üí   437‚ÜíOutcome measurement warrants particular attention. Future work should identify or
   438‚Üí   438‚Üí   438‚Üídevelop measures of identity continuity and personhood, alongside established measures of
   439‚Üí   439‚Üí   439‚Üí

   442‚Üí   442‚Üí   442‚Üídesigns may clarify whether structured voice anchoring is associated with sustained relational
   443‚Üí   443‚Üí   443‚Üístability over time, and eventual controlled trials would be necessary to evaluate efficacy.
   444‚Üí   444‚Üí   444‚ÜíThroughout, interdisciplinary collaboration across gerontology, neuropsychology,
   445‚Üí   445‚Üí   445‚Üícommunication sciences, and caregiving research will be essential to determine the framework's
   446‚Üí   446‚Üí   446‚Üíapplicability and scope.
