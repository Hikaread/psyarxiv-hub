    10→Abstract
    11→Background. Standardized cognitive assessment across clinical sites remains a major
    12→challenge in amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) and related neurodegenerative diseases. The
    13→Edinburgh Cognitive and Behavioral ALS Screen (ECAS) is the most validated and widely-used
    14→ALS-specific cognitive assessment tool, yet paper-and-pencil manual scoring introduces
    15→inter-rater variability that can significantly limit reliability and reproducibility in multi-site clinical
    16→trials. Digitized administration and artificial intelligence-assisted scoring may improve
    17→consistency and scalability, but the current landscape across ALS and related diseases has not
    18→been systematically mapped.
    19→Objective. To map existing evidence on digitized and AI-assisted scoring of cognitive
    20→assessments in ALS and other neurodegenerative and neuroinflammatory diseases, with
    21→emphasis on inter-rater reliability and cross-site consistency.
    22→Methods. We conducted a comprehensive scoping review following Preferred Reporting Items
    23→for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR)
    24→guidelines. Eligible studies evaluated neuropsychological or cognitive assessment tools using
    25→automated, computerized, digital, or AI-assisted scoring in ALS and related conditions, including
    26→Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, dementia, and neuroinflammatory
    27→disease. An initial broad PubMed search was refined to focus on records explicitly addressing
    28→automated or AI-assisted scoring in titles and abstracts. Records were loaded into Rayyan for
    29→independent blinded review by three reviewers, with discrepancies resolved through discussion
    30→until consensus was reached. Data were extracted using standardized templates.
    31→Results. The initial search identified 1,503 records and was refined to 168 records. After
    32→duplicate removal, 166 records underwent title, abstract, and full-text screening against
    33→predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria. Seven primary studies met inclusion criteria: two in
    34→multiple sclerosis, two in the Alzheimer's disease continuum, and three in dementia or mild
    35→cognitive impairment. AI and digital approaches included tablet-based digitization with
    36→automated scoring, convolutional neural networks, and large language models. All seven studies
    37→reported high agreement with human or paper-based reference standards. No primary study
    38→
    39→evaluating AI-assisted scoring of the ECAS or ALS-specific cognitive screens was identified.
    40→Conclusions. This review maps AI-assisted and digitized cognitive assessment scoring in
    41→neurodegenerative and neuroinflammatory diseases. Findings reveal a critical gap in
    42→ALS-specific cognitive tools and ECAS scoring. Methodological approaches from related
    43→diseases, especially automated scoring of verbal and written responses, may inform future
    44→development of standardized AI-assisted ECAS protocols. Such tools could improve
    45→consistency, reliability, and scalability across multi-site ALS clinical trials.
    46→Keywords: amyotrophic lateral sclerosis; ALS; cognitive assessment; ECAS; Edinburgh
    47→Cognitive and Behavioral ALS Screen; artificial intelligence; large language model; automated
    48→scoring; digitized assessment; inter-rater reliability; scoping review; neurodegenerative disease;
    49→neuroinflammatory disease; natural language processing.
    50→Introduction
    51→Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) can present with either bulbar or limb symptoms, and in
    52→some cases both types of symptoms may be present. In addition, up to 50% of people with ALS
    53→develop cognitive and/or behavioral impairment, with ~15% meeting criteria for frontotemporal
    54→dementia spectrum involvement (Strong et al., 2017). The Edinburgh Cognitive and Behavioural
    55→ALS Screen (ECAS) designed for ALS cognitive tool widely used in clinical and research settings
    56→(Abrahams et al., 2014; Abrahams, 2023). However, cognitive assessment in ALS currently
    57→conducted on paper either in person or via teleconferencing/phone, and relies on manual scoring
    58→that may introduce significant inter-rater and intra-rater variability across multi-site studies.
    59→Subjectivity in what is measured and how scores are recorded may lead to inconsistencies
    60→across clinical sites and studies. This lack of scoring consistency and standardization is a central
    61→and unaddressed challenge in ALS cognitive assessment.
    62→At the same time, digital health technologies have matured to the point where high-frequency,
    63→high-quality measures of speech, breathing, and motor function can be collected remotely in
    64→people living with ALS at the comfort of their homes. These digital biomarkers can detect
    65→changes earlier than in-clinic assessments (Neumann et al., 2024; Navar et al., 2025; Peller et
    66→al., 2025), enable identification of disease subtypes and more granular phenotypic
    67→characterization than standard clinical assessments (Acien et al., 2024; Neumann et al., 2024,
    68→Navar et al., 2025), maintain continuity of measurement between visits with interchangeable use
    69→to in-clinic measures (Meyer et al., 2022), and train high-accuracy predictive deep learning
    70→models (Merler et al., 2025). Frequent remote monitoring is particularly valuable for
    71→characterizing temporal disease patterns such as distinguishing fast from slow progressors
    72→(Johnson et al., 2023; Navar et al., 2025), providing objective measures that overcome
    73→limitations of subjective quarterly clinic assessments (Bakker et al., 2017), and substantially
    74→reducing clinical trial sample size requirements while demonstrating changes earlier than
    75→standard visits without sacrificing quality standards (Rutkove et al., 2020; Geronimo & Simmons,
    76→2024; Peller et al., 2025). At the same time, several disease aspects perceived as important by
    77→patients, particularly fatigue and cognition, have received limited attention from digital health
    78→technologies (van Unnik et al., 2025).
    79→Digitized administration of cognitive assessments can improve access and scalability, while
    80→AI-assisted scoring offers the potential to improve scoring consistency across raters and
    81→
    82→multi-sites. In recent years, the broader cognitive assessment field has moved rapidly toward
    83→computerized, tablet-based, and AI-augmented tools. The current state of AI-augmented
    84→neurocognitive screening tests has been recently reviewed by Sirilertmekasakul et al. (2023),
    85→highlighting advantages and limitations of machine learning/deep learning applications,
    86→computer vision (e.g., facial emotions), and automatic speech recognition (ASR). This progress
    87→targets preclinical AD and prognostic approaches for identifying people at risk for developing AD,
    88→other dementing disorders, and MCI, with technology-based assessments potentially enhancing
    89→early detection capabilities and facilitating timely intervention through increased precision and
    90→scale (Öhman et al., 2021; Henkel et al., 2025; Libon et al., 2025). In multiple sclerosis (MS),
    91→Gromisch et al., 2025 identified 32 mobile-based cognitive screening tools (including 16
    92→commercially available), demonstrating field advances. However, these cognitive screening tools
    93→primarily focus on information processing speed, with only half assessing ≥2 cognitive domains,
    94→most frequently nonverbal (67%) and verbal memory (50%) (Gromisch et al., 2025). These
    95→approaches span a continuum from straightforward digitization (paper instruments rendered on
    96→tablets with automated tallying) to advanced machine learning architectures, including
    97→convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image-based tasks and large language models (LLMs)
    98→for handwritten or transcribed verbal responses.
    99→Despite this momentum, the application of these methods designed for ALS cognitive
   100→assessment has not been systematically characterized. Applying digitized and AI assisted
   101→scoring to ALS and related diseases may improve consistency, accessibility, and scalability of
   102→cognitive assessments in both clinical care and research settings, while also supporting more
   103→standardized outcome measurement in clinical trials.
   104→The goal of this scoping review is to map the existing use of digitized and AI-assisted scoring of
   105→cognitive assessments in the neurodegenerative and neuroinflammatory disease space.
   106→Aims and hypothesis
   107→We conducted a scoping review following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews
   108→and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) (Tricco et al., 2018; Page et
   109→al., 2021). This scoping review aims to map existing evidence on digitized and AI-assisted
   110→scoring of cognitive assessments in ALS and other neurodegenerative and neuroinflammatory
   111→diseases, with a focus on inter-rater reliability, cross-site consistency, and cross-disease
   112→applicability. We hypothesized that there would be a significant gap in the application of
   113→digitizing and AI-assisted scoring for ALS cognitive and behavioral screening tools such as
   114→Edinburgh Cognitive and Behavioral ALS Screen (ECAS).
   115→Methods
   116→Search strategy and selection criteria
   117→A two-stage unbiased PubMed search was conducted using the advanced search feature. The
   118→first stage was a broad scoping search, run on 20th April 2026, designed to characterize the
   119→wider landscape of work on cognitive or neuropsychological assessment in ALS and related
   120→neurodegenerative or neuroinflammatory diseases involving broad automation, and digital
   121→approaches. Three concept blocks were combined using Boolean AND: (i) disease (ALS, motor
   122→neuron disease, frontotemporal dementia, and other neurodegenerative diseases), (ii) cognitive
   123→
   124→or neuropsychological assessment, and (iii) artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile apps
   125→or digital health approaches. Each block included both Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) and
   126→title/abstract free-text terms. No date restriction was applied to the search in order to maximize
   127→recall and capture the full historical scope of digitized and AI-assisted cognitive assessment
   128→scoring. This broad search returned 1,503 records, with the earliest retrieved record published
   129→in 1999. This was used to define the conceptual boundaries of the review, the search boolean

   200→Selection process
   201→All the 168 records were uploaded onto Rayyan, a web-based literature screening tool (Ouzzani
   202→et al., 2016). Three independent reviewers blindly screened titles, abstracts, and full texts
   203→against the predefined criteria. Discrepancies were resolved by discussion after unblinding until
   204→consensus was reached. Duplicates were algorithmically identified, then manually checked and
   205→excluded, as were records in languages other than English. The remaining titles and abstracts
   206→were screened and ambiguously relevant reports were flagged and reviewed by all the three
   207→authors. For each paper that was excluded, primary reasons for exclusion were recorded.
   208→Peer-reviewed research articles and planned study reports as well as preprints were included if
   209→they used self-administered and remote assessments. We included only active cognitive tests
   210→(e.g., memory recall, verbal fluency, drawing tasks) and excluded studies using passive
   211→
   212→biomarkers or sensor data as proxies for cognitive impairment (e.g., virtual balance tests,
   213→in-home monitoring), even when active cognitive tests were used for validation. Studies including
   214→other healthy volunteers or other diseases (e.g., cancers, cardiovascular or other diseases,
   215→psychiatric conditions) were excluded, as were studies using the cognitive assessment tool as
   216→an intervention. Data extraction Author(s), year of publication, sample size and group allocation
   217→(if applicable), name of cohort or ongoing study, names and types of cognitive tasks available,
   218→scoring method, adherence and usability metrics, compliance metrics, and reliability metrics
   219→were extracted from each of the selected papers. If certain feasibility or psychometric information
   220→about the tools was not reported in the included articles, this was gathered from other papers
   221→using the same tools, sometimes in separate samples, found using Google Scholar.
   222→Associations with in-person neuropsychiatric assessments and measures of other biomarkers
   223→were also charted. This manuscript was prepared according to the Preferred Reporting Items for
   224→Systematic reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR)
   225→Checklist, which can be found in the Information above.

   250→Study selection
   251→From the 168 records identified by the refined PubMed search, seven primary studies met all
   252→inclusion criteria and were retained for synthesis. Records excluded at full-text review primarily
   253→fell into three categories: (i) machine learning studies focused on diagnosis or biomarker
   254→prediction without a cognitive scoring component; (ii) digital cognitive batteries reporting only
   255→diagnostic discrimination without evaluating the digital scoring procedure itself; and (iii) review
   256→and commentary articles.

   356→Synthesis by disease group
   357→Multiple sclerosis
   358→Two studies addressed MS, both targeting the Brief International Cognitive Assessment for
   359→Multiple Sclerosis (BICAMS), which combines the Symbol Digit Modalities Test (SDMT), the
   360→California Verbal Learning Test–II (CVLT-II), and the Brief Visuospatial Memory Test–Revised
   361→(BVMT-R).
   362→
   363→An iPad implementation (iBICAMS) was validated against the paper version in 139 patients. The
   364→digital implementation introduced standardization elements not present in paper administration:
   365→pre-recorded CVLT word lists with controlled prosody and pacing, automatic 10-second timing
   366→for BVMT-R stimulus exposure, and on-tablet stylus drawing. For the SDMT, the tablet version
   367→also enabled automated timing and digital score capture, reducing manual scoring burden for
   368→administrators. Intraclass correlation coefficients (ICCs) between paper and tablet versions were
   369→0.92 (SDMT), 0.83 (CVLT-II), and 0.82 (BVMT-R), indicating high agreement at the total-score
   370→level. The authors observed an order-of-administration effect for CVLT-II and BVMT-R, attributed
   371→to learning effects and possibly to differences in human prosody during paper administration
   372→(Costabile et al., 2023).
   373→In a separate study, a general-purpose large language model (ChatGPT-4.5) was tested to
   374→replicate human BICAMS scoring on 35 scanned protocols, using two human raters with a
   375→blinded third rater for adjudication. In the pre-public-release phase, ICCs between the LLM and
   376→human raters were very high (CVLT-II 0.992, SDMT 1.000, BVMT-R 0.82–0.85), and the LLM
   377→identified scoring errors that two trained human raters had overlooked. Mean scoring time per
   378→BICAMS protocol was approximately nine minutes. Following the model's broader public
   379→release, the authors observed a decline in scoring accuracy and increased variance (e.g.,
   380→BVMT-R Trial 3 ICC dropped to −0.046), which they attributed to either compute resource
   381→constraints or undisclosed inference-pipeline changes (Jaworski et al., 2025).
   382→Alzheimer's disease continuum
   383→Two studies addressed populations spanning cognitively healthy individuals, MCI, and dementia
   384→within the AD continuum, but used very different cognitive instruments and AI strategies.
   385→An automated scoring system for the Rey Complex Figure Test (RCFT) was developed using a
   386→DenseNet CNN trained on 20,040 scanned images from 6,680 participants in the GARD cohort.
   387→A distinctive methodological feature was an iterative quality-improvement loop: an initial model
   388→was trained, errors were categorized (image quality, scoring, digitalization, model bias), and the
   389→model was retrained on a corrected dataset. Five-fold cross-validation produced a mean
   390→absolute error (MAE) of 0.95 points and an R² of 0.986 against the consensus of 32 trained
   391→psychologists. On an external 150-image test set scored independently by five experienced
   392→psychologists, the AI achieved MAE = 0.64 and R² = 0.994 against the average of the five
   393→experts—within the range observed between expert raters themselves. Predicted scores
   394→significantly differentiated cognitively normal, MCI, and dementia groups (Park et al. 2023).
   395→A tablet self-administered computerized version of the FNAME-12 face–name associative
   396→memory task was developed and named FACEmemory. The tool used voice recognition and
   397→touchscreen input with automatic scoring, and was validated in 276 participants (154 cognitively
   398→healthy, 61 non-amnestic MCI, 61 amnestic MCI). Automatic and manual scoring of the same
   399→protocols correlated at r = 0.98. A cutoff of 31.5 yielded 80.5% sensitivity and 80.3% specificity
   400→for distinguishing cognitively healthy individuals from amnestic MCI. In the subset undergoing
   401→lumbar puncture, FACEmemory total scores correlated with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) Aβ42 (r =
   402→0.62) and the Aβ42/p-tau ratio (r = 0.68) in the amnestic MCI group, providing
   403→biomarker-anchored validity (Alegret et al., 2020).
   404→Dementia and MCI (mixed populations)
   405→
   406→Three studies addressed dementia or MCI populations using diverse cognitive instruments.
   407→An Attentive Pairwise Interaction Network (API-Net), a CNN architecture trained with contrastive
   408→image pairs rather than single images, was used to automate Shulman scoring of the Clock
   409→Drawing Test (CDT). Trained on 3,108 clock drawings, the model incorporated aResNet-152
   410→backbone and achieved an F1-score of 0.79, compared with 0.76 for a standard ResNet-152
   411→baseline. The performance gain was concentrated in the discrimination of visually similar
   412→clock-score pairs (e.g., Shulman 4 vs. 5), the boundary most relevant for early-stage cognitive
   413→change. Score-CAM visualizations indicated that the contrastively trained model attended more
   414→selectively to the actual locus of drawing irregularity than the standard CNN (Raksasat et al.,
   415→2023).
   416→MOGI is a tablet-based self-assessment tool that simulates Japan's official digital cognitive test
   417→for older drivers (the MENKYO system). The MOGI battery comprises a cued recall task and a
   418→time-orientation task, with handwritten responses on tablet converted to text by a Japanese
   419→character recognition engine and scored automatically against the National Police Agency rubric.
   420→In 42 community and clinic participants (mean age 76.4 years), automated and manual scoring
   421→of the same protocols agreed at ICC = 0.97 (95% CI 0.94–0.98). MOGI total scores correlated
   422→with the MMSE-J at Spearman ρ = 0.64 (p < 0.001) and significantly differentiated participants
   423→stratified by MMSE-J cutoffs (≥28, 24–27, ≤23) (Asano et al., 2025).
   424→An Android-based version of the MMSE combined touchscreen input with the Google Speech
   425→API for items requiring spoken responses (registration, recall, sentence repetition). In a
   426→15-resident pilot conducted in a Flemish nursing home, the Bland–Altman analysis between
   427→paper and app total scores showed a mean difference of 0.1 points (SD 2.1; range −4 to +4) and
   428→a Pearson correlation of r = 0.90 (p < 0.01). Per-question score-weighted agreement averaged
   429→74%; the speech-recognition-dependent items showed marginally lower per-item agreement
   430→(~70%) than touchscreen items (Devos et al., 2019).
   431→Synthesis by AI / digital methodology
   432→Across the seven studies, the methodological approaches clustered into four broad strategies.
   433→Tablet digitization with controlled stimulus delivery and automated tallying. iBICAMS
   434→(Costabile et al., 2023) and the digital MMSE (Devos et al., 2019) primarily replace paper with
   435→tablet, adding standardization features (pre-recorded audio, automatic timing, automated
   436→arithmetic) but retaining the original test structure. Reported reliability vs. paper was high (ICCs
   437→0.82–0.92; Bland–Altman mean difference 0.1).
   438→Tablet self-administration with voice recognition and rule-based automatic scoring.
   439→FACEmemory (Alegret et al., 2020) and MOGI (Asano et al., 2025) use voice or handwriting
   440→recognition followed by deterministic rule-based scoring. Auto-vs-manual agreement was very
   441→high (r = 0.98 and ICC = 0.97 respectively), demonstrating that voice recognition and scoring
   442→pipelines can produce scores essentially indistinguishable from human scoring at the total-score
   443→level.
   444→End-to-end deep learning on scanned images. RCFT scoring (Park et al., 2023) and CDT
   445→Shulman scoring (Raksasat et al., 2023) use CNN architectures (DenseNet, ResNet-152 with
   446→API-Net) trained directly on scanned drawings. Performance approached or matched
   447→
   448→inter-expert agreement on independent test sets. Raksasat et al. specifically demonstrated that
   449→contrastive pairwise training improved fine-grained discrimination at clinically critical score
   450→boundaries.
   451→Large language models applied to scanned protocols. Jaworski et al. (2025) used a
   452→general-purpose LLM (ChatGPT-4.5) with prompt engineering to score scanned BICAMS
   453→protocols, achieving high agreement with humans pre-release but exhibiting performance
   454→instability after the model became publicly available, highlighting reproducibility risks specific to
   455→commercial LLM-based pipelines.
   456→Reliability outcomes reported
   457→All seven studies reported some form of agreement statistic against a human or paper-based
   458→reference standard. Reported metrics included intraclass correlation coefficients (Costabile,
   459→Asano, Jaworski), Pearson or Spearman correlations (Park, Alegret, Devos, Asano),
   460→Bland–Altman analyses (Devos), F1-scores and classification accuracy (Park, Raksasat), and
   461→sensitivity/specificity (Alegret). No included study reported a multi-site cross-rater reliability
   462→evaluation in the formal sense of multiple independent clinical sites administering and scoring
   463→the same protocols, although Park et al. used 32 psychologists across one cohort and Jaworski
   464→et al. used multiple human raters with blinded adjudication.
   465→Discussion
   466→Summary of findings
   467→This scoping review identified seven primary studies evaluating digitized or AI-assisted scoring
   468→of cognitive assessments in neurodegenerative or neuroinflammatory diseases. The studies
   469→focused on the assessment of MS, the AD continuum, and mixed dementia/MCI populations,
   470→with methodological strategies ranging from straightforward tablet digitization to deep learning
   471→and large language models. Across all seven studies, AI or digital scoring systems demonstrated
   472→high agreement with human or paper-based reference standards at the total-score level.
   473→The ALS Cognitive Screen gap
   474→Within the seven included records, no primary study reported an AI-assisted or automated digital
   475→scoring system specifically for the ALS cognitive screen. Given that the ECAS is the most
   476→validated cognitive instrument for ALS and that ALS multi-site clinical trials increasingly require
   477→harmonized cognitive endpoints, this absence is consistent with the central challenge articulated
   478→in our background and that scoring inconsistency in ALS cognitive assessment remains
   479→substantively unaddressed by current technology pipelines.
   480→Methodological observations across studies
   481→First, the level of agreement achieved by AI/digital scoring was generally comparable to
   482→inter-rater agreement among human experts when adequate training and a well-defined
   483→reference standard were available (Park et al., 2023; Jaworski et al., 2025; Alegret et al., 2020).
   484→This pattern suggests that the bottleneck for AI-assisted scoring is typically not raw model
   485→capacity but rather the curation of a high-quality reference dataset and the precision of the
   486→scoring rubric.
   487→
   488→Second, fine-grained discrimination at clinically critical score boundaries (e.g., normal vs. mild
   489→impairment) appears to benefit from training strategies designed specifically for that purpose,
   490→rather than from larger generic models. Raksasat et al. (2023) demonstrated that contrastive
   491→pairwise training improved discrimination at the Shulman 4 vs. 5 boundary, where precisely the
   492→boundary relevant for early detection relative to a strong CNN baseline. This is methodologically
   493→relevant for ALS cognitive screening, where distinguishing borderline-normal performance from
   494→early neuropsychological test detectable change is the main clinical question.
   495→Third, iterative training-data correction (Park et al., 2023) substantively improved performance
   496→and surfaced scoring and digitization errors made by human raters that would otherwise have
   497→been encoded as ground truth. This implies that AI-assisted pipelines can serve a
   498→quality-assurance function for the human reference dataset itself, not only as a downstream
   499→scoring tool.
   500→Fourth, the reproducibility risk associated with commercial general-purpose LLMs (Jaworski et
   501→al., 2025) is a meaningful concern for clinical applications. The authors documented a clear
   502→performance change coinciding with broader public availability of the model, with no access to
   503→the underlying inference pipeline to diagnose the cause.
   504→For multi-site clinical trial use, this argues for either locally hosted models with frozen weights,
   505→domain-specific fine-tuned models, or carefully versioned API contracts rather than reliance on
   506→continuously updated AI models.
   507→Although no study evaluated automated scoring of the ECAS or any other ALS cognitive screen,
   508→the studies reviewed highlight several methodological principles directly applicable to future
   509→ALS-focused work, particularly the importance of high-quality reference data, task-specific
   510→design, and reproducibility.
   511→Implications for future ECAS automation
   512→Three observations from this synthesis are directly relevant to the design of an AI-assisted
   513→ECAS scoring system.
   514→
   515→(i) The ECAS includes a number of tests which rely on verbal (language) responses; naming,
   516→spelling, fluency, memory and sentence completion. The included studies establish
   517→methodological precedent for each modality individually: voice recognition with rule-based
   518→scoring for verbal items (Alegret et al., 2020; Devos et al., 2019) and handwriting recognition for
   519→written responses (Asano et al., 2025). An ECAS pipeline could in principle compose these
   520→existing modalities.
   521→(ii) For multi-site trial deployment, locally hosted or domain-specific models with versioned
   522→
   523→weights are likely more appropriate than commercial general-purpose LLM APIs, given the
   524→reproducibility concerns surfaced by Jaworski et al. (2025).
   525→Conclusion
   526→This scoping review provides the first map of AI-assisted and digitized cognitive assessment
   527→scoring across ALS and related neuro-degenerative and neuro-inflammatory diseases. Seven
   528→primary studies were identified across MS, the AD continuum, and broader dementia/MCI
   529→populations. AI/digital scoring systems achieved agreement with human reference standards
   530→comparable to inter-expert agreement when adequately trained and validated.
   531→No primary study evaluating AI-assisted scoring of the ECAS or any ALS-specific cognitive
   532→screen was identified. Methodological templates from related diseases, voice-recognition
   533→pipelines for verbal subtests, contrastive learning for fine-grained boundary discrimination, and
   534→iterative training-data correction, may inform future development of standardized AI scoring
   535→protocols for the ECAS, supporting more consistent multi-site cognitive assessment in future
   536→ALS clinical trials.
