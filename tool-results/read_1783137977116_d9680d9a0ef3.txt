   200→   200→of change in self-experience (Fuchs, 2017; Gallagher, 2005). Across development, individuals must
   201→   201→continuously adapt to bodily transformations associated with growth, puberty, pregnancy,
   202→   202→parenthood, aging, illness, and reproductive transitions. These changes often involve profound
   203→   203→modifications in physiological regulation, emotional functioning, social roles, and self-understanding
   204→   204→(Barrett, 2017; Fotopoulou & Tsakiris, 2017; Tsakiris, 2017). They also require individuals to revise
   205→   205→and integrate new experiences into evolving life narratives and identities (Crocetti et al., 2023;
   206→   206→Luyckx et al., 2006; McAdams & McLean, 2013; McLean & Syed, 2015).
   207→   207→The challenge of selfhood therefore lies not in preserving continuity through an unchanging body,
   208→   208→but in maintaining continuity despite ongoing bodily transformation. Adolescence, parenthood,
   209→   209→menopause, aging, and illness all require individuals to incorporate changing bodily experiences into
   210→   210→evolving models of who they are and who they are becoming. Embodiment contributes to self-
   211→   211→coherence precisely because bodily change must be continuously integrated into a coherent sense of
   212→   212→self. Recent phenomenological accounts similarly emphasize the reciprocal relationship between
   213→   213→embodiment and narrativity, suggesting that bodily experience supports narrative continuity while
   214→   214→simultaneously being interpreted and transformed through narrative self-understanding (Tanaka et
   215→   215→al., 2026).
   216→   216→Predictive processing and active inference frameworks provide a computational account of how such
   217→   217→continuity may emerge (Friston, 2010; Hirsh, 2026; Seth, 2021). According to these models, the brain
   218→   218→continuously generates predictions regarding bodily and environmental states and updates these
   219→   219→predictions through incoming sensory information. Selfhood is therefore not a static representation
   220→   220→but an active process of inference through which the organism continuously models its own bodily
   221→   221→condition. From this perspective, continuity depends not only on the availability of bodily signals,
   222→   222→but also on the capacity to generate relatively stable expectations about oneself across changing
   223→   223→circumstances.
   224→   224→Recent theoretical developments have extended this view by proposing that identity and self-
   225→   225→experience themselves may be understood as temporally extended inferential processes aimed at
   226→   226→maintaining coherent self-models across time (Hirsh, 2026). When such embodied self-models
   227→   227→become disrupted, bodily sensations may lose their familiar meaning and predictability, contributing
   228→   228→to disturbances in self-experience and continuity. Disturbances in interoceptive processing may
   229→   229→therefore challenge not only bodily awareness but also the continuity through which individuals
   230→   230→understand themselves across time (Barca et al., 2023; Barca & Pezzulo, 2020). Consistent with this
   231→   231→view, recent phenomenological accounts of depersonalization suggest that disruptions in embodied
   232→   232→temporal organization may contribute to experiences of estrangement from oneself and a diminished
   233→   233→sense of continuity (Alekseeva & Ciaunica, 2025).
   234→   234→The relationship between embodiment and selfhood is further enriched by developmental and
   235→   235→relational perspectives. According to these approaches, bodily selfhood emerges not in isolation but
   236→   236→6
   237→   237→
   238→   238→through interpersonal processes of affective regulation and embodied interaction (Fotopoulou &
   239→   239→Tsakiris, 2017). Early caregiver–infant relationships provide a context within which bodily states
   240→   240→become predictable, interpretable, and meaningful. The infant's developing experience of self is
   241→   241→therefore shaped not only by internal bodily signals but also by their co-regulation within
   242→   242→interpersonal relationships.
   243→   243→This relational perspective suggests that self-coherence is initially co-constructed before becoming
   244→   244→an individual capacity. Through repeated experiences of attunement, affective touch, and caregiver
   245→   245→responsiveness, bodily sensations become associated with regulation, significance, and predictability.
   246→   246→As a result, embodied continuity is established not only through physiological regulation but also
   247→   247→through interpersonal experience. Emerging evidence further suggests that affective touch plays a
   248→   248→particularly important role in shaping bodily self-awareness, emotional regulation, and social
   249→   249→connectedness, thereby contributing to the development of coherent embodied self-experience
   250→   250→(Crucianelli et al., 2018; Kirsch et al., 2018).
   251→   251→Recent embodied accounts have further emphasized the role of movement and action in the
   252→   252→construction of selfhood. From this perspective, self-experience emerges through ongoing
   253→   253→interactions between bodily states, sensorimotor engagement, and predictive self-models (Barca,
   254→   254→2025; Vaisvaser, 2025). Continuity is therefore not only experienced through the body but also
   255→   255→enacted through the organism's active engagement with the world.
   256→   256→Taken together, these perspectives suggest that self-coherence emerges through the dynamic
   257→   257→integration of bodily regulation, predictive processing, movement, and interpersonal experience. The
   258→   258→body provides a continuously evolving foundation through which experience remains anchored, while
   259→   259→relational and predictive processes contribute to the interpretation and integration of change across
   260→   260→time.
   261→   261→However, bodily continuity alone cannot fully account for the complexity of human selfhood. Across
   262→   262→development, individuals must also integrate changing bodily experiences into broader systems of
   263→   263→identity, meaning, and autobiographical understanding. This observation raises a further question:
   264→   264→how are changing experiences, relationships, and life transitions organized into a coherent sense of
   265→   265→self over time?
   266→   266→4. Identity and Personal Meaning Organizations as Mechanisms of Temporal Self-Maintenance
   267→   267→While embodiment provides a foundation for continuity, bodily regulation alone cannot account for
   268→   268→the complexity and stability of human selfhood. Human beings do not simply experience bodily
   269→   269→continuity; they also construct coherent narratives, commitments, values, and meanings that extend
   270→   270→across years and even decades. This higher level of organization is typically described through the
   271→   271→concept of identity.
   272→   272→Developmental and narrative approaches conceptualize identity as a process through which
   273→   273→individuals integrate experiences into coherent life trajectories. Identity formation allows connections
   274→   274→between past experiences, present commitments, and future aspirations, thereby supporting continuity
   275→   275→across developmental transitions (Crocetti et al., 2023; Habermas & Bluck, 2000; McAdams &
   276→   276→McLean, 2013). Rather than representing a fixed structure, identity constitutes an ongoing process of
   277→   277→temporal integration.
   278→   278→Phenomenological and constructivist perspectives converge with this view while emphasizing the
   279→   279→embodied and relational nature of identity. Stanghellini and colleagues (Stanghellini et al., 2012) have
   280→   280→argued that disturbances of identity often involve alterations in bodily self-experience, including
   281→   281→7
   282→   282→
   283→   283→experiences of estrangement, objectification, and excessive dependence on external validation.
   284→   284→Similarly, (Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009) conceptualize identity as emerging through the dynamic
   285→   285→relationship between self-experience and lived situations. Identity therefore develops not
   286→   286→independently of embodiment or interpersonal experience, but through their continuous integration
   287→   287→within broader structures of meaning.
   288→   288→From this perspective, identity may be understood as a mechanism of temporal integration through
   289→   289→which embodied experiences become organized into coherent self-trajectories. Identity links bodily
   290→   290→experience to autobiographical memory, interpersonal relationships, future projects, and personal
   291→   291→values. Its primary function is not merely to answer the question "Who am I?", but to provide a
   292→   292→coherent framework through which past experiences, present circumstances, and anticipated futures
   293→   293→can be integrated into a meaningful whole.
   294→   294→However, identity alone does not fully explain how continuity is maintained. Individuals may possess
   295→   295→different identities, values, and autobiographical narratives, yet face a common adaptive challenge:
   296→   296→preserving a coherent sense of self despite ongoing bodily, relational, and environmental change.
   297→   297→Importantly, identity is neither singular nor immutable. Across the lifespan, individuals occupy
   298→   298→multiple identity positions that evolve in response to developmental transitions and life events. A
   299→   299→person may simultaneously identify as a parent, professional, partner, friend, or caregiver, while the
   300→   300→relative salience of these identities changes over time. Major life transitions often require the
   301→   301→incorporation of new identity dimensions into existing self-organizations. For example, the transition
   302→   302→to parenthood requires individuals to integrate a new parental identity with previously established
   303→   303→professional, relational, and personal commitments. The challenge is not merely acquiring a new
   304→   304→identity, but reorganizing one's sense of self so that new and existing identities can coexist within a
   305→   305→coherent life trajectory.
   306→   306→Similarly, developmental transitions may challenge continuity even when identity itself remains
   307→   307→intact. Parents experiencing the transition commonly described as the "empty nest" do not lose their
   308→   308→identity as mothers or fathers when their children become autonomous and leave home. Rather, they
   309→   309→may struggle to reorganize continuity when a central source of meaning, reciprocity, and daily
   310→   310→structure is transformed. The psychological challenge is therefore not simply one of identity, but of
   311→   311→integrating changing relational roles into a coherent and evolving sense of self.
   312→   312→These examples suggest an important distinction. Identity primarily concerns the content of self-
   313→   313→definition—who one is at a given moment in time. Self-coherence, in contrast, refers to the dynamic
   314→   314→process through which changing identities, experiences, relationships, and life circumstances are
   315→   315→integrated into a sufficiently continuous sense of self. Whereas identity answers the question "Who
   316→   316→am I?", self-coherence addresses the broader question "How do I remain myself while who I am
   317→   317→continues to change?"
   318→   318→This distinction raises a further question. If identity contents continuously evolve across development,
   319→   319→what allows individuals to preserve continuity despite these transformations? Identity describes what
   320→   320→is integrated, but does not fully explain how such integration is achieved and maintained over time.
   321→   321→This question points toward a broader level of psychological organization concerned not with the
   322→   322→content of self-definition, but with the processes through which continuity is preserved across
   323→   323→changing experiences. Not all individuals maintain continuity in the same way. Understanding these
   324→   324→individual differences has been a central concern of constructivist approaches to personality.
   325→   325→Constructivist approaches offer a useful perspective on this issue through Guidano's concept of
   326→   326→Personal Meaning Organizations (PMOs). Traditionally described as relatively stable organizations
   327→   327→8
   328→   328→
   329→   329→of emotional processing, personal meaning, and self-definition (Guidano, 1991; Guidano & Liotti,
   330→   330→1983), PMOs have subsequently been elaborated within post-rationalist and phenomenological
   331→   331→traditions as organizational frameworks through which individuals structure their experience of
   332→   332→themselves and the world (Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Nardi & Bellantuono, 2008).
   333→   333→Importantly, these individual differences are not conceptualized as pathological categories. Rather,
   334→   334→they reflect alternative organizational styles through which individuals construct and maintain
   335→   335→continuity of self-experience. Contemporary reinterpretations of PMOs have increasingly emphasized
   336→   336→dimensions such as inward- and outward-oriented modes of self-organization, highlighting
   337→   337→differences in the sources of information individuals rely upon to maintain a coherent sense of self
   338→   338→(Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Nardi & Bellantuono, 2008)
   339→   339→From this perspective, some individuals may achieve continuity primarily through the interpretation
   340→   340→of internal bodily and emotional experiences, whereas others may rely more heavily on interpersonal
   341→   341→recognition, social feedback, and relational contexts. These organizational differences are not
   342→   342→inherently maladaptive. Rather, they represent alternative pathways through which self-coherence is
   343→   343→maintained.
   344→   344→Such pathways may influence not only vulnerability to specific life events but also the domains in
   345→   345→which individuals seek meaning, fulfillment, and stability. Individuals whose sense of continuity is
   346→   346→strongly grounded in interpersonal attunement and relational recognition may, in some cases, be
   347→   347→drawn toward caregiving, teaching, counseling, healthcare, or other forms of engagement centered on
   348→   348→human relationships. Others may derive coherence more strongly from personal mastery, autonomy,
   349→   349→intellectual consistency, or the understanding of internal experience, leading them to pursue different
   350→   350→forms of personal and professional engagement. Over time, these recurring preferences may
   351→   351→contribute to characteristic life trajectories through which continuity is actively constructed and
   352→   352→maintained. In this sense, organizational styles may shape not only how individuals respond to
   353→   353→change, but also the kinds of environments, relationships, and life projects through which they seek
   354→   354→coherence.
   355→   355→At the same time, different pathways may be associated with different vulnerabilities. Organizational
   356→   356→styles relying predominantly on external sources of validation may be particularly challenged by
   357→   357→relational disruptions, rejection, or changes in social roles. Conversely, styles grounded more strongly
   358→   358→in bodily and subjective experience may be especially sensitive to bodily transformations, illness, or
   359→   359→disturbances in interoceptive processing. Personal meaning organizations therefore do not determine
   360→   360→psychopathology directly; rather, they shape the conditions under which continuity becomes
   361→   361→vulnerable and requires reorganization.
   362→   362→The same organizational pathways that support continuity during periods of stability may become
   363→   363→sources of vulnerability during developmental transitions that challenge the forms of experience on
   364→   364→which continuity has traditionally relied. However, adaptive functioning may depend not only on the
   365→   365→capacity to maintain continuity through established pathways, but also on the flexibility to reorganize
   366→   366→continuity when those pathways become insufficient. Developmental transitions often require
   367→   367→individuals to identify and integrate new sources of stability, meaning, and self-definition. In this
   368→   368→sense, self-coherence is not achieved through the preservation of fixed forms of experience, but
   369→   369→through the capacity to maintain continuity while reorganizing the foundations upon which continuity
   370→   370→depends.
   371→   371→This perspective reframes psychopathology not primarily as the failure of a particular organizational
   372→   372→style, but as a difficulty in reorganizing continuity when previously reliable sources of self-coherence
   373→   373→become unavailable.

   375→   375→
   376→   376→From the perspective developed here, PMOs may be understood as relatively stable organizational
   377→   377→strategies through which individuals preferentially construct and maintain self-coherence across
   378→   378→development. Although these organizational tendencies display a degree of stability across the
   379→   379→lifespan, they should not be understood as fixed or immutable structures. Major developmental
   380→   380→transitions, life events, and changing relational contexts may require individuals to reorganize the
   381→   381→sources of continuity upon which self-coherence depends. Accordingly, continuity depends not only
   382→   382→on organizational stability, but also on the capacity to reorganize established pathways of self-
   383→   383→coherence in response to change. Rather than defining who individuals are, they shape how continuity
   384→   384→is achieved by privileging particular sources of stability, such as bodily experience, interpersonal
   385→   385→recognition, autobiographical narratives, or relational predictability. Identity provides a narrative
   386→   386→answer to the question of who one is, whereas PMOs organize the pathways through which continuity
   387→   387→is preserved despite change. Together, they support the maintenance of self-coherence across the
   388→   388→lifespan.

   434→   434→5. From Self-Regulation to Self-Coherence
   435→   435→Taken together, the present framework suggests that self-coherence does not arise from any single
   436→   436→psychological function. Rather, it emerges through the dynamic integration of embodied regulation,
   437→   437→identity processes, and personal meaning organization within a temporally extended field of
   438→   438→experience.

   440→   440→Across developmental psychology, personality theory, affective science, and clinical psychology,

   442→   442→11

   445→   445→associated with effective regulation, whereas psychopathology is conceptualized as a consequence of
   446→   446→dysregulation.

   448→   448→problem. Regulation presupposes the existence of a system whose continuity must first be maintained.
   449→   449→Before individuals can regulate their behavior, they must preserve a sufficiently coherent experience
   450→   450→of themselves as persisting subjects across time.

   472→   472→Self-regulation refers to processes that modulate behavior, affect, cognition, and physiology in
   473→   473→response to changing environmental and internal demands (Carver & Scheier, 2001). Its primary
   474→   474→function is adaptation. Self-coherence, in contrast, refers to the maintenance of continuity and
   475→   475→intelligibility across experience (Fuchs, 2017). Its primary function is not adaptation per se, but the
   476→   476→preservation of a coherent sense of self despite ongoing change.
   477→   477→This distinction becomes particularly evident during major life transitions. Consider an individual
   478→   478→who loses a long-term relationship, retires from a valued professional role, or develops a chronic
   479→   479→illness. In each case, successful adaptation requires more than behavioral or emotional regulation.
   480→   480→Individuals must also re-establish continuity between who they were, who they are becoming, and
   481→   481→how they understand their future. The central challenge is therefore not simply regulating emotional
   482→   482→responses to change, but preserving coherence in the face of transformation.
   483→   483→From this perspective, self-regulation and self-coherence represent distinct but complementary levels
   484→   484→of analysis. Self-regulation explains how individuals respond to challenges; self-coherence explains
   485→   485→how they remain themselves while doing so.
   486→   486→12

   488→   488→Importantly, these functions are deeply interconnected. Regulatory processes often contribute to the
   489→   489→maintenance of self-coherence, while threats to self-coherence frequently trigger regulatory
   490→   490→responses. Nevertheless, the distinction remains theoretically significant because it shifts the
   491→   491→explanatory focus from what behavior accomplishes to what behavior protects.
   492→   492→Many actions traditionally interpreted as regulatory strategies may be reinterpreted as attempts to
   493→   493→preserve continuity of self-experience (Hirsh, 2026). Consider an individual who, following
   494→   494→retirement, actively invests in new social activities, hobbies, or community roles. Such behaviors may
   495→   495→regulate uncertainty and emotional distress, but they also help restore continuity by connecting past
   496→   496→competencies and values with new forms of engagement. Self-regulation therefore contributes not
   497→   497→only to adaptation, but also to the reconstruction of self-coherence following major life transitions.
   498→   498→Individuals do not merely regulate anxiety, sadness, uncertainty, or physiological arousal; they
   499→   499→regulate experiences that threaten the coherence of their self-models.
   500→   500→This perspective offers a novel interpretation of uncertainty within predictive processing and active
   501→   501→inference frameworks. Existing models suggest that organisms continuously strive to minimize
   502→   502→uncertainty and maintain adaptive control over bodily and environmental states (Friston, 2010; A. K.
   503→   503→Seth & Friston, 2016). However, uncertainty may become psychologically significant not merely
   504→   504→because it impairs prediction, but because it threatens continuity. This interpretation is consistent with
   505→   505→previous proposals suggesting that interoceptive uncertainty may contribute to disturbances in self-
   506→   506→experience and motivate compensatory attempts to restore stability and predictability (Barca et al.,
   507→   507→2023; Barca & Pezzulo, 2020). When individuals can no longer generate reliable expectations about
   508→   508→themselves, their relationships, or their future trajectories, uncertainty becomes not only a cognitive
   509→   509→challenge but also a challenge to selfhood. This is particularly evident during periods of major
   510→   510→transition, loss, illness, or interpersonal disruption, when previously stable self-models become
   511→   511→difficult to sustain.
   512→   512→The resulting disruption affects not only behavioral adaptation but also the intelligibility of experience
   513→   513→itself. From this perspective, uncertainty reduction can be understood as a means of preserving self-
   514→   514→coherence rather than an end in itself.
   515→   515→This formulation also helps explain why maladaptive behaviors often persist despite their apparent
   516→   516→costs. Restrictive eating, compulsive rituals, excessive reassurance seeking, symptom monitoring,
   517→   517→hypervigilance toward bodily sensations, or rigid interpersonal patterns may provide temporary
   518→   518→experiences of predictability, stability, and continuity. Although such strategies frequently impair
   519→   519→long-term adaptation, they may nevertheless be maintained because they protect continuity when
   520→   520→ordinary mechanisms of self-coherence become fragile.
   521→   521→In this sense, individuals may not simply regulate anxiety, sadness, or uncertainty; they may be
   522→   522→attempting to regulate disruptions in the continuity of self-experience. Their persistence may therefore
   523→   523→derive less from their regulatory effectiveness than from their capacity to provide temporary solutions
   524→   524→to crises of self-coherence.
   525→   525→Such a view resonates with phenomenological accounts of psychopathology, which frequently
   526→   526→describe mental disorders as disruptions of experiential continuity and selfhood (Fuchs, 2013, 2017;
   527→   527→Stanghellini et al., 2012), as well as with constructivist perspectives emphasizing the preservation of
   528→   528→personal identity and meaning structures (Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Guidano, 1991). It also extends
   529→   529→contemporary predictive and embodied theories by suggesting that the ultimate adaptive challenge is
   530→   530→not merely reducing prediction error or regulating bodily states, but maintaining a coherent model of
   531→   531→oneself across time.

   533→   533→
   534→   534→From this perspective, self-regulation may be understood as one of several mechanisms serving a
   535→   535→more fundamental organizational goal: the maintenance of self-coherence. Regulatory processes help
   536→   536→individuals adapt to changing circumstances, but their broader psychological significance lies in
   537→   537→supporting continuity across experience. The primary task of the self is therefore not merely to
   538→   538→regulate behavior, emotion, or physiology, but to maintain continuity of experience while adapting to
   539→   539→an ever-changing world.
   540→   540→6. Future Directions
   541→   541→The present framework also generates testable hypotheses regarding individual differences in the
   542→   542→sources of continuity that individuals prioritize. Future research could examine whether disruptions
   543→   543→in self-coherence are associated with distinct patterns of interoceptive processing, identity integration,
   544→   544→autobiographical reasoning, and interpersonal functioning. Such investigations may help clarify
   545→   545→whether self-coherence represents a transdiagnostic process linking multiple levels of psychological
   546→   546→organization.
   547→   547→A second challenge concerns the role of self-coherence in psychological adaptation and
   548→   548→psychopathology. The proposed model suggests that disturbances in self-coherence may represent a
   549→   549→transdiagnostic source of vulnerability, whereas therapeutic change may involve the restoration of
   550→   550→continuity across embodied, relational, and autobiographical dimensions of experience. Longitudinal
   551→   551→and process-oriented methodologies may be particularly useful for examining these hypotheses and
   552→   552→for clarifying how self-coherence develops, fluctuates, and reorganizes across the lifespan.
   553→   553→Future research should investigate whether self-coherence can be empirically modeled as a higher-
   554→   554→order organizational process integrating constructs traditionally studied in isolation, including
   555→   555→embodiment, identity, autobiographical reasoning, interpersonal functioning, and self-regulation.
   556→   556→Such work will also need to determine the extent to which self-coherence can be empirically
   557→   557→distinguished from related constructs such as self-continuity, identity integration, and self-concept
   558→   558→clarity, thereby clarifying its unique contribution to contemporary theories of selfhood.
   559→   559→7. Conclusion
   560→   560→Existing theories have tended to explain continuity from a single level of analysis, whether bodily,
   561→   561→cognitive, narrative, relational, or regulatory. The perspective advanced here suggests that continuity
   562→   562→is not the outcome of any single psychological process. Rather, it emerges from the dynamic
   563→   563→coordination of embodied, relational, narrative, and meaning-making systems across time.
   564→   564→Accordingly, self-coherence is conceptualized as a fundamental organizational principle of selfhood.
   565→   565→Rather than viewing the self as a static entity, personality trait, or cognitive representation, this model
   566→   566→conceives selfhood as a temporally extended process through which continuity is maintained across
   567→   567→changing bodily, emotional, relational, and environmental conditions.
   568→   568→Within this view, embodiment, predictive processing, identity formation, personal meaning
   569→   569→organization, and self-regulation represent complementary mechanisms operating at different levels
   570→   570→of analysis. Self-coherence does not constitute an additional component of selfhood. Instead, it refers
   571→   571→to the higher-order organizational process through which these mechanisms are integrated, allowing
   572→   572→continuity to be established, maintained, and restored across changing circumstances.
   573→   573→By bringing together phenomenological, embodied, predictive, developmental, and constructivist
   574→   574→perspectives, the proposed model offers a common framework for understanding how individuals
   575→   575→preserve a coherent sense of self while continuously adapting to change. Psychological adaptation
