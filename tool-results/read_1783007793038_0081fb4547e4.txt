   201→   201→decisions are often more complex because experiences are dynamic and reciprocal over time.
   202→   202→2.2 Translating DAG features to a common language via a storm metaphor.
   203→   203→These terms are central to how DAGs work, but can be difficult to engage with without
   204→   204→translation into more familiar language. To support this translation, we used a storm metaphor
   205→   205→to explain these two core features of DAGs: taking a direction and not going in a loop. We chose
   206→   206→a metaphor that did not rely solely on technical or verbal explanations, but instead used familiar
   207→   207→
   208→   208→visual and sensory concepts that would be universally relatable and easy-to-understand for all
   209→   209→ages, cultures, and genders.
   210→   210→We also selected the storm metaphor because storms contain multiple components that can
   211→   211→be used to represent features of DAGs.
   212→   212→First, storm components have flexible temporality: for example, rain may occur before or after
   213→   213→sunshine. This mirrors the challenge of deciding how variables should be ordered when there is
   214→   214→no single obvious temporal sequence, and provides a tool for discussing and debating this
   215→   215→order.
   216→   216→Second, storms are dynamic: their components can change in intensity, such as rain being light
   217→   217→or heavy. This allowed discussion of modifiable and changeable aspects of pain impact, such
   218→   218→as family relationships or symptoms of anxiety, which may also vary in intensity over time.
   219→   219→Third, storm components often carry a broadly understood valence: for example, rainbows may
   220→   220→be viewed positively, rain negatively, and lightning as sudden or threatening. This provided a
   221→   221→language for discussing prioritisation and direction of influence, including whether a concept
   222→   222→may worsen or buffer another process (so both the rain and the cloud might link in the same
   223→   223→direction to the thunder, but the rain makes it worse, and the rainbow makes it better).
   224→   224→We proposed that this metaphor would help researchers, clinicians and experts by experience
   225→   225→think together and co-construct statistical models. In doing so, it created conditions for new
   226→   226→and creative discussion about how to prioritise and order variables in the way that DAG
   227→   227→development requires. The building blocks of a DAG, and how each was translated into a
   228→   228→building block of a storm, are shown in Box 1.
   229→   229→
   230→   230→BOX 1: Mirroring of Epidemiological Concepts with Storm Metaphor
   231→   231→OUTCOMES:
   232→   232→Epidemiology - This is the presenting problem, the end point, symptoms or health condition.
   233→   233→Storm - The storm itself. How long it lasts/how it feels, the impact it has, which component (rain, thunder or
   234→   234→a combination) is experienced as the problem.
   235→   235→EXPOSURES:
   236→   236→Epidemiology - These are possible causes, things that may have led to the problem, triggers/origins,
   237→   237→underlying issues.
   238→   238→Storm - The hot weather that set off the storm perhaps.
   239→   239→MEDIATORS:
   240→   240→Epidemiology - These are things that happen along the way, knock on effects that make things worse,
   241→   241→‘triggers’ or ‘glimmers’ that make things better
   242→   242→Storm - The rainbow or sun breaking through the rain cloud, if the psychological impact of the storm were our
   243→   243→outcome.
   244→   244→MODERATORS:
   245→   245→Epidemiology - These are things that happen along the way, but not in a chain of events, they are outside
   246→   246→influences.
   247→   247→Storm - Elements of the environment that amplify noise or provide shelter.
   248→   248→CONFOUNDERS:
   249→   249→Epidemiology - Common causes of the exposure and outcome, but which do not directly link the pathway
   250→   250→between the cause and the problem.
   251→   251→Storm - the thunder in the rainstorm is also occurring but the thunder is not the part of the storm making you
   252→   252→cold and wet. These factors are important because if you focus on them, it will not help (e.g., if you do not
   253→   253→want to get wet, you need to avoid the rain not the thunder).

   284→   284→2.4 Overall procedure of co-development of the initial DAG live workshops.
   285→   285→At the start of each workshop, attendees were introduced to DAGs and to the concepts of
   286→   286→outcomes, exposures, mediators and confounders using the storm metaphor, presented
   287→   287→through a PowerPoint slide deck (see supplementary file). Each DAG component was then
   288→   288→considered in turn. For each component, RP presented the relevant definition and metaphor,
   289→   289→which remained visible on screen while attendees were given 5 minutes for individual reflection.
   290→   290→During this time, attendees were asked to consider the definition and generate up to five
   291→   291→possible ideas for variables or constructs that might fit. Attendees could record their ideas on
   292→   292→physical or virtual post-it notes, share verbally, or enter into the online chat function.
   293→   293→The session was divided equally across each component, with approximately 10–15 minutes
   294→   294→spent on each. After individual reflection, attendees each outlined their ideas, listened to
   295→   295→others’ suggestions and discussed as a group. For outcomes and exposures, attendees were
   296→   296→then asked to prioritise just one variable or construct. For mediating, moderating and
   297→   297→confounding variables, as many constructs as mentioned could be included.
   298→   298→Discussion followed the structure of the DAG, beginning with the outcome, then identifying
   299→   299→exposures, mediators and confounders. Particular attention was given to temporal ordering,
   300→   300→with participants encouraged to consider when each variable would occur relative to the others,
   301→   301→for example what happens first, what follows, and what may occur simultaneously. This helped
   302→   302→distinguish between the components and ensure that proposed relationships were temporally
   303→   303→plausible.
   304→   304→Agreement was reached through an iterative process. Concepts suggested by multiple
   305→   305→attendees were prioritised and, where possible, the most frequently mentioned option was
   306→   306→taken forward. Where suggestions differed, attendees discussed whether they represented
   307→   307→distinct concepts or different ways of describing a shared underlying idea. For outcomes and
   308→   308→exposures, where only one variable was selected, the facilitator supported further discussion
   309→   309→until a pragmatic consensus was reached. Then attendees considered when each concept
   310→   310→occurred, how it related to other variables, and whether it should be represented as an
   311→   311→exposure, mediator, moderator or confounder. Where disagreements occurred, the group
   312→   312→discussed both the concept itself and its timing, considering alternative ways to represent or
   313→   313→integrate it within the sequence of events. This process produced a shared set of variables and
   314→   314→relationships that formed the building blocks for a coherent research question and subsequent
   315→   315→DAG.
   316→   316→The agreed variables and relationships were then entered into DAGitty, moving the discussion
   317→   317→from generating and prioritising ideas to specification of the causal structure. The visual
   318→   318→placement of variables was was used to reflect the agreed conceptual and temporal order,
   319→   319→while arrows represented the hypothesised direction of influence. This step required attendees
   320→   320→to define and decide each variable’s role from the perspective of the chosen exposure-outcome
   321→   321→pathway, because the same concept may function differently across research questions. For
   322→   322→example, a variable that acts as a mediator in one pathway may be an exposure or confounder
   323→   323→in another.

   409→   409→their DAG, suggesting how some factors intertwined to have better or worse effects on pain. Key

   423→   423→model but were represented as change in variables e.g., multiple bidirectional arrows linked to

   443→   443→4. Discussion
   444→   444→In summary, in using the storm metaphor and iterative visual mapping process we have
   445→   445→demonstrated a structured approach for developing a common language around data and
   446→   446→causal thinking, and for translating this into meaningful and testable DAGs. The process also
   447→   447→supported equitable contribution from a wide and mixed group of attendees by creating
   448→   448→opportunities for all participants to suggest, prioritise and discuss variables, as well as their
   449→   449→placement within the DAG. In this way, decisions about what was included, how concepts were
   450→   450→ordered, and how relationships were represented were shaped collectively, where public
   451→   451→contribution and varied research / clinical opinion could be equally weighted. The following
   452→   452→section outlines the key learning and reflections from the DAG generation exercise.
   453→   453→4. 1 Action 1- Reconceptualising the ‘outcome’ as an ‘exposure-outcome’ relationship
   454→   454→When discussing the ‘problem’ and separating it out from the rest of the mechanisms it became
   455→   455→clear in both groups that the part people wanted to change most was not pain, but the impact
   456→   456→pain has on life. Pain was therefore not viewed as the outcome, but rather ongoing pain that
   457→   457→iteratively impacts lives was the focus. This reflects a temporal aspect to pain, and the
   458→   458→conceptual approach taken by the CRIISP project around pain states and transition (10). It is
   459→   459→thus an exposure-outcome relationship that we wanted to understand, how pain impacts on
   460→   460→lives and how this unfolds over time i.e., gets better, gets worse or is maintained. Utilising the
   461→   461→DAG approach required reconceptualising the outcome as an exposure-outcome relationship.
   462→   462→In DAG terms, pain impact at time 1 (baseline) is the exposure, and the outcome is continued
   463→   463→impact at time 2 (follow-up). Figure 10 demonstrates the detailed representation of the
   464→   464→exposure to outcome parameterisation, that in the integrated DAG was collapsed across
   465→   465→domains of impact and simplified to ‘extent of pain impact’.
   466→   466→
   467→   467→Figure 10. Representation of the exposure to outcome relationship in the DAG process. The
   468→   468→figure shown is the ‘raw’ DAG from the workshops with variables labelled as they were in initial
   469→   469→discussions before consolidation.
   470→   470→4.2 The role of time and identification of key processes
   471→   471→Another area of shared learning was the importance of time in the definition of our exposure and
   472→   472→outcome. Time is a powerful tool in epidemiology, but its complexity can be overlooked. In
   473→   473→DAGs, the timing and sequence of events are often used to guide the direction of arrows:
   474→   474→causes should occur before their consequences. This can help researchers order variables,
   475→   475→trace possible chains of events, and identify appropriate measures across longitudinal
   476→   476→datasets.
   477→   477→However, the order of events can become restrictive if time is taken too literally. For example,
   478→   478→the point at which a life event occurs may differ from the point at which the event had its
   479→   479→greatest impact. Similarly, a reaction to pain that has negative short-term consequence may
   480→   480→have a positive longer-term impact. In the workshops, visual spacing was therefore used to
   481→   481→represent the meaning and perceived impact of events rather than the exact temporal distance
   482→   482→between them.
   483→   483→This was reflected in the initial DAGs, where a large cluster of variables occupied substantial
   484→   484→visual space because they represented the initial reaction to pain, even though this period may
   485→   485→only have lasted a few days. Time is important to keep in mind in DAGs.
   486→   486→
   487→   487→4.3 Usefulness of the approach to disentangle proposed mediators and moderators
   488→   488→The approach enabled the groups to map variables and together make difficult decisions on
   489→   489→whether or not they are mediators or moderators. Key examples of this are given below.
   490→   490→Figure 11. Zoomed in to one key prioritised pathway in the final integrated DAG
   491→   491→4.3.1 Internal mediators (reactivity, confidence and identity)
   492→   492→The initial variables discussed as part of the pathway ‘within’ the chain of events, therefore
   493→   493→acting as mediators in epidemiology terms) involved feelings of uncertainty. Identity loss linked
   494→   494→to uncertainty was placed in the more ‘immediate time’ close to the exposure variables by the
   495→   495→WDG group and agreed in consolidation processes with researchers as an early mediating
   496→   496→variable.
   497→   497→4.3.2 Interpersonal mediators (relationships)
   498→   498→The consideration of relational processes as a ‘mediator’ rather than a potential risk factor (i.e.
   499→   499→exposure) or ‘moderator’ was also carefully considered. In epidemiological terms a moderator is
   500→   500→external to the pathway, but in some way alters the strength or direction between two variables.
   501→   501→The storm metaphor enabled the placement of relationships as a mediator, it provided the
   502→   502→common language which meant public contributors clarified that relationships were not
   503→   503→something outside of the ‘storm’ like a rainbow, but part of the chain of events ‘within’ the core
   504→   504→
   505→   505→sequence that links pain to its wider impact on peoples’ lives. The mechanisms informing the
   506→   506→direction of the link between initial pain and social connections included not wanting to feel like
   507→   507→a burden, pain symptom experience impacting social ‘performance’ and masking of pain.
   508→   508→Representing relational impact as a mediator is then important because this implies that if
   509→   509→psychosocial interventions focus on harnessing positive support in relationships, even if they
   510→   510→don’t reduce pain, they could reduce pain impact.
   511→   511→4.4 Mapping the differences between DAGs
   512→   512→Comparing the two initial DAGs also allowed us to explore differences in how concepts were
   513→   513→prioritised and represented between public contributors and researchers. For example,
   514→   514→medication use, and side effects, were prominent in the experts-by-experience DAG, but not in
   515→   515→the researcher DAG. On discussion, the group agreed that these concepts were partly captured
   516→   516→through the broader concepts of intervention and physical state, with physical state understood
   517→   517→as the combined manifestation of the pain itself and any medication and side effects.
   518→   518→Conversely, sleep was explicitly included in the researchers DAG but not the initial experts-by-
   519→   519→experience DAG. On consolidation the group felt this to be relevant to both the uncertainty and
   520→   520→ongoing impact of pain.
   521→   521→4.5 Comparison to previous co-designed DAGs:
   522→   522→Previous researchers have developed a similar approach, and they share the same emphasis on
   523→   523→the value of the perspective of those with lived experience of chronic pain, and the importance
   524→   524→of ensuring that all aspects of DAGs are explained to those without a clinical or research
   525→   525→background using accessible language (4). However, our approach differs due to the use of the
   526→   526→storm metaphor which provides a systematic creative method to support inclusion. The
   527→   527→metaphor translates the more conceptually challenging and technical features of DAGs (e.g.
   528→   528→the difference between mediators, moderators and confounders) into more familiar concepts
   529→   529→(e.g. the sun, shelter and thunder). This allowed multiple stakeholders to contribute
   530→   530→meaningfully to the development of future research questions. In addition, it was suitable to the
   531→   531→specific psychosocial mechanisms of interest to the consortium.
   532→   532→4.6 Strengths
   533→   533→This approach has several strengths. Firstly, the use of the storm metaphor created a common
   534→   534→language that allowed researchers and experts by experience to have equity in the decisions
   535→   535→made. It placed equal value on variable input as well as where they were placed in the DAGs. It
   536→   536→also created a map to allow us to combine different parts of the mechanisms proposed by the
   537→   537→DAG, but to then isolate individual pathways that can be tested in specific datasets. In the
   538→   538→context of the CRIISP project this was important given different research teams were working on
   539→   539→specific datasets, but the consortium as a whole sought to harmonise research questions,
   540→   540→analytical approaches, and findings across datasets. Having a clear framework provided a way
   541→   541→to test specific pathways where data allowed, while maintaining a common mission and
   542→   542→structure for combining findings later on.
   543→   543→An important by-product of this approach was wider engagement of public contributors, who
   544→   544→had the opportunity to share decision-making power by shaping key choices in what variables
   545→   545→were considered as outcomes and exposures, and the direction of arrows between them. It
   546→   546→
   547→   547→enabled those with lived experience of chronic pain to shape future research and to ensure it
   548→   548→was meaningful to their experiences, while being accessible and enjoyable, which also
   549→   549→increased general engagement with the CRIISP project. Ultimately, the approach has led to
   550→   550→more impactful research. In this specific example, the produced DAG model has been
   551→   551→empirically tested in real data and has led to evidence for important and previously unknown
   552→   552→pathways highlighting the impact of pain in emerging adults on disrupting later social
   553→   553→relationships (11). While the link between pain and relationships was already considered in the
   554→   554→literature, the direction of the association (going from pain impact to relationships) and
   555→   555→specification of relationships as a mediating variable was a direct consequence and change in
   556→   556→model specification following the Dream-DAG co-design. Put simply, if not for the co-design
   557→   557→framework the specific mediating pathway would not have been included.
   558→   558→Limitations
   559→   559→Some limitations of this approach should be noted. For example, DAGs can be incredibly
   560→   560→complex, and it can be impossible to test some pathways due to unavailability of data (for
   561→   561→example, there is no measure of uncertainty). Also, the concept of effect modification can be
   562→   562→difficult to represent in DAGs. Although some guidance does exist (12) it is currently not a
   563→   563→programmed feature in DAGItty. Furthermore, the use of the storm metaphor can also be seen
   564→   564→as limited in its approach where, for example, features of the storm (rain, sun, wind etc) do not
   565→   565→fully mirror the complexity and number of features of most causal models. There is scope,
   566→   566→however, to consider the use of other metaphors in future research, as we hypothesise that it is
   567→   567→not the metaphor itself per se, but rather the process that offers a simplified, universal and
   568→   568→relatable concept that is de-personalised from one’s own story. Furthermore, only a small
   569→   569→number of experts by experience and experts by academic or clinical training were included.
   570→   570→While this was appropriate for the nature of the involvement activities, which required space for
   571→   571→discussion and input, it may have limited the breadth of perspectives represented. The outlined
   572→   572→process/framework can now inform future work that could build on this by engaging with more
   573→   573→diverse groups of contributors.
   574→   574→Conclusion
   575→   575→The final integrated Dream-DAG poses an accessible, interactive consultation and engagement
   576→   576→activity which mapped complex processes and produced unique and testable models. In future
   577→   577→steps the CRIISP team are empirically testing research questions from the CRIISP Dream-DAG
   578→   578→in existing data sets and guiding future decisions on measurement of missing concepts, such as
   579→   579→identity and uncertainty of pain.
