    25→Abstract
    26→Objectives. Intervention fidelity is critical for interpreting trial outcomes, yet fidelity in digital
    27→behaviour change interventions (DBCI) such as ecological momentary interventions (EMIs) is
    28→poorly understood. This qualitative study explored participant experiences of intervention
    29→fidelity (delivery, receipt, enactment) of a randomised controlled trial (RCT) testing habit
    30→degradation strategies including implementation intentions, and the effect of reward on
    31→degrading unhealthy snacking habits in daily life. This study aimed to deepen the understanding
    32→of intervention fidelity from participants’ perspectives, while also generating generalisable
    33→lessons for DBCI.
    34→Design. Preregistered semi-structured follow-up interviews.
    35→Methods. Twenty-two former RCT participants were purposively sampled for heterogeneity
    36→across intervention groups. Interviews were conducted online using personalised graphical
    37→elicitation materials. Data were analysed using template analysis, with initial coding informed
    38→by three fidelity domains: delivery, receipt, and enactment.
    39→Results. Eight themes were identified across three interacting fidelity domains. Delivery
    40→concerns included technical disruptions and cue selection that shaped reward delivery. Receipt
    41→challenges included mismatches between if-then plans and assigned strategies. Enactment
    42→themes revealed that participants generalised behaviour change beyond their if-then plan,
    43→pragmatically employed multiple strategies, and were influenced by physical, social, and
    44→internal factors. Reactions to reward features were mixed, self-monitoring through the app
    45→facilitated behaviour change, and some participants experienced snacking to shift to other
    46→situations.
    47→Conclusions. This qualitative fidelity study revealed participant-experienced processes—
    48→including pragmatic adaptation and self-monitoring reactivity—that typical quantitative fidelity
    49→metrics do not capture. These processes should be anticipated in EMI study design, and
    50→qualitative fidelity assessment routinely integrated into such research.

    54→What is already known on this subject?
    55→Intervention fidelity is essential for interpreting trial outcomes. Three participant-centred
    56→fidelity domains are recognized (delivery, receipt, and enactment). Quantitative fidelity checks
    57→can reveal whether but not how deviations from protocol occur.

    59→• With ecological momentary intervention designs, fidelity domains are interconnected

    65→Introduction
    66→Digital behaviour change interventions (DBCI), including ecological momentary interventions
    67→(EMIs) (Heron & Smyth, 2010) have flourished alongside technological advances and
    68→widespread ownership of smartphones (Arigo et al., 2019). Research on DBCI and EMIs has
    69→devoted considerable attention to conceptualising engagement and its role in intervention
    70→efficacy (Arigo et al., 2019; Cole-Lewis et al., 2019; Eaton et al., 2024; Perski et al., 2017), yet
    71→far less is known about fidelity. Intervention fidelity encompasses the methodological strategies
    72→used to monitor and enhance the reliability and validity of behavioural interventions (Bellg et
    73→al., 2004). Assessing fidelity is essential for interpreting outcomes, as it determines whether
    74→observed effects reflect the intervention as designed or deviations in its implementation (Bellg
    75→et al., 2004; Toomey et al., 2020).
    76→EMIs include interventions delivered by smartphone apps, embedded within daily life, with
    77→content tailored to incoming data (Arigo et al., 2019). A defining feature is that EMI success
    78→depends heavily on how participants understand and translate instructions into action amid daily
    79→life complexities. This creates a need to understand not only whether participants engage with
    80→EMIs, but also whether interventions are enacted as intended—a question of intervention
    81→fidelity.
    82→Five domains are recommended for a comprehensive assessment of intervention fidelity: study
    83→design, provider training, and intervention delivery, receipt, and enactment (Bellg et al., 2004).
    84→Fidelity-related research practices could be improved broadly—including in non-DBCI studies
    85→(Beck et al., 2023; Begum et al., 2021; Kechter et al., 2019; Lambert et al., 2017), though
    86→comprehensive examples do exist (e.g. Lowers et al., 2025). The first two concern researcher
    87→actions before implementation, whereas the latter three concern how participants experience the
    88→intervention. Specifically, delivery refers to whether content is provided as planned; receipt to
    89→whether participants understand and can demonstrate the skills taught; and enactment to
    90→whether they apply those skills in daily life (Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). These
    91→participant-centred fidelity domains are complex and interrelated, which makes qualitative
    92→methods well suited to generating a contextualised understanding of them (Palsola et al.,
    93→2020)—particularly for EMIs, where fidelity remains largely unexplored.
    94→The present study
    95→To fill this gap, the present study investigates intervention fidelity from participants’
    96→perspective, using a qualitative approach, at the example of an ecological momentary unhealthy
    97→snacking habit degradation intervention. Habits are cognitively represented cue–behaviour
    98→4
    99→
   100→associations acquired through the repeated pairing of a cue and behaviour, and are characterised
   101→by automaticity (Fleetwood, 2021; Moors & De Houwer, 2006; Wood & Rünger, 2016). In daily
   102→life, habits can sustain health-risk behaviours such as unhealthy snacking and resist deliberate
   103→change efforts (Gardner et al., 2021, 2024). Theory-based strategies for habit degradation
   104→include substitution (replacing the habitual response with an alternative behaviour at cue
   105→encounters), inhibition (wilfully stopping performance at cue encounters), reduced accessibility
   106→(limiting the availability of the habitual behaviour), and cue discontinuation (avoiding the cue)
   107→(Edgren & Inauen, 2026; Gardner et al., 2024).
   108→A recent intensive longitudinal randomised controlled trial (RCT) investigated the efficacy of
   109→three habit degradation strategies and reward in degrading unhealthy snacking habits in daily
   110→life (Edgren et al., 2026). Participants formulated an implementation intention (Gollwitzer,
   111→1993)—also known as an if-then plan—consistent with their assigned strategy. Habit strength
   112→declined across all groups, with a significantly faster rate of change during the first week in
   113→intervention groups compared to control. However, no credible evidence was found for
   114→differential effects between the strategy or reward conditions (Edgren et al., 2026).
   115→Quantitative fidelity checks in the trial revealed several concerns that might have contributed
   116→to the absence of differences between the degradation strategy conditions (Edgren et al., 2026).
   117→First, 35% of implementation intentions did not match the assigned strategy. Second, a
   118→substantial proportion of participants reported using additional strategies beyond those
   119→assigned, indicating blended strategy use. Third, responding to the event-contingent
   120→questionnaire (completed upon encountering the cue) was infrequent, which limited delivery of
   121→the reward manipulation, as reward was contingent on these responses. These fidelity findings
   122→constrain the conclusions that can be drawn from the trial and point to a need for deeper
   123→understanding of how participants experienced and engaged with the intervention.
   124→Accordingly, the present study addressed the research question: What were participants'
   125→experiences relating to intervention delivery, receipt, and enactment in this habit degradation
   126→EMI? Beyond this intervention, the study offers methodological lessons for intensive
   127→longitudinal EMI and DBCI research.

   130→Methods
   131→This preregistered qualitative study (https://osf.io/kepjr/) employed semi-structured follow-up
   132→interviews with a subsample of participants from the preceding RCT on habit degradation in
   133→daily life (Edgren et al., 2026). Interviews also covered participants' experiences using the Self-
   134→Report Behavioural Automaticity Index (Gardner et al., 2012; Verplanken & Orbell, 2003),
   135→which this manuscript does not address. Study reporting adheres to the Journal Article
   136→Reporting Standards for Qualitative Research (Levitt et al., 2018). See supplementary file for
   137→details on use of artificial intelligence.
   138→Sample
   139→German speaking adults without diabetes or an eating disorder and a habit of eating unhealthy
   140→snacks at home were eligible for the RCT (Edgren et al., 2026). Purposive sampling was used
   141→to recruit a heterogeneous subsample of former RCT participants. Sampling aimed to recruit 20
   142→participants with representation across all intervention groups (5 participants from each of
   143→substitution, inhibition, reduced accessibility and control conditions, including 5 from the
   144→reward condition). Out of 51 email invitations sent, 22 participants provided informed consent
   145→and were interviewed. The mean age of the interviewed sample was 33.1 years (SD = 10.9;
   146→range 19-69). Sixteen participants (72 %) identified with female gender. Sample size by
   147→intervention group was: substitution n = 7, inhibition n = 6, reduced accessibility n = 4, and
   148→control n = 5; of these, 7 participants had been assigned to the reward condition. Interviews
   149→took place between December 2024 and February 2025 and occurred from 24 to 159 days (mean
   150→= 109 days) after the planned end date of participation in the preceding RCT.
   151→Procedure
   152→The preceding RCT used a 3 (strategy: substitution, inhibition, reduced accessibility) × 2
   153→(reward: yes/no) between-person factorial design, plus additional control group (Edgren et al.,
   154→2026). Over 91 days, 313 participants completed daily end-of-day questionnaires through a
   155→purpose-built study app. After a 7-day cue identification phase, participants selected a habitual
   156→snacking-related cue. On Day 7, participants were randomised and those in intervention groups
   157→instructed to formulate an implementation intention consistent with their assigned strategy and
   158→cue. From Day 8, they independently initiated event-contingent questionnaire responses upon
   159→encountering their cue. Additionally, participants in reward conditions received in-app reward
   160→messages contingent on reporting no snacking following a cue encounter. For full details on the
   161→trial design and procedures, see Edgren et al. (2026). Table 1 summarises the intended behaviour
   162→change techniques (Michie et al., 2013) of the RCT. The control group received no BCTs.

   187→Interviews were conducted online in German and Swiss German by three psychology student

   193→Data processing
   194→Audio recordings of the interviews were transcribed verbatim using the aTrain software (Haberl
   195→et al., 2024). All transcripts were manually checked for accuracy and anonymised by AH.
   196→Participant quotes displayed in the manuscript were translated with the DeepL translator (DeepL
   197→SE, 2026) and manually checked for accuracy by AH.
   198→Analysis
   199→Template analysis (King, 2012; Tabari et al., 2020) of the original language data was conducted
   200→with MAXQDA 24 (VERBI Software, 2026), marking a deviation from the protocol’s thematic
   201→analysis. Template analysis is a form of thematic analysis that involves the iterative
   202→7
   203→
   204→development of a coding template, refined as analysis proceeds. Text passages of any length
   205→sufficient to convey relevant information were considered as units of analysis. AH conducted
   206→the primary coding in close collaboration with RE and JI. Initial coding began with two
   207→interviews, after which a feedback and reflexivity meeting was held with RE and JI. Three
   208→additional interviews were then coded, followed by another research group meeting.
   209→Subsequent meetings were held after every five interviews. Previously coded material was
   210→revisited each round to refine the coding template and ensure alignment of themes and quotes.
   211→The self-defined cues and if-then plans participants created during the RCT also served as
   212→analytic materials, as these relate centrally to intervention fidelity (and were verbalised during
   213→the interviews). Participants did not provide feedback on findings.
   214→Initial coding was informed by the three participant centred domains of intervention fidelity
   215→(Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). Here, delivery referred to the extent to which the study
   216→app delivered the intervention as intended by the researchers, receipt referred to the degree to
   217→which participants understood the intervention instructions and could demonstrate the relevant
   218→skills, and enactment referred to the application of the acquired skills in participants' daily lives.
   219→During the development of the coding template, some codes emerged as well-established and
   220→generalisable across participants, having been mentioned recurrently and lending themselves
   221→readily to description at a higher level of abstraction. Other codes were closely tied to individual
   222→participants' accounts and less readily generalisable but nonetheless retained for analysis.
   223→In the results, each quote is accompanied by the participant ID, which indicates group
   224→allocation: substitution (SUB), inhibition (INH), reduced accessibility (RAC), or control
   225→(CON), along with the reward condition: reward (R) or no reward (NR). Line references from
   226→the interview transcripts are also provided.
   227→Researcher descriptions and epistemological stance
   228→The authors RE and JI are actively engaged in intensive longitudinal habit research, and they
   229→designed and conducted the preceding RCT. This proximity to the trial informed expectations
   230→regarding potential fidelity challenges—for instance, anticipating emergence of strategy
   231→blending as theme. AH, who conducted the coding, was not involved in the RCT but did conduct
   232→some of the interviews, which provided familiarity with participants' accounts while
   233→maintaining analytic distance from the trial's design and hypotheses. The epistemological
   234→approach of this study is grounded in descriptive phenomenology (Willig, 2012) whereby
   235→participants' accounts of their experiences with the intervention were taken at face value as
   236→descriptions of their lived experience.

   240→Template analysis resulted in eight themes describing participant experiences relating to
   241→intervention fidelity. For an overview of the themes, see Table 2. Themes are described in detail
   242→below.
   243→Theme 1: Technical disruptions to app use
   244→A few participants experienced technical issues with the study app. Although each problem was
   245→quickly resolved, these incidents still affected app use for a short period: “There was an update
   246→once and then it [the app] stopped working. I reported this afterwards and then it worked again.
   247→[…] It didn’t work for a day or two, and then it worked again” (P42_INH_R: 255). Most
   248→participants did not report any technical difficulties that affected their overall experience:
   249→Overall, I found it [the app] quite intuitive and reliable – it always worked, I never had
   250→any major problems, it never crashed, and I never felt like I was missing out on anything.
   251→[…] from that point of view I really have to say, yes, I’ve had a good experience with it
   252→(P35_CON_NR: 69)

   299→Theme 2: Cue selection affected frequency and location of cue encounters and
   300→opportunities for engaging with intervention
   301→At the beginning of the intervention, participants were instructed to select cues that they
   302→encountered regularly and in the home environment, which participants fulfilled to varying
   303→degrees. For example, one participant who adhered to the cue selection instructions chose the
   304→cue ‘cravings & [partner’s name]’ and reported: “This is a situation that occurs daily, on the
   305→sofa, in the evening, after dinner in front of the TV” (P27_RAC_NR: 27). However, for some
   306→participants, the selected cue did not occur very often or at all during the study, or not
   307→exclusively in the home environment: “I don’t mainly snack at home, but rather mostly when
   308→I’m out and about” (P12_CON_NR: 49).
   309→Infrequent cue encounters led to fewer opportunities for engaging with the reward features of
   310→the app. A participant who initially chose ‘boredom’ as their cue mentioned: “If I had chosen
   311→stress as a trigger, I would probably have used it [reward features] more. Then I would have
   312→gotten more rewards. I think it might have been a bit different there if I had chosen a different
   313→trigger” (P25_SUB_R: 289). Some participants discovered more suitable cues over the course
   314→of the study and expressed a wish to change their cue, which the study protocol did not allow;
   315→the same participant reported: “Over the course of the study, I got the feeling that ‘boredom’
   316→probably wasn’t quite 100% accurate. Perhaps ‘stress’ would have been a better choice. With
   317→‘stress’ as an additional cue, the results might have been even more meaningful” (P25_SUB_R:
   318→225).
   319→Theme 3: Mismatch of if-then plans with assigned strategy and selected cue
   320→Participants in the three intervention conditions (n = 17) were randomly assigned one habit
   321→degradation strategy and asked to form a corresponding if-then plan. Most adhered well: plans
   322→matched the assigned strategy in 11 of 17 cases and incorporated the selected cue in 14 of 17
   323→cases. Where plans diverged, two patterns were evident. Some did not match the assigned
   324→strategy. For instance, a participant with the cue 'Hunger, waiting for dinner' who was assigned
   325→the inhibition strategy formulated the plan: "When I'm hungry and waiting for dinner, I drink a
   326→glass of tap water" (P7_INH_NR), which corresponds to substitution rather than inhibition.
   327→Others did not incorporate their selected cue. For example, a participant with the cue 'coffee
   328→break in the afternoon' formulated the if-then plan: "When I'm stressed, I won't buy any snacks
   329→and will give the open packages to my boyfriend" (P10_RAC_R), which references a different
   330→situational context than the cue originally selected.

   333→Theme 4: Plan visibility, self-monitoring and accountability through the app facilitated
   334→behaviour change
   335→Interviewed participants completed the RCT’s end-of-day questionnaire frequently, providing
   336→on average 71 responses (SD = 11.0; range 38–83), corresponding to a mean compliance rate
   337→of 78% (range 42–91%). Many described this daily engagement as supportive of behaviour
   338→change. Being able to view their if-then plans within the app was one such support: "The fact
   339→that I had it [the if-then plan] written down, that I actually saw it in this app, if that happens,
   340→what do I have to do, how do I do it. The visual aspect helped me a lot" (P10_RAC_R: 241).
   341→Similarly, using the app and answering questionnaires within it increased participants’
   342→awareness of their behaviour and habits, which in turn helped them change their behaviour:
   343→I would say that the daily confrontation with oneself, which one was forced into through
   344→the device and the app, certainly also led to a more general and deeper questioning of
   345→every act of snack consumption. I really noticed that I either snacked less or that I asked
   346→myself beforehand whether it was about stress relief or enjoyment. (P25_SUB_R: 21)
   347→In a similar vein, the questionnaire functioned as a reminder of participants’ goals: “I actually
   348→found it helpful to get a reminder like that [prompt to fill in daily diary] every evening. Partly
   349→just to remind myself to stop [snacking] or cut down on it” (P21_SUB_NR: 21). Moreover,
   350→several participants noted that they missed this support after the study ended: “I was sad when
   351→the three months were up, when the tracking stopped. [...] It would have helped me, if it could
   352→have been used for a longer period of time” (P27_RAC_NR: 236). Notably, the accountability
   353→provided by the app was also valued: “I think it [reducing the snacking] was easier with a plan
   354→and an app, where I always had something to do, where I had a supervisory body. It was easier
   355→with a plan, with a set time frame” (P_ 25_SUB_R: 169).

   406→Inhibition
   407→Participants who were not assigned this strategy reported that they discovered that actively
   408→saying ‘no’ to snacks helped them change their habitual behaviour. This strategy was applied
   409→externally by verbalising it to others and internally through self-directed statements: “I can still
   410→remember it well, it was Thursday, I had the day off and was able to consciously think to myself,
   411→ah, I don’t want to eat any snacks right now” (P10_RAC_R: 169). Participants also reported
   412→that questioning the value of snacking helped:
   413→Because whenever I had a snack in front of me, I would always think, is this a healthy
   414→snack, or is it a less healthy snack? How should I deal with it now? Does it really add
   415→any value for me? (P25_SUB_R: 117)
   416→Some participants implemented this strategy by reminding themselves that they had already
   417→eaten: “Or I remind myself, hey, you’ve eaten, and then in the evening we’ll cook together. That
   418→helped me a lot” (P10_RAC_R: 69). One participant mentioned setting a personal limit: “That’s
   419→Take Two. At some point, this just clicks in your head. If I’m snacking on something unhealthy,
   420→I just take two and that’s enough. That has also helped me quite a bit” (P25_SUB_R: 65).
   421→Reduced accessibility
   422→Participants not assigned to this strategy engaged in deliberate shopping behaviours, such as
   423→refraining from purchasing snacks to reduce availability:
   424→When I have it [snack] at home, um, as soon as I open the packet, I eat it. I can’t control
   425→myself at all. And, um, if I don’t buy it and don’t have it at home, I can’t go out and buy
   426→it in the evening, so that’s actually the solution for me. (P26_CON_NR: 93)
   427→Substitution
   428→Some participants self-selected the unassigned substitution strategy, e.g. they ate a healthy
   429→snack or drank a beverage instead of snacking: “Then I just drank some more tea and then I let
   430→it [snacking] be” (P32_RAC_R: 105). Others mentioned going outside: “That I no longer reach
   431→for unhealthy snacks. So, that I might then drink a glass of water or perhaps go outside quickly”
   432→(P10_RAC_R: 69).
   433→Discontinuation
   434→Although discontinuation was not a habit degradation strategy included in this trial, some
   435→participants nonetheless employed this approach by their own initiative. For example, to avoid
   436→14
   437→
   438→the onset of the cue ‘craving for sweets’, one participant mentioned eating sufficiently at main
   439→meals:
   440→What was very important to me was having enough food at main meals. Not just a small
   441→portion or a salad, but something substantial that keeps you feeling full for a long time.
   442→That way, you don’t get those cravings. I started doing that relatively early on, and it
   443→helped me. (P48_INH_R: 261)
   444→Another participant with the cue ‘stress’ reported doing relaxation exercises in the morning to
   445→prevent stress during the day:
   446→I do a lot more relaxation exercises in my daily life, or perhaps do some yoga exercises
   447→in the morning before work, which really do help me relax a bit. [...] So that you don’t
   448→start the day with a high stress level, which makes things more difficult. (P36_INH_R:
   449→37–41)
   450→Preparatory actions
   451→Some participants found preparatory actions helpful, such as preparing substitutes beforehand.
   452→For instance, one participant prepared a glass of water in the morning in preparation for
   453→encountering the cue ‘seeing snacks when coming home’: “I’d already prepared a glass that
   454→morning – for the glass of water I could drink” (P5_SUB_NR: 137). Other preparatory actions
   455→did not directly relate to the focal cue–behaviour pairing.
   456→Theme 6: Contextual factors (physical, social, internal) impacted behaviour change
   457→Participants described a range of contextual factors that facilitated enactment of the habit
   458→degradation strategies and behaviour change for some and hindered it for others, as
   459→subsequently described.
   460→Physical context
   461→The ease of changing behaviour differed based on location. For instance, being at work was
   462→perceived positively by some: “I didn’t have that many snacks there [at work] because we don’t
   463→have a break room. […] there wasn’t anything else lying around or anything like that”
   464→(P32_RAC_R: 53). Others expressed being at home as an obstacle: “When you have bad habits
   465→and you’re at home, you’re more likely to indulge in them than when I am out and about”
   466→(P26_CON_NR: 301). For some, location determined what they were primarily occupied with:
   467→When you’re out and about, or at certain special occasions, or even if you’ve got
   468→something planned at home or something like that, your focus is just completely
   469→15
   470→
   471→elsewhere. In those situations, you can’t – or don’t want to – focus on snacking anymore,
   472→because other things are simply more important. (P5_SUB_NR: 209)
   473→The availability and visibility of snacks in the physical surroundings was also noted to have an
   474→influence: “if there happens to be a piece of cake or tart lying around, it gets eaten very quickly.
   475→I think it’s also the accessibility of the whole thing. If it’s tucked away in a drawer, it’s less
   476→tempting” (P7_INH_NR: 53). Relatedly, seasonal availability was noted: “what really adds to
   477→that is simply the greater availability of unhealthy snacks in the run-up to Christmas”
   478→(P35_CON_NR: 61). In a similar vein, the absence of healthy alternatives could hinder
   479→behaviour change: “I had a few days when I had finished all the vegetables. Then I hadn’t
   480→cooked anything. I didn’t have anything healthy here. And then I ate one of the unhealthy snacks
   481→I had in the cupboard” (P34_SUB_NR: 185).
   482→Social context
   483→Social factors impacted behaviour change in multiple ways. Some reported that others’
   484→behaviour made changing their habit more difficult: “And then I know that I want to join in. If
   485→she [daughter] eats something, I eat with her” (P36_INH_R: 81). Conversely, some reported
   486→benefiting from social support: “I also told the people around me that I was doing this. So, at
   487→work, to my partner and my family. They were, of course, supportive whenever I craved
   488→something sweet” (P48_INH_R: 261). Social support was also expressed in terms of benefiting
   489→from a collaborative effort:
   490→It helped that I told my roommate I was taking part in the study, and she showed a lot
   491→of interest, because she wanted to lose a bit of weight as well. And then we could always
   492→cook something together and support each other. It was really good to have her support
   493→as well. (P34_SUB_NR: 47)
   494→Interestingly, a sense of belonging to a collective with other study participants was also
   495→reported: “when you suddenly find yourself in a group like that, when you don’t actually see
   496→the people, but you know you’re part of something” (P26_CON_NR: 49).
   497→Internal states
   498→Lastly, internal states were noted as impactful contextual factors. For instance, being ill was
   499→perceived as helpful by one participant: “On top of that, I had a cough, a fever, and a runny
   500→nose. And then you don’t have any cravings” (P32_RAC_R: 189). Similarly, insufficient sleep
   501→was a reported barrier: “When I get very little sleep it [snacking habit] is the worst”
   502→(P46_SUB_NR: 58), as were hormonal influences: “Before my period […] I could eat
   503→16
   504→
   505→absolutely anything, from sweet to savory and back again, all the time” (P32_RAC_R: 219).
   506→Other relevant internal states mentioned were declining motivation and stress: “I’ve noticed
   507→that it’s definitely the stress that causes my snacking behaviour to increase” (P35_CON_NR:
   508→61); low energy: “That’s what I thought during the study, that if I’d had a little more energy, I
   509→would have been able to change my behaviour more consistently” (P30_INH_NR: 43); and
   510→boredom: “It [resisting snacking] was particularly difficult when I was very bored and I watched
   511→a series” (P10_RAC_R: 81).
   512→Theme 7: Mixed reactions to reward features
   513→Reward was delivered contingent on event-contingent responses, which participants logged
   514→infrequently: on average 11 cue encounters were logged among the interviewed participants
   515→(SD = 13; range 1–57). The reward features themselves elicited mixed reactions. Some
   516→participants perceived the reward features and progress bar as motivating:
   517→Of course, it was great to see the bar go up once you’d made it. And then, well, a
   518→medal—or I think I got a trophy or something once. In a way that’s also extra motivation
   519→to stick with it, and it makes you happy, too. I’m ambitious, and I’m happy when I get
   520→bronze and then look at it next time and see that I’ve got silver. I thought that was really
   521→great. (P32_RAC_R: 285)
   522→However, some participants forgot about the reward features: “This function [the level with the
   523→medals], I have to be honest, I forgot about it a bit from time to time” (P10_RAC_R: 289),
   524→while others were indifferent toward these features:
   525→I don’t think it had a huge impact on me. Yes. To be honest, I didn’t really realise that
   526→you could move up the levels. […] I probably always stayed at the same level, I don’t
   527→really know. Yes, but I didn’t necessarily feel that was necessary or anything.
   528→(P48_INH_R: 357)
   529→A few participants even found the reward features stressful:
   530→But for me, that [the points, levels and trophy feature] was rather stressful. It meant that
   531→I was constantly thinking about the fact that I hadn’t eaten anything. That stresses me
   532→much more than just managing it myself and checking in the evening. There might be
   533→people who really like doing that, but for me it was more like — I don’t want to keep
   534→focusing even more on the fact that I haven’t eaten anything. (P36_INH_R: 237)
   535→17
   536→
   537→Furthermore, two participants initially reported uncertainty about the app’s operation in this
   538→regard:
   539→The first three times, I didn’t know if I was using it [the app] correctly because it wasn’t
   540→entirely straightforward. And afterwards, I realized that I still had to fill that [the cue
   541→encounter] in additionally. That wasn’t entirely clear to me at the beginning.
   542→(P42_INH_R: 177)
   543→Theme 8: After successful avoidance, snacking shifted to other situations
   544→Some participants reported an unanticipated side effect of successfully using a habit degradation
   545→strategy. Namely, after successful avoidance of unhealthy snacking in response to their cue,
   546→snacking shifted to other situations not covered by the if-then plan:
   547→It was that I focused very much on the coffee break, so that I really wouldn’t take
   548→anything then. But still, I noticed that the urge shifted. I think it maybe moved to the
   549→evening or right after lunch. That was something I realized after the study, that I have to
   550→pay attention, that I simply say the trigger is the afternoon coffee break, and then I allow
   551→myself maybe to snack in the evening or after lunch. I found it very difficult not to shift
   552→it, just because I want to avoid the trigger. (P10_RAC_R: 201)
   553→Discussion
   554→This study explored participant experiences of intervention fidelity in a habit degradation EMI
   555→(Edgren et al., 2026). Template analysis of semi-structured interviews with 22 former trial
   556→participants yielded eight themes spanning the three participant-centred fidelity domains
   557→delivery, receipt, and enactment (Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). Technical disruptions to
   558→app use (Theme 1) concerned delivery; cue selection (Theme 2) and the mismatch between if-
   559→then plans and assigned strategies or cues (Theme 3) primarily concerned receipt; and Themes
   560→4–8 primarily concerned enactment.
   561→As Figure 1 illustrates, the themes did not operate in isolation but interacted, producing
   562→cascading effects across the three domains. For example, suboptimal cue selection arose
   563→principally from misunderstanding how cues should be chosen (receipt) yet also constrained
   564→how reward could later be delivered (Theme 2); and even a well-chosen cue enabled reward
   565→only once cue-contingent responses were actualised in the app (Theme 7; enactment). Some
   566→themes also didn't fit neatly into a single domain: mixed reactions to rewards (Theme 7)
   567→additionally reflected receipt, through uncertainty about the reward feature. These
   568→18
   569→
   570→interconnections reveal the nuance underlying the fidelity of the preceding trial, as discussed
   571→next.
   572→Figure 1. Diagram of theme distribution and interaction across intervention fidelity domains.
   573→Triangulating present findings with the trial’s quantitative fidelity indicators helps to understand
   574→several observed deviations. First, in the trial 35% of implementation intentions did not match
   575→the assigned strategy (Edgren et al., 2026); the interviews suggest a receipt-level difficulty in
   576→translating brief written instructions into a correctly formulated plan, as some participants
   577→produced plans that drew on a different strategy or omitted their selected cue (Theme 3). Action
   578→planning has similarly been reported as difficult in diabetes prevention fidelity research (Miles
   579→et al., 2022). Delivering instructions in multiple formats (MacDonald et al., 2016)—such as
   580→podcast-style videos (e.g., Champion et al., 2025) or cartoons (e.g., O’Dean et al., 2024)—could
   581→improve receipt. Second, the trial’s finding that participants frequently blended strategies
   582→(Edgren et al., 2026) is consistent with pragmatic adaptation to everyday demands (Theme 5).
   583→Third, the trial recorded a low frequency of event-contingent responses, which limited reward
   584→delivery (Edgren et al., 2026). The interviews point to two processes: some participants
   585→remained uncertain about how the app and event-contingent responding worked (Theme 7), a
   586→receipt-level issue, while others forgot or preferred not to record cue encounters. Crucially,
   587→because reward was contingent on recording cue-behaviour repetitions, participants who had
   588→selected infrequently occurring cues had few opportunities to log encounters and therefore to
   589→receive reward (Theme 2).
   590→19
   591→
   592→Finally, the trial observed habit-strength reductions across all groups, including the control
   593→group, which was tentatively attributed to the self-monitoring inherent in daily diary protocols
   594→(Edgren et al., 2026). The present findings strengthen this: participants across groups described
   595→the end-of-day questionnaire and the visibility of their implementation intentions in the app as
   596→heightening awareness and accountability and reminding them daily of their plan, with this
   597→support being missed once the study had ended (Theme 4). Comparable benefits of self-
   598→monitoring have been reported in qualitative studies of apps for diabetes (Adu et al., 2020) and
   599→cancer self-management (Crafoord et al., 2020). The questionnaire thus functioned as an
   600→unplanned active ingredient, helping to explain how even control participants may have
   601→experienced an intervention effect (Edgren et al., 2026).
   602→Considerations for habit research embedded in daily life
   603→Several findings bear on how habit and its degradation might be understood when interventions
   604→are embedded in daily life. When participants extended their efforts beyond the single cue
   605→named in their if-then plan (Theme 5), they may have intuitively targeted higher-order habits
   606→(Phillips et al., 2019)—habitual snacking that can be enacted in several ways. A second
   607→phenomenon was displacement: some participants who avoided snacking at their chosen cue
   608→found that it shifted to other situations (Theme 8). This may reflect behavioural inertia, whereby
   609→an activated but unactualised habit impulse (Leeho et al., 2026) continues to shape behaviour
   610→after the cue encounter has passed; whether such delayed responses are best understood as
   611→habitual or as urges or cravings warrants further conceptual and empirical work. Alternatively,
   612→successfully avoiding a habitual snack may license a compensatory health belief that permits
   613→other unhealthy snacking (Amrein et al., 2017).
   614→These observations, together with how participants engaged with the intervention, provide
   615→implications for study design. Participants did not confine themselves to their assigned strategy
   616→but drew flexibly on inhibition, substitution, reduced accessibility, and discontinuation (Theme
   617→5), echoing the broader fidelity literature on the gap between intended and enacted interventions
   618→(Hankonen, 2021; Palsola et al., 2020; Toomey et al., 2020). Rather than prescribing a single
   619→strategy, interventions could present and encourage the flexible use of several complementary
   620→strategies. To address higher-order habits, participants could log cue-behaviour pairings as they
   621→occur. Clusters could then be identified—potentially via AI-assisted pattern recognition—to
   622→align the intervention target with daily experience. Given the potential for displacement, single-
   623→cue interventions should be monitored for it. Cue selection warrants guided, iterative support,
   624→including the option to modify cues mid-intervention, as some participants recognised their
   625→20
   626→
   627→initial cue as suboptimal only once underway (Theme 2). Because reward here is contingent on
   628→cue-behaviour repetition, selecting suitable, frequently encountered cues is essential. Finally,
   629→reward features should accommodate personal preferences, as reactions ranged from motivating
   630→to stressful (Theme 7).
   631→Considerations for EMI and DBCI research
   632→Because self-monitoring can itself act as an active ingredient (Theme 4), EMI studies require
   633→carefully designed control conditions. Where a study sits on the explanatory-pragmatic
   634→spectrum (Ford & Norrie, 2016; Loudon et al., 2015; Schwartz & Lellouch, 1967), and how
   635→feasible explanatory inferences remain once embedded in daily life, both merit consideration.
   636→A related tension is that between experimental control and ecological validity (Edgren et al.,
   637→2026): in everyday settings, participants may proactively adopt self-enactable behaviour change
   638→techniques (Knittle et al., 2020; Michie et al., 2013) beyond those assigned, compromising
   639→experimental conditions, so studies should include measures to capture such contamination.
   640→Further, fidelity domains can interact such that cascading adverse effects arise—a vulnerability
   641→to which EMIs may be especially prone (e.g. delivery being contingent on participants'
   642→enactment of an earlier intervention component). Researchers and interventionists should
   643→therefore prioritise high fidelity to components on which later components depend. More
   644→broadly, incorporating qualitative fidelity assessment alongside quantitative metrics, as
   645→demonstrated here, can reveal participant-side processes that quantitative indicators alone miss.
   646→Practical recommendations include clear and accessible instructions for app-based procedures
   647→to minimise confusion, and displaying participant plans, as this was perceived as beneficial
   648→(Theme 4). Collectively, insights from the participant-centred fidelity domains should feed back
   649→into study design—one of the five domains outlined by Bellg et al., (2004)—so that future
   650→interventions reflect the realities of participant experience.
   651→Strengths and limitations
   652→This study has several strengths: the qualitative investigation was preregistered, purposive
   653→sampling ensured heterogeneity across intervention groups, and interviews drew on
   654→personalised elicitation materials. However, limitations should be noted. Interviews were
   655→conducted 24–159 days post-study, creating a risk of retrospective recall bias. The sample
   656→comprised participants who completed the study and agreed to be interviewed, potentially
   657→excluding those with more negative experiences or lower engagement. The subsample of 22
   658→from the 313 trial participants limits how far findings can explain trial-level results, so insights
   659→should be regarded as exploratory and hypothesis-generating. Lastly, while several participants
   660→21
   661→
   662→contributed to each theme, idiosyncratic findings were also present, making it unlikely that
   663→theoretical saturation was reached.
   664→In conclusion, this study makes a dual contribution: participant-centred insights into the fidelity
   665→challenges of the preceding habit degradation RCT, and more generalisable lessons for
   666→implementing and evaluating digital and momentary behaviour change interventions in daily
   667→life. The findings show that delivery, receipt, and enactment interact in EMIs, and that
   668→participant-side processes—such as pragmatic adaptation and self-monitoring reactivity—
   669→shape fidelity in ways that quantitative metrics may miss. Qualitative fidelity assessment should
   670→be more routinely integrated into DBCI research to complement quantitative approaches and
   671→support interpretation of intervention outcomes.
