    18→Abstract
    19→There has been an increased interest in studying language in a more ecologically valid context
    20→over the last years. This includes an increase in investigations of language in its natural home,
    21→namely, in multimodal face-to-face social interaction, where visual behaviour, such as facial
    22→signals, contribute to the meaning of an utterance. An approach that is often used to investigate
    23→the contribution of visual signals in language is to manually annotate visual behaviour.
    24→However, this process is extremely laborious, therefore requiring considerable resources for
    25→the creation and analysis of a large multimodal corpus. One way to overcome this problem is
    26→to use software for the automatic detection of visual behaviour. For instance, OpenFace is a
    27→popular tool for extracting facial signals from video recordings. However, many studies using
    28→OpenFace do not perform any form of quality checking, and surprisingly few peer-reviewed
    29→publications are based on validated algorithms when applying automatic facial signal analysis
    30→using OpenFace. In this paper, we point towards potential challenges and provide
    31→recommendations for using OpenFace for automatically detecting facial signals in
    32→conversational corpora, based on a direct comparison with an annotation manual for facial
    33→signals applied by trained annotators. We give potential suggestions for making use of
    34→OpenFace data while minimizing the negative impact of detection noise. This paper therefore
    35→offers additional tools for improving reliability of multimodal annotations when using
    36→OpenFace, as well as an annotation manual for coding facial signals by hand, which often still
    37→is the most reliable method in many research contexts.
    38→Keywords: facial signals, motion tracking, conversation, feature extraction, manual annotation

    41→1. Introduction
    42→Over the last years, there has been a growing focus on studying language in a more natural
    43→context. While the majority of studies have focused on investigating language use in the written
    44→or spoken modality, there has been a rising trend in studying it in its natural home, namely
    45→multimodal face-to-face social interaction. In this multimodal setting, visual behaviour, such
    46→as facial signals, contribute to the semantic and pragmatic meaning of utterances (Bavelas &
    47→Chovil, 2000; Clark, 1996; Holler & Kuhlen, 2026; Holler & Levinson, 2019; Kendon, 2004;
    48→Levinson & Holler, 2014; McNeill, 1992, 2000; Perniss, 2018). However, studying naturalistic
    49→visual communication presents several methodological challenges.
    50→An approach that is often used to investigate the contribution of visual signals in
    51→language is to manually annotate visual behaviour frame-by-frame from 2D video recordings.
    52→This is often performed using detailed coding schemes by several (expert) raters to compute
    53→interrater reliability, which allows for some objectivity. However, the process is extremely
    54→laborious, therefore requiring considerable resources for the creation and analysis of sizeable
    55→multimodal corpora.
    56→One way to overcome this problem is to apply software for the automatic detection of
    57→visual behaviour. The face provides important signals in social interaction, and efforts to
    58→automatize facial movement detection have used facial Action Units (AUs) as main building
    59→blocks to extract movement. AUs represent specific muscle activations following the Facial
    60→Action Coding System (FACS; Ekman et al., 2002), which is the most commonly known and
    61→comprehensive coding scheme in facial movement-judgments.
    62→
    63→4
    64→By now, many algorithms have been developed to automatically extract facial AUs from
    65→video data (e.g., Bartlett et al., 2006; Li & Deng, 2022; Martinez et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2009).
    66→However, these algorithms are often difficult to translate to a practical implementation in the
    67→lab outside the computer vision community, as one needs the knowledge and resources to
    68→recreate the method. Other platforms provide software packages that are ready to use, but
    69→typically require a subscription, and thus may not be accessible for many researchers. Open-
    70→source software provides a solution by being relatively simple to apply by non-experts. Several
    71→open-source automatic methods have been developed for the recognition of gestures and body
    72→kinematics (e.g., OpenPose: Cao et al., 2021), and facial signals (e.g., OpenGaze: Zhang et al.,
    73→2019, IntraFace: de la Torre et al. 2015; OpenFace: Baltrusaitis et al., 2016, 2018). One
    74→computer software that recognizes changes in facial movements automatically using facial AUs
    75→is OpenFace (Baltrusaitis et al., 2016, 2018).
    76→However, like manual annotation, applying such automatized tools to naturalistic social
    77→interaction or relatively unconstrained recording set-ups does not come without challenges and
    78→limitations. For example, environmental factors such as lighting conditions, the recording
    79→angle, image quality, or background clutter can impact the algorithm’s ability to accurately
    80→detect facial movements. Naturalistic social interaction typically consists of many intertwined
    81→behaviours and can be very noisy. Participants may shift their posture or move their body in a
    82→way that makes tracking their facial movements quite difficult (e.g., slouching, moving the face
    83→out of full sight by turning or tilting it). Image obstructions such as facial hair, hands covering
    84→parts of the face, or head and body orientation can lead the algorithm to misinterpret (partial)
    85→facial information. It may also detect speech-related movements that resemble facial action
    86→units (AUs) associated with the mouth muscles.
    87→
    88→5
    89→The temporal dynamics of facial behaviour consisting of rapid onset and offset times
    90→can also present challenges for the automatic detection of facial movements, since the algorithm
    91→may miss some of the subtle, but socially very relevant facial signals. The performance of facial
    92→movement detection algorithms heavily relies on the diversity as well as quality of the training
    93→data. Limited training data may lead the algorithm to struggle to generalize effectively.
    94→Moreover, researchers aiming to annotate conversational corpora for facial movements are
    95→typically interested in not just any movement but in facial signals — that is, meaningful
    96→movements, produced as part of the message a speaker intends to communicate (Clark, 1996).
    97→Manual annotators may thus distinguish between facial movements related to the pragmatic or
    98→semantic meaning of the utterance (i.e., facial signals) and non-communicative facial
    99→movements (e.g., signs of fatigue or physical discomfort such as an eye twitch, or habitual
   100→gestures like hair twirling, adjusting glasses, relieving an itch). Moreover, researchers would
   101→often be interested in facial signals including smiles or other mouth gestures, but excluding
   102→mouth and jaw movements caused by speaking (or vice versa). Therefore, it remains an open
   103→question whether OpenFace can be reliably used on naturalistic social interaction data and thus
   104→be used to inform or replace manual annotation.
   105→In this paper, we aim to provide an overview of OpenFace performance in the context
   106→of identifying communicative facial signals in a corpus of face-to-face conversational video
   107→recordings. In the next sections, we first give a short summary of the functionalities of
   108→OpenFace, and provide some examples of recent studies using OpenFace to address potential
   109→challenges one may face when using the software. Second, we compare OpenFace annotations,
   110→extracted under a variety of threshold conditions, to a set of 34,413 manual annotations made
   111→by visual annotators trained on a detailed facial signal coding scheme applied to a large
   112→naturalistic and entirely unscripted Dutch face-to-face conversation corpus. The manual
   113→

   115→annotations, which were meant to capture only communicatively relevant signals, are taken as
   116→a baseline for comparison with OpenFace. Next, we discuss performance of OpenFace both in
   117→terms of signal-specific and general (i.e., across all facial signals) detection accuracy, as well
   118→as accuracy in terms of signal onset timing, and we accompany this with words of caution
   119→regarding the use of OpenFace in the absence of manual annotation checks. Then, we discuss
   120→the pros and cons of utilizing OpenFace in various research contexts, and provide some
   121→suggestions for effectively leveraging computer vision applications, including OpenFace, in
   122→multimodal communication research. While there is not likely to be a one-size-fits-all solution,
   123→we provide recommendations for improving OpenFace’s automatic detection of facial signals
   124→in conversational corpora by presenting a simple step-by-step tutorial with accompanying
   125→Python code to implement custom thresholding for improved detection performance.
   126→1.1 Automatic detection of facial movements using OpenFace
   127→OpenFace describes 18 different facial AUs, which are measured in terms of detected presence
   128→(0 for not detected or 1 for detected) and intensity (0-5 continuous scale, with 0 for not detected,
   129→and 1-5 reflecting increasing levels of intensity). The specific AUs captured by OpenFace are
   130→AU01 (inner eyebrow raiser), AU02 (outer eyebrow raiser), AU04 (eyebrow lowerer), AU05
   131→(upper lid raiser), AU06 (cheek raiser), AU07 (lid tightener), AU09 (nose wrinkler), AU10
   132→(upper lip raiser), AU12 (lip corner puller), AU14 (dimpler), AU15 (lip corner depressor),
   133→AU17 (chin raiser), AU20 (lip stretcher), AU23 (lip tightener), AU25 (lips part), AU26 (jaw
   134→drop), AU28 (lip suck), AU45 (blink). These AUs are determined using static (single image
   135→used for estimation) and dynamic (person normalisation in video) prediction models. The
   136→default prediction model for videos is the dynamic model. OpenFace measures a range of
   137→

   139→additional features, such as facial landmark locations using 66 key points (e.g., inner, outer and
   140→middle eyebrow points, points along lip contours; see Figure 1), as well as tracker confidence,
   141→success (0, 1), location of 2D facial landmarks in pixels, and location of 3D facial landmarks
   142→in millimetres.

   149→Moreover, head pose information is provided along vertical (yaw, looking to the side),
   150→horizontal (pitch, looking up and down), and depth (roll, tilting head to the side) dimensions.
   151→Eye gaze location and direction are also provided using 56 key points (Baltrusaitis et al., 2016,
   152→2018). In this article, however, we focus on the detection of facial movements through facial
   153→AUs.
   154→Many studies use OpenFace out-of-the-box, using the default pre-trained models, to
   155→extract facial movements for analysing facial behaviour in corpora (e.g., Chizhikova &
   156→Kimmelman, 2022; Hasan et al., 2019; Heron et al., 2018; Hmamouche, Ochs, Prevot, et al.,
   157→2020; Hmamouche, Ochs, Prévot, et al., 2020; Shiota & Shimada, 2020; Thierry, 2020; Ueno
   158→et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022) or running experimental studies (e.g., Cañigueral et al., 2021;
   159→Dobre et al., 2021; Hessels et al., 2019; Mauersberger et al., 2022). The aforementioned
   160→examples frequently use the AU detection feature of OpenFace, for instance to quantify the
   161→number or intensity of facial movements being produced. This demonstrates the growing
   162→popularity of using these methods. Indeed, there are good reasons for using these models, and
   163→in many cases they may capture the facial activity to be measured well. Yet, many studies did
   164→not report any form of quality checking (i.e., checking for false positives and false negatives
   165→with regard to the movements that were actually produced), meaning we do not know how well
   166→the pre-trained models performed on those studies. In addition, surprisingly few peer-reviewed
   167→publications are based on validated algorithms when applying automatic facial movement
   168→analysis using OpenFace. At least, these studies do not provide any written indication for how
   169→the algorithms performed or were checked. This can make verifying and streamlining such
   170→methodology difficult, and could be seen as suggesting that such checks are not necessary,
   171→which would be misleading for researchers looking to adopt these methods.
   172→

   174→We call for a note of caution when using OpenFace without quality checking, especially
   175→since a number of authors who have explicitly checked tracking quality have noted its
   176→performance limitations (Esselink et al., 2023; Fydanaki & Geradts, 2018; Namba et al., 2021;
   177→Rauzy & Goujon, 2018; Stüber et al., 2022). For instance, Rauzy and Goujon (2018) performed
   178→a validation on a French conversation dataset including spontaneous facial movements. In these
   179→naturalistic settings, the authors noted missing measurements at landmark position levels and
   180→found problems with occlusions. Importantly, both Rauzy and Goujon (2018) and Goujon
   181→(2019) showed a low detection precision of OpenFace for eyebrow movements, and proposed
   182→this was due to OpenFace interpreting eyebrow movements with a high intensity as head nods
   183→because of OpenFace’s Constrained Local Model (CLM-Z) algorithm. They suggested CLM-
   184→Z may give less precise annotations compared to the Supervised Descent Method (SDM)
   185→algorithm used in other facial detection software like IntraFace (de la Torre et al., 2015).
   186→Namba, Sato and Yoshikawa (2021) assessed the correspondence between manual and
   187→automatic AU detection across different viewing angles, using OpenFace, FaceReader, and Py-
   188→Feat. The authors found that, as the view moved further from a portrait view, automatic AU
   189→detection became significantly worse. Similarly, Esselink, Oomen and Roelofsen (2023) found
   190→substantial variability in facial expression tracking by TrueDepth cameras according to viewing
   191→angle. Lastly, Stüber et al. (2022) found that OpenFace did not measure gaze equally well in
   192→all directions, even after improved recording conditions and calibration, with gaze vectors
   193→tending to underestimate more extreme horizontal and vertical eye movements. This was
   194→argued to be caused by inherent challenges of using webcam data.
   195→It could be that the low performance of OpenFace for spontaneous conversations is due
   196→to the fact that the software was only trained and tested on limited datasets (Bosphorus, BP4D
   197→and SEMAINE from FERA2015, DISFA, FERA2011, UNBC, CK+). These datasets often
   198→
   199→10
   200→consist of posed or prototypical expressions (such as from emotion-related tasks) rather than
   201→spontaneous facial behaviour displayed in unscripted conversations (see also review of facial
   202→movement datasets by Krumhuber, 2017). This is problematic, since spontaneous facial
   203→behaviour is different from posed facial behaviour. For example, spontaneous smiles are often
   204→smaller in amplitude, have a longer duration, and are slower in onset and offset time compared
   205→to posed smiles1 (Cohn & Schmidt, 2004). Additionally, spontaneous eyebrow actions (AU01,
   206→AU02, and AU04 from the FACS system; Ekman et al., 2002) were shown to have a different
   207→intensity, duration and occurrence order compared to posed eyebrow actions (Valstar et al.,
   208→2006). Therefore, software trained on posed facial movements may fail to generalise to the rich
   209→variety of facial movements produced in everyday conversation (Zeng et al., 2009). Moreover,
   210→the predictions of the detection and intensity models were trained separately and on different
   211→datasets, which might mean that their predictions are not always consistent (e.g., when the
   212→detection model indicates AU not being present, the intensity model may predict its value to be
   213→above 1).
   214→There have been some attempts to improve OpenFace (e.g., HMAD team
   215→https://github.com/srauzy/HMAD; Santoso & Kusuma, 2018). For example, the HMAD team
   216→developed annotation tools such as EBMAD for eyebrow raises and frowns, BMAD for blinks,
   217→and SMAD for smile intensities using OpenFace (Rauzy & Amoyal, 2023; Rauzy & Goujon,
   218→2018). EBMAD can be pipelined with OpenFace outputs, and calculates eyebrow movements
   219→using their local maxima in the two-dimensional wavelet space. Based on signal processing,
   220→the local maxima are weighted with respect to the signal-to-noise ratio, and the higher the value
   221→of this signal-to-noise, the higher the probability is that it corresponds to a true action (Mallat,

   223→differ in AU activation (Ekman, 1989; Ekman et al., 1990), but recent evidence suggests that these differences
   224→may be much less consistent than previously believed (Girard et al., 2021).
   225→

   227→1999; Rauzy & Goujon, 2018). When comparing OpenFace outputs to the manually annotated
   228→corpus consisting of 573 eyebrow movements on 2h50mn of video extracts, Rauzy and Gujon
   229→(2018) found that the reliability of OpenFace improved when using their tool. While tools like
   230→the ones created by the HMAD team are certainly useful, a general overview of OpenFace
   231→performance is still needed, as well as general recommendations for using OpenFace to study
   232→specific facial signals on one’s own dataset. Furthermore, an evaluation of OpenFace

   234→of facial signals is still missing.
   235→2. Comparison of OpenFace performance with manual corpus annotation
   236→2.1 Conversational face-to-face corpus details
   237→To assess whether OpenFace can be used as-is for reliably capturing facial signals in
   238→conversational corpora, we compare annotations retrieved through OpenFace with manual
   239→annotations of communicative facial movements from a large naturalistic and entirely
   240→unscripted Dutch face-to-face conversation corpus (CoAct corpus; see Nota et al., 2021, 2023b;
   241→ter Bekke et al., 2024; Trujillo & Holler, 2021).
   242→The corpus consists of videos from 34 dyads (mean age = 23.10 ± 8 years; 51 female,
   243→17 male) of Dutch native speakers (all dyad partners were friends or acquaintances), without
   244→motoric or language problems, and with normal or corrected-to-normal vision. Dyad partners
   245→were seated opposite one another at a comfortable conversational distance and asked to engage
   246→in casual conversation for one hour while they were recorded. Conversations were recorded
   247→
   248→12
   249→using two video cameras (Canon XE405) for frontal views of the face/head close-ups of each
   250→participant, two cameras (Canon XF205 Camcorder) mounted at a 45-degree angle towards the
   251→participants’ bodies, two cameras (Canon XF205 Camcorder) were mounted on tripods to
   252→provide a birds-eye view of the participants, and one camera (Canon Legria HF G10) recorded
   253→the scene view (both participants from the side). For the present study, only the frontal view
   254→recordings were used (see Figure 1, panels A and B). The resulting time resolution was 25 fps
   255→(approx. 40 ms).
   256→The recording session was split into three 20-minute sessions. The first session
   257→consisted of free, unguided conversation. In the second session, participants were asked to
   258→discuss one of three themes: data privacy, social media use, or language of instruction in
   259→university teaching. In the third session, participants were asked to discuss their ideal joint
   260→holiday, affordable on their own budget. Written informed consent was obtained before and
   261→after filming2. The original study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Social Sciences
   262→department of the Radboud University Nijmegen (ethics approval code: ECSW 2018-124).

   332→2.3 Manual annotation methods
   333→As the larger project in which this study is embedded focused on question and response pairs,
   334→manual annotation, too, focused on this sequential environment. Questions and responses were
   335→
   336→16
   337→identified and annotated in ELAN (5.5; Wittenburg et al., 2006), based on an annotation scheme
   338→adapted from Stivers and Enfield (2010), as well as a holistic approach that took into
   339→consideration visual signals, conversational context, phrasing, prosody, and addressee
   340→behaviour, with onsets and offsets annotated in Praat (5.1; Boersma & Weenink, 2007) and
   341→imported into ELAN (see Nota et al., 2021 for further details). This resulted in 6778 questions
   342→(duration Mdn = 1114 ms, min = 99ms, max = 13,145 ms, interquartile ratio = 1138 ms), and
   343→4553 responses (duration Mdn = 1045 ms, min = 91 ms, max = 18,615 ms, interquartile ratio
   344→= 1596 ms).
   345→Facial signal annotation was performed in ELAN (5.5) based on the frontal view
   346→cameras (see Figure 1), focusing on a window of 2000 ms before to 2000 ms after each question
   347→or response annotation. These timing windows resulted from a careful qualitative analysis of
   348→the data. Our goal was to find a balanced approach, acknowledging that visual signals may
   349→slightly precede or follow the associated speech. Manual spots checks of the data revealed that
   350→this extended time window was a good solution for mitigating the risk of both omitting relevant
   351→data and including irrelevant data). Facial signals were annotated when they overlapped with
   352→this window with minimally one frame, even if they started or ended outside of the window.
   353→Annotations were created on a frame-by-frame basis by five trained annotators, all native
   354→speakers of Dutch.
   355→Critically, facial signals were annotated when they were judged to be communicative
   356→in nature contributing to the semantic and pragmatic content of a message, based on a holistic
   357→view of the utterance. We also coded all blinks, because their communicative value has been
   358→demonstrated for addressee behaviour (Hömke et al., 2018). However, some of these blinks
   359→(especially shorter ones) may have functioned primarily for wetting the eye. We additionally
   360→coded all gaze shifts, which included cases where speakers may have shifted their gaze for
   361→
   362→17
   363→speech planning purposes or other reasons. Movements such as those associated with
   364→swallowing, inhaling, or laughter without or with inaudible speech, as well as mouth and jaw
   365→movements arising simply due to speech articulation, were not annotated. The following facial
   366→signals were annotated:
   367→1. Eyebrow movements (frowns, raises, frown raises, unilateral raises, lowering)
   368→2. Eye widenings (upper and possibly lower eyelid opening wider than neutral)
   369→3. Squints (narrowing of the eyes due to contraction of lower and possibly upper eyelid)
   370→4. Blinks (closure of the eye)
   371→5. Gaze shifts (gaze away from the addressee, judged by position of the pupil)
   372→6. Nose wrinkles (skin over the nose contracting)
   373→7. Non-articulatory mouth movements (pressed lips, corners down, corners back, smiles)
   374→Signal annotations started at the first evidence of movement until the respective articulator
   375→had moved back to its neutral position. We did not annotate frames of the video that had
   376→speech not related to the question or response, or that was combined with laughter when the
   377→laughter rendered the speech inaudible. We additionally excluded two frames before or after
   378→such unrelated speech events. This was to ensure that any facial signals were more likely to
   379→be related to the question or response. This resulted in the exclusion of 795 (2.3% of the
   380→34,413 annotated signals) signal annotations. The detailed facial signal annotation manual can
   381→be found on the Open Science Framework website
   382→(https://osf.io/t8wbv/?view_only=471c61f7dfc94d0a923484d485582d5d).

   385→Interrater reliability for facial signal annotations was calculated using Krippendorff’s
   386→alpha (Krippendorff, 2019) and Jaccard similarity (1901). To calculate these two measures we
   387→determined whether, for each coding window, a given signal was annotated. This was done for
   388→each annotator, creating an agreement matrix with columns for each annotator (binary, signal
   389→annotated or not), and a row for each possible combination of coding window and facial signal.
   390→This approach focuses more on whether a particular signal was detected within a given
   391→utterance or not, while avoiding known sources of disagreement such as segmentation (e.g.,
   392→was it one long signal, or two consecutive signals with a short dip in intensity between them)
   393→and offset timing. In order to provide a chance-corrected measure of agreement that can be
   394→interpreted on its own, we calculated Krippendorff’s alpha across all manual annotators.
   395→Krippendorff’s alpha was chosen because it is designed to handle multiple annotators, and is
   396→more robust against skewed data with many nulls, which applies to facial signal coding given
   397→that most utterances are unlikely to have all, or even many, of the facial signals. We report
   398→alpha, raw agreement, and prevalence for the minimum, maximum, and average observed
   399→agreement. Prevalence is also included as it can often explain the discrepance between raw
   400→agreement and alpha.
   401→Jaccard similarity, calculated as J(A,B) = ∣A∩B∣ / ∣A∪B∣, is commonly employed to
   402→assess the similarity between two datasets by taking the intersection of the two sets divided by
   403→the union of the two sets. This metric is thus better suited than alternatives (such as Fleiss or
   404→Cohen’s kappa) as it does not have an assumption about multiple coding categories. As Jaccard
   405→similarity is designed for agreement between two annotators, we calculated the average
   406→similarity across all pairs of annotators. It should be noted that Jaccard similarity does not have
   407→standard cut-off bands for interpretation (i.e., for poor vs moderate vs good vs excellent).
   408→Instead, it is typically used as a comparison between methods. In the case of the present
   409→
   410→19
   411→research, we provide Jaccard similarity as a straightforward metric to contrast between human-
   412→human and human-machine reliability. While Jaccard similarity provides a measure of the
   413→proportion of annotations in a given signal category, we also calculated raw agreement, which
   414→considers both positive agreement (i.e., annotators have overlapping annotations with the same
   415→label) and negative agreement (number of annotations, from both/all annotators that are not the
   416→target signal). This calculation of raw agreement, while standard in the version of ELAN that
   417→we used for these analyses when assessing reliability for multiple tiers, can lead to inflated
   418→agreement values. Jaccard similarity therefore provides a useful alternative, and may be closer
   419→to the form of agreement that researchers are typically interested in. However, for completeness
   420→we also include raw agreement calculations.
   421→As the timing of signal onsets was also of interest, we additionally calculated
   422→convergent reliability using mean absolute difference (Mabs, in ms) of the onsets between
   423→manual annotators. We also computed the standard error of measurement (SeM) as a more
   424→precise, data-specific indicator of agreement (see Nota et al., 2021). However, because SeM
   425→relies on Cohen’s kappa (SeM = S × √(1 − r)) and we instead use Jaccard similarity, we focus
   426→on the raw measure of difference provided by Mabs. These calculations were done by randomly
   427→selecting one question and one response in one of the three recording parts for each participant
   428→in all dyads (n = 136, roughly equivalent to 1% of the question and response data in the entire
   429→corpus) resulting in the additional annotation of 223 facial signals for reliability, and allowing
   430→a pairwise comparison between annotators.
   431→We excluded eyebrow lowering and mouth corners pulled back from all further
   432→analyses, since these facial signals did not frequently appear in the corpus resulting in too few
   433→annotations to perform the paired comparisons between annotators. For all other facial signals,
   434→the paired comparisons showed an average alpha of 0.61, with minimum alpha = 0.24 (pressed-
   435→
   436→20
   437→lips; prevalence = 3%) and maximum alpha = 0.90 (gaze; prevalence = 56%), and average
   438→Jaccard similarity of 53% (minimum observed similarity = 16% for pressed-lips, maximum
   439→observed similarity = 91% for gaze). See Table 1 for a more detailed overview of the results
   440→per facial signal. The average alpha only nearly reaches a moderate level of agreement
   441→(Krippendorff, 2019). However, this seems to be primarily due to several low-alpha signals
   442→pulling the average down, as moderate agreement (alpha > 0.67) is achieved for six of the facial
   443→signals. While a higher alpha would be desirable, it is important to note that the skew of the
   444→data (i.e., most utterances do not contain most of the possible facial signals) creates an
   445→artificially lower alpha. This can be seen in the prevalence data, where the signals (n = 6) for
   446→which moderate agreement was achieved (alpha scores: 0.69, 0.83, 0.73, 0.89, 0.77; Jaccard
   447→similarity: 58%, 77%, 61%, 86%, 91%, 74%), have an average prevalence of 35%, but those

   679→3. Discussion
   680→The present study investigated the accuracy of a facial signal detection program used “out-of-
   681→the-box”, OpenFace, for identifying facial signals in naturalistic conversation when compared
   682→to trained manual annotators. We found that, across several thresholds and methods of
   683→extracting OpenFace features, the highest mean reliability values between OpenFace and
   684→manual annotation did not exceed what would be considered weak levels of agreements. When
   685→looking at overall model performance across both the 100 ms and 40 ms timing thresholds, the
   686→dual-model approach, that utilized the intersection between the detection (*_c) model and the
   687→intensity (*_r) model, generally outperformed the intensity-only model, providing slightly
   688→higher F1 scores under optimal levels of intensity.
   689→When considering individual signal performance, the highest observed Jaccard
   690→similarity and F1 were generally quite low, though some facial signals were found to have at
   691→least moderate agreement under certain extraction parameters. For instance, blink detection
   692→
   693→32
   694→performed best (Jaccard = 0.26, F1 = 0.411, Mabs = 438.10 ms, 100 ms dual-model). Signals
   695→such as pressed lips (Jaccard = 0.02, F1 = 0.045, Mabs = 231.20 ms) and mouth corners down
   696→(Jaccard = 0.04, F1 = 0.085, Mabs = 209.10 ms) were particularly poorly detected.
   697→Interestingly, the 100 ms minimum duration threshold typically outperformed the 40 ms
   698→threshold across signals. However, manual annotations were made with a minimum duration
   699→threshold of 40 (i.e., duration of one frame in 25 frames-per-second recording), rather than 100
   700→ms. This seems to indicate a lower bound on the extent to which finer temporal resolution
   701→increases the precision of detecting short signals in OpenFace, even with high quality video
   702→data.
   703→Timing precision, measured using Mabs, also varied widely across facial signals. Even
   704→for relatively well-detected signals like blinks, onset differences of 438 ms may be meaningful
   705→in studies examining fine-grained temporal coordination with speech and facial signals. Less
   706→reliably detected signals, such as smiles (Jaccard = 0.135; F1 = 0.238; Mabs = 1,290.90 ms),
   707→demonstrate that both detection and timing can also deviate considerably from manual
   708→annotation for specific facial signals.
   709→Overall, this discrepancy between manual and automatic annotation seems to be heavily
   710→driven by the large number of false positives produced by OpenFace when using too lenient of
   711→an intensity-model (i.e., precision < 0.45 in all models). However, raising the intensity
   712→threshold too much leads to OpenFace missing a substantial number of true positives, as can
   713→be seen in the fall-of of recall scores after an optimal threshold has been reached. The agreement
   714→between OpenFace and manual annotation is further compromised by the different impact that
   715→particular thresholds have on the detection accuracy of different facial signals, meaning that no
   716→single model or parameter setting will provide a strong detection of all signals. Specifically,
   717→agreement between a human annotator and OpenFace does not plateau after the intensity
   718→
   719→33
   720→threshold with the highest agreement. In other words, when dual-model 0.6 is best for detecting
   721→eyebrow raises (Jaccard = 0.120; F1 = 0.215; Mabs = 649.5 ms), for example, increasing the
   722→threshold to 0.9 or 2.0 does not lead to the same high level of agreement on F1. Instead,
   723→agreement again falls off if the threshold becomes stricter. In fact, reliability fell close to zero
   724→for many signals after a certain intensity threshold was applied.
   725→While tools such as OpenFace allow researchers to more easily process large amounts
   726→of video data to study facial signals, we must continue to be critical towards the data that is
   727→going into our analyses. In this case, using OpenFace without quality control introduces noise
   728→into any analyses being conducted. While more data can be advantageous for dealing with
   729→variability, in the case of noisy data, increasing the amount means that not only the signal of
   730→interest but also the noise is amplified. This issue of “noise accumulation” has been described
   731→as one of the top three challenges for big data (Fan et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2016; Mohan &
   732→Singh, 2022). The issue does not, of course, only pertain to researchers utilizing exceedingly
   733→large corpora that would be traditionally considered “big data”, but anyone using automated
   734→tools to extract visual behaviour features from their videos. Without quality control, much of
   735→the data going into subsequent analysis may be noisy. In this case, noise can refer to false
   736→positives, false negatives (i.e., missing relevant signals and thus underestimating their presence,
   737→or skewing their association with other features under investigation), as well as inaccurate
   738→timing. We therefore strongly recommend that researchers using automated tools for facial
   739→signal extraction or detection take precautions and additional steps in order to verify, and when
   740→needed also correct, the output of these tools prior to analysing their data. As a starting point,
   741→we have provided several recommendations for effective use of automated tools in section 4.
   742→Overall, our results underscore the challenges associated with utilizing OpenFace for
   743→accurate facial signal detection in naturalistic multimodal datasets. It is imperative for
   744→
   745→34
   746→researchers to meticulously examine their data and assess the impact of varying thresholds on
   747→their results. Section 4 provides recommendations for calibrating extraction parameters,
   748→offering insight into the importance of this process. Our results therefore also provide some
   749→indications for dealing with OpenFace output at the level of individual signals. Specifically,
   750→researchers should be aware of the inherent variation in detection accuracy across different
   751→signals, and check the quality of their automated detections at a signal-by-signal basis. Overall,
   752→the obtained reliability values, even when examining best-case models for each signal,
   753→demonstrate that caution is warranted when using automated methods to detect
   754→communicatively relevant facial signals.
   755→The present study utilized a large corpus of naturalistic, face-to-face conversation (Nota
   756→et al., 2021, 2023b; Trujillo & Holler, 2021). This provided a critical test of OpenFace that is
   757→particularly relevant for researchers interested in facial signalling in naturalistic, casual
   758→conversation. The study therefore particularly highlights detection performance in videos that
   759→contain (even relatively) unconstrained movement, even when the head is continuously in view.
   760→Our results are therefore particularly relevant to the growing interest in studying the pragmatic
   761→functions of conversational facial signals (Amoyal & Priego-Valverde, 2019; Bavelas et al.,
   762→2014; Cañigueral et al., 2021; Domaneschi et al., 2017; Nota et al., 2021, 2023a). Future work
   763→aimed at assessing automated facial signal detection algorithms should also consider settings
   764→that may provide alternative viewpoints. For example, the rise in video-mediated conversation
   765→will likely lead to an increase in video data in which speakers are engaging with a webcam,
   766→which may provide a different viewing angle of the speaker. Along the same lines, there is an
   767→increased interest in running online experiments where participants are recorded, or even
   768→interact with one another, via their webcam (Arias Sarah et al., 2023; Yang & Kita, 2023).
   769→Similarly, corpora utilizing head-mounted eye-tracking devices provide a more face-forward
   770→
   771→35
   772→view, albeit at the cost of a potential obstruction of facial signals due to spectacle frames or
   773→camera holding devices positioned under the eyes, or a (likely) decreased video quality.
   774→Moreover, task-focused conversational interactions may involve frequent looks towards task
   775→materials placed on table tops, thus increasing downward orientation of the face or eyes, which
   776→too may influence automated tracking possibilities, as do more unconstrained interactions
   777→where participants move around rather than sitting on chairs facing forwards. Assessing how
   778→OpenFace or other automated software perform in such scenarios will be much needed.
   779→A possible limitation of the analyses described here is that even between-human
   780→interrater reliability is not always strong. However, even if we focus only on the facial signals
   781→where interrater reliability was minimally considered moderate, we see that average human-
   782→human Jaccard similarity was 74% , but only 16% in the human-OpenFace comparison. We
   783→can then be cautiously confident that this pattern will extend to other signals, even if there may
   784→not be sufficient occurrence in this corpus to robustly assess this. It should also be noted that
   785→our results may be influenced by the way our comparisons were set up. Namely, we assessed
   786→the ability of OpenFace to accurately capture the same annotations as were found through
   787→manual annotation of communicative visual signals. Of course, there is the possibility that what
   788→our analyses considered false positives (i.e., OpenFace annotations that did not correspond to
   789→a manual annotation) were actually true positives in the sense that OpenFace identified a facial
   790→signal that was communicatively relevant, albeit subtle enough to be missed in manual
   791→annotation. Such effects would be difficult to tease out of our analyses. However, if visual
   792→signals were detected by OpenFace but not visible to the human eye, even at close up and
   793→repeated frame by frame inspection of the video material, it is unlikely that any such
   794→movements would have been perceptible to the interactants themselves and thus most likely
   795→not communicatively relevant. We therefore consider the comparison as we applied it here to
   796→
   797→36
   798→be highly informative for research aiming to use OpenFace for automated analyses of
   799→communicative facial movements.
   800→Finally, it should be noted that our comparisons focused on the AU annotations
   801→generated by OpenFace, but did not address the facial landmark tracking (i.e., the continuous
   802→coordinate tracking of each landmark, without regard for detecting separate “actions”). It would
   803→be quite useful for future methodological work to also assess the accuracy of this “rawer”
   804→tracking data, and whether this could also be utilized for automatic detection, potentially by
   805→again fusing this with the AU detection models. Landmark tracking may also be of sufficient
   806→accuracy to be used for kinematic analysis of facial signals, based on quality-checked
   807→annotations.
   808→4. Recommendations for effective use of OpenFace for the annotation of facial signals in dyadic
   809→face-to-face conversational corpora
   810→1. Follow the steps as noted by the authors of OpenFace to improve base reliability (e.g.,
   811→perform additional model training, or set camera calibration parameters by providing
   812→the focal length and the optical centre; see also
   813→https://docs.opencv.org/2.4/doc/tutorials/calib3d/camera_calibration/camera_calibrati
   814→on.html; Baltrusaitis et al., 2016, 2018).
   815→2. If using Action Unit (AU) detection, always check AU annotations in the video itself,
   816→using a program such as ELAN (Wittenburg et al., 2006) for making manual annotations
   817→and calculating reliability. Given that “big data” can lead to bigger noise, researchers
   818→should check random subsets of their data to ensure that the AU annotations are
   819→sensible, even when working with big data, or when not all data can be checked
   820→
   821→37
   822→manually. For example, ensuring that a trained manual annotator can annotate a
   823→representative portion of one’s data and perform interrater reliability analyses on this
   824→subset could already provide a good indication of whether the automated method is
   825→capturing what the researchers are intending (either on an AU basis, or AUs matched to
   826→signals which may require combining some of the AUs). To do so, researchers may
   827→want to use the facial signal annotation manual we applied, which can be found on the
   828→Open Science Framework website (https://osf.io/x2udw/ ).
   829→3. If interested in the timing of visual signals (e.g., in relation to onset of other signals,
   830→synchrony with linguistic units, using measures such as Mabs), researchers should use
   831→the AU annotations only as a starting point, but refine manually. In other words, it is
   832→advisable to always manually check the AU annotations and adjust the starting and (if
   833→wanted) the end times. Although this requires more manual labour, simply adjusting
   834→annotations should still be a significant reduction in time when compared to looking for
   835→and identifying every signal manually.
   836→4. Use and calibrate intensity thresholds as a way to filter the data. These will need to be
   837→adjusted and calibrated by visually inspecting the annotations that are extracted at a
   838→range of thresholds. The modified ExploFace scripts that we used (Supplementary
   839→Materials on the Open Science Framework project website https://osf.io/x2udw/)
   840→provide an easy way to extract AUs at several threshold intervals. Concretely,
   841→calibration can be performed by taking the best-performing model parameters for one’s
   842→facial signal of interest based on the results of this study as a starting point, and by
   843→manually annotating a representative portion of the data to be used. The next step would
   844→involve testing interrater reliability (for example, as performed in the present study)
   845→between the manual annotation and 1) the best performing model as described in the
   846→
   847→38
   848→present paper, as well as 2) one intensity threshold above and below the best performing
   849→threshold. If reliability is better with the stricter or more conservative threshold, the
   850→next step would be again taking one intensity threshold further in the same direction
   851→(e.g., if comparing 0.3, 0.6, 0.9, and 0.9 shows highest reliability, adding a comparison
   852→with 1.2). This process should be continued until further adjusting the intensity
   853→threshold no longer improves reliability.
   854→5. In general, if one intends to use the output of OpenFace for big data analyses with
   855→minimal manual annotation, we suggest using a dual thresholding procedure, such as
   856→the dual-model we created with a threshold of 0.9 described in this paper, at least for
   857→data and recordings similar to the ones analysed here. We also highly recommend still
   858→visually checking instances of the data after applying the dual-model and thresholds by
   859→going through some files as mentioned in our point above, to maximise the quality of
   860→annotations being utilized in the analyses (and minimise the frequency of false positives
   861→or negatives).
   862→6. Overall, if using data from only one of the two (*_r, or *_c) models, our results suggest
   863→that the dual-model provides the most reliable detections. However, a threshold should
   864→still be applied as low intensity detections greatly increase noise in the data.
   865→7. Finally, the overall low reliability scores shown for all signals across all models warns
   866→against using automated methods (or at least OpenFace 2.2.0) for detecting
   867→communicatively relevant facial signals in spontaneous, conversational data. Although
   868→less straightforward (at the time of writing this), training the AU detection models on
   869→spontaneous conversational data, where multiple viewing angles of the face are present,
   870→will likely be required in order to produce reliable results.
   871→
   872→39
   873→5. Conclusion
   874→Our analyses demonstrate that, in high-quality recordings of naturalistic conversation,
   875→OpenFace is unable to provide accurate detection of many facial signals when utilized “out-of-
   876→the-box”, particularly due to a high number of false positives. This conclusion holds even when
   877→limiting the comparison to frequent signals for which human-human interrater reliability is
   878→strongest, i.e., the most easily detected visual signals. We provide some suggestions for making
   879→use of OpenFace data in a more reliable way while minimizing the negative impact of detection
   880→noise. However, we primarily note that the low reliability obtained across thresholding
   881→procedures suggests caution when using current facial signal detection software. Even with
   882→calibrations in place, it is paramount that researchers verify the quality of the automatic
   883→annotations with manual data annotation checks such as with the annotation manual provided.
   884→Particularly when using AU detection, additional steps for quality control should be taken,
   885→including filtering, and (when needed) fine-tuning of outputs. Although such additional
   886→filtering and fine-tuning provide some increase in reliability, we caution against using
   887→automated methods when dealing with spontaneous, conversational data without manual
   888→validation.
