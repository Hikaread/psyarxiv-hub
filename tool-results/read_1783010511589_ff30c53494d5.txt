    25→    25→others diverged, offering new hypotheses. Heavier drinkers reported significantly higher
    26→    26→craving levels following video exposure and craving correlated moderately with video
    27→    27→engagement. No significant associations were found between craving and SD or demand
    28→    28→characteristics. However, unlike the initial study, repeated measurements showed no significant
    29→    29→effects or interactions with alcohol consumption.
    30→    30→Reanalysis of the original data highlighted a gender-based interaction, where the effect of
    31→    31→the interaction between repeated craving measurements and alcohol consumption was
    32→    32→significant in men but not in women. These results suggest partial replication of prior findings
    33→    33→and underscore the need to further explore contextual and demographic influences on alcohol
    34→    34→craving. Moreover, this study confirmed a significant role of video engagement in craving.

    39→    39→1. Introduction
    40→    40→Craving is a key feature of drug addiction. It has been widely studied since its inclusion
    41→    41→as a diagnostic criterion in the DSM-V [1–7]. However, despite decades of research, its
    42→    42→usefulness and precise role in addiction is still debated [6,8]. Yet, the concept of craving is part
    43→    43→of most theoretical models of addiction and has been consistently shown to be implicated in
    44→    44→drug use and relapse [9]. The American Psychiatric Association defined craving as “an intense
    45→    45→urge or desire for a drug” [10], although recent evidence shows that it also relates to non-
    46→    46→substance-related behavioral addictions [11]. Based on extensive research and current
    47→    47→theoretical knowledge, it is now widely accepted that craving is a complex multidimensional
    48→    48→phenomenon, including automatic, cognitive, and physiological (interoceptive) components
    49→    49→[12,13]. Regarding alcohol use disorder (AUD) more specifically, craving has for instance been
    50→    50→shown to be associated with relapse [14–17, although see 18], disorder severity [19], and
    51→    51→attentional biases [20,21]. Given its crucial role, it is essential to measure craving as accurately
    52→    52→as possible. Various methods were developed to measure alcohol craving [22], but the most
    53→    53→widely used are self-reported scales.
    54→    54→As with other measurements based on self-reports, there are many potential sources of
    55→    55→bias that could impact craving accuracy measurement. Over 20 year ago, Sayette [23] already
    56→    56→pointed out that craving research should take into account the multiple factors that could
    57→    57→potentially affect the measurement of craving through self-reported questionnaires, citing for
    58→    58→instance social desirability (SD), response styles, and memory biases. We have recently argued
    59→    59→that using self-reported craving scales repeatedly in the same experiment (as is usually done
    60→    60→when craving changes are expected after an experimental manipulation), could impair its proper
    61→    61→evaluation at the end of the experiment [24]. Indeed, the initial completion of the craving scale
    62→    62→by the participants could directly act as an alcohol cue, evoking alcohol consumption and
    63→    63→therefore possibly promoting or altering the subjective experience of craving [25]. In other
    64→    64→
    65→    65→4
    66→    66→words, the initial craving scale could have a priming effect on alcohol craving. Additionally,
    67→    67→self-reported craving scales are generally very explicit and therefore possibly vulnerable to
    68→    68→various experimental biases, such as social desirability and demand effects. Social desirability,
    69→    69→or the tendency to respond in a manner that makes one “look good” [26] might significantly
    70→    70→affect the measurement of craving, although this has been little investigated. Demand effects
    71→    71→on the other hand, are related to the fact that participants are not passive responders to the
    72→    72→experimental manipulations. They typically seek to offer a valuable contribution [27] and
    73→    73→therefore to behave according to their understanding of the aim of the study. As a consequence,
    74→    74→any explicit or implicit information about the purpose of the study is likely to affect their
    75→    75→responses and behavior. All the explicit and implicit clues about the purpose of the study are
    76→    76→called “demand characteristics”, because participants use these clues to identify what the
    77→    77→experimenter is “demanding” of them. More recently, demand effects were conceptualized in
    78→    78→three sub-dimensions [28]: cue interpretation (i.e., "the analysis and interpretation of these cues
    79→    79→by the participants to produce a set of conjectures about the purpose of the research"),
    80→    80→sensitivity to demand characteristics (i.e., an expression of interest on behalf of the respondent,
    81→    81→seeking to make sense of the questioning situation with which they are confronted"), and
    82→    82→response motivation (i.e., "the willingness of the individuals being questioned to rely on their
    83→    83→interpretation of the demand characteristics in order to engage in specific response behaviors).
    84→    84→Based on these premises, the explicit nature of self-reported craving scales, especially when
    85→    85→administered twice in the same experiment, is likely to induce specific demand effects in the
    86→    86→participants.
    87→    87→In a recent experiment, we investigated the impact of a pre-test measurement of alcohol
    88→    88→craving on its final assessment, in relation to social desirability and demand effects [24].
    89→    89→Participants were randomly allocated to two experimental groups: a “pre-post” group in which
    90→    90→craving was assessed twice, before and after watching an alcohol-related video; or a “post-only”
    91→    91→
    92→    92→5
    93→    93→group in which craving was assessed only after the video. Social desirability, demand effects,
    94→    94→engagement toward the video, and alcohol consumption levels were also assessed using
    95→    95→validated questionnaires. The results showed that the levels of craving after watching an alcohol
    96→    96→video were higher in heavier drinkers relative to low drinkers. Additionally, the pre-test
    97→    97→assessment of craving had a significant impact on post-test craving, but only in heavier alcohol
    98→    98→drinkers. More precisely, in high alcohol drinkers, alcohol craving was significantly higher after
    99→    99→the video when craving was also assessed before the video. No such effect was observed in low
   100→   100→alcohol drinkers. In contrast, no relationship between self-reported craving and SD or demand
   101→   101→effects was found in that study. Finally, exploratory analyses indicated that engagement toward
   102→   102→the alcohol-related video was a significant mediator of subsequent craving increases.
   103→   103→Since this initial study took place during the COVID-19 lockdown period and considering
   104→   104→its modest sample size, we aimed to assess the robustness of these findings in a non-lockdown
   105→   105→context, using a larger sample size. We took advantage of the participant pool at the University
   106→   106→of Liège to replicate the study on a sample of first-year psychology students.
   107→   107→2. Methods
   108→   108→The present study used the exact same design as the initial study (see [24]). The only
   109→   109→modification was the replacement of the DS36 social desirability scale [29] by the Social
   110→   110→Desirability Scale-17 (SDS-17)[30], measuring social desirability with a one-factor structure
   111→   111→instead of the two-factor structure (DS36). This modification was chosen to shorten the study
   112→   112→duration and because our primary interest was in 'impression management'—the act of
   113→   113→consciously presenting oneself in a favorable light to others—rather than 'self-deceptive
   114→   114→enhancement,' which involves unconsciously seeing oneself in a highly positive manner.
   115→   115→Participants were a hundred-and-forty-two 1st-year psychology students (92% female,
   116→   116→mean age= 19.33, sd= 1.81), recruited through a “participant pool” device of the Faculty of
   117→   117→
   118→   118→6
   119→   119→Psychology at the University of Liège. This high percentage of female participants reflects the
   120→   120→gender distribution within the faculty. Students received course credit for their participation and
   121→   121→took part in an interactive educational session about the study. Exclusion criteria were strictly
   122→   122→the same as in the initial study. Participants were randomly assigned to one of the two
   123→   123→experimental groups: pre-post group (N= 72) vs. post-only group (N= 70). This study was
   124→   124→approved by the Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Psychology, Speech and Language Therapy
   125→   125→and Education of the University (on October the 3rd 2023, reference number: 10968) and was
   126→   126→conducted in accordance with the ethical standards described in the Declaration of Helsinki
   127→   127→(1964) and institutional guidelines. The privacy rights of participants have been observed and
   128→   128→informed consent was obtained.
   129→   129→Following the initial study, we aimed for a sample size of at least 77 participants.
   130→   130→However, given the participant pool setup, we anticipated obtaining a higher number of
   131→   131→subjects.
   132→   132→Statistical analyses were run using R [31]. To examine the effect of repeated measures of
   133→   133→craving, alcohol consumption, and their interaction on final craving levels, a multiple linear
   134→   134→regression analysis was conducted. However, the normality of residuals was not achieved for
   135→   135→this model. Consequently, craving data were square root transformed, which improved the
   136→   136→distribution significantly, but residual normality was still not met due to a floor effect (Shapiro-
   137→   137→Wilk p-value ranging from <.0001 to .007). Despite this, as homoscedasticity was satisfied
   138→   138→(Breusch-Pagan test: p=0.49), the same linear regression model as in the previous study was
   139→   139→applied. To determine if there was a relationship between craving and engagement with the
   140→   140→alcohol-related video, similar to the previous study, a Pearson correlation coefficient was
   141→   141→computed. Finally, Kendall’s Tau correlation coefficients were used to investigate social
   142→   142→desirability and demand characteristics.

   218→   218→4. Discussion
   219→   219→The primary objective of this study was to determine whether our initial findings could be
   220→   220→self-replicated outside the context of the covid-19 lockdown. The initial study examined
   221→   221→whether repeated measurements of craving influenced its assessment after watching an alcohol-
   222→   222→related video, considering social desirability and demand effects. The findings of the initial
   223→   223→study revealed significant effects of the repeated measurement of craving, alcohol consumption
   224→   224→level, and their interaction, suggesting that pre-experimental craving measurements elevated
   225→   225→post-experimental levels, but only in heavy drinkers. No evidence was found linking these
   226→   226→
   227→   227→11
   228→   228→changes to social desirability or demand effects. Further analyses also indicated that the increase
   229→   229→in craving in heavy drinkers was mediated by a stronger engagement with the alcohol-related
   230→   230→video.
   231→   231→The present study replicated most results of the initial study, strengthening the robustness
   232→   232→of the initial findings. In both studies, level of alcohol consumption significantly influenced
   233→   233→cue-induced craving, and, when repeated assessments were conducted, craving levels increased
   234→   234→following cue exposure compared to pre-exposure levels, indicating successful craving
   235→   235→induction. Similar effects were documented in the literature over recent decades [25,32–35].
   236→   236→These results demonstrate that the video cue exposure used in both studies successfully induced
   237→   237→subjective craving and effectively differentiated between light and heavy drinkers.
   238→   238→Notably, the impact of engagement was also replicated, with a correlation of similar
   239→   239→magnitude in the two studies (initial study: r = .44; present study: r = .42). This finding provides
   240→   240→further support for our hypothesis that the level of engagement with alcohol-related stimuli
   241→   241→(whether in a video format or other media) is a critical factor in craving induction.
   242→   242→In both studies, there was no indication of a statistically significant effect of social
   243→   243→desirability, or demand characteristics on cue-induced craving. This indicates that self-reported
   244→   244→alcohol craving was not significantly affected by participants' tendencies to present themselves
   245→   245→in a socially desirable manner or to conform to perceived experimental expectations. The
   246→   246→absence of such biases enhances the validity of the experimental findings on craving, suggesting
   247→   247→that the responses were mainly driven by experimental manipulations, such as alcohol cue
   248→   248→exposure. However, future research could further validate this conclusion by using implicit
   249→   249→measures of craving or incorporating additional checks to detect potential subtle influences of
   250→   250→social desirability and demand characteristics.
   251→   251→
   252→   252→12
   253→   253→Since the effects of repeated measurements of craving were not replicated in the present
   254→   254→study, we questioned what differences between the two studies might explain these
   255→   255→discrepancies. A major difference was the gender ratio. Participants in the second study were
   256→   256→recruited from a "participant pool" in the Faculty of Psychology, resulting in a very high
   257→   257→proportion of female participants, reflecting the faculty's gender distribution. Consequently, we
   258→   258→reanalyzed the data from the initial study to explore potential gender effects. This analysis
   259→   259→revealed a significant gender effect (Figure 3) with a large interaction effect between alcohol
   260→   260→consumption and repeated measurements in men, whereas this effect was absent in women.
   261→   261→Therefore, the two studies yielded similar effects when gender is included in the analyses. We
   262→   262→also checked whether differences in AUDIT-C scores or levels of engagement could act as
   263→   263→confounding variables explaining the observed gender difference; however, this was not the
   264→   264→case in our data (AUDIT-C: W= 745, p=0.35, r= 0.11 CI95% -0.12 - 0.21; Engagement:
   265→   265→W=680.5, p=0.83, r=0.026, CI 95%, -0.22 – 0.05). However, these analyses were not part of
   266→   266→the initial study design, and the sample sizes were relatively small (20 women and 15 men in
   267→   267→the pre-post group, and 24 women and 15 men in the post-only group). Therefore, further
   268→   268→research is needed to confirm these conclusions.
   269→   269→This observed gender difference might be related to gender stereotypes. For women, the
   270→   270→reported levels of post-video craving were consistent with their usual alcohol consumption:
   271→   271→light drinkers reported low craving, while heavier drinkers reported higher levels of craving,
   272→   272→regardless of the repeated measurements. In contrast, a different pattern emerged for men. When
   273→   273→craving was assessed only after the video, the reported levels were unrelated to their drinking
   274→   274→habits. Their responses seemed to be directly influenced by the cue exposure itself, potentially
   275→   275→activating social stereotypes that portray heavy drinking as socially positive for men. However,
   276→   276→the pre-video measurement of craving appeared to anchor their reported levels to their drinking
   277→   277→
   278→   278→13
   279→   279→habits, subsequently influencing their post-video responses. This speculative explanation
   280→   280→clearly requires further confirmation in future studies.
   281→   281→In conclusion, the present results suggest that an initial pre-exposure craving measurement
   282→   282→could significantly impact the final craving assessment in men, but not in women. Moreover,
   283→   283→the way individuals engage with alcohol-related videos is significantly related to increased
   284→   284→craving for alcohol afterwards. These findings also confirm the absence of social desirability
   285→   285→and demand effects observed in our previous study. Further investigations are needed to
   286→   286→determine whether these effects of an initial craving measure are beneficial or represent a
   287→   287→significant bias in craving studies, and why they may not apply to women.
