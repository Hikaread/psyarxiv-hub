    34→    34→Abstract
    35→    35→Few measures assess the specific indicators of disruptive mood dysregulation disorder
    36→    36→(DMDD). While the E-SWAN DMDD questionnaire evaluates DMDD symptoms, its length
    37→    37→increases response burden, and limits use in resource-constrained settings. Using data from 3,835
    38→    38→participants in the Healthy Brain Network (HBN; mean age = 10.25 years; 63% male), item
    39→    39→responses were calibrated using the Graded Response Model and computerized adaptive testing
    40→    40→(CAT) simulations were conducted to develop an abbreviated questionnaire. The four most
    41→    41→frequently selected items comprise the short form, which correlated highly with the full form
    42→    42→(Levy-corrected r = 0.776), and demonstrated moderate associations with related behavioral
    43→    43→constructs (ρ = 0.120 - 0.424, p < .001). Compared to the full form, the short form demonstrated
    44→    44→good specificity (a weaker association with the general psychopathology factor) while
    45→    45→maintaining strong associations with externalizing behavior. Taken together, the short form
    46→    46→offers a brief assessment with strong validity that decreases response burden.
    47→    47→Keywords: disruptive mood dysregulation disorder, irritability, externalizing behavior,
    48→    48→development, item response theory, computerized adaptive testing, youth psychopathology

    50→    50→Introduction
    51→    51→Studies suggest that 3% of children and adolescents suffer from severe irritability
    52→    52→(Brotman et al., 2006), and rising irritability is associated with increased suicide risk in youth
    53→    53→(Benarous et al., 2019; Leibenluft et al., 2024; Orri et al., 2019). Irritability is defined by the
    54→    54→emotion of anger and behavioral temper outbursts, often involving being easily annoyed
    55→    55→(Stringaris, 2011). Previous work has characterized chronic irritability in youth under the broad
    56→    56→phenotype of pediatric bipolar disorder, termed severe mood dysregulation (Leibenluft et al.,
    57→    57→2003). In contrast to bipolar affective disorder, severe mood dysregulation captured consistent
    58→    58→acute irritability and temper outbursts, rather than distinct periods of hypomania or mania
    59→    59→(Leibenluft et al., 2003; McTate & Leffler, 2017). Individuals diagnosed with severe mood
    60→    60→dysregulation also displayed symptoms of hyperarousal, such as distractibility and agitation
    61→    61→(Leibenluft et al., 2003). Despite this attempt to isolate irritability as a construct distinct from
    62→    62→mania, the American Psychiatric Association (APA) still recognized a need to improve the
    63→    63→classification of nonepisodic irritability and prevent misdiagnosis with pediatric bipolar disorder
    64→    64→(Roy et al., 2014; Stringaris et al., 2018). Disruptive mood dysregulation disorder (DMDD),
    65→    65→therefore, emerged in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (5th ed., text
    66→    66→rev.; DSM-5) as a new diagnosis assigned to children between the ages of 6 to 18 (APA, 2022).
    67→    67→Individuals diagnosed with DMDD experience at least three temper outbursts per week, with
    68→    68→symptoms in at least two of the following settings: at home, at school, and with peers (APA,
    69→    69→2022). However, despite these clear diagnostic criteria, there is limited research directly focused
    70→    70→on the assessment of DMDD (McTate & Leffler, 2017; Tapia & John, 2018). The current paper
    71→    71→describes the development of a short form version of the Extended Strengths and Weaknesses
    72→    72→Assessment of Normal Behavior (E-SWAN) DMDD scale (Alexander et al., 2020).
    73→    73→
    74→    74→Given that irritability is common in children and adolescents, and observed in multiple
    75→    75→psychiatric disorders (Evans et al., 2023; Stringaris, 2011; Stringaris et al., 2018), distinguishing
    76→    76→DMDD-specific symptoms from those observed in overlapping conditions is complex (Tapia &
    77→    77→John, 2018). While comorbidities often occur across disruptive behavior problems, existing
    78→    78→evidence suggests that youth and caregivers perceive irritability as a top problem, frequently
    79→    79→precipitating mental health treatment (Evans et al., 2023). Despite this, some argue that the
    80→    80→current assessment approach for DMDD is insufficient (Roy et al., 2014). Many tools (e.g., self-
    81→    81→report questionnaires, structured interviews) capture relevant, but nonspecific, indicators of
    82→    82→DMDD (Tapia & John, 2018). For example, the Affective Reactivity Index (ARI) measures self,
    83→    83→parent, and teacher reported irritability in youth (Stringaris et al., 2012). However, persistent
    84→    84→irritable mood is associated with other developmental psychiatric disorders, spanning both
    85→    85→internalizing (e.g., anxiety disorders; Leibenluft et al., 2024; Shimshoni et al., 2020) and
    86→    86→externalizing (e.g., oppositional defiant disorder, intermittent explosive disorder; Roy et al.,
    87→    87→2014) categories. The transdiagnostic relevance of irritability reflects the phenomenon in
    88→    88→psychopathology in which many disorders share common features (Lahey et al., 2012). This has
    89→    89→led investigators to develop measures that attempt to disentangle behavioral features most closely
    90→    90→associated with DMDD from other disorders (Leibenluft et al., 2024; Mürner-Lavanchy et al.,
    91→    91→2023) as well as the general psychopathology “p” factor (Caspi et al., 2014).
    92→    92→While honing a DMDD assessment approach, it is also important to mitigate the impact
    93→    93→of response burden (i.e., response fatigue). Previous work on measures of transdiagnostic
    94→    94→irritability emphasizes a need for brief screening tools with clinical utility in a youth psychiatric
    95→    95→population (Stringaris et al., 2012). However, the Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and
    96→    96→Schizophrenia Present and Lifetime Version (K-SADS-PL; Kaufman et al., 1997) is still the
    97→    97→
    98→    98→most frequently employed diagnostic measure in studies of DMDD (Mürner-Lavanchy et al.,
    99→    99→2023). Although the more recent K-SADS-PL-5 includes a DMDD checklist, a trained assessor
   100→   100→must still administer all items in the format of a semi-structured diagnostic interview; this can
   101→   101→take up to 75 minutes in a psychiatric sample but does cover multiple domains of
   102→   102→psychopathology (Kaufman et al., 1997; Mürner-Lavanchy et al., 2023). In addition to this
   103→   103→significant time burden, diagnostic interviews are associated with high administration costs seen
   104→   104→across research and clinical settings (Boyle et al., 2017). Self-report questionnaires decrease this
   105→   105→professional time involved in administering clinical interviews, which is particularly relevant in
   106→   106→measuring childhood psychopathology (Boyle et al., 2017; Zimmerman et al., 2006).
   107→   107→Existing literature indicates that implementing self-report questionnaires or screening
   108→   108→tools may be strategic in assessing for DMDD. Compared to interview assessments,
   109→   109→questionnaires are more easily integrated in resource-limited or time-constrained clinical or
   110→   110→research settings focused on measuring irritability in development (Wilson et al., 2022). The
   111→   111→Extended Strengths and Weaknesses Assessment of Normal Behavior (E-SWAN) DMDD
   112→   112→(Alexander et al., 2020) scale is a recently developed 30-item self-report measure designed to
   113→   113→capture the specific symptoms of DMDD dimensionally. In particular, the E-SWAN DMDD
   114→   114→scale offers an enhanced alternative to traditional interview assessments of broad pediatric
   115→   115→irritability. By considering a hierarchy of symptoms (i.e., a continuum of severity), rather than
   116→   116→binary classifications produced by interview assessments (Alexander et al., 2020; Boyle et al.,
   117→   117→2017), the scale captures clinical nuance outside of diagnostic categories. The E-SWAN DMDD
   118→   118→scale assesses symptoms as behaviors at home, at school, and with peers, which also reflects how
   119→   119→the DSM-5 criteria for DMDD occur across multiple settings. Despite these clear advantages,
   120→   120→existing literature still suggests that substantial response burden can result from self-report
   121→   121→
   122→   122→questionnaires, and may be more pronounced in children (Stone, 2007). Respondents may feel
   123→   123→especially burdened while completing repetitive measures of symptoms, quality of life, and
   124→   124→psychosocial functioning in a single self-report battery of questionnaires (Zimmerman et al.,
   125→   125→2006). As such, one clear gap in the field is the need for a shorter version of the relatively
   126→   126→lengthy E-SWAN DMDD scale.
   127→   127→In the current study, we describe the development of a short form E-SWAN DMDD
   128→   128→scale. To do so, we employed Item Response Theory (IRT) measurement models combined with
   129→   129→computerized adaptive testing (CAT) simulation, a frequently used technique for developing
   130→   130→short form questionnaires featured in previous studies (Lawson et al., 2020; Moore et al., 2015;
   131→   131→Roalf et al., 2016). IRT provides useful parameter estimates for items that could comprise a short
   132→   132→form, and when combined with CAT, prevents the administration of items that provide little
   133→   133→information (Roalf et al., 2016; Segall, 2005; Weiss & Kingsbury, 1984). The existing literature
   134→   134→demonstrates that shorter questionnaires can not only increase data quality, but also reduce
   135→   135→response burden (Rolstad et al., 2011). Especially in research or clinical contexts where the time
   136→   136→allotted to respondents for completing assessments is constrained, guidelines maintain that short
   137→   137→form scales used to measure symptoms of psychopathology (e.g., distress, depression, anxiety)
   138→   138→performed comparably to their full versions (Lantos et al., 2023). Therefore, a short form of the
   139→   139→E-SWAN DMDD scale may be particularly useful in both identifying the core symptoms of
   140→   140→DMDD dimensionally, and decreasing burden for youth respondents faced with chronic
   141→   141→irritability.
   142→   142→Methods
   143→   143→Participants. The current study included 3,835 participants from the Healthy Brain Network
   144→   144→(HBN) initiative, developed by the Child Mind Institute (Alexander et al., 2017). HBN includes
   145→   145→
   146→   146→densely sampled cross-sectional brain, behavioral, and lifestyle data from a diverse community
   147→   147→sample of youth (ages 5-21 years old) in the New York City metropolitan area. The current study
   148→   148→utilized responses to behavioral questionnaires obtained from a subset of these HBN participants
   149→   149→(mean age = 10.25 years old; 63% male) from the April 2024 data release. These data were made
   150→   150→accessible in the HBN Biobank, obtained through a data use agreement (DUA). The study was
   151→   151→approved by the Chesapeake Institutional Review Board. Written informed consent was obtained
   152→   152→from participants ages 18 or older. For participants younger than 18, written consent was
   153→   153→obtained from a parent or legal guardian, and written assent was obtained from the participant
   154→   154→(Alexander et al., 2017).
   155→   155→Behavioral assessments. HBN participants were assigned a battery of online self-administered
   156→   156→questionnaires designed to evaluate behavioral indicators of developmental psychopathology.
   157→   157→These questionnaires assessed constructs such as family structure, stress, trauma, and substance
   158→   158→use. In addition, HBN collected observer-report questionnaires completed by participants’
   159→   159→parents and teachers. The current study utilized measures from the “Social, Emotional, and
   160→   160→Behavioral Function” and “Family Structure, Stress, and Trauma” domains of the HBN
   161→   161→assessment protocol. See Alexander et al. (2017) for more information about the HBN behavioral
   162→   162→phenotyping protocol.
   163→   163→Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder
   164→   164→The Extended Strengths and Weaknesses Assessment of Normal Behavior Disruptive
   165→   165→Mood Dysregulation Disorder (E-SWAN DMDD; Alexander et al., 2020) is a 30-item self-report
   166→   166→measure that evaluates setting-specific symptoms of DMDD (e.g., non-episodic irritability,
   167→   167→frequent severe temper outbursts). The E-SWAN DMDD questionnaire represents the full form
   168→   168→assessment used in the current study. Respondents indicate the extent of their symptomology
   169→   169→
   170→   170→using a 7-point scale (“Far above average” to “Far below average”) within the following three
   171→   171→domains: at home, at school, and with peers. Scores are calculated by generating the average of
   172→   172→the individual item values. Negative scores indicate greater strengths, and positive scores
   173→   173→indicate greater weaknesses (i.e., increased psychopathology). The E-SWAN framework builds
   174→   174→upon the Strengths and Weaknesses Assessment of ADHD Symptoms and Normal Behavior
   175→   175→(SWAN; Swanson et al., 2015). Similar to the SWAN, the E-SWAN seeks to measure the
   176→   176→dimensions of psychiatric symptoms across the full population distribution. Prior work has
   177→   177→demonstrated that the E-SWAN DMDD shows high internal consistency and reliability across
   178→   178→the full latent trait range (Alexander et al., 2020).
   179→   179→Predictors Used in the Validation Sample
   180→   180→We examined evidence for convergent validity between the E-SWAN DMDD short form,
   181→   181→full form, and related constructs (e.g., irritability, distress tolerance) in developmental
   182→   182→psychopathology, as well as known-groups validity using participant demographics (sex).
   183→   183→Irritability
   184→   184→The Affective Reactivity Index (ARI; Stringaris et al., 2012) is a 7-item questionnaire
   185→   185→that evaluates chronic irritability over the past six months. Irritability refers to a mood of easy
   186→   186→annoyance and touchiness that is often associated with anger and temper outbursts (Stringaris,
   187→   187→2011). The ARI is specifically intended to assess both parent- and self-reported irritability in
   188→   188→youth. The ARI includes six items targeting symptoms (i.e., feelings, behaviors) and one item
   189→   189→measuring impairment due to irritability. Furthermore, these items aim to identify (a) the
   190→   190→threshold for having an anger reaction, (b) the frequency of angry feelings or behaviors, and (c)
   191→   191→the duration of such angry feelings or behaviors. Higher scores indicate greater severity of
   192→   192→irritability symptoms. The current study only included the self-report version of the ARI and
   193→   193→
   194→   194→utilized the ARI total score, calculated as the sum of the six symptom items.
   195→   195→Emotional Distress Tolerance
   196→   196→The Distress Tolerance Scale (DTS; Simons & Gaher, 2005) is a 15-item self-report
   197→   197→questionnaire that evaluates four key concepts associated with distress tolerance. Distress
   198→   198→tolerance refers to the ability to experience and endure negative psychological conditions. The
   199→   199→DTS considers (a) the perceived ability to tolerate emotional distress, (b) the subjective appraisal
   200→   200→of distress, (c) the extent of an individual’s attention that is absorbed by negative emotion, and
   201→   201→(d) the emotion regulation efforts used in attempt to alleviate distress. Higher total scores
   202→   202→indicate increased distress tolerance across the four subscales: Tolerance, Appraisal, Absorption,
   203→   203→and Regulation. The current study utilized the DTS total score, calculated as the sum of all items.
   204→   204→Child Behavior Checklist
   205→   205→The Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL; Achenbach, 1991) is a 113-item observer-report
   206→   206→questionnaire for which a parent rates their child’s problem behaviors and competencies. The
   207→   207→CBCL can also be completed by other individuals (e.g., a primary caregiver) who know the child
   208→   208→well. Moreover, the CBCL can be used as a measure to assess changes in a child’s behavior over
   209→   209→time or following a treatment intervention. The current study relied upon a harmonized bifactor
   210→   210→model of the CBCL described in Shafiei et al. (2025), a publicly available online neuroimaging
   211→   211→resource which includes HBN. The current study utilized the harmonized Overall
   212→   212→Psychopathology p-factor and Externalizing scores from Shafiei et al. (2025).
   213→   213→Teacher Report Form
   214→   214→The Child Behavior Checklist–Teacher Report Form (TRF; Achenbach, 1991) is a 113-
   215→   215→item observer-report questionnaire for which a teacher who knows a child well rates the child’s
   216→   216→problem behaviors and competencies. Teachers indicate how many months they have known the
   217→   217→
   218→   218→child, their subjective evaluation of how “well” they know the child (i.e., not well, moderately
   219→   219→well, very well), and the amount of time that the child spends in their class or service each week.
   220→   220→Similar to the parent-report CBCL, the TRF can be used as a measure to assess changes in a
   221→   221→child’s behavior over time or following a treatment intervention. The TRF includes 20 social
   222→   222→competency items related to the child’s participation in activities (e.g., sports, hobbies, jobs),
   223→   223→their ability to get along with others, their ability to work and play alone, and their school
   224→   224→functioning. TRF raw scores are calculated as the sum of the individual item level ratings. The
   225→   225→current study utilized t-scores corresponding to selected syndrome (e.g., Rule-Breaking
   226→   226→Behavior, Aggressive Behavior, Externalizing) and DSM-Oriented subscales (e.g., Oppositional
   227→   227→Defiant Problems).
   228→   228→Statistical Analyses
   229→   229→Identifying Items for the E-SWAN DMDD Short Form
   230→   230→We implemented a published method (Moore et al., 2015) that utilizes Item Response
   231→   231→Theory (IRT; Embretson & Reise, 2013) and Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) to create
   232→   232→the E-SWAN DMDD short form. IRT is a psychometric method that focuses on the unique
   233→   233→discrimination and difficulty of each individual item in a questionnaire (Roalf et al., 2016). The
   234→   234→IRT procedure was performed using the mirt package (Chalmers, 2012) within the RStudio
   235→   235→statistical environment (V 2025.5.1.513; Posit team, 2025). In the context of the current study,
   236→   236→we employed IRT and CAT simulation to identify which items from the original E-SWAN
   237→   237→DMDD are most indicative (i.e., discriminating) of disruptive mood dysregulation while
   238→   238→simultaneously having threshold parameters (difficulty) in optimal locations across the trait
   239→   239→continuum. Here we used the Graded Response Model (Samejima, 1969). The GRM works
   240→   240→similarly to the two-parameter (2PL) IRT model, incorporating additional difficulty parameters
   241→   241→
   242→   242→reflecting thresholds. These thresholds indicate where the likelihood of responding in one Likert
   243→   243→category (e.g., the lowest response option) is overtaken by the likelihood of responding in the
   244→   244→subsequent category (e.g., the second lowest response option; Moore et al., 2023).
   245→   245→E-SWAN DMDD items share variance for three reasons: (1) they are all influenced by
   246→   246→the common overall trait, disruptive mood dysregulation; (2) some ask about the same behavior
   247→   247→(e.g., controlling the intensity of anger); and (3) some refer to the same situation or location
   248→   248→(home, school, or with peers). We are interested in #1, while the covariance due to #2 and #3
   249→   249→could be considered “nuisance” covariance that needs to be controlled for. We therefore needed
   250→   250→to use a multidimensional IRT model that includes latent factors explaining all three sources of
   251→   251→variance–i.e., a trifactor model. A trifactor model is a straightforward extension of the more
   252→   252→commonly known bifactor model (Gibbons et al., 2007; Reise, 2012), in which each item loads
   253→   253→on both a general factor (comprising all items) and a specific factor (comprising only items that
   254→   254→share variance for a more specific reason). Here, the “more specific reason” is #2 above (asking
   255→   255→about the same behavior), and because there is a third source of covariance (#3 above), we
   256→   256→needed to add a third factor, forming a trifactor model. This was done using the flexible
   257→   257→mirt.model() argument within the mirt() command in mirt.
   258→   258→CAT Simulation
   259→   259→The overarching goal of the CAT approach is to avoid administering items that provide
   260→   260→minimal information about a respondent (Moore et al., 2015). The trifactor GRM produced item
   261→   261→parameter estimates that were then used as the item bank in the CAT simulation. These simulated
   262→   262→sessions were used to identify which items from the original E-SWAN DMDD would be selected
   263→   263→most often, indicating that they possessed the most optimal combinations of discrimination and
   264→   264→difficulty (threshold) parameters. These most-selected items were identified for the E-SWAN
   265→   265→
   266→   266→DMDD short form. Similar methods have been employed in past studies (Moore et al., 2015;
   267→   267→Roalf et al., 2016).
   268→   268→CAT is a method of item administration that updates a respondent’s trait level estimates
   269→   269→as they respond to a series of items (Wainer et al., 2000). These estimates are used to determine
   270→   270→which item from a given item bank (i.e., the original E-SWAN DMDD questionnaire) provides
   271→   271→the most information about the respondent at the current/interim trait level. This most
   272→   272→informative item is administered, and the respondent is re-scored. This process of administering
   273→   273→the most informative item, re-scoring, administering the newly most-informative item, etc. is
   274→   274→repeated until a stopping criterion is reached–e.g., the respondent’s standard error of
   275→   275→measurement reached an acceptably low level (Wang et al., 2021). For our purposes, when CAT
   276→   276→is simulated, it reveals which items will be most frequently selected across testing sessions. The
   277→   277→CAT sessions used in the current study indicated which E-SWAN DMDD items revealed the
   278→   278→most information about overall E-SWAN DMDD performance. In other words, if the items that
   279→   279→were not frequently administered (due to being too difficult/easy or not discriminating) were
   280→   280→removed, the overall information about DMDD would drop only negligibly (Lawson et al.,
   281→   281→2020). All simulations described in the current study were performed using the real data from
   282→   282→HBN respondents.
   283→   283→Instrument Validation
   284→   284→With the most informative items selected, we then examined evidence for validity of the
   285→   285→E-SWAN DMDD short form. First, we assessed the Spearman correlations between the short and
   286→   286→full form versions. Second, we examined correlations between the short form and full form
   287→   287→versions with the predictors (e.g., theoretically related behavioral questionnaires) previously
   288→   288→described. These include the ARI (Stringaris et al., 2012), the DTS (Simons & Gaher, 2005), the
   289→   289→
   290→   290→CBCL (Achenbach, 1991), and the TRF (Achenbach, 1991). Similar correlations between each
   291→   291→form with the predictors provided validity evidence for the short form. We then gathered
   292→   292→additional validity evidence by examining correlations with sex. Differences between the short
   293→   293→form and full form versions were also tested for statistical significance using bootstrapping
   294→   294→(Efron, 1979). For each bootstrap iteration, the difference between the forms was recorded,
   295→   295→resulting in a distribution of 10,000 resamples.
   296→   296→External Dataset Validation
   297→   297→To evaluate the external construct validity of the E-SWAN DMDD short form, we sought
   298→   298→to replicate our analyses in an independent sample of youth and young adults (ages 8-30). The
   299→   299→current study utilized responses to behavioral questionnaires from a subset of participants (N =
   300→   300→244; mean age = 16.7 years old; 58% female) who completed prospective studies of brain
   301→   301→development conducted at the University of Pennsylvania. These studies were approved by the
   302→   302→University of Pennsylvania Institutional Review Board. Written informed consent was obtained
   303→   303→from participants ages 18 or older. For participants younger than 18, written consent was
   304→   304→obtained from a parent or legal guardian, and written assent was obtained from the participant.
   305→   305→First, we calculated E-SWAN DMDD short form scores in the external sample using response
   306→   306→scores to the four items selected from the CAT simulation in the primary analyses. We then
   307→   307→examined evidence for the validity of the E-SWAN DMDD short form with correlations between
   308→   308→the ARI and sex.
   309→   309→Results
   310→   310→Item Response Theory
   311→   311→Table 1 shows the models estimated using the Item Response Theory (IRT) Graded
   312→   312→Response Model. Item parameters convey estimates for the individual discrimination and
   313→   313→
   314→   314→difficulty level of each E-SWAN DMDD item. The discrimination parameter (α) refers to how
   315→   315→well each item differentiates between respondents at distinct levels of the latent trait (e.g.,
   316→   316→externalizing behavior). For example, a high discrimination value indicates that an item
   317→   317→effectively discriminates between respondents. In the current study, discrimination parameters
   318→   318→ranged from 2.35 to 5.35 (M = 3.74). The most highly discriminating item was Item 3B (“Avoid
   319→   319→or control ‘taking anger out on objects or throwing things at people’ with friends”; α = 5.35). The
   320→   320→least discriminating item was Item 5A (“Return quickly to baseline mood and behavior when
   321→   321→frustrated at home”; α = 2.35). The difficulty parameter (β ) refers to the severity on the
   322→   322→3
   323→   323→spectrum of the latent trait (e.g., externalizing behavior) that is necessary for a respondent to
   324→   324→endorse the behavior at a particular response level. In the current study, difficulty parameters
   325→   325→ranged from -1.61 to 0.25 (M = -0.66). The most difficult item was Item 3C (“Avoid or control
   326→   326→‘taking anger out on objects or throwing things at people’ at school”; β = 0.25). The least
   327→   327→3

   474→   474→Instrument Validation
   475→   475→Correlations between the E-SWAN DMDD Short and Full Forms
   476→   476→In order to examine validity evidence for the E-SWAN DMDD short form, we first
   477→   477→measured the Levy-corrected correlation with the original E-SWAN DMDD questionnaire. E-
   478→   478→SWAN DMDD scores are calculated as the average of the individual item values. E-SWAN
   479→   479→DMDD short form scores were highly correlated with those of the E-SWAN DMDD full form
   480→   480→(Levy-corrected r = 0.776), indicating strong correspondence between the forms.
   481→   481→Relationships with Related Constructs
   482→   482→We next examined associations between the E-SWAN DMDD short form, full form, and
   483→   483→related external validators. Figure 1 displays correlations between z-standardized E-SWAN
   484→   484→DMDD scores and measures of related constructs. Spearman correlations with the selected
   485→   485→observer- and self-report behavioral questionnaires provided evidence for convergent validity. E-
   486→   486→SWAN DMDD short form scores were moderately positively associated with the Affective
   487→   487→Reactivity Index (ARI; ρ = 0.231, p < .001) and the Distress Tolerance Scale (DTS; ρ = 0.120, p
   488→   488→
   489→   489→< .001). Similar patterns were observed for the harmonized Externalizing domain of the Child
   490→   490→Behavior Checklist (CBCL; ρ = 0.378, p < .001) and the Externalizing Behavior (ρ = 0.394, p <
   491→   491→.001), Aggressive Behavior (ρ = 0.389, p < .001), Rule-Breaking Behavior (ρ = 0.345, p < .001),
   492→   492→and Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ρ = 0.388, p < .001) domains of the Teacher Report Form
   493→   493→(TRF).
   494→   494→In addition, evidence for discriminant validity of the E-SWAN DMDD short form was
   495→   495→demonstrated by a weaker association with the overall psychopathology p-factor than the full
   496→   496→form (ρ = 0.424 vs. 0.530, p < .001). A test of the difference between the two dependent
   497→   497→correlations with the CBCL harmonized p-factor confirmed this difference was statistically
   498→   498→significant (t = -8.684, p < .001). Finally, the short form showed a stronger negative biserial
   499→   499→correlation with sex (r = -0.181, p < .001) than the full form (r = -0.157, p < .001). A test of the
   500→   500→b b
   501→   501→difference between the two dependent correlations with sex displayed that this difference was
   502→   502→statistically significant (t = -2.239, p = 0.025), however, the effect size was negligible (Cohen’s q
   503→   503→= 0.025).
   504→   504→External Dataset Validation
   505→   505→After examining for relationships between the E-SWAN DMDD short form, full form,
   506→   506→and related constructs within the HBN sample, we then tested for construct validity in the
   507→   507→external sample collected at the University of Pennsylvania. Similar to within the HBN sample,
   508→   508→E-SWAN DMDD short form scores showed a moderately positive association with the ARI (ρ =
   509→   509→0.307, p < .001).
   510→   510→Discussion
   511→   511→In this study, we developed a concise short form version of the Extended Strengths and
   512→   512→Weaknesses Assessment of Normal Behavior Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder (E-
   513→   513→
   514→   514→SWAN DMDD; Alexander et al., 2020) scale. We employed an established method (Moore et
   515→   515→al., 2015) that utilizes Item Response Theory (Embretson & Reise, 2013) and computerized
   516→   516→adaptive testing (CAT) to select the most informative items from the original 30-item E-SWAN
   517→   517→DMDD full form questionnaire. Using responses from the Healthy Brain Network initiative
   518→   518→(HBN; Alexander et al., 2017), analyses indicated that the four-item short form is correlated with
   519→   519→the full form and shows comparable relationships with external criteria, providing evidence of
   520→   520→validity. Together, the E-SWAN DMDD short form advances the rapid assessment of DMDD in
   521→   521→youth.
   522→   522→As described in previous work (Lawson et al., 2020; Moore et al., 2015; Roalf et al.,
   523→   523→2016), the approach employed in the current study aims to balance the discrimination and
   524→   524→difficulty parameters of each item to select the most informative items. Notably, the original E-
   525→   525→SWAN DMDD scale includes items that broadly assess features like baseline mood and emotion
   526→   526→regulation skills. The four items retained on the E-SWAN DMDD short form provided maximum
   527→   527→combined information, on average, and address dysregulation across settings (e.g., at home, at
   528→   528→school, and with peers). These include items 2B, 3B, 3C, and 6A from the full form E-SWAN
   529→   529→DMDD questionnaire. These items included on the short form are most relevant for examining
   530→   530→the severity of externalizing behavior (e.g., getting into fights, taking anger out on objects or
   531→   531→throwing things, handling anger after not getting what he/she wants).
   532→   532→To assess validity evidence for the abbreviated form, we examined relationships with
   533→   533→theoretically related constructs in developmental psychopathology. We found that E-SWAN
   534→   534→DMDD short form scores were moderately positively associated with selected self-report and
   535→   535→observer-report behavioral questionnaires, including the ARI (Stringaris et al., 2012). Notably,
   536→   536→we replicated associations with the ARI in an independent sample. These overall results are
   537→   537→
   538→   538→consistent with past work on the interscale relationship between the E-SWAN DMDD full form
   539→   539→and measures of childhood irritability (e.g., the ARI; Alexander et al., 2020). In the HBN dataset,
   540→   540→both the short and full forms were most highly correlated with the Externalizing domain of the
   541→   541→Teacher Report Form (TRF; Achenbach, 1991).
   542→   542→In addition, our analyses demonstrated that the full form was more highly correlated with
   543→   543→the overall psychopathology p-factor. This finding implies that the short form may serve as a
   544→   544→more specific measure of DMDD, rather than broad transdiagnostic symptoms of
   545→   545→psychopathology. Finally, our analyses revealed a significantly stronger negative biserial
   546→   546→correlation between the short form and sex, although both forms’ correlations with sex were
   547→   547→small. This finding reflects previous evidence that DMDD symptoms are more prevalent in
   548→   548→males than females (Mayes et al., 2019), and suggests that both forms may be useful in
   549→   549→illuminating sex differences in the DMDD phenotype.
   550→   550→While the E-SWAN DMDD full form questionnaire (Alexander et al., 2020) provides a
   551→   551→broader assessment of DMDD, a short form measure allows for increased utility in clinical and
   552→   552→research settings. The majority of youth diagnosed with DMDD have a comorbid psychiatric
   553→   553→disorder (Bruno et al., 2019), and the assessment tools historically employed to measure DMDD
   554→   554→(e.g., the Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia Present and Lifetime
   555→   555→Version) capture symptoms that exist across diagnostic categories (McTate & Leffler, 2017).
   556→   556→Therefore, a succinct self-report measure may better reveal the specific symptoms of DMDD,
   557→   557→and reduce the burden associated with exhaustive self-administered or interview assessments for
   558→   558→respondents (Boyle et al., 2017; Zimmerman et al., 2006). Especially given that DMDD is
   559→   559→diagnosed in children and adolescents between the ages of 6 and 18, a short form may mitigate
   560→   560→the response burden that is associated with self-report assessments in youth (Stone, 2007).
   561→   561→
   562→   562→Together, this study highlights the capacity for the proposed short form to reduce
   563→   563→response burden and decrease administration time, while focusing on specific indicators of
   564→   564→DMDD. It is nonetheless important to acknowledge several limitations. First, correlations
   565→   565→between the full and short forms of the E-SWAN DMDD were obtained using a sub-set of items
   566→   566→administered as part of the full-form–i.e., while the correlations between short and full were
   567→   567→Levy-corrected for overlapping items, they were still obtained from the same administration
   568→   568→(“sitting”) of the full scale. A more realistic assessment of the correlation between the full and
   569→   569→short forms would be to administer them separately with some brief amount of time in between.
   570→   570→Second, the current study only included youth self-report responses to the original E-SWAN
   571→   571→DMDD questionnaire. Future studies could benefit from adopting a multi-informant approach.
   572→   572→Examining parent-report responses to both forms may provide greater evidence for the utility of
   573→   573→the short form version to determine symptom severity across both the child’s and parent’s
   574→   574→perspectives. These limitations notwithstanding, we expect that the proposed short form tool will
   575→   575→allow both research and clinical settings to quickly assess the severity of externalizing behaviors
   576→   576→associated with DMDD in youth. In addition, by targeting items specific to the DMDD
   577→   577→phenotype, this measure contributes to efforts to distinguish DMDD from broader
   578→   578→psychopathology in youth.
