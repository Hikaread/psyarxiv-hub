   201→   201→analysis. Template analysis is a form of thematic analysis that involves the iterative
   202→   202→7
   203→   203→
   204→   204→development of a coding template, refined as analysis proceeds. Text passages of any length
   205→   205→sufficient to convey relevant information were considered as units of analysis. AH conducted
   206→   206→the primary coding in close collaboration with RE and JI. Initial coding began with two
   207→   207→interviews, after which a feedback and reflexivity meeting was held with RE and JI. Three
   208→   208→additional interviews were then coded, followed by another research group meeting.
   209→   209→Subsequent meetings were held after every five interviews. Previously coded material was
   210→   210→revisited each round to refine the coding template and ensure alignment of themes and quotes.
   211→   211→The self-defined cues and if-then plans participants created during the RCT also served as
   212→   212→analytic materials, as these relate centrally to intervention fidelity (and were verbalised during
   213→   213→the interviews). Participants did not provide feedback on findings.
   214→   214→Initial coding was informed by the three participant centred domains of intervention fidelity
   215→   215→(Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). Here, delivery referred to the extent to which the study
   216→   216→app delivered the intervention as intended by the researchers, receipt referred to the degree to
   217→   217→which participants understood the intervention instructions and could demonstrate the relevant
   218→   218→skills, and enactment referred to the application of the acquired skills in participants' daily lives.
   219→   219→During the development of the coding template, some codes emerged as well-established and
   220→   220→generalisable across participants, having been mentioned recurrently and lending themselves
   221→   221→readily to description at a higher level of abstraction. Other codes were closely tied to individual
   222→   222→participants' accounts and less readily generalisable but nonetheless retained for analysis.
   223→   223→In the results, each quote is accompanied by the participant ID, which indicates group
   224→   224→allocation: substitution (SUB), inhibition (INH), reduced accessibility (RAC), or control
   225→   225→(CON), along with the reward condition: reward (R) or no reward (NR). Line references from
   226→   226→the interview transcripts are also provided.
   227→   227→Researcher descriptions and epistemological stance
   228→   228→The authors RE and JI are actively engaged in intensive longitudinal habit research, and they
   229→   229→designed and conducted the preceding RCT. This proximity to the trial informed expectations
   230→   230→regarding potential fidelity challenges—for instance, anticipating emergence of strategy
   231→   231→blending as theme. AH, who conducted the coding, was not involved in the RCT but did conduct
   232→   232→some of the interviews, which provided familiarity with participants' accounts while
   233→   233→maintaining analytic distance from the trial's design and hypotheses. The epistemological
   234→   234→approach of this study is grounded in descriptive phenomenology (Willig, 2012) whereby
   235→   235→participants' accounts of their experiences with the intervention were taken at face value as
   236→   236→descriptions of their lived experience.

   554→   554→This study explored participant experiences of intervention fidelity in a habit degradation EMI
   555→   555→(Edgren et al., 2026). Template analysis of semi-structured interviews with 22 former trial
   556→   556→participants yielded eight themes spanning the three participant-centred fidelity domains
   557→   557→delivery, receipt, and enactment (Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). Technical disruptions to
   558→   558→app use (Theme 1) concerned delivery; cue selection (Theme 2) and the mismatch between if-
   559→   559→then plans and assigned strategies or cues (Theme 3) primarily concerned receipt; and Themes
   560→   560→4–8 primarily concerned enactment.
   561→   561→As Figure 1 illustrates, the themes did not operate in isolation but interacted, producing
   562→   562→cascading effects across the three domains. For example, suboptimal cue selection arose
   563→   563→principally from misunderstanding how cues should be chosen (receipt) yet also constrained
   564→   564→how reward could later be delivered (Theme 2); and even a well-chosen cue enabled reward
   565→   565→only once cue-contingent responses were actualised in the app (Theme 7; enactment). Some
   566→   566→themes also didn't fit neatly into a single domain: mixed reactions to rewards (Theme 7)
   567→   567→additionally reflected receipt, through uncertainty about the reward feature. These

   578→   578→planning has similarly been reported as difficult in diabetes prevention fidelity research (Miles

   581→   581→improve receipt. Second, the trial’s finding that participants frequently blended strategies
   582→   582→(Edgren et al., 2026) is consistent with pragmatic adaptation to everyday demands (Theme 5).

   586→   586→receipt-level issue, while others forgot or preferred not to record cue encounters. Crucially,
   587→   587→because reward was contingent on recording cue-behaviour repetitions, participants who had
   588→   588→selected infrequently occurring cues had few opportunities to log encounters and therefore to
   589→   589→receive reward (Theme 2).
