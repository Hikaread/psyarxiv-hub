    13â†’Abstract
    14â†’Background: The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) chatbots into mental health practices has grown substantially over the past few years. Objective: To examine peopleâ€™s attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support. Moreover, we investigated individualsâ€™ attitudes towards AI in general. Additionally, we examined demographic variables that influence these attitudes. Methods: We conducted a cross-sectional study in January 2026. We employed a convenience sample in Greece. We used the Artificial Intelligence in Mental Health Scale (AIMHS) to measure attitudes towards AI chatbots for mental health support. Also, we used the Artificial Intelligence Attitude Scale (AIAS-4) to measure general attitude toward AI. We examined the impact of demographic variables on attitudes towards AI through multivariable regression models. Results: AIMHS scores demonstrated a moderate level of positive attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support. Likewise, AIAS-4 scores reflected a moderate degree of favorable attitudes toward artificial intelligence in general. We found a statistically significant positive correlation between AIMHS and AIAS-4. Multivariable linear regression analysis showed that males and higher educated participants had more positive attitudes towards AI chatbots for mental health support. Moreover, increased age was associated with increased positive attitudes towards AI mental health chatbots. Age, daily use of social media/websites and competence in digital technologies were associated with increased positive attitudes towards AI in general. Also, higher educated participants had more positive attitudes towards AI in general. Conclusion: We identified moderate levels of positive attitudes toward AI mental health chatbots and AI in general. Several demographic characteristics affected individualsâ€™ attitudes towards AI.

    17â†’Introduction
    18â†’Mental health disorders represent one of the most significant and escalating global health challenges. Recent estimates indicate that more than one billion individuals worldwide live with a mental health condition, with anxiety and depressive disorders constituting the largest share of this burden [1,2]. Mental disorders are among the leading causes of years lived with disability globally, with sustained increases observed over the past decades across regions and age groups [3,4]. Persistent barriers to care, such as shortages of mental health professionals, long waiting times, high costs, and stigma, continue to limit access to traditional mental healthcare services [5â€“7]. Within the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), such external constraints create conditions in which alternative technologies may be perceived as particularly valuable, as increased unmet need is likely to heighten perceived usefulness, a central determinant of attitudes toward technology adoption [8â€“10].
    19â†’In response to these challenges, artificial intelligence (AI) technologies have been increasingly applied in the mental health domain. AI-based systems are now used for screening and assessment, symptom monitoring, predictive analytics, and the delivery of psychological support through conversational agents or chatbots [11,12]. Several reviews suggest that AI chatbots demonstrate potential benefits in reducing symptoms of depression, anxiety, and psychological distress, particularly in non-clinical or subclinical populations [13â€“16]. From a TAM perspective, such outcomes may enhance perceived usefulness, while conversational interfaces may increase perceived ease of use. However, concerns regarding data privacy, limited emotional understanding, algorithmic bias, lack of transparency, and insufficient clinical validation may negatively affect trust and perceived reliability [11,17,18]. Evidence emphasizes that usersâ€™ willingness to rely on intelligent systems depends not only on performance, but also on beliefs regarding competence, predictability, and ethical integrity; factors that are particularly salient in mental health applications [19,20].
    20â†’Attitudes toward AI in general constitute an important antecedent of acceptance of AI-based healthcare applications. General beliefs about AI influence both perceived usefulness and trust, thereby shaping attitudes and behavioral intentions. Research consistently shows that while many individuals perceive AI as innovative and beneficial, concerns related to autonomy, transparency, and social consequences undermine trust, particularly in high-stakes domains such as healthcare [21,22]. Demographic factors including age, education, income, ethnicity, and prior exposure to technology have been shown to influence trust in and acceptance of AI systems [23â€“26]. These findings highlight the importance of examining demographic determinants when studying attitudes toward AI and its applications in healthcare contexts. 
    21â†’Attitudes toward AI-driven mental health chatbots represent a more context-specific form of technology acceptance, situated at the intersection of AI perceptions and beliefs about mental health care. Literature indicates that while some individuals view mental health chatbots as accessible, stigma-free, and convenient sources of support, overall attitudes are often neutral to cautious, with concerns regarding effectiveness, emotional sensitivity, and safety remaining prominent [27â€“29]. Trust plays a particularly central role in this context, as users must be willing to disclose emotionally sensitive information and rely on non-human agents for psychological support. Acceptance of mental health chatbots is associated with both general trust in AI and perceptions of chatbot-specific risks and benefits [30â€“32]. Trust-in-AI models posit that such acceptance depends on the calibration between perceived trustworthiness and perceived risk, underscoring the need to examine both general AI attitudes and domain-specific beliefs.
    22â†’In this context, the present cross-sectional study aims to examine individualsâ€™ attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support, alongside their attitudes toward AI in general. Additionally, the study investigates the influence of demographic variables on these attitudes. This study seeks to contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the factors that shape public acceptance of AI-based mental health interventions and to inform their responsible and ethical implementation in real-world settings.
    23â†’Materials and Methods
    24â†’Study design
    25â†’We conducted a cross-sectional study in Greece. We collected our data through an online survey during January 2026. We created an online version of the study questionnaire with Google forms, and then we posted it on social media such as Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn and TikTok. Thus, we obtained a convenience sample. Participants were required to be adults, possess an active account on at least one social media platform and provide informed consent to complete the questionnaire. We applied the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) guidelines [33] in our study.
    26â†’The sample size was determined using G*Power version 3.1.9.2. We applied the following parameters in the calculation: (a) probability of type I error equals to 5%, (b) power equals to 95%, (c) number of independent variables equal to 6, and (d) low effect size (f2=0.03) between each demographic variable (independent variable) and attitudes towards AI (dependent variable). We calculated a sample size of 436 participants.
    27â†’Measurements 
    28â†’We used the Artificial Intelligence in Mental Health Scale (AIMHS) [34] to measure attitudes towards AI chatbots for mental health support. The AIMHS includes five items and two factors, i.e., the factor "technical advantages" and the factor "personal advantages". Answers are on a 5-point Likert scale from 1 (totally disagree) to 5 (totally agree). Adding the responses to the items in the factor and dividing by the total number of items yields the total score on the factor. Thus, score on each factor ranges from 1 to 5. Higher scores indicate more positive attitudes towards AI mental health chatbots. Two negative items require reverse scoring. We used the valid Greek version of the AIMHS [34]. In our study, Cronbachâ€™s alpha for the factors â€śtechnical advantagesâ€ť, and â€śpersonal advantagesâ€ť was 0.753 and 0.894, respectively.
    29â†’To assess participantsâ€™ overall attitudes toward artificial intelligence, we employed the Artificial Intelligence Attitude Scale (AIAS-4) [35]. This instrument comprises four items rated on a 10-point Likert scale, ranging from 1 (not at all) to 10 (completely agree). The total score is calculated as the mean of all item scores, yielding a range from 1 to 10, with higher values reflecting more positive attitudes toward AI. For this study, the validated Greek version of the AIAS-4 [36] was utilized, and the scale demonstrated excellent internal consistency, with a Cronbachâ€™s alpha of 0.934.
    30â†’We measured the following demographic variables: (a) sex, (b) age, (c) educational level, (d) financial status, (e) daily use of social media/websites (minutes), and (f) competence in digital technologies such as smartphones, personal computers, AI applications and tablets. We measured financial status as a self-perceived variable on a scale from 0 (very poor) to 10 (very good). Competence in digital technologies was measured as self-perceived variable on a scale from 0 (very poor) to 10 (very good).
    31â†’Ethical issues
    32â†’The study was conducted in compliance with the principles outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki [37]. Ethical approval was obtained from the Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Nursing at the National and Kapodistrian University of Athens (Protocol Approval #01, September 14, 2025). Data collection was carried out anonymously and on a voluntary basis. Participants were fully informed about the studyâ€™s objectives and design and provided written informed consent prior to participation.
    33â†’Statistical analysis
    34â†’We present categorical variables as numbers and percentages. Also, we use mean, standard deviation (SD), median, and interquartile range to present continuous variables. We used the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and Q-Q plots to examine the distribution of continuous variables. We found that continuous variables followed normal distribution. Demographic variables were the independent variables, while scores on AIMHS and AIAS-4 were the dependent variables. We performed simple and multivariable linear regression analysis since the dependent variables were continuous variables that follow the normal distribution. We performed multivariable linear regression analysis to control for confounding. We used variance inflation factors (VIFs) to assess multicollinearity in the multivariable models. A VIF greater than 5 indicates multicollinearity between independent variables. Additionally, we examined histograms of the residuals to check for multivariable normality. We examined scatterplots of residuals versus predicted values to check for homoscedasticity and linearity. We present unadjusted and adjusted coefficients beta, 95% confidence intervals (CI), and p-values. We used Pearsonâ€™s correlation coefficient to examine correlation between AIMHS and AIAS-4. P-values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant. We used the IBM SPSS 28.0 (IBM Corp. Released 2021. IBM SPSS Statistics for Windows, Version 28.0. Armonk, NY: IBM Corp) for the analysis.

    55â†’Discussion
    56â†’The present study examined individualsâ€™ attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support alongside their attitudes toward AI in general, while also exploring the influence of demographic variables on these perceptions. Since literature on this research field is limited especially about individualsâ€™ attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support, this study seeks to contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the factors that shape public acceptance of AI-based mental health interventions and to inform their implementation in real-world settings.
    57â†’First, the present study found that participants reported a moderate level of positive attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support, alongside a moderate degree of favorable attitudes toward artificial intelligence in general. This pattern suggests a stance of cautious openness toward AI technologies rather than unequivocal endorsement or rejection. Similar attitudinal profiles have been reported in prior studies, where public perceptions of AI, particularly in healthcare context, tend to cluster around neutrality to moderate positivity, reflecting both perceived benefits and persistent concerns [38â€“40]. From the perspective of the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), these moderate attitudes may indicate that perceived usefulness of AI technologies is present but not sufficiently strong to generate high levels of acceptance. AI systems, including mental health chatbots, are often perceived as offering convenience, accessibility, and efficiency, which contribute positively to attitudes. At the same time, uncertainties regarding accuracy, accountability, and appropriateness, especially in emotionally sensitive domains, may limit the extent to which these technologies are viewed as fully acceptable or desirable. This balance between enabling and constraining beliefs is consistent with TAM-based research showing that attitudes toward healthcare technologies often stabilize at moderate levels when perceived usefulness is tempered by perceived risk or ambiguity [8â€“10]. Trust-in-AI and trust-in-automation frameworks further illuminate these findings. Trust in intelligent systems is conceptualized as a dynamic and context-dependent psychological state influenced by perceptions of competence, predictability, and ethical reliability [20]. Moderate attitudes toward AI and AI-based mental health chatbots may therefore reflect partial or conditional trust, whereby individuals are willing to consider AI technologies but remain cautious about relying on them fully. Prior work in healthcare contexts indicates that lay populations often express tentative trust in AI systems, particularly when decisions involve personal vulnerability or potential harm, such as mental health disclosure and psychological support [21,22]. The parallel findings of moderate attitudes toward AI in general and toward AI mental health chatbots also suggest that general technological beliefs may function as an important antecedent of domain-specific acceptance. Individuals who hold ambivalent or moderately positive views of AI overall may also extend similar evaluations to specific AI applications, including those in healthcare and mental health. The consistency between general and context-specific attitudes supports the fact that foundational beliefs about AI shape how new and specialized implementations are evaluated. Importantly, moderate positivity should not be interpreted as resistance to AI adoption. Instead, it may reflect a calibrated and reflective stance, particularly appropriate given the ethical, emotional, and relational dimensions of mental health care. Previous studies have shown that individuals often view mental health chatbots as acceptable supplementary tools rather than replacements for human clinicians, valuing their accessibility while remaining cautious about their therapeutic depth [17,27]. From this perspective, the observed attitudes may represent a fertile ground for responsible implementation, where transparency, evidence of effectiveness, and ethical safeguards could strengthen trust and enhance acceptance over time.
    58â†’Additionally, we found that participants with higher levels of education reported significantly more positive attitudes toward both AI-based mental health chatbots and AI in general. This finding indicates that educational attainment is a key determinant of attitudes toward emerging technologies, particularly in healthcare contexts. From the perspective of the Technology Acceptance Model, higher educational levels may be associated with greater perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use of AI technologies. Individuals with more advanced education are more likely to possess higher levels of digital literacy, analytical skills, and familiarity with complex technological concepts, which may reduce perceived uncertainty and increase confidence when engaging with AI systems. Previous studies applying TAM in healthcare technology adoption have consistently shown that education positively predicts favorable attitudes toward digital health tools, including AI-driven applications [27,30]. Trust-in-AI frameworks provide further explanatory insight into this association. Trust in intelligent systems is shaped by individualsâ€™ ability to understand system functionality, limitations, and intended use. Higher educational attainment may facilitate more nuanced mental models of AI, enabling users to calibrate trust appropriately rather than responding with generalized fear or skepticism. Higher education has been associated with greater trust in healthcare AI, lower perceived risk, and greater acceptance of algorithm-assisted decision making [23,41]. Individuals with higher education levels are more likely to endorse the utility, acceptability, and ethical permissibility of such tools, often viewing them as complementary rather than threatening human-delivered care. In contrast, individuals with lower educational attainment tend to express greater concerns regarding data privacy, emotional authenticity, and potential misuse, which may hinder acceptance [27]. Importantly, education appears to influence not only general attitudes toward AI but also the translation of these attitudes into domain-specific evaluations. Research suggests that favorable general beliefs about AI are more readily extended to sensitive applications, such as mental health support, among individuals with higher educational backgrounds, reinforcing the role of general AI attitudes as antecedents of chatbot-specific acceptance [21]. These findings suggest that targeted educational and informational efforts may be particularly important for fostering informed acceptance among individuals with lower educational attainment, thereby promoting more equitable access to and engagement with emerging digital mental health technologies.
    59â†’An additional finding of the present study was that increased age was associated with more positive attitudes toward both AI-based mental health chatbots and AI in general. From a theoretical perspective, this finding can be interpreted through both the Technology Acceptance Model and trust-in-AI frameworks. While younger individuals are often assumed to be more technologically receptive, TAM emphasizes that attitudes are shaped primarily by perceived usefulness rather than age per se. Older individuals may perceive AI mental health chatbots as particularly useful due to increased health-related needs, greater familiarity with healthcare systems, and heightened awareness of barriers such as limited access, cost, or stigma associated with traditional mental health services. In this sense, age may indirectly enhance perceived usefulness, leading to more favorable attitudes. Trust-in-automation models may also help explain this association. Trust in AI is shaped by experience, goal alignment, and perceived system reliability. Older adults may evaluate AI tools more pragmatically, focusing on functional benefits rather than novelty, and may therefore exhibit greater acceptance when AI is perceived as supporting rather than replacing human care. Research in healthcare contexts suggests that older individuals may develop more stable and calibrated trust in automated systems when such systems are framed as assistive and clinically relevant [20,41]. For example, Asan et al. (2020) found that older adults expressed comparable or higher trust in medical AI systems when they perceived clear benefits for patient care [41]. Similarly, Sagona et al. emphasized that trust in healthcare AI may increase with age when systems are embedded within trusted institutional or clinical contexts [22]. Another explanation for our finding could be that younger individuals may adopt a more critical stance toward AI due to greater exposure to public discourse surrounding algorithmic bias, surveillance, and data misuse. In contrast, older adults may evaluate AI technologies more instrumentally, focusing on immediate practical benefits rather than abstract societal risks. The positive association between age and attitudes toward AI mental health chatbots underscores the importance of avoiding age-based assumptions in the design and deployment of AI-driven mental health interventions. Instead, these technologies should be developed and communicated in ways that emphasize clinical relevance, transparency, and supportive functionality across the lifespan.
    60â†’Our findings demonstrated that increased daily use of social media and websites, as well as higher self-reported competence in digital technologies, were associated with more positive attitudes toward AI in general. Frequent engagement with digital platforms can be conceptualized as a facilitating condition that enhances both perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness. Individuals who regularly interact with digital environments may be more comfortable navigating complex technological systems and more likely to perceive AI as an extension of existing digital tools rather than as a disruptive or opaque innovation. Greater technological exposure and digital literacy predict more favorable attitudes toward advanced technologies, including AI and algorithmic systems [30]. Moreover, familiarity with digital technologies may reduce uncertainty and perceived risk; two key inhibitors of trust in automated systems. Individuals with higher digital competence are more likely to understand, at least at a basic level, how AI systems function and what their limitations are, enabling more calibrated and less fear-based evaluations. Digital literacy and technological self-efficacy are positively associated with trust in AI, transparency perceptions, and acceptance of AI-assisted decision making [21,41]. Additionally, frequent use of social media and web platforms may also contribute to more positive AI attitudes through increased exposure to AI-mediated features, such as recommendation algorithms, content filtering, chatbots, and personalization systems. Regular interaction with such technologies may normalize AI as a functional and beneficial presence in everyday life, thereby strengthening positive attitudes. Moreover, digital competence appears to function as an independent resource that shapes how individuals interpret and evaluate AI technologies. Individuals with higher digital skills are better equipped to critically assess benefits and risks, reducing generalized anxiety or mistrust while maintaining informed caution [23]. On the other hand, limited digital literacy may exacerbate skepticism and perceived threat associated with AI, particularly among individuals who feel excluded from or overwhelmed by technological developments. 
    61â†’Limitations
    62â†’Several limitations of the present study should be acknowledged. First, the cross-sectional design precludes any causal interpretations of the observed associations. Although associations were identified between demographic factors and attitudes toward AI and AI-based mental health chatbots, the directionality of these associations cannot be determined. Longitudinal or experimental designs would be necessary to examine how attitudes toward AI evolve over time and whether changes in digital competence or exposure directly influence acceptance. Second, the study relied on self-reported data, which may be subject to response biases such as social desirability, recall bias, or overestimation of digital competence and technology use. Participants may have reported more favorable attitudes toward AI or greater technological proficiency than is reflected in actual behavior. Future research may benefit from incorporating objective indicators of technology use or mixed-methods approaches to triangulate findings. Third, attitudes were assessed rather than actual behavioral use of AI mental health chatbots. While attitudes are important predictors of adoption, they do not necessarily translate into engagement or sustained use in real-world contexts. Factors such as system quality, content accuracy, clinical oversight, and contextual availability may influence behavior beyond attitudinal acceptance. Future studies should examine usage patterns, adherence, and outcomes associated with AI mental health tools. Fourth, the scope of the study focused on general attitudes toward AI and AI mental health chatbots, without differentiating between specific chatbot types, functionalities, or levels of clinical integration. Attitudes may vary depending on whether chatbots are perceived as informational tools, self-help aids, or therapeutic agents, as well as the degree of human oversight involved. Greater specificity in describing AI systems may yield more nuanced insights into acceptance. Finally, although several demographic variables were examined, other potentially relevant factors, such as mental health history, prior experiences with digital mental health interventions, cultural beliefs, or trust in healthcare institutions, were not included. These variables may further shape attitudes toward AI in mental health contexts and warrant inclusion in future research to provide a more comprehensive model of AI acceptance.
    63â†’Conclusions
    64â†’This study examined attitudes toward AI chatbots for mental health support and artificial intelligence in general, while exploring the role of demographic factors in shaping these attitudes. Overall, the findings indicate that participants held moderately positive attitudes toward both AI mental health chatbots and AI more broadly, reflecting a stance of cautious openness rather than uncritical acceptance or resistance. Also, the study identified several factors associated with more favorable attitudes toward AI and AI-based mental health chatbots. Higher educational attainment, increased age, greater daily use of social media and websites, and higher competence in digital technologies were all linked to more positive evaluations.
    65â†’From a practical perspective, these findings underscore the importance of digital literacy, transparency, and education in promoting informed and equitable acceptance of AI-based mental health interventions. Moderate attitudes should not be viewed as barriers to implementation, but rather as indicators of a receptive yet discerning public, for whom clear communication about effectiveness, ethical safeguards, and appropriate use may strengthen trust and acceptance over time. Attention should be given to engaging individuals with lower digital competence or educational attainment, to prevent the emergence of new forms of digital inequality in mental health care.
    66â†’In conclusion, the present study contributes to the growing literature on AI acceptance by demonstrating that attitudes toward AI mental health chatbots are interrelated with general AI attitudes and shaped by demographic factors. By integrating insights from technology acceptance and trust frameworks, the findings offer valuable guidance for the responsible development, evaluation, and implementation of AI-based mental health technologies in ways that are both user-centered and socially inclusive.
