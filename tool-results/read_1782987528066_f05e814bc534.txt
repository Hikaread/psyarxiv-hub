    25ÔćĺAbstract
    26ÔćĺNeuroticism is one of the most consequential personality traits for individuals and society.
    27ÔćĺInterventions that facilitate changes in this trait may thus have far-reaching benefits, with initial
    28Ôćĺevidence supporting that interventions can foster lasting neuroticism change. Most personality
    29Ôćĺtheories posit that changes in broad, enduring traits are mediated via lower-level changes in
    30Ôćĺmomentary states and intermediary habits. However, there are few direct tests of this theorized
    31Ôćĺbottom-up pathway, leaving the mechanisms, direction, and timing of effects unclear. Using
    32Ôćĺdata from the Changing How I Live Life Study (N = 444), which evaluated a digital intervention
    33Ôćĺto reduce neuroticism, we examined 1) the shape and magnitude of intervention-induced
    34Ôćĺneuroticism change across three timescales, 2) concurrent and lagged associations among these
    35Ôćĺchanges, and 3) whether these associations varied across treatment groups receiving strategies
    36Ôćĺtargeting different neuroticism levels. We found that neuroticism changed nonlinearly across
    37Ôćĺthe intervention; changes in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism measures reliably
    38Ôćĺpredicted one another, with bottom-up effects often being larger in magnitude; and mean-level
    39Ôćĺchanges and coupled changes in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism measures were
    40Ôćĺlarger in the intervention groups than in the active control group. These results support the
    41Ôćĺconceptual and causal importance of both bottom-up and top-down (momentary, specific ÔčĚ
    42Ôćĺlong-term, broad) change processes. Together, this study tested key theoretical predictions about
    43Ôćĺthe process and directionality of intervention-induced personality change to help elucidate
    44Ôćĺmechanisms underlying intentional personality changes and generate insights about how to
    45Ôćĺenhance intervention efficacy.
    46ÔćĺKeywords: personality; volitional change; bottom-up process; timescale; neuroticism
    47ÔćĺPublic Significance Statement
    48ÔćĺA smartphone intervention successfully decreased momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism
    49Ôćĺmeasures in motivated members of the public, with groups receiving state-level interventions
    50Ôćĺshowing stronger effects. Intervention-induced changes are driven by both bottom-up and top-
    51Ôćĺdown processes (i.e., changes in momentary states lead to changes in more enduring traits, and
    52Ôćĺvice versa), highlighting many theoretical mechanisms that can be used to target neuroticism.

    55ÔćĺIntervention-Induced Neuroticism Change Across Multiple Timescales
    56ÔćĺPeople with high levels of neuroticism tend to be highly sensitive to threats, experience
    57Ôćĺfrequent negative affect, and strongly react to stress (Barlow et al., 2014). Neuroticism has been
    58Ôćĺlinked to many detrimental outcomes, including physical and mental health problems, adverse
    59Ôćĺlife events, and premature mortality (Jokela et al., 2013; Wright & Jackson, 2023). Many people
    60Ôćĺwould like to be less neurotic (Baranski et al., 2021), and interventions that can reduce
    61Ôćĺneuroticism stand to widely benefit public health and personal well-being (Bleidorn et al.,
    62Ôćĺ2019). Growing research suggests that interventions can successfully promote lasting changes
    63Ôćĺin personality traits like neuroticism (Stieger et al., 2021; Wright, Haehner, et al., 2025).
    64ÔćĺHowever, the timescale and mechanisms of these intervention-induced changes remain unclear.
    65ÔćĺPersonality can be assessed over multiple timescales and conceptualized across different
    66Ôćĺfunctional levels that range from momentary, situation-specific states; intermediate, contextual
    67Ôćĺhabits; to broad, enduring traits (Allemand & Fl├╝ckiger, 2017; Baumert et al., 2017). These
    68Ôćĺdifferent levels can reciprocally influence each other over time (i.e., states ÔčĚ habits ÔčĚ traits),
    69Ôćĺwith most theories positing that personality change is primarily driven by bottom-up processes
    70Ôćĺwhereby changes in momentary states form into novel, intermediate habits that generalize to
    71Ôćĺlonger-lasting, broad trait change (Geukes et al., 2018; Hudson, 2021; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).
    72ÔćĺAlthough theories converge on directional pathways across levels, there are few causal tests of
    73Ôćĺthe link between changes in states, habits, and traits (K├╝chler et al., 2025; Stieger et al., 2022).
    74ÔćĺIn this study, we investigated how intervention-induced neuroticism changes measured
    75Ôćĺover three timescales were related and whether their relations varied across groups receiving
    76Ôćĺintervention strategies targeting different levels of neuroticism. To do so, we used data from the
    77ÔćĺChanging How I Live Life (CHILL) Study (Haehner et al., 2025). The CHILL Study tested a
    78Ôćĺdigital neuroticism intervention in which participants were randomized to one of four treatment
    79Ôćĺgroups (TGs) and reported on their neuroticism during specific moments, at the end of each
    80Ôćĺ
    81ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 4
    82Ôćĺday, and at the end of each week before, during, and after the intervention. This allowed us to
    83Ôćĺtest predictions from contemporary personality theories about the timescales and mechanisms
    84Ôćĺof intervention-induced personality change and generate ways to optimize intervention efficacy.
    85ÔćĺVolitional Personality Trait Changes
    86ÔćĺAlthough once thought to be highly stable, robust evidence shows that personality traits
    87Ôćĺcan change across the lifespan (Bleidorn et al., 2022; Seifert et al., 2024; Wright, Kr├Ąmer, et al.,
    88Ôćĺ2025). Evidence of this malleability led to questions of whether people can intentionally change
    89Ôćĺtheir personality. These changes may help people achieve better career, relationship, and health
    90Ôćĺoutcomes as downstream effects (Bleidorn et al., 2019). Neuroticism in particular is highly
    91Ôćĺconsequential for individuals and society due to its far-reaching implications (Lahey, 2009). For
    92Ôćĺexample, Wright and Jackson (2023) found that higher levels of and increases in neuroticism
    93Ôćĺprospectively predicted worse health status, more health limitations, and job loss. NeuroticismÔÇÖs
    94Ôćĺpublic health costs even surpass those of mental disorders like depression and anxiety (Cuijpers
    95Ôćĺet al., 2010), underscoring the potential value of its volitional change efforts.
    96ÔćĺInitial research suggests that interventions can promote lasting changes in self-reported
    97Ôćĺpersonality traits (see Haehner et al., 2024, for a review). For example, the digital intervention
    98Ôćĺthat we examine in the present study has been found to help motivated members of the public
    99Ôćĺdecrease their neuroticism trait levels (|d| = 0.24 to 0.40; Wright, Haehner, et al., 2025). Similar
   100Ôćĺto other smartphone interventions (e.g., Stieger et al., 2021), effects emerged across self-reports
   101Ôćĺand some peer-reports, with intervention-induced changes exceeding normative changes and
   102Ôćĺdemand effects during a waiting period and persisting at a 2-month follow-up. We extend this
   103Ôćĺwork by including neuroticism measures at other timescales, testing the direction(s) over which
   104Ôćĺchanges unfold, and examining mechanisms of these cross-timescale intentional changes.
   105ÔćĺTheories on Personality Trait Changes
   106ÔćĺCommon across theories is a distinction between different levels of personality that vary
   107Ôćĺ

   109Ôćĺin breadth and stability (Allemand & Fl├╝ckiger, 2017; Wright et al., 2026). At the narrowest
   110Ôćĺlevel, personality states are momentary affects, behaviors, or cognitions that are assessed at fine
   111Ôćĺtemporal resolutions (e.g., hourly; Fleeson & Jayawickreme, 2015). At the intermediate level,
   112Ôćĺhabits are domain-specific tendencies that are assessed at moderate temporal resolutions (e.g.,
   113Ôćĺdaily) to capture repeated affects/behaviors/cognitions in various contexts (Gardner et al.,
   114Ôćĺ2012). At the broadest level, traits are dispositional patterns of affects, behaviors, and cognitions
   115Ôćĺthat are assessed over longer time periods (e.g., monthly) or in general. In this study, we use the
   116Ôćĺtimescales at which different measures were assessed and their instructed reference periods to
   117Ôćĺdistinguish between levels (e.g., ÔÇťright now,ÔÇŁ ÔÇťpast dayÔÇŁ, ÔÇťpast weekÔÇŁ).1
   118ÔćĺBuilding on this three-level model, theories have posited that personality change occurs
   119Ôćĺin a bottom-up or top-down manner. The more common theoretical premise is that personality
   120Ôćĺchange is best captured by a bottom-up process, whereby repeated changes in momentary
   121Ôćĺpersonality states lead to changes in trait-relevant habits that, over time, generalize to enduring
   122Ôćĺchanges in peopleÔÇÖs generalized trait self-concepts (Figure 1; Haehner et al., 2024; Hennecke et
   123Ôćĺal., 2014; Hudson, 2021). For example, someone wishing to become less neurotic must decrease
   124Ôćĺtheir momentary negative emotions or thoughts in specific situations, practice enacting those
   125Ôćĺchanges frequently, maintain changes long enough for them to become automatic in or linked
   126Ôćĺto certain contexts, and have these habitual changes generalize into more enduring trait change.
   127ÔćĺOther theoretical perspectives also posit a top-down pathway of personality change that
   128Ôćĺoriginates at the trait level, shifts trait-relevant habits, then alters momentary personality states
   129Ôćĺ(Allemand & Fl├╝ckiger, 2017; Jackson & Wright, 2024). From this perspective, someone may
   130Ôćĺstart perceiving themselves as less anxious, for example, due to new feedback or reference

   186ÔćĺThe Present Study
   187ÔćĺThis study aimed to advance insight into the timing, direction, and process of intentional
   188Ôćĺpersonality change. We used data from the CHILL Intervention that has been found to elicit
   189Ôćĺlasting changes in self- and partly peer-report trait neuroticism beyond normative changes,
   190Ôćĺregression-to-the-mean, and demand effects (Wright, Haehner, et al., 2025). Participants were
   191Ôćĺrandomized to four TGs that received different combinations of strategies targeting the trait,
   192Ôćĺhabit, and/or state levels of neuroticism (Wright et al., 2026). Participants completed
   193Ôćĺmomentary, daily, and weekly surveys before, during, and after the 6-week intervention.
   194ÔćĺIn line with theory (Hennecke et al., 2014; Jackson & Wright, 2024; Wrzus & Roberts,
   195Ôćĺ2017), we expected significant declines across measures, such that the intervention would elicit
   196Ôćĺmomentary-, daily-, and weekly-level decreases. At the between-person level, we expected
   197Ôćĺdeclines in shorter-timescale measures to be related to declines in longer-timescale measures.
   198ÔćĺConsistent with bottom-up views, we hypothesized that within-person changes in narrower,
   199Ôćĺshorter-term measures would predict within-person changes in broader, longer-term measures.
   200ÔćĺFinally, we expected TGs receiving strategies targeting state, habit, and/or trait neuroticism to
   201Ôćĺvary in amounts, patterns, and directions of cross-timescale change. Specifically, we expected
   202Ôćĺchanges in shorter-timescale measures and bottom-up effects from lower to higher levels to be
   203Ôćĺmore pronounced for TGs in which these lower levels were directly targeted (Table 1).

   753ÔćĺDiscussion
   754ÔćĺA core premise of contemporary personality change theories is that repeated changes in
   755Ôćĺmomentary personality states lead to more enduring changes in generalized trait self-concepts
   756Ôćĺthrough bottom-up processes (Geukes et al., 2018; Hudson, 2021; Jackson & Wright, 2024;
   757ÔćĺWrzus & Roberts, 2017). In this paper, we tested this hypothesis by examining intervention-
   758Ôćĺinduced neuroticism changes across three timescales and testing their relations over time.
   759ÔćĺWe found significant declines in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism measures
   760Ôćĺacross the intervention. Declines in weekly neuroticism occurred more rapidly than declines in
   761Ôćĺdaily and momentary measures, but the latter two had larger total declines that continued across
   762Ôćĺthe intervention. Within 4 weeks, momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism changes in the
   763Ôćĺintervention groups significantly exceeded changes in the active control group, supporting the
   764Ôćĺcausal utility of the intervention to elicit cross-timescale neuroticism changes. Changes in all
   765Ôćĺmeasures were also strongly related, indicating that people with greater declines in shorter-
   766Ôćĺ
   767ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 23
   768Ôćĺtimescale measures also had greater declines in longer-timescale measures. Then, in line with
   769Ôćĺtheory, significant bottom-up effects emerged across all timescales, with changes in shorter-
   770Ôćĺtimescale measures predicting changes in longer-timescale measures. Although corresponding
   771Ôćĺtop-down effects in the sample were also significant, indicating that changes in more enduring
   772Ôćĺneuroticism measures likewise predicted changes in more transient measures, top-down effects
   773Ôćĺtended to be smaller than bottom-up effects. Finally, there were some differences in the timing
   774Ôćĺand coupling of cross-timescale neuroticism changes associated with the different level-specific
   775Ôćĺstrategies assigned to TGs, providing some indications on how to enhance intervention efficacy.
   776ÔćĺOverall, our study offers insights into the timescales over which changes in neuroticism
   777Ôćĺunfold, directional mechanisms of intervention-induced change, and potential ways to optimize
   778Ôćĺintervention efficacy by targeting different levels of personality. We discuss these points below.
   779ÔćĺTiming of Changes in Neuroticism Across Different Timescales
   780ÔćĺWith 8 weeks of intensive longitudinal data with momentary, daily, and weekly surveys,
   781Ôćĺthis study provided a fine-grained test of intervention-driven personality change across multiple
   782Ôćĺtimescales. Neuroticism changed relatively quickly during the intervention, with 50% of all
   783Ôćĺdeclines occurring within the first 2 to 4 weeks of the intervention. This finding is in line with
   784Ôćĺrecent research showing that most intervention-driven personality trait change occurs early in
   785Ôćĺinterventions (Allemand et al., 2024; Roberts et al., 2017; Stieger et al., 2022), contradicting
   786Ôćĺformer views of personality as stable, fixed characteristics (Bleidorn, 2024).
   787ÔćĺFurther, we found that changes during the 6-week intervention plateaued around Week
   788Ôćĺ5. This corresponds with results by Stieger et al. (2022), who found that intervention-induced
   789Ôćĺchanges in personality states and traits plateaued after approximately 4 weeks. Generally, these
   790Ôćĺfindings suggest that insights from clinical dose-effect research may be applied to personality
   791Ôćĺtrait interventions, whereby rapid changes occur in the first few weeks of treatment and plateau
   792Ôćĺthereafter (Robinson et al., 2020). As such, we may now tentatively conclude that personality
   793Ôćĺ
   794ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 24
   795Ôćĺinterventions lasting a few weeks are sufficient to elicit lasting personality trait changes
   796Ôćĺ(Roberts et al., 2017; Stieger et al., 2021). However, future research on the long-term effects of
   797Ôćĺpersonality interventions, and the effects of possible booster sessions, is still needed.
   798ÔćĺOur study also offers novel insights into the shape of intervention-induced neuroticism
   799Ôćĺchanges across timescales. Interestingly, we found that total declines in weekly neuroticism
   800Ôćĺoccurred more rapidly than total declines in momentary states and intermediate, daily habits.
   801ÔćĺThis result may be surprising as most theories highlight the relevance of bottom-up processes,
   802Ôćĺwith changes in momentary states theorized to drive changes in broader personality traits
   803Ôćĺ(Hennecke et al., 2014; Hudson, 2021; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). One possible explanation is
   804Ôćĺthat rapid, early declines in the weekly measure reflect biases in self-perceptions of participants
   805Ôćĺwho expected their neuroticism levels to change, because they were participating in an
   806Ôćĺintervention (Kr├Ąmer et al., 2025). Alternatively, this pattern may highlight the relevance of
   807Ôćĺcognitive (reflective) processes in personality interventions: If people regularly think about their
   808Ôćĺtrait levels and changes, they may perceive changes in their general trait expressions prior to
   809Ôćĺany corresponding enacted changes. If these self-concept changes are salient in certain
   810Ôćĺsituations, this may influence how they think, feel, and behave in given moments, thereby
   811Ôćĺleading to corresponding changes in trait-relevant states and habits. To explicitly address this
   812Ôćĺpossibility, we examined how changes in timescale-specific neuroticism levels were related.
   813ÔćĺTop-Down and Bottom-Up Effects of Personality Trait Interventions
   814ÔćĺPersonality development theories posit that changes in different levels of personality are
   815Ôćĺrelated, with most theories outlining a bottom-up process whereby changes in momentary states
   816Ôćĺpredict changes in daily, trait-relevant habits that, subsequently, predict changes in broader traits
   817Ôćĺ(Hudson, 2021; Jackson & Wright, 2024; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). In line with these theories,
   818Ôćĺwe found significant bottom-up effects across all timescale-specific neuroticism measures. This
   819Ôćĺsuggests that intervention-induced changes in neuroticism states indeed broaden into novel
   820Ôćĺ
   821ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 25
   822Ôćĺneuroticism-related habits over time, with these intermediate habitual changes then generalizing
   823Ôćĺto changes in more enduring neuroticism trait levels. These results support the colloquial idea
   824Ôćĺof ÔÇťfake it till you make it,ÔÇŁ such that they highlight the efficacy of acting out of character in a
   825Ôćĺgiven moment to change oneÔÇÖs broader trait levels over time (Jackson & Wright, 2024).
   826ÔćĺAlthough our results support the presence of this bottom-up pathway, we also found the
   827Ôćĺreverse effect, with changes in broader, higher levels of neuroticism predicting future changes
   828Ôćĺin narrower, lower levels (i.e., top-down effects). In contrast to repetition and generalization
   829Ôćĺwhich are focal aspects for successful bottom-up change, our top-down effects rather point to
   830Ôćĺthe relevance of cognitive processes (e.g., associative, reflective) in personality interventions
   831Ôćĺ(Jackson & Wright, 2024; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). A top-down effect from a personality trait
   832Ôćĺto state could, for example, indicate that people must first perceive a change in their trait self-
   833Ôćĺconcepts. Perceiving these changes may encourage someone to enact such changes in their daily
   834Ôćĺlife or to select into different situations that then afford them greater opportunities to behave in
   835Ôćĺways that align with their perceived changes. These top-down effects thus imply that ÔÇťif you
   836Ôćĺbelieve it, you can achieve itÔÇŁ may be another mechanism to foster lasting personality changes.
   837ÔćĺBy testing top-down and bottom-up effects in the model, this study moved beyond
   838Ôćĺexisting research, as prior studies have typically focused on bottom-up effects as the dominating
   839Ôćĺmechanism (K├╝chler et al., 2025; Olaru et al., 2025). Overall, our findings support the causal
   840Ôćĺrelevance of both bottom-up and top-down processes for intervention-induced personality as
   841Ôćĺboth effects emerged over different timescales. One caveat to this conclusion, though, is that
   842Ôćĺbottom-up effects tended to be larger in magnitude than their corresponding top-down effects.
   843ÔćĺIn particular, bottom-up effects originating at the state level always exceeded corresponding
   844Ôćĺtop-down effects in our main analyses, and our sensitivity analyses indicated that all bottom-up
   845Ôćĺeffects significantly exceeded their associated top-down effects. Nonetheless, theories and
   846Ôćĺfuture research on volitional personality change should likely incorporate both directions of
   847Ôćĺ
   848ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 26
   849Ôćĺeffects and examine specific hypotheses on how these processes unfold over time (Jackson &
   850ÔćĺWright, 2024). For example, future studies could test whether and how specific cognitive
   851Ôćĺmechanisms, like reflective processes, drive top-down effects (Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).
   852ÔćĺTreatment Group Differences
   853ÔćĺA key feature of the present study was examining intervention-induced neuroticism
   854Ôćĺchanges across TGs that received distinct combinations of intervention strategies theorized to
   855Ôćĺtarget different levels of neuroticism. In contrast to the active control group that received
   856Ôćĺtheoretically inert trait-level strategies, we found that the three intervention groups that also
   857Ôćĺreceived habit and/or state strategies had stronger, longer-lasting changes in all timescale-
   858Ôćĺspecific measures. Specifically, mean-level trends in the joint intervention groups significantly
   859Ôćĺexceeded trends in the active control group across analyses and their rates of decline for
   860Ôćĺmomentary, daily, and weekly measures surpassed declines in the active control group within 2
   861Ôćĺto 4 weeks. Moreover, the joint intervention groups had larger bottom-up effects from
   862Ôćĺmomentary to daily changes, whereas the active control group had more prominent top-down
   863Ôćĺeffects in this model. Such findings support the causal relevance of the intervention to elicit
   864Ôćĺmean-level changes in neuroticism across timescales and to initiate the theorized bottom-up
   865Ôćĺprocess of personality change.
   866ÔćĺFurther, we found tentative evidence that targeting the state level of neuroticism is
   867Ôćĺparticularly critical for intervention efficacy. The State and Habit+State Groups, which both
   868Ôćĺreceived state strategies, evidenced stronger changes than the active control group in all
   869Ôćĺneuroticism measures. The Habit+State Group also had stronger changes across measures than
   870Ôćĺthe Habit Group, and the State Group similarly had stronger weekly changes than the Habit
   871ÔćĺGroup. This suggests that targeting habits, in addition to states, may be particularly effective
   872Ôćĺfor lasting intervention-driven personality change. Additionally, the State Group consistently
   873Ôćĺhad more pronounced bottom-up effects than other TGs, again underscoring the utility of
   874Ôćĺ
   875ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 27
   876Ôćĺtargeting states to initiate theorized bottom-up processes of personality trait changes. Together,
   877Ôćĺtargeting momentary statesÔÇôÔÇôthereby capitalizing on bottom-up effects originating from this
   878Ôćĺlowest level to influence intermediate habits and broader traitsÔÇôÔÇômay be particularly fruitful for
   879Ôćĺfuture interventions.
   880ÔćĺOverall, though, interventions that targeted specific levels yielded mixed effects. Results
   881Ôćĺsuggested that, regardless of which level is targeted, state-level change is a key component of
   882Ôćĺbottom-up pathways of change, with intermediate, habit-level changes being less efficacious
   883Ôćĺfor driving trait-level change. For example, the State and Habit+State Groups (both receiving
   884Ôćĺstate strategies) had larger bottom-up effects for momentary to daily neuroticism change than
   885Ôćĺthe active control group and Habit Group (neither receiving state strategies), and they similarly
   886Ôćĺhad stronger bottom-up effects from daily to weekly change than the Habit Group. One possible
   887Ôćĺreason for this finding is that intermediate habit changes are still mostly driven by narrow state
   888Ôćĺchanges. Habits are formed via repeated instances of novel states (Gardner et al., 2012), so
   889Ôćĺtrying to form habits without an equal emphasis on generating novel states may elicit less
   890Ôćĺchange. Thus, the State Group may have had larger effects of intermediate changes predicting
   891Ôćĺbroader changes than the Habit Group because these repeated novel states are necessary for
   892Ôćĺhabit formation and eventual broader trait change.
   893ÔćĺOther potential reasons for the mixed effects are that strategiesÔÇÖ targeted levels may not
   894Ôćĺalign as expected with the timescale-specific measures or that strategies may not be as specific
   895Ôćĺas expected regarding targeted levels. For example, a state strategy, like Physical Activity, could
   896Ôćĺelicit changes in both states (e.g., people go for a run) and habits (e.g., establish a new workout
   897Ôćĺroutine). Additionally, other TG differences (e.g., required cognitive resources, targeted aspects
   898Ôćĺof neuroticism) may obscure effects of targeting specific levels. For instance, strategies also
   899Ôćĺdiffer in whether they primarily target affective, behavioral, and cognitive aspects of
   900Ôćĺneuroticism (Wright et al., 2026), and initial research suggests that targeting affective aspects
   901Ôćĺ
   902ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 28
   903Ôćĺmay be particularly effective for changing neuroticism (Haehner et al., 2026). Future work
   904Ôćĺtesting effects of single strategies instead of combinations of strategies is needed to better
   905Ôćĺunderstand their implications and identify the most successful strategies (Collins et al., 2024).
   906ÔćĺLimitations
   907ÔćĺFirst, the CHILL Study used a highly educated sample of young adults who were mostly
   908Ôćĺwomen and primarily stemmed from three Western democratic countries. As such, it remains
   909Ôćĺan open question whether findings generalize to other cultures or demographic groups. Second,
   910Ôćĺself-report measures may be subject to biases like socially desirable responding or demand
   911Ôćĺeffects (Kr├Ąmer et al., 2025; Paulhus & Vazire, 2007). Although the CHILL Study included peer
   912Ôćĺand implicit measures, showing effects extend beyond self-reports for generalized trait
   913Ôćĺneuroticism, observed changes in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism could be biased by
   914Ôćĺsocially desirable responding and peopleÔÇÖs expectation to decrease in neuroticism. Third, to
   915Ôćĺapproximate theoretical models that distinguish states, habits, and traits, we used momentary,
   916Ôćĺdaily, and weekly measures. However, this is an imperfect conceptualization. Personality traits
   917Ôćĺare typically captured using measures without specific reference periods in instructions. Among
   918Ôćĺthe intensive longitudinal measures available in the CHILL Study, though, the weekly measure
   919Ôćĺdoes best capture neuroticism trait levels.
   920ÔćĺConclusion
   921ÔćĺInterventions that can change consequential personality traits like neuroticism stand to
   922Ôćĺwidely benefit public health and well-being. However, successfully changing personality traits
   923Ôćĺrequires a comprehensive understanding of the timescales, directions, and mechanisms of
   924Ôćĺintervention-driven change. Contributing to this understanding, we found that neuroticism
   925Ôćĺassessed across different timescales changed at nonconstant rates during an intervention, with
   926Ôćĺmost change occurring by Intervention-Week 4. Changes in momentary measures predicted
   927Ôćĺchanges in daily and weekly measures and vice versa, highlighting the relevance of both
   928Ôćĺ
   929ÔćĺTIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 29
   930Ôćĺbottom-up and top-down processes. Interventions targeting states tended to elicit larger total
   931Ôćĺneuroticism changes, with this possibly being even more true when targeting states and habits.
   932ÔćĺFuture interventions should target multiple levels of neuroticism, especially momentary and
   933Ôćĺintermediate levels, to elicit maximal change.
