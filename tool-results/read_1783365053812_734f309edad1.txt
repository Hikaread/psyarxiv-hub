    46‚Üí42 ABSTRACT
    47‚Üí43 Addiction has been widely viewed as an example of reinforcement learning gone awry, often
    48‚Üí44 attributed to impaired model-based (MB) control and a compensatory shift toward model-free
    49‚Üí45 (MF) strategies. However, such ‚ÄúMB-MF‚ÄĚ dichotomy is largely grounded in single-task
    50‚Üí46 settings and may not capture how learning operates in environments that require generalization
    51‚Üí47 across multiple tasks. Here, we examine learning in individuals with substance use disorders
    52‚Üí48 (SUD) using a two-step, multi-task paradigm in which participants navigate a tree-structured
    53‚Üí49 environment to achieve goals with varying reward contingencies. Our results show that, across
    54‚Üí50 groups, behavior is not well explained by canonical MB or MF accounts. Instead, participants
    55‚Üí51 acquire successor features that serve as reusable components for generalization across tasks.
    56‚Üí52 Leveraging information theory, we develop a resource-rational model that balances expected
    57‚Üí53 reward and cognitive cost in multi-task learning. This model successfully reproduced several
    58‚Üí54 characteristic behaviors in both groups. Critically, individuals with SUD show increased
    59‚Üí55 sensitivity to cognitive cost and preferentially adopt simplified policies and reuse them across
    60‚Üí56 tasks, at the expense of reward maximization. Individual differences in cost sensitivity and
    61‚Üí57 inferred cognitive effort predict addiction severity. These findings challenge the dominant
    62‚Üí58 MB‚ÄďMF dichotomy of addiction and instead point to a systematic shift in how cognitive
    63‚Üí59 resources are allocated during learning. More broadly, our findings highlight resource-rational
    64‚Üí60 generalization as a key principle for understanding atypical behavior in complex, multi-task
    65‚Üí61 environments.
    66‚Üí62
    67‚Üí63 Key words: addiction, substance use disorder, multi-task learning, model-based learning,
    68‚Üí64 model-free learning, successor feature, resource-rationality.

    73‚Üí67 INTRODUCTION
    74‚Üí68 Substance use disorder (SUD) is a growing global public health crisis, currently affecting an
    75‚Üí69 estimated 27 million individuals worldwide1,2. Chronic methamphetamine use, in particular,
    76‚Üí70 has been associated with severe cognitive impairments3, elevated rates of psychiatric
    77‚Üí71 comorbidities4,5, and neurotoxic damage to dopaminergic and frontostriatal brain systems6.
    78‚Üí72 Despite decades of research, relapse rates remain high7, and effective pharmacological
    79‚Üí73 treatments are still lacking8. These challenges underscore the urgent need to better understand
    80‚Üí74 the cognitive mechanisms that drive compulsive drug-seeking behavior.
    81‚Üí75 In recent years, reinforcement-learning-based theories of addiction have offered an
    82‚Üí76 influential perspective on how addiction develops and is maintained. Classical theories of
    83‚Üí77 reinforcement learning propose that addiction involves a shift from model-based (MB) to
    84‚Üí78 model-free (MF) learning systems9-11. In this framework, MB learning acquires an internal
    85‚Üí79 model of the environment by estimating the transition structure between states, thereby
    86‚Üí80 supporting flexible, goal-directed behavior such as planning. However, learning and
    87‚Üí81 maintaining such a model imposes substantial computational demands. By contrast, MF
    88‚Üí82 learning relies on cached action‚Äďreward associations without explicitly representing task
    89‚Üí83 structure. Although computationally efficient, MF learning is inherently inflexible: when task
    90‚Üí84 contingencies change, previously learned associations must be relearned, and generalization to
    91‚Üí85 unexperienced states is limited. Based on these considerations, prior work has proposed that
    92‚Üí86 addiction biases behavior toward MF control, potentially due to reduced computational
    93‚Üí87 resources. This view has received empirical support across various substances, including
    94‚Üí88 methamphetamine11, cocaine12, nicotine13, and alcohol14.
    95‚Üí89 However, prior studies have largely focused on learning within a single task, in which
    96‚Üí90 individuals optimize behavior under a fixed reward-action mapping. This focus limits the
    97‚Üí91 explanatory scope of the MB‚ÄďMF dichotomy in real-world settings, where behavior typically
    98‚Üí92 unfolds across multiple tasks under constrained cognitive resources. For example, a university
    99‚Üí93 professor must concurrently prepare lectures, advise students, respond to administrative
   100‚Üí94 demands, and revise grant proposals. In such multi-task environments, both MB and MF
   101‚Üí95 strategies can become computationally burdensome, albeit for different reasons. MB learning
   102‚Üí96 requires constructing and maintaining detailed models of each task‚Äôs structure to support
   103‚Üí97 flexible, goal-directed behavior, a demand that scales rapidly with task complexity. By contrast,
   104‚Üí98 MF learning is less demanding within a given task but lacks a representation of shared structure
   105‚Üí99 across tasks. As a result, it requires learning separate policies for each task, leading to
   106‚Üí100 redundant representations and limited generalization.
   107‚Üí4
   108‚Üí
   109‚Üí101 Recent advances in human and machine reinforcement learning suggest that behavior
   110‚Üí102 in multi-task settings may rely on an intermediate strategy known as successor feature (SF)
   111‚Üí103 learning15-17. SF learning lies between MB and MF approaches: it neither constructs a full
   112‚Üí104 model of state transitions nor relies solely on cached action‚Äďreward associations. Instead, it
   113‚Üí105 offers a compromise between computational cost and behavioral flexibility. The central idea
   114‚Üí106 of SF learning is to represent each state in terms of its successor features, which capture
   115‚Üí107 expected future feature occupancy and can be reused across tasks. These features provide a
   116‚Üí108 shared representational basis that supports efficient generalization to novel goals. Consistent
   117‚Üí109 with this account, SF learning explains characteristic patterns of human behavior in multi-task
   118‚Üí110 settings15 and has been increasingly adopted in machine learning as a framework for
   119‚Üí111 generalizable learning16. Despite its promise in learning efficiency, it remains unclear whether
   120‚Üí112 and how SF learning contributes to the atypical learning patterns observed in addiction such as
   121‚Üí113 SUD.
   122‚Üí114 Learning across multiple tasks typically incurs substantial cognitive effort. A large
   123‚Üí115 body of theoretical and empirical work in psychology and neuroscience suggests that behavior
   124‚Üí116 is shaped not only by expected reward but also by the cognitive effort required to obtain it.
   125‚Üí117 This perspective, often formalized as a resource-rational framework18,19, accounts for a range
   126‚Üí118 of seemingly suboptimal behaviors in perception20,21 and decision-making22,23, as well as
   127‚Üí119 abnormal behaviors in psychiatric disorders such as schizophrenia24. From this viewpoint, both
   128‚Üí120 the proposed shift from MB to MF learning and the use of SF learning can be understood as
   129‚Üí121 strategies that reduce cognitive demands during learning. Such tendencies may be especially
   130‚Üí122 pronounced in psychiatric disorders, including addiction. However, studies of addiction have
   131‚Üí123 typically inferred cognitive effort indirectly from behavioral performance rather than
   132‚Üí124 quantifying the computational costs of learning itself25,26. Moreover, the mechanisms by which
   133‚Üí125 sensitivity to cognitive effort shapes learning strategies remain poorly understood. A unified
   134‚Üí126 framework is therefore needed to link cognitive effort, learning strategies (MB, MF, and SF),
   135‚Üí127 and behavior in multi-task environments.
   136‚Üí128 To understand how these challenges manifest in addiction, we adopted a multi-task
   137‚Üí129 reinforcement learning paradigm to compare individuals with SUD to demographically
   138‚Üí130 matched healthy controls. We found that individuals with SUD tend to rely on simple, fixed
   139‚Üí131 policies across tasks, whereas HC flexibly adapted their policies to changing task demands.
   140‚Üí132 Computational modeling revealed that both groups preferred SF learning during multi-task
   141‚Üí133 learning scenarios. Critically, by explicitly modeling cognitive effort, we found that both
   142‚Üí134 groups indeed balanced rewards and cognitive effort during SF learning, but to a different
   143‚Üí5
   144‚Üí
   145‚Üí135 extent. The SUD group‚Äôs behavior was primarily driven by reduced cognitive effort, which
   146‚Üí136 was further predicted by the severity of addiction symptoms. These findings provide new
   147‚Üí137 insights into the long-standing MB-MF dichotomy of addiction. These results provide a
   148‚Üí138 normative account for how the trade-off between rewards and cognitive effort gives rise to
   149‚Üí139 atypical learning behaviors in addiction.

   355‚Üí226 The third finding is that both HC and SUD participants cannot effectively differentiate
   356‚Üí227 best and second-best room in Type A tasks. In an ideal scenario, participants were expected to
   357‚Üí228 reach the optimal room most frequently in both task types (Room 8 for Type A and Room 4

   363‚Üí234 types.
   364‚Üí235 The fourth finding is that individuals with substance use disorders (SUD) showed a

   488‚Üí311 Human multi-task learning balances reward and cognitive effort
   489‚Üí312 Based on the failure of all three models, we propose that the key missing factor is the limited
   490‚Üí313 cognitive resources of the human brain. Learning multiple tasks may require cognitive
   491‚Üí314 resources that exceed the brain‚Äôs capacity. According to the theory of resource-rationality18,19
   492‚Üí315 human learning and decision-making should not only maximize potential rewards but also take
   493‚Üí316 into account cognitive effort required to achieve those rewards. This computational goal
   494‚Üí317 fundamentally shapes the strategies a participant might adopt in multi-task scenarios.

   855‚Üí458 DISCUSSION
   856‚Üí459 The impact of addiction on reward processing has been a pivotal issue in psychology,
   857‚Üí460 psychiatry, and neuroscience. In addition to the classic MB and MF learning theories of
   858‚Üí461 addiction, we demonstrated that SF learning is a powerful mechanism that mediates the
   859‚Üí462 behavior of both SUD and healthy participants in multi-task reinforcement learning. We found
   860‚Üí463 that, during SF learning, all participants balanced the expected rewards and the cognitive effort
   861‚Üí464 required to obtain the rewards. The RRSF model is consistent with the tendency of SUD
   862‚Üí465 participants to use a single default policy to complete multiple tasks in order to save cognitive
   863‚Üí466 cost. According to our model, this inflexibility is associated with reduced task-dependent
   864‚Üí467 cognitive effort, which in turn relates to individual differences in psychiatric symptoms.
   865‚Üí468 Our findings provide evidence for a fundamental debate surrounding the nature of
   866‚Üí469 human learning in the context of the classical MB versus MF framework. Recent discourse has
   867‚Üí470 moved beyond the idea of simple arbitration between the two systems, with some proposing
   868‚Üí471 that human behavior may be explained exclusively by an MB or an MF system alone15. Our
   869‚Üí472 research offers a potential resolution to this dichotomy by suggesting that learning in a multi-
   870‚Üí473 task context may be best described by an SF mechanism. SF learning acts as an intermediate
   871‚Üí474 and unifying strategy; it captures the predictive, structural knowledge characteristic of MB
   872‚Üí475 learning while retaining the computational simplicity of MF systems by caching expectations
   873‚Üí476 about future state features. The results show that the SF learning models indeed outperformed
   874‚Üí477 other models. This suggests that instead of a competition between two distinct modules, the
   875‚Üí478 brain may employ a flexible SF architecture that inherently blends the advantages of both MB
   876‚Üí479 and MF learning.
   877‚Üí480 SF learning is significant in neuroscience and machine learning. SF learning is thought
   878‚Üí481 to be a key mechanism by which the human brain balances MB and MF approaches15. Previous
   879‚Üí482 studies have shown that humans learn successor features to navigate complex tasks15.
   880‚Üí483 However, the SF formulation in our study differs slightly from these earlier definitions:
   881‚Üí484 previous studies proposed learning separate sets of features for each task16,17, but our approach
   882‚Üí485 assumes learning a single, shared set of successor features across four training tasks. This
   883‚Üí486 simplified approach provided a better fit to human behavioral data (Supplemental Fig. S6).
   884‚Üí487 Future research should continue to investigate whether humans consistently use shared
   885‚Üí488 representations across various tasks or maintain task-specific ones, providing further evidence
   886‚Üí489 to test this hypothesis in the context of substance use disorder (SUD).
   887‚Üí490 Reduced cognitive effort may underlie the cognitive control deficits observed in
   888‚Üí491 individuals with SUD. Such deficits, characterized by difficulties in regulating thoughts and
   889‚Üí20
   890‚Üí
   891‚Üí492 actions, are a hallmark of addiction and contribute to impaired decision-making40,41. Although
   892‚Üí493 they have often been linked to some fundamental constraints on cognitive capacity, such as
   893‚Üí494 working memory capacity42, direct evidence for this association has been limited. Our findings
   894‚Üí495 provide evidence that addiction may impair behavioral flexibility by reducing cognitive effort.
   895‚Üí496 We also extend this conclusion by showing that these deficits in SUD are not limited to classic
   896‚Üí497 decision-making but also impact reward processing, highlighting how altered cognitive control
   897‚Üí498 interacts with the learning and evaluation of rewards to shape behavior.
   898‚Üí499 Quantifying cognitive effort at the individual level holds unique significance for
   899‚Üí500 applying information theory to psychiatric disorders. We emphasize that the cognitive effort
   900‚Üí501 defined in our study differs from the cognitive capacity as defined in classical information
   901‚Üí502 theory. According to information theory31,32, cognitive capacity represents the total cognitive
   902‚Üí503 resources available for processing, storing, and manipulating information during task
   903‚Üí504 performance, analogous to the size of a gas tank. Cognitive effort reflects the number of
   904‚Üí505 resources utilized, akin to the gas consumed. Our findings reveal atypical patterns of cognitive
   905‚Üí506 effort in individuals with SUD, but we cannot concretely conclude reduced cognitive capacity.
   906‚Üí507 Instead, reduced cognitive effort may stem from factors such as lower motivation to exert effort,
   907‚Üí508 challenges in learning accurate action values, or atypical attentional processes31. Our study
   908‚Üí509 opens a new avenue for understanding maladaptive decision-making in SUD and informs
   909‚Üí510 future computational psychiatry research.
   910‚Üí511 Our paradigm offers a robust framework for future computational psychiatry studies.
   911‚Üí512 In the prior study15, a single generalization trial (ūĚźį = [1, 1, 1]) was included to evaluate
   912‚ÜíūĚĎĒ
   913‚Üí513 participants‚Äô capacity to apply learned knowledge to an untrained task. However, their
   914‚Üí514 emphasis on generalization required a large sample size (300 - 500 participants), which is often
   915‚Üí515 infeasible in clinical populations like those with SUD. Instead, our study develops a process-
   916‚Üí516 oriented cognitive model to capture trial-by-trial learning dynamics in multi-task settings.
   917‚Üí517 Despite these distinct focuses, both studies converge on a shared insight: humans rely on
   918‚Üí518 successor features to represent environmental structures and navigate task variation. This
   919‚Üí519 finding underscores the critical role of successor features in enabling flexible decision-making
   920‚Üí520 across diverse tasks, providing a foundation for advancing computational models of
   921‚Üí521 maladaptive behaviors in SUD.
   922‚Üí522 The neural basis of our findings remains an important question for future work.
   923‚Üí523 Although the present study did not include neural measures, our model suggests several testable
   924‚Üí524 predictions. First, in multi-task scenarios, people need to tailor their policy to the current task.
   925‚Üí21
   926‚Üí
   927‚Üí525 This suggests that the brain should represent task or goal information, a function often linked
   928‚Üí526 to orbitofrontal cortex and ventromedial prefrontal cortex 43,44. Second, the model suggests that
   929‚Üí527 humans maintain reusable successor features. The neural basis of successor features remains
   930‚Üí528 less directly established, but related work on successor representations and predictive maps
   931‚Üí529 suggests that such representations may depend on hippocampal and prefrontal circuits45,46.
   932‚Üí530 Third, cognitive effort, defined here as the deviation from a default policy to a task-specific
   933‚Üí531 policy, may recruit cognitive control systems, including dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC)
   934‚Üí532 and lateral prefrontal cortex (lateral PFC) regions47. Our model suggests that individuals with
   935‚Üí533 SUD rely more strongly on the default policy. This may be reflected in reduced dACC and
   936‚Üí534 lateral PFC control signals for task-dependent policy adjustment.

   987‚Üí581 Experimental paradigm
   988‚Üí582 On each trial, participants were first presented with the prices of three resources. They then
   989‚Üí583 pressed the spacebar to enter the castle. Upon entry, three doors appeared, and participants were
   990‚Üí584 required to select one using the left, up, or right arrow keys. This choice led to a second room,
   991‚Üí585 where two new doors were presented. Participants then chose one using the left or right arrow
   992‚Üí586 key to proceed to the final room. In the final room, participants were shown the quantities of
   993‚Üí587 wood, stone, and iron they received during the trial. The system automatically sold these
   994‚Üí588 resources based on the displayed prices and calculated the total reward for the day as the sum
   995‚Üí589 of each resource‚Äôs quantity multiplied by its respective price. To aid memory, the daily prices
   996‚Üí24
   997‚Üí
   998‚Üí590 remained visible in the top-left corner of the screen throughout each trial. The task consisted
   999‚Üí591 of 80 training trials and one testing trial, representing 81 days of exploration. The testing trial
  1000‚Üí592 was excluded from further analysis due to insufficient data.
  1001‚Üí593 According to the instructions, participants took on the role of a ‚Äúmerchant‚ÄĚ exploring a
  1002‚Üí594 castle to collect resources. Three types of resources were available: wood, stone, and iron. In
  1003‚Üí595 each trial, participants made two sequential choices (i.e., opened two doors) to obtain a certain
  1004‚Üí596 quantity of these resources. The resources could be sold for profit based on fluctuating daily
  1005‚Üí597 prices; depending on the day‚Äôs prices, participants could gain, lose, or break even in earnings.
  1006‚Üí598 The objective was to maximize total profit across trials.
  1007‚Üí599 The experiment featured five resource price combinations, one of which ([1, 1, 1]) was
  1008‚Üí600 used exclusively for the testing trial and was not included in the analysis. The remaining four
  1009‚Üí601 price sets ‚ÄĒ [1, -1, 0], [-1, 1, 0], [1, -2, 0], and [-2, 1, 0]‚ÄĒwere used in the 80 training trials,
  1010‚Üí602 with each price combination appearing in 20 trials in a randomized order. The task involved a
  1011‚Üí603 total of nine doors: the first room always contained three fixed doors, and the second room
  1012‚Üí604 contained six fixed doors. The quantities of resources behind each of the second-stage doors
  1013‚Üí605 were predefined and remained constant throughout the experiment (see Fig. 1B). The multi-
  1014‚Üí606 task reinforcement learning task was implemented using jsPsych 6.3.0
  1015‚Üí607 (https://www.jspsych.org/6.3/) and was accessible through a web browser.
  1016‚Üí608 Before the formal experiment, all participants received task instructions and completed
  1017‚Üí609 a comprehension test to ensure they understood the task and procedures. Participants who
  1018‚Üí610 demonstrated 100% accuracy in the comprehension test were permitted to proceed; those who
  1019‚Üí611 did not meet this criterion were provided with additional explanations by the experimenter until
  1020‚Üí612 full understanding was demonstrated. Upon passing the comprehension test, participants
  1021‚Üí613 undertook a total of 81 trials, including 80 formal trials and one testing trial. Upon completion
  1022‚Üí614 of the task, participants were presented with their total earnings and requested to recollect and
  1023‚Üí615 disclose the quantity of resources located behind each second-stage door (see task details in
  1024‚Üí616 following sections).
