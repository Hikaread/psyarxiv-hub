   289→Civilian mild TBI
   290→This section summarizes 108 papers. Results for all patient populations are summarized in Figure 3 (within
   291→network connectivity) and Figure 2 (between network connectivity).
   292→Default mode network and parietal memory network
   293→Given the spatial and historic overlap between the DMN and PMN,18 we report these results together, as
   294→none of the papers used the terminology "parietal memory network." The PMN, defined in 2015, includes portions of
   295→the precuneus and PCC.
   296→In the acute phase, studies generally report hypoconnectivity among DMN nodes.53–58 Two studies report
   297→hyperconnectivity, but one retrospectively limited results to patients who achieved clinical recovery by six months
   298→post-injury59 and the other used a very short TR in a pediatric sample.60 One study reports a mixed pattern, with
   299→localized hyperconnectivity alongside a decoupling of DMN hubs.61 A data-driven sparse-coding analysis of acute
   300→and subacute mTBI similarly identifies altered DMN composition relative to controls, with reduced PCC and
   301→parietal contributions alongside increased medial prefrontal contribution.62 When acute patients are stratified by
   302→serum α-synuclein, those with higher levels show increased anterior DMN FC in the anterior cingulate and
   303→ventromedial prefrontal cortex alongside decreased precuneus FC, an anterior-posterior dissociation tracking a
   304→biomarker rather than a group contrast.63 Group moderated the association between SN-DMN FC and
   305→psychological resilience in children, with the direction of the association differing between concussion and
   306→orthopedic injury groups64 Madhavan et al. 2019 propose a compensatory role for early hyperconnectivity,
   307→reporting that acute hyperconnectivity between the DMN and other networks predicts lower symptom severity at
   308→three months.65 Subacute results are more blended. One study reports an anterior-posterior pattern with reduced
   309→coherence between DMN hubs,66 while others report DMN-internetwork hyperconnectivity,67–69 within-DMN
   310→hypoconnectivity,67,70,71 or DMN-internetwork hypoconnectivity in pediatric cohorts.69 Females show a mix of hyper-
   311→and hypoconnectivity largely absent in males.72 Several studies report null findings.64,73–75 Methodological factors
   312→contribute substantially: the same group using seed-based analysis identifies decreased within-DMN FC alongside
   313→DMN-lateral-prefrontal hyperconnectivity,67 then reports no reliable abnormalities in a larger ICA-based
   314→follow-up.73 Stringent whole-brain correction can render subtle subacute effects nonsignificant,74 and studies
   315→using orthopedic-injury controls report more circumscribed or null effects,64,69 likely because OI controls adjust for
   316→non-specific consequences of acute injury that uninjured controls do not.
   317→Postacute results vary with recruitment. Bharath et al. 2015 recruited only asymptomatic patients and
   318→report a shift from acute hyperconnectivity to postacute hypoconnectivity.59 Van der Horn et al. 2017 report that
   319→higher anterior–posterior DMN FC at 1 month post-injury predicts greater complaint burden at 3 months in patients
   320→with persisting symptoms.76 This may be age-dependent, as a pediatric study from Van der Horn et al. 2025 finds
   321→DMN hyperconnectivity despite 80% of the sample achieving clinical recovery.60 At the other end of the lifespan in
   322→
   323→a geriatric, microbleed-positive cohort, within-DMN FC is reduced in a pattern equivalent to Alzheimer-related
   324→degradation, with acute cognitive scores predicting the extent of chronic deviation.77 Mayer et al. 2011 did not
   325→constrain their sample and report sustained within-DMN hypoconnectivity alongside DMN-internetwork
   326→hyperconnectivity,67 also reported by Bharath et al.59 Studies in children suggest DMN inter-network FC can be
   327→compensatory or maladaptive depending on the regions involved.78–80 Multiple studies report null results.57,75,81,82
   328→In the chronic phase, recruitment remains important: samples from clinical/rehabilitation facilities81,83–88
   329→may differ from those recruited from naturalistic settings such as EDs at the time of injury and followed
   330→longitudinally.51,54,89–102 Some patterns persist across both, with multiple studies reporting within-DMN
   331→hypoconnectivity,83,88,89,93–95,98,103 in some cases specifically in patients with a history of multiple mTBIs98 or
   332→persisting symptoms.83 Several studies report intra-DMN hyperconnectivity91,100 or mixed results,90 potentially
   333→influenced by age at injury,91 small sample size and mixed severity,100 or CBF-scaled processing that may more
   334→accurately reflect neural synchronization.90 Disruptions to the triple network model are reported,87,97,104 and one
   335→study reports more diffuse DMN internetwork hyperconnectivity.84 Several studies report null results.54,60,81,96
   336→Longitudinal studies report partial DMN normalization,54,60,97,105 while patients with persisting symptoms show
   337→sustained and evolving alterations.57,97
   338→Frontoparietal network
   339→In the acute phase, hypoconnectivity within the FPN and between the FPN and other networks is the most
   340→common finding in adults,54,61,106 with within-network hypoconnectivity negatively correlated with post-concussive
   341→symptom severity,54 FPN-DMN hypoconnectivity associated with attention and language deficits,61 and
   342→FPN-caudate hypoconnectivity resolving by the subacute phase, with parallel improvement in headache impact
   343→and processing speed.106 Acute hyperconnectivity is less consistently reported and tends to be tied to specific
   344→clinical presentations or demographic factors, including post-traumatic headache107 and male sex.108 Several
   345→studies report null results at the network level.59,109,110 In a pediatric sample, acute FPN-VN hyperconnectivity is
   346→positively correlated with psychological resilience, suggesting a potential protective mechanism in youth.64
   347→Subacutely, findings diverge depending on sample characteristics and analytic approach. Reduced FC in
   348→frontal FPN nodes is reported in a large adult study stratified by radiological findings,70 and reduced
   349→interhemispheric dlPFC FC at one month is present specifically in patients with persisting symptoms, correlating
   350→with poorer cognitive performance,58 while within-FPN and FPN-DMN hyperconnectivity is reported in studies with
   351→more homogeneous samples or classification-based analyses.67,111 In an ED-recruited cohort spanning the
   352→subacute-postacute phases, mTBI shows FPN hyperconnectivity, interpreted as compensatory recruitment.112 One
   353→comprehensive study using multiple analytic methods (ROI-based, ICA, and fALFF) reports no significant FPN
   354→differences.74 In pediatric samples, within-FPN and FPN-SN hyperconnectivity alongside FPN-DMN
   355→hypoconnectivity is observed at four weeks post-injury,69 while null results are reported at two weeks when
   356→comparing typical and delayed recovery trajectories.113
   357→Postacutely, both hypo- and hyperconnectivity are reported in adults, often in relation to symptom status.
   358→FPN-thalamic hypoconnectivity is observed at six weeks but partially normalizes by four months,114 and FPN-SN
   359→hypoconnectivity is present only in patients with persisting symptoms, correlating with anxiety and depression
   360→severity.115 Conversely, FPN-DMN and FPN-SN hyperconnectivity is reported in patients with memory complaints,
   361→negatively correlated with objective short-term memory performance,68 and within-FPN hyperconnectivity
   362→emerges at three months in a longitudinal study that reports no alterations at 36 hours.59 Two studies report null
   363→results at approximately one month post-injury,75,115 an earlier timepoint than the positive findings above.68,75 In
   364→pediatric mTBI, postacute FPN alterations are driven by clinical and demographic factors: FPN-SN
   365→hyperconnectivity is observed in adolescents with persistent post-traumatic headaches at four months,
   366→hypothesized to reflect a top-down attempt to suppress pain,116 and sex-specific effects are reported at one
   367→month, with females demonstrating mixed FPN FC alterations largely absent in males.72
   368→Chronically, within-FPN and FPN-DMN hypoconnectivity is the most consistent adult finding,85,89,93,95,103
   369→
   370→although within-FPN and FPN-SMN hyperconnectivity is reported in symptomatic females and correlates
   371→positively with cognitive performance, suggesting a compensatory response.87 One longitudinal study reports
   372→widespread FPN hyperconnectivity at six months following earlier null results and localized hyperconnectivity at
   373→three months.59 Mixed findings are common and vary by seed location,83 sex,81 or analytic approach, including
   374→normalization by cerebral blood flow.90 A mixed-chronicity pediatric sample from the Adolescent Brain Cognitive
   375→Development study (ABCD) reports FPN-amygdala hypoconnectivity in adolescents with a history of mTBI.99
   376→Several factors contribute to this heterogeneity. Symptom status drives directional differences within similar
   377→cohorts, with hypoconnectivity reported predominantly in patients with persisting symptoms and
   378→hyperconnectivity emerging in symptomatic subgroups stratified by sex or in longitudinal trajectories tracking
   379→compensatory upregulation. Studies normalizing rsfMRI by cerebral blood flow report different FC patterns than
   380→those using standard preprocessing, suggesting that some FPN alterations may reflect vascular rather than neural
   381→changes. Recruitment source adds further variability, with chronic patients drawn from clinical or rehabilitation
   382→settings overrepresenting those with persistent difficulties relative to ED-recruited longitudinal cohorts.
   383→Longitudinal studies generally suggest that acute and subacute disruptions transition toward normalization or
   384→compensatory hyperconnectivity in later phases.54,59,64,70,96,106,114
   385→Salience network and cinguloopercular network
   386→Given the overlap between the SN and CON in regions, functions, and how they have been reported in the
   387→literature, we discuss them together here. The majority of papers refer to the SN.
   388→In the acute phase, there is no consistent change in FC following mTBI. Five studies report
   389→hyperconnectivity in core SN regions including the anterior insula, dACC, and supramarginal gyrus (SMG), often
   390→interpreted as a compensatory response to maintain cognitive performance and attentional control.53,59,109,110 Three
   391→studies report hypoconnectivity in core SN regions,61,106,117 one reports null results,104 and three report
   392→mixed-direction findings.107,118,119 For example, increased SN-FPN FC has been observed alongside decreased
   393→SN-sensorimotor FC.118 Three factors contribute to this heterogeneity. First, timing within the acute window
   394→matters: studies reporting hyperconnectivity often capture ultra-acute timepoints (within 24-72 hours),53,59,64 while
   395→studies with a wider 7-14 day window more often report mixed or hypoconnectivity findings.61,118,119 Second, the
   396→influence of spatial resolution: subdividing the insula into ventral anterior, dorsal anterior, and posterior regions
   397→reveals simultaneously increased FC from posterior insula and decreased FC from anterior subdivisions,107 a
   398→dissociation averaged out when the SN is treated as a single network. Finally, cohort phenotype shapes findings as
   399→well, with studies stratifying by post-traumatic headache118 or cognitive impairment110 reporting patterns specific
   400→to those phenotypes rather than general acute-mTBI signal.
   401→The subacute phase shifts toward predominantly reduced FC in core SN hubs,70,89,106 which is associated
   402→with poorer self-reported emotional functioning,70,106 Other studies report no detectable subacute changes,64,74
   403→potentially reflecting normalization, while one reports increased within-network FC.69 Postacute SN findings are
   404→heterogeneous, with reduced FC,115 increased FC,59,120 and null findings121 all reported. This variability reflects three
   405→interacting factors: stringent FDR or FWE corrections produce more null or localized findings while uncorrected
   406→thresholds capture more widespread effects; cited studies span approximately one to three months post-injury,
   407→capturing different recovery stages; and sub-regional analyses identify localized alterations in SN hubs that
   408→whole-network analyses obscure, returning null findings while seed- or hub-level analyses identify reduced FC tied
   409→to symptom status or injury complication. In the chronic phase, both hypo- and hyperconnectivity are reported,
   410→with time since injury as a moderator. Hypoconnectivity occurs primarily in higher-order SN regions such as the
   411→insula89 and dorsal anterior cingulate,88 and has been interpreted as reflecting persistent structural damage.
   412→Hyperconnectivity has been associated with emotional dysregulation and increased cognitive effort,83,84,122
   413→consistent with the compensatory interpretation offered for acute findings. Studies with longer average times
   414→since injury tend to report null results,85,95,104 raising the possibility that SN alterations gradually normalize over
   415→extended recovery periods.

   417→Dorsal and ventral attention networks
   418→The DAN and VAN have received moderate attention in the civilian mTBI literature, with the most notable
   419→findings relating to between-network interactions and the moderating role of comorbidities.
   420→In the acute phase, several studies report no significant intra- or inter-network changes involving the
   421→broader attention network (AN), DAN, or VAN after post-hoc corrections,61,74,76 although one study reports
   422→increased FC between the AN and the visual network.61 Subacute findings further reflect this variability: localized
   423→hypoconnectivity is reported within the parietal node of the DAN and between the DAN and basal ganglia, as well as
   424→between the VAN and auditory networks.70 Another subacute investigation observes reduced DMN-VAN and
   425→visual-VAN FC alongside auditory-VAN hyperconnectivity,110 illustrating that FC changes can occur in opposing
   426→directions across different network interactions simultaneously.
   427→Postacutely, DAN hyperconnectivity is observed, with evidence that it may persist chronically.59 A mixed
   428→severity pediatric sample reports DAN-SMN hyperconnectivity, correlated with better motor control.80 In blended
   429→(postacute/chronic) or strictly chronic samples the DAN and VAN show a range of intra- and inter-network
   430→alterations.90 Whole-brain DAN hypoconnectivity89 has been reported, alongside isolated DAN-cingulate
   431→hyperconnectivity.83 One study similarly shows broad DAN hypoconnectivity with hyperconnectivity between ANs
   432→and VIS/AUD networks.103 In a chronic clinic-recruited cohort, hyperconnectivity in superior parietal and angular
   433→regions of the DAN is reported, with SMG hyperconnectivity correlating with poorer verbal fluency, interpreted as
   434→ineffective compensatory recruitment.88 Multiple chronic-phase studies report null results.85,96 Cohort selection
   435→strategies appear to drive much of this divergence: studies excluding patients with persistent post-concussion
   436→symptoms report normalization of earlier alterations, while studies specifically recruiting symptomatic patients or
   437→stratifying by symptom status identify sustained FC changes that distinguish recovery trajectories.
   438→Methodological choices contribute additional variability. One study reports that normalizing rsfMRI by cerebral
   439→blood flow eliminates several apparent DAN FC differences, suggesting that some BOLD-based findings may
   440→reflect vascular rather than neural alterations.90 Within a single symptomatic cohort, ICA and seed-to-voxel
   441→analyses can yield conflicting results, illustrating that DAN FC findings are highly sensitive to the chosen analytic
   442→approach.
   443→Somatomotor lateral and somatomotor dorsal networks
   444→Most papers do not separate the SML and SMD, discussing them jointly as the somatomotor network (SMN).
   445→In the acute phase, findings are divergent and largely track with methodological choice. ICA-based analyses of
   446→within-network coherence identify generalized hypoconnectivity including in pre- and post-central gyri,54 analyses
   447→of between-network interactions identify disrupted SMN integration with other networks rather than
   448→within-network changes,61 and seed-based analyses anchored on specific hubs identify localized mixes of hypo-
   449→and hyperconnectivity.59,107 Patterns may be sex-specific, with acute hyperconnectivity in motor-related networks
   450→identified in males.108
   451→Subacute and postacute findings shift toward normalization, with frequent null results in subacute
   452→samples70,75,121 particularly after rigorous whole-brain corrections,74 and mostly decreased connections with
   453→localized precentral hyperconnectivity in asymptomatic postacute patients with uncomplicated mTBI.59 Broader
   454→longitudinal and mixed-severity cohorts diverge from this normalization pattern, with persisting decreases in SMN
   455→interhemispheric FC123 and broader reductions in both intra- and extranetwork FC,120 and one ED-recruited cohort
   456→reports thalamo-somatosensory hyperconnectivity in symptomatic patients at one month.58
   457→Chronically, SMN findings remain heterogeneous, reflecting differences in injury severity, structural
   458→integrity, and methodological choices around vascular correction. In pure mTBI samples, network-level measures
   459→often normalize by 6-12 months post-injury,59,96 though reduced interhemispheric motor FC may persist, likely due
   460→to corpus callosum damage.123 Results are sensitive to processing steps, with cerebral blood flow scaling
   461→suggesting some disruptions may reflect vascular rather than neural changes.90 Mixed-severity samples show
   462→
   463→more persistent disruptions, including enduring inter-network alterations between the SMN, DMN, and FPN,85 and
   464→comorbid depression may further modulate these patterns, potentially driving long-term hyperconnectivity.122 A
   465→chronic clinic-recruited cohort similarly reports precentral and supplementary motor hyperconnectivity.88
   466→Longitudinal studies show within-subject SMN changes over the first six months post-injury: one reports
   467→increased precentral gyrus FC alongside symptom improvement,54 while another reports a multiphasic pattern
   468→with early decreases in the first three months followed by increases toward normal levels.59
   469→Auditory and visual networks
   470→In the acute phase, hypoconnectivity within the AUD54 and VIS61,108 is common, along with hypoconnectivity
   471→between the VIS110 and other brain regions, although these alterations may be sex-108 and/or
   472→symptom-dependent.118 Localized hyperconnectivity is also reported, though these findings come from studies
   473→that either focused on patients without persisting symptoms59 or used a white matter functional-approach rather
   474→than a conventional gray-matter resting state network analysis.96 One study reports that acute VIS-FPN
   475→hyperconnectivity and VIS-motor hypoconnectivity predicts persisting symptoms,65 suggesting that VIS FC
   476→disruption, rather than direction alone, may have prognostic value. Subacutely, hyperconnectivity within the VIS is
   477→noted,70 alongside null results.74 In the postacute phase, two studies report hypoconnectivity within the VIS,120,121
   478→one reports hyperconnectivity between the VIS and cerebellum,124 and one reports null results.75 Chronically,
   479→hyperconnectivity is noted between the VIS and FPN83 and mixed changes are noted within the VIS85 in patients
   480→without chronic symptoms. Reduced FC between central white matter regions and extrastriate visual cortex
   481→(MT+/LO) is reported in symptomatic patients,125 and one study reports normalization of earlier
   482→hyperconnectivity.96
   483→Parietooccipital and medial temporal lobe networks
   484→We did not identify any studies reporting on the PON. In the MTL, acute studies show hyperconnectivity
   485→between the hippocampus and posterior brain regions53,126 and hypoconnectivity with frontal brain regions,53 with
   486→hippocampal hyperconnectivity associated with later development of chronic pain.126 Postacutely, ICA-based
   487→analyses demonstrate that the hippocampus and parahippocampus exhibit abnormal coupling across several
   488→distinct functional networks, including frontostriatal and primary visual/anterior cingulate networks.120 Greater
   489→postconcussive symptom burden in this phase is associated with enhanced FC (hyperconnectivity) in these MTL
   490→structures.120 These findings suggest that while acute MTL disruption can manifest as either hyper- or
   491→hypoconnectivity depending on the target region, postacute symptom burden is more consistently associated with
   492→hyperconnectivity. In the chronic phase, by contrast, clinic-recruited cohorts report MTL hypoconnectivity:
   493→temporal pole, anterior hippocampus, and amygdala FC is reduced relative to controls, with preserved
   494→temporal-pole FC correlating with better verbal fluency,88 and global hippocampal FC is reduced bilaterally, with
   495→slower hippocampal perfusion correlating with higher depression and anxiety scores, though these neurovascular
   496→coupling associations are exploratory and do not survive correction.127
   497→Graph theory
   498→Graph theory studies in civilian mTBI suggest a temporal pattern in which increased network segregation
   499→characterizes the acute phase, followed by relative normalization subacutely, with decreased integration emerging
   500→chronically. Definitions of the specific graph metrics discussed may be found in Supplementary Note 1.
   501→In the acute phase, increased global segregation, reflected in higher clustering coefficient, is the most
   502→consistent finding, reported in four studies,119,128–130 with two others reporting preserved segregation.109,131 Findings
   503→for global integration are less consistent: three studies report reduced efficiency or longer path lengths,,109,128,129
   504→while three others report preserved integration,119,131,132 and one reports higher integration.130 Despite these
   505→individual metric changes, small-worldness, which captures the balance between segregation and integration, is
   506→generally preserved,119,128–131 suggesting that the network retains its small-world architecture even as absolute
   507→
   508→clustering, efficiency, and path length values shift. Centrality measures (degree, betweenness centrality, and rich
   509→club organization) show the most consistent acute-phase alterations,119,128–130,133 with only two studies reporting
   510→preserved centrality.131,132 Nodal-level changes in efficiency and segregation are less reliably observed.132
   511→Subacutely, global integration and segregation are generally preserved,97,134–136with one exception showing
   512→decreased integration and increased segregation.137 Nodal-level metrics similarly show few alterations97,136 except
   513→among participants with posttraumatic complaints136 and one report showing both lower nodal integration and
   514→segregation.135 Centrality measures remain altered in nearly all subacute studies,97,134,135,137 suggesting that while
   515→global segregation normalizes relatively quickly, centrality remains altered. Longitudinal studies spanning the
   516→postacute phase yield mixed results, with one reporting decreased global efficiency, nodal efficiency, nodal
   517→clustering and centrality,132 while another reports higher efficiency and reduced small-world topology
   518→approximately three months post-injury, along with improvements in local clustering or centrality.131 Chronically,
   519→one study reports lower global integration,93 but null results for integration and segregation are also reported.97 In a
   520→mixed-severity pediatric cohort scanned in the chronic phase, graph analysis finds lower modularity across injury
   521→severities and higher smallworldness in complicated mild TBI, with lower modularity correlating with poorer
   522→working memory.138
   523→Taken together, these studies suggest that network topology changes may be characterized by increased
   524→segregation acutely, relative normalization subacutely, and decreased integration chronically, with the transition
   525→between these states occurring gradually during the postacute phase. Centrality alterations appear to be the most
   526→persistent graph theory finding across phases.
   527→Other
   528→This section covers findings from studies using seeds, regions, or FC approaches that do not map onto the
   529→network-specific sections above. Most of these findings come from one or two papers each and should be
   530→interpreted accordingly.
   531→Several studies examined subcortical seeds in the acute phase. Two studies focused on the
   532→periaqueductal gray (PAG) report links between altered PAG FC and chronic pain outcomes, including loss of
   533→typical negative FC between the PAG and primary somatosensory cortex, and between the nucleus accumbens
   534→(NAc) and the motor cortex, predicts later chronic pain,139 and reduced PAG-DMN FC in patients with acute
   535→post-traumatic headache predicts persistent headache at three months.140 One study examining the NAc
   536→specifically reports null results.141
   537→Thalamic FC appears to shift over the acute window, with widespread hyperconnectivity with sensorimotor
   538→regions and the DMN reported within the first 10 days post-injury.142 At approximately three weeks, one cohort
   539→reports thalamocortical hyperconnectivity to frontal, temporal, and cingulate regions, accompanied by reduced
   540→inter-hemispheric thalamic symmetry,143 and a large cohort scanned at approximately two weeks similarly reports
   541→thalamocortical hyperconnectivity localized to the ventral anterior and ventral lateral dorsal nuclei, with no regions
   542→of hypoconnectivity144 while another reports primarily hypoconnectivity within the thalamus and between the
   543→thalamus and fronto-temporal regions at a similar timepoint,145 indicating that direction in this window is
   544→cohort-dependent. In the same cohort as the latter, widespread disruptions in hypothalamic FC are also observed,
   545→with the loss of both positive and negative FC with other brain regions.146 By six weeks, thalamic-DAN FC remains
   546→hypoconnected but normalizes by four months,114 suggesting continued evolution of thalamic FC patterns into the
   547→subacute phase. In a chronically symptomatic fatigue cohort (median five years post-injury), thalamic FC is
   548→reduced and correlates with self-reported fatigue severity.147 Focusing on the substantia nigra in the acute phase,
   549→one study reports a mix of hyper- and hypoconnectivity with other brain regions, with hyperconnectivity to the
   550→precuneus negatively correlated with cognitive performance.148 A separate substantia nigra investigation in a
   551→mixed-severity adult sample reports reduced left substantia nigra FC to the superior medial frontal gyrus and
   552→angular gyrus, with substantia nigra-angular gyrus FC correlating positively with anxiety and negatively with
   553→depressive symptoms.149 Hyperconnectivity between deep white matter networks and primary somatomotor
   554→
   555→regions is also reported acutely.96
   556→Beyond subcortical FC, several studies examine specific clinical presentations. Homotopic FC in
   557→pain-related ROIs was weaker at one month post-injury but recovered by five months, paralleling partial
   558→improvement of pain symptoms.150 Patients with postconcussion vestibular dysfunction show increased FC
   559→between a vestibular-sensorimotor network and the prefrontal cortex,151 and a graph analysis in the same condition
   560→reports reduced centrality of vestibular and affective hubs alongside increased centrality of visual and motor
   561→regions, with FC predicting postural control.152 In chronic mTBI patients with comorbid depressive symptoms,
   562→hyperconnectivity is observed between the amygdala and the DAN, SN, SMN, and VN.122
   563→Civilian mild TBI summary
   564→The civilian mTBI literature is the largest body of rsfMRI research reviewed here, and several patterns
   565→emerge when findings are organized by network and recovery phase. Acutely, within-network hypoconnectivity is
   566→the predominant finding, most consistently in the DMN53–56,61 but also in the FPN,54,61,106 AUD,54 VIS,61,108 and SMN.54,61
   567→The SN is more evenly divided between hyper- and hypoconnectivity,53,59,61,106,109,110 with sub-regional analyses of the
   568→insula identifying clinically meaningful associations not apparent at the whole-network level.65,107,117
   569→Between-network alterations emerge early and carry prognostic significance: acute DMN-motor
   570→hyperconnectivity predicts lower symptom severity at three months, while VIS-FPN hyperconnectivity paired with
   571→VIS-motor hypoconnectivity predicts persisting symptoms, and DMN-hippocampal hyperconnectivity is
   572→associated with the development of chronic pain.126 Higher anterior-posterior DMN FC at one month also predicts
   573→greater complaint burden at three months in patients with persisting symptoms.76
   574→The subacute phase is transitional. DMN findings shift toward dissociations within or between anterior and
   575→posterior components,66,91,120 and acute hypoconnectivity within the emotional processing network normalizes to
   576→healthy control levels.106 Other SN connections trend toward hypoconnectivity associated with poorer emotional
   577→functioning,115,153 and graph theory metrics show normalization of global segregation while centrality measures
   578→remain altered.97,134,135,137 DAN and VAN findings show localized hypoconnectivity within parietal nodes alongside
   579→opposing-direction between-network changes that can occur simultaneously.70,110
   580→From the postacute phase onward, findings are most consistently influenced by symptom status.
   581→Asymptomatic patients tend toward normalization,59 while those with persisting symptoms show sustained or
   582→evolving alterations,57,76,97 most consistently within-DMN hypoconnectivity and between-network DMN
