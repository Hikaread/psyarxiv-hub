   401→we rescaled the composite past-year ideation scores of both cohorts to create an equivalent
   402→scale where all participants scored between 0 (absence of suicidal ideation) and 1 (highly
   403→frequent/intense suicidal ideation; see Supplementary Materials, 2, for further information).
   404→Past-fortnight suicidal ideation. In Part 2 of analyses, we used a time-sensitive
   405→indicator of recent suicidal ideation: item 9 of the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-960),
   406→which assesses suicidal ideation over the past fortnight (from absent [0] to present nearly
   407→every day [3]).
   408→Past-year and lifetime suicide attempts. Both studies operationalised lifetime
   409→suicide attempts via two SITBI questions. The first asks about lifetime suicide attempts,
   410→yielding a variable ranging from no [0], one [1], two [2], three or four [3], or five or more [4]
   411→attempts. A follow-up question asking about the recency of the latest suicide attempt, allowed
   412→us to create a binary index reflecting the presence (1) or absence (0) of past-year suicide
   413→attempts.
   414→Psychopathological and psychosocial correlates of STB
   415→Depressive symptoms. As a correlate of STB and NSSI in autistic and non-autistic
   416→people,61,62 we measured depressive symptoms in both cohorts using the aforementioned
   417→PHQ-960. Validated in both autistic and non-autistic people,60,63 the PHQ-9 is scored between
   418→0-27, with scores >8 indicative of depression.64 Our Part 1 analyses utilised the PHQ-9 total
   419→score (with excellent internal consistency in autistic and non-autistic participants [α=.90, .91,
   420→respectively]).
   421→Perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness. Two cognitive-affective
   422→states important to suicidal feelings in both autistic and non-autistic people are perceived
   423→burdensomeness (perceiving oneself as worthless and a liability to others) and thwarted
   424→belongingness (perceiving oneself as alone, unaccepted and/or estranged from others).55,65
   425→Having rescaled group scores to reflect operationalisation of these constructs as per the
   426→19
   427→
   428→Interpersonal Needs Questionnaire-10 (INQ-1066) (see Supplementary Materials 2), scores
   429→ranged from 10 to 100, with higher scores indicative of higher perceived burdensomeness and
   430→thwarted belongingness at the present time. Internal consistency was good for perceived
   431→burdensomeness (α=.93 and .95 for autistic and non-autistic participants) and thwarted
   432→belongingness (α=.81 and .88 for autistic and non-autistic participants).
   433→Analysis
   434→The two parts of our analyses were performed using R v4.4.1 (R Core Team, 2021)
   435→and are summarised as follows. In both, we performed Monte-Carlo simulations, confirming
   436→adequate power for each group; these, along with methodological details, are in
   437→Supplementary Materials 3.
   438→Part 1: Network analysis of NSSI behaviours, STB and related constructs
   439→Network analysis is a means of representing associations (edges) between variables
   440→(nodes), controlling for the effects of other, potentially interacting variables in multivariate
   441→datasets. Associations are thus estimated as regularised partial correlations. In our first
   442→analyses, we included only participants with a history of NSSI (n=327 autistic, n=260 non-
   443→autistic); we had previously used participant NSSI-AT responses to exclude participants from
   444→Studies 1 (N=1) and 2 (N=2) who engaged in self-injury only or principally as a means of
   445→practising or attempting suicide. We modelled 21 variables: 15 binary variables reflecting the
   446→presence/absence of specific NSSI behaviours, 3 measures of STB (past-year suicidal
   447→ideation, lifetime and past-year suicide attempts), and 3 STB-related constructs (depressive
   448→symptoms, perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness). We also modelled sex
   449→assigned at birth (1=female, 2=male) and age as covariates, given NSSI-related variance by
   450→sex67,68 and the significant age differences between autistic and non-autistic participants. Due
   451→20
   452→
   453→to slight missingness (with one autistic and one non-autistic participant not completing the
   454→PHQ-9), we constructed the regularised network with pairwise deletion.
   455→We constructed separate networks for each group, using a mixed graphical model via
   456→the mgm v1.2-14 package69 to model our 21 binary and continuous variables with optimal
   457→specificity and sensitivity. We applied multidimensional scaling to the resulting network
   458→matrices, such that distance between nodes reflected the strength of their association to aid
   459→visual interpretability.70
   460→To further analyse the networks, we calculated the following measures:
   461→Modularity. Modules in a network are groups or communities comprised of closely
   462→inter-connected nodes: behaviours and phenomena which co-occur or correlate strongly with
   463→each other, and less so with nodes elsewhere in the network. Operationalised in Q
   464→(modularity) scores between -1 and 1, positive scores indicate the presence of communities of
   465→densely connected nodes within the network.
   466→Centrality. Centrality measures different aspects of how a node is positioned in the
   467→network. Strength centrality reflects how strongly a node is connected to all other nodes;
   468→closeness centrality reflects how near a node is to all others; and between centrality reflects
   469→how often a node acts as a bridge, being the shortest path between nodes. Higher centrality
   470→scores indicate behaviours that frequently co-occur and are related to many other behaviours.
   471→Accuracy and stability. After constructing the networks, we evaluated the reliability
   472→and stability of the edges and centrality indices, using the bootnet package v1.671 to estimate
   473→the sample distribution with 1000 bootstrapped samples.
   474→Comparing networks of autistic and non-autistic people. We compared the two
   475→networks using the NetworkComparisonTest v2.2.2 package.72 We performed 1000
   476→permutations to assess differences in edge weights and overall connectivity strength.
   477→21
   478→
   479→Part 2: Associations between NSSI behaviours (T1) and STB (T2)
   480→Our second analysis involved autistic repeaters (n=133), with and without experience
   481→of NSSI, who had T1 and T2 data from Studies 1 and 2. T1 variables included 15 binary
   482→variables reflecting specific NSSI behaviours; recent perceptions of burdensomeness and
   483→thwarted belongingness; suicidal ideation over the past fortnight (PHQ-9 item 9), as well as
   484→our past-year suicidal ideation composite; lifetime and past-year suicide attempts; and age
   485→and sex as covariates. T2 variables included perceptions of burdensomeness and
   486→belongingness, suicidal ideation over the past fortnight and the past year; and past-year
   487→suicide attempts.
   488→As a preliminary step, we computed zero-order correlations between each NSSI
   489→behaviour and T1 and T2 suicidality outcomes, adjusting p-values using the Benjamini–
   490→Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) setting α=.05. We further aimed to examine whether
   491→NSSI behaviours predicted suicidality-related outcomes in autistic people beyond T1
   492→suicidality and demographic covariates. While our power analyses estimated 80% power to
   493→detect effects (r=.24) of NSSI behaviours on T2 STB, suicidality at T1 would be expected to
   494→account for much of the outcome variance,73 with NSSI behaviours competing to explain only
   495→the residual variance. With 133 participants and 18 predictors, many of which would be
   496→expectedly interrelated, the ratio of sample size to predictors was below the recommended
   497→threshold for stable estimation in standard regression models.74,75 We therefore
   498→employed Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) regression, a
   499→regularised regression that produces more stable and parsimonious models with improved
   500→generalisability to unseen data. We used the glmnet v4.1-8 package76 to predict, from T1
   501→NSSI behaviours, T2 outcomes including past fortnight and past-year suicidal ideation,
   502→perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness (past-year suicide attempts had
   503→insufficient variance to be modelled). At the same time, we controlled for sex assigned at
   504→22
   505→
   506→birth, age (T1), and the corresponding T1 STB variable to that T2 outcome. Having
   507→standardised all variables, we used ten-fold cross-validation to select optimal penalty
   508→parameters for each model, ensuring clinically important covariates from T1 (such as T1
   509→suicidality) were preserved. We report both in-sample R² and cross-validated R², the latter
   510→providing an estimate of out-of-sample predictive accuracy and model generalisability.77
   511→Finally, we refitted the model using rlassoEffects function from the hdm v0.3.2 package78 to
   512→obtain corrected p-values and confidence intervals.79

   665→As autistic people engage in NSSI in varied ways6 and for different reasons,7,8,17,19,48
   666→there may be clinical utility in identifying behaviours with particular association to STB.
   667→Leveraging data from two studies, we explored cross-sectional relationships between NSSI
   668→behaviours, STB and related psychopathological and psychosocial constructs in autistic and
   669→non-autistic people (Part 1). Moreover, using data from autistic participants who completed
   670→both studies, we examined predictive relationships between specific NSSI behaviours
   671→6 Most repeaters completed Study 2 just over 1 year (1.1 years) after completing Study 1 (range: .9 to 1.7 years).
   672→Therefore, consistency between T1 and T2 perceived burdensomeness and past-fortnight suicidal ideation
   673→(PHQ-9 item 9), assessment periods for which were the two weeks prior to participation in both studies, reflects
   674→consistency across just over a year for most participants. As the past-year suicidal ideation variable involved
   675→participants reflecting on the year prior to participation at both points when it was collected, it shows
   676→consistency over a longer period.

   679→measured at T1 and STB-related outcomes approximately one year later (Part 2). Our
   680→findings suggest that, as hypothesised on the basis qualitative literature,8,19,48 there are
   681→constellations of NSSI behaviours which often co-occur in autistic people, unidentical to the
   682→behaviours which co-occur in non-autistic people. Our findings equally support the
   683→interpretation that certain behaviours, including cutting, may be more closely related to STB.
   684→NSSI behaviours co-occur differently in autistic and non-autistic people
   685→Our first analyses revealed differences in network connectivity between non-autistic
   686→and autistic participants, alongside descriptive differences in their organisation. Global
   687→strength was significantly lower in autistic participants, which might reflect less consistent or
   688→weaker relationships among NSSI behaviours and between NSSI and STB-related constructs,
   689→as perhaps might be expected with greater heterogeneity across participants. Although overall
   690→network structure did not differ significantly between groups, community detection identified
   691→different patterns of co-occurring behaviours in autistic and non-autistic groups.
   692→Among autistic people, three distinct constellations of NSSI behaviour comprised 1)
   693→self-scratching, self-biting, hitting oneself and objects; 2) self-burning, skin tearing and
   694→wound interference; and 3) cutting, skin-carving, and ingesting dangerous substances. The
   695→first group was negatively linked to suicide attempts through self-scratching, and indirectly
   696→connected to suicidal ideation via its association with behaviours in a second group. Self-
   697→burning linked the second group to the third via its association with cutting; this third group
   698→was strongly interconnected and most proximal to suicide attempts. This tripartite structure in
   699→autistic participants diverged from the single NSSI community observed in non-autistic
   700→participants, which comprised skin-tearing, self-biting, hitting objects, wound interference (a
   701→linking node to the community including suicide attempts and that including suicidal
   702→ideation), and self-hitting (a linking node to depression in the suicidal ideation community).
   703→33
   704→
   705→Cutting and ingesting harmful substances/sharps emerged as NSSI behaviours closely
   706→related to suicide attempts in autistic and non-autistic people alike. We had hypothesised
   707→particularly strong associations between cutting and STB on the basis of existing
   708→literature,3,8,19,26,34,42-45,49,52 and ingesting substances has also been linked to STB in non-
   709→autistic people.47 Both are behaviours which, allowing habituation, can be straightforwardly
   710→extended to lethal effect.37,40,42 Interestingly, in autistic people, these behaviours linked the
   711→third NSSI group to that containing suicide attempts, whereas for non-autistic people, these
   712→behaviours were included within the group containing suicide attempts. Amongst non-autistic
   713→people, then, cutting and ingesting harmful substances/sharps are forms of NSSI closely
   714→linked to suicidal behaviour, while NSSI behaviours in the other cluster, more likely to co-
   715→occur with one another, are less frequently or strongly linked to STB. In contrast, for autistic
   716→people, clusters of NSSI behaviours more likely to co-occur are more distinct from STB and
   717→sometimes related to it through other groups of NSSI behaviour, suggesting more varied
   718→presentations.
   719→The findings confirm that NSSI behaviours co-occur in meaningful ways, and with
   720→differential relevance to STB in autistic vs. non-autistic people, as suggested by qualitative
   721→work.8,19,48 Therein, autistic people distinguish between meltdown-related NSSI
   722→behaviours8,19,48 (such as self-hitting, head-banging, or more clearly unintentional
   723→dysregulated behaviours); NSSI behaviours felt to address sensory needs8,48 (such as wound
   724→interference, slapping or skin picking); and “considered”19, “intentional”48 or “allistic”8 (i.e.
   725→non-autistic) NSSI behaviours linked to life stressors, emotional distress, mental illness and
   726→negative self-image (such as cutting). To some extent, the data may support an apparent
   727→distinction between NSSI and repetitive, unhidden and apparently unpremeditated ”SIB” in
   728→people with intellectual disabilities.11 Where some scholars distinguish between the two on
   729→basis of intent (SIB agnostic to intent, NSSI intended to cause physical harm without trying to
   730→34
   731→
   732→die)80, our data shows that some self-injurious behaviours are, indeed, statistically more distal
   733→and only indirectly associated with STB or its psychopathological correlates. However, the
   734→data also indicates relationships between groups of behaviours, suggesting that while certain
   735→groups of behaviours may have particular psychopathological and clinical correlates, they do
   736→co-occur, corroborating early findings that NSSI, and perhaps specific NSSI behaviours, may
   737→fulfil different functional needs over time.7,17
   738→In broader relationships between STB and psychopathological and psychosocial
   739→constructs, we also observed points of convergence and divergence across autistic and non-
   740→autistic networks. Suicidal ideation and behaviour were grouped separately in both,
   741→corroborating the established independence of factors associated with each outcome.81
   742→Among autistic people, perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness were
   743→proximal to depression and to suicidal ideation in a manner consistent with the interpersonal
   744→theory of suicide,65,82 which conceptualised both as the driving factors for suicidal ideation.65
   745→Greater closeness of perceived burdensomeness to suicidal ideation, however, is consistent
   746→with a previous network analysis in autistic people,55 where thwarted belongingness was only
   747→distally connected with suicidal ideation via its relationship to burdensomeness and low
   748→mood. In non-autistic people, too, perceived burdensomeness is a consistently stronger
   749→predictor of suicidal ideation than thwarted belongingness83. Research in clinical non-autistic
   750→samples has found that, in contravention of the original theory, perceived burdensomeness
   751→may also predict suicidal behaviour84,85; while ours was a predominantly student sample, the
   752→grouping of perceived burdensomeness with suicide attempts corroborates this. Both autistic
   753→and non-autistic networks support the relevance of depression, thwarted belongingness and
   754→perceived burdensomeness to models of STB, highlighting their relevance to risk assessment
   755→in both populations.
   756→35
   757→
   758→Prediction over time in autistic people
   759→Cross-sectional networks cannot establish whether specific NSSI behaviours
   760→prospectively heighten risk of suicidal thoughts and behaviour. We examined the predictive
   761→utility of NSSI behaviours over time, in our small sample of autistic “repeaters”, both via
   762→zero-order correlations and LASSO regression. Zero-order correlations corroborated, as per
   763→Part 1 analyses, some interrelatedness amongst NSSI behaviours, and differential strength of
   764→relationships between NSSI behaviours and STB-related constructs. Behaviours associated
   765→with recent and past-year suicidal ideation were cutting, self-burning, self-hitting, skin-
   766→tearing, breaking bones, and self-biting – the latter remained significantly associated with
   767→recent and past-year suicidal ideation in LASSO regression. As per our first analyses, cutting
   768→and self-hitting were associated with past-year suicide attempts; both were also significantly
   769→associated with perceived burdensomeness, whereas skin-tearing was associated with
   770→thwarted belongingness (though none of these three relationships remained significant in
   771→LASSO regression after controlling for T1 STB).
   772→The zero-order correlations and regressions of our second analyses afforded closer
   773→examination of short- and longer-term associations between NSSI, STB and related
   774→psychosocial constructs. Given the dearth of longitudinal research on STB in autistic people,
   775→the stability of suicidal thoughts and perceived burdensomeness over approximately 1-2 years
   776→is notable. These strong relationships are likely to partially or wholly explain why some of
   777→the associations between specific NSSI behaviours and STB-related constructs in our first
   778→analyses, such as relationships between cutting, ingesting harmful substances/sharps and
   779→suicide attempts, were not evident in prediction of STB-related constructs in our second
   780→analyses.
   781→Finally, we note associations between fighting to get hurt and perceived
   782→burdensomeness at T2. This behaviour from the NSSI-AT fails to meet certain prerequisites
   783→36
   784→
   785→for NSSI80: chiefly, the physical injury is indirect and incurred by proxy. Fighting has, in the
   786→general population, been variably described as a form of NSSI-by-proxy86, or as ‘indirect
