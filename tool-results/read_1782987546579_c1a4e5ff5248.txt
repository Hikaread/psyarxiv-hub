    23‚Üí    23‚ÜíAbstract
    24‚Üí    24‚ÜíBackground
    25‚Üí    25‚ÜíDespite the importance of social factors in the initiation and escalation of alcohol use,
    26‚Üí    26‚Üísocial processes related to alcohol use severity are typically studied in isolation. Because real-
    27‚Üí    27‚Üíworld social processes occur concurrently and may interact with one another as well as with
    28‚Üí    28‚Üíage and sex, a network approach may provide a more ecologically valid framework. Hence, we
    29‚Üí    29‚Üíused a network approach to examine associations between different social cognition measures
    30‚Üí    30‚Üíand alcohol use severity, while also assessing the roles of age and sex.
    31‚Üí    31‚ÜíMethods
    32‚Üí    32‚ÜíAlcohol users (N=936; 16-81yrs) completed the social and conformity subscales of the
    33‚Üí    33‚ÜíDrinking Motives Questionnaire Revised, the Social Reward Questionnaire, the Social
    34‚Üí    34‚ÜíAttunement Questionnaire, and measures of alcohol use severity. Using network analysis, we
    35‚Üí    35‚Üíexamined unique associations between social measures and alcohol use severity, the
    36‚Üí    36‚Üímoderating role of age, and sex differences.
    37‚Üí    37‚ÜíResults
    38‚Üí    38‚ÜíAlcohol use severity was linked to social drinking motives and sociability (i.e., the
    39‚Üí    39‚Üítendency to participate in social gatherings). In the age-moderated network, associations were
    40‚Üí    40‚Üílargely stable across age, except for the association between sociability and alcohol use
    41‚Üí    41‚Üíseverity, which was weaker in older participants. We found no sex differences in the pattern of
    42‚Üí    42‚Üíassociations.
    43‚Üí    43‚ÜíDiscussion
    44‚Üí    44‚ÜíAlcohol use severity was primarily associated with general social engagement, while
    45‚Üí    45‚Üímore specific social-cognitive measures were only indirectly related. Social drinking contexts
    46‚Üí    46‚Üímay therefore be a key pathway to greater severity, especially in younger drinkers. Associations
    47‚Üí    47‚Üídid not differ by sex, suggesting previously reported sex differences may reflect differences in
    48‚Üí    48‚Üíprevalence, rather than sex moderating social factors.
    49‚Üí    49‚ÜíKeywords: Alcohol, alcohol use disorder, social cognition, age, sex

    52‚Üí    52‚ÜíIntroduction
    53‚Üí    53‚ÜíThe most common context in which alcohol consumption takes place is social, for
    54‚Üí    54‚Üíexample, with friends or colleagues, especially when individuals are young [1,2]. Although the
    55‚Üí    55‚Üíimportance of social factors in relation to both initiation and escalation of alcohol use is well-
    56‚Üí    56‚Üírecognized [3,4], they are often studied in isolation. Furthermore, despite our knowledge on
    57‚Üí    57‚Üíage [5,6] and sex differences in social processing and alcohol use [7‚Äì9], these factors are often
    58‚Üí    58‚Üíignored. Network analysis may provide valuable insights by offering a birds-eye view of how
    59‚Üí    59‚Üídifferent social measures may be associated, while modelling their unique associations after
    60‚Üí    60‚Üíaccounting for intercorrelations. Hence, this study used a network approach to examine the
    61‚Üí    61‚Üícross-sectional pattern of associations between key social predictors of alcohol use severity,
    62‚Üí    62‚Üíwhile considering the roles of age and sex in these associations.
    63‚Üí    63‚ÜíAmong the social factors commonly associated with alcohol use severity, social and
    64‚Üí    64‚Üíconformity drinking motives such as drinking to enhance social interactions or to fit into a
    65‚Üí    65‚Üígroup are the most extensively studied [10]. While endorsement and strength of social motives
    66‚Üí    66‚Üíare more commonly associated with binge drinking and frequency of use [11], conformity
    67‚Üí    67‚Üímotives are more commonly associated with lower levels of use [10]. Social and conformity
    68‚Üí    68‚Üímotives are associated with alcohol use severity during young adulthood (16-36yrs) and
    69‚Üí    69‚Üídecline in both endorsement and predictive value later in life [12]. Historically, research has
    70‚Üí    70‚Üísuggested that men are more inclined to drink for social and conformity reasons than women
    71‚Üí    71‚Üí[13], however, recent research indicates that this gap might be narrowing [14].
    72‚Üí    72‚ÜíThe higher endorsement of social and conformity motives during adolescence and young
    73‚Üí    73‚Üíadulthood may reflect heightened social reward sensitivity, which has consistently been linked
    74‚Üí    74‚Üíto alcohol use severity across an extensive body of literature [4,15‚Äì17]. Social reward can be
    75‚Üí    75‚Üídefined as the reward value attributed to social situations, such as: 1) reflecting the value
    76‚Üí    76‚Üíattributed to being liked by others (admiration); 2) the enjoyment of antisocial behavior
    77‚Üí    77‚Üí3
    78‚Üí    78‚Üí
    79‚Üí    79‚Üí(negative social potency); 3) the preference for more passive social roles (passivity); 4) an
    80‚Üí    80‚Üíoverall desire for social interactions (sociability); 5) the enjoyment of reciprocal peer
    81‚Üí    81‚Üírelationships (prosocial interactions); 6) the enjoyment of sexual experiences (sexual
    82‚Üí    82‚Üírelationships) [18]. Altikula√ß et al. showed age-related changes in social reward processing in
    83‚Üí    83‚Üíeach domain except for sociability and sexual relationships (which was omitted) [19].
    84‚Üí    84‚ÜíAdmiration showed a quadratic effect peaking at approximately the age of 21, prosocial
    85‚Üí    85‚Üíinteractions showed a linear increase with age, and passivity showed a dip in the quadratic
    86‚Üí    86‚Üíeffect with a minimum observed at approximately the age of 15. Negative social potency
    87‚Üí    87‚Üídecreased with age for women but remained stable for men, and women scored higher on
    88‚Üí    88‚Üíprosocial interactions compared to men across all ages, further highlighting the moderating
    89‚Üí    89‚Üíroles of age and sex.
    90‚Üí    90‚ÜíSocial learning is thought to play an important role in shaping social drinking behaviours
    91‚Üí    91‚Üí[20]. One approach to studying social influences on drinking behaviour is the relatively novel
    92‚Üí    92‚Üíconcept of social attunement: the tendency to align behavior with the social environment
    93‚Üí    93‚Üí[21,22]. Social attunement has been associated with higher alcohol use in younger individuals
    94‚Üí    94‚Üí(16-19yrs) with peers who engage in relatively heavy drinking [22]. Furthermore, Kuhns et al.
    95‚Üí    95‚Üíshowed that adolescents with higher neural responses to social alcohol cues compared to non-
    96‚Üí    96‚Üísocial alcohol cues, were also more inclined to harmonize their behavior with their social
    97‚Üí    97‚Üíenvironment, while in adults, the opposite was the case [23]. Likewise, social attunement was
    98‚Üí    98‚Üíassociated with increased alcohol use over time in adolescents but decreased use in adults [23].
    99‚Üí    99‚ÜíRomein et al. found that associations between social attunement and alcohol use severity may
   100‚Üí   100‚Üívary across age and social settings: in social situations with soda, social attunement was
   101‚Üí   101‚Üípositively associated with alcohol use in younger participants, whereas this association became
   102‚Üí   102‚Üínegative in older participants [24]. Concurrently, in social settings without drinks, the opposite
   103‚Üí   103‚Üípattern was observed. When exploring the role of gender in relation to social attunement, it was
   104‚Üí   104‚Üí4
   105‚Üí   105‚Üí
   106‚Üí   106‚Üífound that men scored higher on the behavioral subscale of the Social Attunement
   107‚Üí   107‚ÜíQuestionnaire (SAQ; i.e., the degree to which individuals report adjusting their behavior to
   108‚Üí   108‚Üítheir social environment) compared to women [22], suggesting that men could be more inclined
   109‚Üí   109‚Üíto adapt their behavior to their social environment compared to women.
   110‚Üí   110‚ÜíOverall, drinking motives, social reward, and social attunement, each represent a
   111‚Üí   111‚Üídifferent element in the pathway connecting social processes to alcohol use. Social reward may
   112‚Üí   112‚Üícapture the more immediate reinforcing value of drinking in a social context. Social motives
   113‚Üí   113‚Üícapture broader social reasons for drinking and may therefore be closely associated with social
   114‚Üí   114‚Üíreward. In contrast, conformity motives emphasize drinking to fit in socially, and may thus be
   115‚Üí   115‚Üímore closely associated with social attunement, which captures sensitivity to and alignment
   116‚Üí   116‚Üíwith the social environment. Social processes need to be studied in combination rather than in
   117‚Üí   117‚Üíisolation, along with age and sex, to better understand their connections with alcohol use
   118‚Üí   118‚Üíseverity. Hence, the aims of the present study were threefold and exploratory: 1) to assess how
   119‚Üí   119‚Üísocial attunement, social reward, and social drinking motives are associated with each other as
   120‚Üí   120‚Üíwell as alcohol use severity; 2) to assess whether age moderates these associations; 3) to
   121‚Üí   121‚Üíexamine whether these associations differ by sex.
   122‚Üí   122‚ÜíMethods
   123‚Üí   123‚ÜíSample
   124‚Üí   124‚ÜíData from two studies were merged for analysis, including a total of 1050 participants
   125‚Üí   125‚Üí(16-81yrs). Study 1 was conducted between 2022 and 2025 and contained 811 participants
   126‚Üí   126‚Üí[25]. Study 2 took place between 2023 and 2026 and included 239 participants. Participants for
   127‚Üí   127‚Üíboth studies were recruited through social media and in-person distribution of flyers, whereas
   128‚Üí   128‚Üífor study 1 recruitment also took place via Prolific [26] and the Erasmus University Rotterdam
   129‚Üí   129‚Üíparticipant pool.
   130‚Üí   130‚Üí5
   131‚Üí   131‚Üí
   132‚Üí   132‚ÜíFor study 1, inclusion criteria were proficiency in either Dutch or English, and age over
   133‚Üí   133‚Üí15. For study 2, data collection was part of an MRI-study with stricter inclusion criteria (Table
   134‚Üí   134‚ÜíS1) covering MRI-contraindications and alcohol and cannabis use criteria. Study 2 consisted
   135‚Üí   135‚Üíof three groups containing: heavy alcohol users (n=80), heavy cannabis users (n=79), and
   136‚Üí   136‚Üícontrols (n=80) which were combined into a single sample. Individuals that did not use alcohol
   137‚Üí   137‚Üí(n=140) were removed from the analyses, resulting in a final sample of n=936 participants.
   138‚Üí   138‚ÜíInformed consent was given prior to participation and full study protocols were approved by
   139‚Üí   139‚Üíthe Ethics Review Committees of Erasmus University Rotterdam (study 1; ETH2122-0311)
   140‚Üí   140‚Üíand the University of Amsterdam (study 2: FMG-1073-2023).
   141‚Üí   141‚ÜíProcedure
   142‚Üí   142‚ÜíStudy 1 took place online in English or Dutch, depending on preference of the participant.
   143‚Üí   143‚ÜíTwo blocks of questionnaires were separated by a series of experimental tasks. The first block
   144‚Üí   144‚Üíof questionnaires focused on demographics, whereas the second block contained
   145‚Üí   145‚Üíquestionnaires on substance use and mental health. Compensation was provided through a
   146‚Üí   146‚Üílottery where a gift card worth ten euros was provided to every one in five participants. A
   147‚Üí   147‚Üísecond lottery of four prepaid credit cards worth 50 euros each was raffled across all
   148‚Üí   148‚Üíparticipants.
   149‚Üí   149‚ÜíData for Study 2 was partly collected in-person onsite at the University of Amsterdam
   150‚Üí   150‚Üíbehavioral lab. In preparation for an MRI-session, participants completed a one-hour set of
   151‚Üí   151‚Üíquestionnaires online which was similar in structure to study 1. Following the MRI-scan,
   152‚Üí   152‚Üíparticipants completed questionnaires focusing on substance use. Monetary compensation
   153‚Üí   153‚Üí(‚Ç¨40) was provided after completion of the onsite session.
   154‚Üí   154‚Üí6
   155‚Üí   155‚Üí
   156‚Üí   156‚ÜíQuestionnaires
   157‚Üí   157‚ÜíDemographics and substance use: single-item questions were used to assess age, sex
   158‚Üí   158‚Üíassigned at birth, highest level of completed education, nationality, alcohol use age of onset,
   159‚Üí   159‚Üíand lifetime use of tobacco, cannabis, and other drugs (‚Äúyes‚Äù / ‚Äúno‚Äù). Alcohol use severity
   160‚Üí   160‚Üíassessed using the 10-item Alcohol Use Disorder Identification Test, each item was scored on
   161‚Üí   161‚Üía scale from 0 to 4, except for items 9 and 10 which were scored 0, 2, or 4. The total score (0-
   162‚Üí   162‚Üí40) was calculated as a sum of all items, with an AUDIT score ‚â• 8 indicative of at-risk use
   163‚Üí   163‚Üí[27]. Quantity of alcohol use (standard alcohol units), tobacco use (cigarettes), and cannabis
   164‚Üí   164‚Üíuse (grams) in the 14 days prior to study participation was measured using the timeline follow-
   165‚Üí   165‚Üíback method [28].
   166‚Üí   166‚ÜíSocial measures: social and conformity motives were measured using the Drinking
   167‚Üí   167‚ÜíMotive Questionnaire Revised [29,30]. Social motives reflect the desire to drink to enhance
   168‚Üí   168‚Üísocial experiences (e.g., ‚ÄúI drink to have fun with friends‚Äù), whereas the conformity subscale
   169‚Üí   169‚Üíreflects the desire to follow group norms (e.g., ‚ÄúI drink to fit in with a group I like‚Äù). Both
   170‚Üí   170‚Üísubscales consisted of 5 items scored from 1 to 5, with sum scores used as total subscale scores.
   171‚Üí   171‚ÜíSocial reward was assessed using the Social Reward Questionnaire [18]. The SRQ
   172‚Üí   172‚Üíconsists of six subscales of which one was omitted (sexual relationships): 1) admiration (4
   173‚Üí   173‚Üíitems), reflecting the value of being admired by others; 2) negative social potency (5 items),
   174‚Üí   174‚Üíreflecting enjoyment from malicious social behavior; 3) passivity (3 items), assesses tendency
   175‚Üí   175‚Üíto prefer more passive social roles; 4) sociability (3 items), captures an overall desire for social
   176‚Üí   176‚Üíinteraction; 5) pro-social interactions (5 items), reflects a sense of reward derived from helping,
   177‚Üí   177‚Üícooperation, and positive social exchanges. Each item was scored from 1 to 7, subscale scores
   178‚Üí   178‚Üíwere calculated by averaging across items.
   179‚Üí   179‚ÜíSocial attunement was measured using the 11-item Social Attunement Questionnaire
   180‚Üí   180‚Üí[22], consisting of two subscales: 1) cognitions subscale (5 items), relating to the extent to
   181‚Üí   181‚Üí7
   182‚Üí   182‚Üí
   183‚Üí   183‚Üíwhich someone thinks about their own behavior and how others perceive their behavior; 2)
   184‚Üí   184‚Üíbehaviors subscale (6 items), the degree to which someone adjusts their behavior to attune to
   185‚Üí   185‚Üíthe social behavior of others. Each item was scored from 1 to 7, with sum scores used as total
   186‚Üí   186‚Üísubscale scores.
   187‚Üí   187‚ÜíAnalyses
   188‚Üí   188‚ÜíSample characteristics
   189‚Üí   189‚ÜíGroup comparisons (male-female) on sample characteristics were performed using
   190‚Üí   190‚ÜíWilcoxon rank-sum- or independent samples t-tests depending on data normality (continuous)
   191‚Üí   191‚Üíand Chi-square (categorical) tests in R (V4.4.1). To accommodate the distributional
   192‚Üí   192‚Üíassumptions of our data, we computed a Spearman correlation matrix (Table S9) to assess zero-
   193‚Üí   193‚Üíorder associations between all variables, including their associations with age in JASP (JASP
   194‚Üí   194‚ÜíTeam, 2026; V0.96).
   195‚Üí   195‚ÜíNetwork models
   196‚Üí   196‚ÜíNetwork models were estimated in R (V4.4.1) with the bootnet package [32] and
   197‚Üí   197‚Üívisualized using the qgraph R [33] package. We estimated LASSO regularized Gaussian
   198‚Üí   198‚ÜíGraphical Models (GGM) with the extended Bayesian information criterion least absolute
   199‚Üí   199‚Üíshrinkage and selection operator (EBICglasso) with gamma set to 0.5, using Spearman
   200‚Üí   200‚Üícorrelations. Variables were standardized prior to estimation of the network models.
   201‚Üí   201‚ÜíFirst, to assess how social measures were related to each other as well as alcohol use
   202‚Üí   202‚Üíseverity, the SAQ subscales‚Äô scores, SRQ subscales‚Äô scores, social and conformity drinking
   203‚Üí   203‚Üímotive scores, and AUDIT scores were included in the first network (Network 1), resulting in
   204‚Üí   204‚Üía total of 10 nodes.
   205‚Üí   205‚Üí8
   206‚Üí   206‚Üí
   207‚Üí   207‚ÜíSecond, to assess the moderating role of age using the mgm (mixed graphical models)
   208‚Üí   208‚Üípackage [34], age was added to this model (Network 2). The network was estimated using
   209‚Üí   209‚ÜíLASSO regularization with EBIC model selection (Œ≥=0.25).
   210‚Üí   210‚ÜíThird, to assess how associations between social measures and alcohol use severity may
   211‚Üí   211‚Üívary across sexes, separate network models were generated with the bootnet package [35] for
   212‚Üí   212‚Üímale (Network 3) and female (Network 4) participants. Subsequently, a network comparison
   213‚Üí   213‚Üítest was performed between networks 3 and 4, to assess differences in strength centrality (Table
   214‚Üí   214‚ÜíS7), structure, and edge weights (Table S8), using 10000 permutations. The network
   215‚Üí   215‚Üícomparison test was implemented using the NetworkComparisonTest R package [36].
   216‚Üí   216‚ÜíWe used non-parametric bootstrapping (1000 resamples) to evaluate edge stability
   217‚Üí   217‚Üí(Figure S1, S4 S5) [32]. Stability of strength centrality was evaluated via case-dropping
   218‚Üí   218‚Üíbootstrap resampling. Correlation-stability (CS-C) coefficients were computed to assess the
   219‚Üí   219‚Üíreliability of the associations, with a CS-C > 0.25 indicating minium stability and CS-C > 0.50
   220‚Üí   220‚Üíindicating good stability [35].
   221‚Üí   221‚ÜíAs the age moderated network was estimated using a mixed graphical model, CS-
   222‚Üí   222‚Üícoefficients and centrality measures were not interpreted, as node connectivity is conditional
   223‚Üí   223‚Üídepending on the level of the moderator and therefore not fixed across the network. However,
   224‚Üí   224‚Üíedge weights and moderation effects were bootstrapped (Figure S3) to ensure the stability of
   225‚Üí   225‚Üíthe reported findings.
   226‚Üí   226‚ÜíNode placement for network 1 was based on the Fruchterman-Reingold algorithm [37],
   227‚Üí   227‚Üíensuring that variables with stronger links appeared closer together. The same network
   228‚Üí   228‚Üístructure was maintained for subsequent networks to facilitate comparison across networks,
   229‚Üí   229‚Üíhowever, for networks 2, 3, and 4, the placement of the negative social potency node was
   230‚Üí   230‚Üíslightly shifted to the right to avoid obscuring its associations with other nodes. Participants
   231‚Üí   231‚Üí9
   232‚Üí   232‚Üí
   233‚Üí   233‚Üíwho did not report male or female sex (n=5) were included in Networks 1 and 2 but excluded
   234‚Üí   234‚Üíin the sex specific networks.
   235‚Üí   235‚ÜíResults
   236‚Üí   236‚ÜíSample characteristics

   243‚Üí   243‚Üínegatively associated with all social measures, except SAQ cognition and conformity motives,
   244‚Üí   244‚Üíand negatively with alcohol use severity.

   288‚Üí   288‚ÜíDiscussion
   289‚Üí   289‚ÜíWe assessed network associations between social measures and alcohol use severity,
   290‚Üí   290‚Üíwhether age moderates these associations, and whether they differ across sex. In the network
   291‚Üí   291‚Üíonly including social measures and alcohol use severity (Figure 2), social measures from the
   292‚Üí   292‚Üísame scale tended to cluster together, and alcohol use severity was only linked to social
   293‚Üí   293‚Üídrinking motives and SRQ sociability. When assessing the role of age, the age-moderated
   294‚Üí   294‚Üínetwork (Figure 3) showed negative direct associations between age and SRQ admiration, SRQ
   295‚Üí   295‚Üísociability, SAQ behavior, and social motives. Furthermore, associations within the network
   296‚Üí   296‚Üíremained largely stable across age, except for the SRQ sociability ‚Äì AUDIT relationship, which
   297‚Üí   297‚Üíwas moderated by age, such that the association was weaker in older participants. Similarly,
   298‚Üí   298‚Üídespite sex differences being observed in scores on almost half of the social measures (Table
   299‚Üí   299‚Üí1), associations in the network remained consistent across sex (Figure 4). Taken together, these
   300‚Üí   300‚Üínetwork findings complement more conventional bivariate approaches and suggest that
   301‚Üí   301‚Üíobserved age and sex differences in the role of drinking motives, social reward, and social
   302‚Üí   302‚Üíattunement in alcohol use severity largely reflect differences in the prevalence of specific social
   303‚Üí   303‚Üífactors ‚Äî rather than differences in the underlying mechanisms linking these factors to
   304‚Üí   304‚Üídrinking.
   305‚Üí   305‚Üí12
   306‚Üí   306‚Üí
   307‚Üí   307‚ÜíThe network analyses revealed a relatively dense network in which each social subscale
   308‚Üí   308‚Üíwas associated with subscales of the same questionnaire and at least subscales of one of the
   309‚Üí   309‚Üíother questionnaires. Within the network (Figure 2), only social motives and SRQ sociability
   310‚Üí   310‚Üíwere directly associated with alcohol use severity. Social motives refer to drinking for social
   311‚Üí   311‚Üíreasons, such as enjoying social events or enhancing social interactions [29], whereas
   312‚Üí   312‚Üísociability reflects a general preference for social interaction and participating in social events
   313‚Üí   313‚Üí[18]. Hence, in the context of alcohol use, broad social engagement may be a more central
   314‚Üí   314‚Üídriver than specific social traits such as prosocial interactions. However, these specific social
   315‚Üí   315‚Üítraits remain informative in the context of alcohol use, as many are directly related to social
   316‚Üí   316‚Üímotives and sociability, and thereby indirectly associated with severity of use. These findings
   317‚Üí   317‚Üíhighlight the benefit of the network approach and demonstrate that associations commonly
   318‚Üí   318‚Üífound between social measures and alcohol use severity may largely reflect indirect pathways
   319‚Üí   319‚Üírather than unique relationships, as indicated by the broader pattern in bivariate associations
   320‚Üí   320‚Üí(Figure 1), many of which did not emerge in the network. Looking beyond pairwise
   321‚Üí   321‚Üíassociations, the network structure may also offer insight into the underlying social constructs
   322‚Üí   322‚Üícaptured by each social measure. For example, the SAQ behavior subscale emerged as the most
   323‚Üí   323‚Üícentral node in the network (Figure S2), suggesting it may be a useful indicator of more general
   324‚Üí   324‚Üísocial tendencies. Thus, while social motives and SRQ sociability may be most informative in
   325‚Üí   325‚Üíthe context of alcohol use, the SAQ behavior subscale may better capture social behavior more
   326‚Üí   326‚Üígenerally.
   327‚Üí   327‚ÜíIn the age-moderated network (Figure 3), all associations remained stable across age,
   328‚Üí   328‚Üíexcept for SRQ sociability-AUDIT which weakened with age. This may be particularly
   329‚Üí   329‚Üírelevant in the context of ‚Äúmaturing out of addiction‚Äù ‚Äì the natural reduction of substance use
   330‚Üí   330‚Üías individuals age into adulthood [21,38,39]. To illustrate, the social plasticity hypothesis
   331‚Üí   331‚Üí(SPH) [21] proposes that shifts in social environments across age initially render social factors
   332‚Üí   332‚Üí13
   333‚Üí   333‚Üí
   334‚Üí   334‚Üírisk-enhancing in adolescence and young-adulthood, but increasingly protective later in life
   335‚Üí   335‚Üíwhen norms around substance use change, paralleling transitions to more adult-like
   336‚Üí   336‚Üíresponsibilities (e.g., starting a family, work). The SPH suggests that social attunement drives
   337‚Üí   337‚Üíthese changes, as individuals adapt to shifting expectations and norms around substance use.
   338‚Üí   338‚ÜíOur results are broadly consistent with this hypothesis, but with an important difference: the
   339‚Üí   339‚ÜíSPH emphasizes changes in drinking behavior in relation to changing norms and expectations
   340‚Üí   340‚Üísurrounding substance use, our findings suggest that people might instead simply participate
   341‚Üí   341‚Üíless in alcohol-centered social gatherings. As an example, as people age, sociability may still
   342‚Üí   342‚Üípredict alcohol use, but the association weakens because social contexts may shift, as drinking
   343‚Üí   343‚Üímay become more linked to routine and health [40‚Äì42], and social events may function less as
   344‚Üí   344‚Üía cue for drinking [23]. This is broadly consistent with our previous results, showing that [12]
   345‚Üí   345‚Üícoping and enhancement motives predicted alcohol use severity across most of the lifespan,
   346‚Üí   346‚Üíwhereas social and conformity motives were only predictors in earlier stages of life. Our
   347‚Üí   347‚Üífindings suggest that sociability may be a key factor in this age-related pattern, given its
   348‚Üí   348‚Üíassociation with social motives and the age-related attenuation of its link with alcohol use
   349‚Üí   349‚Üíseverity. Furthermore, in the correlation matrix (Figure 1), age was negatively associated with
   350‚Üí   350‚Üínearly all social measures and alcohol use severity, suggesting broad age-related decreases.
   351‚Üí   351‚ÜíHowever, in the network model, after controlling for interrelations, half of these associations
   352‚Üí   352‚Üíremained, highlighting the benefit of the network approach in isolating unique associations,
   353‚Üí   353‚Üíwith age moderating only a single edge.
   354‚Üí   354‚ÜíSimilar to age, even though males and females differed in average levels of social
   355‚Üí   355‚Üímeasures and alcohol use severity (Table 1), associations between social measures and alcohol
   356‚Üí   356‚Üíuse severity remained consistent across sex (Figure 4). Prior research has shown mixed results
   357‚Üí   357‚Üíregarding sex differences in the associations between social measures and alcohol use
   358‚Üí   358‚Üí[24,43,44], suggesting that these associations may be multidirectional and context dependent.
   359‚Üí   359‚Üí14
   360‚Üí   360‚Üí
   361‚Üí   361‚ÜíOur results suggest that previously observed effects of sex may partly reflect shared variance
   362‚Üí   362‚Üíacross social measures, rather than a direct effect of sex and gender on these associations. In
   363‚Üí   363‚Üíessence, mean-level differences across sex may create the impression of sex-specific effects in
   364‚Üí   364‚Üísimpler analyses, our network approach corrects for these differences across all measures,
   365‚Üí   365‚Üísuggesting that associations remain largely stable across sex. However, the absence of sex
   366‚Üí   366‚Üídifferences across network structures may also reflect a narrowing gender gap not only in
   367‚Üí   367‚Üíalcohol use, but also in social motives for drinking [14,45,46]. Furthermore, while the same
   368‚Üí   368‚Üínetwork may exist across sexes, mean level differences may still influence how the network is
   369‚Üí   369‚Üíexpressed within each group. For instance, males scored higher on SRQ negative social potency
   370‚Üí   370‚Üíand DMQ conformity, and females higher on SAQ cognition, SRQ prosocial interactions, and
   371‚Üí   371‚ÜíSRQ sociability (Table 1), the latter directly linked to alcohol use severity.
   372‚Üí   372‚ÜíThe present study is the first to assess associations between social measures and alcohol
   373‚Üí   373‚Üíuse severity combined, rather than in isolation. However, several important limitations need to
   374‚Üí   374‚Üíbe considered. Age-related changes may reflect cohort effects rather than true developmental
   375‚Üí   375‚Üívariation due to the cross-sectional design of the study. Furthermore, the moderation effect
   376‚Üí   376‚Üíinvolving age should be interpreted with caution. Although the effect was retained in 65% of
   377‚Üí   377‚Üíbootstrap samples, indicating some robustness, its stability was only moderate and the
   378‚Üí   378‚Üíbootstrap interval approached zero. Future studies with larger samples are needed to establish
   379‚Üí   379‚Üíthe reliability of age-related differences in the network structure. Participants may have been
   380‚Üí   380‚Üíintoxicated during participation due to the online nature of the study. The sample included a
   381‚Üí   381‚Üíhigher proportion of younger, higher educated, and cisgender individuals, limiting the
   382‚Üí   382‚Üígeneralizability of the results. Moreover, social drinking norms may vary across cultures
   383‚Üí   383‚Üí[47,48], therefore, our results may not translate to all contexts. Additionally, the included social
   384‚Üí   384‚Üímeasures may not capture the full scope of social cognition, future research may include
   385‚Üí   385‚Üíadditional questionnaires (e.g., [49,50]). Furthermore, the scenarios presented in the
   386‚Üí   386‚Üí15
   387‚Üí   387‚Üí
   388‚Üí   388‚Üíquestionnaire may not be representative of social situations commonly encountered by older
   389‚Üí   389‚Üíindividuals. Lastly, we pooled data from two samples with markedly different designs. Study
   390‚Üí   390‚Üí2 used an extreme-groups sampling approach, which can distort cross-sectional associations
   391‚Üí   391‚Üírelative to a continuously distributed sample.
   392‚Üí   392‚ÜíConclusion
   393‚Üí   393‚ÜíThe network approach allows us to examine unique associations between social measures
   394‚Üí   394‚Üíand alcohol use severity, while controlling for all measures. Our results demonstrate the added
   395‚Üí   395‚Üívalue of the network approach, as many associations observed in bivariate analyses did not
   396‚Üí   396‚Üípersist and appear to reflect indirect pathways, with broader social tendencies such as
   397‚Üí   397‚Üísociability emerging as central drivers of alcohol use severity in the network. Furthermore, age
   398‚Üí   398‚Üíplayed a specific role in shaping this association which was weaker in older participants. Future
   399‚Üí   399‚Üíresearch should assess whether the network structure changes across age by tracking
   400‚Üí   400‚Üíindividuals longitudinally. Further assessing the roles of age and sex for identifying those at
   401‚Üí   401‚Üílong-term risk of alcohol use disorder remains crucial.
