    13→Abstract
    14→A prior study during COVID-19 lockdown (Heck et al., 2024) examined whether a pre-
    15→test alcohol craving measurement influenced posttest assessment and tested its relationship with
    16→social desirability (SD) and demand effects (the consequences of participants trying to uncover
    17→the study’s aim). Results suggested that the first craving measurement increased its post-
    18→experimental levels in heavy drinkers, with no associations found for SD or demand effects.
    19→Engagement with the alcohol-related video was linked to craving. This study aimed to replicate
    20→these findings three years later outside the lockdown context.
    21→A hundred and forty-two psychology students were randomly allocated to either a “pre-
    22→post” or a “post-only” craving assessment group. They watched an alcohol-related video. SD,
    23→demand effects, engagement, and alcohol consumption levels were also assessed.
    24→Several findings supported the robustness of effects observed in the initial study, while
    25→others diverged, offering new hypotheses. Heavier drinkers reported significantly higher
    26→craving levels following video exposure and craving correlated moderately with video
    27→engagement. No significant associations were found between craving and SD or demand
    28→characteristics. However, unlike the initial study, repeated measurements showed no significant
    29→effects or interactions with alcohol consumption.
    30→Reanalysis of the original data highlighted a gender-based interaction, where the effect of
    31→the interaction between repeated craving measurements and alcohol consumption was
    32→significant in men but not in women. These results suggest partial replication of prior findings
    33→and underscore the need to further explore contextual and demographic influences on alcohol
    34→craving. Moreover, this study confirmed a significant role of video engagement in craving.
    35→Keywords: Alcohol craving measurement, demand characteristics, engagement, social
    36→desirability.

    39→1. Introduction
    40→Craving is a key feature of drug addiction. It has been widely studied since its inclusion
    41→as a diagnostic criterion in the DSM-V [1–7]. However, despite decades of research, its
    42→usefulness and precise role in addiction is still debated [6,8]. Yet, the concept of craving is part
    43→of most theoretical models of addiction and has been consistently shown to be implicated in
    44→drug use and relapse [9]. The American Psychiatric Association defined craving as “an intense
    45→urge or desire for a drug” [10], although recent evidence shows that it also relates to non-
    46→substance-related behavioral addictions [11]. Based on extensive research and current
    47→theoretical knowledge, it is now widely accepted that craving is a complex multidimensional
    48→phenomenon, including automatic, cognitive, and physiological (interoceptive) components
    49→[12,13]. Regarding alcohol use disorder (AUD) more specifically, craving has for instance been
    50→shown to be associated with relapse [14–17, although see 18], disorder severity [19], and
    51→attentional biases [20,21]. Given its crucial role, it is essential to measure craving as accurately
    52→as possible. Various methods were developed to measure alcohol craving [22], but the most
    53→widely used are self-reported scales.
    54→As with other measurements based on self-reports, there are many potential sources of
    55→bias that could impact craving accuracy measurement. Over 20 year ago, Sayette [23] already
    56→pointed out that craving research should take into account the multiple factors that could
    57→potentially affect the measurement of craving through self-reported questionnaires, citing for
    58→instance social desirability (SD), response styles, and memory biases. We have recently argued
    59→that using self-reported craving scales repeatedly in the same experiment (as is usually done
    60→when craving changes are expected after an experimental manipulation), could impair its proper
    61→evaluation at the end of the experiment [24]. Indeed, the initial completion of the craving scale
    62→by the participants could directly act as an alcohol cue, evoking alcohol consumption and
    63→therefore possibly promoting or altering the subjective experience of craving [25]. In other
    64→
    65→4
    66→words, the initial craving scale could have a priming effect on alcohol craving. Additionally,
    67→self-reported craving scales are generally very explicit and therefore possibly vulnerable to
    68→various experimental biases, such as social desirability and demand effects. Social desirability,
    69→or the tendency to respond in a manner that makes one “look good” [26] might significantly
    70→affect the measurement of craving, although this has been little investigated. Demand effects
    71→on the other hand, are related to the fact that participants are not passive responders to the
    72→experimental manipulations. They typically seek to offer a valuable contribution [27] and
    73→therefore to behave according to their understanding of the aim of the study. As a consequence,
    74→any explicit or implicit information about the purpose of the study is likely to affect their
    75→responses and behavior. All the explicit and implicit clues about the purpose of the study are
    76→called “demand characteristics”, because participants use these clues to identify what the
    77→experimenter is “demanding” of them. More recently, demand effects were conceptualized in
    78→three sub-dimensions [28]: cue interpretation (i.e., "the analysis and interpretation of these cues
    79→by the participants to produce a set of conjectures about the purpose of the research"),
    80→sensitivity to demand characteristics (i.e., an expression of interest on behalf of the respondent,
    81→seeking to make sense of the questioning situation with which they are confronted"), and
    82→response motivation (i.e., "the willingness of the individuals being questioned to rely on their
    83→interpretation of the demand characteristics in order to engage in specific response behaviors).
    84→Based on these premises, the explicit nature of self-reported craving scales, especially when
    85→administered twice in the same experiment, is likely to induce specific demand effects in the
    86→participants.
    87→In a recent experiment, we investigated the impact of a pre-test measurement of alcohol
    88→craving on its final assessment, in relation to social desirability and demand effects [24].
    89→Participants were randomly allocated to two experimental groups: a “pre-post” group in which
    90→craving was assessed twice, before and after watching an alcohol-related video; or a “post-only”
    91→
    92→5
    93→group in which craving was assessed only after the video. Social desirability, demand effects,
    94→engagement toward the video, and alcohol consumption levels were also assessed using
    95→validated questionnaires. The results showed that the levels of craving after watching an alcohol
    96→video were higher in heavier drinkers relative to low drinkers. Additionally, the pre-test
    97→assessment of craving had a significant impact on post-test craving, but only in heavier alcohol
    98→drinkers. More precisely, in high alcohol drinkers, alcohol craving was significantly higher after
    99→the video when craving was also assessed before the video. No such effect was observed in low
   100→alcohol drinkers. In contrast, no relationship between self-reported craving and SD or demand
   101→effects was found in that study. Finally, exploratory analyses indicated that engagement toward
   102→the alcohol-related video was a significant mediator of subsequent craving increases.
   103→Since this initial study took place during the COVID-19 lockdown period and considering
   104→its modest sample size, we aimed to assess the robustness of these findings in a non-lockdown
   105→context, using a larger sample size. We took advantage of the participant pool at the University
   106→of Liège to replicate the study on a sample of first-year psychology students.
   107→2. Methods
   108→The present study used the exact same design as the initial study (see [24]). The only
   109→modification was the replacement of the DS36 social desirability scale [29] by the Social
   110→Desirability Scale-17 (SDS-17)[30], measuring social desirability with a one-factor structure
   111→instead of the two-factor structure (DS36). This modification was chosen to shorten the study
   112→duration and because our primary interest was in 'impression management'—the act of
   113→consciously presenting oneself in a favorable light to others—rather than 'self-deceptive
   114→enhancement,' which involves unconsciously seeing oneself in a highly positive manner.
   115→Participants were a hundred-and-forty-two 1st-year psychology students (92% female,
   116→mean age= 19.33, sd= 1.81), recruited through a “participant pool” device of the Faculty of
   117→
   118→6
   119→Psychology at the University of Liège. This high percentage of female participants reflects the
   120→gender distribution within the faculty. Students received course credit for their participation and
   121→took part in an interactive educational session about the study. Exclusion criteria were strictly
   122→the same as in the initial study. Participants were randomly assigned to one of the two
   123→experimental groups: pre-post group (N= 72) vs. post-only group (N= 70). This study was
   124→approved by the Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Psychology, Speech and Language Therapy
   125→and Education of the University (on October the 3rd 2023, reference number: 10968) and was
   126→conducted in accordance with the ethical standards described in the Declaration of Helsinki
   127→(1964) and institutional guidelines. The privacy rights of participants have been observed and
   128→informed consent was obtained.
   129→Following the initial study, we aimed for a sample size of at least 77 participants.
   130→However, given the participant pool setup, we anticipated obtaining a higher number of
   131→subjects.
   132→Statistical analyses were run using R [31]. To examine the effect of repeated measures of
   133→craving, alcohol consumption, and their interaction on final craving levels, a multiple linear
   134→regression analysis was conducted. However, the normality of residuals was not achieved for
   135→this model. Consequently, craving data were square root transformed, which improved the
   136→distribution significantly, but residual normality was still not met due to a floor effect (Shapiro-
   137→Wilk p-value ranging from <.0001 to .007). Despite this, as homoscedasticity was satisfied
   138→(Breusch-Pagan test: p=0.49), the same linear regression model as in the previous study was
   139→applied. To determine if there was a relationship between craving and engagement with the
   140→alcohol-related video, similar to the previous study, a Pearson correlation coefficient was
   141→computed. Finally, Kendall’s Tau correlation coefficients were used to investigate social
   142→desirability and demand characteristics.

   145→3. Results
   146→In relation to the initial study, several effects observed in the current study support their
   147→robustness. However, some effects differed from those in the original study or did not reach
   148→significance, generating new hypotheses.
   149→Consistent Effects.
   150→Firstly, the effect of alcohol consumption levels on craving was significant, with heavier
   151→drinkers reporting higher craving levels following video exposure (Figure 1). This effect was
   152→medium-sized (t = 2.107, p = .036, η²p = .077), aligning with previous results (t = 3.47, p <
   153→.001, η²p = .09). Another effect that closely replicated the original study was the increase in
   154→craving after video exposure within the “pre-post group” (V = 1315, p = .003, r = .29).

   192→New/Non-Replicated Effects.
   193→Contrary to the initial study, no significant effect of repeated measurements was found
   194→(t = -1.605, p = .110, η²p = .005), nor was there a significant interaction with alcohol
   195→consumption level (t = 0.805, p = .422, η²p < .001) (see Figure 1). The regression model
   196→accounted for 8.95% of the variance in self-reported craving (F(3,138)= 5.621; p = .001,
   197→adjusted R2 = .089). The correlations between AUDIT-C and craving did not differ significantly
   198→between groups as they did in the initial study (q=0.08), although this correlation was only
   199→statistically significant in the “pre-post” group (pre-post group: τ =0.25, p=0.005; post-only
   200→group: τ =0.17, p=0.05).
   201→Re-examination of Initial Data.
   202→Given the lack of replication of the original interaction effect between repeated alcohol
   203→craving measurements and alcohol consumption levels, we questioned whether this discrepancy
   204→might be due to the predominantly female sample in the present study. To investigate this
   205→possibility, we reanalyzed the original data, examining potential gender effects. Figure 3 shows
   206→the linear regression analysis from the initial study, stratified by gender. Results indicated a

   218→4. Discussion
   219→The primary objective of this study was to determine whether our initial findings could be
   220→self-replicated outside the context of the covid-19 lockdown. The initial study examined
   221→whether repeated measurements of craving influenced its assessment after watching an alcohol-
   222→related video, considering social desirability and demand effects. The findings of the initial
   223→study revealed significant effects of the repeated measurement of craving, alcohol consumption
   224→level, and their interaction, suggesting that pre-experimental craving measurements elevated
   225→post-experimental levels, but only in heavy drinkers. No evidence was found linking these
   226→
   227→11
   228→changes to social desirability or demand effects. Further analyses also indicated that the increase
   229→in craving in heavy drinkers was mediated by a stronger engagement with the alcohol-related
   230→video.
   231→The present study replicated most results of the initial study, strengthening the robustness
   232→of the initial findings. In both studies, level of alcohol consumption significantly influenced
   233→cue-induced craving, and, when repeated assessments were conducted, craving levels increased
   234→following cue exposure compared to pre-exposure levels, indicating successful craving
   235→induction. Similar effects were documented in the literature over recent decades [25,32–35].
   236→These results demonstrate that the video cue exposure used in both studies successfully induced
   237→subjective craving and effectively differentiated between light and heavy drinkers.
   238→Notably, the impact of engagement was also replicated, with a correlation of similar
   239→magnitude in the two studies (initial study: r = .44; present study: r = .42). This finding provides
   240→further support for our hypothesis that the level of engagement with alcohol-related stimuli
   241→(whether in a video format or other media) is a critical factor in craving induction.
   242→In both studies, there was no indication of a statistically significant effect of social
   243→desirability, or demand characteristics on cue-induced craving. This indicates that self-reported
   244→alcohol craving was not significantly affected by participants' tendencies to present themselves
   245→in a socially desirable manner or to conform to perceived experimental expectations. The
   246→absence of such biases enhances the validity of the experimental findings on craving, suggesting
   247→that the responses were mainly driven by experimental manipulations, such as alcohol cue
   248→exposure. However, future research could further validate this conclusion by using implicit
   249→measures of craving or incorporating additional checks to detect potential subtle influences of
   250→social desirability and demand characteristics.
   251→
   252→12
   253→Since the effects of repeated measurements of craving were not replicated in the present
   254→study, we questioned what differences between the two studies might explain these
   255→discrepancies. A major difference was the gender ratio. Participants in the second study were
   256→recruited from a "participant pool" in the Faculty of Psychology, resulting in a very high
   257→proportion of female participants, reflecting the faculty's gender distribution. Consequently, we
   258→reanalyzed the data from the initial study to explore potential gender effects. This analysis
   259→revealed a significant gender effect (Figure 3) with a large interaction effect between alcohol
   260→consumption and repeated measurements in men, whereas this effect was absent in women.
   261→Therefore, the two studies yielded similar effects when gender is included in the analyses. We
   262→also checked whether differences in AUDIT-C scores or levels of engagement could act as
   263→confounding variables explaining the observed gender difference; however, this was not the
   264→case in our data (AUDIT-C: W= 745, p=0.35, r= 0.11 CI95% -0.12 - 0.21; Engagement:
   265→W=680.5, p=0.83, r=0.026, CI 95%, -0.22 – 0.05). However, these analyses were not part of
   266→the initial study design, and the sample sizes were relatively small (20 women and 15 men in
   267→the pre-post group, and 24 women and 15 men in the post-only group). Therefore, further
   268→research is needed to confirm these conclusions.
   269→This observed gender difference might be related to gender stereotypes. For women, the
   270→reported levels of post-video craving were consistent with their usual alcohol consumption:
   271→light drinkers reported low craving, while heavier drinkers reported higher levels of craving,
   272→regardless of the repeated measurements. In contrast, a different pattern emerged for men. When
   273→craving was assessed only after the video, the reported levels were unrelated to their drinking
   274→habits. Their responses seemed to be directly influenced by the cue exposure itself, potentially
   275→activating social stereotypes that portray heavy drinking as socially positive for men. However,
   276→the pre-video measurement of craving appeared to anchor their reported levels to their drinking
   277→
   278→13
   279→habits, subsequently influencing their post-video responses. This speculative explanation
   280→clearly requires further confirmation in future studies.
   281→In conclusion, the present results suggest that an initial pre-exposure craving measurement
   282→could significantly impact the final craving assessment in men, but not in women. Moreover,
   283→the way individuals engage with alcohol-related videos is significantly related to increased
   284→craving for alcohol afterwards. These findings also confirm the absence of social desirability
   285→and demand effects observed in our previous study. Further investigations are needed to
   286→determine whether these effects of an initial craving measure are beneficial or represent a
   287→significant bias in craving studies, and why they may not apply to women.
