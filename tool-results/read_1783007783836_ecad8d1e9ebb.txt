    28→Abstract
    29→Resting-state functional MRI (rsfMRI) has become a widely-deployed methodology in human neuroscience, and its
    30→application to traumatic brain injury (TBI) has produced a rapidly growing literature. Efforts to synthesize this
    31→literature are complicated by methodological heterogeneity, including differences in data cleaning, denoising, and,
    32→critically, brain parcellation, leaving the field without consensus regarding the network response to brain injury.
    33→injury. We conducted a systematic review of 219 rsfMRI studies spanning the full TBI spectrum: civilian mild TBI,
    34→sports-related head impacts, military TBI, pediatric TBI, moderate-to-severe TBI, and intimate partner
    35→violence-related brain injury. All findings are remapped to a common network atlas (Seitzman) and organized by
    36→patient population, injury phase, and network. Our primary observation is that much of the apparent inconsistency
    37→reflects meaningful variability rather than noise. Coherent patterns emerge when findings are organized this way:
    38→within-network hypoconnectivity predominates acutely in civilian mTBI, sports-related concussion shows the
    39→opposite pattern of early within-network hyperconnectivity, moderate-to-severe TBI follows a protracted
    40→trajectory from early hypoconnectivity through compensatory hyperconnectivity toward eventual normalization or
    41→permanent disconnection, and pediatric findings are shaped by sex, symptom status, and developmental stage.
    42→Across populations, the default mode and frontoparietal networks show the most consistent alterations, and
    43→graph theory studies provide a coherent temporal framework. Methodological factors, including motion mitigation
    44→(e.g., global signal regression) and analytic approach, account for a substantial portion of between-study
    45→divergence. This review concludes that rsfMRI is a viable approach for modeling systems-level plasticity after TBI
    46→and identifies key methodological priorities for the field moving forward.

    48→Introduction
    49→The neuroplastic response after traumatic brain injury (TBI) is heterogeneous and dynamic, challenging
    50→efforts to identify generalizable biomarkers of recovery. Decades of structural neuroimaging — lesion
    51→quantification, volumetrics, morphometric analysis — have produced only modest links between visible brain
    52→alterations and symptom profiles.1 This finding is unsurprising as each cubic millimeter of MRI signal aggregates an
    53→enormous amount of neural and synaptic activity, and structural scaffolding does not map onto functional output
    54→one-to-one.2
    55→Given the limitations of using brain structure to predict complex behavioral output, the clinical
    56→neurosciences turned to dynamic measurements of function to understand the consequences of neural disruption
    57→in vivo. The first functional MRI (fMRI) study of TBI was published in 1999, using a working memory task in mild TBI
    58→(mTBI).3 Over the following decade, a substantial literature accumulated, but efforts failed to converge on an
    59→identifiable task-based brain response attributable to injury.4 The emerging consensus was that fMRI was not
    60→specific enough to produce meaningful biomarkers for TBI. In the early 2000s, however, a paradigm shift in human
    61→brain imaging began to approximate large-scale brain connectomics using resting-state fMRI (rsMRI).5,6 One of the
    62→earliest applications of rsfMRI in clinical samples examined recovery from moderate-to-severe TBI (msTBI) at 3-
    63→and 6-months post-injury, with network analyses revealing sensitivity to large-scale plasticity.7 The number of
    64→papers using rsfMRI in TBI has grown dramatically since, with over 50 new papers published each year since 2021.8
    65→fMRI is now the most widely deployed methodology in human neuroscience and a core application of fMRI methods
    66→is examining the brain at “rest”.5 There are attractive advantages to rsfMRI, including relatively straight-forward
    67→data acquisition (lying still in the scanner) while allowing the examiner to model the brain as a complex, integrated
    68→system.
    69→The simplicity of rsfMRI data collection belies the complexity of its analysis, however, both at the stages of
    70→preprocessing9 and network construction,10 effectively guaranteeing methodological divergence between studies.
    71→The cost of this methodological flexibility is interpretive: early attempts to aggregate findings in TBI have proven
    72→difficult. Hallquist and Hillary (2017) identified over 50 distinct parcellation approaches across the 106 TBI studies
    73→they reviewed, making integrated summary essentially impossible.11
    74→More than a decade into the application of network theory to brain connectomics post-TBI, there is still no
    75→consensus on the typical neuroplastic response to injury.12 Searches for consistency in network findings point to
    76→two accounts that are difficult to reconcile at face value: that TBI and associated gray and white matter disruption
    77→leads to increased network response (hyperconnectivity), and that TBI results in network disconnection
    78→accounting for a range of symptoms (hypoconnectivity). Both positions — hyperconnectivity13 and
    79→hypoconnectivity14 — have substantial support in the literature, and each has been advanced as a possible
    80→biomarker of TBI outcome,14,15 with little movement toward resolution.
    81→Goal of this review
    82→The rsfMRI TBI literature has reached the critical mass required for systematic synthesis and critique. We
    83→conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation of this literature across the spectrum of TBI injury mechanism and
    84→severity, drawing on 220 papers spanning civilian mTBI, sports-related head impacts, military TBI, intimate
    85→partner violence (IPV)-related brain injury, pediatric TBI, and msTBI. Our central hypothesis is that much of the
    86→apparent inconsistency in this literature reflects meaningful variability rather than noise. When findings are
    87→carefully organized by patient population, injury phase, and network, and when methodological factors known to
    88→influence functional connectivity (FC) estimates are considered, coherent patterns emerge. To advance this
    89→argument, we re-map all results onto a consistent atlas, concatenate findings across samples, and treat time
    90→post-injury as a primary axis of analysis alongside demographic (e.g., age at injury) and clinical factors (e.g., injury
    91→severity, mechanism of injury) that shape the network response to brain injury. The goal is to extract meaningful
    92→signal from a noisy literature, demonstrating that rsfMRI captures a dynamic trajectory of systems plasticity after
    93→TBI, while identifying the methodological gaps that can be addressed moving forward.

    95→Methodological caveats and terminology for this review
    96→Network Definitions
    97→One of the factors that complicates efforts to synthesize research to date using rsfMRI in TBI is the variable
    98→terminology, in particular with respect to network definitions.16 For this systematic review, we translated all results
    99→to the networks delineated in Seitzman et al.,17 (described and displayed in Table 1 and Figure 1) although we
   100→referenced several other atlases to draft this summary.16,18–20 Table 1 also includes the network abbreviations that
   101→will be utilized throughout the manuscript.
   102→Network Interactions
   103→The brain functions with a balance of integration and segregation, requiring analysis of both within- and
   104→between-network FC to understand the consequences of network disruption secondary to brain disorders. Two
   105→prominent and partially overlapping models describe how this balance is maintained (Figure 2). The triple network
   106→model casts the salience network (SN) as a switch, detecting salient events and triggering transitions between the
   107→DMN/task-negative and task-positive networks — the frontoparietal network (FPN) and SN itself.21 When the
   108→default mode network (DMN) is active, the FPN and SN are not, and breakdown of this segregation after TBI may
   109→lead to one network interfering with another, requiring additional neural resources to maintain function. The
   110→flexible hub model casts the FPN as the primary orchestrator, dynamically coupling with the DMN during internally
   111→directed tasks and with the dorsal attention network (DAN) during externally directed ones.17,22 Once a task-positive
   112→state is engaged, the cinguloopercular network (CON) supports sustained task-set maintenance.17,22 The DMN and
   113→DAN are also reliably anticorrelated, reflecting the opposition between internally and externally directed
   114→cognition,¹⁸ while the DAN exerts top-down modulation on visual cortex23 and works in coordination with the
   115→ventral attention network (VAN), which is suppressed during focused attention and comes online when stimuli
   116→require reorienting. Several networks support overlapping memory functions: the medial temporal lobe network
   117→(MTL) for episodic encoding and retrieval,17,22 the parietoocipital network (PON) for contextual and spatial
   118→memory,17,22 and the PMN for discriminating old and new information during retrieval,18 although how they
   119→coordinate during memory processes is not well-understood.
   120→Primary rsfMRI Methods
   121→Researchers calculate FC in several ways, but with respect to brain parcellation to determine network
   122→nodes, three approaches dominate the literature: seed-based FC, atlas-based FC, and independent component
   123→analysis (ICA). Graph theory provides a separate analytical framework that can be applied to any of these.
   124→Seed-based FC measures the timecourse of a region of interest (ROI) over a scan and identifies brain
   125→regions whose timecourses correlate positively or negatively with the seed. Positive FC reflects coordinated
   126→activity and is typically interpreted as functional coupling, while negative FC (anticorrelation) reflects regions
   127→activating in opposition and is interpreted as functional segregation between competing networks. This was the
   128→original approach used by Biswal et al. to identify resting-state FC in the motor cortex.24 Canonical networks have
   129→common seed regions: the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) and medial prefrontal cortex most reliably extract the
   130→DMN, while the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) is often used to study the FPN. Atlas-based FC is a variation
   131→in which the brain is parcellated into ROIs based on an atlas (e.g., Desikan-Killiany,25 Seitzman17, Schaefer,20
   132→Gordon,26 Power,27 Brainnetome28), and the timecourse of each ROI is correlated with all others to produce an NxN
   133→FC matrix. Specific networks can then be extracted from this matrix, or the matrix can be analyzed using graph
   134→theory.
   135→ICA is a data-driven approach that decomposes the whole-brain rsfMRI signal into spatially- or temporally-
   136→independent components without user input to specify a seed or parcellation a priori.29 Each component
   137→represents a spatial map of regions with correlated activity, and canonical networks (e.g., DMN, FPN) emerge as
   138→individual components. The number of components is typically specified between 7 and 30,30,31 which influences
   139→
   140→network granularity: lower numbers extract broader canonical networks, while higher numbers may split these into
   141→subnetworks.
   142→Graph theory is a mathematical framework for describing the relations between items in an adjacency
   143→matrix32 and can be applied to FC matrices derived from any of the above approaches. Key metrics quantify how
   144→networks balance segregation (local clustering within densely interconnected communities) and integration
   145→(efficient communication across the whole network), as well as the importance of individual nodes as hubs,
   146→bridges, or members of a tightly interconnected core. We describe specific graph theory findings in terms of what
   147→they reflect about network organization rather than the exact metric used. Definitions for specific graph metrics
   148→may be found in Supplementary Note 1.
   149→Methodological Challenges:
   150→For more extensive discussion on how methodological considerations may impact results, and
   151→recommendations for generating reproducible research, please see Caeyenberghs et al., 2024.33 Some of the
   152→methodological considerations that may impact results include:
   153→Patient sample considerations
   154→● Sample size: smaller samples are generally less likely to detect reproducible results.
   155→● Time interval since injury: brain alterations may be fundamentally different across phases of
   156→recovery (acute versus subacute versus chronic versus late chronic), and the feasibility and safety
   157→of imaging may differ based on the clinical setting at each phase.
   158→● Injury severity: patterns of change and resolution may be shaped by factors related to underlying
   159→pathology and injury severity, including mechanism of force, presence and extent of focal
   160→pathology, and loss of consciousness duration.
   161→● Injury heterogeneity: group studies have historically incompletely addressed heterogeneity in
   162→location of injury (e.g., focal lesions) and the nature and mechanism of injury (e.g., blunt versus
   163→blast, sports versus motor vehicle collision), which can produce divergent patterns even within the
   164→same severity category.
   165→● Recruitment strategy: recruiting from trauma units or clinical rehabilitation facilities will often yield
   166→more severely impacted patients than those recruited acutely at emergency departments and
   167→followed longitudinally.
   168→● Comparison sample: there are patient population-specific considerations for selecting comparison
   169→participants, such as non-contact or non-collision athletes for sports, children with orthopedic
   170→injury for pediatric studies, and women with a history of IPV without head trauma and/or brain
   171→injury for IPV studies. Extensive discussion of this can be found in prior papers34–40
   172→Acquisition and processing considerations
   173→● Repetition time (TR): sampling brain activity every 3 seconds versus every 0.8 seconds may show
   174→different patterns.
   175→● Voxel size: larger voxel sizes mean less spatial resolution and will impact the representation of
   176→small brain structures.
   177→● Global signal regression (GSR): this can significantly alter the distribution of positive versus
   178→negative correlations.
   179→● Motion correction and scrubbing: motion can have a significant impact on FC outputs, and studies
   180→vary in how they control for and correct for motion during the scan and during processing. Different
   181→thresholds for "motion scrubbing" (removing problematic volumes) mean more strict versus lenient
   182→inclusion of volumes and can impact calculations.

   184→Materials and Methods
   185→Patient Groups and Chronicity
   186→The post-injury period is characterized by dynamic changes in structure and function and heterogeneous
   187→clinical outcomes influenced by a wide variety of premorbid, comorbid, and post-injury factors. We interpret
   188→results separately first by patient population and second by chronicity. Patient populations, some of which
   189→overlap, include moderate-to-severe TBI (msTBI), military-relevant TBI, sports-related concussion and head
   190→impacts, pediatric TBI, civilian mTBI (not including sports), and intimate partner violence (IPV)-related TBI. We
   191→recognize that there are significant initiatives currently to use new TBI classification methods,41,42 moving away
   192→from the traditional mild/moderate/severe definition of TBI, primarily based on the Glasgow Coma Scale score
   193→administered shortly after injury and other acute markers of injury, towards a more dynamic and comprehensive
   194→injury severity characterization, but we keep the traditional terminology given that existing literature to date
   195→heavily favors this terminology.
   196→Given that recovery timeline is directly linked to injury severity, we used distinct windows of time to
   197→indicate sample chronicity. In the case of mTBI, we define the acute phase as imaging collected within a week of
   198→injury, subacute as 1 week - 1 month, postacute as 1-6 months, and chronic as over 6 months post-injury. For msTBI
   199→these recovery windows are more protracted with few studies occurring in the first days post injury. We recognize
   200→that imposes an arbitrary demarcation on what is a continuous biological recovery process, but the highly dynamic
   201→nature of FC necessitates some division, as the balance of hyper- vs. hypoconnectivity appears to change based
   202→on time since injury. This division is based on prior papers by our group,43–45 as well as the study design of the
   203→sources we review. When possible, we review longitudinal changes in rsfMRI to match the dynamic nature of
   204→recovery.
   205→While most TBI involves a blunt impact, there are some distinct injury mechanisms that are common in
   206→specific patient populations. In military populations, blast-related TBI is common, particularly among service
   207→members who have served since Sept 11, 2001. Blast-related TBI causes damage through several mechanisms:
   208→primary effects (pressure waves inducing tissue stress through compression, tension, and cavitation), secondary
   209→effects (impact from blast-driven debris), and tertiary effects (physical displacement of the service member
   210→against solid objects). Blast exposure can lead to blast over- and underpressure waves moving through the body,
   211→impacting tissues differentially based on mechanisms - structures adjacent to fluid- or air-filled spaces may
   212→experience greater displacement, and tissue stiffness may impact the ability of the tissue to reform after the
   213→impact.46 While blast-related TBI is most common in military populations, there are a growing number of civilians
   214→experiencing these injuries as well in active conflict zones (e.g. Ukraine, the Middle East). Repetitive head impacts
   215→(RHI) can refer to any subconcussive impacts occurring in close succession, but are most common in contact and
   216→collision sports populations. A growing body of research is showing that RHI, measured over the course of a
   217→season or even before and after a game, can produce lasting changes in brain structure and function.47–49 These
   218→impacts are by definition mild, but repeated mild impacts show evidence that even during short timepoints they
   219→may alter on-going recovery processes, and prolong the state of injury. Studies of subconcussive head impacts are
   220→discussed here as they provide a complementary perspective on how repetitive neurotrauma may influence
   221→network FC, even in the absence of clinically diagnosed concussion.
   222→We followed PRISMA guidelines for systematic reviews (Supplementary Figure 1).50 A literature search was
   223→structured with the assistance of an academic librarian, with search terms listed in the Transparency, Rigor, and
   224→Reproducibility section and the end of the manuscript (Supplementary Notes 2-5). No limits were placed on the
   225→search dates and the final searches were conducted by one researcher between April 2-29, 2025. 921 entries were
   226→retrieved from PubMed, 2,338 from EMBASE, 1,496 from Web of Science, and 850 from Medline. The search was
   227→repeated on December 19th, 2025, yielding another 82 from PubMed, 76 from EMBASE, 70 from Web of Science,
   228→and 41 records from Medline. Duplicate records were removed and records received a preliminary screening based
   229→on title, and abstract if needed, to determine whether they were within scope. Records were removed for the
   230→following reasons: not published in the English language or no English translation available, conference poster
   231→
   232→presentations, reviews/editorials/consensus statements, case reports or series, and preprints if the final version
   233→was published. The remaining 442 records were imported into Covidence for a more detailed review, where
   234→information about patient population and methods were extracted to facilitate data synthesis. Papers focused on
   235→fractional amplitude of low frequency fluctuations (fALFF), regional homogeneity (ReHo), and/or
   236→effective/dynamic connectivity were removed, as were those solely focused on disorders of consciousness (DOC -
   237→coma, vegetative state, minimally conscious state) or those that assessed treatment effects. 307 records
   238→remained after review in Covidence. Conflicts were reviewed and resolved by discussion between the researchers
   239→until consensus was reached.
   240→Source Review
   241→The 307 records were reviewed by the authors to extract two sets of information: (1) a quality rating (see
   242→Supplementary Note 6 and Supplementary Table 2) and (2) a summary of findings, organized by patient
   243→population and chronicity and mapped to the Seitzman17 rsfMRI network definitions. Results were summarized as
   244→hyperconnectivity, hypoconnectivity, mixed, null, or not examined. Following human-led data extraction, we used
   245→NotebookLM (Google), an AI-powered research assistant, as a secondary extraction tool. The AI's knowledge base
   246→was restricted to the peer-reviewed neuroimaging studies included in this review, ensuring that all outputs were
   247→grounded solely in the reviewed literature. The AI was prompted to extract findings into a standardized format
   248→including study identification, injury chronicity, directionality of FC alterations, and a summary of key findings for
   249→each network, along with a narrative synthesis of temporal progression. Initial outputs required iterative
   250→refinement, as the model tended to truncate large datasets rather than producing exhaustive summaries;
   251→structured prompting strategies were developed to ensure complete capture of all reviewed sources
   252→(Supplementary Note 7). Claude (Anthropic) was used as an editorial assistant during manuscript preparation for
   253→prose tightening, condensation of body sections, restructuring of synthesis paragraphs, and verification of
   254→consistency between body sections and the summary. All AI-generated suggestions were reviewed, edited, and
   255→approved by the authors before incorporation, and all factual claims were verified against the primary source
   256→literature.
   257→Through this process, an additional 88 papers were determined to be out of scope, leaving 219 sources. All
   258→219 sources are listed in Supplementary Table 1, including patient population and chronicity period(s), and
   259→summarized in Table 2. The patient population could not be determined for one paper, which is included in the
   260→table for completeness but not in the synthesis.51 Several recruitment and analysis details and pertinent rsfMRI
   261→parameters are listed in Supplementary Table 3.
   262→Source Quality Assessment
   263→Study quality was evaluated using a 30-item checklist adapted from the informal quality assessment
   264→criteria included in Lindsey et al., 2023,52 which were selected based on author consensus; the 30 quality
   265→assessment items used presently are listed in Supplementary Note 6. Methodological quality was assessed for all
   266→included studies using a 30-item checklist organized into seven domains: sample quality (D1), clinical
   267→characterization (D2), measurement quality (D3), reporting transparency (D4), statistical rigor (D5), bias and
   268→limitations (D6), and longitudinal considerations (D7, applied only to longitudinal studies). Each item was rated Yes
   269→(1), No (0), Unclear (0.5), Partial (0.5), or NA (excluded from the denominator). Domain scores were calculated as the
   270→proportion of endorsed items among those applicable, and a weighted composite score (0–100) was derived using
   271→pre-specified domain weights (D1=20%, D2=15%, D3=20%, D4=15%, D5=20%, D6=10%; D6 was excluded when both
   272→blinding items were NA, with remaining weights renormalised). Studies were classified as High (≥75), Moderate
   273→(50–74), or Low (<50) quality.
   274→All 219 included study entries were initially rated by individual trained raters. A targeted rescoring
   275→procedure was then applied in three stages to improve accuracy and internal consistency. First, all studies with an
   276→initial composite score below 65 (n = 26) were independently rescored using calibrated decision rules established
   277→
   278→through iterative rater reconciliation sessions; discrepancies were adjudicated and consensus scores adopted.
   279→Second, a systematic item-level audit identified 17 studies with logically impossible rating combinations,
   280→including: no control group coded alongside N>30 scored as satisfied (Q2=No, Q4=Yes; n = 7), no control group
   281→coded alongside group matching scored as present (Q2=No, Q5=Yes; n = 5), a control group present but matching
   282→excluded from scoring (Q2=Yes, Q5=NA; n = 3), and identical scanner parameters coded alongside inconsistent
   283→measurements (Q10=Yes, Q11=No; n = 3); these were corrected to internally consistent ratings. Third, a further 43
   284→studies were selected for targeted review based on item-level patterns suggesting potential over- or
   285→under-scoring, particularly inconsistencies in the statistical rigor domain (e.g., multiple comparisons correction
   286→and p-value reporting items). Final composite scores ranged from 46.8 to 96.7 (mean = 77.8±9.34), with 145 study
   287→entries rated High quality, 73 Moderate, and 1 Low (Supplementary Table 1).
   288→Review of the literature
   289→Civilian mild TBI
   290→This section summarizes 108 papers. Results for all patient populations are summarized in Figure 3 (within
   291→network connectivity) and Figure 2 (between network connectivity).
   292→Default mode network and parietal memory network
   293→Given the spatial and historic overlap between the DMN and PMN,18 we report these results together, as
   294→none of the papers used the terminology "parietal memory network." The PMN, defined in 2015, includes portions of
   295→the precuneus and PCC.
   296→In the acute phase, studies generally report hypoconnectivity among DMN nodes.53–58 Two studies report
   297→hyperconnectivity, but one retrospectively limited results to patients who achieved clinical recovery by six months
   298→post-injury59 and the other used a very short TR in a pediatric sample.60 One study reports a mixed pattern, with
   299→localized hyperconnectivity alongside a decoupling of DMN hubs.61 A data-driven sparse-coding analysis of acute
   300→and subacute mTBI similarly identifies altered DMN composition relative to controls, with reduced PCC and
   301→parietal contributions alongside increased medial prefrontal contribution.62 When acute patients are stratified by
   302→serum α-synuclein, those with higher levels show increased anterior DMN FC in the anterior cingulate and
   303→ventromedial prefrontal cortex alongside decreased precuneus FC, an anterior-posterior dissociation tracking a
   304→biomarker rather than a group contrast.63 Group moderated the association between SN-DMN FC and
   305→psychological resilience in children, with the direction of the association differing between concussion and
   306→orthopedic injury groups64 Madhavan et al. 2019 propose a compensatory role for early hyperconnectivity,
   307→reporting that acute hyperconnectivity between the DMN and other networks predicts lower symptom severity at
   308→three months.65 Subacute results are more blended. One study reports an anterior-posterior pattern with reduced
   309→coherence between DMN hubs,66 while others report DMN-internetwork hyperconnectivity,67–69 within-DMN
   310→hypoconnectivity,67,70,71 or DMN-internetwork hypoconnectivity in pediatric cohorts.69 Females show a mix of hyper-
   311→and hypoconnectivity largely absent in males.72 Several studies report null findings.64,73–75 Methodological factors
   312→contribute substantially: the same group using seed-based analysis identifies decreased within-DMN FC alongside
   313→DMN-lateral-prefrontal hyperconnectivity,67 then reports no reliable abnormalities in a larger ICA-based
   314→follow-up.73 Stringent whole-brain correction can render subtle subacute effects nonsignificant,74 and studies
   315→using orthopedic-injury controls report more circumscribed or null effects,64,69 likely because OI controls adjust for
   316→non-specific consequences of acute injury that uninjured controls do not.
   317→Postacute results vary with recruitment. Bharath et al. 2015 recruited only asymptomatic patients and
   318→report a shift from acute hyperconnectivity to postacute hypoconnectivity.59 Van der Horn et al. 2017 report that
   319→higher anterior–posterior DMN FC at 1 month post-injury predicts greater complaint burden at 3 months in patients
   320→with persisting symptoms.76 This may be age-dependent, as a pediatric study from Van der Horn et al. 2025 finds
   321→DMN hyperconnectivity despite 80% of the sample achieving clinical recovery.60 At the other end of the lifespan in
   322→
   323→a geriatric, microbleed-positive cohort, within-DMN FC is reduced in a pattern equivalent to Alzheimer-related
   324→degradation, with acute cognitive scores predicting the extent of chronic deviation.77 Mayer et al. 2011 did not
   325→constrain their sample and report sustained within-DMN hypoconnectivity alongside DMN-internetwork
   326→hyperconnectivity,67 also reported by Bharath et al.59 Studies in children suggest DMN inter-network FC can be
   327→compensatory or maladaptive depending on the regions involved.78–80 Multiple studies report null results.57,75,81,82
   328→In the chronic phase, recruitment remains important: samples from clinical/rehabilitation facilities81,83–88
   329→may differ from those recruited from naturalistic settings such as EDs at the time of injury and followed
   330→longitudinally.51,54,89–102 Some patterns persist across both, with multiple studies reporting within-DMN
   331→hypoconnectivity,83,88,89,93–95,98,103 in some cases specifically in patients with a history of multiple mTBIs98 or
   332→persisting symptoms.83 Several studies report intra-DMN hyperconnectivity91,100 or mixed results,90 potentially
   333→influenced by age at injury,91 small sample size and mixed severity,100 or CBF-scaled processing that may more
   334→accurately reflect neural synchronization.90 Disruptions to the triple network model are reported,87,97,104 and one
   335→study reports more diffuse DMN internetwork hyperconnectivity.84 Several studies report null results.54,60,81,96
   336→Longitudinal studies report partial DMN normalization,54,60,97,105 while patients with persisting symptoms show
   337→sustained and evolving alterations.57,97
   338→Frontoparietal network
   339→In the acute phase, hypoconnectivity within the FPN and between the FPN and other networks is the most
   340→common finding in adults,54,61,106 with within-network hypoconnectivity negatively correlated with post-concussive
   341→symptom severity,54 FPN-DMN hypoconnectivity associated with attention and language deficits,61 and
   342→FPN-caudate hypoconnectivity resolving by the subacute phase, with parallel improvement in headache impact
   343→and processing speed.106 Acute hyperconnectivity is less consistently reported and tends to be tied to specific
   344→clinical presentations or demographic factors, including post-traumatic headache107 and male sex.108 Several
   345→studies report null results at the network level.59,109,110 In a pediatric sample, acute FPN-VN hyperconnectivity is
   346→positively correlated with psychological resilience, suggesting a potential protective mechanism in youth.64
   347→Subacutely, findings diverge depending on sample characteristics and analytic approach. Reduced FC in
   348→frontal FPN nodes is reported in a large adult study stratified by radiological findings,70 and reduced
   349→interhemispheric dlPFC FC at one month is present specifically in patients with persisting symptoms, correlating
   350→with poorer cognitive performance,58 while within-FPN and FPN-DMN hyperconnectivity is reported in studies with
   351→more homogeneous samples or classification-based analyses.67,111 In an ED-recruited cohort spanning the
   352→subacute-postacute phases, mTBI shows FPN hyperconnectivity, interpreted as compensatory recruitment.112 One
   353→comprehensive study using multiple analytic methods (ROI-based, ICA, and fALFF) reports no significant FPN
   354→differences.74 In pediatric samples, within-FPN and FPN-SN hyperconnectivity alongside FPN-DMN
   355→hypoconnectivity is observed at four weeks post-injury,69 while null results are reported at two weeks when
   356→comparing typical and delayed recovery trajectories.113
   357→Postacutely, both hypo- and hyperconnectivity are reported in adults, often in relation to symptom status.
   358→FPN-thalamic hypoconnectivity is observed at six weeks but partially normalizes by four months,114 and FPN-SN
   359→hypoconnectivity is present only in patients with persisting symptoms, correlating with anxiety and depression
   360→severity.115 Conversely, FPN-DMN and FPN-SN hyperconnectivity is reported in patients with memory complaints,
   361→negatively correlated with objective short-term memory performance,68 and within-FPN hyperconnectivity
   362→emerges at three months in a longitudinal study that reports no alterations at 36 hours.59 Two studies report null
   363→results at approximately one month post-injury,75,115 an earlier timepoint than the positive findings above.68,75 In
   364→pediatric mTBI, postacute FPN alterations are driven by clinical and demographic factors: FPN-SN

   390→interpreted as a compensatory response to maintain cognitive performance and attentional control.53,59,109,110 Three
