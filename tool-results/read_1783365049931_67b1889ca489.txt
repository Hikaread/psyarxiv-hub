     1→Bridging the Gap: A Systematic Review of Training Needs and Barriers for Link
     2→Workers in Social Prescribing for Mental Health Needs
     3→Booth, R. J.1,*, ^, Page, J.1,*, Sticpewich, L.1, Empson, L. 1, Fancourt, D.1, Hayes, D1
     4→*Joint first author

     7→University College London, London, United Kingdom
     8→Abstract
     9→Background: Social prescribing has gained momentum as a community-based
    10→approach to health and wellbeing, with Link Workers (LWs) playing a key role in
    11→connecting individuals to non-clinical support. However, as their remit increasingly
    12→includes people with complex mental health needs, concerns have emerged regarding
    13→whether training is adequate, consistent, and aligned with practice realities. Methods:
    14→This systematic review explored training provision, needs, and barriers affecting LWs
    15→supporting mental health and wellbeing. Following PRISMA guidance, a systematic
    16→search of peer-reviewed and grey literature was conducted across four databases.
    17→Eligible studies examined LW training, support needs, or barriers to effective role
    18→delivery in mental health and wellbeing contexts. Seventeen sources were synthesised
    19→thematically. Results: The synthesis identified multi-layered training needs and barriers
    20→across five themes. Needs included (1) experiential learning for relationally intensive,
    21→emotionally demanding practice; (2) core capability development in communication,
    22→boundary-setting, self-care, mental health literacy, risk navigation and safeguarding or
    23→escalation-related knowledge; and (3) contextual competence in social determinants of
    24→health, inequalities, and population-specific needs. Barriers included (1) role ambiguity
    25→and referral mismatch; and (2) fragmented, under-resourced training systems.
    26→Supervision, mentoring, shadowing, reflective spaces, and face-to-face learning were
    27→valued, while generic or procedural formats were often insufficient. Reliance on prior
    28→experience risked creating inequities across the workforce. Discussion and
    29→Conclusion: Findings suggest that mental health-focused social prescribing often
    30→
    31→involves relational-support work under constrained conditions, and that current training
    32→models do not always match the complexity of practice. Training gaps reflect not only
    33→missing content, but also a mismatch between role demands and infrastructure
    34→originally designed for lighter-touch coordination. Strengthening preparation requires
    35→action at practitioner, organisational, and system levels, including experiential learning,
    36→clearer role definition, referrer education, and more consistent training and support
    37→structures. Addressing these issues is important for workforce sustainability, service
    38→safety, and effective delivery of social prescribing.
    39→Introduction
    40→In recent years, social prescribing (SP) has gained momentum internationally as
    41→a community-based approach to health and wellbeing, with policy and practice now
    42→reported across a wide range of health-system contexts. Social prescribing has been
    43→described across at least 17 countries in Europe, Asia, Australia and North America,
    44→and more recent global mapping has documented developments in 31 countries,
    45→underscoring the adaptability of the model across different systems and settings (Khan,
    46→Giurca, et al., 2024; Morse et al., 2022). While England was the first country to integrate
    47→social prescribing into national health policy, other countries are also rapidly adopting
    48→and scaling social prescribing approaches (Khan et al., 2024). Within this broader
    49→international landscape, Link Workers (LWs), or equivalent roles, have become central
    50→to connecting individuals with non-clinical and community-based support that
    51→addresses social determinants of health and improves overall quality of life
    52→(Linceviciute et al., 2023; Wildman et al., 2019).
    53→Within the UK, SP schemes have expanded across NHS and third-sector
    54→partnerships, and expectations placed on LWs have increased substantially (Hazeldine
    55→et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021). This expansion has occurred alongside wider
    56→pressures in primary care and community mental health systems, including rising
    57→demand, long waiting times, and constrained specialist capacity (Al-Haboubi &
    58→Oladimeji, 2022; Buivydaite et al., 2025; NHS England, 2026). LWs are now frequently
    59→tasked with supporting individuals who present with complex emotional, psychological,
    60→and social needs, often without the clinical infrastructure to support them (Mulholland
    61→et al., 2025; Sandhu et al., 2022). Despite their pivotal role, concerns persist around the
    62→
    63→adequacy of training and preparation for LWs, particularly in managing mental health
    64→difficulties (Makanjuola, 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021).
    65→For example, a UK-based qualitative study found that many LWs felt their job
    66→descriptions failed to capture the emotional and cognitive demands of their roles
    67→(Rhodes & Bell, 2021). These accounts suggest not simply gaps in knowledge, but
    68→potential misalignment between job expectations and the relational and emotional
    69→demands of day-to-day practice. Similarly, an evaluation by Makanjuola (2021) found
    70→that while LWs frequently receive complex mental health referrals, their formal training
    71→often does not align with the needs of such cases, particularly in voluntary and third-
    72→sector contexts.
    73→Unlike clinical professionals, LWs in SP typically enter the role through a range of
    74→non-clinical pathways, such as youth work, peer support, community development, and
    75→welfare advice (Makanjuola et al., 2023; Moore et al., 2023). This diversity is evident
    76→across both NHS and third-sector schemes (Holding et al., 2020; Rhodes & Bell, 2021)
    77→and is widely regarded as a strength, bringing a relationally attuned and community-
    78→connected ethos to the role (Turk et al., 2024; Wildman et al., 2019). For example, in
    79→Australian qualitative work, Sharman et al. (2022) observed that many LWs came from
    80→peer support or social care backgrounds, with strong interpersonal skills that foster
    81→trust and rapport with service users. Yet this diversity also creates variability in
    82→preparedness, particularly where formal training frameworks do not account for the
    83→emotional intensity, boundary complexity, and risk navigation increasingly required in
    84→mental health-focused roles. A meta-ethnography by Turk et al. (2024) found that
    85→training was often minimal and highly inconsistent, leaving many LWs to rely on prior,
    86→and sometimes unrelated, experience when managing emotionally complex cases.
    87→Wildman et al. (2019) similarly acknowledged the strengths of LWs' non-clinical
    88→backgrounds but highlighted the absence of tailored training frameworks, especially for
    89→the relational and emotionally demanding aspects of the role.
    90→Evidence indicates that current training provision is often generic, fragmented,
    91→and designed around coordination or community navigation and connection functions
    92→rather than sustained relational-support work in complex mental health contexts
    93→(Frostick & Bertotti, 2019; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Wildman et al., 2019). Additionally,
    94→supervision, widely recognised as a key support mechanism in health and social care, is
    95→
    96→inconsistently embedded within LW development pathways (Hazeldine et al., 2021;
    97→Sharman et al., 2022). The need for a more coherent and context-sensitive training
    98→strategy is particularly urgent when LWs work with children, young people, or other
    99→vulnerable populations. Emerging research highlights the additional challenges of youth
   100→engagement, safeguarding, and neurodiversity-informed practice (Farina et al., 2024;
   101→Gordon et al., 2023; Hayes et al., 2024). Although this review was global in scope, UK
   102→studies are particularly visible in this introduction because much of the empirical
   103→literature specifically examining link worker training, support needs, and role
   104→implementation has been conducted within UK primary care and third-sector settings.
   105→While several reviews have examined the implementation, components, and
   106→experiences of social prescribing programmes, none have specifically synthesised the
   107→landscape of training provision, capability gaps, and structural barriers for link workers
   108→supporting mental health and wellbeing. Existing syntheses describe role variability and
   109→implementation challenges but do not examine how training needs may be shaped by
   110→the evolving relational intensity of the role and the constrained systems in which it
   111→operates. To address this gap, we conducted a systematic review to explore the
   112→following question: What training is currently available to LWs supporting individuals
   113→with mental health and wellbeing needs, and what gaps and barriers exist in training
   114→provision? This review draws on both academic and grey literature to inform practice,
   115→policy, and future implementation strategies.
   116→Methods
   117→Protocol and Registration
   118→This systematic review was registered on 15 December 2023 with PROSPERO
   119→(registration number CRD42023493626) to ensure transparency and methodological
   120→rigour.
   121→Eligibility Criteria
   122→Studies were eligible if they explored the availability of training, gaps in training
   123→provision, training needs, and potential barriers or facilitators for Link Workers (LWs)
   124→supporting individuals with mental health and wellbeing needs. Qualitative,
   125→
   126→quantitative, and mixed-methods studies were included where appropriate. Grey
   127→literature from organisations involved in SP was included if it met the eligibility criteria.
   128→Participants
   129→Studies were included if they captured the experiences and perspectives of LWs
   130→(or equivalent roles, such as Community Connectors) working with individuals to
   131→support mental health, wellbeing, and quality of life.
   132→Given the broad scope of social prescribing internationally, eligibility was
   133→determined based on role function rather than job title, in line with the international
   134→definition of social prescribing (Muhl et al., 2023). Participants were therefore included
   135→where their role involved developing collaborative relationships with individuals to
   136→identify needs and facilitate access to community-based support. Individuals involved
   137→in the design, establishment, or delivery of mental health and wellbeing navigation
   138→services were included where their role aligned with link worker functions.
   139→Interventions
   140→To be eligible, studies were required to include data exploring existing and/or
   141→desired training opportunities for LWs supporting individuals with mental health and
   142→wellbeing needs. This included facilitated workshops, online training, shadowing
   143→opportunities, peer support groups, supervision, and formal education or qualifications.
   144→Studies examining specific training relevant to mental health-focused SP (e.g.
   145→motivational interviewing, mental health first aid) were also included. Studies reporting
   146→gaps in training provision, perceived training needs, recommendations for workforce
   147→development, or barriers and facilitators affecting LWs’ ability to access or engage in
   148→training were eligible.
   149→Studies were excluded if their primary focus was not mental health or
   150→psychological wellbeing (e.g. social prescribing services aimed exclusively at reducing
   151→cholesterol levels). Studies that did not include LWs or equivalent roles were also
   152→excluded.
   153→Outcome
   154→
   155→Eligible studies reported on existing training resources available to LWs and/or
   156→identified gaps between current provision and required competencies for supporting
   157→individuals with mental health, wellbeing, or quality of life needs.
   158→Study Setting, Language and Date Restrictions
   159→There were no limitations on study settings for eligible studies, but all studies
   160→needed to be published in the English Language.
   161→Information Sources
   162→A systematic search was conducted on 30 November 2023 across four
   163→electronic databases: Embase, OVID MEDLINE, APA PsycINFO, and Social Policy and
   164→Practice. The initial search identified 551 records. Following duplicate removal, 356
   165→records were screened at title and abstract stage. An updated search of the same
   166→databases, using the identical search strategy (available in supplementary material S1),
   167→was conducted on 03 July 2025 to identify newly published studies and ensure the
   168→review reflected the current evidence base. The updated search identified 238
   169→additional records, all of which were screened at title and abstract stage.
   170→The search strategy combined three core concepts: (1) Link Worker terminology,
   171→(2) training and workforce development terms, and (3) mental health and wellbeing-
   172→related terms. The search string was developed iteratively through consultation within
   173→the research team and informed by terminology used in previous social prescribing
   174→literature. The final strategy was peer reviewed by a second researcher prior to
   175→implementation.
   176→The full search syntax for each database is provided in the supplementary
   177→material (S1). Searches were limited to English-language publications.
   178→Selection of Sources of Evidence
   179→Study selection was completed using a two-stage screening process in line with
   180→PRISMA guidance. In Stage 1, titles and abstracts were independently screened by two
   181→researchers (JP and LE) against the predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria. Studies
   182→deemed irrelevant were excluded at this stage. In Stage 2, full texts of potentially eligible
   183→articles were independently assessed by two researchers (JP and LE). Any
   184→
   185→disagreements between reviewers were resolved through discussion, with a third
   186→reviewer consulted where necessary. Across the 594 records screened at title and
   187→abstract stage across the two search rounds, 94 full-text reports were assessed for
   188→eligibility, and 17 studies met the inclusion criteria. Reasons for exclusion at full-text
   189→stage were recorded and are summarised in the PRISMA flow diagram (Figure 1).
   190→Figure 1. PRISMA flow diagram of study selection.
   191→Data Collection and coding
   192→Data extraction was conducted by one researcher with verification checks being
   193→undertaken by the senior author. Studies identified through the updated search
   194→underwent the same screening, eligibility assessment, and extraction process as those
   195→identified in the initial search. Any uncertainties or discrepancies were resolved through
   196→discussion with the wider research team.
   197→The following data were extracted:
   198→• Bibliographic details: author(s), year of publication, journal, country, and
   199→language.
   200→• Study characteristics: aims, design, setting, sample characteristics, and
   201→methods of data collection.
   202→
   203→• Findings relating to training provision, training gaps, barriers to training, and
   204→specific capability needs for Link Workers supporting individuals with mental
   205→health and wellbeing needs.
   206→• Details of training format (e.g., online, face-to-face, blended), supervision
   207→structures, and workforce development supports where reported.
   208→Extracted data were analysed using thematic synthesis. An initial stage of open
   209→coding was undertaken independently by two reviewers. Codes were compared and
   210→grouped into candidate themes relating to training provision, capability needs, and
   211→structural barriers. Themes were iteratively refined through discussion within the
   212→research team to ensure conceptual coherence and alignment with the review aims.
   213→Critical appraisal of included sources
   214→Study quality was appraised using the Critical Appraisal Skills Programme
   215→(CASP) checklists (Critical Appraisal Skills Programme, 2018), selecting the checklist
   216→most appropriate to source type. Eight qualitative studies were appraised using the
   217→CASP Qualitative Checklist and two review papers using the CASP Systematic Review
   218→Checklist. The remaining sources were non-standard evidence types (e.g. editorial,
   219→commentary, learning report, survey, and mixed-methods realist or implementation
   220→evaluations) and were therefore appraised narratively rather than forced into an
   221→inappropriate CASP template. Appraisal outcomes are summarised in supplementary
   222→material (S2) and were used to inform interpretation rather than to exclude studies.

   403→The evidence base was UK-heavy: 13 of the 17 included sources were UK-based
   404→or UK-focused, including nine drawing directly on English data. The remaining four
   405→sources comprised one Australian qualitative study, one Singaporean implementation
   406→update, one realist evaluation spanning Italy and Portugal, and one US implementation
   407→evaluation. Across primary care, third-sector, community-based, hospital, and
   408→specialist service settings, most sources were qualitative and explored link worker
   409→experiences, with fewer examining formal training interventions or wider
   410→implementation infrastructure. Across these varied contexts, direct qualitative and
   411→realist evidence described LW work as extending beyond community navigation and
   412→connection to sustained relational support, navigation of social determinants, and
   413→management of complexity, particularly in English primary care, Australian link worker
   414→practice, youth-focused settings, and community-based social prescribing for adults
   415→
   416→with long-term conditions (Bertotti et al., 2025; Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell,
   417→2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al., 2024; Wildman et al., 2019). Findings are
   418→presented across two sections: training needs (Themes 1–3) and barriers to effective
   419→training and delivery (Themes 4–5).
   420→Training needs
   421→1) Experiential learning required by relational and emotional demands
   422→Across the literature, LWs described a form of practice that is relationally
   423→intensive and emotionally demanding, particularly when supporting people
   424→experiencing distress, trauma, or extended periods of waiting for mental health care. In
   425→this context, training was frequently framed as most useful when it was experiential and
   426→relational, rather than solely informational. Supervision, mentoring, and structured
   427→spaces for reflection were repeatedly described as mechanisms through which LWs
   428→learn to manage complex cases, regulate emotional impact, and sustain professional
   429→boundaries (Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022).
   430→Wildman et al. (2019) similarly showed that link workers viewed their role as more
   431→intensive than simple signposting, particularly where clients needed support with
   432→immediate social concerns such as poverty, food insecurity, housing, welfare, and
   433→other wider determinants before they could focus on health improvement. These
   434→supports were not always labelled as “training” by participants, but functioned as core
   435→learning infrastructure, facilitating practice development and reducing isolation in roles
   436→that can involve lone working or dispersed teams (Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Tredinnick-
   437→Rowe et al., 2024).
   438→Several studies highlighted supervision as both protective and developmental. In
   439→settings where reflective or clinically informed supervision was limited, accounts
   440→described increased emotional strain, boundary pressure, or isolation, with concerns
   441→raised about sustainability where experiential learning was not built into service models
   442→(Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al., 2024). Westlake et al.
   443→(2024) further conceptualised “holding” as an often-unacknowledged component of
   444→LW practice, where practitioners provide ongoing emotional containment and
   445→
   446→continuity for service users navigating distress or service delays. In this framing, limited
   447→structured supervision was associated with unsupported emotional labour and
   448→concerns about burnout and retention (Westlake et al., 2024). Alongside supervision,
   449→shadowing and co-working were described as practical forms of situated learning,
   450→although access depended on whether services had sufficient staffing density or
   451→opportunities for co-location (Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Tredinnick-Rowe et al., 2024).
   452→Mode of delivery also mattered. LWs expressed a preference for face-to-face
   453→training for sensitive areas such as mental health and suicide awareness, emphasising
   454→the value of interactive learning, discussion, and confidence-building for real-time
   455→scenarios (Rhodes & Bell, 2021). Although not a workforce training study, Dadswell and
   456→Bungay (2025) offered complementary insight into relational, community-embedded
   457→work over time, and described peer-led and outreach-based mechanisms that may
   458→help practitioners develop relational skills through doing, observing, and adapting
   459→within communities.
   460→2) Core capability development for mental health-focused social prescribing
   461→While Theme 1 focuses on the relational and experiential learning opportunities
   462→through which link workers develop and sustain practice, Theme 2 identifies the
   463→specific capability domains required to undertake mental health-focused social
   464→prescribing safely and effectively. Across the literature, these capability needs
   465→clustered around three linked areas: interpersonal and communication skills for
   466→engagement and safe disclosure; emotional and boundary-management capabilities
   467→for sustaining practice; and mental health, safeguarding, and risk-management
   468→capabilities for responding appropriately to complexity.
   469→Beyond system navigation, studies consistently described the LW role as
   470→requiring advanced interpersonal and mental health-related capabilities.
   471→Communication was positioned as central to engagement, trust, and safe disclosure,
   472→particularly where service users present with trauma, anxiety, or complex psychosocial
   473→stressors (Bertotti et al., 2025; Hayes et al., 2024; Sharman et al., 2022). Across
   474→qualitative studies, LWs described the value of empathic listening, trust-building, and
   475→
   476→relationally attuned conversations that moved beyond transactional or customer-
   477→service style interactions (Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Bertotti et al.,
   478→2025; Wildman et al., 2019). Youth-focused work amplified these relational
   479→requirements, with evidence emphasising sustained engagement, coaching and
   480→mentoring approaches, and the need to adapt communication to developmental needs
   481→and complex life circumstances (Bertotti et al., 2025; Hayes et al., 2024).
   482→A recurring capability gap related to boundary-setting and self-care. Studies
   483→described tension between being sufficiently personable and supportive while
   484→maintaining professional limits, particularly where LWs were drawn into prolonged
   485→support in under-resourced systems or while service users waited for other care
   486→(Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al., 2024; Wildman et al.,
   487→2019). Wildman et al. (2019), a qualitative study in England, specifically highlighted
   488→boundary-setting as a key challenge, with link workers needing to manage the risk of
   489→client dependency and emotional over-involvement while maintaining a trusting and
   490→supportive relationship. Additionally, a UK primary-care commentary and qualitative
   491→studies suggested that training addressing boundaries and emotional self-care was
   492→important but inconsistently provided, particularly given the risk of over-involvement in
   493→emotionally intense situations (Frostick & Bertotti, 2019; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman
   494→et al., 2022; Wildman et al., 2019).
   495→Mental health literacy and risk navigation featured as a prominent training need
   496→across the evidence base. Studies referenced Mental Health First Aid and related
   497→training as useful starting points but also indicated a need for deeper preparation for
   498→managing complexity, recognising when a case is beyond remit, and understanding
   499→thresholds and escalation pathways (Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021;
   500→Sharman et al., 2022). Hazeldine et al. (2021) highlighted concern that LWs may be
   501→referred individuals with complex or crisis-level mental health needs despite lacking
   502→clinical training, suggesting that training needs include not only skills but also system
   503→knowledge about role remit and onward referral. For children and young people,
   504→safeguarding and safety planning were highlighted as particularly salient, requiring
   505→developmentally appropriate guidance and procedures rather than reliance on adult-
   506→
   507→focused materials (Hayes et al., 2024). In this way, core capability needs spanned
   508→interpersonal practice, emotional sustainability, mental health literacy, risk navigation,
   509→and safeguarding- or escalation-related knowledge.
   510→Across studies, training needs were reported to differ across service contexts
   511→and populations, particularly where link workers are supporting children and young
   512→people or individuals with more complex mental health needs, raising questions about
   513→the adequacy of generic training approaches (Hazeldine et al., 2021; Hayes et al.,
   514→2024).
   515→3) Building contextual and equity-focused competence
   516→Across studies, training needs encompassed contextual literacy about the
   517→social determinants of health and practical issues that often underpin mental distress.
   518→LWs described the relevance of knowledge in areas such as housing, welfare and
   519→benefits, immigration, domestic violence, addiction, and broader socio-economic
   520→stressors, emphasising that mental health support in SP frequently requires holistic
   521→navigation rather than symptom-focused guidance (Bertotti et al., 2025; Rhodes & Bell,
   522→2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Wildman et al., 2019). Wildman et al. (2019) showed that
   523→link workers required further preparation in benefits, housing, community development,
   524→motivational interviewing, mental health, and long-term conditions, reflecting the
   525→breadth of issues encountered before clients could meaningfully engage with health or
   526→wellbeing goals. This point aligns with review-level evidence highlighting that social
   527→prescribing often operates at the intersection of clinical and social complexity,
   528→requiring structured needs assessment, holistic care planning, and the ability to
   529→connect individual distress with wider material, relational, and community contexts
   530→(Melam et al., 2025; Yadav et al., 2024).
   531→Equity and inclusion considerations were especially visible in studies focusing
   532→on marginalised groups. Dadswell and Bungay (2025) highlighted how stigma, low
   533→confidence, language barriers, and cultural meanings attached to the concepts of
   534→“mental health” and “activities” can shape engagement, suggesting that training must
   535→include culturally responsive practice and strategies for reducing exclusion (Dadswell &
   536→
   537→Bungay, 2025). Rafiei et al. (2025) similarly emphasised the boundary-spanning nature
   538→of LW work and the need for training to reflect cross-sector complexity and cultural
   539→brokerage for groups experiencing stigma or access barriers (Rafiei et al., 2025). In
   540→youth contexts, Bertotti et al. (2025) described relational work with young people not in
   541→education, employment or training (NEET) facing layered social and emotional
   542→challenges, reinforcing that contextual competence is central to engagement and
   543→sustained support (Bertotti et al., 2025).
   544→Several studies also highlighted population-specific and emerging domains. A
   545→learning report on social prescribing for older people in the UK facing financial hardship
   546→described targeted training to build confidence and competence in addressing financial
   547→insecurity with older adults, including practical, scenario-based approaches to
   548→sensitive conversations about entitlements and poverty (Sewel, 2025). Meanwhile, a
   549→Singapore implementation update highlighted digital literacy and digital inclusion as
   550→emerging social determinants, describing how “Wellbeing Coordinators” supported
   551→older adults to engage with essential digital tools, thereby broadening the domain of
   552→contextual competence relevant to social prescribing practice (Nah et al., 2024).
   553→As such, across studies, training needs included both core contextual
   554→knowledge and the ability to work flexibly with inequalities, cultural contexts, and
   555→population-specific requirements.
   556→Barriers
   557→4) Role ambiguity and referral mismatch
   558→A central barrier identified across the literature was ambiguity in expectations of
   559→LWs and understanding of SP across systems. Multiple sources described a mismatch
   560→between job descriptions and day-to-day reality, with LWs reporting that they moved
   561→from anticipated “signposting” to more intensive relational support, advocacy, and
   562→coordination, often with limited preparatory guidance (Frostick & Bertotti, 2019;
   563→Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022). These discrepancies
   564→contributed to feelings of being underprepared and increased perceived professional
   565→
   566→risk, particularly where escalation pathways and responsibility boundaries were
   567→unclear (Hayes et al., 2024; Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021).
   568→Role ambiguity was compounded by referral mismatch. Qualitative studies and
   569→a UK primary-care commentary described concerns that referrers, particularly in
   570→primary care, may not fully understand LW remit, leading to referral of people with
   571→needs exceeding what SP services can safely provide without enhanced training and
   572→support (Frostick & Bertotti, 2019; Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman
   573→et al., 2022; Wildman et al., 2019). Hazeldine et al. (2021) suggested that this mismatch
   574→may be partly obscured where LWs happen to have prior mental health experience,
   575→while the system as a whole lacks consistent training standards. Calls for referrer
   576→education and shared understanding appeared as a recurring strand, linking role clarity
   577→to both training needs and risk management (Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al.,
   578→2022; Tredinnick-Rowe et al., 2024; Wildman et al., 2019)
   579→Role confusion was also evident at community level. Dadswell and Bungay
   580→(2025) described blurred boundaries between formal SP roles and informal community
   581→support, with uncertainty about terminology and responsibilities, indicating that the
   582→“LW role” can be interpreted differently across settings and sectors (Dadswell &
   583→Bungay, 2025). Rafiei et al. (2025) similarly described cross-sector confusion involving
   584→primary care, third-sector partners, and policymakers, which can complicate both
   585→implementation and training design. Across studies, training barriers were described as
   586→extending beyond individual-level capability gaps to include a lack of shared system-
   587→wide role definitions and expectations.
   588→5) Training systems are fragmented, variable, and under-resourced
   589→A further barrier concerned the structure and resourcing of training itself.
   590→Qualitative studies and review-level sources described training access and quality as
   591→shaped by funding constraints, local variation, and inconsistent organisational
   592→priorities, resulting in uneven preparation and limited opportunities for ongoing
   593→professional development (Melam et al., 2025; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al.,
   594→2022; Wildman et al., 2019). In Rhodes and Bell (2021), LWs described mandatory
   595→
   596→training as limited or overly generic, while voluntary sector settings were sometimes
   597→perceived to offer more flexible options but still faced resource constraints and
   598→misalignment between training provision and role demands. Similarly, Wildman et al.
   599→(2019) found that generic health trainer training did not adequately prepare some link
   600→workers for the practical complexity and severity of the issues they encountered in
   601→practice. Sharman et al. (2022) reported that resource scarcity could restrict training
   602→and development opportunities, particularly within insecure or short-term funding
   603→arrangements.
   604→Mode and format of training delivery also acted as a barrier. Some training was
   605→described as procedural or “tick-box,” with participants perceiving online modules as
   606→less helpful for complex or sensitive topics than interactive face-to-face delivery
   607→(Rhodes & Bell, 2021). Lack of protected time for training and reflection was an implicit
   608→constraint across accounts describing heavy caseloads, lone working, and limited
   609→access to supervision, amplifying the gap between training content and the lived
   610→realities of practice (Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al., 2024).
   611→Finally, several sources highlighted limitations in training frameworks,
   612→particularly for work with specific populations such as children and young people.
   613→Hayes et al. (2024) described the need for youth-specific guidance and procedures
   614→rather than adapting adult materials, while Rafiei et al. (2025) emphasised the value of
   615→co-produced and context-sensitive approaches to reduce variation in training and
   616→implementation across settings.
   617→Separate but related concerns were raised around governance and
   618→implementation support. Rafiei et al. (2025) highlighted wider policy and system-level
   619→variability affecting how social prescribing roles are implemented, while two
   620→implementation-focused sources outside the core LW evidence base pointed to the
   621→value of practical systems that support delivery. A US primary-care behavioural health
   622→implementation evaluation described the use of structured digital tools embedded into
   623→routine care processes to support assessment, referral, and follow-up, which were
   624→associated with improved provider confidence and reduced access barriers (Youn et
   625→al., 2025). A Singapore social prescribing implementation update similarly described
   626→
   627→digital referral systems and community asset-mapping resources designed to support
   628→coordination and reduce the burden on individual practitioners (Nah et al., 2024).
   629→Taken together, the qualitative, review, and implementation sources described a
   630→training landscape shaped by variability and resource constraints, with implications for
   631→workforce preparedness and sustainability.
   632→Discussion
   633→The central implication of this review is that the question is not simply whether
   634→link workers receive enough training, but whether current training models match the
   635→relational, emotionally demanding, and system-facing realities of mental health-
   636→focused social prescribing. Rather than treating training as a discrete set of modules,
   637→the evidence suggests it should be understood as part of the infrastructure that enables
   638→safe, sustainable, and context-responsive practice (Hazeldine et al., 2021; Melam et
   639→al., 2025; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al., 2024; Wildman et
   640→al., 2019). In doing so, this review extends earlier syntheses of social prescribing that
   641→have examined link worker roles, intervention components, implementation
   642→challenges, and broader experiences of practice (e.g. Linceviciute et al., 2023;
   643→Mulholland et al., 2025; Sandhu et al., 2022; Turk et al., 2024), by specifically drawing
   644→together evidence on training provision, capability gaps, and structural barriers in
   645→mental health-focused link worker practice. The implications therefore sit at
   646→practitioner, organisational, and system levels. At the same time, mental health-
   647→focused social prescribing is not a uniform category. Across the included sources, role
   648→intensity, governance arrangements, referral thresholds, and population needs varied
   649→substantially across settings, meaning that supervision, escalation procedures, and
   650→capability requirements are unlikely to be identical in every context.
   651→Reframing “training” as resourcing relational-support provision
   652→An important implication is that workforce development for link workers should
   653→be designed less around information transfer and more around supported judgement in
   654→practice. Preparation for mental health-focused social prescribing appears to depend
   655→
   656→on supervision, reflective space, mentoring, and opportunities to work through
   657→complexity with others, rather than relying on standardised modular training alone
   658→(Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al.,
   659→2024). Framed this way, training is not an optional supplement to service delivery but
   660→part of the service model itself, which is consistent with the recent Best Practice
   661→Guidelines for Child and Youth Social Prescribing, where supervision, peer support,
   662→protected time, and recognition of emotional labour are positioned as essential
   663→components of safe and sustainable practice (Hayes et al., 2024).
   664→Role ambiguity and referral mismatch as drivers of risk and emotional load
   665→Training needs cannot be addressed in isolation from the wider implementation
   666→environment. These findings should not be interpreted as suggesting that link workers
   667→replace clinical mental health professionals. Rather, they highlight the emotional and
   668→relational complexity that can arise within non-clinical roles when referral thresholds
   669→are high, services are delayed, or escalation routes are unclear. Viewed through
   670→emotion work and street-level bureaucracy lenses, the evidence suggests that some
   671→apparent “training gaps” reflect not only missing competencies, but the fact that link
   672→workers are often required to absorb uncertainty, emotional load, and discretionary
   673→decision-making generated elsewhere in the system (Erasmus, 2014; Zapf et al., 2021).
   674→The findings suggest that workforce development is unlikely to be sufficient if it focuses
   675→on link workers alone: it also requires clearer role definitions, referrer education,
   676→shared thresholds, explicit escalation routes, and appropriate clinical or safeguarding
   677→oversight, so that link workers are not left compensating for ambiguity in remit or
   678→limited service capacity (Hayes et al., 2024; Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021;
   679→Sharman et al., 2022; Tredinnick-Rowe et al., 2024).
   680→Contextual and equity-focused capability as central to relational-support work
   681→A further implication is that equitable workforce development cannot rely on
   682→individual link workers bringing prior mental health, cultural, or community expertise
   683→into post. When fragmented systems depend on personal background to compensate
   684→for inconsistent training, capability becomes uneven and service users are effectively
   685→
   686→left to be “lucky” in who they encounter. One potential approach would be to combine a
   687→standardised core training offer for all link workers with structured, needs-led
   688→additional training tailored to local populations and contexts, for example work with
   689→children and young people, culturally and linguistically diverse communities, poverty
   690→and welfare issues, or digital exclusion (Bertotti et al., 2025; Dadswell & Bungay, 2025;
   691→Hayes et al., 2024; Hazeldine et al., 2021; Nah et al., 2024; Rafiei et al., 2025). This
   692→would embed contextual competence systematically rather than leaving it to chance. It
   693→would also help services avoid over-reliance on ad hoc prior experience while ensuring
   694→that additional training is proportionate to local population need rather than expected
   695→universally across all roles.
   696→Practice and workforce implications at practitioner, organisational and system
   697→levels
   698→Practically, the findings point to a tiered response. At practitioner level, training
   699→may usefully prioritise communication for safe disclosure, boundary-setting, mental
   700→health literacy, risk navigation, motivational interviewing and interactive delivery
   701→formats reinforced through supervision rather than left as one-off online provision
   702→(Hazeldine et al., 2021; Rhodes & Bell, 2021; Sharman et al., 2022; Wildman et al.,
   703→2019; Yadav et al., 2024). At organisational level, services may need to build protected
   704→time, reflective practice, mentoring, and manageable caseloads into routine delivery,
   705→particularly where roles involve lone working or dispersed teams (Rhodes & Bell, 2021;
   706→Sharman et al., 2022; Westlake et al., 2024). At a system level, commissioners and
   707→service leaders may need to align referral criteria, role definitions, escalation routes,
   708→and local infrastructure, because training content alone will not resolve mismatch
   709→where governance, community capacity, and interprofessional understanding remain
   710→weak (Rafiei et al., 2025; Sharman et al., 2022; Tredinnick-Rowe et al., 2024). Taken
   711→together, these findings suggest that effective workforce development will require a
   712→layered model in which core competencies are reinforced through supervision and
   713→organisational support, while wider system conditions are aligned to reduce
   714→inappropriate demand and role drift.
   715→Limitations and future research
   716→
   717→As a systematic review, this synthesis provides a structured overview of the
   718→available evidence on training needs and barriers affecting link workers in social
   719→prescribing for mental health and wellbeing. Nevertheless, the predominance of
   720→qualitative studies means the field still lacks strong comparative evidence on which
   721→training models are most effective in improving practice and outcomes. In addition to
   722→qualitative papers, the evidence base reviewed also included a small number of review
   723→papers, implementation updates, a survey, grey literature, and commentary sources,
   724→which were useful for contextual and infrastructure insight, but did not all provide direct
   725→empirical evidence on LW training experiences. Although the review was global in
   726→scope, the included evidence base was weighted towards UK studies and a small
   727→number of other high-income settings, meaning the findings may not transfer directly to
   728→contexts with different social prescribing infrastructures, referral systems, workforce
   729→models, or governance arrangements.
   730→Future research should therefore move beyond describing training needs to
   731→evaluating whether different approaches, such as supervised experiential models,
   732→modular context-specific training, and protected-time workforce development, improve
   733→preparedness, retention, referral quality, boundary clarity, and service safety (Garside
   734→et al., 2025; Westlake et al., 2024). More work is also needed across different service,
   735→population, and national contexts to determine which training elements should be
   736→standardised across link worker roles, which should be locally tailored, and where the
   737→findings from this review may require adaptation rather than direct application (Bertotti
   738→et al., 2025; Hayes et al., 2024; Nah et al., 2024; Rafiei et al., 2025).
   739→Conclusion
   740→Across the included qualitative, review, and implementation sources, mental
   741→health and wellbeing-focused social prescribing was commonly described as involving
   742→relational-support roles shaped by complexity, constrained service environments, and
   743→uneven shared expectations about remit across sectors. The evidence suggests that
   744→strengthening LW training requires more than additional modules; it requires resourcing
   745→experiential learning infrastructure, including supervision, mentoring, reflective spaces,
   746→and protected time, alongside clearer role definition and referrer education to reduce
   747→
   748→mismatch and inappropriate referrals. Across the core qualitative and realist evidence,
   749→link workers’ practice in social prescribing often extended beyond supported
   750→connection to community resources into more open-ended relational-support work
   751→under conditions of long waiting lists, high eligibility thresholds, and under-resourcing.
   752→Training “gaps” are therefore not just missing learning opportunities; they reflect a
   753→misfit between a role characterised by intensive 1-to-1 relational support and a training
   754→infrastructure built for lighter-touch coordination. Addressing these issues is important
   755→for workforce sustainability, service safety, and effective delivery of social prescribing.
