    14→Abstract
    15→Objective. This study investigated the effectiveness of a newly implemented routine outcome
    16→monitoring and feedback system, the Greifswald Psychotherapy Navigation System (GPNS).
    17→Methods. 261 patients (M_age = 33 years, 79% female) completed individual cognitive-
    18→behavioral therapy (CBT) from 61 psychotherapists. Using Bayesian instrumental variable
    19→estimation, we estimated the local average treatment effect (LATE) of using the GPNS on
    20→post-treatment symptom severity and personality impairment. Sensitivity analyses were
    21→performed to estimate the robustness of our estimates.
    22→Results. Using the GPNS significantly reduced post-treatment symptom severity (LATE = -
    23→0.11, 95% CrI: -0.18; -0.03) and personality impairment (LATE = -2.65, 95% CrI: -3.72; -
    24→1.58). Sensitivity analyses showed that the effects were robust to plausible residual
    25→confounding.
    26→Conclusion. The GPNS shows promise for improving outcomes of CBT. Future prospective
    27→trials should be conducted to obtain a more generalizable estimate of its effect.
    28→
    29→Introduction
    30→Measurement-based care (MBC) is a practice that uses routine assessments of the
    31→patient’s perspective on important process and outcome variables, such as symptoms,
    32→functioning, the therapeutic alliance, engagement, or treatment motivation. These
    33→assessments have been implemented as comprehensive feedback systems that process the
    34→collected data and provide an easy interpretation using cut-off values or more sophisticated
    35→approaches, like expected treatment response curves based on similar patients . Based on the
    36→feedback, therapists can notice sudden improvements or deteriorations, a general lack of
    37→response, or approaching remission. They can collaboratively review the data with their
    38→patients, decide on adaptations of the treatment approach, and, in training settings, use it as a
    39→source of information during supervision. More advanced approaches utilize Clinical Support
    40→Tools (CSTs) which include evidence-based decision trees to enable the data-informed
    41→personalization of treatment. The effects of using feedback have been studied extensively.
    42→Current meta-analyses suggest that using feedback improves therapeutic outcomes and
    43→reduces dropout with small, but robust and additive effects ranging between g = 0.14 and g =
    44→0.15 without and g = 0.36 and g = 0.49 with CSTs compared to control conditions that did not
    45→use feedback (De Jong et al., 2021; Lambert et al., 2018).
    46→The Greifswald Psychotherapy Navigation System (GPNS; Demir et al., 2025) is a
    47→digital feedback system that uses set of brief weekly questionnaires on symptom severity, the
    48→therapeutic relationship and insight/understanding. Before the beginning of the session,
    49→patients fill out a transdiagnostic symptom scale, a suicidality screening as well as a set of
    50→questions about therapy-related processes in between sessions. After the session, patients fill
    51→out a process scale that measures the therapeutic relationship as well as the extent of insight
    52→and understanding of their progress. At the beginning of the session, therapists and patients
    53→can collaboratively review the current symptom severity, as well as the relationship and
    54→insight/understanding reported after the last session, via a brief feedback screen. Depending
    55→on the therapist’s clinical judgment, the patient’s experiences and preferences, as well as the
    56→results of their collaborative review, the data can be used to modify the course of therapy.
    57→The GPNS was designed with a modular structure to allow empirical testing of newly
    58→implemented components and to advance understanding of possible working mechanisms.
    59→Evaluating the effects of implementing a new practice in each clinical context has
    60→various advantages. On the one hand, it allows the intervention to be examined under the
    61→same conditions under which it is intended to operate. Effectiveness studies conducted in
    62→
    63→naturalistic settings capture the variability and complexity of patients, therapists, and
    64→organizational factors that characterize routine care, but are typically strongly constrained in
    65→clinical trials (Barkham & Margison, 2007; Castonguay et al., 2015). On the other hand, the
    66→stronger collaboration between researchers and practitioners allows for accompanying
    67→effectiveness studies by qualitative research on how therapists and patients experience the
    68→change, how they interpret and use the additional information, and whether their clinical
    69→decision-making changes over time (Låver et al., 2024).
    70→From a methodological perspective, the gradual rollout succeeded by a hard
    71→policy change also creates opportunities for quasi-experimental evaluation. As is often the
    72→case in naturalistic studies, the non-random assignment of patients to use the GPNS was
    73→guided by therapist self-selection. While this challenges causal identification of treatment
    74→effects, the policy shift from voluntary to mandatory use that was based on administrative
    75→policy rather than clinical criteria results in a “natural experiment” that can help separate the
    76→effects of the GPNS itself from those of selection or therapist enthusiasm. This allows us to
    77→preserve the ecological validity of practice-based outcome studies while allowing us to
    78→identify and estimate causal effects under plausible conditions (Shadish et al., 2002). Thus
    79→far, the effectiveness of the GPNS has not been evaluated.
    80→In this study, we used an instrumental variable approach to estimate the causal effect
    81→of using the GPNS in routine cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) on symptom severity and
    82→personality functioning. With these results, future research can disentangle the effects of
    83→different components of the GPNS, as the effect of GPNS and the effects of potential new
    84→components (such as newly developed CSTs) can be tested separately.

   138→Intervention
   139→All patients received individual, non-standardized cognitive behavioral therapy. The
   140→exact therapeutic techniques varied depending on the diagnosis and case conceptualization.
   141→Cognitive and behavioral therapy for depression, exposure therapy for anxiety and post-
   142→traumatic disorders, the Unified Protocol for emotional disorders, as well as various third-
   143→wave approaches such as Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) or Cognitive-
   144→Behavioral Analytic System of Psychotherapy (CBASP) are frequently used.
   145→In the intervention group, patients were treated while their therapists had access to the GPNS.
   146→After the GPNS became available, an online training course by the authors was provided to
   147→
   148→all therapists. The course included guidance for interpretation of the weekly assessments and
   149→possible strategies for “not on track” cases following basic recommendations for feedback
   150→systems (Harmon et al., 2004). Courses were accessible throughout the study.
   151→The sample was divided into two groups: patients who were treated with CBT while
   152→the GPNS was in use (“GPNS group”, n = 175) and a “treatment as usual” (TAU) group who
   153→were treated without GPNS (n = 86). Of the GPNS group, 157 patients were treated while
   154→using the system after it became mandatory, and 18 before that. Of the TAU group, 80 were
   155→not treated while using the GPNS before it was mandatory, and six after that. Thus, in total,
   156→24 non-compliers (9.2%) were observed.
   157→Instrumental Variable Design
   158→Instrumental variable (IV) designs allow for the estimation of causal effects in the
   159→presence of non-random treatment assignment or unmeasured confounding, which is a
   160→common challenge in naturalistic psychotherapy research. IV addresses the problem of
   161→confounding using an instrument - a variable that is associated with the exposure to
   162→treatment, not directly associated with the outcome, except through its effect on treatment,
   163→and not associated with unmeasured confounders of the treatment-outcome relationship
   164→(Angrist et al., 1996). In this study, we exploited a change of policy that mandated the use of
   165→the GPNS feedback system for all patients starting their therapies on or after December 1,
   166→2021. This date was determined for administrative and logistical reasons unrelated to this
   167→evaluation. This cutoff serves as a natural experiment: while patients initiating treatment
   168→before this date could still receive GPNS at their therapist's discretion, patients entering after
   169→this date were required to receive it. We define an indicator variable that distinguishes
   170→between patients treated before vs. after this mandate and use it as an instrument for actual
   171→GPNS use.
   172→The IV approach estimates the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE), that is, the
   173→causal effect of using the GPNS for the subgroup of patients whose treatment assignment was
   174→influenced by the policy change. In IV terminology, these patients are referred to as
   175→“compliers”. They received the GPNS because it became mandatory and would not have
   176→received it otherwise. The LATE is different from the average treatment effect (ATE)
   177→because it only applies to this subgroup. While this limits the generalizability of the estimate,
   178→it does not require the strong assumptions that all confounders of treatment and outcome are
   179→controlled.
   180→
   181→Assumptions
   182→Our study satisfies the assumptions for valid IV designs under three conditions. First,
   183→relevance: the instrument must be strongly associated with treatment assignment. In our case,
   184→this would mean that the policy change mandating GPNS use significantly increased the
   185→probability that patients received feedback, which is empirically testable via the first-stage
   186→model. Second, we must assume monotonicity: no patients (or therapists) exist who
   187→systematically do the opposite of what the instrument suggests (i.e., no "defiers" who used
   188→GPNS only when it was not mandated and stopped using it once it became mandatory). This
   189→assumption is not testable, but plausible in our context because the mandate required GPNS
   190→use for new patients, and there is no theoretical reason to expect therapists would
   191→systematically avoid compliance in response to the policy. Third, we assume the exclusion
   192→restriction holds: the policy variable must not affect outcomes except through its effect on
   193→GPNS usage. This is plausible because the policy was introduced for administrative reasons
   194→unrelated to changes in patient characteristics or therapeutic procedures, and the mandate
   195→itself did not alter any other aspect of treatment delivery beyond enabling access to the
   196→feedback system.
   197→Outcomes
   198→Our primary research question was whether using the GPNS leads to an improvement
   199→in therapy outcomes as measured by post-treatment symptom severity. For this, we used the
   200→total score (“Global Severity Index”, GSI) of the German version of the Brief Symptom
   201→Inventory (Franke, 2000), which is a well-established transdiagnostic self-report instrument
   202→for a wide variety of problem areas. The internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha) of the BSI in
   203→this sample was 0.95, the hierarchical McDonald’s omega was 0.66, and the total
   204→McDonald’s omega was 0.96. As a secondary outcome, the German version of the Level of
   205→Personality Functioning Scale Brief Form 2.0 (LPFS-BF 2.0; Spitzer et al., 2021), a self-
   206→report instrument, was included. This scale is a self-report measure of the core aspects of
   207→personality disorders, as defined in the alternative model of personality disorders (AMPD) of
   208→the DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). We chose personality functioning as
   209→an additional outcome as it was found to be predictive of the long-term stability of changes
   210→achieved in psychotherapy (Huber et al., 2017). The internal consistency of the LPFS-BF 2.0
   211→was alpha = 0.84, the hierarchical McDonald’s omega was 0.57, and the total omega was
   212→0.86.

   214→Statistical analysis
   215→To estimate the causal effect of GPNS feedback on clinical outcomes, we used a
   216→Bayesian instrumental variable (IV) approach implemented via the {brms} package in R
   217→(Bürkner, 2017). The model jointly estimates a first-stage treatment equation predicting
   218→GPNS use and second-stage outcome equations for post-treatment symptom severity (GSI)
   219→and personality functioning (LPFS) within a single hierarchical Bayesian framework. This
   220→joint estimation propagates uncertainty from the treatment model to the outcome models. The
   221→binary treatment variable (1 = GPNS used; 0 = no GPNS used) was modeled as a function of
   222→the policy-mandated rollout indicator (1 = GPNS use mandatory; 0 = not mandatory), as well
   223→as baseline GSI and LPFS scores and their interactions with the treatment variable. The
   224→rollout indicator serves as the instrument, identifying variation in treatment uptake
   225→attributable to the policy assignment rather than individual patient characteristics. The
   226→outcome models included GPNS use, the same baseline covariates, and random intercepts for
   227→therapists to account for clustering. In addition, we allowed subject-level random intercepts to
   228→be correlated across treatment and outcome equations, capturing shared latent heterogeneity
   229→after conditioning on observed covariates. This specification permits residual dependence
   230→between treatment propensity and outcomes while identifying the causal effect through
   231→variation induced by the instrument. Figure 1 depicts the assumed causal model for this study.

   238→All models were estimated using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) with four
   239→chains, 1000 warmup draws, and 4000 iterations per chain. Because the model includes
   240→interactions between treatment and baseline severity, the regression coefficient for the
   241→treatment variable no longer captures a single, directly interpretable quantity. Following
   242→Rohrer and Arel-Bundock (2026), we derive a clearly defined target quantity rather than
   243→interpreting coefficients directly. Specifically, we compute the local average treatment effect
   244→(LATE) by generating counterfactual predictions for each observed unit under treatment and
   245→control conditions and averaging the differences across the sample. This quantity can be
   246→interpreted as the average effect of GPNS usage among compliers, i.e., patients whose
   247→treatment assignment was influenced by the policy change, averaged over the observed
   248→distribution of baseline severity. Uncertainty was quantified using the posterior distribution of
   249→the Bayesian model. All marginal effects were computed using the marginaleffects package
   250→for R (Arel-Bundock et al., 2024).
   251→
   252→Basic standardized mean differences (SMDs) were obtained by dividing the effect
   253→estimates by the standard deviations of the pre-treatment values of the outcome variables. As
   254→the GPNS usage variable was binary, we modeled it using the Bernoulli distributional family
   255→with a logit link function. The post-treatment GSI score was skewed due to floor effects, so
   256→that we modeled it using a skewed normal distribution. Posterior predictive checks were used
   257→to ascertain the adequacy of the chosen distributional families.
   258→Despite the use of an instrumental variable design, residual bias remains possible if
   259→the core IV assumptions are violated. The independence assumption - that the policy cutoff
   260→date is unrelated to patient outcomes except through GPNS use - is supported by the
   261→administrative rationale for the December 2021 date, which was determined by logistical
   262→rather than clinical considerations. Nevertheless, unmeasured time-varying confounders (e.g.,
   263→seasonal intake patterns, concurrent clinical changes) could hypothetically introduce a
   264→residual association between the instrument and outcomes. Similarly, the exclusion restriction
   265→could be violated if the mandate affected outcomes through pathways beyond GPNS use
   266→itself, such as broader changes in therapist monitoring behavior or accompanying
   267→implementation activities. To assess the robustness of our estimates to such violations, we
   268→conducted a sensitivity analysis quantifying how large a residual correlation between the
   269→instrument and outcomes would need to be to nullify our findings. Specifically, we examined
   270→how varying degrees of residual latent dependence between the treatment propensity (as
   271→modeled in the first-stage equation) and the post-treatment outcome scores (GSI and LPFS)
   272→would affect the posterior distribution of the treatment effect. We introduced a sensitivity
   273→parameter (ρ) representing the correlation between latent subject-level propensities for
   274→treatment and outcome after conditioning on observed covariates in both equations. Because
   275→residual correlations are not directly defined for non-Gaussian outcomes, this correlation is
   276→defined on the latent (linear predictor) scale. For each value of ρ, we adjusted posterior draws
   277→of the treatment effect using a linear bias approximation:

   391→Discussion
   392→In routine care settings, it can be challenging to estimate the effects of newly
   393→introduced procedures. Using a study design that is relatively new to psychotherapy research,
   394→we could show that adding a psychometric feedback system to usual treatment at the
   395→outpatient clinic improved treatment outcomes. The Bayesian modeling approach showed to
   396→
   397→have some benefits over more traditional implementations like Two-Stage Least-Squares
   398→(2SLS) regression, as it allowed us to include multiple outcomes and to model residual
   399→correlations between outcomes and the treatment variable, thus allowing for sensitivity
   400→analyses. For the BSI-GSI outcome, the estimated effect is robust to small levels of residual
   401→latent confounding and would require moderately strong confounding to be fully attenuated.
   402→For LPFS, the posterior mean effect is highly robust, requiring strong latent confounding to
   403→be reduced to zero, whereas interval estimates are more sensitive, requiring moderate residual
   404→confounding to include zero.
   405→For clinical practice, these results imply a benefit of institutional mandates to use
   406→feedback. However, considering the effect of therapist attitude on the association of feedback
   407→with improved therapeutic outcomes (Lutz et al., 2021), institutional mandates should be
   408→accompanied by sufficient background information regarding the benefits of feedback. This
   409→might counter potential biases and reduce the research-practice gap. The influence of
   410→therapist expectations on the frequency and effectiveness of feedback usage has been
   411→proposed as a plausible effect modifier (Herzog et al., 2023), empirical research on this
   412→hypothesis is still lacking. Other working mechanisms might be plausible as well. Recently,
   413→using a single-case experimental design, Demir et al. (2025) demonstrated that the therapeutic
   414→alliance improved after introducing brief, structured discussions of PROM results at the
   415→beginning of therapy sessions. This further substantiates results from a mediation analysis
   416→(Brattland et al., 2019). A qualitative study on patient-therapist dyads using the GPNS
   417→suggested that clinicians might indeed underestimate the benefits and relevance of feedback,
   418→while on the patient side, themes of increased self-awareness, reflection, and understanding
   419→emerged. Future research could focus on measures to predict feedback usage and based on
   420→these results, develop suitable interventions to increase effective feedback usage.
   421→While the effects on symptom severity are in line with meta-analytic findings, the
   422→effect on personality functioning exceeds them, with interaction effects indicating an
   423→increased benefit for patients with more severe personality impairment. Currently, there is
   424→little empirical evidence for the effect of feedback systems on this construct. Some evidence
   425→suggests that patients with certain personality disorders benefit less from PROM systems (de
   426→Jong et al., 2018) while Demir et al. (2025) did not find that personality functioning
   427→moderates the effects of the GPNS on the therapeutic alliance. One potential explanation
   428→might be that increases in self-awareness and insight (Demir, Wrase, et al., 2025) specifically
   429→help patients who show a lack of self-knowledge (Kaiser, 2026).
   430→
   431→Some limitations of our study need to be acknowledged. Firstly, our study was not
   432→preregistered and merely took advantage of a natural experiment for evaluation purposes.
   433→Second, the LATE only applies to patients whose therapists adhered to the policy change in
   434→their cases. While compliance was satisfactory and defiance unlikely, caution is advised when
   435→generalizing to other treatment settings. Third, the limited sample size affects the precision of
   436→parameter estimates and thus the certainty of our findings. Furthermore, due to the setting, the
   437→study population consisted mostly of younger and highly educated outpatients, limiting the
   438→generalizability of these results to older, more diverse and higher impaired populations or
   439→inpatient settings. In future research, the causal effect of the GPNS should be evaluated in
   440→other clinics, settings and with other forms of psychotherapy. Randomized controlled trials
   441→should further substantiate our findings.
   442→To conclude, GPNS feedback appears to cause an additional symptom reduction and
   443→improvement in personality functioning in routine CBT when implemented via a clinic-wide
   444→policy change. Future research should explore working mechanisms especially in patients
   445→with personality impairment, and validate the findings in prospective, randomized controlled
   446→trials.
