    21→Abstract
    22→The Russo-Ukrainian War (RUW) led to encompassing and detrimental
    23→consequences for millions of Ukrainian civilians abroad and in their war-torn homeland.
    24→Recent representative studies found considerable psychological consequences, suggesting
    25→high prevalences of mental disorders, and war-related distress alike. Continuous traumatic
    26→stress (CTS) may constitute a valid construct describing psychopathological symptoms such
    27→as depression, anxiety, PTSD, and DSO in war-affected Ukrainian civilians. This study
    28→provides modest empirical evidence to the construct of CTS and the psychological distress
    29→Ukrainian civilians face following the RUW. In the cross-sectional online study N = 113
    30→datasets of Ukrainian civilians (M = 40 years; 87.6% female) in Austria (n = 67) and Ukraine
    31→(n = 46) were collected in 2025. Associations of CTS with psychopathological symptoms, the
    32→influence of emotion dysregulation (ED) on these associations, and differences in symptom
    33→levels between two subsamples were examined. CTS predicted between 28% and 47% of
    34→the variance in psychopathological symptoms. Preliminary findings showed small moderation
    35→effects of ED on the association between CTS and psychopathological symptoms. Contrary
    36→to hypotheses, participants in Austria reported higher symptom levels than participants in
    37→Ukraine. Exploratory analyses revealed high prevalences of questionnaire-derived diagnoses
    38→throughout the sample. Findings support the construct of CTS and its clinical value. Empirical
    39→evidence indicates higher psychological distress in internationally displaced Ukrainian
    40→civilians. High associations between CTS, ED, and psychopathological symptoms highlight
    41→the need for qualitative and longitudinal data. Implications for trauma-focused research are
    42→discussed.
    43→Keywords: continuous traumatic stress, psychopathology, Ukrainian civilians, Russo-
    44→Ukrainian War, cross-sectional study

    49→Psychological Consequences of the Russo-Ukrainian War
    50→The full-scale Russian invasion of Ukraine on 24th of February 2022 marked an
    51→unprecedented event in recent European history and constitutes the first military attack on a
    52→European nation since the Second World War. The subsequent Russo-Ukrainian War (RUW)
    53→marks a turning point in global history and enfolded a vast array of political, economic,
    54→environmental, and humanitarian consequences (Hryhorczuk et al., 2024; Martsenkovskyi et
    55→al., 2024; Poberezhets, 2022). In 2024, the United Nations (UN) reported 6,813,900
    56→Ukrainian refugees globally (United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees [UNHCR],
    57→2024). As of January 2026, the UN verified a total of 55,600 civilian casualties (i.e., being
    58→injured or killed) since the full-scale Russian invasion in 2022 (Office of the High
    59→Commissioner for Human Rights [OHCHR], 2026). The data conveys the bleak reality of war
    60→and demonstrates civilian life as profoundly affected by war’s severe disruptions to society.
    61→News agencies frequently reported targeted destruction of civilian infrastructure and large-
    62→scale drone attacks during the RUW (Malenko, 2024; Reuters, 2024). Therefore, civilians in
    63→war-affected regions potentially experience war-related stressors such as hearing air raid
    64→sirens, witnessing artillery fire, destruction of own property or local infrastructure, invading
    65→military forces, relatives falling victim to war, financial hardship, or being forced to
    66→displacement (Karatzias et al., 2023). In consequence, war-affected Ukrainian civilians may
    67→continuously face traumatizing real-world threats to the own physical and psychological
    68→integrity. However, while information on the RUW may be widely accessible through news
    69→and media agencies, the psychological consequences of war, flight and displacement are
    70→potentially less overt.
    71→Recent research showed strong interest in investigating the psychological
    72→consequences in war-affected Ukrainians, resulting in a considerable amount of empirical
    73→data (Fornaro et al., 2025; Hyland et al., 2023; Karatzias et al., 2023; Levin et al., 2023;
    74→Martsenkovskyi et al., 2024). In a sample of N = 2,050 Ukrainian adults assessed in
    75→September 2023, 36.3% screened positive for any of seven psychological disorders, of which
    76→generalized anxiety disorder, prolonged grief disorder and post-traumatic stress disorder
    77→were the most prevalent (Martsenkovskyi et al., 2024). Furthermore, comorbidity of
    78→psychological disorders in the sample was high, and financial worries were reported to be a
    79→risk factor. Lastly, higher prevalences of psychological disorders were reported for people in
    80→Eastern parts of Ukraine, whereas rates were lower in Western Ukraine. Participants
    81→reported high prevalences of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and complex post-
    82→traumatic stress disorder (CPTSD).
    83→
    84→4
    85→Furthermore, in a nationwide sample of N = 2,004 Ukrainian parents evidence of a
    86→dose-response relationship between war-related stressors (e.g., hearing air raid sirens,
    87→witnessing destruction of infrastructure, experiencing financial hardship, being forced to
    88→displace) and meeting criteria for PTSD and CPTSD was reported (Karatzias et al., 2023). In
    89→this sample, Hyland et al. (2023) found a significant proportion of participants reporting
    90→worsened symptoms of anxiety (71.9%), depression (62.1%), and loneliness (46.8%)
    91→following the Russian invasion of Ukraine, with a dose-response effect correlating with
    92→increased mental health challenges. These findings highlight the considerable impact and
    93→psychological toll the RUW has had on Ukrainian civilians.
    94→Continuous Traumatic Stress
    95→These findings suggest that Ukrainian civilians experience ongoing traumatic stress,
    96→linked to real-world threats due to the RUW (Fornaro et al., 2025; Hyland et al., 2023;
    97→Karatzias et al., 2023; Levin et al., 2023; Martsenkovskyi et al., 2024). Continuous traumatic
    98→stress (CTS) is defined as the ongoing experience of extreme stressors with potentially
    99→traumatic value, due to real-world threats that shape the context of an individual in the
   100→present (Bachem & Maercker, 2024; Goral et al., 2021; Stevens et al., 2013; Straker, 2013).
   101→In PTSD and CPTSD exposure to traumatic stressors lies distinctly in the past, with
   102→psychological symptoms, e.g., re-experiencing traumatic events and avoidance behaviors,
   103→forming the current experience of individuals (World Health Organization [WHO], 2019). In
   104→contrast, CTS describes the psychological consequences of traumatic stressors, which are
   105→ongoing and currently shape the environment of affected individuals (Bachem & Maercker,
   106→2024; Goral et al., 2021; Stevens et al., 2013; Straker, 2013).
   107→The construct was first described in literature by Straker and the Sanctuaries
   108→Counselling Team (1987) during the Apartheid in South Africa. Originally rooted in civil
   109→conflict, it stemmed from observations of the psychological consequences civilians
   110→experienced in facing ongoing traumatic threats and violence in their daily lives. CTS had a
   111→political and societal dimension, describing contexts in which the state law was part of the
   112→problem and oppressive systems act on discriminated and disenfranchised individuals. This
   113→was later revised by Straker (2013) in an ambition to render the construct more valid in
   114→describing psychological consequences of ongoing traumatic stressors in further contexts.
   115→Straker (2013) proposed the context as defining feature of CTS and modified its definition to
   116→simply involving multiple extreme stressors that are current and encompassing. Since then,
   117→the construct of CTS was transferred to contexts of war-related stressors, as evident in
   118→military conflicts (Greene et al., 2018; Itzhaky et al., 2017; Levin et al., 2024; Zasiekina et al.,
   119→2024). Notably, CTS has shown meaningful associations with the core symptoms of PTSD
   120→and CPTSD (Goral et al., 2021; Greene et al., 2018; Lahav, 2020; Zasiekina et al., 2024).
   121→
   122→5
   123→PTSD is defined as a mental disorder resulting from exposure to an extremely
   124→threatening or horrific event or series of events. It is characterized by three core symptoms;
   125→vivid re-experiencing of traumatic events (i.e., intrusive memories, flashbacks, nightmares),
   126→avoidance of thoughts and memories related to traumatic events, and a persistent perception
   127→of heightened current threat (e.g., hypervigilance or enhanced startle reaction). The
   128→symptoms persist for at least several weeks and cause significant impairment in individual
   129→functioning (WHO, 2019).
   130→CPTSD is a disorder following exposure to an extremely threatening or horrific event
   131→or series of events, which most often represent prolonged or repetitive events from which
   132→escape is difficult or impossible (e.g., torture, repeated childhood abuse). It encompasses all
   133→diagnostic criteria of PTSD, with additional criteria of disturbances in self-organization (DSO).
   134→These symptoms are defined by three dimensions: problems in affect regulation, beliefs of
   135→the self as diminished (e.g., defeated, worthless, accompanied by feelings of shame, guilt or
   136→failure) related to the traumatic event, and difficulties in maintaining relationships and
   137→closeness to others. The symptoms must cause significant impairment in individual
   138→functioning (WHO, 2019).
   139→Studies also suggest that the symptomatology of CTS is more complex and
   140→comprises an array of additional symptoms (Goral et al., 2021; Greene et al., 2018; Lahav,
   141→2020; Zasiekina et al., 2024). Previous studies reported high associations of r = .67-.80
   142→between CTS and psychopathological symptoms, such as anxiety, depression, PTSD and
   143→DSO (Greene et al., 2018; Zasiekina et al., 2024). Mental health practitioners and clinicians
   144→have recognized the importance of empirical evidence on the construct of CTS, and it was
   145→discussed at the International Classification of Diseases conference of the WHO in 2015
   146→(Bachem & Maercker, 2024). However, empirical evidence on CTS and its associations with
   147→other mental health outcomes is still scarce and further research is needed to fill this
   148→research gap (Maercker & Brewin, 2024; Pat-Horenczyk & Schiff, 2019).
   149→Emotion Dysregulation and Psychopathology
   150→Furthermore, empirical evidence suggests that CTS is associated with emotion
   151→dysregulation (ED), such as emotional numbing or difficulties with impulse control (Goral et
   152→al., 2017; Hallion et al., 2018; Levin et al., 2023). Whereas emotion regulation can be
   153→described as the ability to modulate an emotion or a set of emotions and to employ behavior
   154→to act on a priori goals, ED describes maladaptive forms of modulating emotional responses
   155→(Gratz & Roemer, 2004; Gross, 2015). ED is a multifaceted construct encompassing a variety
   156→of different emotion regulation strategies, such as lack of awareness, understanding, or
   157→acceptance of emotional responses, lack of access to adaptive strategies for modulating
   158→emotions, impulse control difficulties, and inability to engage in goal-directed behaviors when
   159→experiencing emotional distress. Practical examples of these maladaptive emotion regulation
   160→
   161→6
   162→strategies may include emotional avoidance or suppression, rumination, denial, aggression,
   163→and venting (D’Agostino et al., 2017). ED was first described in borderline personality
   164→disorder and later established as a transdiagnostic and dimensional construct implicated in
   165→many different psychopathologies, including internalizing disorders, externalizing disorders,
   166→and psychotic disorders (Beauchaine & Cicchetti, 2019; Gratz & Roemer, 2004).
   167→Concordantly, studies in adult populations indicate that ED is instrumental to both
   168→development and maintenance of psychopathological symptoms, such as PTSD, bipolar
   169→disorder, and schizophrenia spectrum disorder (Carmassi et al., 2022). Further, ED
   170→potentially mediates associations of cumulative lifetime adversity on depressive symptoms in
   171→adulthood, with data suggesting that both ED and cumulative lifetime adversity may explain
   172→50% of variance in symptomatology (Abravanel & Sinha, 2015). These findings indicate ED
   173→to be implicated in Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) and Generalized Anxiety Disorder
   174→(GAD), which in turn are associated with CTS (Gratz & Roemer, 2004; Hallion et al., 2018).
   175→MDD is defined as depressive mood (e.g., irritable, empty) or loss of pleasure which
   176→are accompanied by cognitive (e.g., difficulty concentrating), behavioral (e.g., diminished
   177→interest in activities), or neurovegetative symptoms (e.g., changes in appetite or sleep) that
   178→significantly affect the individual’s ability to function (WHO, 2019). Single Episode Depressive
   179→Disorder is characterized by presentation of these symptoms most of the day, almost every
   180→day, for at least two weeks with no prior history of depressive episodes.
   181→GAD is defined by distinct symptoms of anxiety that persist for at least several
   182→months for more days than not, which are characterized by either general apprehension (i.e.
   183→‘free-floating anxiety’) or excessive worry concerning multiple everyday events. Additional
   184→symptoms include muscular tension, sympathetic autonomic over-activity, or subjective
   185→experience of nervousness. Additionally, these symptoms result in significant distress or
   186→impairment of the individual’s ability to function (WHO, 2019).
   187→Moreover, a systematic review of 34 studies found ED to potentially mediate
   188→vulnerability to PTSD, but also to predict the severity and maintenance of PTSD symptoms
   189→(Conti et al., 2023). In a prospective study, Pencea et al. (2020) showed ED to predict
   190→chronic PTSD development in a trauma-exposed adult sample, and suggested interventions
   191→to improve emotion regulation capabilities. Further, a clinical trial found that ED moderated
   192→treatment response of cognitive behavioral therapy for PTSD with co-occurring substance
   193→use disorder (Hien et al., 2017). In trauma-focused research, it was previously suggested
   194→that ED is also implicated in CTS reactions and trauma-related mental disorders (Cloitre et
   195→al., 2018; Dieujuste et al., 2025; Goral et al., 2017; Levin et al., 2023). Furthermore, existing
   196→literature demonstrate considerable associations of ED with psychopathology in
   197→heterogenous trauma-exposed adult populations (Blay et al., 2025; Carmassi et al., 2022;
   198→Seligowski et al., 2015). Therefore, findings suggest more severe psychopathological
   199→
   200→7
   201→symptomatology for higher levels of ED. While ED represents a growing field of research,
   202→further empirical evidence is needed to validate and specify effects of general ED in context
   203→of civilian trauma reactions and trauma-related mental disorders.
   204→Geographical Distance to the Conflict Zone and Mental Health
   205→The theoretical framework of CTS postulates that environments with ongoing
   206→traumatic stressors lead to CTS (Straker, 2013). Researchers also emphasize the
   207→importance of contexts and external safety for people currently affected by CTS (Fornaro et
   208→al., 2025; Itzhaky et al., 2017; Kaminer et al., 2016; Straker, 2013). Notably, under contexts
   209→of CTS, core symptoms of PTSD and CPTSD, e.g., hypervigilance and perceptions of
   210→heightened threat, may constitute a functional reaction to current threatful environments
   211→(Itzhaky et al., 2017). For instance, civilians in war-affected regions may only have seconds
   212→to seek shelter in response to emerging threats, such as missile strikes (Itzhaky et al., 2017).
   213→Notably, Kalaitzaki et al. (2024) found differences in mental health outcomes in
   214→relation to the geographical proximity to the RUW. Kalaitzaki et al. (2024) investigated mental
   215→health outcomes across 11 different countries including Ukraine, bordering countries (e.g.,
   216→Poland and Romania) and more distal ones (e.g., Italy, Peru). Importantly, Ukrainians
   217→reported the worst mental health indices, i.e., the highest levels of perceived stress, anxiety,
   218→depression, PTSD and CPTSD, and traumatic exposure (largest effect size; η2 = .336), while
   219→simultaneously reporting lowest resilience, compared to non-Ukrainians. People from
   220→countries near Ukraine were more frequently informed about the RUW, reported greater
   221→stress about the likelihood of war, and worse mental health outcomes due to war news and
   222→economic crisis, compared to those located further away from Ukraine (Kalaitzaki et al.,
   223→2024). These findings indicate that traumatic stress and its detrimental impacts on mental
   224→health due to the RUW may be mitigated by the distance to the conflict zone.
   225→However, this assumption remains controversial since there is also evidence that
   226→nondisplaced Ukrainian civilians report less psychological symptoms, i.e., anxiety, stress and
   227→depression, than displaced Ukrainian civilians due to the RUW (Fornaro et al., 2025). It is
   228→hypothesized that displaced people experienced threats to personal safety, financial security,
   229→damage to their environment, and uncertainties related to the war to a greater extent than
   230→those not willing or forced to resettle, which may encompass people whose safety was not
   231→that critically endangered (Fornaro et al., 2025).

   249→Methods
   250→Sampling Procedures
   251→This non-experimental cross-sectional study was conducted using an online
   252→questionnaire. Two independent ad-hoc samples of adult civilians from Ukraine were tested
   253→at a single time point from 7th of May until 30th of July 2025. Participants had to be civilians of
   254→Ukraine, at least 18 years of age, without history of mental disorders, and residing in Ukraine
   255→or in Austria, to be eligible for participation. Individuals without Ukrainian nationality, under
   256→the age of 18, former or current military personnel, and individuals with current diagnoses of
   257→mental disorders (e.g., PTSD, anxiety disorders) or prior psychiatric hospitalization, were
   258→excluded. If any exclusion criteria were met, participation was terminated and the participant
   259→was informed accordingly. No financial incentives were offered to the participants.
   260→Two independent ad-hoc samples of Ukrainian civilians were recruited. In total, 11
   261→organizations distributed the study’s flyer to eligible participants in Austria and Ukraine. In
   262→Austria, participant recruitment was conducted in collaboration with psychological counseling
   263→services for displaced Ukrainian civilians, and associations of emigrated Ukrainian civilians.
   264→Public counseling services such as Caritas, Diakonie, and HEMAYAT distributed the study in
   265→Vienna. Further, asylkoordination österreich distributed the study within a nationwide Austrian
   266→network of Ukrainian civilians who engaged in psychological counseling. In Ukraine,
   267→participants were recruited in collaboration with Ukrainian researchers from the Ukrainian
   268→Science Diaspora and the Ukrainian Psychotrauma Center (Research Unit at Lesya Ukrainka
   269→Volyn National University), disseminating the study within their respective networks. The
   270→study employed opportunistic sampling methods due to restricted resources, as controlled
   271→sampling methods were not feasible. In Ukraine, participants were recruited from six different
   272→geographical regions: Eastern Ukraine, Western Ukraine, Kyiv, Northern Ukraine, Central
   273→Ukraine, and Southern Ukraine.
   274→Measurement Instruments
   275→
   276→9
   277→All measurement instruments rely on self-reports and were provided in two
   278→languages, English and Ukrainian. The instruments were openly available and sourced from
   279→the International Trauma Consortium (ITC) (The International Trauma Consortium, 2024).
   280→Continuous Traumatic Stress Response Scale
   281→The original English-language Continuous Traumatic Stress Response (CTSR) scale
   282→developed by Goral et al. (2021) used a sample of 313 adults in Southern Israel exposed to
   283→ongoing security threats between late 2016 and early 2017. The measure with 11 items
   284→showed a distinct three-factor structure of Exhaustion and Detachment, Rage and Betrayal,
   285→as well as Fear and Helplessness. Internal consistency was reported to be 𝛼 = .90 (total
   286→scale), 𝛼 = .86 (Exhaustion and Detachment), 𝛼 = .82 (Rage and Betrayal), and 𝛼 = .74 (Fear
   287→and Helplessness). The CTSR scale by Zasiekina et al. (2024) is a Ukrainian-language
   288→cross-cultural adaptation and psychometric validation in a war-affected Ukrainian sample.
   289→Notably, the Ukrainian adaptation of the CTSR scale, using a sample of 619 participants who
   290→applied for psychosocial support in Ukraine in early 2023, showed a two-factor structure of
   291→Exhaustion and Rage, and Fear and Betrayal. The authors reported high internal consistency
   292→of 𝛼 = .86 for the total CTSR scale. Further, findings indicate satisfactory test-retest reliability,
   293→with no significant differences within participants’ scores after a one-month period, and
   294→concurrent construct validity as the scale was associated with trauma-related mental health
   295→symptoms, i.e., PTSD, depression, anxiety, and moral injury (Zasiekina et al., 2024).
   296→International Trauma Questionnaire
   297→The International Trauma Questionnaire (ITQ) from Cloitre et al. (2018) is an 18-item
   298→measurement instrument developed to assess PTSD and CPTSD according to ICD-11
   299→(WHO, 2019). The internal consistency was reported as satisfactory for all subscales of
   300→PTSD and DSO (𝛼 ≥ .79). Ho et al. (2023) confirmed the factor structure and internal
   301→consistency (𝛼’s = .73–.88) for all subscales of the ITQ in a sample of 2,004 adults from the
   302→general population of Ukraine six months after the full-scale Russian invasion in 2022.
   303→Additionally, a diagnostic algorithm in accordance with ICD-11 diagnostic criteria was
   304→implemented (WHO, 2019). Symptom ratings were determined clinically significant for
   305→responses of 3 (moderately), or higher (Cloitre et al. (2018). Diagnostic criteria for PTSD
   306→were met if at least one item from each of the three PTSD-cluster (i.e., re-experiencing in the
   307→here and now, avoidance, sense of threat) exceeded clinical cut-offs, and functional
   308→impairment was reported in at least one domain (e.g., relationships, work, any other
   309→important part of life). Furthermore, the diagnostic criteria for CPTSD were met if all
   310→diagnostic criteria for PTSD were fulfilled, and additionally participants’ responses exceeded
   311→the clinical cut-off for at least one item in each of the three DSO clusters (i.e., affective
   312→dysregulation, negative self-concept, disturbances in relationships). Importantly, in
   313→accordance with ICD-11 diagnostic criteria, administration of diagnoses for either PTSD or
   314→
   315→10
   316→CPTSD are valid, but not both (WHO, 2019). Thus, if participants fulfilled diagnostic criteria of
   317→both PTSD and CPTSD, diagnosis of CPTSD was administered but not PTSD.
   318→Trauma Exposure Life Events Scale
   319→The Trauma Exposure Life Events Scale (LEC-5) by Weathers et al. (2013) is a 17-
   320→item measurement instrument designed to screen for lifetime exposure to potentially
   321→traumatic events. The LEC-5 assesses exposure to 16 events known to potentially result in
   322→traumatic stress or PTSD and includes one additional item assessing any other potentially
   323→stressful event experienced (Weathers et al., 2013). Participants could report multiple
   324→exposures to each event. If at least one scale point from 1 (Happened to me) to 4 (Part of my
   325→job) was reported for an event, the item was scored as 1, indicating potential traumatic
   326→exposure to this event. If responses of 5 (Not sure) or 6 (Doesn’t apply) were given, the item
   327→was scored as 0, indicating absence of traumatic exposure to this event. The scoring
   328→algorithm is in line with the DSM-5 criterion A for PTSD and CPTSD (Weathers et al., 2013).
   329→This computational algorithm was utilized in previous studies and results in sum scores from
   330→0 to 17 (Cloitre et al. (2018). Culturally adapted versions of the LEC-5 scale reported
   331→satisfactory estimates of 𝛼 = .67 in a sample of Korean psychiatric patients (Bae et al., 2008),
   332→𝛼 = .70 in a sample of Pakistani school students (Tariq & Wong, 2024), and 𝛼 = .87 in a
   333→sample of Malaysian undergraduates (Tan et al., 2023).
   334→International Depression Questionnaire and International Anxiety Questionnaire
   335→The International Depression Questionnaire (IDQ) and the International Anxiety
   336→Questionnaire (IAQ) by Shevlin et al. (2023) are self-report measures that align with the
   337→diagnostic rules of the ICD-11 (WHO, 2019). The IDQ is a 10-item measure that assesses
   338→diagnostic criteria of ICD-11 Single Episode Depressive Disorder. The IAQ measures ICD-11
   339→Generalized Anxiety Disorder on a scale with nine items. Cut-offs for clinically significant
   340→values were determined to be 4 (Most days), or higher (Shevlin et al., 2023). Diagnostic
   341→criteria for Single Episode Depressive Disorder (IDQ) were met if at least one core symptom
   342→(i.e., item 1, 2) exceeded clinical cut-offs, at least five items in total have been responded
   343→with clinically significant values, and functional impairment was reported. Diagnostic criteria
   344→for Generalized Anxiety Disorder were fulfilled if at least one core symptom (i.e., item 1, 2)
   345→was reported as clinically significant, at least four items in total exceeded clinical cut-offs, and
   346→functional impairment was reported. Validation was done using a community sample (N =
   347→2058) representative of the United Kingdom adult population (Shevlin et al., 2023). Findings
   348→indicate that both scales were unidimensional, and internal consistency of the IDQ (𝜔 = 0.96)
   349→and IAQ (𝜔 = 0.96) were high.
   350→Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale
   351→The Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale (DERS) assesses emotion dysregulation
   352→(ED) and was first developed by Gratz and Roemer (2004). The 36 items of the DERS
   353→
   354→11
   355→encompass six subscales, i.e., Nonacceptance of Emotional Responses (Nonacceptance),
   356→Difficulties Engaging in Goal-Directed Behavior (Goals), Impulse Control Difficulties
   357→(Impulse), Lack of Emotional Awareness (Awareness), Limited Access to Emotion Regulation
   358→Strategies (Strategies), and Lack of Emotional Clarity (Clarity). Gratz and Roemer (2004)
   359→reported high internal consistency for the total scale (𝛼 = .93), and for all subscales (𝛼 ≥ .80).
   360→A cross-culturally adapted and validated version in Ukrainian language was developed by
   361→Sak and Fedotova (2023). Sufficient internal consistency for initial testing (𝛼 = .70) and for
   362→retesting after 14 days (𝛼 = .70) was reported. Previous findings have shown that all
   363→subscales represent a higher order emotion regulation construct, with exception of the
   364→subscale DERS-Awareness which does not seem to share sufficient variance with a general
   365→ED factor (Bardeen et al., 2012; Hallion et al., 2018). These findings could be explained by
   366→the ambiguous effects of being aware of one’s emotional state, which might not necessarily
   367→lead to adaptive modulation of emotion (Bardeen et al., 2012). Furthermore, studies indicate
   368→that excluding the subscale DERS-Awareness improves the measure’s validity in assessing
   369→ED (Bardeen et al., 2012; Hallion et al., 2018). Therefore, the subscale DERS-Awareness
   370→was excluded, and all other subscales have been summed to a total score.
   371→Statistical Analysis
   372→A priori power analysis was conducted to estimate required sample size using
   373→G*Power 3.1.9.6 for Macintosh (Faul et al., 2007). IBM SPSS Statistics Version 30 (IBM
   374→Corp, 2024) and RStudio (Posit Software, 2025) for R (R Core Team, 2023) were used to
   375→conduct statistical analyses. A priori power estimations utilized an alpha error of α = .05 and
   376→a power of .80. To test Hypothesis 1, multiple simple linear regression models were
   377→estimated with one predictor (i.e., CTS), and four respective outcomes (i.e., depression,
   378→anxiety, PTSD and CPTSD). Minimum required sample size was estimated to be N = 55,
   379→considering a mean effect size of ƒ² = .15 (Goral et al., 2021; Greene et al., 2018; Lahav,
   380→2020; Zasiekina et al., 2024) for fixed simple linear regression models looking at the R²
