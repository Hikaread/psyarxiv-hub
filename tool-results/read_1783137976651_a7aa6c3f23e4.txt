   200→   200→consist of posed or prototypical expressions (such as from emotion-related tasks) rather than
   201→   201→spontaneous facial behaviour displayed in unscripted conversations (see also review of facial
   202→   202→movement datasets by Krumhuber, 2017). This is problematic, since spontaneous facial
   203→   203→behaviour is different from posed facial behaviour. For example, spontaneous smiles are often
   204→   204→smaller in amplitude, have a longer duration, and are slower in onset and offset time compared
   205→   205→to posed smiles1 (Cohn & Schmidt, 2004). Additionally, spontaneous eyebrow actions (AU01,
   206→   206→AU02, and AU04 from the FACS system; Ekman et al., 2002) were shown to have a different
   207→   207→intensity, duration and occurrence order compared to posed eyebrow actions (Valstar et al.,
   208→   208→2006). Therefore, software trained on posed facial movements may fail to generalise to the rich
   209→   209→variety of facial movements produced in everyday conversation (Zeng et al., 2009). Moreover,
   210→   210→the predictions of the detection and intensity models were trained separately and on different
   211→   211→datasets, which might mean that their predictions are not always consistent (e.g., when the
   212→   212→detection model indicates AU not being present, the intensity model may predict its value to be
   213→   213→above 1).
   214→   214→There have been some attempts to improve OpenFace (e.g., HMAD team
   215→   215→https://github.com/srauzy/HMAD; Santoso & Kusuma, 2018). For example, the HMAD team
   216→   216→developed annotation tools such as EBMAD for eyebrow raises and frowns, BMAD for blinks,
   217→   217→and SMAD for smile intensities using OpenFace (Rauzy & Amoyal, 2023; Rauzy & Goujon,
   218→   218→2018). EBMAD can be pipelined with OpenFace outputs, and calculates eyebrow movements
   219→   219→using their local maxima in the two-dimensional wavelet space. Based on signal processing,
   220→   220→the local maxima are weighted with respect to the signal-to-noise ratio, and the higher the value
   221→   221→of this signal-to-noise, the higher the probability is that it corresponds to a true action (Mallat,
   222→   222→1 Genuine and posed smiles (i.e., so-called Duchenne and non-Duchenne smiles) have also been reported to
   223→   223→differ in AU activation (Ekman, 1989; Ekman et al., 1990), but recent evidence suggests that these differences
   224→   224→may be much less consistent than previously believed (Girard et al., 2021).
   225→   225→
   226→   226→11
   227→   227→1999; Rauzy & Goujon, 2018). When comparing OpenFace outputs to the manually annotated
   228→   228→corpus consisting of 573 eyebrow movements on 2h50mn of video extracts, Rauzy and Gujon
   229→   229→(2018) found that the reliability of OpenFace improved when using their tool. While tools like
   230→   230→the ones created by the HMAD team are certainly useful, a general overview of OpenFace
   231→   231→performance is still needed, as well as general recommendations for using OpenFace to study
   232→   232→specific facial signals on one’s own dataset. Furthermore, an evaluation of OpenFace
   233→   233→performance on a large naturalistic and unscripted conversation corpus including a wider range
   234→   234→of facial signals is still missing.
   235→   235→2. Comparison of OpenFace performance with manual corpus annotation
   236→   236→2.1 Conversational face-to-face corpus details
   237→   237→To assess whether OpenFace can be used as-is for reliably capturing facial signals in
   238→   238→conversational corpora, we compare annotations retrieved through OpenFace with manual
   239→   239→annotations of communicative facial movements from a large naturalistic and entirely
   240→   240→unscripted Dutch face-to-face conversation corpus (CoAct corpus; see Nota et al., 2021, 2023b;
   241→   241→ter Bekke et al., 2024; Trujillo & Holler, 2021).
   242→   242→The corpus consists of videos from 34 dyads (mean age = 23.10 ± 8 years; 51 female,
   243→   243→17 male) of Dutch native speakers (all dyad partners were friends or acquaintances), without
   244→   244→motoric or language problems, and with normal or corrected-to-normal vision. Dyad partners
   245→   245→were seated opposite one another at a comfortable conversational distance and asked to engage
   246→   246→in casual conversation for one hour while they were recorded. Conversations were recorded
   247→   247→
   248→   248→12
   249→   249→using two video cameras (Canon XE405) for frontal views of the face/head close-ups of each
   250→   250→participant, two cameras (Canon XF205 Camcorder) mounted at a 45-degree angle towards the
   251→   251→participants’ bodies, two cameras (Canon XF205 Camcorder) were mounted on tripods to
   252→   252→provide a birds-eye view of the participants, and one camera (Canon Legria HF G10) recorded
   253→   253→the scene view (both participants from the side). For the present study, only the frontal view
   254→   254→recordings were used (see Figure 1, panels A and B). The resulting time resolution was 25 fps
   255→   255→(approx. 40 ms).
   256→   256→The recording session was split into three 20-minute sessions. The first session
   257→   257→consisted of free, unguided conversation. In the second session, participants were asked to
   258→   258→discuss one of three themes: data privacy, social media use, or language of instruction in
   259→   259→university teaching. In the third session, participants were asked to discuss their ideal joint
   260→   260→holiday, affordable on their own budget. Written informed consent was obtained before and
   261→   261→after filming2. The original study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Social Sciences

   332→   332→2.3 Manual annotation methods
   333→   333→As the larger project in which this study is embedded focused on question and response pairs,
   334→   334→manual annotation, too, focused on this sequential environment. Questions and responses were
   335→   335→
   336→   336→16
   337→   337→identified and annotated in ELAN (5.5; Wittenburg et al., 2006), based on an annotation scheme
   338→   338→adapted from Stivers and Enfield (2010), as well as a holistic approach that took into
   339→   339→consideration visual signals, conversational context, phrasing, prosody, and addressee
   340→   340→behaviour, with onsets and offsets annotated in Praat (5.1; Boersma & Weenink, 2007) and
   341→   341→imported into ELAN (see Nota et al., 2021 for further details). This resulted in 6778 questions
   342→   342→(duration Mdn = 1114 ms, min = 99ms, max = 13,145 ms, interquartile ratio = 1138 ms), and
   343→   343→4553 responses (duration Mdn = 1045 ms, min = 91 ms, max = 18,615 ms, interquartile ratio
   344→   344→= 1596 ms).
   345→   345→Facial signal annotation was performed in ELAN (5.5) based on the frontal view
   346→   346→cameras (see Figure 1), focusing on a window of 2000 ms before to 2000 ms after each question
   347→   347→or response annotation. These timing windows resulted from a careful qualitative analysis of
   348→   348→the data. Our goal was to find a balanced approach, acknowledging that visual signals may
   349→   349→slightly precede or follow the associated speech. Manual spots checks of the data revealed that
   350→   350→this extended time window was a good solution for mitigating the risk of both omitting relevant
   351→   351→data and including irrelevant data). Facial signals were annotated when they overlapped with
   352→   352→this window with minimally one frame, even if they started or ended outside of the window.
   353→   353→Annotations were created on a frame-by-frame basis by five trained annotators, all native
   354→   354→speakers of Dutch.
   355→   355→Critically, facial signals were annotated when they were judged to be communicative
   356→   356→in nature contributing to the semantic and pragmatic content of a message, based on a holistic
   357→   357→view of the utterance. We also coded all blinks, because their communicative value has been
   358→   358→demonstrated for addressee behaviour (Hömke et al., 2018). However, some of these blinks
   359→   359→(especially shorter ones) may have functioned primarily for wetting the eye. We additionally
   360→   360→coded all gaze shifts, which included cases where speakers may have shifted their gaze for
   361→   361→
   362→   362→17
   363→   363→speech planning purposes or other reasons. Movements such as those associated with
   364→   364→swallowing, inhaling, or laughter without or with inaudible speech, as well as mouth and jaw
   365→   365→movements arising simply due to speech articulation, were not annotated. The following facial
   366→   366→signals were annotated:
   367→   367→1. Eyebrow movements (frowns, raises, frown raises, unilateral raises, lowering)
   368→   368→2. Eye widenings (upper and possibly lower eyelid opening wider than neutral)
   369→   369→3. Squints (narrowing of the eyes due to contraction of lower and possibly upper eyelid)
   370→   370→4. Blinks (closure of the eye)
   371→   371→5. Gaze shifts (gaze away from the addressee, judged by position of the pupil)
   372→   372→6. Nose wrinkles (skin over the nose contracting)
   373→   373→7. Non-articulatory mouth movements (pressed lips, corners down, corners back, smiles)
   374→   374→Signal annotations started at the first evidence of movement until the respective articulator
   375→   375→had moved back to its neutral position. We did not annotate frames of the video that had
   376→   376→speech not related to the question or response, or that was combined with laughter when the
   377→   377→laughter rendered the speech inaudible. We additionally excluded two frames before or after
   378→   378→such unrelated speech events. This was to ensure that any facial signals were more likely to
   379→   379→be related to the question or response. This resulted in the exclusion of 795 (2.3% of the
   380→   380→34,413 annotated signals) signal annotations. The detailed facial signal annotation manual can
   381→   381→be found on the Open Science Framework website

   385→   385→Interrater reliability for facial signal annotations was calculated using Krippendorff’s
   386→   386→alpha (Krippendorff, 2019) and Jaccard similarity (1901). To calculate these two measures we
   387→   387→determined whether, for each coding window, a given signal was annotated. This was done for
   388→   388→each annotator, creating an agreement matrix with columns for each annotator (binary, signal
   389→   389→annotated or not), and a row for each possible combination of coding window and facial signal.
   390→   390→This approach focuses more on whether a particular signal was detected within a given
   391→   391→utterance or not, while avoiding known sources of disagreement such as segmentation (e.g.,
   392→   392→was it one long signal, or two consecutive signals with a short dip in intensity between them)
   393→   393→and offset timing. In order to provide a chance-corrected measure of agreement that can be
   394→   394→interpreted on its own, we calculated Krippendorff’s alpha across all manual annotators.
   395→   395→Krippendorff’s alpha was chosen because it is designed to handle multiple annotators, and is
   396→   396→more robust against skewed data with many nulls, which applies to facial signal coding given
   397→   397→that most utterances are unlikely to have all, or even many, of the facial signals. We report
   398→   398→alpha, raw agreement, and prevalence for the minimum, maximum, and average observed
   399→   399→agreement. Prevalence is also included as it can often explain the discrepance between raw
   400→   400→agreement and alpha.
   401→   401→Jaccard similarity, calculated as J(A,B) = ∣A∩B∣ / ∣A∪B∣, is commonly employed to
   402→   402→assess the similarity between two datasets by taking the intersection of the two sets divided by
   403→   403→the union of the two sets. This metric is thus better suited than alternatives (such as Fleiss or
   404→   404→Cohen’s kappa) as it does not have an assumption about multiple coding categories. As Jaccard
   405→   405→similarity is designed for agreement between two annotators, we calculated the average
   406→   406→similarity across all pairs of annotators. It should be noted that Jaccard similarity does not have
   407→   407→standard cut-off bands for interpretation (i.e., for poor vs moderate vs good vs excellent).
   408→   408→Instead, it is typically used as a comparison between methods. In the case of the present
   409→   409→
   410→   410→19
   411→   411→research, we provide Jaccard similarity as a straightforward metric to contrast between human-
   412→   412→human and human-machine reliability. While Jaccard similarity provides a measure of the
   413→   413→proportion of annotations in a given signal category, we also calculated raw agreement, which
   414→   414→considers both positive agreement (i.e., annotators have overlapping annotations with the same
   415→   415→label) and negative agreement (number of annotations, from both/all annotators that are not the
   416→   416→target signal). This calculation of raw agreement, while standard in the version of ELAN that
   417→   417→we used for these analyses when assessing reliability for multiple tiers, can lead to inflated
   418→   418→agreement values. Jaccard similarity therefore provides a useful alternative, and may be closer
   419→   419→to the form of agreement that researchers are typically interested in. However, for completeness
   420→   420→we also include raw agreement calculations.
   421→   421→As the timing of signal onsets was also of interest, we additionally calculated
   422→   422→convergent reliability using mean absolute difference (Mabs, in ms) of the onsets between
   423→   423→manual annotators. We also computed the standard error of measurement (SeM) as a more
   424→   424→precise, data-specific indicator of agreement (see Nota et al., 2021). However, because SeM
   425→   425→relies on Cohen’s kappa (SeM = S × √(1 − r)) and we instead use Jaccard similarity, we focus

   430→   430→a pairwise comparison between annotators.
   431→   431→We excluded eyebrow lowering and mouth corners pulled back from all further
   432→   432→analyses, since these facial signals did not frequently appear in the corpus resulting in too few
   433→   433→annotations to perform the paired comparisons between annotators. For all other facial signals,
