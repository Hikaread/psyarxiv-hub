    29→    29→Abstract
    30→    30→While the scientific community is increasingly recognizing the relevance of addressing
    31→    31→insufficient effort responding (IER) in survey data, the reliance of current IER detection
    32→    32→approaches on cut-offs limits their implementation and complexifies the identification,
    33→    33→prevalence estimation, and measurement of the impact of IER. The present article illustrates,
    34→    34→across four simulation studies, an innovative application of a fair-cut forest algorithm by
    35→    35→introducing the multidimensional Insufficient Effort Responding detection approach (mIERda).
    36→    36→Built on an unsupervised learning approach, the mIERda was designed to streamline the IER
    37→    37→screening process, address methodological gaps associated with current detection approaches,
    38→    38→and fit a maximum of survey specifications. In the present article, the discriminative potential of
    39→    39→various indicators of IER (Study 1a) and classification accuracy of the mIERda (Study 1b) were
    40→    40→assessed and compared to the current gold standard approach across three scales and various
    41→    41→settings applied using standard thresholds recommended from the literature. The classification
    42→    42→accuracy was then assessed in datasets containing real human data (Study 2) contaminated with
    43→    43→simulated IER respondents and when multiple scales are screened at once (Study 3). Overall, we
    44→    44→found the mIERda to be more accurate than our application of the gold standard in simulated and
    45→    45→human attentive data. The mIERda was also found to have greater levels of balanced accuracy in
    46→    46→longer scales and when the severity of IER was higher. The conditions impacting the
    47→    47→classification performance of the mIERda are also discussed. Overall, results provide evidence of
    48→    48→the criterion validity of the mIERda and position it as an accurate IER detection approach.
    49→    49→Keywords: Insufficient effort responding; Careless responding; Content non-responsivity; Survey
    50→    50→research methods.

    55→    55→Technical Abstract
    56→    56→Research conclusions are only as valid and reliable as the data they are based on. In
    57→    57→survey research, ensuring the quality of data is critical. It is why researchers relying on survey
    58→    58→data need tools to identify participants who failed to put forth a sufficient effort when responding
    59→    59→to their questionnaire, a phenomenon known as careless or insufficient effort responding (IER).
    60→    60→While the potential pervasive impact of IER is well-established, identifying these participants
    61→    61→currently remains challenging. In the present article, we introduce the multidimensional
    62→    62→Insufficient Effort Responding detection approach (mIERda), a novel tool designed to facilitate
    63→    63→the detection of IER in survey data. The mIERda streamlines the detection of IER by deferring
    64→    64→the interpretation of IERI to an algorithm, hence relieving researchers from the need to establish
    65→    65→cutoffs and individually interpret IERI. This research sheds light on the discriminative potential
    66→    66→of various indicators of IER and examines the classification accuracy of the mIERda across three
    67→    67→scales and various settings in simulated attentive responses. The classification accuracy was then
    68→    68→assessed in datasets containing real human data contaminated with simulated IER respondents
    69→    69→and when multiple scales are screened at once. Overall, the results provided evidence of the
    70→    70→criterion validity of the mIERda and position it as an accurate IER detection approach.

    75→    75→“Sorting the Good from the mIERda” – Introducing and Testing the performance of the
    76→    76→Multidimensional Insufficient Effort Responding Detection Approach (mIERda)
    77→    77→Researchers from multiple fields and disciplines rely on surveys to find answers to their
    78→    78→research questions. As these researchers collect data using surveys, the integrity of these data
    79→    79→rely on the assumption that the responses provided by the respondents accurately portray their
    80→    80→variables of interest. It is assumed that respondents are attentive and deploy the efforts required
    81→    81→to properly read, interpret and respond to survey items. This belief is, however, being challenged
    82→    82→by a growing body of research supporting that a significant number of respondents engage in
    83→    83→insufficient effort responding (IER) when participating in survey research (Bowling et al., 2016;
    84→    84→Huang et al., 2012; Huang, Liu, Bowling, et al., 2015). Also known as careless responding, IER
    85→    85→is defined as a problematic response style characterized by low or little motivation to follow
    86→    86→survey instructions, interpret item content, and provide accurate responses (Huang et al., 2012;
    87→    87→Meade & Craig, 2012). As they engage in IER, respondents deviate from the core assumption
    88→    88→that their responses result from an interaction between their latent trait and the items’
    89→    89→characteristics. Instead, these respondents provide content-independent responses that are a
    90→    90→representation of another unrelated underlying process.
    91→    91→Impact of IER
    92→    92→While early research suggested that IER had a relatively low prevalence in research
    93→    93→samples (Johnson, 2005), it is currently estimated that IER respondents represent between 3%
    94→    94→and 50% of research samples, in rates varying greatly between studies (Francavilla et al., 2019;
    95→    95→Maniaci & Rogge, 2014; Meade & Craig, 2012). Unless they adequately address IER in their
    96→    96→samples, researchers unknowingly end up conducting their analyses on partially spurious and
    97→    97→potentially misleading data. If left unaddressed, this can affect the reliability of their results by

   132→   132→Indicators of Insufficient Effort Responding
   133→   133→Various IERI have been introduced in the literature over the past years to facilitate the
   134→   134→identification of IER respondents and limit its impact on data quality. For a full review of these
   135→   135→IERI, refer to Curran (2016), Desimone et al. (2015), Huang et al. (2012); Huang, Liu, et al.
   136→   136→(2015), and Ward and Meade (2023).
   137→   137→Current methods to identify IER respondents include (1) adding attention checks meant to
   138→   138→measure respondents’ attentiveness (e.g., instructed items and bogus items), (2) relying on
   139→   139→paradata collected during data collection (e.g., completion time), or (3) by using a data-driven
   140→   140→approach (e.g., Mahalanobis Distance; intra-individual variability; person-total correlation; long
   141→   141→string index; psychometric synonyms and antonyms; even-odd consistency).
   142→   142→The first category of IERI embeds instructed items for which an expected response is
   143→   143→provided to the participant (e.g., “Respond strongly agree to this item”; Gummer et al., 2018) and
   144→   144→bogus items for which there is a sole correct response (e.g., “I have never used a computer”;
   145→   145→Beach, 1989). These items are inconspicuously added amongst the other survey items and are
   146→   146→designed to capture inattentive respondents. This approach is built on the assumption that IER
   147→   147→respondents will be identified either by failing to properly respond to the item or by willingly
   148→   148→disclosing their inattentiveness at the end of the survey. These approaches have been criticized
   149→   149→since they disturb the flow of the survey, can annoy respondents, or prompt humoristic responses
   150→   150→(Costa & McCrae, 2008; DeSimone et al., 2018) leading researchers to also rely on paradata and
   151→   151→statistical methods.

   187→   187→How do IER Respondents Differ in Terms of Response Patterns?
   188→   188→While all IER respondents exhibit signs of carelessness, not all of them manifest the same
   189→   189→symptoms of IER. Not only can respondents differ in the onset and location of IER (i.e., IER
   190→   190→from the beginning to the end vs becomes IER throughout the survey) within a given survey, but
   191→   191→they also differ in terms of response patterns. Response patterns emerging from an IER process
   192→   192→are often classified into two broad categories: (uniformly) random and non-(uniformly) random
   193→   193→(Meade & Craig, 2012). A random response pattern refers to a response process where, for a
   194→   194→given respondent, each Likert point has an approximately equal probability of being selected
   195→   195→across items. This results in a response set with no distinguishable response pattern or trend,
   196→   196→which could be modelled using a discrete uniform random distribution, although humans’
   197→   197→responses cannot perfectly reproduce a fully random distribution (Figurska et al., 2008). The
   198→   198→non-random category, in contrast, describes any content non-responsive responding pattern
   199→   199→where a distinguishable trend or tendency can be observed. Response sets under this category
   200→   200→can be classified in two sub-categories: (a) invariant (e.g., responding “5” to all the items of a
   201→   201→given scale), or (b) non-invariant where responses are positioned to both ends of the Likert scale
   202→   202→(e.g., alternating between “1” and “7”), centred around the midpoint of the scale (alternating
   203→   203→between “3”, “4”, and “5”), or patterned (e.g., responding “4-5-6-5-6”, for example). The latter
   204→   204→category could be modelled, respectively, by a bimodal distribution, normal distribution, or a
   205→   205→Markov Chain.

   210→   210→Given that IER respondents differ in their response patterns, the sample-wide sensitivity
   211→   211→of the most commonly used IERI (e.g., psychometric synonyms and antonyms, long string index,
   212→   212→person-total correlation, etc.) is often limited. As demonstrated by Meade and Craig (2012), any
   213→   213→given IERI is vastly more specific than sensitive in any given sample. This happens since each
   214→   214→IERI is sensitive to a particular IER response pattern. For instance, while some IERI target
   215→   215→excessive invariance (e.g., long string index), other target excessive variance (e.g., intra-
   216→   216→individual variability) or inconsistency (e.g., person-total correlation). In other words, this means
   217→   217→that using different IERI inherently leads to different operationalization of IER. As each IERI
   218→   218→captures approximately distinct problematic response patterns, to increase the overall sensitivity
   219→   219→of the screening process, researchers have been encouraged to use multiple IERI when
   220→   220→performing IER screening. Such process is known as the sequential approach (Curran, 2016;
   221→   221→Ward & Meade, 2023).
   222→   222→Sequential Approach
   223→   223→The sequential approach is an IER detection approach which relies on an iterative
   224→   224→screening process of a selection of IERI. The sequential approach relies on a serial process
   225→   225→where, in order to be deemed attentive, a respondent must “pass” each IERI. The implementation
   226→   226→of the sequential approach relies on three major steps. To implement this approach, researchers
   227→   227→must (1) select the IERI to be used, (2) establish a threshold (i.e., cutoff) for each IERI of what
   228→   228→constitutes attentiveness vs IER, and (3) compute the IERI and screen the data using these
   229→   229→cutoffs. Respondents whose score on any given IERI falls outside the prespecified cutoff, are
   230→   230→deemed IER and are consequently excluded from the analytical dataset. This process is described
   231→   231→in greater detail below.

   236→   236→Step 1 – Selection of the IERI. To implement the sequential approach, researchers must
   237→   237→decide the combination of IERI to be computed and used for screening. Given the limited
   238→   238→sensitivity of each IERI, this selection should seek to cover a maximum of response patterns
   239→   239→(Meade & Craig, 2012). It is for this reason that various reviews were published to provide
   240→   240→guidance to researchers interested in performing IER screening (Curran, 2016; Ward & Meade,
   241→   241→2023). Ward and Meade (2023) states that if IER screening is considered prior to data collection,
   242→   242→researchers should consider adding instructed and bogus items, and to measure page-level
   243→   243→response time. These IERI, consequently, cannot be implemented after data collection or in a
   244→   244→simulation setting. Additionally, it is generally recommended for researchers to implement at
   245→   245→least one IERI targeting invariance (e.g. long string index), another targeting multivariate outliers
   246→   246→(i.e., Mahalanobis distance) and multiple IERI targeting consistency (e.g. psychometric
   247→   247→synonyms and person-total correlation). For the needs of the present study, the sequential
   248→   248→approach relies on the moderate screening level laid out by Ward & Meade (2023). The moderate
   249→   249→screening approach states that the sequential approach should rely on one invariance indicator
   250→   250→(i.e., long string index), one indicator of multivariate normality (i.e., Mahalanobis Distance), and
   251→   251→two consistency indicators.

   257→   257→facilitate the implementation of these IERI (e.g., Barnes, 2018; Desimone et al., 2015; Wang &
   258→   258→Hau, 2024; Yentes, 2020). For instance, it is supported that a respondent should be considered

   270→   270→Limitations of the Sequential IER Detection Approach
   271→   271→The sequential approach requires researchers to select IERI and cutoffs to perform IER
   272→   272→screening. However, most of past research pertaining to the identification of IER has been
   273→   273→dedicated to the development of IERI and to the demonstration of their discriminative potential
   274→   274→(Dupuis et al., 2018; Marjanovic et al., 2015; Meade & Craig, 2012). While doing so represents a
   275→   275→first step to facilitate the detection of IER, researchers using these IERI must establish a cutoff
   276→   276→value beyond which a respondent should be considered IER rather than attentive. Nonetheless,
   277→   277→this step is often skipped in the literature which seriously limits their application. Furthermore,
   278→   278→this reliance on cutoffs prevents the implementation of other promising IERI, such as response
   279→   279→coherence and response reliability, for which they are no known or generalizable cutoffs. In
   280→   280→previous research, these IERI were found to have a strong discriminative potential (Dupuis et al.,
   281→   281→2015, 2018), nonetheless, the sequential approach’s reliance on cutoffs limits their
   282→   282→implementation.
   283→   283→While rules of thumb have been introduced to facilitate the application of some IERI
   284→   284→(e.g., Barnes, 2018; Desimone et al., 2015; Wang & Hau, 2024; Yentes, 2020), the sequential
   285→   285→approach’s reliance on cutoffs limits the accuracy of the screening performed. Although relying

   290→   290→on specific and pre-established cutoffs may be intuitive, doing so fails to acknowledge that the
   291→   291→optimal cutoff for each IERI may vary across scales and samples based on various factors (e.g.,
   292→   292→the number of items, factorial structure, correlation between items, the Likert scale of each scale;
   293→   293→Falk et al., 2025; Ilagan & Falk, 2023). As such, implementing such fixed cutoff can impact the
   294→   294→discriminative potential of each IERI, which ultimately can alter the quality of the screening
   295→   295→performed. To address the complexities inherently tied to the use of the sequential approach,
   296→   296→there is a need to move beyond cutoffs and to introduce an easily implementable flexible data-
   297→   297→adaptive approach.
   298→   298→Machine learning based approaches for IER Detection
   299→   299→The use of machine learning approaches has become increasingly common (Pugliese et
   300→   300→al., 2021) and offer powerful opportunities to leverage the information provided by multiple
   301→   301→indicators, without the need to establish specific cutoffs or make underlying distributional
   302→   302→assumptions, making these approaches especially promising in the context of IER detection.
   303→   303→In the recent past, IER detection approaches built on supervised (Schroeders et al., 2022)
   304→   304→and unsupervised (Alfons & Welz, 2024) learning approaches have been introduced. Shroeder et
   305→   305→al. (2022) applied supervised learning to IER detection using stochastic gradient boosted trees
   306→   306→which resulted in poor performance in real-life settings due to the necessity of valid empirical
   307→   307→training data. In contrast, the auto-encoder approach introduced by Alfons and Welz (2024)
   308→   308→demonstrated better potential to address IER due to the unsupervised nature of the algorithm
   309→   309→used. However, the applicability of the approach to a broad audience remains limited due to the
   310→   310→heavy computational nature of the algorithm. Machine learning approaches are also gaining
   311→   311→traction in the field of bot detection (Falk et al., 2025; Ilagan & Falk, 2023). However, the
   312→   312→applicability of such approach in an IER detection context is unknown. While bots necessarily
   313→   313→This paper has not been peer-reviewed

   317→   317→provide content-independent responses to the entirety of items (Falk et al., 2025), humans
   318→   318→engaging in IER might provide partially, inconsistent, or fully content-independent responses
   319→   319→(Hong et al., 2020). Bots and humans thus differ in terms of their interactions with any given
   320→   320→survey. Therefore, while we can expect an approach validated in an IER context to maintain a
   321→   321→satisfactory level of performance for bots’ detection, the performance is not guaranteed in the
   322→   322→reverse direction. Contrarily to bots, intra-individual factors such as personality (Bowling et al.,
   323→   323→2016; Grau et al., 2019), momentary motivation (Bowling et al., 2016; Hasselhorn et al., 2025),
   324→   324→motivation towards research and global motivation (Cheung, 2019) can impact how, if, and when
   325→   325→one engages in IER. As such, there remains a need for a performant and accessible machine
   326→   326→learning approach to IER detection (i.e., low computational demand and limited data
   327→   327→preparation); such approach has yet to be introduced in the literature.
   328→   328→Fair-Cut Forest Algorithm
   329→   329→The FCF is a tree-based approach (Breiman et al., 1984) which originates from the
   330→   330→Isolation Forest (IF; Liu et al., 2008), and is an unsupervised machine learning anomaly
   331→   331→detection algorithm. The IF has been used to detect anomalies in various contexts such as cases
   332→   332→of credit card fraud (Ounacer et al., 2018), health care data (Abirami et al., 2025), instances of
   333→   333→cyber-attacks (Ahmed et al., 2019), anomalous test-takers (Bulut et al., 2024), and fraudulent
   334→   334→survey data (Myöhänen, 2021). In contrast to other anomaly detection approaches, such as
   335→   335→distance-based and clustering-based methods, IF does not profile normality in order to identify
   336→   336→abnormal observations. Instead, IF relies on isolation trees (i.e., a type of decision tree) to build
   337→   337→partial models by drawing subsamples from the data and by selecting indicators (i.e., features)
   338→   338→uniformly at random. Such approach makes it more robust to swamping (i.e., labeling normal
   339→   339→observations as anomalies) and masking (i.e., labeling anomalies as normal observations) than
   340→   340→This paper has not been peer-reviewed
   341→   341→DRAFT VERSION 1.2 30/06/2026
   342→   342→

   344→   344→typical outlier detection approaches, especially in the absence of abnormalities, in high
   345→   345→dimensional problems and when some features are uninformative (Liu et al., 2008). This makes
   346→   346→IF particularly suitable in a context of IER detection where the prevalence of IER can fluctuate
   347→   347→greatly and where the informativeness of each IERI varies greatly depending on the response
   348→   348→patterns displayed by IER respondents.
   349→   349→Over the years, multiple versions of IF were introduced to respond to specific needs. In
   350→   350→fact, after past research has demonstrated that the initial version of the IF might struggle to
   351→   351→identified “clusters” of anomalies (i.e., group of anomalous data points/respondents with similar
   352→   352→characteristics) an adaptation of isolation trees, called fair-cut trees, were introduced to facilitate
   353→   353→the detection of such instances (Cortes, 2021). In contrast with isolation trees which divides the
   354→   354→data by applying uniformly random split, fair-cut trees work by applying splits which minimizes
   355→   355→the pooled variance to facilitate the detection of clustered outliers (Cortes, 2021), making it
   356→   356→particularly suitable to IER detection applications. An ensemble of fair-cut trees can be used to
   357→   357→generate a fair-cut forest (FCF). Relying on an ensemble of fair-cut trees rather than individual
   358→   358→trees can increase the robustness of the screening process and reduce error; it is why ensemble
   359→   359→methods tend to be more accurate than approaches relying on a single classifier (Dietterich,
   360→   360→2000). Resulting anomaly scores range between 0 and 1; a score closer to 0 represents inlierness
   361→   361→whereas a score closer to 1 represents a great likelihood of outlierness (Liu et al., 2008). In what
   362→   362→follows, we build on this new stream of research and introduce the multidimensional Insufficient
   363→   363→Effort Responding detection approach (mIERda) a novel IER detection approach driven by a
   364→   364→tree-based unsupervised machine learning approach.

   369→   369→Multidimensional Insufficient Effort Responding Detection Approach (mIERda)
   370→   370→To implement a novel methodology for IER detection, we propose the use of the FCF
   371→   371→algorithm and introduce the mIERda which proposes an innovative approach designed to be an
   372→   372→easily implementable, interpretable, and standardizable IER detection tool for researchers
   373→   373→working with survey data. Instead of interpreting IERI individually or in a sequential manner, the
   374→   374→mIERda proposes a multidimensional approach which iteratively screens the data using
   375→   375→combinations of IERI metrics under the FCF framework.
   376→   376→By doing so, not only does the mIERda minimize the requirement for researchers to
   377→   377→establish cutoffs for each IERI, but doing so also maximizes the information provided by each
   378→   378→IERI score. By removing the need to establish subjective cutoffs for IER detection, this also
   379→   379→allows for the inclusion of IERI for which they are no known cutoffs (i.e., response coherence
   380→   380→and reliability).
   381→   381→Identification of IER Respondents using the mIERda
   382→   382→The mIERda is built on the assumption that we can distinguish between attentive and IER
   383→   383→respondents by iteratively splitting the data based on the joint distribution of pairs of IERI.
   384→   384→Building on the mechanism of FCF, a data-driven cutoff can be established by finding the point
   385→   385→which minimizes the variance in each resulting distribution. Doing so, the attentive and IER
   386→   386→groups are defined by mIERda scores in rank order and split by a threshold which is chosen to
   387→   387→minimize the between-group sum of squared errors. This cutoff was selected as it represents a
   388→   388→natural extension of the FCF splits applied within each tree.
   389→   389→To put it otherwise, screening the data using a FCF removes the need to set and establish
   390→   390→cutoffs at an IERI level, which allows the algorithm to leverage all the information provided by
   391→   391→each IERI. In transferring the need for a cutoff to an FCF-level, this allows for the raw IERI

   397→   397→IERI metrics. This not only reduces the number of cutoffs to specify but also allows the mIERda
   398→   398→to establish classification from a robust score.

   586→   586→Performance Measures
   587→   587→To estimate ability of the various IERI to differentiate attentive and IER respondents (i.e.,
   588→   588→discriminative potential), the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) was
   589→   589→computed. In a first instance, the AUC of the IERI included in the sequential approach (i.e., long
   590→   590→string index, Mahalanobis distance, person-total correlation, and psychometric synonyms) was
   591→   591→assessed, with (i.e., discrimination at the available threshold) and without a cutoff. Then, the
   592→   592→AUC of other IERI (i.e., intra-individual variability, response coherence and response reliability)
   593→   593→was also computed. The AUC was interpreted in terms of distance from chance performance
   594→   594→(AUC = .50), where values closer to .00 or 1.00 are indicative of a strong discriminative
   595→   595→potential, and values closer to .50 suggesting poorer discriminative potential. The AUC was
   596→   596→interpreted as such because the direction of certain IERI classifiers is expected to shift depending
   597→   597→on the type of IER response pattern being simulated. For instance, it is expected that a bimodal
   598→   598→IER participant would end up with a high intra-individual variability score while a highly
   599→   599→invariant IER participant would have low intra-individual variability score. AUCs of .15 and .85
   600→   600→were thus considered equally discriminant, simply in opposite directions.

   602→   602→three performance metrics, namely balanced accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity (refer to Table
   603→   603→3 for the formula used to compute each performance metrics). Although, accuracy is typically
   604→   604→used to assess the proportion of correctly classified respondents amongst attentive and IER cases,
   605→   605→its interpretation can be misleading in imbalanced datasets (Brodersen et al., 2010). Accuracy
   606→   606→can be misleading because it may appear high even if the classification tool performs poorly in

   611→   611→the minority class (i.e., smallest group). As such, the balanced accuracy was used, as it corrects
   612→   612→for class imbalance by computing the average of correctly classified respondents across classes,
   613→   613→which gives equal weight to both classes. A value closer to 1, on any of these measures, indicates
   614→   614→a stronger classification performance, whereas a value close to 0 denotes a poor classification
