Three participant-centred\nfidelity domains are recognized (delivery, receipt, and enactment). Quantitative fidelity checks\ncan reveal whether but not how deviations from protocol occur.\nWhat does this study add?\n• With ecological momentary intervention designs, fidelity domains are interconnected\n• Fidelity deviations in daily life may reflect pragmatic adaptation\n• Participants may perceive daily self-report measurement as a self-monitoring\nintervention\n3\n\nIntroduction\nDigital behaviour change interventions (DBCI), including ecological momentary interventions\n(EMIs) (Heron & Smyth, 2010) have flourished alongside technological advances and\nwidespread ownership of smartphones (Arigo et al., 2019). Research on DBCI and EMIs has\ndevoted considerable attention to conceptualising engagement and its role in intervention\nefficacy (Arigo et al., 2019; Cole-Lewis et al., 2019; Eaton et al., 2024; Perski et al., 2017), yet\nfar less is known about fidelity. Intervention fidelity encompasses the methodological strategies\nused to monitor and enhance the reliability and validity of behavioural interventions (Bellg et\nal., 2004). Assessing fidelity is essential for interpreting outcomes, as it determines whether\nobserved effects reflect the intervention as designed or deviations in its implementation (Bellg\net al., 2004; Toomey et al., 2020).\nEMIs include interventions delivered by smartphone apps, embedded within daily life, with\ncontent tailored to incoming data (Arigo et al., 2019). A defining feature is that EMI success\ndepends heavily on how participants understand and translate instructions into action amid daily\nlife complexities. This creates a need to understand not only whether participants engage with\nEMIs, but also whether interventions are enacted as intended—a question of intervention\nfidelity.\nFive domains are recommended for a comprehensive assessment of intervention fidelity: study\ndesign, provider training, and intervention delivery, receipt, and enactment (Bellg et al., 2004).\nFidelity-related research practices could be improved broadly—including in non-DBCI studies\n(Beck et al., 2023; Begum et al., 2021; Kechter et al., 2019; Lambert et al., 2017), though\ncomprehensive examples do exist (e.g. Lowers et al., 2025). The first two concern researcher\nactions before implementation, whereas the latter three concern how participants experience the\nintervention. Specifically, delivery refers to whether content is provided as planned; receipt to\nwhether participants understand and can demonstrate the skills taught; and enactment to\nwhether they apply those skills in daily life (Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). These\nparticipant-centred fidelity domains are complex and interrelated, which makes qualitative\nmethods well suited to generating a contextualised understanding of them (Palsola et al.,\n2020)—particularly for EMIs, where fidelity remains largely unexplored.\nThe present study\nTo fill this gap, the present study investigates intervention fidelity from participants’\nperspective, using a qualitative approach, at the example of an ecological momentary unhealthy\nsnacking habit degradation intervention. Habits are cognitively represented cue–behaviour\n4\n\nassociations acquired through the repeated pairing of a cue and behaviour, and are characterised\nby automaticity (Fleetwood, 2021; Moors & De Houwer, 2006; Wood & Rünger, 2016). In daily\nlife, habits can sustain health-risk behaviours such as unhealthy snacking and resist deliberate\nchange efforts (Gardner et al., 2021, 2024). Theory-based strategies for habit degradation\ninclude substitution (replacing the habitual response with an alternative behaviour at cue\nencounters), inhibition (wilfully stopping performance at cue encounters), reduced accessibility\n(limiting the availability of the habitual behaviour), and cue discontinuation (avoiding the cue)\n(Edgren & Inauen, 2026; Gardner et al., 2024).\nA recent intensive longitudinal randomised controlled trial (RCT) investigated the efficacy of\nthree habit degradation strategies and reward in degrading unhealthy snacking habits in daily\nlife (Edgren et al., 2026). Participants formulated an implementation intention (Gollwitzer,\n1993)—also known as an if-then plan—consistent with their assigned strategy. Habit strength\ndeclined across all groups, with a significantly faster rate of change during the first week in\nintervention groups compared to control. However, no credible evidence was found for\ndifferential effects between the strategy or reward conditions (Edgren et al., 2026).\nQuantitative fidelity checks in the trial revealed several concerns that might have contributed\nto the absence of differences between the degradation strategy conditions (Edgren et al., 2026).\nFirst, 35% of implementation intentions did not match the assigned strategy. Second, a\nsubstantial proportion of participants reported using additional strategies beyond those\nassigned, indicating blended strategy use. Third, responding to the event-contingent\nquestionnaire (completed upon encountering the cue) was infrequent, which limited delivery of\nthe reward manipulation, as reward was contingent on these responses. These fidelity findings\nconstrain the conclusions that can be drawn from the trial and point to a need for deeper\nunderstanding of how participants experienced and engaged with the intervention.\nAccordingly, the present study addressed the research question: What were participants'\nexperiences relating to intervention delivery, receipt, and enactment in this habit degradation\nEMI? Beyond this intervention, the study offers methodological lessons for intensive\nlongitudinal EMI and DBCI research.\n5\n\nMethods\nThis preregistered qualitative study (https://osf.io/kepjr/) employed semi-structured follow-up\ninterviews with a subsample of participants from the preceding RCT on habit degradation in\ndaily life (Edgren et al., 2026). Interviews also covered participants' experiences using the Self-\nReport Behavioural Automaticity Index (Gardner et al., 2012; Verplanken & Orbell, 2003),\nwhich this manuscript does not address. (P36_INH_R:\n37–41)\nPreparatory actions\nSome participants found preparatory actions helpful, such as preparing substitutes beforehand.\nFor instance, one participant prepared a glass of water in the morning in preparation for\nencountering the cue ‘seeing snacks when coming home’: “I’d already prepared a glass that\nmorning – for the glass of water I could drink” (P5_SUB_NR: 137). Other preparatory actions\ndid not directly relate to the focal cue–behaviour pairing.\nTheme 6: Contextual factors (physical, social, internal) impacted behaviour change\nParticipants described a range of contextual factors that facilitated enactment of the habit\ndegradation strategies and behaviour change for some and hindered it for others, as\nsubsequently described.\nPhysical context\nThe ease of changing behaviour differed based on location. For instance, being at work was\nperceived positively by some: “I didn’t have that many snacks there [at work] because we don’t\nhave a break room. […] there wasn’t anything else lying around or anything like that”\n(P32_RAC_R: 53). Others expressed being at home as an obstacle: “When you have bad habits\nand you’re at home, you’re more likely to indulge in them than when I am out and about”\n(P26_CON_NR: 301). For some, location determined what they were primarily occupied with:\nWhen you’re out and about, or at certain special occasions, or even if you’ve got\nsomething planned at home or something like that, your focus is just completely\n15\n\nelsewhere. In those situations, you can’t – or don’t want to – focus on snacking anymore,\nbecause other things are simply more important. (P5_SUB_NR: 209)\nThe availability and visibility of snacks in the physical surroundings was also noted to have an\ninfluence: “if there happens to be a piece of cake or tart lying around, it gets eaten very quickly.\nI think it’s also the accessibility of the whole thing. If it’s tucked away in a drawer, it’s less\ntempting” (P7_INH_NR: 53). Relatedly, seasonal availability was noted: “what really adds to\nthat is simply the greater availability of unhealthy snacks in the run-up to Christmas”\n(P35_CON_NR: 61). In a similar vein, the absence of healthy alternatives could hinder\nbehaviour change: “I had a few days when I had finished all the vegetables. Then I hadn’t\ncooked anything. I didn’t have anything healthy here. And then I ate one of the unhealthy snacks\nI had in the cupboard” (P34_SUB_NR: 185).\nSocial context\nSocial factors impacted behaviour change in multiple ways. Some reported that others’\nbehaviour made changing their habit more difficult: “And then I know that I want to join in. If\nshe [daughter] eats something, I eat with her” (P36_INH_R: 81). Conversely, some reported\nbenefiting from social support: “I also told the people around me that I was doing this. So, at\nwork, to my partner and my family. They were, of course, supportive whenever I craved\nsomething sweet” (P48_INH_R: 261). Social support was also expressed in terms of benefiting\nfrom a collaborative effort:\nIt helped that I told my roommate I was taking part in the study, and she showed a lot\nof interest, because she wanted to lose a bit of weight as well. And then we could always\ncook something together and support each other. It was really good to have her support\nas well. (P34_SUB_NR: 47)\nInterestingly, a sense of belonging to a collective with other study participants was also\nreported: “when you suddenly find yourself in a group like that, when you don’t actually see\nthe people, but you know you’re part of something” (P26_CON_NR: 49).\nInternal states\nLastly, internal states were noted as impactful contextual factors. For instance, being ill was\nperceived as helpful by one participant: “On top of that, I had a cough, a fever, and a runny\nnose. And then you don’t have any cravings” (P32_RAC_R: 189). Similarly, insufficient sleep\nwas a reported barrier: “When I get very little sleep it [snacking habit] is the worst”\n(P46_SUB_NR: 58), as were hormonal influences: “Before my period […] I could eat\n16\n\nabsolutely anything, from sweet to savory and back again, all the time” (P32_RAC_R: 219).\nOther relevant internal states mentioned were declining motivation and stress: “I’ve noticed\nthat it’s definitely the stress that causes my snacking behaviour to increase” (P35_CON_NR:\n61); low energy: “That’s what I thought during the study, that if I’d had a little more energy, I\nwould have been able to change my behaviour more consistently” (P30_INH_NR: 43); and\nboredom: “It [resisting snacking] was particularly difficult when I was very bored and I watched\na series” (P10_RAC_R: 81).\nTheme 7: Mixed reactions to reward features\nReward was delivered contingent on event-contingent responses, which participants logged\ninfrequently: on average 11 cue encounters were logged among the interviewed participants\n(SD = 13; range 1–57). The reward features themselves elicited mixed reactions. Some\nparticipants perceived the reward features and progress bar as motivating:\nOf course, it was great to see the bar go up once you’d made it. And then, well, a\nmedal—or I think I got a trophy or something once. In a way that’s also extra motivation\nto stick with it, and it makes you happy, too. I’m ambitious, and I’m happy when I get\nbronze and then look at it next time and see that I’ve got silver. I thought that was really\ngreat. (P32_RAC_R: 285)\nHowever, some participants forgot about the reward features: “This function [the level with the\nmedals], I have to be honest, I forgot about it a bit from time to time” (P10_RAC_R: 289),\nwhile others were indifferent toward these features:\nI don’t think it had a huge impact on me. Yes. To be honest, I didn’t really realise that\nyou could move up the levels. […] I probably always stayed at the same level, I don’t\nreally know. Yes, but I didn’t necessarily feel that was necessary or anything.\n(P48_INH_R: 357)\nA few participants even found the reward features stressful:\nBut for me, that [the points, levels and trophy feature] was rather stressful. It meant that\nI was constantly thinking about the fact that I hadn’t eaten anything. That stresses me\nmuch more than just managing it myself and checking in the evening. There might be\npeople who really like doing that, but for me it was more like — I don’t want to keep\nfocusing even more on the fact that I haven’t eaten anything. (P36_INH_R: 237)\n17\n\nFurthermore, two participants initially reported uncertainty about the app’s operation in this\nregard:\nThe first three times, I didn’t know if I was using it [the app] correctly because it wasn’t\nentirely straightforward. And afterwards, I realized that I still had to fill that [the cue\nencounter] in additionally. That wasn’t entirely clear to me at the beginning.\n(P42_INH_R: 177)\nTheme 8: After successful avoidance, snacking shifted to other situations\nSome participants reported an unanticipated side effect of successfully using a habit degradation\nstrategy. Namely, after successful avoidance of unhealthy snacking in response to their cue,\nsnacking shifted to other situations not covered by the if-then plan:\nIt was that I focused very much on the coffee break, so that I really wouldn’t take\nanything then. But still, I noticed that the urge shifted. I think it maybe moved to the\nevening or right after lunch. That was something I realized after the study, that I have to\npay attention, that I simply say the trigger is the afternoon coffee break, and then I allow\nmyself maybe to snack in the evening or after lunch. I found it very difficult not to shift\nit, just because I want to avoid the trigger. (P10_RAC_R: 201)\nDiscussion\nThis study explored participant experiences of intervention fidelity in a habit degradation EMI\n(Edgren et al., 2026). Template analysis of semi-structured interviews with 22 former trial\nparticipants yielded eight themes spanning the three participant-centred fidelity domains\ndelivery, receipt, and enactment (Bellg et al., 2004; Hankonen, 2021). Technical disruptions to\napp use (Theme 1) concerned delivery; cue selection (Theme 2) and the mismatch between if-\nthen plans and assigned strategies or cues (Theme 3) primarily concerned receipt; and Themes\n4–8 primarily concerned enactment.\nAs Figure 1 illustrates, the themes did not operate in isolation but interacted, producing\ncascading effects across the three domains. For example, suboptimal cue selection arose\nprincipally from misunderstanding how cues should be chosen (receipt) yet also constrained\nhow reward could later be delivered (Theme 2); and even a well-chosen cue enabled reward\nonly once cue-contingent responses were actualised in the app (Theme 7; enactment). Some\nthemes also didn't fit neatly into a single domain: mixed reactions to rewards (Theme 7)\nadditionally reflected receipt, through uncertainty about the reward feature. These\n18\n\ninterconnections reveal the nuance underlying the fidelity of the preceding trial, as discussed\nnext.\nFigure 1. Diagram of theme distribution and interaction across intervention fidelity domains.\nTriangulating present findings with the trial’s quantitative fidelity indicators helps to understand\nseveral observed deviations. First, in the trial 35% of implementation intentions did not match\nthe assigned strategy (Edgren et al., 2026); the interviews suggest a receipt-level difficulty in\ntranslating brief written instructions into a correctly formulated plan, as some participants\nproduced plans that drew on a different strategy or omitted their selected cue (Theme 3). Action\nplanning has similarly been reported as difficult in diabetes prevention fidelity research (Miles\net al., 2022). Delivering instructions in multiple formats (MacDonald et al., 2016)—such as\npodcast-style videos (e.g., Champion et al., 2025) or cartoons (e.g., O’Dean et al., 2024)—could\nimprove receipt. Second, the trial’s finding that participants frequently blended strategies\n(Edgren et al., 2026) is consistent with pragmatic adaptation to everyday demands (Theme 5).\nThird, the trial recorded a low frequency of event-contingent responses, which limited reward\ndelivery (Edgren et al., 2026). The interviews point to two processes: some participants\nremained uncertain about how the app and event-contingent responding worked (Theme 7), a\nreceipt-level issue, while others forgot or preferred not to record cue encounters. Crucially,\nbecause reward was contingent on recording cue-behaviour repetitions, participants who had\nselected infrequently occurring cues had few opportunities to log encounters and therefore to\nreceive reward (Theme 2).\n19\n\nFinally, the trial observed habit-strength reductions across all groups, including the control\ngroup, which was tentatively attributed to the self-monitoring inherent in daily diary protocols\n(Edgren et al., 2026). The present findings strengthen this: participants across groups described\nthe end-of-day questionnaire and the visibility of their implementation intentions in the app as\nheightening awareness and accountability and reminding them daily of their plan, with this\nsupport being missed once the study had ended (Theme 4). Comparable benefits of self-\nmonitoring have been reported in qualitative studies of apps for diabetes (Adu et al., 2020) and\ncancer self-management (Crafoord et al., 2020). The questionnaire thus functioned as an\nunplanned active ingredient, helping to explain how even control participants may have\nexperienced an intervention effect (Edgren et al., 2026).\nConsiderations for habit research embedded in daily life\nSeveral findings bear on how habit and its degradation might be understood when interventions\nare embedded in daily life. When participants extended their efforts beyond the single cue\nnamed in their if-then plan (Theme 5), they may have intuitively targeted higher-order habits\n(Phillips et al., 2019)—habitual snacking that can be enacted in several ways. A second\nphenomenon was displacement: some participants who avoided snacking at their chosen cue\nfound that it shifted to other situations (Theme 8). This may reflect behavioural inertia, whereby\nan activated but unactualised habit impulse (Leeho et al., 2026) continues to shape behaviour\nafter the cue encounter has passed; whether such delayed responses are best understood as\nhabitual or as urges or cravings warrants further conceptual and empirical work. Alternatively,\nsuccessfully avoiding a habitual snack may license a compensatory health belief that permits\nother unhealthy snacking (Amrein et al., 2017).\nThese observations, together with how participants engaged with the intervention, provide\nimplications for study design. Participants did not confine themselves to their assigned strategy\nbut drew flexibly on inhibition, substitution, reduced accessibility, and discontinuation (Theme\n5), echoing the broader fidelity literature on the gap between intended and enacted interventions\n(Hankonen, 2021; Palsola et al., 2020; Toomey et al., 2020). Rather than prescribing a single\nstrategy, interventions could present and encourage the flexible use of several complementary\nstrategies. To address higher-order habits, participants could log cue-behaviour pairings as they\noccur. Clusters could then be identified—potentially via AI-assisted pattern recognition—to\nalign the intervention target with daily experience. Given the potential for displacement, single-\ncue interventions should be monitored for it. Cue selection warrants guided, iterative support,\nincluding the option to modify cues mid-intervention, as some participants recognised their\n20\n\ninitial cue as suboptimal only once underway (Theme 2). Because reward here is contingent on\ncue-behaviour repetition, selecting suitable, frequently encountered cues is essential. Finally,\nreward features should accommodate personal preferences, as reactions ranged from motivating\nto stressful (Theme 7).\nConsiderations for EMI and DBCI research\nBecause self-monitoring can itself act as an active ingredient (Theme 4), EMI studies require\ncarefully designed control conditions. Where a study sits on the explanatory-pragmatic\nspectrum (Ford & Norrie, 2016; Loudon et al., 2015; Schwartz & Lellouch, 1967), and how\nfeasible explanatory inferences remain once embedded in daily life, both merit consideration.\nA related tension is that between experimental control and ecological validity (Edgren et al.,\n2026): in everyday settings, participants may proactively adopt self-enactable behaviour change\ntechniques (Knittle et al., 2020; Michie et al., 2013) beyond those assigned, compromising\nexperimental conditions, so studies should include measures to capture such contamination.\nFurther, fidelity domains can interact such that cascading adverse effects arise—a vulnerability\nto which EMIs may be especially prone (e.g. delivery being contingent on participants'\nenactment of an earlier intervention component). Researchers and interventionists should\ntherefore prioritise high fidelity to components on which later components depend. More\nbroadly, incorporating qualitative fidelity assessment alongside quantitative metrics, as\ndemonstrated here, can reveal participant-side processes that quantitative indicators alone miss.\nPractical recommendations include clear and accessible instructions for app-based procedures\nto minimise confusion, and displaying participant plans, as this was perceived as beneficial\n(Theme 4). Collectively, insights from the participant-centred fidelity domains should feed back\ninto study design—one of the five domains outlined by Bellg et al., (2004)—so that future\ninterventions reflect the realities of participant experience.\nStrengths and limitations\nThis study has several strengths: the qualitative investigation was preregistered, purposive\nsampling ensured heterogeneity across intervention groups, and interviews drew on\npersonalised elicitation materials. However, limitations should be noted. Interviews were\nconducted 24–159 days post-study, creating a risk of retrospective recall bias. The sample\ncomprised participants who completed the study and agreed to be interviewed, potentially\nexcluding those with more negative experiences or lower engagement. The subsample of 22\nfrom the 313 trial participants limits how far findings can explain trial-level results, so insights\nshould be regarded as exploratory and hypothesis-generating. Lastly, while several participants\n21\n\ncontributed to each theme, idiosyncratic findings were also present, making it unlikely that\ntheoretical saturation was reached.\nIn conclusion, this study makes a dual contribution: participant-centred insights into the fidelity\nchallenges of the preceding habit degradation RCT, and more generalisable lessons for\nimplementing and evaluating digital and momentary behaviour change interventions in daily\nlife. The findings show that delivery, receipt, and enactment interact in EMIs, and that\nparticipant-side processes—such as pragmatic adaptation and self-monitoring reactivity—\nshape fidelity in ways that quantitative metrics may miss. Qualitative fidelity assessment should\nbe more routinely integrated into DBCI research to complement quantitative approaches and\nsupport interpretation of intervention outcomes.\n22\n\nReferences\nAdu, M.
