    29→ABSTRACT
    30→ADHD symptoms in children are often attributed to the parenting that they experience, despite strong evidence for genetic influences. Here, we tested whether associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms reflected shared family-level confounding. At the population level, random-intercept cross-lagged panel models (RI-CLPM) suggested significant bidirectional processes between children’s ADHD symptoms and their experiences of harsh discipline, constructive parenting, and negative parent-child feelings across the ages 8, 12, and 14 years (N = 7,429, including 2,762 monozygotic twins, 2393 same-sex dizygotic twins, and 4,017 girls). In twin-difference CLPM and RI-CLPM that controlled for family-level confounding, the associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms were attenuated and became non-significant. Our findings challenge claims that parenting can cause ADHD in children and underscore the importance of accounting for family-level confounding when studying parent–child dynamics in families affected by ADHD.

    33→Key words: ADHD; constructive parenting; harsh discipline; negative parent-child feelings; twin difference model; longitudinal; 
    34→Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a common neurodevelopmental condition that affects children and adults worldwide (Faraone et al., 2024; Merikangas et al., 2010; Polanczyk et al., 2007). It is characterised by persistent symptoms of inattention, such as difficulties in organising tasks and distractability, and hyperactivity-impulsivity, including fidgeting and excessive talking. Children and adolescents with ADHD symptoms often show clinically significant impairments across multiple domains of functioning (see Barkley, 2015, for a review) that persist into adulthood (e.g., Di Lorenzo, 2021; Jennum et al., 2025). 
    35→Parenting is often framed as a salient contributor to children’s ADHD symptoms, despite scientific consensus that ADHD is strongly influenced by genetic factors. Parenting-causal narratives, including claims about (lack of) discipline, attachment disruption, overmedicalisation, or environmental mismanagement, continue to shape the public discourse on children’s ADHD (e.g., Amen, 2013; Haidt, 2024; Komisar, 2021; Maté, 2023). Such narratives may reinforce parental blame, undermine public acceptance of neurodevelopmental accounts of ADHD, and reduce support for effective interventions and policies that target children, not parents. Establishing robust empirical evidence on the interplay of parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms is therefore an urgent priority – not only to advance psychological science, but also to counter unsupported causal narratives that may influence stigma, treatment preferences, and policy decisions. 
    36→ADHD is highly heritable at about 80%, meaning associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms are likely to reflect shared genetic liabilities (i.e., genetic transmission; Faraone & Larsson, 2018; Gidziela et al., 2023; Pingault et al., 2023; Polderman et al., 2015; de Zeeuw et al., 2020). In this case, associations observed at the population level (i.e., between families, or in one child per family) would be attenuated or absent in within-family analyses that examine whether differences in parenting between siblings are associated with their differences in ADHD symptoms. 
    37→No previous study has directly tested whether parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms are associated within families, independent of genetic and environmental confounding at the family level (cf., Agnew-Blais et al., 2022; D’Onofrio et al., 2014; Pettersson et al., 2015; Rosenqvist et al., 2019). To address this gap, we charted here the developmental interplay between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms from middle childhood into adolescence, modelling bidirectional processes over time while controlling for family-level confounding. We asked whether earlier parenting predicted later ADHD symptoms within children and vice versa, and whether these associations were consistent with interpretations of causal influences.

    39→Parenting in families affected by ADHD
    40→Children with ADHD symptoms often exhibit behaviours that are challenging for parents, such as inattention, rule-breaking, and difficulties initiating and completing tasks. These behaviours may elicit suboptimal parenting responses, including harsh discipline (e.g., shouting, smacking) and negative feelings (e.g., frustration, anger), as parents attempt to manage behaviour. Suboptimal parenting, in turn, may exacerbate children’s ADHD symptoms, giving rise to bidirectional transactional processes over time (Belsky, 1984). 
    41→Because ADHD is a highly heritable neurodevelopmental disorder, children with ADHD are likely to have parents who also experience ADHD symptoms (Minde et al., 2003). Parenting requires patience, sustained attention, planning, and problem solving – capabilities that are often impaired in adults with ADHD (e.g., Murray & Johnston, 2006; Mokrova et al., 2010). ADHD symptoms can interfere with constructive parenting, for example remaining calm and providing consistent, firm feedback when children misbehave. This may contribute to increased negative parent–child interactions, cumulating in a reciprocal escalation process that complicates the regulation of both parenting and child behaviours. It is plausible that constructive parenting may help reduce children’s ADHD symptoms over time, while harsh discipline and negative feelings may exacerbate them. Conversely, children who show higher levels of ADHD symptoms may elicit more harsh discipline, less constructive parenting, and negative parent-child feelings, consistent with bidirectional processes. However, such bidirectional influences may reflect shared familial influences rather than causal effects (Pingault et al., 2023; de Zeeuw et al., 2020). 
    42→
    43→The current study 
    44→We tested here whether parents’ ratings of children’s ADHD symptoms at earlier ages predicted children’s reports of the parenting that they received at later ages, and vice versa. We opted for this cross-informant approach for two reasons. First, parents tend to be the first observers of, and responders to, children’s ADHD symptoms, including informing clinical assessments (Faraone et al., 2024). Conversely, children are the recipients and interpreters of parenting: their perceptions of parenting tend to predict more strongly child outcomes than their parents’ reports (De Los Reyes & Kazdin, 2005). Second, our cross-informant approach reduces the risk of shared method variance affecting our findings (i.e., variance due to observer bias that affect the reporting of both parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms). 
    45→We focused on three parenting constructs, including harsh discipline, such as smacking and shouting, constructive parenting, for example being firm and transparent, and negative parent-child feelings, including experiences of frustration, anger, and impatience. Harsh discipline and negative parent-child feelings may emerge in response to challenging child behaviours and can contribute to escalating parent–child conflicts, while constructive parenting may help children manage difficulties in attention, impulse control, and emotional regulation. Together, these parenting constructs capture distinct but complementary aspects of the parent–child dynamic that are particularly salient in the context ADHD. 
    46→We hypothesized bidirectional associations, such that children who showed more ADHD symptoms experienced more harsh discipline and negative parent-child feelings, and less constructive parenting over time, and that within-children changes in parenting predicted within-children’s changes in ADHD symptoms. We also expected that these associations would occur at the population level but become attenuated and non-significant in within-family analyses of dizygotic and monozygotic twins, respectively. Such patterns would be consistent with non-causal explanations for the transmission of ADHD due to shared genetic and environmental factors (Corfield et al., 2016; Pingault et al., 2023; de Zeeuw et al., 2020). Finding that parenting is unlikely to exert causal influences on children’s ADHD symptoms will help to further dispel persistent myths of ‘poor parenting’ as driver of children’s neurodevelopmental problems. Finding that parenting is associated with children’s ADHD symptoms independent of family-level confounding would recommend it as an effective target for intervention.

    57→METHODS
    58→
    59→Sample
    60→Data came from the Twins Early Development Study (TEDS), which recruited over 10,000 families with twins born between 1994 and 1996 in England and Wales (Lockhart et al., 2023). At the twins’ age of 18 months, 13,759 families participated; by the time of middle childhood, 9,129 families contributed data, and when the twins reached adolescence, 8,711 families participated (Rimfeld et al., 2019). TEDS families were broadly representative of other UK families in 1990s (Rimfeld et al., 2019). For example, 93% of the TEDS families identified as white versus 93% UK families, and 44% of TEDS mothers being employed at twins’ ages 2-4 years versus 50% of UK mothers (Rimfeld et al., 2019). TEDS project approval (05.Q0706/228) was granted by the ethics committee for the Institute of Psychiatry, Psychology and Neuroscience at King’s College London.
    61→The maximum size of the current study’s analysis sample was N = 7,429, including 2,762 monozygotic twins, 2,393 same-sex dizygotic twins, and 2,274 opposite-sex dizygotic twins. Fifty-four percent of the sample were girls (N = 4,017). The analysis sample included all individual twins for whom were available (a) two or more waves of parent-ratings of ADHD between the ages of 8 and 14 years, (b) two or more waves of one domain of child-reported parenting, including harsh discipline, constructive parenting, and negative parent-child feelings, between the ages of 9 and 14 years, and (c) gender and zygosity. These criteria were applied to ensure sufficient within-person variation for estimating longitudinal associations while retaining the largest possible sample under full information maximum likelihood estimation (FIML). 
    62→
    63→Measures
    64→
    65→ADHD symptoms
    66→At twins’ ages 8, 12, and 14 years, parents completed the ADHD subscale of the Conners’ Parent Rating Scale–Revised (Conners ADHD; Conners, Sitarenios, Parker, & Epstein, 1998). Conners ADHD comprises 18 items that are closely linked with the DSM-IV criteria for ADHD. The scale assesses hyperactivity-impulsivity and inattentiveness with 9 items each. Parents rated children’s behaviours on a 4-point Likert scale from (0) ‘not true at all’ to (3) ‘very much true’. The maximum possible score was 54. Individuals with clinical ADHD diagnoses score on average higher on the measure than controls (Conners et al., 1998), supporting its construct validity.
    67→Parents and children also completed the Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire (SDQ; Goodman, 1997) at twins’ ages 9, 12, and 16 years. The SDQ is a widely used, brief screening questionnaire for 2-17-year-olds to detect emotional or behavioural problems in childhood (Goodman, 2001; Goodman et al., 2010). It includes five items that assess children’s tendencies for hyperactivity and inattention. Parents rated each child, and each twin provided self-reports, using a 3-point scale from (0) ‘not true’ to (1) ‘somewhat true’ to (2) ‘certainly true’. The maximum possible score was 10. 
    68→
    69→Harsh discipline and constructive parenting
    70→At twins’ ages 9, 12, and 14 years, parents and children reported on whether smacking and shouting were used to discipline a child, as well as whether parents were firm and calm and reasoned with a child when they had misbehaved. Responses were recorded from ‘not true’ (0) to ‘quite true’ (1) to ‘very true’ (2). Two subscales of two items each were derived, including harsh discipline (shout, smack) and constructive parenting (reasoning, being firm and calm). Average sum scores were computed for each subscale, with scores ranging from 0 to 2. Children’s ratings of harsh discipline correlated on average .30 across time, while parent-ratings correlated .46, and the average cross-informant correlation was .33. For constructive parenting, children’s ratings correlated on average .24 over time and parents at .32, with the average cross-informant correlation at .16.
    71→Negative parent-child feelings
    72→At twins’ ages 9, 12, and 14 years, parents and children rated whether they felt towards each other angry, frustrated, impatient, or needing space, on a scale ranging from ‘rarely or never’ (0) to ‘sometimes’ (1) to ‘often’ (2). The items were adapted from the Parent Feelings Questionnaire (Deater-Deckard, 2000). Average sum scores were computed, with scores ranging from 0 to 2. Children’s ratings of negative parent-child feelings correlated on average .37 across ages, while parent-ratings correlated .57, and the average cross-informant correlation was .40.

    78→Parents reported zygosity at twins’ age 18 months using questionnaires that were 95% accurate (Price et al., 2000). DNA testing was offered in cases where zygosity was unclear (Lockhart et al., 2023).

    80→Statistical analysis
    81→All analyses were conducted in R; models were fitted using lavaan (Rosseel, 2012). Model fit was evaluated using χ², CFI, and RMSEA with 95% Confidence Intervals (CI). Nested models (e.g., multi-group models testing gender differences) were compared using χ² difference tests and changes in CFI, whereas non-nested models (e.g., across zygosity groups or informants) were compared based on parameter estimates. 
    82→To test reciprocal longitudinal associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms, we fitted Random Intercept Cross-Lagged Panel Models (RI-CLPM), which separate stable between-individual differences from within-individual fluctuations over time. The cross-lagged paths therefore capture whether deviations from an individual’s typical level of ADHD symptoms, as specified by the random intercept, predict subsequent deviations in parenting, and vice versa. Models were estimated across ages 8, 12, and 14 years for Conners’ ADHD, and ages 9, 12, and 14 years for harsh discipline, constructive parenting, and negative parent-child feelings. Cross-lagged paths were specified between the closest available waves (e.g., ADHD at age 8 predicting parenting at age 9, and parenting at age 9 predicting ADHD at age 12). Although the earliest assessment of AHDH and parenting were not identical (age 8 and age 9 years), they reflect comparable developmental periods. RI-CLPM were fitted for each parenting measure (i.e., three models), with Standard Errors clustered at the family level to account for the nested data structure (i.e., twins). 
    83→Because gender differences have been reported in both level and distribution of ADHD (Arnett et al., 2015; Williams et al., 2025), gender may moderate the association between parenting and ADHD symptoms. We therefore included gender as a time-invariant covariate predicting the random intercepts, to account for mean-level differences between boys and girls in ADHD symptoms and parenting. In a next step, we fitted a multi-group RI-CLPM, comparing an unconstrained model, in which autoregressive and cross-lagged paths are allowed to vary across boys and girls, to a constrained model where these paths were fixed to equality. This model was fitted to a subsample including one randomly selected twin per pair.
    84→To test whether the cross-lagged paths between parenting and ADHD symptoms were consistent with causal influences, we fitted twin-difference models, in subsamples of same-sex dizygotic (DZ) and monozygotic (MZ) twins. Twin-difference models control for family-level confounding by accounting for shared genetic and environmental influences. Such models allow comparing population-level estimates (i.e., based on one child per family or between-family model) to estimates that are derived within families (e.g., Corfield et al., 2016). In MZ twins, who are genetically identical, within-family estimates can provide evidence consistent with causal processes, because all variance that makes the twins similar has been controlled for (i.e., 100% shared genes and shared family environment). If within-pair differences in ADHD symptoms predict differences in parenting, this would be consistent with ADHD having causal influences on parenting. Conversely, if within-pair differences in parenting predict differences in ADHD, this would be consistent with parenting affecting ADHD in a causal fashion, conditional on model assumptions. As with all observational studies, however, these models cannot establish causality definitively; rather, persistence of within-family associations after controlling for familial confounding would be consistent with causal interpretations.
    85→ We fitted cross-lagged panel models (CLPM) to the signed twin difference estimates (twin 1 – twin 2, with ordering held constant across waves; twin 1 and 2 were randomly allocated) to test whether earlier within-pair differences in ADHD predict later within-pair differences in parenting (and vice versa) after controlling for family-level confounding. We fitted the CLPM first for same-sex DZ twins and then for MZ twins. Next, we fitted RI-CLPM to the twin difference estimates, which additionally removes stable within-pair differences over time. If an effect exists in CLPM but disappears in RI-CLPM, the association was likely driven by stable differences, not by dynamic processes, meaning that one twin scores consistently higher on ADHD and consistently receives different parenting. In this case, changes in ADHD would not cause changes in parenting, or vice versa.

    87→         RESULTS
    88→
    89→Descriptives for all measures are in Table S1, and fit index estimates for all models are in Table S2. Density plots for gender differences in parenting and ADHD symptoms are in Figure S1. Across RI-CLPM, boys were rated higher than girls on ADHD symptoms (βs = .28–.29, p < .001), and they experienced on average more harsh discipline (β = .15, p < .001) and negative parent-child feelings (β = .19, p < .001) than girls. No gender difference emerged for constructive parenting (β = −.04, p = .122). In multi-group RI-CLPM (one randomly selected twin per pair, N = 1,710 boys and N = 2,004 girls), gender did not moderate the constructs stability and the models’ cross-lagged dynamics (i.e., harsh discipline: χ² diff(8) = 7.39, p = .496; constructive parenting: χ² diff(8) = 12.11, p = .146; negative parent-child feelings: χ² diff(8) = 11.73, p = .164). We therefore proceeded to interpreting the RI-CLPM results with gender as time-invariant covariate of the latent intercept factors for ADHD and parenting.
    90→Tables S3-S5 include all path estimates for the RI-CLPM at the population level. Within-person ADHD deviations showed moderate temporal stability, while parenting processes were less stable over time. That is, children’s ADHD symptoms appeared more trait-like, while parenting were state-dependent and dynamic. Children who reported more harsh discipline and greater negative parent-child feelings at earlier ages later showed increases in ADHD, while elevated ADHD predicted increases in harsh parenting and negative parent-child feelings. These findings suggest that harsh discipline and negative parent-child feelings could be involved in reinforcing cycles with children’s ADHD symptoms, akin to a reciprocal escalation process. By contrast, within-children changes in reports of constructive parenting did not predict later ADHD symptoms, and associations in the reverse direction were inconsistent. 
    91→The twin difference scores showed sufficient variance to proceed with the twin-difference CLPM and RI-CLPM models (details are in the Supplementary Materials). Tables S6-S11 show path estimates from the twin-difference CLPM in MZ and DZ twins. In same-sex DZ twin pairs (N = 1,164), associations between harsh discipline and children’s ADHD symptoms were attenuated, suggesting that their reciprocal effects at the population level reflected familial confounding due to shared environmental influences. Likewise, the effects negative parent-child feelings on children’s ADHD symptoms became attenuated in the CLPM in DZ, while children’s ADHD symptoms on negative parent-child feelings were robust and similar to those at the population level. For constructive parenting, none of the cross-lagged paths were significant in the CLPM in DZ. 
    92→In the MZ twin pairs (N = 1,346), cross-lagged associations with children’s ADHD symptoms were not significant across all three parenting measures. That is, the child-to-parent effects that were independent of family-level confounding due to shared environmental influences could be accounted for by genetic confounding at the family-level. We found these findings confirmed in the twin-difference RI-CLPM (path estimates are in Tables S12-14). In summary, our findings suggests that the reciprocal associations seen at the population level between ADHD and parenting are fully accounted for by family-level confounding due to genetic and environmental influences (Figure 1).
    93→We conducted a series of sensitivity analyses, in line with data availability (see details in the Supplementary Materials). Briefly, we specified our models across informant combinations (e.g., child-reported ADHD and parent-reported parenting), using the SDQ to assess children’s ADHD symptoms at ages 9, 12, and 16 years. Our findings from the sensitivity analyses were similar to those observed in the main analyses, confirming their robustness across informant and measurement instruments.

   103→DISCUSSION
   104→We charted developmental interplay between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms from middle childhood into adolescence, while testing whether observed associations were consistent with causal influences. At the population level, we observed evidence for reciprocal associations between ADHD symptoms and children’s experiences of harsh discipline and negative parent–child feelings. Associations between ADHD symptoms and constructive parenting were weaker and less consistent. Overall, these findings confirmed our hypothesis and aligned with other reports of significant associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms apparent at the population level (e.g., Dvorsky & Langberg, 2016; Mokrova et al., 2010; Murray & Johnston, 2006; Park et al., 2017). 
   105→
   106→We also found that family-level confounding accounted for these population-level associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms. That is, differences in parenting between two twins in a family did not predict the twins’ later differences in ADHD symptoms, nor did differences in ADHD symptoms predict differences in parenting within families. The longitudinal reciprocal dynamics that we observed at the population level are therefore unlikely to reflect causal effects. Instead, our findings suggest that shared familial influences, including genetic factors, simultaneously shape children’s ADHD symptoms and the parenting that they experience. Our study is the first to test directly whether associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms can be explained by confounding at the family level. Our findings corroborate the conclusions of polygenic score-based studies, which suggested that the intergenerational transmission of ADHD is due to the genetic variants that parents and offspring share (e.g., Pingault et al., 2023; de Zeeuw et al., 2020). 
   107→
   108→We caution that our results should not be interpreted to imply that parenting is unimportant for children with ADHD. Instead, our findings contest claims that typical variation in parenting practices can cause children’s ADHD (cf., Amen, 2013; Haidt, 2024; Komisar, 2021; Mate, 2023). Narratives that children’s attentional difficulties follow from poor discipline, insufficient boundaries, or attachment problems are appealing, in part because the association between parenting and ADHD is observable in everyday life (i.e., at the population level). Yet, inferences of casual influences of parenting on ADHD symptoms or vice versa are very likely mistaken. Reporting robust empirical evidence, like we do here, is a first step to dispel the myth that parenting causes children’s neurodevelopmental disorders. The next challenge is to effectively communicate the findings, perhaps even through formal instruction (Donovan et al., 2020), so that families can receive optimal long-term support. 
   109→ 
   110→Although our findings suggest that parenting may not be an effective target for efforts that seek to reduce children’s ADHD symptoms, parenting interventions can provide important benefits, such as improving family relationships, reducing conflict, and helping parents manage children’s challenging behaviours (Coates et al., 2015). However, the effectiveness of such interventions should not be interpreted as evidence that parenting causes ADHD. Instead, interventions that involve parents may be most successful when they acknowledge ADHD as a neurodevelopmental condition and focus on adapting to its consequences, rather than aiming to alter children’s symptoms. 
   111→
   112→Limitations
   113→Our findings should be interpreted considering the study’s key limitations. First, whether children received any treatment for their ADHD symptoms was not recorded. However, pharmacologic as well as nonpharmacologic treatments can affect changes in the associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms (cf. Coates et al., 2015). Second, parenting was assessed via brief scales, which can lead to low reliability and validity and thus, increase measurement error. The average stability of our parenting domains across ages and informants aligned, however, with estimates from samples of similar ages, including measures with larger numbers of items (Carrasco et al., 2011; Korelitz & Garber, 2016; Van Leeuwen et al., 2004). In addition, our results were consistent with our hypotheses, as well as across informants and measures of ADHD symptoms, suggesting that measurement error had likely minimal effects on our findings. Third, the assessments of parenting and ADHD symptoms were not all identical in time, even though they mapped closely onto comparable developmental periods. Finally, our twin-difference models provided a stringent test of whether within-family differences in parenting were associated with within-family differences in ADHD symptoms over time. However, these models cannot directly estimate how much of the population-level association between parenting and ADHD is explained by familial confounding, for which a children-of-twins design is needed (McAdams et al., 2018). 
   114→
   115→Conclusions
   116→Our study showed that associations between parenting and children’s ADHD symptoms that are apparent at the population level are likely non-causal and can be 
   117→explained by family-level confounding. These results emerged robustly across informants (i.e., parents versus children) and measures of ADHD. Our findings directly contradict claims that parenting causes children’s ADHD symptoms, exposing the fallaciousness of parent blame, which sadly continues to accompany discussions of children’s neurodevelopmental disorders.
