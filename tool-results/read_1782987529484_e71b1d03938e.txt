    23→Abstract
    24→Background
    25→Despite the importance of social factors in the initiation and escalation of alcohol use,
    26→social processes related to alcohol use severity are typically studied in isolation. Because real-
    27→world social processes occur concurrently and may interact with one another as well as with
    28→age and sex, a network approach may provide a more ecologically valid framework. Hence, we
    29→used a network approach to examine associations between different social cognition measures
    30→and alcohol use severity, while also assessing the roles of age and sex.
    31→Methods
    32→Alcohol users (N=936; 16-81yrs) completed the social and conformity subscales of the
    33→Drinking Motives Questionnaire Revised, the Social Reward Questionnaire, the Social
    34→Attunement Questionnaire, and measures of alcohol use severity. Using network analysis, we
    35→examined unique associations between social measures and alcohol use severity, the
    36→moderating role of age, and sex differences.
    37→Results
    38→Alcohol use severity was linked to social drinking motives and sociability (i.e., the
    39→tendency to participate in social gatherings). In the age-moderated network, associations were
    40→largely stable across age, except for the association between sociability and alcohol use
    41→severity, which was weaker in older participants. We found no sex differences in the pattern of
    42→associations.
    43→Discussion
    44→Alcohol use severity was primarily associated with general social engagement, while
    45→more specific social-cognitive measures were only indirectly related. Social drinking contexts
    46→may therefore be a key pathway to greater severity, especially in younger drinkers. Associations
    47→did not differ by sex, suggesting previously reported sex differences may reflect differences in
    48→prevalence, rather than sex moderating social factors.
    49→Keywords: Alcohol, alcohol use disorder, social cognition, age, sex

    52→Introduction
    53→The most common context in which alcohol consumption takes place is social, for
    54→example, with friends or colleagues, especially when individuals are young [1,2]. Although the
    55→importance of social factors in relation to both initiation and escalation of alcohol use is well-
    56→recognized [3,4], they are often studied in isolation. Furthermore, despite our knowledge on
    57→age [5,6] and sex differences in social processing and alcohol use [7–9], these factors are often
    58→ignored. Network analysis may provide valuable insights by offering a birds-eye view of how
    59→different social measures may be associated, while modelling their unique associations after
    60→accounting for intercorrelations. Hence, this study used a network approach to examine the
    61→cross-sectional pattern of associations between key social predictors of alcohol use severity,
    62→while considering the roles of age and sex in these associations.
    63→Among the social factors commonly associated with alcohol use severity, social and
    64→conformity drinking motives such as drinking to enhance social interactions or to fit into a
    65→group are the most extensively studied [10]. While endorsement and strength of social motives
    66→are more commonly associated with binge drinking and frequency of use [11], conformity
    67→motives are more commonly associated with lower levels of use [10]. Social and conformity
    68→motives are associated with alcohol use severity during young adulthood (16-36yrs) and
    69→decline in both endorsement and predictive value later in life [12]. Historically, research has
    70→suggested that men are more inclined to drink for social and conformity reasons than women
    71→[13], however, recent research indicates that this gap might be narrowing [14].
    72→The higher endorsement of social and conformity motives during adolescence and young
    73→adulthood may reflect heightened social reward sensitivity, which has consistently been linked
    74→to alcohol use severity across an extensive body of literature [4,15–17]. Social reward can be
    75→defined as the reward value attributed to social situations, such as: 1) reflecting the value
    76→attributed to being liked by others (admiration); 2) the enjoyment of antisocial behavior
    77→3
    78→
    79→(negative social potency); 3) the preference for more passive social roles (passivity); 4) an
    80→overall desire for social interactions (sociability); 5) the enjoyment of reciprocal peer
    81→relationships (prosocial interactions); 6) the enjoyment of sexual experiences (sexual
    82→relationships) [18]. Altikulaç et al. showed age-related changes in social reward processing in
    83→each domain except for sociability and sexual relationships (which was omitted) [19].
    84→Admiration showed a quadratic effect peaking at approximately the age of 21, prosocial
    85→interactions showed a linear increase with age, and passivity showed a dip in the quadratic
    86→effect with a minimum observed at approximately the age of 15. Negative social potency
    87→decreased with age for women but remained stable for men, and women scored higher on
    88→prosocial interactions compared to men across all ages, further highlighting the moderating
    89→roles of age and sex.
    90→Social learning is thought to play an important role in shaping social drinking behaviours
    91→[20]. One approach to studying social influences on drinking behaviour is the relatively novel
    92→concept of social attunement: the tendency to align behavior with the social environment
    93→[21,22]. Social attunement has been associated with higher alcohol use in younger individuals
    94→(16-19yrs) with peers who engage in relatively heavy drinking [22]. Furthermore, Kuhns et al.
    95→showed that adolescents with higher neural responses to social alcohol cues compared to non-
    96→social alcohol cues, were also more inclined to harmonize their behavior with their social
    97→environment, while in adults, the opposite was the case [23]. Likewise, social attunement was
    98→associated with increased alcohol use over time in adolescents but decreased use in adults [23].
    99→Romein et al. found that associations between social attunement and alcohol use severity may
   100→vary across age and social settings: in social situations with soda, social attunement was
   101→positively associated with alcohol use in younger participants, whereas this association became
   102→negative in older participants [24]. Concurrently, in social settings without drinks, the opposite
   103→pattern was observed. When exploring the role of gender in relation to social attunement, it was
   104→4
   105→
   106→found that men scored higher on the behavioral subscale of the Social Attunement
   107→Questionnaire (SAQ; i.e., the degree to which individuals report adjusting their behavior to
   108→their social environment) compared to women [22], suggesting that men could be more inclined
   109→to adapt their behavior to their social environment compared to women.
   110→Overall, drinking motives, social reward, and social attunement, each represent a
   111→different element in the pathway connecting social processes to alcohol use. Social reward may
   112→capture the more immediate reinforcing value of drinking in a social context. Social motives
   113→capture broader social reasons for drinking and may therefore be closely associated with social
   114→reward. In contrast, conformity motives emphasize drinking to fit in socially, and may thus be
   115→more closely associated with social attunement, which captures sensitivity to and alignment
   116→with the social environment. Social processes need to be studied in combination rather than in
   117→isolation, along with age and sex, to better understand their connections with alcohol use
   118→severity. Hence, the aims of the present study were threefold and exploratory: 1) to assess how
   119→social attunement, social reward, and social drinking motives are associated with each other as
   120→well as alcohol use severity; 2) to assess whether age moderates these associations; 3) to
   121→examine whether these associations differ by sex.
   122→Methods
   123→Sample
   124→Data from two studies were merged for analysis, including a total of 1050 participants
   125→(16-81yrs). Study 1 was conducted between 2022 and 2025 and contained 811 participants
   126→[25]. Study 2 took place between 2023 and 2026 and included 239 participants. Participants for
   127→both studies were recruited through social media and in-person distribution of flyers, whereas
   128→for study 1 recruitment also took place via Prolific [26] and the Erasmus University Rotterdam
   129→participant pool.
   130→5
   131→
   132→For study 1, inclusion criteria were proficiency in either Dutch or English, and age over
   133→15. For study 2, data collection was part of an MRI-study with stricter inclusion criteria (Table
   134→S1) covering MRI-contraindications and alcohol and cannabis use criteria. Study 2 consisted
   135→of three groups containing: heavy alcohol users (n=80), heavy cannabis users (n=79), and
   136→controls (n=80) which were combined into a single sample. Individuals that did not use alcohol
   137→(n=140) were removed from the analyses, resulting in a final sample of n=936 participants.
   138→Informed consent was given prior to participation and full study protocols were approved by
   139→the Ethics Review Committees of Erasmus University Rotterdam (study 1; ETH2122-0311)
   140→and the University of Amsterdam (study 2: FMG-1073-2023).
   141→Procedure
   142→Study 1 took place online in English or Dutch, depending on preference of the participant.
   143→Two blocks of questionnaires were separated by a series of experimental tasks. The first block
   144→of questionnaires focused on demographics, whereas the second block contained
   145→questionnaires on substance use and mental health. Compensation was provided through a
   146→lottery where a gift card worth ten euros was provided to every one in five participants. A
   147→second lottery of four prepaid credit cards worth 50 euros each was raffled across all
   148→participants.
   149→Data for Study 2 was partly collected in-person onsite at the University of Amsterdam
   150→behavioral lab. In preparation for an MRI-session, participants completed a one-hour set of
   151→questionnaires online which was similar in structure to study 1. Following the MRI-scan,
   152→participants completed questionnaires focusing on substance use. Monetary compensation
   153→(€40) was provided after completion of the onsite session.
   154→6
   155→
   156→Questionnaires
   157→Demographics and substance use: single-item questions were used to assess age, sex
   158→assigned at birth, highest level of completed education, nationality, alcohol use age of onset,
   159→and lifetime use of tobacco, cannabis, and other drugs (“yes” / “no”). Alcohol use severity
   160→assessed using the 10-item Alcohol Use Disorder Identification Test, each item was scored on
   161→a scale from 0 to 4, except for items 9 and 10 which were scored 0, 2, or 4. The total score (0-
   162→40) was calculated as a sum of all items, with an AUDIT score ≥ 8 indicative of at-risk use
   163→[27]. Quantity of alcohol use (standard alcohol units), tobacco use (cigarettes), and cannabis
   164→use (grams) in the 14 days prior to study participation was measured using the timeline follow-
   165→back method [28].
   166→Social measures: social and conformity motives were measured using the Drinking
   167→Motive Questionnaire Revised [29,30]. Social motives reflect the desire to drink to enhance
   168→social experiences (e.g., “I drink to have fun with friends”), whereas the conformity subscale
   169→reflects the desire to follow group norms (e.g., “I drink to fit in with a group I like”). Both
   170→subscales consisted of 5 items scored from 1 to 5, with sum scores used as total subscale scores.
   171→Social reward was assessed using the Social Reward Questionnaire [18]. The SRQ
   172→consists of six subscales of which one was omitted (sexual relationships): 1) admiration (4
   173→items), reflecting the value of being admired by others; 2) negative social potency (5 items),
   174→reflecting enjoyment from malicious social behavior; 3) passivity (3 items), assesses tendency
   175→to prefer more passive social roles; 4) sociability (3 items), captures an overall desire for social
   176→interaction; 5) pro-social interactions (5 items), reflects a sense of reward derived from helping,
   177→cooperation, and positive social exchanges. Each item was scored from 1 to 7, subscale scores
   178→were calculated by averaging across items.
   179→Social attunement was measured using the 11-item Social Attunement Questionnaire
   180→[22], consisting of two subscales: 1) cognitions subscale (5 items), relating to the extent to
   181→7
   182→
   183→which someone thinks about their own behavior and how others perceive their behavior; 2)
   184→behaviors subscale (6 items), the degree to which someone adjusts their behavior to attune to
   185→the social behavior of others. Each item was scored from 1 to 7, with sum scores used as total
   186→subscale scores.
   187→Analyses
   188→Sample characteristics
   189→Group comparisons (male-female) on sample characteristics were performed using
   190→Wilcoxon rank-sum- or independent samples t-tests depending on data normality (continuous)
   191→and Chi-square (categorical) tests in R (V4.4.1). To accommodate the distributional
   192→assumptions of our data, we computed a Spearman correlation matrix (Table S9) to assess zero-
   193→order associations between all variables, including their associations with age in JASP (JASP
   194→Team, 2026; V0.96).
   195→Network models
   196→Network models were estimated in R (V4.4.1) with the bootnet package [32] and
   197→visualized using the qgraph R [33] package. We estimated LASSO regularized Gaussian
   198→Graphical Models (GGM) with the extended Bayesian information criterion least absolute
   199→shrinkage and selection operator (EBICglasso) with gamma set to 0.5, using Spearman
   200→correlations. Variables were standardized prior to estimation of the network models.
   201→First, to assess how social measures were related to each other as well as alcohol use
   202→severity, the SAQ subscales’ scores, SRQ subscales’ scores, social and conformity drinking
   203→motive scores, and AUDIT scores were included in the first network (Network 1), resulting in
   204→a total of 10 nodes.
   205→8
   206→
   207→Second, to assess the moderating role of age using the mgm (mixed graphical models)
   208→package [34], age was added to this model (Network 2). The network was estimated using
   209→LASSO regularization with EBIC model selection (γ=0.25).
   210→Third, to assess how associations between social measures and alcohol use severity may
   211→vary across sexes, separate network models were generated with the bootnet package [35] for
   212→male (Network 3) and female (Network 4) participants. Subsequently, a network comparison
   213→test was performed between networks 3 and 4, to assess differences in strength centrality (Table
   214→S7), structure, and edge weights (Table S8), using 10000 permutations. The network
   215→comparison test was implemented using the NetworkComparisonTest R package [36].
   216→We used non-parametric bootstrapping (1000 resamples) to evaluate edge stability
   217→(Figure S1, S4 S5) [32]. Stability of strength centrality was evaluated via case-dropping
   218→bootstrap resampling. Correlation-stability (CS-C) coefficients were computed to assess the
   219→reliability of the associations, with a CS-C > 0.25 indicating minium stability and CS-C > 0.50
   220→indicating good stability [35].
   221→As the age moderated network was estimated using a mixed graphical model, CS-
   222→coefficients and centrality measures were not interpreted, as node connectivity is conditional
   223→depending on the level of the moderator and therefore not fixed across the network. However,
   224→edge weights and moderation effects were bootstrapped (Figure S3) to ensure the stability of
   225→the reported findings.
   226→Node placement for network 1 was based on the Fruchterman-Reingold algorithm [37],
   227→ensuring that variables with stronger links appeared closer together. The same network
   228→structure was maintained for subsequent networks to facilitate comparison across networks,
   229→however, for networks 2, 3, and 4, the placement of the negative social potency node was
   230→slightly shifted to the right to avoid obscuring its associations with other nodes. Participants
   231→9
   232→
   233→who did not report male or female sex (n=5) were included in Networks 1 and 2 but excluded
   234→in the sex specific networks.
   235→Results
   236→Sample characteristics
   237→On average, participants were 32.08 (SD = 13.89) years old, and most participants were
   238→female (52.6%), educated at (pre-) university level (81.4%), and non-Dutch citizen (51.2%;
   239→Table 1). Participants scored on average 8.16 (SD = 6.25) on the AUDIT, with 40.5% exceeding
   240→a score of 7, indicating at-risk alcohol use [27]. Spearman correlations among network
   241→variables revealed positive bivariate associations between each social measure and alcohol use
   242→severity, except for SRQ pro-social interactions (Figure 1; full overview in Table S9). Age was
   243→negatively associated with all social measures, except SAQ cognition and conformity motives,
   244→and negatively with alcohol use severity.

   288→Discussion
   289→We assessed network associations between social measures and alcohol use severity,
   290→whether age moderates these associations, and whether they differ across sex. In the network
   291→only including social measures and alcohol use severity (Figure 2), social measures from the
   292→same scale tended to cluster together, and alcohol use severity was only linked to social
   293→drinking motives and SRQ sociability. When assessing the role of age, the age-moderated
   294→network (Figure 3) showed negative direct associations between age and SRQ admiration, SRQ
   295→sociability, SAQ behavior, and social motives. Furthermore, associations within the network
   296→remained largely stable across age, except for the SRQ sociability – AUDIT relationship, which
   297→was moderated by age, such that the association was weaker in older participants. Similarly,
   298→despite sex differences being observed in scores on almost half of the social measures (Table
   299→1), associations in the network remained consistent across sex (Figure 4). Taken together, these
   300→network findings complement more conventional bivariate approaches and suggest that
   301→observed age and sex differences in the role of drinking motives, social reward, and social
   302→attunement in alcohol use severity largely reflect differences in the prevalence of specific social
   303→factors — rather than differences in the underlying mechanisms linking these factors to
   304→drinking.

   306→
   307→The network analyses revealed a relatively dense network in which each social subscale
   308→was associated with subscales of the same questionnaire and at least subscales of one of the
   309→other questionnaires. Within the network (Figure 2), only social motives and SRQ sociability
   310→were directly associated with alcohol use severity. Social motives refer to drinking for social
   311→reasons, such as enjoying social events or enhancing social interactions [29], whereas
   312→sociability reflects a general preference for social interaction and participating in social events
   313→[18]. Hence, in the context of alcohol use, broad social engagement may be a more central
   314→driver than specific social traits such as prosocial interactions. However, these specific social
   315→traits remain informative in the context of alcohol use, as many are directly related to social
   316→motives and sociability, and thereby indirectly associated with severity of use. These findings
   317→highlight the benefit of the network approach and demonstrate that associations commonly
   318→found between social measures and alcohol use severity may largely reflect indirect pathways
   319→rather than unique relationships, as indicated by the broader pattern in bivariate associations
   320→(Figure 1), many of which did not emerge in the network. Looking beyond pairwise
   321→associations, the network structure may also offer insight into the underlying social constructs
   322→captured by each social measure. For example, the SAQ behavior subscale emerged as the most
   323→central node in the network (Figure S2), suggesting it may be a useful indicator of more general
   324→social tendencies. Thus, while social motives and SRQ sociability may be most informative in
   325→the context of alcohol use, the SAQ behavior subscale may better capture social behavior more
   326→generally.
   327→In the age-moderated network (Figure 3), all associations remained stable across age,
   328→except for SRQ sociability-AUDIT which weakened with age. This may be particularly
   329→relevant in the context of “maturing out of addiction” – the natural reduction of substance use
   330→as individuals age into adulthood [21,38,39]. To illustrate, the social plasticity hypothesis
   331→(SPH) [21] proposes that shifts in social environments across age initially render social factors
   332→13
   333→
   334→risk-enhancing in adolescence and young-adulthood, but increasingly protective later in life
   335→when norms around substance use change, paralleling transitions to more adult-like
   336→responsibilities (e.g., starting a family, work). The SPH suggests that social attunement drives
   337→these changes, as individuals adapt to shifting expectations and norms around substance use.
   338→Our results are broadly consistent with this hypothesis, but with an important difference: the
   339→SPH emphasizes changes in drinking behavior in relation to changing norms and expectations
   340→surrounding substance use, our findings suggest that people might instead simply participate
   341→less in alcohol-centered social gatherings. As an example, as people age, sociability may still
   342→predict alcohol use, but the association weakens because social contexts may shift, as drinking
   343→may become more linked to routine and health [40–42], and social events may function less as
   344→a cue for drinking [23]. This is broadly consistent with our previous results, showing that [12]
   345→coping and enhancement motives predicted alcohol use severity across most of the lifespan,
   346→whereas social and conformity motives were only predictors in earlier stages of life. Our
   347→findings suggest that sociability may be a key factor in this age-related pattern, given its
   348→association with social motives and the age-related attenuation of its link with alcohol use
   349→severity. Furthermore, in the correlation matrix (Figure 1), age was negatively associated with
   350→nearly all social measures and alcohol use severity, suggesting broad age-related decreases.
   351→However, in the network model, after controlling for interrelations, half of these associations
   352→remained, highlighting the benefit of the network approach in isolating unique associations,
   353→with age moderating only a single edge.
   354→Similar to age, even though males and females differed in average levels of social
   355→measures and alcohol use severity (Table 1), associations between social measures and alcohol
   356→use severity remained consistent across sex (Figure 4). Prior research has shown mixed results
   357→regarding sex differences in the associations between social measures and alcohol use
   358→[24,43,44], suggesting that these associations may be multidirectional and context dependent.
   359→14
   360→
   361→Our results suggest that previously observed effects of sex may partly reflect shared variance
   362→across social measures, rather than a direct effect of sex and gender on these associations. In
   363→essence, mean-level differences across sex may create the impression of sex-specific effects in
   364→simpler analyses, our network approach corrects for these differences across all measures,
   365→suggesting that associations remain largely stable across sex. However, the absence of sex
   366→differences across network structures may also reflect a narrowing gender gap not only in
   367→alcohol use, but also in social motives for drinking [14,45,46]. Furthermore, while the same
   368→network may exist across sexes, mean level differences may still influence how the network is
   369→expressed within each group. For instance, males scored higher on SRQ negative social potency
   370→and DMQ conformity, and females higher on SAQ cognition, SRQ prosocial interactions, and
   371→SRQ sociability (Table 1), the latter directly linked to alcohol use severity.
   372→The present study is the first to assess associations between social measures and alcohol
   373→use severity combined, rather than in isolation. However, several important limitations need to
   374→be considered. Age-related changes may reflect cohort effects rather than true developmental
   375→variation due to the cross-sectional design of the study. Furthermore, the moderation effect
   376→involving age should be interpreted with caution. Although the effect was retained in 65% of
   377→bootstrap samples, indicating some robustness, its stability was only moderate and the
   378→bootstrap interval approached zero. Future studies with larger samples are needed to establish
   379→the reliability of age-related differences in the network structure. Participants may have been
   380→intoxicated during participation due to the online nature of the study. The sample included a
   381→higher proportion of younger, higher educated, and cisgender individuals, limiting the
   382→generalizability of the results. Moreover, social drinking norms may vary across cultures
   383→[47,48], therefore, our results may not translate to all contexts. Additionally, the included social
   384→measures may not capture the full scope of social cognition, future research may include
   385→additional questionnaires (e.g., [49,50]). Furthermore, the scenarios presented in the
   386→15
   387→
   388→questionnaire may not be representative of social situations commonly encountered by older
   389→individuals. Lastly, we pooled data from two samples with markedly different designs. Study
   390→2 used an extreme-groups sampling approach, which can distort cross-sectional associations
   391→relative to a continuously distributed sample.
   392→Conclusion
   393→The network approach allows us to examine unique associations between social measures
   394→and alcohol use severity, while controlling for all measures. Our results demonstrate the added
   395→value of the network approach, as many associations observed in bivariate analyses did not
   396→persist and appear to reflect indirect pathways, with broader social tendencies such as
   397→sociability emerging as central drivers of alcohol use severity in the network. Furthermore, age
   398→played a specific role in shaping this association which was weaker in older participants. Future
   399→research should assess whether the network structure changes across age by tracking
   400→individuals longitudinally. Further assessing the roles of age and sex for identifying those at
   401→long-term risk of alcohol use disorder remains crucial.
