    23→Abstract
    24→Need crafting denotes the active pursuit of experiences that satisfy the basic psychological needs for
    25→autonomy, competence, and relatedness. This RCT evaluated an online need crafting intervention among
    26→299 adolescents (M =16.34; 55% girls), examining general and domain-specific (school, leisure, social
    27→age
    28→media) effectiveness. The intervention directly improved need crafting and need satisfaction,
    29→particularly among engaged participants. Serial mediation analyses confirmed that intervention-induced
    30→increases in need crafting were associated with higher need satisfaction and lower need frustration, in
    31→turn relating to improved well-being and reduced ill-being. These effects were replicated in the school
    32→and leisure domains, but not for social media. The findings provide initial evidence for need crafting
    33→interventions in adolescence. This study was pre-registered on AsPredicted (bv8pj7; November 27,
    34→2024).

    40→Adolescence is a developmental period marked by increasing independence, agency, and responsibility,
    41→particularly in how time is allocated across daily life domains. For example, study time is often
    42→monitored less closely by parents, and adolescents gain greater decision-making autonomy in how and
    43→with whom they choose to spend their time (Lionetti et al., 2019). Against this background, the
    44→substantial increase in adolescents’ engagement with social media (e.g., Faverio & Sidoti, 2025; Nagata
    45→et al., 2025) has been widely cited as a potential contributor to secular trends of declining adolescent
    46→mental health (e.g., Faverio & Sidoti, 2025; Nagata et al., 2025). Based on the assumption that time
    47→spent on social media displaces engagement in other, more enriching activities (Hall & Liu, 2022), recent
    48→national policy initiatives, recommendations to parents, and interventions aimed at improving
    49→adolescents’ mental health have largely focused on limiting adolescents’ social media use. However,
    50→such approaches show small or inconsistent mental health effects (Ferguson et al., 2025; Ferguson, 2025;
    51→Orben et al., 2026) and do not automatically translate into greater engagement in more enriching
    52→activities (Hall et al., 2019; Hall & Liu, 2022; Röhlke, 2025). Although social media use has become a
    53→highly visible focal point in contemporary debates on adolescent mental health, an exclusive focus on
    54→this single context risks obscuring more fundamental psychological processes that operate across
    55→multiple life domains.
    56→Rather than being determined solely by the amount of time spent on particular activities or in
    57→specific (digital) contexts, Self-Determination Theory (SDT; Ryan & Deci, 2017; Ryan, 2023)
    58→emphasizes that adolescents’ mental health depends largely on whether their daily activities and social
    59→interactions are accompanied by satisfaction or frustration of the basic psychological needs for
    60→autonomy, competence, and relatedness (Vansteenkiste et al., 2020; 2023). Across various contexts such
    61→as school, leisure, and sport, need satisfaction is consistently associated with better adjustment and well-
    62→being (e.g., Evans, 2023; Meng et al., 2026), a pattern that extends to social media, where need-based
    63→experiences were found to matter more for mental health than mere time spent (Van de Casteele et al.,
    64→2023). Building on this observation, interventions may benefit from shifting their focus from restricting
    65→specific activities (e.g., social media) toward strengthening adolescents’ ability to proactively seek need-
    66→fulfilling experiences across different life domains, a skill that may foster resilience when coping with
    67→rapidly changing developmental contexts (e.g., new technologies). This approach aligns with the
    68→3

    71→emphasis in positive psychology on the cultivation of strengths and resources rather than the mere
    72→reduction of risk behaviors (Csikszentmihalyi & Seligman, 2000).
    73→The capacity to recognize need-satisfying activities and persons in one’s life and to proactively
    74→select and give priority to these activities and relationships has been conceptualized as need crafting
    75→(Laporte et al., 2021). Given its proactive and malleable nature and its robust association with
    76→adolescents' mental health, need crafting has been highlighted as a particularly relevant target for
    77→intervention (Laporte et al., 2021, Laporte et al., 2022). Although intervention studies with university
    78→students and adults suggest that need crafting can be trained (Laporte et al., 2024; van den Bogaard,
    79→2024, 2025), the effectiveness for adolescents and their generalizability across multiple life domains
    80→remain largely unexplored (van den Bogaard et al., 2026). Therefore, the current study evaluates a brief
    81→online intervention designed to strengthen adolescents’ general capacity for need crafting, thereby
    82→examining not only effects on general need satisfaction and mental health but also on need satisfaction
    83→and well-being in the specific life domains of school, leisure, and social media.
    84→Basic Psychological Needs Underlying Adolescent Mental Health Across Contexts
    85→To move beyond narrow explanations of adolescent mental health (e.g., Ferguson et al., 2025;
    86→Odgers, 2024), a theoretical framework is needed that captures the underlying psychological processes
    87→shaping adolescents’ well-being across life domains. Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT;
    88→Vansteenkiste et al., 2020; 2023), a critical mini-theory within the larger SDT (Ryan, 2023), offers such
    89→a framework by focusing on fundamental psychological needs that are assumed to be universal across
    90→contexts and fluctuating circumstances. Within BPNT, individual differences in mental health, both
    91→within and across contexts, are largely explained by variations in the satisfaction and frustration of the
    92→basic psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness (Vansteenkiste et al., 2020; 2023).
    93→Autonomy refers to experiencing a sense of volition and psychological freedom in one’s actions,
    94→thoughts, and feelings. Competence reflects experiences of effectiveness and mastery in everyday tasks
    95→and in the pursuit of meaningful goals. Relatedness involves experiencing a sense of belonging and
    96→feeling cared for in close and reciprocal relationships.
    97→These needs are considered essential psychological nutrients: whereas their satisfaction
    98→promotes well-being and optimal functioning (“bright pathway”), their active frustration undermines

   102→psychological adjustment and increases vulnerability for mental health problems (“dark pathway”)
   103→(Vansteenkiste et al., 2023). Research demonstrates the relevance of this dual-process across the lifespan
   104→(Soenens & Vansteenkiste, 2023) and specifically during adolescence, a developmental period in which
   105→higher levels of need satisfaction have consistently been associated with better psychosocial adjustment
   106→and higher well-being, whereas need frustration relates to a range of maladaptive outcomes, including
   107→emotional dysregulation, difficulties in identity development, and internalizing symptoms (Campbell,
   108→Boone, et al., 2018; Emery et al., 2016; Luyckx et al., 2009; Lorenz et al., 2026). Importantly, these
   109→effects manifest across life domains that are central during adolescence, including school (Meng et al.,
   110→2026), leisure activities (e.g., Evans, 2023; Leversen et al., 2012), and more recently social media use
   111→(Van de Casteele et al., 2023, 2024). These findings underscore that adolescents’ well-being is not
   112→shaped within a single domain, but emerges across multiple, interrelated life contexts. In addition, need-
   113→based experiences in one domain may amplify, compensate for, or undermine experiences in another
   114→domain (Milyavskaya et al., 2013; Vansteenkiste et al., 2020). For example, adolescents who experience
   115→a more balanced pattern of need satisfaction across important life domains have been found to report
   116→higher well-being and better school adjustment, above and beyond the level of need satisfaction within
   117→each domain separately (Milyavskaya et al., 2009). At the same time, not all life domains may contribute
   118→equally to well-being. To illustrate, while both offline and online (social media-based) need experiences
   119→relate to adolescents’ mental health, offline need satisfaction was found to play a more pronounced role
   120→for mental health outcomes than need satisfaction during social media use (Van de Casteele et al., 2023).
   121→This points to the importance not only of need-supportive environments, but also of adolescents' own
   122→active role in seeking out such experiences.
   123→Need Crafting as an Agentic Pathway to Psychological Need Fulfillment
   124→Need crafting refers to the capacity to proactively reflect on, select, and shape activities,
   125→relationships, and contexts that support one’s basic psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and
   126→relatedness (Laporte et al., 2021). Need crafting comprises two closely intertwined components:
   127→awareness and action. Awareness concerns insight into which activities, interactions, and contexts
   128→support one’s psychological needs. Although research consistently shows associations between need
   129→satisfaction and better mental health, the specific sources and pathways through which these needs are
   130→5
   131→
   132→NEED CRAFTING INTERVENTION STUDY
   133→fulfilled vary substantially between individuals (Vansteenkiste et al., 2023). To illustrate, some
   134→adolescents derive need satisfaction from soccer practice, while others find greater fulfillment in
   135→activities such as music or creative hobbies. Developing awareness of one’s own sources of need
   136→satisfaction is thus a crucial prerequisite for effective need crafting. Knowing what is personally need-
   137→satisfying, however, is not sufficient on its own; adolescents are more likely to experience need
   138→satisfaction when they are both willing and able to act on this awareness. The action component involves
   139→taking concrete steps to increase the likelihood of encountering need-satisfying experiences. This may
   140→involve initiating particular activities, modifying how one engages in them, or seeking out specific social
   141→partners (Laporte et al., 2021). Importantly, this skill requires a degree of experimentation and
   142→exploration, as individuals need to try out different possibilities to discover what personally fosters a
   143→sense of autonomy, competence, and relatedness.
   144→Given adolescents’ increasing cognitive capacities and the central role of exploration during this
   145→developmental period, adolescence is a particularly salient and perhaps critical phase for the
   146→development of need crafting skills (Laporte et al., 2021), which may help protect and sustain mental
   147→health (van den Bogaard et al., 2025). Whereas younger children largely depend on parents, teachers,
   148→and other socialization figures to have their psychological needs met, adolescents gradually gain greater
   149→agency in selecting need-conducive activities, relationships, and contexts (Soenens & Vansteenkiste,
   150→2023). Research has begun to demonstrate the dynamic role of need crafting in mental health during
   151→adolescence. Longitudinal and diary studies showed that adolescents’ need crafting fluctuates
   152→substantially within adolescents across time, with daily within-person increases in need crafting being
   153→systematically associated with more need satisfaction and well-being (Laporte et al., 2022; van den
   154→Bogaard et al., 2025). This protective and dynamic role of need crafting is promising in the light of
   155→recent calls for interventions among adolescents that foster strengths and resilience in this age group
   156→rather than merely reducing vulnerabilities (e.g., Vrijen & Soenens, 2026).
   157→Building on this rationale, LifeCraft was developed – an online, structured, and individual
   158→intervention comprising a mixture of psychoeducation, brainstorms, and action plans to engage in need
   159→crafting. An initial study during the COVID-19 pandemic showed that adults who completed the training
   160→showed improvements in need crafting, need satisfaction, and well-being compared to a passive control
   161→6
   162→

   164→group, with effects persisting for up to one month after the intervention (Laporte et al., 2024). These
   165→effects occurred mainly among participants displaying high engagement during the online program.
   166→Subsequent studies have replicated and extended these findings to university students (van den Bogaard
   167→et al., 2024, 2025). To date, however, research on need crafting interventions in adolescent samples
   168→remains scarce (van den Bogaard et al., 2026). Consequently, it is important to examine whether
   169→LifeCraft is also effective for adolescents, who may benefit strongly from preventive attempts to
   170→strengthen their mental health.
   171→Moreover, the effectiveness of need crafting interventions has to date only been examined at the
   172→level of general need satisfaction, leaving open the question of whether training need crafting yields
   173→domain-specific effects across life domains that are central to adolescents (e.g., school, leisure, social
   174→media). This is a theoretically relevant question, given that within SDT, need-based experiences are
   175→understood to operate at multiple levels, including the general, domain, and experience level, each of
   176→which independently and jointly contributes to well-being (Milyavskaya et al., 2012; Vallerand, 1997).
   177→Although prior correlational research points to the potential beneficial role of need crafting in specific
   178→domains such as education (Huang et al., 2024; Reeve & Tseng, 2011), physical activity (Sheldon et al.,
   179→2020), and social media use (Van de Casteele et al., 2025), differential effects of need crafting across
   180→contexts have not yet been experimentally tested. The LifeCraft intervention program is domain-
   181→unspecified, leaving participants free to reflect on and choose in which domain they want to pursue
   182→need-based experiences. Consequently, finding variety across domains would provide indirect evidence
   183→that adolescents spontaneously invest their newly developed need crafting skills more in contexts that
   184→are deeply conducive to need fulfillment, consistent with SDT's organismic dialectical perspective (Ryan
   185→& Vansteenkiste, 2023) and with findings suggesting that, for example, offline need-based experiences
   186→contribute more strongly to mental health than those arising from social media use (Van de Casteele et
   187→al., 2024). Alternatively, similar effectiveness across domains would highlight need crafting as a trans-
   188→contextual skill, suggesting that strength and resilience interventions may be as effective as, or even
   189→preferable to, context-specific approaches.

   194→The present study examines the effectiveness of an adolescent-adapted version of LifeCraft
   195→(Laporte et al., 2024; van den Bogaard et al., 2024, 2025), using a Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)
   196→including an intervention and passive control group. Program effects were first examined at the general
   197→level, capturing adolescents’ general need crafting, need-based experiences (i.e., need satisfaction,
   198→frustration), and mental health (i.e., well-being, ill-being). Second, effects were tested at the domain-
   199→level, by evaluating the effects of the intervention on domain-specific need crafting, need satisfaction,
   200→and well-being. Three domains were selected based on prior work (e.g., Van de Casteele et al., 2023),
   201→that is school, leisure, and social media. Three overarching hypotheses guided the evaluation of the
   202→program’s effectiveness.
   203→First, the study examined whether participation in LifeCraft compared to the control condition
   204→predicted direct changes in adolescents’ need crafting (i.e., hypothesized mechanism of change), need-
   205→based experiences (i.e., primary outcomes), and mental health (i.e., secondary outcomes). We expected
   206→significant program effects (Hypothesis 1), as reflected in stronger increases in need crafting, need
   207→satisfaction, and well-being, and stronger decreases in need frustration and ill-being from pre- to post-
   208→measurement among participants in the experimental condition. Given the short duration of the
   209→intervention, we expected that these effects might attenuate at follow-up but would remain detectable.
   210→As for the domain-specific tests, given the paucity of prior research, no explicit hypotheses were
   211→formulated regarding the domains in which the effects would be most pronounced.
   212→Second, the study examined whether the effectiveness of the program would depend on
   213→participants’ engagement within the intervention (i.e., homework assignments). Building on prior
   214→interventions (Laporte et al., 2024; van den Bogaard et al., 2024, 2025), we expected that adolescents
   215→with higher engagement will experience more positive changes in need crafting, need-based
   216→experiences, and mental health, whereas weaker or no effects would emerge among participants with
   217→low engagement relative to the control group (Hypothesis 2). Again, this hypothesis was first tested at
   218→the general level and consequently at the domain-specific level.
   219→Third, the study examined whether need crafting functioned as a mechanism explaining
   220→potential program effects. We expected indirect effects on mental health (i.e., secondary outcomes)
   221→through changes in need crafting (i.e., the mechanism of change) and subsequent changes in need-based
   222→8
   223→
   224→NEED CRAFTING INTERVENTION STUDY
   225→experiences (i.e., primary outcomes) from pre- to post- measurement (Hypothesis 3). The domain-level
   226→tests are restricted to the “bright pathway” from intervention-induced need crafting to well-being via
   227→need satisfaction separately for the domains of school, leisure, and social media.

   229→Participants and Procedure
   230→The sample, which was collected in two waves (October 2023 and October 2024), consisted of
   231→299 adolescents (M = 16.34; SD = .77; range 15-18), with 55% identifying as female, 66% following

   233→an academic track and 97.9% having a Belgian nationality. Participants were randomly assigned to the
   234→control (46.5%) or intervention (53.5%) condition. All participants filled out a baseline questionnaire.

   236→Participant recruitment and follow-up were embedded in a Developmental Psychology course,
   237→in which each undergraduate student recruited one adolescent participant (aged 15 to 18 years),
   238→excluding close relationships (e.g., family members or individuals from their youth associations). To
   239→ensure an equal gender distribution, students were assigned to recruit either a male or female participant.

   247→SMS schedule to remind participants of upcoming intervention sessions. All students signed a
   248→confidentiality agreement prior to contacting potential participants, committing to not disclose any
   249→information obtained from participants.

   251→for questions while participants completed a baseline questionnaire. All other questionnaires and
   252→intervention links, including a reminder if no reply was given, were sent by the first author through

   265→Program
   266→To ensure age-appropriateness, focus groups with adolescents were conducted prior to
   267→implementation, in which participants evaluated existing intervention materials (Laporte et al., 2024;
   268→van den Bogaard et al., 2024, 2025) on three dimensions: (1) clarity (e.g., vocabulary), (2) format (e.g.,
   269→session length, spacing), and (3) appeal (e.g., types of videos). Feedback was subsequently used to
   270→develop new intervention material (e.g., examples, video material) to the specific needs and preferences
   271→of an adolescent population.
   272→The intervention consisted of six online sessions spread across two weeks (Monday,
   273→Wednesday, Friday). In session one, participants were introduced to the targeted concepts (i.e., basic
   274→psychological needs and need crafting) through an adolescent-friendly animated video. Each of the six
   275→sessions focused on one basic psychological need (i.e., autonomy, competence or relatedness) and
   276→included: (a) a challenge to seek a need satisfying experience, which was introduced via a short video
   277→message, (b) an exercise to brainstorm potential activities, (c) peer testimonials presented in video
   278→format, and (d) completion of an action plan in which participants specified what activity they would
   279→undertake, when, where, and with whom, as well as potential obstacles and strategies to address them
   280→(Seo et al., 2017). A seventh optional session consisted of a set of consolidation exercises. Table S1
   281→(Supplementary Material) provides an overview of the intervention content in more detail.

   292→Need Crafting. A 12-item version of the Need Crafting Scale (NCS; Laporte et al., 2021, see
   293→for example van den Bogaard et al., 2025) was used to measure need crafting at the general-level. For
   294→each psychological need, four items were retained: two assessing awareness (e.g., “last week it was
   295→clear to me which people love me, and which people I love”) and two assessing action (e.g., “last week
   296→I tried to do things as much as possible that I really wanted to do rather than things that had to be
   297→done”). In addition, three domain-specific action items were used per domain to assess need crafting
   298→within school, leisure and social media (e.g., “[domain specific stem + I tried as much as possible
   299→to]…to do things I am good at (competence crafting).” All items were rated on a 7-point Likert scale
   300→ranging from 1 (“completely not true”) to 7 (“completely true”).
   301→Need Satisfaction and -Frustration. Need satisfaction and frustration were assessed using a
   302→12-item version of the Basic Psychological Need Satisfaction and Frustration Scale (BPNSNF; Chen et
   303→al., 2015; see for example Van de Casteele et al., 2023) within each of the three domains (i.e., school,
   304→leisure, social media). Need satisfaction (e.g., “[domain specific stem]…I felt confident that I could do
   305→things well”) and need frustration (e.g., “[domain specific stem]…most things I did felt like ‘I had to’”)
   306→were each time assessed using six items. All items were rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1
   307→(“completely not true”) to 5 (“completely true”). As no general-level measure of need satisfaction and
   308→frustration was included in the questionnaire, composite scores were computed by averaging the
   309→domain-specific scores across the three domains, serving as proxies for general need satisfaction and
   310→frustration, respectively. In the domain-level analyses, domain-specific scores were used.

   312→subjective vitality and life satisfaction. Subjective vitality was measured with the six items of the vitality
   313→measure (Ryan & Frederick, 2023; e.g., “Last week I felt a sense of liveliness and spark.”). Life
   314→satisfaction was assessed with one item from the Satisfaction with Life Scale (SWLS; Diener et al.,
   315→1985; “Last week I was satisfied with my life”). In addition, domain-specific well-being was assessed

   327→Program Engagement
   328→In the beginning of the online sessions, adolescents in the experimental condition indicated the
   329→extent to which they had executed their action plan prior to the introduction of the next challenge. This
   330→item was rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“not executed at all”) to 5 (“fully executed”).
   331→Responses of 4 (“almost completely executed”) and 5 (“completely executed”) were interpreted as
   332→indicating sufficient engagement and were therefore recoded as 1. Responses ranging from 1 to 3 were
   333→recoded as 0, reflecting low engagement. These dichotomous scores were summed across modules to
   334→obtain a total engagement score ranging from 0 to 6. Participants were classified as highly engaged
   335→(coded as 1) if they obtained a total score of 4 or higher, and as lowly engaged (coded as 0) if they scored
   336→3 or lower. This operationalization is consistent with previous LifeCraft studies (Laporte et al., 2024;
   337→van den Bogaard et al., 2024).
   338→Analytic Strategy
   339→The analytic strategy followed prior intervention studies (Laporte et al., 2024; van den Bogaard
   340→et al., 2024). All data processing, R-codes and Mplus files can be consulted via OSF
   341→(https://osf.io/4bv7y). The main hypotheses were examined using latent change models (LCMs) in
   342→Mplus X (Muthén & Muthén, 2017). LCMs estimates change over time by modelling latent variables
   343→for both intercepts (initial levels) and slopes (change) (Beyers & Goossens, 2008). Model fit was
   344→evaluated using RMSEA, SRMR, and CFI, with values of RMSEA and SRMR < .08 and CFI ≥ .90
   345→indicating acceptable fit. Missing data were handled using full information maximum likelihood.

   349→Unconditional Latent Change Models. First, unconditional models were specified including
   350→a latent intercept and two latent change factors (T1-T2 and T2-T3). These models captured mean-level
   351→change and individual differences in initial levels and change. Interpretation of condition effects on
   352→slopes depends on the direction of mean-level change. For constructs that were multidimensional, an
   353→internal-consistency approach was applied (Kishton & Widaman, 1994), using the corresponding
   354→subscales as indicators of the latent variables. Need crafting was indicated by the autonomy,
   355→competence, and relatedness subscales. For need satisfaction and frustration, domain-specific autonomy,
   356→competence, and relatedness subscales were used, with general scores computed by averaging across
   357→domains. Well-being was indicated by life satisfaction and vitality, and ill-being by depressive
   358→symptoms and fatigue. In the domain-specific models, need crafting and well-being were modeled at
   359→the item level, whereas need satisfaction was specified using domain-specific subscales.
   360→Hypothesis Testing. All hypotheses were examined using a two-step approach: first, general-
   361→level models testing overall effects; second, domain-specific models examining generalization across
   362→school, leisure, and social media. All primary analyses statistically controlled for age, gender, education
   363→type, and the timing of the baseline questionnaire. To test hypothesis 1, a first series of LCMs included
   364→condition as a predictor (dummy coded: 0 = control condition; 1 = experimental condition). To address
   365→Hypothesis 2, a second series of LCMs examined the role of program engagement. Two dummy
   366→variables were created, contrasting the control group with the high- (dummy 1) and low-engagement
   367→(dummy 2) groups. To test hypothesis 3, serial mediation LCMs were estimated to examine whether

   512→Discussion
   513→Grounded in SDT (Ryan, 2023) and aligned with a positive psychology perspective
   514→(Csikszentmihalyi & Seligman, 2000), the present study evaluated the effectiveness of an intervention
   515→designed to train adolescents in need crafting – the skill to proactively recognize and seek out
   516→experiences that satisfy one's basic psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness. To
   517→this end, 299 adolescents were randomly assigned to either a six-session online intervention or a passive
   518→control condition, with assessments at baseline, post-intervention, and a two-week follow-up. Given the
   519→promising associations between need crafting and mental health outcomes (Laporte et al., 2021; 2022;
   520→van den Bogaard et al., 2025), need crafting represents a particularly relevant intervention target,
   521→positioning adolescents as proactive agents who actively shape their day-to-day activities and
   522→relationships rather than merely responding to their environment. By examining intervention effects
   523→across multiple life domains, namely school, leisure, and social media, the present study also speaks to
   524→the potential of need crafting as a trans-contextual skill that may foster resilience across changing
   525→developmental conditions (e.g., emergence of new technologies, shifting peer norms, changes in
   526→academic demands).

   534→extend these findings to an adolescent-tailored version of the program. Three overarching findings
   535→replicated prior work: (1) the intervention successfully increased need crafting (i.e., hypothesized
   536→mechanism of change) and need satisfaction (i.e., primary outcome), (2) program engagement played a
   537→crucial role in intervention effectiveness, and (3) the hypothesized sequential process was confirmed
   538→through the serial indirect effects model.
   539→Specifically, having gone through the intervention, adolescents showed more favorable changes
   540→in general need crafting and need satisfaction from pre- to post-measurement compared to those in the
   541→control condition. However, only participants who engaged more actively with the program (i.e., who
   542→completed more than four of the daily modules) differed significantly from the control group, whereas
   543→those with lower engagement did not. These effects were maintained for at least two weeks after the
   544→intervention as the gains observed during the intervention phase did not decrease by the time of the
   545→follow-up assessment. This pattern is consistent with findings from positive psychology interventions,
   546→which show that intervention-induced gains are typically maintained rather than further amplified at
   547→follow-up (Martínez-Martínez et al., 2025). In addition, the findings should be interpreted against the
   548→backdrop of the average developmental trends in the outcomes. More precisely, mean-level changes
   549→from pre- to post-intervention, as well as from post-intervention to follow-up, showed that adaptive
   550→variables (need crafting, need satisfaction, well-being) decreased or remained stable, whereas
   551→maladaptive variables increased or stayed stable. Rather than acting as a facilitator of continued growth,
   552→LifeCraft therefore functioned primarily as a buffer, protecting participants against an average trend of
   553→worsening development observed in the control group. This interpretation is further supported by the
   554→timing of the measurements: the post- and follow-up assessments partly overlapped with the weeks
   555→leading up to adolescents' exams, a period known to challenge psychological need satisfaction and well-
   556→being (Campbell, Soenens, et al., 2018).
   557→Finally, analyses indicated indirect effects of the program on the mental health outcomes via a
   558→chain of intervening mechanisms: intervention-induced increases (or less pronounced decreases) in need
   559→crafting were associated with higher need satisfaction and lower need frustration, which in turn related
   560→19

   563→to higher well-being and lower ill-being, respectively. Together, these findings support the role of need
   564→crafting as the key mechanism through which the intervention influenced adolescents’ psychological
   565→functioning. Extending prior work among adults (Laporte et al., 2024) and higher education students
   566→(van den Bogaard et al., 2024, 2025), these results provide the first evidence that need crafting is a
   567→trainable skill among adolescents, with positive repercussions for their mental health.
   568→Not all findings, however, were consistent with prior work. First, whereas prior studies among
   569→adults – albeit only among highly engaged participants (Laporte et al., 2024) – and university students
   570→observed program-induced decreases in need frustration, no such effects emerged in the present study,
   571→neither for the overall program effect nor for the program engagement contrasts. Second, the present
   572→LifeCraft intervention among adolescents did not yield direct effects on the secondary outcomes, that
   573→is, on well-being and ill-being. Although well-being and ill-being represent more distal outcomes, which
   574→typically require more time to change than the more proximal outcomes targeted by the intervention
   575→(Klasnja et al., 2015), this finding contrasts with results among adults (Laporte et al., 2024) and one of
   576→two trials among university students (van den Bogaard et al., 2024).
   577→This variability across studies may partly reflect differences in study context: two of the previous
   578→LifeCraft interventions (with adults and university students) were conducted during the COVID-19
   579→pandemic, a period of heightened psychological distress that may have left greater room for intervention-
   580→induced improvements in well-being. This observation aligns with a broader pattern in the positive
   581→psychology literature, where interventions tend to yield stronger effects among individuals who are at
   582→greater risk for mental health problems (Bolier et al., 2013). The context of the present study is more
   583→similar to van den Bogaard et al. (2024), who also examined the effectiveness of LifeCraft in the period
   584→leading up to exams (albeit among university students). These weeks before the exams are also a period
   585→of heightened stress. Yet, unlike university students, who typically have a dedicated exam preparation
   586→period without regular classes, students in secondary education must prepare for exams while continuing
   587→to attend classes, leaving fewer opportunities for self-directed or preferred activities. Moreover, although
   588→adolescents have greater decision-making autonomy than younger children (Lionetti et al., 2018), they
   589→remain subject to substantially more parental monitoring than emerging adults (Hill & Blokland, 2023),
   590→and arguably even more during exams, further limiting their freedom to craft their needs. As such, rather

   594→than creating space for need crafting, the exam period may have introduced an additional source of
   595→pressure for some adolescents, potentially offsetting some of the intervention's benefits. Consistent with
   596→this reasoning, up to 35.2% of participants in the experimental group raised homework or studying for
   597→exams as a hinderance for completing the homework assignments. Lastly, although the core components
   598→of the LifeCraft interventions are similar across studies, other study characteristics, including differences
   599→in sample size, recruitment strategies, and intervention timing (see Table S6, supplementary materials,
   600→for an overview), may also have contributed to the observed variability in findings. Together, these
   601→factors offer a tentative account of why the present study only partially replicated the effects observed
   602→in prior need crafting interventions.
   603→Domain-Specific Effects in Leisure, School and Social Media
   604→In the domain-specific analyses, we focused exclusively on the “bright pathway” of the model,
   605→examining changes in need crafting, need satisfaction, and well-being in the school, leisure and social
   606→media domain. Examining intervention effects across three life domains served three purposes. First, it
   607→allowed for an internal replication of the intervention effects across different contexts. Second, it
   608→provided insight into the domains in which adolescents spontaneously applied the recommended
   609→intervention strategies. Third, it allowed examining whether need crafting interventions can strengthen
   610→adolescents’ psychological functioning across multiple life domains, which is theoretically important
   611→from a preventive perspective given that adolescents continuously navigate changing contexts and life
   612→circumstances.
   613→School and Leisure Domain
   614→Consistent with the findings from the general-level models, the domain-specific analyses
   615→indicated that direct intervention effects emerged for need crafting and need satisfaction in the school
   616→and leisure domains, where the serial pattern of indirect effects was replicated as well. These effects
   617→were again driven primarily by adolescents who actively engaged with the intervention challenges. No
   618→effects were found for the social media domain. Taken together, the effects observed across the school
   619→and leisure domains suggest that the benefits of need crafting training are not limited to a single life
   620→context but extend to domain-specific well-being outcomes across multiple life domains, thereby
   621→21

   624→broadening this growing line of work on need crafting (van den Bogaard et al., 2026) and highlighting
   625→need crafting as a trans-contextual skill.
   626→At the same time, some findings warrant further consideration. Notably, in the intervention
   627→group, leisure-specific need crafting and school-specific need satisfaction decreased from post-
   628→intervention to follow-up, suggesting a potential counterproductive effect. Importantly, however, this
   629→pattern was not observed among highly engaged participants, suggesting that it may be specific to those
   630→with lower program engagement. A possible interpretation is that low-engaged participants, who were
   631→exposed to some components of the intervention (i.e., psychoeducation, reflection, and initial action
   632→planning) but did not consistently commit to entire program, may have developed greater awareness of
   633→the importance of need crafting without consistently translating this awareness into action. This may
   634→have increased the perceived discrepancy between their current experiences and their desired level of
   635→need satisfaction, which could help explain why low-engaged participants sometimes reported less
   636→favorable outcomes than those in the control group. Alternatively, these effects might partly reflect pre-
   637→existing differences between engagement groups at baseline. Indeed, less engaged participants reported
   638→significantly lower need crafting and need satisfaction compared to the high engaged participants. These
   639→baseline differences suggest that lower-engaged adolescents may have represented a more vulnerable
   640→subgroup who may require more intensive or personalized support during the intervention.
   641→Social Media Domain
   642→A notable finding of the present study was that the intervention effects observed in the other
   643→domains were not replicated in the social media domain. This absence of effects suggests that training
   644→adolescents in general need crafting may not automatically transfer to how they navigate online contexts
   645→such as social media. This may indicate that some adolescents do not perceive social media platforms
   646→as equally important sources of need satisfaction as other life domains or feel less affinity with them
   647→more generally (see also Van de Casteele et al., 2023). From an SDT perspective, this finding may be
   648→in line with the organismic dialectical growth tendency, the inherent human propensity to direct energy
   649→toward contexts that are most conducive to growth and need satisfaction (Ryan & Vansteenkiste, 2023).
   650→In other words, adolescents may have intuitively prioritized domains where need crafting felt most
   651→meaningful and attainable, which in itself represents an adaptive motivational process.

   655→This interpretation should be nuanced, however, as the effects reported here reflect group-level
   656→statistics that inevitably mask individual differences. Consistent with prior research showing
   657→considerable heterogeneity in adolescents' experiences of the opportunities and risks associated with
   658→social media use (e.g., Beyens et al., 2021), social media may represent an equally or even more
   659→important source of need satisfaction for some adolescents, for whom crafting needs in this domain may
   660→be both relevant and beneficial. A complementary explanation relates to the nature of the domains
   661→themselves. School and leisure contexts represent more universal domains of adolescents’ daily lives,
   662→which may make them more suitable targets for preventive interventions. Supporting this reasoning, the
   663→unconditional models indicated mean-level changes in the school and leisure domains, whereas no
   664→comparable mean-level changes were observed in the social media domain; variability in change over
   665→time was more limited in the social media domain. This might suggest that there was less room for
   666→within-person change during the study period, and thus fewer opportunities to detect intervention-related
   667→effects (see Table S5).

   669→intervention may be warranted. Three directions might be worth exploring. A first option would be to
   670→adapt the current intervention to include more explicit attention to social media as a need crafting
   671→domain, for instance by incorporating concrete examples and challenges such as consciously curating
   672→one's feed, actively engaging with content aligned with personal interests, or initiating more meaningful
   673→interactions with peers. A second direction concerns need frustration rather than need satisfaction. Need
   674→crafting as currently conceptualized focuses primarily on maximizing need satisfaction, yet the central
   675→challenge in the social media context might be more about avoiding or coping with need frustration, for
   676→example by unfollowing certain profiles or developing coping skills when confronted with potentially
   677→harmful content (e.g., exposure to idealized or extreme content). A third direction draws on research
   678→regarding the balance between online and offline need-based experiences (Van de Casteele et al., 2025).
   679→Person-centered profile analyses among higher education students suggest that combining online and
   680→offline need crafting is associated with broader mental health benefits, whereas online crafting alone
   681→primarily predicts online well-being outcomes. Notably, only a small minority of participants (12%)
   682→engaged in such combined crafting, suggesting that future interventions might explicitly target the

   691→Limitations and Future Directions
   692→The present findings add to the growing literature on need crafting and positive psychology
   693→interventions more broadly, providing the first evidence that need crafting can be trained among
   694→adolescents, a finding that is particularly meaningful given that adolescence represents a formative
   695→window for cultivating need crafting as a lifelong skill (Laporte et al., 2021; Soenens & Vansteenkiste,
   696→2023). Several limitations should be acknowledged, however. First, the timing of the intervention and
   697→follow-up may have influenced the observed effects. As discussed earlier, the post- and follow-up
   698→assessments overlapped with the period leading up to adolescents' exams, potentially constraining
   699→opportunities for need crafting and limiting the detection of well-being effects. Future research would
   700→benefit from implementing need crafting interventions at neutral points in the academic calendar to
   701→disentangle intervention effects from contextual stressors. Moreover, the follow-up period was relatively
   702→short compared to prior need crafting interventions (Laporte et al., 2024; van den Bogaard et al., 2024,
   703→2025); longer follow-up assessments would allow for a more robust evaluation of whether intervention
   704→gains are maintained or erode over time, and whether effects on more distal outcomes such as well-being
   705→and ill-being emerge at later time points. Second, the present study was implemented as a self-guided
   706→online intervention, with undergraduate students assisting with participant recruitment and follow-up.
   707→Although a standardized manual was provided to these students, variability in how they encouraged
   708→participation and monitored progress may have introduced engagement-related variability unrelated to
   709→the intervention content itself. Future studies could address this by using more standardized recruitment
   710→procedures or by embedding the intervention within existing school structures or counselling practices
   711→(i.e., hybrid version of the intervention), which may also facilitate reach among lower-engaged
   712→adolescents. A further limitation concerns the measurement and modeling of need-based experiences.
   713→In the general-level analyses, need satisfaction was operationalized as a composite score derived by
   714→24
   715→
   716→NEED CRAFTING INTERVENTION STUDY
   717→aggregating the need satisfaction scales across the three domains. As such, this measure does not reflect
   718→a purely domain-general construct. In contrast, domain-specific measures that were used were also brief,
   719→with need crafting assessed using only three action items and well-being using three vitality items per
   720→domain. While these decisions were well-considered to reduce participant burden, future research would
   721→benefit from more comprehensive and psychometrically robust domain-specific measures, as well as a
   722→distinct domain-general need crafting assessment. Addressing these measurement limitations in future
   723→work would allow for a more precise understanding of the mechanisms through which need crafting
   724→interventions exert their effects. A final limitation concerns the demographic composition of the sample.
   725→Although nationality was assessed, race and ethnicity were not measured, which limits the
   726→generalizability of the findings to more diverse adolescent populations and precludes examination of
   727→potential differential effects across racial and ethnic groups.
   728→Conclusions
   729→The present study shows that need crafting can be trained in adolescence and may serve as a promising
   730→pathway to support mental health. In line with the principles of positive psychology, this program has
   731→the potential to foster positive development rather than merely reducing symptoms. Domain-specific
   732→effects were found in the school and leisure domains, but not in the social media domain, suggesting
   733→that adolescents are less spontaneously inclined to apply need crafting to the social media domain than
   734→to offline domains. Together, these results underscore the potential of need crafting as a preventive,
   735→trans-contextual skill that may equip adolescents to navigate diverse and evolving life domains – though
   736→the extent to which this potential generalizes across all contexts, including social media, warrants further
   737→investigation.
