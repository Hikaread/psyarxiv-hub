    38→    38→Abstract
    39→    39→Background and Aims:
    40→    40→Alcohol use disorder (AUD) involves day-to-day fluctuations in cognitive, psychological, and
    41→    41→contextual factors that are difficult to fully capture using retrospective or cross-sectional
    42→    42→assessment alone. Computational markers, such as discounting rate, ambiguity tolerance, and
    43→    43→risk preference, may provide useful indicators of day-level drinking risk, but have rarely been
    44→    44→assessed repeatedly in real-world settings or examined together with daily app-based self-reports
    45→    45→and passive smartphone-derived measures. This study examined whether these multimodal day-
    46→    46→level measures were associated with same-day nighttime drinking, focusing on whether within-
    47→    47→person changes in computational markers remained associated with drinking after accounting for
    48→    48→concurrent self-reported and contextual measures.
    49→    49→Design:
    50→    50→A 28-day longitudinal observational study using repeated smartphone-based assessments and
    51→    51→passive sensing.
    52→    52→Setting:
    53→    53→Daily-life smartphone-based monitoring of community-recruited participants in the Republic of
    54→    54→Korea.
    55→    55→Participants:
    56→    56→The final analytic sample included 143 adults meeting DSM-5 criteria for AUD.
    57→    57→Measurements:
    58→    58→The primary outcome was same-day nighttime drinking, assessed using daily app-based self-
    59→    59→reports. Daily predictors covered three domains. App-based self-reports included mood, alcohol
    60→    60→craving, meal intake, and sleep-related variables. Computational predictors were derived from
    61→    61→delay discounting and choice under risk and ambiguity tasks. Passive smartphone-derived
    62→    62→predictors included time spent at home, calls, steps, and foreground app use. Generalized linear
    63→    63→mixed-effects models examined associations between nighttime drinking and multimodal
    64→    64→predictors decomposed into within-person and between-person components.
    65→    65→Findings:
    66→    66→A multimodal model integrating computational parameters, daily app-based self-reports, and
    67→    67→passive smartphone-derived measures showed the highest explanatory power for same-day
    68→    68→nighttime drinking (marginal R² = 0.22). In this final model, higher within-person delay
    69→    69→discounting [log(k)], alcohol craving, and positive mood, as well as lower meal intake and
    70→    70→percent time at home, were associated with a higher likelihood of nighttime drinking. At the
    71→    71→between-person level, only lower average meal intake was associated with a higher likelihood of
    72→    72→nighttime drinking. Within-person fluctuations in log(k) were largely uncorrelated with other
    73→    73→day-level predictors and showed no clear next-day association.
    74→    74→Conclusions:
    75→    75→Associations with same-day nighttime drinking were observed primarily at the within-person
    76→    76→level, suggesting that day-level changes may be more informative than stable individual
    77→    77→2
    78→    78→
    79→    79→differences. Within-person increases in delay discounting remained associated with nighttime
    80→    80→drinking after accounting for other modalities, suggesting that multimodal monitoring may help
    81→    81→identify periods of elevated drinking risk.
    82→    82→Keywords: alcohol use disorder; delay discounting; multimodal monitoring; computational
    83→    83→psychiatry; within-person variability; generalized linear mixed model

    86→    86→INTRODUCTION
    87→    87→Alcohol use disorder (AUD) is a major contributor to global morbidity and mortality (1).
    88→    88→AUD is characterized by dynamic fluctuations in drinking behavior that unfold over days and
    89→    89→weeks (2,3). These fluctuations are shaped by cognitive, psychological, and contextual factors,
    90→    90→which may vary in both magnitude and timing across individuals and situations (3,4). Capturing
    91→    91→these dynamic processes is critical for understanding how alcohol use unfolds in daily life. Yet
    92→    92→fluctuating risk states may be difficult to detect through retrospective or cross-sectional
    93→    93→assessment alone (3,5,6).
    94→    94→To address this, ecological momentary assessment (EMA) and daily diary methods have
    95→    95→been widely used to track within-person fluctuations in psychological states, including mood,
    96→    96→craving, and sleep, and to examine their associations with substance use in naturalistic settings
    97→    97→(3,4,6). In addition, passive smartphone-derived measures, which are broadly referred to as
    98→    98→digital phenotyping, can capture complementary information about behavioral and contextual
    99→    99→changes in daily life (5,7–9). However, these approaches primarily capture subjective states that
   100→   100→participants can consciously report or behavioral patterns that are expressed externally, and may
   101→   101→not directly access latent cognitive processes that also contribute to drinking risk.
   102→   102→In this context, prior work has focused on latent cognitive processes relevant to substance
   103→   103→use disorders, as indexed by computational markers derived from decision-making tasks. These
   104→   104→markers include delay discounting, risk preference, and ambiguity tolerance (10,11). Delay
   105→   105→discounting, or the tendency to prefer smaller immediate rewards over larger delayed rewards,
   106→   106→has been consistently associated with greater addiction severity and substance use (12–15).
   107→   107→Decision-making under uncertainty has also been linked to addiction vulnerability. In particular,
   108→   108→greater tolerance for ambiguity has been associated with elevated relapse risk in opioid and
   109→   109→nicotine use disorders (16,17). These findings suggest that computational markers can capture
   110→   110→latent cognitive mechanisms relevant to substance use vulnerability. However, most prior work
   111→   111→has been cross-sectional, despite evidence that drinking risk fluctuates dynamically over time (3).
   112→   112→It remains important to determine whether these computational markers vary meaningfully from
   113→   113→day to day and track real-world changes in drinking-related states (18,19).
   114→   114→However, only a small number of studies have used repeated assessments to examine
   115→   115→within-person changes in computational markers (16,17,20). A key reason such studies have
   116→   116→been difficult to conduct is that traditional tasks used to estimate computational markers are
   117→   117→often lengthy and repetitive, reducing participant motivation and lowering data quality,
   118→   118→especially in clinical populations (21). To minimize participant burden and enable frequent
   119→   119→assessments, adaptive design optimization (ADO) offers a machine-learning-based approach that
   120→   120→shortens tasks to as few as 20-30 trials while maintaining reliable parameter estimation (22,23).
   121→   121→ADO thus makes it feasible to assess computational markers daily in longitudinal designs.
   122→   122→If computational markers do fluctuate meaningfully from day to day, a further question is
   123→   123→whether these fluctuations provide information about drinking risk beyond that captured by self-
   124→   124→report or contextual measures alone. Examining computational markers in combination with
   125→   125→daily app-based self-reports and passive smartphone-derived measures may help address this
   126→   126→question while providing a more comprehensive account of how drinking risk unfolds in daily
   127→   127→life. Although some studies have examined daily fluctuations in cognitive control alongside self-
   128→   128→4
   129→   129→
   130→   130→reported states (24), relatively few longitudinal alcohol studies have integrated these modalities
   131→   131→within the same design, particularly alongside repeatedly assessed computational parameters.
   132→   132→To address these challenges, we conducted a 28-day longitudinal monitoring study
   133→   133→among adults meeting DSM-5 criteria for alcohol use disorder, combining daily decision-making
   134→   134→tasks, daily app-based self-reports, and passive smartphone sensing. Participants completed two
   135→   135→well-established decision-making tasks daily, both administered via ADO. These included the
   136→   136→delay discounting task (25), indexing temporal impulsivity in preference for smaller immediate
   137→   137→over larger delayed rewards (12,13), and the choice under risk and ambiguity task (26), which
   138→   138→assesses risk and ambiguity preferences. Participants also completed daily app-based self-reports,
   139→   139→while passive smartphone sensing data, including GPS location, phone call logs, step counts, and
   140→   140→app usage were continuously collected throughout the study period (7).
   141→   141→Rather than treating drinking risk as a static trait, we conceptualized same-day nighttime
   142→   142→drinking as a dynamic outcome that fluctuates over time and may be associated with time-
   143→   143→varying changes across cognitive, psychological, and behavioral domains. We aimed to examine
   144→   144→whether day-level computational parameters, daily app-based self-reports, and passive
   145→   145→smartphone-derived measures were jointly associated with same-day nighttime drinking among
   146→   146→adults meeting DSM-5 criteria for alcohol use disorder. More specifically, we expected within-
   147→   147→person changes in computational markers would be associated with same-day drinking even after
   148→   148→accounting for concurrent self-reported and contextual measures.
   149→   149→METHODS
   150→   150→Participants
   151→   151→We recruited 213 participants (46.7% male; age M = 33.24, SD = 9.45) through online
   152→   152→advertisements, all of whom reported experiencing emotional, social, or functional problems
   153→   153→related to alcohol use. Before enrollment, all participants underwent a clinical interview with a
   154→   154→board-certified psychiatrist. AUD diagnosis and severity (mild, moderate, or severe) were
   155→   155→determined based on DSM-5 criteria based on this interview.
   156→   156→Participants were enrolled into two cohorts based on whether they underwent additional
   157→   157→laboratory assessments. One cohort participated only in the 28-day mobile app study (app-only
   158→   158→group; initially n = 143), whereas the other completed the same app protocol as part of a broader
   159→   159→research protocol that also included neuroimaging and cognitive assessments (comprehensive
   160→   160→assessment group; n = 70). These additional laboratory data were not used in the present
   161→   161→analyses. All procedures were approved by the Institutional Review Board of Seoul National
   162→   162→University (2302/002-002). All participants provided written informed consent. Data were
   163→   163→collected from July 2023 to September 2024. No formal a priori sample size calculation was
   164→   164→conducted for the present analysis. Of the 213 recruited participants, 143 were included in the
   165→   165→final analytic sample after excluding those who dropped out, did not meet DSM-5 diagnostic
   166→   166→criteria for AUD, or lacked usable data for the multimodal analyses (app-only group, n = 100;
   167→   167→comprehensive assessment group, n = 43; Figure S1).

   170→   170→Procedure
   171→   171→All participants completed a baseline questionnaire in person on the day the app was
   172→   172→installed, before beginning app use. Two mobile apps were installed on participants’
   173→   173→smartphones for data collection. The Harujjan app delivered nightly psychological surveys on
   174→   174→daily experiences and drinking behavior and collected passive smartphone sensing data
   175→   175→throughout the 28-day study period. The Cocomo app delivered two decision-making tasks each
   176→   176→day. After the 28-day study period, participants returned for a follow-up visit to complete a post-
   177→   177→study survey (Figure 1).
   178→   178→Measures
   179→   179→Baseline psychological questionnaires
   180→   180→Participants completed baseline psychological questionnaires during their initial visit,
   181→   181→including the Korean version of the Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test (AUDIT-K) (27),
   182→   182→Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI) (28), Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) (29), and the Barratt
   183→   183→Impulsiveness Scale 11-revised (BIS-11-R) (30).
   184→   184→Daily app-based self-report
   185→   185→Daily self-report data were collected using the Harujjan mobile app. Participants were
   186→   186→instructed to complete brief self-reports assessing affective states (mood, general interest), daily
   187→   187→functioning variables (meal amount, sleep duration, sleep satisfaction, sleep disturbance), and
   188→   188→alcohol-related outcomes (daily alcohol consumption, craving) before going to bed each day.
   189→   189→Along with drinking, participants also provided contextual information about the episode, such
   190→   190→as the type and amount of alcohol consumed, the time and duration of the drinking episode,
   191→   191→drinking companions, location, and drinking motives (Figure 2A). To account for alcohol use
   192→   192→occurring after the initial nightly report, participants could update the previous day’s entry by the
   193→   193→following day when necessary. In the primary analyses, self-report records were assigned to the
   194→   194→calendar day to which they referred, rather than to the submission date.
   195→   195→Daily app-based self-report measures included in the model were constructed as follows.
   196→   196→Mood was defined as the mean of the mood and general interest ratings, reflecting overall
   197→   197→positive emotional state. Alcohol craving was indexed using the daily craving rating. The meal
   198→   198→index was defined as the sum of meal amounts across breakfast, lunch, and dinner. Sleep
   199→   199→disturbance was indexed using the daily sleep disturbance rating.
   200→   200→Computational parameters from daily decision-making tasks
   201→   201→Participants completed two brief decision-making tasks each day within the Cocomo
   202→   202→mobile application. The delay discounting task (25) measured temporal discounting tendencies
   203→   203→(Figure 2B), and the choice under risk and ambiguity task assessed risk preference and ambiguity
   204→   204→tolerance (Figure 2C) (26). These tasks yielded daily estimates of key computational parameters,
   205→   205→including the delay discounting rate (𝑘), risk preference (𝛼), and ambiguity tolerance (1−𝛽).
   206→   206→6
   207→   207→
   208→   208→Because the application followed a Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)-based server schedule,
   209→   209→the daily reset occurred at 09:00 Korea Standard Time (KST) rather than 00:00 KST. To align
   210→   210→task data with participants’ local calendar day and the intended once-daily assessment schedule,
   211→   211→task records completed before 09:00 KST were excluded prior to analysis. This affected 5.8% of
   212→   212→delay discounting records and 5.8% of choice under risk and ambiguity records. Detailed
   213→   213→modeling and estimation procedures are provided in the Computational modeling section.
   214→   214→Passive smartphone-derived measures
   215→   215→Participants’ smartphones provided four types of passive smartphone sensing: GPS
   216→   216→location, phone call logs, step counts, and app usage. These data were passively recorded through
   217→   217→the sensing functions enabled by installing the Harujjan application and collected continuously
   218→   218→throughout the 28-day study period.
   219→   219→% Time at home. Location data consisted of latitude–longitude coordinates with
   220→   220→timestamps. From these data, we derived daily indices capturing mobility patterns. Points of
   221→   221→interest (PoIs) were identified using the DPLocate framework (31), and the most visited PoI was
   222→   222→classified as each participant’s home location (32). Percentage of time at home was calculated as
   223→   223→the proportion of timestamped GPS records assigned to the home PoI on a given day.
   224→   224→Foreground time. Passive smartphone logs recorded which applications were active in the
   225→   225→foreground and the duration of each foreground app-use episode. Foreground time was
   226→   226→calculated as the cumulative duration of foreground app activity for each participant-day.
   227→   227→Steps. Smartphone step logs were used to calculate the total number of steps recorded for
   228→   228→each participant-day.
   229→   229→Calls. Phone call logs were used to calculate the total number of incoming and outgoing
   230→   230→calls for each participant-day.
   231→   231→Computational modeling
   232→   232→Adaptive Design Optimization (ADO)
   233→   233→We estimated computational parameters for both the delay discounting and choice under
   234→   234→risk and ambiguity tasks using ADO. ADO is a machine learning–based active-learning method
   235→   235→that selects the most informative stimulus on each trial based on the participant’s previous
   236→   236→responses. After each choice, the posterior distribution over model parameters was updated using
   237→   237→grid-based Bayesian inference with uniform priors, and the next trial’s design variables (e.g.,
   238→   238→receipt delay, reward amount, winning probability, reward amounts, ambiguity level) were
   239→   239→chosen to maximize expected information gain. All procedures were implemented using the
   240→   240→Python package ADOpy (33).

   283→   283→Analytical approach
   284→   284→Modeling of same-day nighttime drinking
   285→   285→To examine associations between day-level factors and same-day nighttime drinking, we
   286→   286→used a generalized linear mixed-effects model. Nighttime drinking was defined as a binary
   287→   287→outcome (drinking vs. no drinking) based on participants’ evening self-report of whether they
   288→   288→had consumed alcohol that day. Because daily observations were nested within participants
   289→   289→8
   290→   290→
   291→   291→across the 28-day period, we included subject-level random intercepts. A logit link was used
   292→   292→because the outcome was binary.
   293→   293→To distinguish within-person fluctuations from stable between-person differences, each
   294→   294→daily predictor was decomposed into within-person and between-person components (35,36).
   295→   295→The between-person component was defined as each participant’s mean across all available study
   296→   296→days and was grand-mean centered before standardization. The within-person component was
   297→   297→defined as the deviation of each daily value from that participant’s mean. To facilitate
   298→   298→comparison across predictors, both components were standardized by dividing them by the total
   299→   299→standard deviation of the corresponding raw variable before being entered into the model.
   300→   300→To examine the temporal specificity of these associations, we additionally fit a lagged
   301→   301→GLMM predicting next-day nighttime drinking from today's within-person predictor values, with
   302→   302→same-day nighttime drinking included as a covariate. Only consecutive day-pairs were included
   303→   303→in this analysis.

   349→   349→RESULTS
   350→   350→Participant characteristics
   351→   351→A total of 143 participants were included in the final analytic sample (43.4% male; age:
   352→   352→M = 33.35, SD = 8.44; Table 1). In cohort comparisons, the comprehensive assessment group
   353→   353→was older and had more years of education than the app-only group, whereas there was no clear
   354→   354→evidence of cohort differences in gender, marital status, AUDIT-K, BIS-11-R, BAI, or BDI
   355→   355→scores (Table S2). Based on DSM-5 criterion counts, 60 (42.0%) met criteria for mild, 53
   356→   356→(37.1%) for moderate, and 30 (21.0%) for severe AUD (Figure S1B). Across the 28-day study
   357→   357→period, participants completed a median of 18 assessment days (interquartile range: 11.5–23),
   358→   358→yielding 2,441 participant-days included in the final multimodal model. Nighttime drinking was
   359→   359→reported on 436 of 2,441 participant-days (17.9%).
   360→   360→Daily-level associations with nighttime drinking
   361→   361→Among the candidate models, the model integrating daily app-based self-reports,
   362→   362→computational parameters, and passive smartphone-derived measures showed the highest
   363→   363→marginal R² of 0.22 (Table S3). In this final integrated model, at the within-person level, higher
   364→   364→delay discounting (log(k)), higher alcohol craving, more positive mood, lower meal intake, and
   365→   365→lower percent time at home were associated with a higher likelihood of nighttime drinking. At
   366→   366→the between-person level, only lower average meal intake was associated with a higher
   367→   367→likelihood of nighttime drinking. Baseline AUDIT-K score, included as a covariate, was also
   368→   368→positively associated with nighttime drinking (Figure 3; Table S4). The same pattern of
   369→   369→significant predictors and effect directions was reproduced across the broader-sample analysis
   370→   370→10
   371→   371→
   372→   372→before DSM-based exclusion, the ≥70% adherence analysis, and the timestamp-based analysis
   373→   373→excluding delayed self-report records (Figures S2–S4).
   374→   374→To further illustrate this within-person association, we fitted a separate single-predictor
   375→   375→generalized linear mixed-effects model including only within-person deviations in log(k) and a
   376→   376→subject-level random intercept. Higher within-person log(k) was associated with a higher
   377→   377→likelihood of same-day nighttime drinking (β = 0.34, 95% CI [0.10, 0.58], p = .006; Figure 4A).
   378→   378→Notably, within-person fluctuations in log(k) showed minimal correlation with other within-
   379→   379→person predictors, including alcohol craving, mood, and percent time at home, with correlations
   380→   380→close to zero (Figure S5). This suggests that daily variation in log(k) captured information
   381→   381→largely distinct from these other day-level predictors.
   382→   382→To examine the temporal specificity of these associations, we fit lagged generalized
   383→   383→linear mixed-effects models predicting next-day drinking from today’s predictor values while
   384→   384→including same-day nighttime drinking as a covariate. There was no clear evidence that today’s
   385→   385→within-person predictors were associated with next-day nighttime drinking (Figure S6).
   386→   386→DISCUSSION
   387→   387→The present study examined whether daily computational parameters, daily app-based
   388→   388→self-reports, and passive smartphone-derived measures were jointly associated with same-day
   389→   389→nighttime drinking in adults meeting DSM-5 criteria for AUD. Among the candidate models, the
   390→   390→multimodal model integrating all three data modalities showed the highest explanatory power
   391→   391→(marginal R² = 0.22). Associations with same-day nighttime drinking were observed primarily at
   392→   392→the within-person level, indicating that day-level deviations from an individual’s own average
   393→   393→were more consistently linked to drinking than stable between-person differences. Within-person
   394→   394→associations were observed across multiple domains: higher delay discounting [log(k)], higher
   395→   395→alcohol craving, more positive mood, lower meal intake, and lower percent time at home were
   396→   396→each associated with a higher likelihood of nighttime drinking. Among these predictors, within-
   397→   397→person log(k) was associated with same-day drinking even after accounting for concurrent self-
   398→   398→reported and contextual measures in the full model, suggesting that day-level variation in delay
   399→   399→discounting may capture information about drinking risk that is not already available from other
   400→   400→modalities.
   401→   401→Prior research has consistently shown that individuals with AUD exhibit steeper delay
   402→   402→discounting compared with controls (12,15), and that the degree of discounting is associated with
   403→   403→treatment outcomes (13). This literature has predominantly focused on who discounts more
   404→   404→steeply. The present findings suggest that delay discounting also varies meaningfully within
   405→   405→individuals from day to day and that these within-person fluctuations are informative about
   406→   406→same-day drinking behavior. On days when individuals discounted delayed rewards more steeply
   407→   407→than usual, they were more likely to drink that night. This association is notable in two respects.
   408→   408→Within-person fluctuations in log(k) showed minimal correlation with other within-person
   409→   409→predictors, indicating that log(k) captures a dimension of day-level variation that is largely
   410→   410→independent of self-reported psychological states and contextual indicators. Furthermore, when
   411→   411→the same model was tested with one-day-lagged predictors, there was no clear evidence of next-
   412→   412→day associations for log(k) or any other within-person variable, suggesting that the association
   413→   413→may reflect a same-day state rather than a carryover process.
   414→   414→11
   415→   415→
   416→   416→These findings indicate that the window of elevated risk may be narrow and that repeated
   417→   417→assessment of delay discounting may offer information about day-level vulnerability that is not
   418→   418→available from daily app-based self-reports or passive sensing alone.
   419→   419→The absence of a significant between-person effect of log(k) may seem unexpected given
   420→   420→the well-established cross-sectional association between delay discounting and addiction
   421→   421→severity. One possible factor is that the analytic sample was restricted to individuals meeting
   422→   422→DSM-5 criteria for AUD and did not include a non-AUD comparison group. As a result,
   423→   423→between-person differences in average discounting may have been less pronounced than in
   424→   424→studies contrasting clinical and healthy control groups (39). In samples with restricted between-
   425→   425→person variance, within-person fluctuations may become the primary source of meaningful
   426→   426→variation.
   427→   427→Neither risk preference (α) nor ambiguity tolerance (1−β) showed clear evidence of
   428→   428→association with same-day nighttime drinking. Prior work has linked greater ambiguity tolerance
   429→   429→to relapse vulnerability in opioid and nicotine use disorders, particularly among patients who
   430→   430→continue substance use despite uncertainty about negative consequences (16,17). However, those
   431→   431→studies examined relapse, defined as a categorical transition from abstinence to use, among
   432→   432→patients who had initiated outpatient treatment, whereas the present study modeled daily drinking
   433→   433→episodes in community-recruited adults who met DSM-5 criteria for AUD and were not
   434→   434→necessarily seeking abstinence. It is also possible that delay discounting is more proximally
   435→   435→relevant to daily drinking decisions, which involve a concrete tradeoff between immediate
   436→   436→reward and delayed consequences, than are risk or ambiguity preferences, which may be more
   437→   437→relevant under conditions of explicit uncertainty about outcomes.
   438→   438→Daily self-reported psychological and behavioral states were also associated with same-
   439→   439→day nighttime drinking. Higher craving, more positive mood, and lower meal intake showed
   440→   440→significant within-person associations. These findings are consistent with prior EMA work
   441→   441→linking day-level fluctuations in craving and affective states to alcohol consumption in
   442→   442→naturalistic settings (3,6). The within-person association between more positive mood and
   443→   443→nighttime drinking is consistent with prior EMA and daily-diary evidence linking daily positive
   444→   444→affect to alcohol use (40). Because mood and drinking were assessed in the same nightly report,
   445→   445→however, the current design cannot determine whether positive mood preceded drinking or
   446→   446→reflected the affective context of drinking days, such as socially rewarding settings in which
   447→   447→positive mood and alcohol use co-occurred. Meal intake was the only predictor that also showed
   448→   448→a significant between-person effect, suggesting that lower average food consumption may reflect
   449→   449→a more stable behavioral pattern associated with elevated drinking risk.
   450→   450→Among the passive smartphone-derived measures, spending less time at home was the
   451→   451→only variable significantly associated with same-day nighttime drinking in the full model. This
   452→   452→pattern may reflect greater exposure to contexts in which drinking opportunities are available,
   453→   453→consistent with prior work integrating geospatial data with EMA and smartphone sensing
   454→   454→approaches (41). Foreground time, steps, and calls were not significantly associated with
   455→   455→nighttime drinking after accounting for the other modalities. One possibility is that these passive
   456→   456→measures capture behavioral patterns that are only indirectly related to drinking risk, such as
   457→   457→contextual factors associated with social activity or location. At the day level, these signals may
   458→   458→12
   459→   459→
   460→   460→have provided limited incremental information once more proximal self-report and
   461→   461→computational measures were included in the model.
   462→   462→Several limitations should be considered. First, because participants were community-
   463→   463→recruited adults meeting DSM-5 criteria for AUD and were not necessarily seeking treatment, the
   464→   464→findings may not generalize to treatment-seeking or more clinically severe AUD populations.
   465→   465→Second, although daily assessments captured short-term variability, the observational design
   466→   466→precludes causal inference about the temporal ordering of discounting and drinking.
   467→   467→Furthermore, because the analysis plan was not pre-registered, the findings should be interpreted
   468→   468→as exploratory and hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory.
   469→   469→Despite these limitations, the present findings have implications for understanding
   470→   470→alcohol use in daily life. The observation that associations with nighttime drinking were
   471→   471→concentrated at the within-person level, with no clear evidence of lagged associations, suggests
   472→   472→that the relevant risk window may be narrow and that monitoring approaches may benefit from
   473→   473→detecting day-level changes rather than assessing stable traits. That within-person log(k) was
   474→   474→associated with same-day drinking independently of self-reported states and contextual measures
   475→   475→suggests that brief, repeated computational assessments may add information to multimodal
   476→   476→monitoring systems. More broadly, integrating computational markers with daily app-based self-
   477→   477→reports and passive sensing may help identify within-person states of elevated drinking risk and
   478→   478→inform future efforts to design timely, individualized interventions in real-world settings (42).
