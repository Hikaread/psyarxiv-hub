    35вҶ’Abstract
    36вҶ’The Russo-Ukrainian War (RUW) led to encompassing and detrimental
    37вҶ’consequences for millions of Ukrainian civilians abroad and in their war-torn homeland.
    38вҶ’Recent representative studies found considerable psychological consequences, suggesting
    39вҶ’high prevalences of mental disorders, and war-related distress alike. Continuous traumatic
    40вҶ’stress (CTS) may constitute a valid construct describing psychopathological symptoms such
    41вҶ’as depression, anxiety, PTSD, and DSO in war-affected Ukrainian civilians. This study
    42вҶ’provides modest empirical evidence to the construct of CTS and the psychological distress
    43вҶ’Ukrainian civilians face following the RUW. In the cross-sectional online study N = 113
    44вҶ’datasets of Ukrainian civilians (M = 40 years; 87.6% female) in Austria (n = 67) and Ukraine
    45вҶ’(n = 46) were collected in 2025. Associations of CTS with psychopathological symptoms, the
    46вҶ’influence of emotion dysregulation (ED) on these associations, and differences in symptom
    47вҶ’levels between two subsamples were examined. CTS predicted between 28% and 47% of
    48вҶ’the variance in psychopathological symptoms. Preliminary findings showed small moderation
    49вҶ’effects of ED on the association between CTS and psychopathological symptoms. Contrary
    50вҶ’to hypotheses, participants in Austria reported higher symptom levels than participants in
    51вҶ’Ukraine. Exploratory analyses revealed high prevalences of questionnaire-derived diagnoses
    52вҶ’throughout the sample. Findings support the construct of CTS and its clinical value. Empirical
    53вҶ’evidence indicates higher psychological distress in internationally displaced Ukrainian
    54вҶ’civilians. High associations between CTS, ED, and psychopathological symptoms highlight
    55вҶ’the need for qualitative and longitudinal data. Implications for trauma-focused research are
    56вҶ’discussed.
    57вҶ’Keywords: continuous traumatic stress, psychopathology, Ukrainian civilians, RussoUkrainian War, cross-sectional study

    62вҶ’Psychological Consequences of the Russo-Ukrainian War
    63вҶ’The full-scale Russian invasion of Ukraine on 24th of February 2022 marked an
    64вҶ’unprecedented event in recent European history and constitutes the first military attack on a
    65вҶ’European nation since the Second World War. The subsequent Russo-Ukrainian War (RUW)
    66вҶ’marks a turning point in global history and enfolded a vast array of political, economic,
    67вҶ’environmental, and humanitarian consequences (Hryhorczuk et al., 2024; Martsenkovskyi et
    68вҶ’al., 2024; Poberezhets, 2022). In 2024, the United Nations (UN) reported 6,813,900
    69вҶ’Ukrainian refugees globally (United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees [UNHCR],
    70вҶ’2024). As of January 2026, the UN verified a total of 55,600 civilian casualties (i.e., being
    71вҶ’injured or killed) since the full-scale Russian invasion in 2022 (Office of the High
    72вҶ’Commissioner for Human Rights [OHCHR], 2026). The data conveys the bleak reality of war
    73вҶ’and demonstrates civilian life as profoundly affected by warвҖҷs severe disruptions to society.
    74вҶ’News agencies frequently reported targeted destruction of civilian infrastructure and largescale drone attacks during the RUW (Malenko, 2024; Reuters, 2024). Therefore, civilians in
    75вҶ’war-affected regions potentially experience war-related stressors such as hearing air raid
    76вҶ’sirens, witnessing artillery fire, destruction of own property or local infrastructure, invading
    77вҶ’military forces, relatives falling victim to war, financial hardship, or being forced to
    78вҶ’displacement (Karatzias et al., 2023). In consequence, war-affected Ukrainian civilians may
    79вҶ’continuously face traumatizing real-world threats to the own physical and psychological
    80вҶ’integrity. However, while information on the RUW may be widely accessible through news
    81вҶ’and media agencies, the psychological consequences of war, flight and displacement are
    82вҶ’potentially less overt.
    83вҶ’Recent research showed strong interest in investigating the psychological
    84вҶ’consequences in war-affected Ukrainians, resulting in a considerable amount of empirical
    85вҶ’data (Fornaro et al., 2025; Hyland et al., 2023; Karatzias et al., 2023; Levin et al., 2023;
    86вҶ’Martsenkovskyi et al., 2024). In a sample of N = 2,050 Ukrainian adults assessed in
    87вҶ’September 2023, 36.3% screened positive for any of seven psychological disorders, of which
    88вҶ’generalized anxiety disorder, prolonged grief disorder and post-traumatic stress disorder
    89вҶ’were the most prevalent (Martsenkovskyi et al., 2024). Furthermore, comorbidity of
    90вҶ’psychological disorders in the sample was high, and financial worries were reported to be a
    91вҶ’risk factor. Lastly, higher prevalences of psychological disorders were reported for people in
    92вҶ’Eastern parts of Ukraine, whereas rates were lower in Western Ukraine. Participants
    93вҶ’reported high prevalences of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and complex posttraumatic stress disorder (CPTSD).
    94вҶ’
    95вҶ’4
    96вҶ’Furthermore, in a nationwide sample of N = 2,004 Ukrainian parents evidence of a
    97вҶ’dose-response relationship between war-related stressors (e.g., hearing air raid sirens,
    98вҶ’witnessing destruction of infrastructure, experiencing financial hardship, being forced to
    99вҶ’displace) and meeting criteria for PTSD and CPTSD was reported (Karatzias et al., 2023). In
   100вҶ’this sample, Hyland et al. (2023) found a significant proportion of participants reporting
   101вҶ’worsened symptoms of anxiety (71.9%), depression (62.1%), and loneliness (46.8%)
   102вҶ’following the Russian invasion of Ukraine, with a dose-response effect correlating with
   103вҶ’increased mental health challenges. These findings highlight the considerable impact and
   104вҶ’psychological toll the RUW has had on Ukrainian civilians.
   105вҶ’Continuous Traumatic Stress
   106вҶ’These findings suggest that Ukrainian civilians experience ongoing traumatic stress,
   107вҶ’linked to real-world threats due to the RUW (Fornaro et al., 2025; Hyland et al., 2023;
   108вҶ’Karatzias et al., 2023; Levin et al., 2023; Martsenkovskyi et al., 2024). Continuous traumatic
   109вҶ’stress (CTS) is defined as the ongoing experience of extreme stressors with potentially
   110вҶ’traumatic value, due to real-world threats that shape the context of an individual in the
   111вҶ’present (Bachem & Maercker, 2024; Goral et al., 2021; Stevens et al., 2013; Straker, 2013).
   112вҶ’In PTSD and CPTSD exposure to traumatic stressors lies distinctly in the past, with
   113вҶ’psychological symptoms, e.g., re-experiencing traumatic events and avoidance behaviors,
   114вҶ’forming the current experience of individuals (World Health Organization [WHO], 2019). In
   115вҶ’contrast, CTS describes the psychological consequences of traumatic stressors, which are
   116вҶ’ongoing and currently shape the environment of affected individuals (Bachem & Maercker,
   117вҶ’2024; Goral et al., 2021; Stevens et al., 2013; Straker, 2013).
   118вҶ’The construct was first described in literature by Straker and the Sanctuaries
   119вҶ’Counselling Team (1987) during the Apartheid in South Africa. Originally rooted in civil
   120вҶ’conflict, it stemmed from observations of the psychological consequences civilians
   121вҶ’experienced in facing ongoing traumatic threats and violence in their daily lives. CTS had a
   122вҶ’political and societal dimension, describing contexts in which the state law was part of the
   123вҶ’problem and oppressive systems act on discriminated and disenfranchised individuals. This
   124вҶ’was later revised by Straker (2013) in an ambition to render the construct more valid in
   125вҶ’describing psychological consequences of ongoing traumatic stressors in further contexts.
   126вҶ’Straker (2013) proposed the context as defining feature of CTS and modified its definition to
   127вҶ’simply involving multiple extreme stressors that are current and encompassing. Since then,
   128вҶ’the construct of CTS was transferred to contexts of war-related stressors, as evident in
   129вҶ’military conflicts (Greene et al., 2018; Itzhaky et al., 2017; Levin et al., 2024; Zasiekina et al.,
   130вҶ’2024). Notably, CTS has shown meaningful associations with the core symptoms of PTSD
   131вҶ’and CPTSD (Goral et al., 2021; Greene et al., 2018; Lahav, 2020; Zasiekina et al., 2024).
   132вҶ’
   133вҶ’5
   134вҶ’PTSD is defined as a mental disorder resulting from exposure to an extremely
   135вҶ’threatening or horrific event or series of events. It is characterized by three core symptoms;
   136вҶ’vivid re-experiencing of traumatic events (i.e., intrusive memories, flashbacks, nightmares),
   137вҶ’avoidance of thoughts and memories related to traumatic events, and a persistent perception
   138вҶ’of heightened current threat (e.g., hypervigilance or enhanced startle reaction). The
   139вҶ’symptoms persist for at least several weeks and cause significant impairment in individual
   140вҶ’functioning (WHO, 2019).
   141вҶ’CPTSD is a disorder following exposure to an extremely threatening or horrific event
   142вҶ’or series of events, which most often represent prolonged or repetitive events from which
   143вҶ’escape is difficult or impossible (e.g., torture, repeated childhood abuse). It encompasses all
   144вҶ’diagnostic criteria of PTSD, with additional criteria of disturbances in self-organization (DSO).
   145вҶ’These symptoms are defined by three dimensions: problems in affect regulation, beliefs of
   146вҶ’the self as diminished (e.g., defeated, worthless, accompanied by feelings of shame, guilt or
   147вҶ’failure) related to the traumatic event, and difficulties in maintaining relationships and
   148вҶ’closeness to others. The symptoms must cause significant impairment in individual
   149вҶ’functioning (WHO, 2019).
   150вҶ’Studies also suggest that the symptomatology of CTS is more complex and
   151вҶ’comprises an array of additional symptoms (Goral et al., 2021; Greene et al., 2018; Lahav,
   152вҶ’2020; Zasiekina et al., 2024). Previous studies reported high associations of r = .67-.80
   153вҶ’between CTS and psychopathological symptoms, such as anxiety, depression, PTSD and
   154вҶ’DSO (Greene et al., 2018; Zasiekina et al., 2024). Mental health practitioners and clinicians
   155вҶ’have recognized the importance of empirical evidence on the construct of CTS, and it was
   156вҶ’discussed at the International Classification of Diseases conference of the WHO in 2015
   157вҶ’(Bachem & Maercker, 2024). However, empirical evidence on CTS and its associations with
   158вҶ’other mental health outcomes is still scarce and further research is needed to fill this
   159вҶ’research gap (Maercker & Brewin, 2024; Pat-Horenczyk & Schiff, 2019).
   160вҶ’Emotion Dysregulation and Psychopathology
   161вҶ’Furthermore, empirical evidence suggests that CTS is associated with emotion
   162вҶ’dysregulation (ED), such as emotional numbing or difficulties with impulse control (Goral et
   163вҶ’al., 2017; Hallion et al., 2018; Levin et al., 2023). Whereas emotion regulation can be
   164вҶ’described as the ability to modulate an emotion or a set of emotions and to employ behavior
   165вҶ’to act on a priori goals, ED describes maladaptive forms of modulating emotional responses
   166вҶ’(Gratz & Roemer, 2004; Gross, 2015). ED is a multifaceted construct encompassing a variety
   167вҶ’of different emotion regulation strategies, such as lack of awareness, understanding, or
   168вҶ’acceptance of emotional responses, lack of access to adaptive strategies for modulating
   169вҶ’emotions, impulse control difficulties, and inability to engage in goal-directed behaviors when
   170вҶ’experiencing emotional distress. Practical examples of these maladaptive emotion regulation
   171вҶ’
   172вҶ’6
   173вҶ’strategies may include emotional avoidance or suppression, rumination, denial, aggression,
   174вҶ’and venting (DвҖҷAgostino et al., 2017). ED was first described in borderline personality
   175вҶ’disorder and later established as a transdiagnostic and dimensional construct implicated in
   176вҶ’many different psychopathologies, including internalizing disorders, externalizing disorders,
   177вҶ’and psychotic disorders (Beauchaine & Cicchetti, 2019; Gratz & Roemer, 2004).
   178вҶ’Concordantly, studies in adult populations indicate that ED is instrumental to both
   179вҶ’development and maintenance of psychopathological symptoms, such as PTSD, bipolar
   180вҶ’disorder, and schizophrenia spectrum disorder (Carmassi et al., 2022). Further, ED
   181вҶ’potentially mediates associations of cumulative lifetime adversity on depressive symptoms in
   182вҶ’adulthood, with data suggesting that both ED and cumulative lifetime adversity may explain
   183вҶ’50% of variance in symptomatology (Abravanel & Sinha, 2015). These findings indicate ED
   184вҶ’to be implicated in Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) and Generalized Anxiety Disorder
   185вҶ’(GAD), which in turn are associated with CTS (Gratz & Roemer, 2004; Hallion et al., 2018).
   186вҶ’MDD is defined as depressive mood (e.g., irritable, empty) or loss of pleasure which
   187вҶ’are accompanied by cognitive (e.g., difficulty concentrating), behavioral (e.g., diminished
   188вҶ’interest in activities), or neurovegetative symptoms (e.g., changes in appetite or sleep) that
   189вҶ’significantly affect the individualвҖҷs ability to function (WHO, 2019). Single Episode Depressive
   190вҶ’Disorder is characterized by presentation of these symptoms most of the day, almost every
   191вҶ’day, for at least two weeks with no prior history of depressive episodes.
   192вҶ’GAD is defined by distinct symptoms of anxiety that persist for at least several
   193вҶ’months for more days than not, which are characterized by either general apprehension (i.e.
   194вҶ’вҖҳfree-floating anxietyвҖҷ) or excessive worry concerning multiple everyday events. Additional
   195вҶ’symptoms include muscular tension, sympathetic autonomic over-activity, or subjective
   196вҶ’experience of nervousness. Additionally, these symptoms result in significant distress or
   197вҶ’impairment of the individualвҖҷs ability to function (WHO, 2019).
   198вҶ’Moreover, a systematic review of 34 studies found ED to potentially mediate
   199вҶ’vulnerability to PTSD, but also to predict the severity and maintenance of PTSD symptoms
   200вҶ’(Conti et al., 2023). In a prospective study, Pencea et al. (2020) showed ED to predict
   201вҶ’chronic PTSD development in a trauma-exposed adult sample, and suggested interventions
   202вҶ’to improve emotion regulation capabilities. Further, a clinical trial found that ED moderated
   203вҶ’treatment response of cognitive behavioral therapy for PTSD with co-occurring substance
   204вҶ’use disorder (Hien et al., 2017). In trauma-focused research, it was previously suggested
   205вҶ’that ED is also implicated in CTS reactions and trauma-related mental disorders (Cloitre et
   206вҶ’al., 2018; Dieujuste et al., 2025; Goral et al., 2017; Levin et al., 2023). Furthermore, existing
   207вҶ’literature demonstrate considerable associations of ED with psychopathology in
   208вҶ’heterogenous trauma-exposed adult populations (Blay et al., 2025; Carmassi et al., 2022;
   209вҶ’Seligowski et al., 2015). Therefore, findings suggest more severe psychopathological
   210вҶ’
   211вҶ’7
   212вҶ’symptomatology for higher levels of ED. While ED represents a growing field of research,
   213вҶ’further empirical evidence is needed to validate and specify effects of general ED in context
   214вҶ’of civilian trauma reactions and trauma-related mental disorders.
   215вҶ’Geographical Distance to the Conflict Zone and Mental Health
   216вҶ’The theoretical framework of CTS postulates that environments with ongoing
   217вҶ’traumatic stressors lead to CTS (Straker, 2013). Researchers also emphasize the
   218вҶ’importance of contexts and external safety for people currently affected by CTS (Fornaro et
   219вҶ’al., 2025; Itzhaky et al., 2017; Kaminer et al., 2016; Straker, 2013). Notably, under contexts
   220вҶ’of CTS, core symptoms of PTSD and CPTSD, e.g., hypervigilance and perceptions of
   221вҶ’heightened threat, may constitute a functional reaction to current threatful environments
   222вҶ’(Itzhaky et al., 2017). For instance, civilians in war-affected regions may only have seconds
   223вҶ’to seek shelter in response to emerging threats, such as missile strikes (Itzhaky et al., 2017).
   224вҶ’Notably, Kalaitzaki et al. (2024) found differences in mental health outcomes in
   225вҶ’relation to the geographical proximity to the RUW. Kalaitzaki et al. (2024) investigated mental
   226вҶ’health outcomes across 11 different countries including Ukraine, bordering countries (e.g.,
   227вҶ’Poland and Romania) and more distal ones (e.g., Italy, Peru). Importantly, Ukrainians
   228вҶ’reported the worst mental health indices, i.e., the highest levels of perceived stress, anxiety,
   229вҶ’depression, PTSD and CPTSD, and traumatic exposure (largest effect size; О·2 = .336), while
   230вҶ’simultaneously reporting lowest resilience, compared to non-Ukrainians. People from
   231вҶ’countries near Ukraine were more frequently informed about the RUW, reported greater
   232вҶ’stress about the likelihood of war, and worse mental health outcomes due to war news and
   233вҶ’economic crisis, compared to those located further away from Ukraine (Kalaitzaki et al.,
   234вҶ’2024). These findings indicate that traumatic stress and its detrimental impacts on mental
   235вҶ’health due to the RUW may be mitigated by the distance to the conflict zone.
   236вҶ’However, this assumption remains controversial since there is also evidence that
   237вҶ’nondisplaced Ukrainian civilians report less psychological symptoms, i.e., anxiety, stress and
   238вҶ’depression, than displaced Ukrainian civilians due to the RUW (Fornaro et al., 2025). It is
   239вҶ’hypothesized that displaced people experienced threats to personal safety, financial security,
   240вҶ’damage to their environment, and uncertainties related to the war to a greater extent than
   241вҶ’those not willing or forced to resettle, which may encompass people whose safety was not
   242вҶ’that critically endangered (Fornaro et al., 2025).

   258вҶ’Methods
   259вҶ’Sampling Procedures
   260вҶ’This non-experimental cross-sectional study was conducted using an online
   261вҶ’questionnaire. Two independent ad-hoc samples of adult civilians from Ukraine were tested
   262вҶ’at a single time point from 7th of May until 30th of July 2025. Participants had to be civilians of
   263вҶ’Ukraine, at least 18 years of age, without history of mental disorders, and residing in Ukraine
   264вҶ’or in Austria, to be eligible for participation. Individuals without Ukrainian nationality, under
   265вҶ’the age of 18, former or current military personnel, and individuals with current diagnoses of
   266вҶ’mental disorders (e.g., PTSD, anxiety disorders) or prior psychiatric hospitalization, were
   267вҶ’excluded. If any exclusion criteria were met, participation was terminated and the participant
   268вҶ’was informed accordingly. No financial incentives were offered to the participants.
   269вҶ’Two independent ad-hoc samples of Ukrainian civilians were recruited. In total, 11
   270вҶ’organizations distributed the studyвҖҷs flyer to eligible participants in Austria and Ukraine. In
   271вҶ’Austria, participant recruitment was conducted in collaboration with psychological counseling
   272вҶ’services for displaced Ukrainian civilians, and associations of emigrated Ukrainian civilians.
   273вҶ’Public counseling services such as Caritas, Diakonie, and HEMAYAT distributed the study in
   274вҶ’Vienna. Further, asylkoordination Г¶sterreich distributed the study within a nationwide Austrian
   275вҶ’network of Ukrainian civilians who engaged in psychological counseling. In Ukraine,
   276вҶ’participants were recruited in collaboration with Ukrainian researchers from the Ukrainian
   277вҶ’Science Diaspora and the Ukrainian Psychotrauma Center (Research Unit at Lesya Ukrainka
   278вҶ’Volyn National University), disseminating the study within their respective networks. The
   279вҶ’study employed opportunistic sampling methods due to restricted resources, as controlled
   280вҶ’sampling methods were not feasible. In Ukraine, participants were recruited from six different
   281вҶ’geographical regions: Eastern Ukraine, Western Ukraine, Kyiv, Northern Ukraine, Central
   282вҶ’Ukraine, and Southern Ukraine.
   283вҶ’Measurement Instruments
   284вҶ’
   285вҶ’9
   286вҶ’All measurement instruments rely on self-reports and were provided in two
   287вҶ’languages, English and Ukrainian. The instruments were openly available and sourced from
   288вҶ’the International Trauma Consortium (ITC) (The International Trauma Consortium, 2024).
   289вҶ’Continuous Traumatic Stress Response Scale
   290вҶ’The original English-language Continuous Traumatic Stress Response (CTSR) scale
   291вҶ’developed by Goral et al. (2021) used a sample of 313 adults in Southern Israel exposed to
   292вҶ’ongoing security threats between late 2016 and early 2017. The measure with 11 items
   293вҶ’showed a distinct three-factor structure of Exhaustion and Detachment, Rage and Betrayal,
   294вҶ’as well as Fear and Helplessness. Internal consistency was reported to be рқӣј = .90 (total
   295вҶ’scale), рқӣј = .86 (Exhaustion and Detachment), рқӣј = .82 (Rage and Betrayal), and рқӣј = .74 (Fear
   296вҶ’and Helplessness). The CTSR scale by Zasiekina et al. (2024) is a Ukrainian-language
   297вҶ’cross-cultural adaptation and psychometric validation in a war-affected Ukrainian sample.
   298вҶ’Notably, the Ukrainian adaptation of the CTSR scale, using a sample of 619 participants who
   299вҶ’applied for psychosocial support in Ukraine in early 2023, showed a two-factor structure of
   300вҶ’Exhaustion and Rage, and Fear and Betrayal. The authors reported high internal consistency
   301вҶ’of рқӣј = .86 for the total CTSR scale. Further, findings indicate satisfactory test-retest reliability,
   302вҶ’with no significant differences within participantsвҖҷ scores after a one-month period, and
   303вҶ’concurrent construct validity as the scale was associated with trauma-related mental health
   304вҶ’symptoms, i.e., PTSD, depression, anxiety, and moral injury (Zasiekina et al., 2024).
   305вҶ’International Trauma Questionnaire
   306вҶ’The International Trauma Questionnaire (ITQ) from Cloitre et al. (2018) is an 18-item
   307вҶ’measurement instrument developed to assess PTSD and CPTSD according to ICD-11
   308вҶ’(WHO, 2019). The internal consistency was reported as satisfactory for all subscales of
   309вҶ’PTSD and DSO (рқӣј вүҘ .79). Ho et al. (2023) confirmed the factor structure and internal
   310вҶ’consistency (рқӣјвҖҷs = .73вҖ“.88) for all subscales of the ITQ in a sample of 2,004 adults from the
   311вҶ’general population of Ukraine six months after the full-scale Russian invasion in 2022.
   312вҶ’Additionally, a diagnostic algorithm in accordance with ICD-11 diagnostic criteria was
   313вҶ’implemented (WHO, 2019). Symptom ratings were determined clinically significant for
   314вҶ’responses of 3 (moderately), or higher (Cloitre et al. (2018). Diagnostic criteria for PTSD
   315вҶ’were met if at least one item from each of the three PTSD-cluster (i.e., re-experiencing in the
   316вҶ’here and now, avoidance, sense of threat) exceeded clinical cut-offs, and functional
   317вҶ’impairment was reported in at least one domain (e.g., relationships, work, any other
   318вҶ’important part of life). Furthermore, the diagnostic criteria for CPTSD were met if all
   319вҶ’diagnostic criteria for PTSD were fulfilled, and additionally participantsвҖҷ responses exceeded
   320вҶ’the clinical cut-off for at least one item in each of the three DSO clusters (i.e., affective
   321вҶ’dysregulation, negative self-concept, disturbances in relationships). Importantly, in
   322вҶ’accordance with ICD-11 diagnostic criteria, administration of diagnoses for either PTSD or
   323вҶ’
   324вҶ’10
   325вҶ’CPTSD are valid, but not both (WHO, 2019). Thus, if participants fulfilled diagnostic criteria of
   326вҶ’both PTSD and CPTSD, diagnosis of CPTSD was administered but not PTSD.
   327вҶ’Trauma Exposure Life Events Scale
   328вҶ’The Trauma Exposure Life Events Scale (LEC-5) by Weathers et al. (2013) is a 17item measurement instrument designed to screen for lifetime exposure to potentially
   329вҶ’traumatic events. The LEC-5 assesses exposure to 16 events known to potentially result in
   330вҶ’traumatic stress or PTSD and includes one additional item assessing any other potentially
   331вҶ’stressful event experienced (Weathers et al., 2013). Participants could report multiple
   332вҶ’exposures to each event. If at least one scale point from 1 (Happened to me) to 4 (Part of my
   333вҶ’job) was reported for an event, the item was scored as 1, indicating potential traumatic
   334вҶ’exposure to this event. If responses of 5 (Not sure) or 6 (DoesnвҖҷt apply) were given, the item
   335вҶ’was scored as 0, indicating absence of traumatic exposure to this event. The scoring
   336вҶ’algorithm is in line with the DSM-5 criterion A for PTSD and CPTSD (Weathers et al., 2013).
   337вҶ’This computational algorithm was utilized in previous studies and results in sum scores from
   338вҶ’0 to 17 (Cloitre et al. (2018). Culturally adapted versions of the LEC-5 scale reported
   339вҶ’satisfactory estimates of рқӣј = .67 in a sample of Korean psychiatric patients (Bae et al., 2008),
   340вҶ’рқӣј = .70 in a sample of Pakistani school students (Tariq & Wong, 2024), and рқӣј = .87 in a
   341вҶ’sample of Malaysian undergraduates (Tan et al., 2023).
   342вҶ’International Depression Questionnaire and International Anxiety Questionnaire
   343вҶ’The International Depression Questionnaire (IDQ) and the International Anxiety
   344вҶ’Questionnaire (IAQ) by Shevlin et al. (2023) are self-report measures that align with the
   345вҶ’diagnostic rules of the ICD-11 (WHO, 2019). The IDQ is a 10-item measure that assesses
   346вҶ’diagnostic criteria of ICD-11 Single Episode Depressive Disorder. The IAQ measures ICD-11
   347вҶ’Generalized Anxiety Disorder on a scale with nine items. Cut-offs for clinically significant
   348вҶ’values were determined to be 4 (Most days), or higher (Shevlin et al., 2023). Diagnostic
   349вҶ’criteria for Single Episode Depressive Disorder (IDQ) were met if at least one core symptom
   350вҶ’(i.e., item 1, 2) exceeded clinical cut-offs, at least five items in total have been responded
   351вҶ’with clinically significant values, and functional impairment was reported. Diagnostic criteria
   352вҶ’for Generalized Anxiety Disorder were fulfilled if at least one core symptom (i.e., item 1, 2)
   353вҶ’was reported as clinically significant, at least four items in total exceeded clinical cut-offs, and
   354вҶ’functional impairment was reported. Validation was done using a community sample (N =
   355вҶ’2058) representative of the United Kingdom adult population (Shevlin et al., 2023). Findings
   356вҶ’indicate that both scales were unidimensional, and internal consistency of the IDQ (рқң” = 0.96)
   357вҶ’and IAQ (рқң” = 0.96) were high.
   358вҶ’Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale
   359вҶ’The Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale (DERS) assesses emotion dysregulation
   360вҶ’(ED) and was first developed by Gratz and Roemer (2004). The 36 items of the DERS
   361вҶ’
   362вҶ’11
   363вҶ’encompass six subscales, i.e., Nonacceptance of Emotional Responses (Nonacceptance),
   364вҶ’Difficulties Engaging in Goal-Directed Behavior (Goals), Impulse Control Difficulties
   365вҶ’(Impulse), Lack of Emotional Awareness (Awareness), Limited Access to Emotion Regulation
   366вҶ’Strategies (Strategies), and Lack of Emotional Clarity (Clarity). Gratz and Roemer (2004)
   367вҶ’reported high internal consistency for the total scale (рқӣј = .93), and for all subscales (рқӣј вүҘ .80).
   368вҶ’A cross-culturally adapted and validated version in Ukrainian language was developed by
   369вҶ’Sak and Fedotova (2023). Sufficient internal consistency for initial testing (рқӣј = .70) and for
   370вҶ’retesting after 14 days (рқӣј = .70) was reported. Previous findings have shown that all
   371вҶ’subscales represent a higher order emotion regulation construct, with exception of the
   372вҶ’subscale DERS-Awareness which does not seem to share sufficient variance with a general
   373вҶ’ED factor (Bardeen et al., 2012; Hallion et al., 2018). These findings could be explained by
   374вҶ’the ambiguous effects of being aware of oneвҖҷs emotional state, which might not necessarily
   375вҶ’lead to adaptive modulation of emotion (Bardeen et al., 2012). Furthermore, studies indicate
   376вҶ’that excluding the subscale DERS-Awareness improves the measureвҖҷs validity in assessing
   377вҶ’ED (Bardeen et al., 2012; Hallion et al., 2018). Therefore, the subscale DERS-Awareness
   378вҶ’was excluded, and all other subscales have been summed to a total score.
   379вҶ’Statistical Analysis
   380вҶ’A priori power analysis was conducted to estimate required sample size using
   381вҶ’G*Power 3.1.9.6 for Macintosh (Faul et al., 2007). IBM SPSS Statistics Version 30 (IBM
   382вҶ’Corp, 2024) and RStudio (Posit Software, 2025) for R (R Core Team, 2023) were used to
   383вҶ’conduct statistical analyses. A priori power estimations utilized an alpha error of Оұ = .05 and
   384вҶ’a power of .80. To test Hypothesis 1, multiple simple linear regression models were
   385вҶ’estimated with one predictor (i.e., CTS), and four respective outcomes (i.e., depression,
   386вҶ’anxiety, PTSD and CPTSD). Minimum required sample size was estimated to be N = 55,
   387вҶ’considering a mean effect size of Ж’ВІ = .15 (Goral et al., 2021; Greene et al., 2018; Lahav,
   388вҶ’2020; Zasiekina et al., 2024) for fixed simple linear regression models looking at the RВІ
   389вҶ’deviation from zero. To test Hypothesis 2, a moderation analysis using PROCESS for SPSS
   390вҶ’(Hayes, 2022) assessed interaction effects by adding ED and its interaction term with CTS to
   391вҶ’the same four linear regression models. Minimum required sample size was estimated at N =
   392вҶ’77, considering a mean effect size of Ж’ВІ = .15 (Bardeen et al., 2012; Goral et al., 2017;
   393вҶ’Hallion et al., 2018; Levin et al., 2023) for fixed multiple linear regression models looking at
   394вҶ’the change in RВІ with three predictors in the model. To test Hypothesis 3, the author
   395вҶ’conducted a multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) for independent samples. Minimum
   396вҶ’required sample size was estimated at N = 98, considering a mean effect size of PillaiвҖҷs trace
   397вҶ’(V) = .13 (Fornaro et al., 2025; Kalaitzaki et al., 2024) to test global effects on means of two
   398вҶ’groups with six outcomes. The pre-analysis plan (SteinkГӨmper & Lueger-Schuster, 2025)
   399вҶ’was preregistered on the Open Science Framework (Foster & Deardorff, 2017).

  1301вҶ’Given the high intercorrelations among the variables assessed in this study, the
  1302вҶ’author examined the semantic similarity of questionnaire items. All questionnaire items used
  1303вҶ’in this study were evaluated for overlapping descriptions and semantic similarity. Based on
  1304вҶ’this assessment, several item pairs among the questionnaires indicated semantic overlap.
  1305вҶ’For example: вҖңHave had difficulty concentrating?вҖқ (IDQ) and вҖңHad difficulty concentrating?вҖқ
  1306вҶ’(IAQ), вҖңFeeling jumpy or easily startled?вҖқ (ITQ) and вҖңFelt physically tense or agitated?вҖқ (IAQ),
  1307вҶ’or вҖңWhen I am upset, it takes me a long time to calm downвҖқ (ITQ) and вҖңWhen IвҖҷm upset, it
  1308вҶ’takes me a long time to feel betterвҖқ (DERS). Thus, judged by face validity, multiple
  1309вҶ’measurement instruments employed in this study show inherent similarities in their semantic
  1310вҶ’content. Furthermore, the similarity of questionnaire items was assessed with the Harmony
  1311вҶ’software, using the harmonydata package for R (Wood et al., 2022). Harmony is a free-touse tool developed for harmonization of mental health questionnaires (McElroy et al., 2024).
  1312вҶ’It employs natural language processing to represent each text question into a numeric vector,
  1313вҶ’and judge its similarity based on its respective distance to other vectors. A cosine similarity
  1314вҶ’index is computed ranging from -1 to +1, with values closer to 1 reflecting a greater semantic
  1315вҶ’match. A moderate correlation was found between the cosine indices of similarity and the
  1316вҶ’Spearman coefficients in a test battery of 39 mental health questions. McElroy et al. (2024)
  1317вҶ’examined a holdout sample of questionnaires, which were not used to train the language
  1318вҶ’
  1319вҶ’19
  1320вҶ’model, and found that the cosine similarity indices could predict the real-world correlation
  1321вҶ’coefficients of questionnaire-items. Notably, the IDQ, IAQ (Shevlin et al., 2023), and ITQ
  1322вҶ’(Cloitre et al., 2018) employed in this study were used in the holdout sample, and similar
  1323вҶ’intercorrelations and correlational networks were found between those questionnaires. Thus,
  1324вҶ’the author implemented Harmony to objectively assess the semantic similarities among the
  1325вҶ’questionnaires used in this study. For analysis, the author disabled matches of item pairs
  1326вҶ’within instruments, permitted multiple matches for a single questionnaire item, and set the
  1327вҶ’cut-off for relevant similarities to a cosine similarity index greater than or equal to .70. The
  1328вҶ’language model produced 11 matches of item pairs with relevant cosine similarity indices
  1329вҶ’(see Appendix D). The matches largely overlap the similarity assessment judged by face
  1330вҶ’validity.
  1331вҶ’Furthermore, post-hoc power analyses revealed less statistical power than a priori
  1332вҶ’anticipated, estimating a power of 48.6% for PTSD, 37.0% for anxiety, 18.3% for DSO, and
  1333вҶ’6.4% for depressive symptoms. Post-hoc power analysis of interaction effects was conducted
  1334вҶ’using the Shiny App InteractionPoweR, an openly available tool to estimate statistical power
  1335вҶ’of moderated multiple linear regression models (Finsaas et al., 2021). A review of 261
  1336вҶ’articles in psychology journals found that interaction effects tend to be quite small with a
  1337вҶ’median effect of r = .04 (Aguinis et al., 2005). In personality science, replicable interaction
  1338вҶ’effects tend to be even smaller with a median effect size of r = .02 (Vize et al., 2022). In
  1339вҶ’consequence, incorrect power estimations may lead to largely underpowered studies.
  1340вҶ’Baranger et al. (2023) described power estimations for interaction effects as a function of
  1341вҶ’residual variance explained by the model, which depends on the correlations between each
  1342вҶ’predictor and the outcome, as well as the correlation of the predictors with each other. While
  1343вҶ’larger correlations of the respective predictors with the outcome can increase the residual
  1344вҶ’variance explained by the interaction term, a larger correlation between the predictors and
  1345вҶ’the interaction term can both decrease or increase statistical power. The influence on power
  1346вҶ’estimations for interaction effects depends on whether the total variance explained by the
  1347вҶ’interaction term is greater or less than the sum of their individual effects (Baranger et al.,
  1348вҶ’2023). In this study, the effects of ED decreased the individual effects of CTS on the outcome
  1349вҶ’variables, leading to less total variance explained by the interaction term and decreased
  1350вҶ’statistical power.
  1351вҶ’Discussion
  1352вҶ’Continuous traumatic stress (CTS) describes the psychological reaction to contexts of
  1353вҶ’multiple potentially traumatic stressors, which are experienced as ongoing and
  1354вҶ’encompassing. The theoretical framework postulates encompassing psychological reactions
  1355вҶ’that are associated with, but distinct from, trauma-related psychopathologies. CTS symptoms
  1356вҶ’largely overlap with ICD-11 diagnostic categories, such as CPTSD and MDD (WHO, 2019).
  1357вҶ’
  1358вҶ’20
  1359вҶ’Nonetheless, symptoms in specific contexts (i.e., civilians in war-threatened regions) may not
  1360вҶ’represent manifest psychopathologies but psychological distress due to ongoing and
  1361вҶ’encompassing traumatic stressors. Moreover, these reactions may constitute a functional
  1362вҶ’reaction to threatful environments (Itzhaky et al., 2017). The WHO would include CTS in the
  1363вҶ’ICD-11 if sufficient empirical evidence were provided (Bachem & Maercker, 2024). Therefore,
  1364вҶ’this study investigated associations between CTS and psychopathological symptoms in two
  1365вҶ’samples of Ukrainian civilians in Austria and Ukraine in 2025. Further, effects of emotion
  1366вҶ’dysregulation (ED) on these associations and group differences in levels of
  1367вҶ’psychopathological symptoms between both subsamples were examined. Exploratory
  1368вҶ’analyses comprised frequencies of questionnaire-derived diagnoses and index traumas, as
  1369вҶ’well as post-hoc power analyses, and semantic similarity assessments of measurement
  1370вҶ’instruments.
  1371вҶ’Findings show considerable associations of CTS with psychopathological symptoms,
  1372вҶ’accounting for 28% to 47% of variance in depressive, anxiety, PTSD, and DSO symptoms.
  1373вҶ’The residence of participants (i.e., Eastern Ukraine vs. Austria) did not explain variance in
  1374вҶ’psychopathological symptoms beyond CTS. ED emerged as an additional clinically relevant
  1375вҶ’factor, predicting depressive, anxiety, and DSO symptoms. Further, ED influenced the
  1376вҶ’associations of CTS with anxiety, PTSD, and DSO symptoms, attenuating these
  1377вҶ’associations. However, the incremental variance explained by the interaction term was small,
  1378вҶ’and post-hoc power analyses indicated that moderation effects were largely underpowered.
  1379вҶ’Contrary to hypotheses, participants residing in Austria reported higher levels of PTSD and
  1380вҶ’DSO symptoms, a greater number of traumatic exposures, and a significantly higher
  1381вҶ’prevalence of questionnaire-derived CPTSD diagnoses. Exploratory analyses revealed that
  1382вҶ’two-thirds (66.4%) of participants met questionnaire-derived diagnostic criteria for one or
  1383вҶ’more ICD-11 disorders. Lastly, high comorbidity of questionnaire-derived diagnoses
  1384вҶ’throughout the sample and large associations between all measurement instruments were
  1385вҶ’observed.
  1386вҶ’The strong overlap within internalizing symptoms aligns with the Hierarchical
  1387вҶ’Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) model (Kotov et al., 2017, 2021), suggesting that a
  1388вҶ’general distress factor may underlie the observed symptom patterns and that CTS potentially
  1389вҶ’represents a context-specific construct of this distress dimension. At the same time, the size
  1390вҶ’of observed effects underscores the necessity of a solid theoretical framework, as these
  1391вҶ’associations cannot be interpreted meaningfully from a purely data-driven perspective.
  1392вҶ’Therefore, the mechanisms of these relationships remain unclear. However, all
  1393вҶ’psychopathological symptoms assessed (i.e., MDD, GAD, PTSD, CPTSD) relate to this
  1394вҶ’distress subfactor, which may act as a theoretical framework explaining the large
  1395вҶ’associations observed in this study. These findings also pertain to the construct validity of
  1396вҶ’
  1397вҶ’21
  1398вҶ’CTS and support the construct in describing psychological reactions to encompassing and
  1399вҶ’current traumatic stressors. Therefore, CTS may act as a context-specific construct
  1400вҶ’describing the encompassing psychological reactions to ongoing traumatic stressors.
  1401вҶ’Preliminarily evidence indicates a nuanced but limited role of ED in shaping symptom
  1402вҶ’severity. However, ED may act as a predictor, outcome, or potential collider variable of
  1403вҶ’psychopathological symptoms (Beauchaine & Cicchetti, 2019; Carmassi et al., 2022; Conti et
  1404вҶ’al., 2023; Hien et al., 2017; Lincoln et al., 2022). Given the limited statistical power and the
  1405вҶ’exploratory nature of the a priori model specification, the findings must be interpreted with
  1406вҶ’caution and require replication within more robust theoretical models. Rather than indicating
  1407вҶ’a simple geographical effect, findings suggest that the living conditions of Ukrainian civilians
  1408вҶ’may encompass more relevant contextual factors as predictors of symptom burden.
  1409вҶ’Moreover, effects of geographical distance to the conflict zone may be offset by individualsвҖҷ
  1410вҶ’contextual living conditions, such as occupational status or perceived threat and concern.
  1411вҶ’This is supported by anecdotal evidence of participantsвҖҷ e-mails describing personal
  1412вҶ’experiences with war and displacement to Austria (see Appendix E, F), which emphasize the
  1413вҶ’psychological distress in Ukrainian civilians who were forced or obliged to flee abroad. A
  1414вҶ’meta-analysis reported similar effects, indicating that some civilians who have not been
  1415вҶ’forced or obliged to flee experienced less threats to the own security initially (Fornaro et al.,
  1416вҶ’2025). Collectively, the findings highlight the psychological distress and symptom burden
  1417вҶ’experienced by war-affected Ukrainian civilians, both abroad and in their war-torn homeland.
  1418вҶ’Moreover, no participant reported prior diagnoses of a mental disorder or psychiatric
  1419вҶ’hospitalization, nonetheless about two-thirds of participants met criteria for at least one
  1420вҶ’questionnaire-derived psychopathology. Notably, these results are in line with existing
  1421вҶ’literature from 1945 to 2022, reporting prevalences of depression (33.3%), anxiety (38.6%)
  1422вҶ’and PTSD (25.7%) in civilian populations in war- or conflict-afflicted areas (Lim et al., 2022).
  1423вҶ’Considering these findings, the observed prevalences may constitute a somewhat normal
  1424вҶ’reaction to extreme situations. Furthermore, previous findings indicate more conceptual
  1425вҶ’similarity and larger associations of CTS with CPTSD compared to PTSD (Eagle & Kaminer,
  1426вҶ’2013). Higher prevalences of questionnaire-derived CPTSD in relation to PTSD diagnoses in
  1427вҶ’this sample support this notion (see Table 6). Moreover, data suggests civilian trauma
  1428вҶ’reactions and accompanying symptoms as encompassing, multidimensional, and nonspecific. High comorbidity of questionnaire-derived diagnoses and large associations
  1429вҶ’between all measurement instruments indicate non-specificity and multidimensionality of
  1430вҶ’ongoing trauma reactions. Collectively, these findings emphasize CTS as a clinically relevant
  1431вҶ’construct in describing the encompassing symptoms of ongoing traumatic reactions in waraffected civilians.

  1433вҶ’22
  1434вҶ’Several limitations in regard to the sample, research design and statistical inference
  1435вҶ’restrict the validity and generalizability of findings. First, the cross-sectional, correlational
  1436вҶ’design precludes causal inference and limits internal validity of findings. The observed
  1437вҶ’associations indicate potential effects, however the directionality and underlying mechanisms
  1438вҶ’of effects CTS remain unresolved. Longitudinal designs are necessary to examine symptom
  1439вҶ’trajectories, remission following contextual changes, or transitions into manifest
  1440вҶ’psychopathology (Maercker & Brewin, 2024; Straker, 2013). Controlled sampling methods
  1441вҶ’were not feasible in data collection. However, matching of sociodemographic variables
  1442вҶ’between samples (i.e., matched samples) and repeated measurement designs would have
  1443вҶ’increased internal validity. Additionally, the data was characterized by high intercorrelations
  1444вҶ’among constructs (see Table 2), which have been established in existing literature on
  1445вҶ’psychopathology (Kotov et al., 2017, 2021). These intercorrelations are potentially due to
  1446вҶ’conceptual overlap and non-specificity of symptoms, such as difficulty concentrating,
  1447вҶ’diminished interest, or changes in appetite or sleep. Furthermore, intercorrelations were
  1448вҶ’potentially facilitated by the semantic similarities among measurement instruments (see
  1449вҶ’Appendix D), thus increased redundancy in measurement and attenuated statistical power in
  1450вҶ’this study. Although this augmented the convergent validity of CTS (i.e., Hypothesis 1), the
  1451вҶ’specificity between constructs remained limited. Consequently, interpretation of findings
  1452вҶ’requires a strong theoretic framework rather than solely data-driven reasoning. Furthermore,
  1453вҶ’CTS levels potentially reflect general psychological distress, which may not be specific to
  1454вҶ’traumatic stressors but relate to secondary stressors in this highly burdened sample. Further,
  1455вҶ’questions remain if CTS accurately reflects ongoing exposure to extreme stressors or
  1456вҶ’broader psychological distress to secondary stressors (Lock et al., 2012). This may be
  1457вҶ’especially relevant for the subsample in Austria, as ongoing war-exposure was not evident.
  1458вҶ’The notion of transnosological psychological distress is further supported by high symptom
  1459вҶ’levels, intercorrelations among symptoms, and comorbidity of questionnaire-derived
  1460вҶ’diagnoses in this sample. However, these findings are consistent with the distress subfactor
  1461вҶ’of the internalizing spectrum in the HiTOP model (Kotov et al., 2017, 2021).
  1462вҶ’The model specification for Hypothesis 2 lacked a sufficiently established theoretical
  1463вҶ’basis and was partly exploratory. Notably, existing literature reported inconclusive and
  1464вҶ’heterogenous effects of ED on psychopathology, stating ED as a risk factor, moderator, as
  1465вҶ’well as an outcome of psychopathology (Beauchaine & Cicchetti, 2019; Carmassi et al.,
  1466вҶ’2022; Conti et al., 2023; Hien et al., 2017; Lincoln et al., 2022). Thus, it is likely that ED
  1467вҶ’exhibits diverse effects in context of CTS and trauma-related psychopathology and
  1468вҶ’potentially constitutes both a predictor and outcome of psychopathology. These findings limit
  1469вҶ’the proposed model specification of Hypothesis 2, such that ED constitutes a moderator of
  1470вҶ’associations between CTS and psychopathological symptoms. Moreover, ED shares
  1471вҶ’
  1472вҶ’23
  1473вҶ’conceptual overlap and semantic similarities with CTS and DSO (see Appendix D),
  1474вҶ’accounting for substantive variance in these constructs (see Table 2), as emotion regulation
  1475вҶ’difficulties are also part of CTS and DSO symptoms. Collectively, this attenuated statistical
  1476вҶ’power due to shared variance and resulted in insufficient statistical power for Hypothesis 2.
  1477вҶ’Further, existing literature suggests that ED shows heterogenous effects depending on the
  1478вҶ’population, contexts, psychopathology, and ED strategies assessed (Blay et al., 2025;
  1479вҶ’Carmassi et al., 2022; Conti et al., 2023; Lincoln et al., 2022; Weiss et al., 2020). Therefore,
  1480вҶ’the present findings on ED should be interpreted as preliminary, and effect sizes as modest
  1481вҶ’at most.
  1482вҶ’Lastly, subsamples in Austria and Ukraine were unequal due to the non-probabilistic
  1483вҶ’ad-hoc data collection. Recruitment strategies differed substantially (i.e., psychological
  1484вҶ’counseling services in Austria vs. academic contexts in Ukraine), such that the subsample in
  1485вҶ’Austria included individuals with explicit help-seeking behavior which was not evident in the
  1486вҶ’subsample in Ukraine. Additionally, subsamples differed significantly in age, education level,
  1487вҶ’and presumably in help-seeking behavior as well as occupation. Lower symptom levels of
  1488вҶ’participants in Ukraine were potentially result of sampling, which was conducted in academic
  1489вҶ’networks of Ukrainian researchers. This is supported by early findings during the RUW,
  1490вҶ’which identified female sex as a risk factor, whereas higher education appeared to be a
  1491вҶ’protective factor for PTSD severity (Zasiekina et al., 2023). Evidently, two different
  1492вҶ’subsamples of Ukrainian civilians emerged during data collection, which differed in
  1493вҶ’sociodemographic (i.e., age and education level) and clinical dimensions (i.e., help-seeking
  1494вҶ’behavior). This sample is not representative of the Ukrainian civilian population and appear
  1495вҶ’to comprise distinctive subpopulations. Therefore, group differences in symptom levels
  1496вҶ’should not only be attributed to the country of residence but partially to the distinct
  1497вҶ’subpopulations. The heterogeneity of the Ukrainian population, which includes internally or
  1498вҶ’externally displaced individuals, varying levels of education, and diverse histories of
  1499вҶ’traumatic exposures restrict generalizability to the general population. High attrition rates
  1500вҶ’(i.e., 50.2% response rate) further limit external validity. However, it is also important to note
  1501вҶ’that the studyвҖҷs sampling may reflect a selection bias. Presumably, civilians who were forced
  1502вҶ’or obliged to flee abroad due to high-risk environments or active threats had a higher
  1503вҶ’psychological burden initially (i.e., more traumatic exposure, less resources), than those who
  1504вҶ’did not have to flee (Fornaro et al., 2025), which is supported by the present studyвҖҳs data.
  1505вҶ’Lastly, high prevalences of PTSD and CPTSD in this sample may be influenced by the
  1506вҶ’predominantly female sample, as women are at higher risk for PTSD (Kessler et al., 2017).
  1507вҶ’Therefore, this study may overestimate prevalences in the general population of Ukrainian
  1508вҶ’civilians.

  1511вҶ’Future research should employ multi-method research designs emphasizing clinical
  1512вҶ’interviews to inform interpretation of observed effects. Qualitative assessment of the
  1513вҶ’subjective meaning of symptoms could facilitate interpretation and theoretical grounding of
  1514вҶ’the observed effects. Meaningful factors may constitute war-related stressors and perceived
  1515вҶ’threat and concern, which are associated with symptom burden and can significantly impact
  1516вҶ’civilian life in war-threatened areas (Eagle & Kaminer, 2013; Kalaitzaki et al., 2024; Kaminer
  1517вҶ’et al., 2016; Levin et al., 2023). These findings are also in line with Lock et al. (2012), who
  1518вҶ’identified secondary stressors (e.g., economic, health, or social stressors) as equally
  1519вҶ’important to primary stressors (e.g., witnessing death or destruction). Secondary stressors
  1520вҶ’may include worries about relatives (e.g., separation), difficulties with compensation (e.g.,
  1521вҶ’insurance claims), economics (e.g., loss of income), media (e.g., negative reports), or a
  1522вҶ’general loss of agency. Such war-related stressors have also shown associations with PTSD
  1523вҶ’and CPTSD (Kalaitzaki et al., 2024). Thus, war-related stressors should be regarded as a
  1524вҶ’contributing factor to symptom burden in further research. Furthermore, probabilistic
  1525вҶ’sampling approaches (i.e., stratified sampling) are needed to generate more representative
  1526вҶ’data, facilitating external validity and generalizability of findings to the heterogenous
  1527вҶ’population of war-affected Ukrainian civilians. Lastly, future research should acknowledge the
  1528вҶ’complex, context-specific living conditions of civilians under CTS, which may ultimately
  1529вҶ’shape symptom severity and symptom burden. It is further recommended to establish a solid
  1530вҶ’theoretical framework for effects of ED in context of CTS, employ qualitative (e.g., clinical
  1531вҶ’interviews) and longitudinal data, to inform interpretation of effects. Lastly, the specific living
  1532вҶ’contexts of civilians should be acknowledged and contextual conditions examined (e.g.,
  1533вҶ’occupational status, war-related stressors, perception of threat).
  1534вҶ’In conclusion, the present study provides meaningful empirical evidence to CTS as a
  1535вҶ’context-specific construct describing encompassing and ongoing trauma reactions, which are
  1536вҶ’closely associated with, and may partly explain, psychopathological symptoms in waraffected civilians. However, important questions regarding the specificity of the CTS construct
  1537вҶ’remain. Findings underscore the psychological distress in internationally displayed Ukrainian
  1538вҶ’civilians and the heterogeneity within war-affected civilian populations, emphasizing the need
  1539вҶ’for controlled designs. Counterintuitive findings show lower symptom levels of Ukrainian
  1540вҶ’civilians in Ukraine and underscore the importance of contextual conditions in ongoing
  1541вҶ’trauma reactions, which is consistent with the CTS framework. While preliminary evidence
  1542вҶ’indicates ED to be implicated in trauma reactions and to influence associations between CTS
  1543вҶ’and psychopathological symptoms, its precise effects require further theoretical clarification
  1544вҶ’and well-powered designs. Further research with longitudinal data is necessary to clarify
  1545вҶ’mechanisms, refine theoretical models, and inform context-specific research. In particular,
  1546вҶ’clinical interviews on the subjective meaning of individualsвҖҷ symptoms and war-related
  1547вҶ’
  1548вҶ’25
  1549вҶ’stressors are essential to contextualize interpretation of data, given the non-specific and
  1550вҶ’multidimensional nature of ongoing trauma reactions. While effects are complex and many
  1551вҶ’questions remain regarding the psychological reactions to current traumatic stressors among
  1552вҶ’war-threatened civilians, the present findings highlight their detrimental psychological
  1553вҶ’consequences and support the relevance of CTS in assessing ongoing trauma reactions.
