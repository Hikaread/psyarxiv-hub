    19→Abstract
    20→As global aging accelerates, retirement has become a critical transition in older adults’ lives, with
    21→profound implications for mental health. Depression trajectories around retirement are inconsistent,
    22→with some studies suggesting worsening symptoms and others indicating improvement. Using HRS
    23→(United States, 1994–2020), SHARE (Europe and Israel, 2004–2022), CHARLS (China, 2011–2020),
    24→and KLoSA (South Korea, 2006–2020), this study examined depressive symptom trajectories around
    25→retirement. We emphasized three key components of the retirement transition: retirement age, pre-
    26→retirement phase, and post-retirement phase. Using multilevel models, we estimated changes in
    27→depressive symptoms around retirement transition, accounting for cross-national and historical
    28→differences. Results showed that depressive symptom trajectories varied across datasets and historical
    29→periods, but average changes were generally modest. HRS showed pre-retirement increases, an
    30→immediate rise at retirement, and post-retirement decline. SHARE showed mainly a small immediate
    31→decrease, CHARLS showed a steady increase across the retirement transition, and KLoSA showed an
    32→immediate increase followed by post-retirement decline. Period-specific analyses showed further
    33→variation, although many trajectories remained close to stable; in CHARLS, later retirement periods
    34→showed steeper post-retirement increases. The findings show that retirement-related changes in
    35→depressive symptoms are often modest at the group level, while small departures from stability may
    36→vary by temporal phase and historical context.

    39→With the accelerating global aging process (WHO, 2024) and the economic challenges ahead
    40→(World Bank, 2025), the implications of retirement have undergone profound changes. It is no longer
    41→simply a withdrawal from one’s working life; rather, it has become a pivotal and uncertain transition in
    42→the life course (Beier & Fisher, 2025). Meanwhile, depression among older adults is increasingly
    43→coming to the forefront (Scazufca & Seward, 2024). Specifically, research has identified an inflection
    44→point in depression trajectories for individuals in their 60s (Abrams & Mehta, 2019; Y. Zhang & Ma,
    45→2025), which has focused growing attention on the impact of retirement as a significant turning point
    46→for psychological well-being (Chia et al., 2024; Spears et al., 2025; Zhan et al., 2023). However, the
    47→evidence remains mixed: two meta analyses reach opposing conclusions, Li et al. (2021) link retirement
    48→to higher depressive symptoms, whereas Odone et al. (2021) report a protective association. One key
    49→limitation is that many studies overlook the perspective of retirement as a transition (M. Wang et al.,
    50→2011), while also paying insufficient attention to the factor of age at retirement. Additionally, as both
    51→the nature of retirement and the socio-economic context in which it occurs continue to evolve (M. Wang
    52→& Shi, 2014), previous research has not adequately addressed the macro-level perspective across
    53→different retirement periods. Accordingly, under a transition lens, we define the retirement transition by
    54→three core components: the age of retirement as a key variable shaping the timing of labor force exit, a
    55→pre-retirement phase, and a post-retirement phase. We examine how depressive trajectories evolve
    56→across this transition and how the historical retirement period shapes these dynamics.
    57→Micro-Level Perspective
    58→Retirement marks a profound life transition, often accompanied by sociopsychological shifts.
    59→Role theory (Carter & Cook, 1995; George, 1993) suggests that this change may result either in “role-
    60→less role” (Rosow et al., 1976), increasing the risk of depression, or in the release from role strain (J. E.
    61→2
    62→
    63→Kim & Moen, 2001), which can ease depressive symptoms. The crisis view further emphasizes the
    64→potential losses of retirement, particularly when relinquishing a valued occupational identity erodes
    65→one’s sense of worth, social recognition, and well-being (Mutran & Reitzes, 1981). Continuity theory
    66→(Atchley, 1989), by contrast, contends that middle-aged and older adults adapt to retirement by
    67→preserving internal and external structures, drawing on familiar strategies rooted in their past to sustain
    68→psychological stability (M. Wang et al., 2011). A complementary explanation comes from the
    69→conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll et al., 2018), which highlights resource loss and gain as
    70→central to adaptation. Primary losses of income, identity, and social ties may trigger secondary depletion
    71→and depression (Zhan et al., 2023), whereas resource gains, such as family support and new pursuits,
    72→can buffer losses and promote resilience (M. Wang et al., 2011).
    73→An accumulating body of research has begun to focus on personality development in later
    74→adulthood (Bleidorn et al., 2022; Donnellan & Lucas, 2008; Lucas & Donnellan, 2011), emphasizing
    75→its critical role in older adults’ psychological well-being and the consequential life choices surrounding
    76→retirement. Notably, personality traits are associated with mental health (Klein et al., 2011): high
    77→neuroticism, low extraversion, and low conscientiousness are closely linked to depressive symptoms
    78→(Hakulinen et al., 2015), while high self-esteem serves as a protective factor against depression (Orth
    79→et al., 2016). However, these traits are subject to developmental changes in old age. Research on mean-
    80→level change indicates that several traits decline (Bleidorn et al., 2022), particularly conscientiousness—
    81→a trend especially pronounced among retirees (Lucas & Donnellan, 2011)—alongside observed
    82→divergences in agreeableness between retirees and non-retirees (Löckenhoff et al., 2009). These
    83→developmental dynamics are particularly salient during the retirement transition. Beyond direct
    84→associations, personality traits influence both the selection of life events (e.g., retirement timing) and
    85→3
    86→
    87→the reaction to them through mechanisms such as differential coping strategies and variations in social
    88→resources. Specific trajectory patterns emerge during this period: self-esteem exhibits a significant,
    89→sudden decline followed by stabilization (Bleidorn & Schwaba, 2018), whereas emotional stability
    90→demonstrates a pattern of continuous growth (Schwaba & Bleidorn, 2019). Within this dynamic context,
    91→individuals with high extraversion maintain more positive psychological outcomes, whereas those high
    92→in neuroticism experience significant declines in autonomy and social support (Hansson et al., 2020).
    93→Collectively, these perspectives portray retirees as active agents who negotiate role transitions
    94→and resource dynamics to manage their psychological well-being during retirement transition (Bordia
    95→et al., 2020), and they imply that depressive symptoms may rise, fall, or remain stable before and after
    96→retirement (Asselmann & Specht, 2024).
    97→Whether retirement protects or threatens mental health remains a contested question. Rather
    98→than being contradictory, these perspectives highlight the heterogeneity of retirement experiences.
    99→When retirement is treated as a one-off state change, some studies suggest that retirement can have
   100→positive effects (Coe & Zamarro, 2011; Gorry et al., 2018; La Rue et al., 2022; Odone et al., 2021). For
   101→example, a meta-analysis identified fewer physical health symptoms and the ease of maintaining social
   102→relationships as key predictors of successful post-retirement adjustment (La Rue et al., 2022), with
   103→mental health benefits particularly evident among women and blue-collar workers (Coe & Zamarro,
   104→2011). Other research, however, has associated retirement with an increase in depressive symptoms
   105→(Calvo et al., 2013; Heller-Sahlgren, 2017; Li et al., 2021; Segel-Karpas et al., 2018). In these cases,
   106→factors such as whether the retirement was voluntary (Li et al., 2021), the nature of preretirement job
   107→experiences (Hetschko et al., 2019), and individuals’ preretirement physical and mental health status
   108→(Segel-Karpas et al., 2018) appear to play a significant role in shaping outcomes. Together with
   109→4
   110→
   111→personality traits, these individual characteristics imply a fundamental selection effect (Denissen et al.,
   112→2019; Orth & Luciano, 2015). Specifically, they not only influence mental health directly but also
   113→predispose individuals to experience specific life events or select themselves into particular
   114→environments. However, when focusing on the retirement transition as a process, we argue that
   115→regardless of how these micro-level factors influence decision-making, they ultimately converge on a
   116→single critical variable—the age of retirement. While factors such as physical health, occupational
   117→experience, and personality traits are often studied independently, they are essentially key determinants
   118→of when individuals retire (G. G. Fisher et al., 2016), retirement age functions as a pivotal yet
   119→overlooked nexus in explaining the trajectory of well-being across this life transition.
   120→Although prior studies have yielded valuable insights into the mental health effects of
   121→retirement, it is important to note that retirement is not a single event but rather an adjustment process
   122→(M. Wang et al., 2011; M. Wang & Shi, 2014)—encompassing both the transition out of work and the
   123→adaptation that follows. In other words, this perspective emphasizes both the anticipation effects that
   124→precede retirement and the socialization effects that follow it (Asselmann & Specht, 2024; Reitz et al.,
   125→2022). However, even studies that conceptualize retirement as a process continue to yield conflicting
   126→results. Some have found that older adults experience lower levels of mental health (Asselmann &
   127→Specht, 2024) or life satisfaction (Luhmann et al., 2012a) before retirement, but show improvements
   128→after retirement (Asselmann & Specht, 2024; Kettlewell et al., 2020). Others report the opposite pattern,
   129→with mental health improving in anticipation of retirement (Henning et al., 2022; Lahdenperä et al.,
   130→2022; Vo & Phu-Duyen, 2023), and remaining stable afterward (Lahdenperä et al., 2022). Still others
   131→identify a short-term increase in mental health following retirement (Mosconi et al., 2023; Wetzel et al.,
   132→2016), but long-term trajectories vary depending on individuals’ resources (Wetzel et al., 2016).
   133→5
   134→
   135→These divergent findings reveal that micro-level factors, while crucial, are insufficient on their
   136→own. Crucially, the resources an individual brings to retirement are inherently situated within, and
   137→structured by, the broader societal context (Ferraro et al., 2009). This context, comprising economic
   138→policies, institutional frameworks, and cultural norms, determines the pathways available for resource
   139→development. Acknowledging this embeddedness makes it imperative to adopt an integrated micro-
   140→macro perspective to untangle the complex effects of retirement on mental health.
   141→Macro-Level Perspective
   142→An integrated perspective reveals that the retirement experience is systematically shaped by
   143→macro-level forces such as historical economic contexts, national polices, and sociocultural norms
   144→(Beier & Fisher, 2025). The influence of these broad factors, combined with micro-level individual
   145→characteristics, converges on a single, powerful indicator: the age of retirement. Defined as the point
   146→when a worker exits their primary career path, retirement age is highly context-dependent, varying with
   147→national pension policies and cultural expectations (G. G. Fisher et al., 2016). A comprehensive model
   148→of retirement timing (G. G. Fisher et al., 2016) demonstrates that individual (e.g., health, wealth, and
   149→personality traits), work-related, and macroeconomic factors act as antecedents determining this age,
   150→which in turn predicts subsequent outcomes like mental health. Therefore, we posit that retirement age
   151→mediates the effects of both micro- and macro-level factors on the retirement transition, while
   152→simultaneously confounding the association between time relative to retirement and depressive
   153→symptoms at a given time point. This dual role is formalized in the left panel of Figure 1 using a directed
   154→acyclic graph (DAG).
   155→Consequently, retirement age is not merely a number but, as an integral component of the
   156→retirement transition, a composite indicator that encapsulates the culmination of an individual’s life
   157→6
   158→
   159→course, reflecting their accumulated health capital, financial security, and their navigation of both social
   160→expectations and institutional mandates. To dissect this complexity, adopting the lens of life course
   161→theory offers valuable insights. The conceptualization of the life course is a function of economic,
   162→demographic, and sociocultural forces (Elder, 1994). Retirement age varies across national contexts and
   163→historical periods, shaped by evolving policy regimes and shifting socio-economic environments. Even
   164→within a single country, individuals may retire at different ages depending on the prevailing institutional
   165→configurations and historical conditions. Accordingly, the retirement transition should be viewed as a
   166→dynamic process shaped by the interactions between macro-structural forces and individual agency
   167→(Moen, 1996).
   168→On the one hand, it is essential to recognize that retirement, as an institutionally-defined
   169→transition, operate within a multifaceted system of objectives that not only evolves with historical
   170→context (Atchley, 1982) but is also deeply embedded in broader socioeconomic cycles and public policy
   171→(M. Wang, 2013, p. 3). Retirement-income arrangements vary across countries—for example, China
   172→combines notional and funded defined-contribution elements (Williamson et al., 2012), whereas the
   173→United States’ public pension is a pay-as-you-go defined-benefit system, with Defined Contribution
   174→plans prevalent in the occupational pillar (Davies et al., 2022). Specifically, retirement age sits at the
   175→core of macro-level retirement policy change: as historical periods unfold, many countries have raised
   176→the statutory retirement age (Pilipiec et al., 2021; Rabaté et al., 2024), albeit with different designs and
   177→effects. In 2022, the average normal retirement age across the Organization for Economic Co-operation
   178→and Development (OECD) was 64.4 for men and 63.6 for women; in Asian economies, the averages
   179→were 59.1 and 57.3, respectively, underscoring distinct regional gaps. The gap is likely to widen: OECD
   180→ages are set to rise—Italy is projected to reach 71 for today’s entrants—while few Asian economies
   181→7
   182→
   183→have comparably long-horizon reforms (OECD, 2024b). Accordingly, cohorts born in different years
   184→face different retirement ages, for example, in the United States, the full retirement age is 66 for those
   185→born in 1954 or earlier and 67 for those born in 1960 or later (Behaghel & Blau, 2012).
   186→Beyond raising retirement ages, governments under fiscal and demographic pressure also lower
   187→benefits or raise contribution rates, with feasibility shaped by national political economies (J. Kim &
   188→Dougherty, 2020). Individuals from different cohorts who retire under different policy regimes thus
   189→display different levels of psychological well-being around retirement transition (Henning et al., 2022).
   190→Studies have reported improvements in life satisfaction around the retirement threshold over historical
   191→time in Germany and Switzerland (Henning et al., 2023). However, in Southern European countries,
   192→tightened pension policies resulting from economic crises have often meant that retirees face greater
   193→hardship (Hinrichs, 2021a). Moreover, during economic downturns, retirement not only recalibrates
   194→cohort-specific expectations about retirement timing (Szinovacz et al., 2014) but may also confer short-
   195→term mental-health benefits in periods and regions most severely affected by recessions (Belloni et al.,
   196→2016).
   197→On the other hand, even within structural constraints, individuals retain meaningful agency over
   198→the timing and form of retirement (G. G. Fisher et al., 2016; Moen, 1996). These decisions are not made
   199→in a vacuum; they reflect a deliberate appraisal of health status, financial security, social participation,
   200→and work characteristics (Scharn et al., 2018). Some individuals retire early in response to deteriorating
   201→health, whereas others delay their exit due to economic pressures or a sustained sense of vocational
   202→purpose, reflecting the tension between push and pull factors that shape retirement timing (Shultz et al.,
   203→1998). Whether retirement occurs early, at the statutory age, or later, each pathway carries distinct
   204→implications for mental health, and the accumulation of such choices can widen disparities among age
   205→8
   206→
   207→peers (Hershey & Henkens, 2014; Shultz & Wang, 2011). Agency, however, is exercised within the
   208→constraints of prevailing policy regimes. As statutory retirement ages increase and exit routes become
   209→more limited, adapting to extended working life is rarely straightforward and may entail significant
   210→psychological costs (Henkens et al., 2018). At the same time, institutional provisions for alternative
   211→retirement pathways such as partial or phased retirement can contribute to improved mental well-being
   212→(Sohier et al., 2021). The mental-health consequences of retirement transition therefore depend not only
   213→on cross-national difference in institutional arrangements and historical period effects but also on the
   214→scope of agency individuals can exercise within those structures.
   215→Taken together, this body of evidence underscore that the age at which retirement occurs and
   216→the cross-national and historical contexts in which it takes place are critical for understanding the
   217→psychological consequences of retirement transition.
   218→Conceptual Framework and Identification Strategy
   219→Figure 1 summarizes our conceptual framework: The upper panel presents a directed acyclic
   220→graph (DAG) that motivates our identification strategy and covariate adjustment, and the lower panel
   221→depicts stylized trajectory patterns to clarify the logic of trajectory identification. We operationalize the
   222→temporal distance to the transition as the interview date at Wave n minus the respondent-reported
   223→retirement date, and we analyze depressive symptoms measured at Wave n. Within this structure, age at
   224→retirement is treated as a key pre-transition attribute that captures systematic variation in when
   225→individuals retire and is related to depressive symptoms. The DAG further indicates that micro-level
   226→characteristics and macro-level environments act as antecedents that jointly shape retirement age and
   227→depressive symptoms at the pre-retirement phase. Furthermore, time-invariant factors such as sex,
   228→education, and retirement age act as individual-level moderators, while countries and historical periods
   229→9
   230→
   231→function as contexts in which these retirement-related dynamics may vary.
   232→We define our trajectory constructs as comprising the depressive symptom trajectories across
   233→the pre-retirement phase and post-retirement phase, anchored by the individual’s age at retirement. We
   234→parameterize these constructs using a piecewise model that includes: (a) a pre-retirement slope to
   235→capture theorized anticipation effects (i.e., trajectory changes preceding the event); and (b) a retirement-
   236→point indicator and post-retirement slope to capture socialization effects (i.e., immediate level shifts
   237→and/or altered rates of change following the transition). By incorporating age at retirement as a defining
   238→component of the construct, this specification strictly aligns the pre- and post-retirement phases relative
   239→to the specific timing of labor force exit.
   240→The DAG distinguishes between pre-retirement determinants (which drive selection into
   241→retirement) and post-retirement variables that may themselves be affected by the transition. To address
   242→the selection effect—whereby individuals select into retirement at specific times based on pre-existing
   243→characteristics—we treat these characteristics as antecedents that converge on the age of retirement.
   244→Given the assumptions formalized in Figure 1, conditioning on age at retirement is intended to reduce
   245→confounding by blocking prominent backdoor paths from these micro- and macro-level antecedents to
   246→the time-relative-to-retirement variable. This adjustment effectively isolates the trajectory of the
   247→transition itself from the selection processes that determine when the transition occurs. In contrast,
   248→adjusting for post-retirement time-varying factors (e.g., financial status, physical health) risks
   249→introducing bias, as these factors function as mediators (e.g., retirement alters financial strain or health,
   250→which subsequently relates to depressive symptoms) or colliders (e.g., if post-retirement physical health
   251→is jointly influenced by both retirement status and depressive symptoms, conditioning on health would
   252→induce a spurious association between the two). As a result, the model specification avoids adjusting
   253→10
   254→
   255→for post-retirement time-varying variables, relying instead on age at retirement as the primary pre-
   256→transition adjustment for estimating depression trajectories across the retirement transition.
   257→Current study
   258→In this registered report, we examine changes in depressive trajectories across the retirement
   259→transition. We estimate eight multilevel models in which repeated measurement occasions are nested
   260→within participants over time (Doss et al., 2009; Schwaba & Bleidorn, 2019) (see the right panel Figure
   261→1). From these candidates we select the best-fitting model to represent the depressive trajectory around
   262→retirement. Guided by the conceptualization of retirement as an adjustment process, our hypotheses are
   263→further informed by empirical research in personality psychology demonstrating that changes
   264→surrounding life events are often discontinuous (Bleidorn et al., 2018; Luhmann et al., 2014).
   265→Acknowledging that such transitions can trigger abrupt level shifts or alter trajectory slopes, we
   266→hypothesize a significant level shift in depressive symptoms at retirement (H1a) and a change in the
   267→rate of change before versus after retirement (H1b).
   268→We also widen the lens to the macroeconomic environment and social policy. We analyze four
   269→large, harmonized aging studies—HRS (United States), CHARLS (China), SHARE (Europe), and
   270→KLoSA (South Korea)—spanning periods in which countries experienced common global shocks, such
   271→as the 2008 global financial crisis and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, while recoveries followed distinct
   272→national economic cycles and policy adjustment paths. In the context of accelerating population aging,
   273→governments activated different retirement-system levers (Beier & Fisher, 2025), including statutory
   274→retirement ages, eligibility criteria, early or partial-retirement pathways, and pension policies. Some
   275→countries tightened these levers during downturns, whereas others expanded flexibility during
   276→recoveries. We therefore expect cross-national heterogeneity in the effect of the retirement transition on
   277→11
   278→
   279→depressive trajectories (H2).
   280→South Korea and China face particularly severe demographic challenges. South Korea’s
   281→population is aging faster than that of any other advanced economy, with its old-age dependency ratio
   282→projected to surpass Japan’s around 2050 (OECD, 2018). China confronts the unique consequences of
   283→its one-child policy (Cai & Feng, 2021). By contrast, population aging in the United States and Europe,
   284→while substantial, has proceeded more gradually. The shared challenge across these settings is to manage
   285→a long-anticipated transformation within mature and deeply entrenched welfare states (Börsch-Supan et
   286→al., 2014). Accordingly, we hypothesize that in developing or emerging contexts retirement is more
   287→likely to be associated with increases in depressive symptoms (H2a), whereas in advanced contexts
   288→retirement is more likely to be associated with decreases (H2b).
   289→Finally, heterogeneity across countries structures the timing and mode of exit from work and
   290→the resources available thereafter, yielding contrasts that are expressed in each country’s historical
   291→retirement period (Henning et al., 2022). We therefore hypothesize that the historical retirement period
   292→moderates depressive trajectories around retirement (H3) and treat this period—the macroeconomic and
   293→policy context prevailing when retirement occurs—as a macro-level moderator to test how economic
   294→regimes and institutional designs shape the psychological consequences of retirement.
   295→Altogether, this study treat retirement as a transition and trace how depressive symptoms evolve
   296→from pre-retirement to post-retirement. We estimate multilevel models to detect level shifts and slope
   297→changes, and we argue that controlling for retirement age is necessary for unbiased estimation of the
   298→association between retirement and depression. We then leverage four harmonized international cohort
   299→studies to examine heterogeneity across countries and across historical periods. By jointly considering
   300→individual agency together with macroeconomic cycles and national policy regimes, the study aims to
   301→12
   302→
   303→reconcile divergent findings, specify the conditions under which retirement is psychologically
   304→beneficial or harmful, and inform the design of retirement arrangements that safeguard mental well-
   305→being.
   306→Methods
   307→Data
   308→This study draws on longitudinal panel data from four internationally recognized aging studies:
   309→the Health and Retirement Study (HRS), the Survey of Health, Ageing and Retirement in Europe
   310→(SHARE), the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), and the Korean
   311→Longitudinal Study of Aging (KLoSA). These studies offer rich data on retirement and mental health
   312→across diverse populations. To ensure data comparability and completeness, we included waves 2–15
   313→for HRS (1994–2020), waves 1–9 for SHARE (2004–2022), excluding wave 3, waves 1–5 for CHARLS
   314→(2011–2020), and waves 1–8 for KLoSA (2006–2020). Specifically, HRS wave 1 was excluded due to
   315→a change in measurement operationalization; the CES-D scale shifted from a frequency-based measure
   316→to a binary format, rendering responses incomparable to subsequent waves as noted in the data
   317→documentation (Bugliari et al., 2024). SHARE wave 3 (SHARELIFE) was not included, as it focused
   318→on retrospective life histories and was not part of the harmonized panel data (Wilkens et al., 2024).
   319→Our sample was derived from four datasets of retired individuals. To ensure consistency, a
   320→uniform filtering process was applied. First, we included only participants whose age at both interview
   321→and retirement conformed to the range defined by the respective dataset. To ensure our focus remained
   322→on the transition to retirement, we then excluded individuals who reported multiple retirement dates or
   323→had no observations prior to retiring. We further removed cases with observations more than 60 months
   324→before or after retirement, those who did not report their retirement age, and individuals with missing
   325→13
   326→
   327→data on depressive symptoms. For a detailed flowchart, see Supplementary Figure S1. The final analytic
   328→sample consisted of 9,216 participants and 38,390 observations from HRS, 14,861 participants and
   329→37,300 observations from SHARE, 498 participants and 1,601 observations from CHARLS, and 1,473
   330→participants and 6,241 observations from KLoSA.
   331→Measures
   332→Retirement. The retirement variable was derived from four harmonized datasets. In the HRS,
   333→participants were asked, “At this time, do you consider yourself to be completely retired, partly retired,
   334→or not retired at all?” Only those who reported being completely retired were classified as retired. In
   335→SHARE, retirement status was based on self-reported current job status as “retired”. In CHARLS,
   336→participants were considered retired if they reported being retired in their labor force status. In KLoSA,
   337→only those who indicated being completely retired were classified as retired. We constructed a binary
   338→retirement variable, coded as 1 for retired and 0 for not retired.
   339→Retirement transition. We operationalize the retirement transition using three components: pre-
   340→retirement phase, post-retirement phase, and retirement age. To compute time relative to retirement, we
   341→use the interview date as an exogenous variable, assumed to be independent of individual, country, and
   342→period effects. Subtracting the respondent’s retirement date from the interview date yields months
   343→to/since retirement; values are negative pre-retirement and positive post-retirement.
   344→Depressive symptoms. Depressive symptoms were assessed using different instruments across
   345→datasets. HRS, CHARLS, and KLoSA used versions of the Center for Epidemiologic Studies
   346→Depression Scale (CES-D) (Radloff, 1977): an 8-item scale (0–8) in HRS, and 10-item scales (0–30) in
   347→both CHARLS and KLoSA. In SHARE, depressive symptoms were measured using the EURO-D scale
   348→(Prince et al., 1999), with a range from 0 to 12. Detailed item descriptions are provided in Table S1. To
   349→14
   350→
   351→facilitate comparisons across these varying instruments, scores at each wave were converted to the
   352→Percent of Maximum Possible (POMP; Cohen et al., 1999), calculated as the raw score divided by the
   353→scale’s maximum possible score multiplied by 100.
   354→Retirement age. Numerous factors, such as age, sex, education, financial status, retirement
   355→planning, and pre-retirement job experience, may confound the relation between retirement and
   356→depression (La Rue et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; M. Wang & Shi, 2014). Particularly in many studies
   357→examining the impact of work or retirement transitions on changes in life satisfaction (Hansson et al.,
   358→2020; Henning et al., 2022; Reitz et al., 2022), age is often controlled as a time-varying variable. From
   359→the perspective of the DAG (see Figure 1), the relation between the retirement transition and depressive
   360→symptoms is confounded. The DAG shows that the effects of all macro- and micro-level confounders
   361→on the retirement transition are fully mediated by an individual’s age at retirement. Moreover, our study
   362→imposes no restrictions on this variable, individuals may retire earlier or later than the statutory
   363→retirement age. By retaining this variation, we acknowledge and preserve the individual selection effects
   364→inherent in the timing of retirement. Therefore, conditioning on age at retirement is sufficient to block
   365→prominent backdoor paths, as it serves as a good control (Cinelli et al., 2024) to substantially reduce
   366→confounding without introducing bias. This ensures a more accurate estimation of the association
   367→between retirement transition and depressive symptoms.
   368→Micro-level factors. Sex was coded as 0 for male and 1 for female. Education was harmonized
   369→into three categories: less than upper secondary, upper secondary and vocational training, and tertiary
   370→education.

   372→relation between retirement transitions and depression, with distinct patterns and varying levels of

   441→The research plan is outlined in Table 1. We derived analytic samples from four datasets using
   442→a standardized data-cleaning workflow. We then estimated trajectories of depressive symptoms across
   443→the retirement transition within each dataset. To illustrate the analytic procedure, we presented a pilot

   447→We used multilevel modeling to fit eight trajectory models to examine the changes in depression
   448→trajectories before and after retirement (see the right panel of Figure 1). Model 1a includes a single fixed
   449→intercept along with random effects for individuals. Building on Model 1a, Model 2a adds a time
   450→variable, months to retirement, to estimate the linear change before and after retirement, along with an
   451→additional random effect. Model 1b includes a variable indicating whether an individual is retired,
   452→representing a change in intercept before and after retirement. Model 2b builds on Model 1b by adding
   453→months to retirement, assuming the same linear trend before and after retirement. Model 1c assumes a
   454→shared intercept before and after retirement, with no change before retirement and a linear change
   455→afterward. Model 2c allows for different linear growth slopes before and after retirement, while
   456→maintaining the same intercept. Model 1d assumes no change before retirement, with a change after
   457→retirement, and different intercepts for the pre- and post-retirement time; Model 2d assumes different
   458→intercepts and slopes for both pre- and post-retirement time. Except for Model 1a, all the other models
   459→include both random intercepts and random slopes. All eight models are controlled for retirement age.
   460→We used the likelihood ratio test (LRT) to compare two nested models and determine whether
   461→the more complex model provides a statistically significant improvement over the simpler one. The
   462→likelihood ratio statistic quantifies this improvement, with higher values indicating a greater
   463→enhancement by the more complex model. Additionally, we assessed model fit using the Akaike
   464→Information Criterion (AIC), Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and log-likelihood (logLik). Lower
   465→values of these indices suggest better model fit. In cases where the results from AIC and BIC conflict,
   466→we prioritized the model with the smallest BIC as the final choice, given our objective to identify a
   467→parsimonious and correct model that best fits the data (Burnham & Anderson, 2004). Based on the
   468→comparison of the eight candidate models, we first selected the best-fitting model that characterizes the
   469→19
   470→
   471→average trajectory of depressive symptoms.
   472→Upon identifying this optimal structure, we extended our analysis to examine individual
   473→differences in these trajectories. Beyond estimating mean-level changes (fixed effects), we focused on
   474→the variability in individual responses (random effects). We conducted model comparison to determine
   475→whether the variance components for the level shift and slope changes are significantly different from
   476→zero. Specifically, we compared the fit of our final unconstrained model (allowing for random effects)
   477→against constrained nested models where specific random slope variances are fixed to zero. A significant
   478→improvement in model fit would indicate significant heterogeneity in how individuals react to the
   479→retirement transition (Bleidorn & Schwaba, 2018).
   480→For the best-fitting model, we used the retirement period as a moderator to examine whether it
   481→accounts for individual differences in depression during the transition to retirement. Subsequently, we
   482→conducted subgroup regressions for each retirement period to assess whether depression trajectories
   483→vary across these periods.

   485→depressive symptoms across the retirement transition. We then fitted country-specific multilevel models.
   486→In countries where sample sizes do not support random-slope specifications, we estimated random-

   490→Micro-level moderators
   491→We tested a set of individual-level moderators to account for the individual differences. We
   492→focused on three time-invariant moderators: sex, education, and age at retirement. Guided by our DAG,
   493→20
   494→
   495→we excluded post-retirement time-varying factors to avoid conditioning on colliders or mediators.
   496→Chronological age is omitted due to its linear dependency on retirement age and time relative to
   497→retirement. Employment-related variables (e.g., job satisfaction) were excluded to minimize selection
   498→bias arising from substantial missing data.
   499→To operationalize moderation, we augmented the optimal unconditional model by introducing
   500→these predictors and testing their interactions with the retirement transition components. We examined
   501→two-way interactions between each moderator and each piecewise time term to determine whether the
   502→shape of the depression trajectory varies as a function of sex, education, or age at retirement. Model
   503→improvement at each step was evaluated using likelihood ratio tests, with models fit using maximum
   504→likelihood.
   505→Robustness checks
   506→Developmental psychology research suggests that personality changes surrounding life
   507→transitions often exhibit non-linear trajectories (Bleidorn et al., 2020; Levelt et al., 2025; Luhmann et
   508→al., 2014). To address the potential limitations of assuming strict linearity in our primary analysis, we
   509→conducted additional sensitivity analyses to examine whether depressive symptom trajectories
   510→surrounding the retirement transition exhibit non-linear features. Drawing on the discontinuous change
   511→framework proposed by Doss et al. (2009) and situated within the broader piecewise regression
   512→classifications discussed by Levelt et al. (2025), we fitted four alternative non-linear models
   513→incorporating quadratic terms to test specific theoretical shapes. The first specification (continuous
   514→quadratic) assumes a smooth, progressive continuous curve with no sudden shift at the moment of
   515→retirement; the second specification (quadratic with level shift) allows for an instantaneous intercept
   516→change at retirement while the trajectory shape remains constant; the third specification (quadratic with
   517→21
   518→
   519→slope change) depicts a continuous trajectory where the curvature or slope changes at retirement without
   520→a level shift; and the fourth specification (quadratic with level shift and slope change) serves as a flexible
   521→specification allowing for both curvilinear changes and a discontinuous level shift at retirement. We
   522→used the BIC to compare these alternative specifications against our primary piecewise linear model.
   523→Where the non-linear models demonstrated substantially superior fit and yielded distinct theoretical
   524→insights, we reported these findings alongside the piecewise results. However, we must acknowledge
   525→that identifying within-person curvature requires at least three observations. Furthermore, as noted by
   526→Levelt et al. (2025), achieving stable estimates for more complex non-linear specifications often
   527→necessitates substantially more data points (e.g., ≥5). Given our data structure, the proportion of
   528→participants with ≥5 observations is limited. This scarcity implies that non-linear estimates rely heavily
   529→on between-person variation or on individuals with sustained participation, potentially exacerbating
   530→selection bias. Accordingly, we interpreted any apparent evidence of non-linearity in light of these
   531→identification constraints and treated improvements in fit as sensitivity evidence rather than definitive
   532→proof of within-person curvilinear change.
   533→To address potential inconsistencies arising from the use of different depression scales across
   534→datasets, we conducted a supplementary robustness check. As outlined in Table S1, we identified three
   535→conceptually similar items across the CES-D and EURO-D scales. We constructed a harmonized
   536→depressive symptom index using only these overlapping items for all participants and re-estimate our
   537→primary piecewise multilevel models using this restricted metric. This approach is intended to enhance
   538→cross-study comparability to some extent, but it necessarily narrows content coverage and does not
   539→establish full measurement equivalence across instruments. It should therefore be interpreted as a
   540→supplementary robustness check rather than a replacement for the original depression measures.

   601→Power analysis
   602→We conducted a simulation-based power analysis using the simr package (Green & MacLeod,
   603→2016) to determine the sample size required to detect the effects of interest in our specified multilevel
   604→models. The effect sizes used in the simulations were based on the estimates from our pilot study of the
   605→HRS data. We calculated power for each of the eight models across a range of sample sizes, from 400
   606→to 2,000 individuals, in increments of 100. For each sample size, we ran 1,000 simulations with a
   607→significance level of 0.05. For seven of the eight models, power was assessed for the overall effect of
   608→the retirement transition predictors using a likelihood ratio test, where the full model was compared to
   609→a null model excluding the key fixed effects related to retirement. For the simplest model (Model 1a),
   610→power was calculated for the single fixed effect of age at retirement. Power increased monotonically
   611→with the number of participants and reached 0.80 at approximately 600 participants for Models 2a, 1b,
   612→2b, 2c, and 1d; 700 participants for Model 2d; 800 participants for Model 1c; and 900 participants for
   613→25
   614→
   615→Model 1a. With 1,200 or more participants, all models exceeded 0.95 power (see Figure S3). These
   616→estimates are conditional on the pilot (HRS) effect sizes used in the simulations.
   617→The analytic samples for both SHARE (14,861 participants) and KLoSA (1,473 participants)
   618→substantially exceed the thresholds required to achieve 80% power as determined by our simulations.
   619→Consequently, we expect sufficient statistical power in these datasets to detect effect sizes comparable
   620→to or smaller than those estimated from the HRS data. In contrast, the sample size for CHARLS, at 498
   621→participants, is near the 75% power threshold for several key models. Given this limitation, we
   622→acknowledge that the CHARLS analysis may be underpowered and interpret the findings from this
   623→specific sample with appropriate caution.

   738→retirement-related increase in depressive symptoms was smaller among women than among men.
   739→We next assessed whether retirement-related changes in depressive symptoms varied across
   740→historical periods, contextualized by the policy reforms and major events shown in Figure S6. Relative
   741→to 2013–2016, the immediate level shift around retirement was more pronounced in 2008–2009 and
   742→2017–2020. The post-retirement slope was also significantly more negative in 2017–2020. No
   743→significant period differences were observed in pre-retirement slopes (Table S32). To characterize
   744→period-specific patterns, we fitted separate models within each retirement period (Fig. 2; Table S34).
   745→Retirement was associated with a sharp immediate increase in depressive symptoms in 2006–2007, as
   746→indicated by the random-intercept Model 1b (β = 6.739, 95% CI [3.741, 9.737], p < .001). A similar
   747→immediate increase was observed in 2008–2009, followed by a significant post-retirement decline. In
   748→2010–2012, the selected Model 2c suggested a pattern in which depressive symptoms increased before
   749→retirement and declined after retirement, without evidence of an abrupt level shift at the transition point.
   750→By contrast, the 2013–2016 model showed no significant retirement-related level shift or post-
   751→retirement change. In 2017–2020, retirement was again associated with a significant immediate increase
   752→in depressive symptoms (β = 4.529, 95% CI [2.226, 6.831], p < .001), followed by the largest estimated
   753→post-retirement decline across periods. Table 2 presents the best-fitting model results for the four

   756→We tested whether the findings were robust to differences in depression measures across

   766→Discussion
   767→Retirement is not a single psychological event but a temporally structured transition whose
   768→consequences depend on when change begins, whether the moment of exit produces an abrupt
   769→discontinuity, and how symptoms unfold afterward. Across four harmonized aging studies, we found
   770→four broad patterns. In HRS depressive symptoms rose before retirement, increased at the point of exit,
   771→and declined gradually afterward. In SHARE, retirement was associated mainly with a small immediate
   772→decrease in depressive symptoms, with limited evidence of sustained pre- or post-retirement change in
   773→the pooled model. In CHARLS, depressive symptoms increased steadily across the retirement window,
   774→without an overall discontinuity at retirement. In KLoSA, retirement was marked by a pronounced
   775→immediate increase followed by post-retirement decline. Despite this variability, the estimated effects
   776→were generally modest. On the shared POMP scale, the level shifts and slopes were small, and many
   777→country-specific and period-specific trajectories were close to flat. The most robust feature of the results
   778→is thus not a particular shape of change but the limited and inconsistent nature of the group-level
   779→association between retirement and depressive symptoms. This pattern helps reconcile conflicting
   780→conclusions in prior research by showing that detectable change can appear as anticipatory strain, acute
   781→32
   782→
   783→disruption, short-term relief, gradual deterioration, or recovery, depending on temporal phase and social
   784→context. Still, the small magnitudes and uneven directions mean that retirement was often not associated
   785→with large average changes in depressive symptoms at the group level.
   786→Reframing retirement as a dynamic transition
   787→This study’s central contribution is to move beyond asking whether retirement is, on average,
   788→beneficial or detrimental to mental health and instead examine when and how psychological change
   789→unfolds across the retirement transition. This temporal reframing is reflected in our preregistered focus
   790→on two forms of change: an immediate level shift at the point of labor-force exit (H1a) and differences
   791→between pre- and post-retirement rates of change (H1b). It also extends psychological theories of
   792→retirement that conceptualize retirement as more than a discrete endpoint, instead treating it as a
   793→dynamic adjustment process shaped by changing roles, resources, identities, and social contexts over
   794→time (J. E. Kim & Moen, 2002; M. Wang et al., 2011; M. Wang & Shi, 2014). From this perspective,
   795→apparently inconsistent findings in prior research may partly reflect differences in which phase of the
   796→transition is being captured: anticipatory change before exit, an abrupt discontinuity at the retirement
   797→point, or longer-term socialization. This logic is consistent with broader life-event research showing
   798→that major transitions can involve pre-event differences or anticipatory change, nonlinear or
   799→discontinuous shifts, and partial or complete adaptation rather than a single uniform trajectory (Bleidorn
   800→et al., 2018; Luhmann et al., 2014).
   801→A complementary explanation concerns the modest size of retirement-related change. Meta-
   802→analytic evidence indicates that life events are associated with reliable but relatively small changes in
   803→personality-related outcomes (Bühler et al., 2024). In the present study, the modest size of the estimated
   804→changes may help explain why patterns varied across datasets. When average retirement-related change
   805→33
   806→
   807→is small, differences in sample composition, measurement, institutional setting, and historical timing
   808→can more readily shape whether a trajectory appears stable, improving, or worsening.
   809→Pre-retirement anticipation
   810→The pre-retirement phase shows that psychological change may begin before formal labor-force
   811→exit. Major life transitions are embedded in a longer temporal process in which individuals may begin
   812→to anticipate or adjust before the transition formally occurs (Luhmann et al., 2012b, 2014). Recent
   813→evidence further suggests that anticipatory changes are especially pronounced for predictable major life
   814→events, with retirement showing one of the largest average anticipatory shifts (Dugan et al., 2024).
   815→Retirement may be particularly likely to elicit such anticipatory change because it entails a foreseeable
   816→reorganization of the resources, roles, and routines that structure everyday life. This interpretation is
   817→consistent with the resource-based perspective on retirement adjustment, which emphasizes that
   818→psychological adaptation depends on changes in financial, social, and psychological resources over time
   819→(M. Wang et al., 2011).
   820→In our study, evidence consistent with anticipation effects was present but limited and uneven
   821→across datasets. Depressive symptoms increased during the 60 months before retirement in the HRS
   822→sample, particularly in the pre-2000, 2004–2007, and 2011–2015 periods. Similar pre-retirement
   823→increases appeared in SHARE during 2010–2013 and in KLoSA during 2010–2012, whereas SHARE
   824→during 2014–2016 showed a pre-retirement decline. No comparable period-specific pre-retirement
   825→change was observed in CHARLS. These mixed patterns do not support a general anticipatory effect of
   826→retirement on depressive symptoms. They instead point to small and context-dependent departures from
   827→an otherwise modest group-level pattern. In some settings, the years before retirement may be marked
   828→by strain, uncertainty, or declining resources; in others, retirement may be planned, expected, or
   829→34
   830→
   831→experienced as relief from work-related demands.
   832→Level shifts
   833→Changes surrounding retirement transitions may be discontinuous, such that depressive
   834→symptoms shift abruptly at the retirement point (Lawes et al., 2025; Levelt et al., 2025). In our study,
   835→the retirement-point level change captures this immediate discontinuity, as estimated from panel
   836→observations aligned to each participant’s reported retirement date. Conceptually, this component
   837→indexes short term change around labor force exit, which may reflect an acute response to retirement in
   838→some settings. A positive level shift may occur when retirement removes a valued work role before
   839→individuals have established alternative identities, or sources of social connection, requiring a rapid
   840→reconstruction of role identity and daily continuity (Atchley, 1989; Bordia et al., 2020; Mutran &
   841→Reitzes, 1981). A negative level shift, by contrast, may occur when retirement immediately relieves
   842→work demands, role strain, or accumulated occupational stress (Eibich, 2015; Wetzel et al., 2016).
   843→Our findings provided partial support for H1a. In the HRS sample, depressive symptoms
   844→increased abruptly at retirement, with similar positive shifts in the pre-2000 and 2001–2003 periods.
   845→KLoSA showed an even clearer pattern of immediate deterioration, both overall and in the 2006–2007,
   846→2008–2009, and 2017–2020 periods. These patterns suggest that, in the United States and South Korea,
   847→retirement often carried a short-term psychological cost. In contrast, SHARE showed a small immediate
   848→decrease overall, and the 2010–2013 period also showed a downward level shift, suggesting that
   849→retirement may have functioned as a short-term release in some European contexts. CHARLS showed
   850→no overall discontinuity, although an immediate increase appeared in 2014–2015. These level shifts
   851→were nonetheless small on the POMP scale. They therefore point to limited retirement-point change at
   852→the group level, even though some dataset-specific patterns remain compatible with short-term
   853→35
   854→
   855→disruption or relief.
   856→Post-retirement socialization
   857→The post-retirement phase captures the linear change in depressive symptoms within the five-
   858→year window after retirement. In life-event research, the post-event slope is conceptualized as a
   859→socialization effect, reflecting gradual psychological adjustment in the years following the transition
   860→(Asselmann & Specht, 2020; Denissen et al., 2019). For retirement, this component indicates whether
   861→individuals recover, continue to deteriorate, or remain stable after leaving work.
   862→Our H1b therefore expected depressive-symptom trajectories to differ before and after
   863→retirement, reflecting distinct anticipation and socialization processes. This hypothesis was supported
   864→most clearly in HRS, where depressive symptoms increased around the transition but declined during
   865→the post-retirement phase, suggesting partial recovery after an initially disruptive exit from work. The
   866→pattern was not consistent across datasets. In KLoSA, depressive symptoms declined after retirement,
   867→suggesting recovery from the initial deterioration, but without comparable anticipatory change. In
   868→CHARLS, the best-fitting model indicated a steady linear increase across the retirement window,
   869→making it difficult to distinguish anticipation from post-retirement adaptation. In SHARE, the pooled
   870→model showed a small immediate decrease at retirement but no sustained linear change before or after,
   871→suggesting that retirement-related change was concentrated at the transition point. These divergent
   872→patterns are consistent with post-retirement socialization being contextually patterned rather than
   873→uniform. This conclusion should remain cautious because the post-retirement slopes represented only
   874→limited change over the five-year window. Contextual variation may help explain some departures from
   875→stability, while limited overall change remains central to describing the post-retirement phase.
   876→Institutional meaning of retirement
   877→36
   878→
   879→Retirement is an institutionally organized transition. Pension rules, labor-market arrangements,
   880→health-care access, family-support systems, and social norms shape not only when people retire but also
   881→what retirement means psychologically (Beier & Fisher, 2025; Henkens & van Solinge, 2021). Our
   882→findings offer mixed support for H2. Depression trajectories differed across datasets, yet the estimated
   883→changes were generally modest. These differences are consistent with the possibility that retirement
   884→institutions, labor-market conditions, and social protections shape retirement experiences, but the
   885→present design does not identify institutional mechanisms directly. Because depression scales differed
   886→across datasets, cross-national contrasts are best interpreted as contextualized trajectory patterns, not as
   887→strict comparisons of effect sizes or mean levels.
   888→In the United States, retirement income combines Social Security with employer-sponsored and
   889→private retirement arrangements that have shifted substantially from defined-benefit to defined-
   890→contribution plans (Davies et al., 2022; Dushi et al., 2011). This arrangement places considerable
   891→responsibility for retirement security on individual savings, benefit-claiming decisions, and market-
   892→dependent assets. The HRS pattern can therefore be interpreted as a possible short-term disruption in a
   893→relatively individualized retirement system, followed by partial adaptation as retirees reorganize income,
   894→health coverage, and daily routines.
   895→In Europe, many welfare states provide stronger public pension protection and more universal
   896→health-care coverage than the more individualized U.S. retirement system, which may reduce the
   897→immediate financial threat of leaving work. Yet SHARE also revealed substantial heterogeneity across
   898→countries. The pooled SHARE model showed a small immediate decrease in depressive symptoms,
   899→whereas country-specific models often indicated modest improvements or no detectable retirement-
   900→related change. Notably, the most common country-level result was the absence of detectable change:
   901→37
   902→
   903→even in settings where welfare protections might be expected to shape the retirement transition, most
   904→European samples showed little measurable change in depressive symptoms. This heterogeneity is
   905→consistent with broader cross-national differences in European welfare regimes, including differences
   906→in pension adequacy, redistributive emphasis, health and social protection, and labor-market institutions
   907→(Bambra et al., 2009; Eikemo et al., 2008). The pattern suggests that retirement in Europe may
   908→sometimes function as relief from occupational strain. However, its psychological meaning remains
   909→contingent on national pension generosity, labor-market protections, and the extent of recent pension
   910→reforms, especially in countries affected by austerity and pension retrenchment after the financial and
   911→sovereign debt crises (Hinrichs, 2021b; Karanikolos et al., 2013).
   912→China represents a different institutional configuration. Its pension system has expanded rapidly,
   913→yet benefit adequacy and pension resources remain uneven across employment sectors and the urban–
   914→rural divide (Yang, 2021; X. Zhang & Chand, 2025). In CHARLS, depressive symptoms increased
   915→steadily across the retirement window. This pattern suggests that retirement in China may be less a
   916→single psychological shock than part of a broader process of accumulating late-life vulnerability,
   917→especially where pension benefits are limited and intergenerational support is under pressure. South
   918→Korea, by contrast, showed an immediate but modest increase at retirement followed by recovery. This
   919→pattern is compatible with short-term disruption in a context characterized by rapid population aging
   920→and old-age income insecurity (OECD, 2022b), while later adaptation may occur as individuals
   921→reorganize daily life and mobilize available public support. Overall, these findings provide mixed
   922→support for H2. The psychological meaning of retirement varied across institutional settings, with the
   923→post-retirement trajectory more favorable in advanced welfare states and depressive symptoms
   924→continuing to rise in developing contexts. However, in both HRS and KLoSA retirement was marked
   925→38
   926→
   927→by a significant immediate increase at the transition point; our classification of these advanced-context
   928→cases as partial support for H2b rests on the subsequent post-retirement decline, not on the immediate
   929→shift, which ran opposite to prediction.
   930→Historical contingency in retirement transitions
   931→Beyond cross-national heterogeneity, we examined whether the historical retirement period
   932→moderated depressive-symptom trajectories. At the confirmatory level, H3 was tested through the
   933→registered overall interaction between retirement period and the trajectory components. Significant
   934→interactions between retirement period and the trajectory components were present in every dataset
   935→(Tables S7, S14, S25, S32), and on this basis we regard H3 as supported.
   936→The period-specific subgroup models serve a different purpose. They are exploratory
   937→descriptions of where these interactions may have arisen. Because they partition each sample into much
   938→smaller subsamples, some of which fall below the power thresholds above, they may be underpowered
   939→to distinguish among the candidate trajectory models. We therefore do not treat the BIC-selected model
   940→within any single period as evidence of a substantive period pattern by itself, because a best-fitting
   941→model will be selected even when true retirement-related change is absent. We interpret only statistically
   942→significant trajectory parameters, and the macro-historical accounts below are offered as contextual
   943→interpretations of those significant patterns rather than as confirmatory evidence.
   944→From a life-course perspective, the consequences of any major transition depend not only on
   945→individual characteristics and stable institutional arrangements, but also on the sociocultural and
   946→macroeconomic conditions that prevail at the time of the transition (Elder, 1994; Moen, 1996).
   947→Recessions, pension reforms, pandemic shocks, and welfare expansions each modify the resources
   948→available to retirees and the meaning of work-exit itself. In our study, period differences were detectable,
   949→39
   950→
   951→but they occurred against a background of generally modest retirement-related change. Historical
   952→context may therefore help explain why some trajectories departed from stability.
   953→In the United States, the pre-2000 period was characterized by rising depressive symptoms
   954→before retirement with an immediate increase at the transition point, a pattern consistent with
   955→anticipatory strain followed by an acute retirement shock. In 2001–2003, retirement was associated
   956→mainly with an immediate increase. By contrast, in 2004–2007 and 2011–2015, the data showed pre-
   957→retirement increases followed by post-retirement declines, which may indicate strain before exit and
   958→gradual adaptation afterward. After 2016, depressive symptoms increased smoothly across the
   959→retirement window. In SHARE, early periods showed little detectable retirement-related change,
   960→whereas 2010–2013 produced the clearest historically contingent pattern: depressive symptoms
   961→increased before retirement but declined immediately at the transition point. This pattern may reflect
   962→the broader post-crisis austerity context in Europe, in which fiscal consolidation, labor-market
   963→uncertainty, and pressure on health and social protection systems may have shaped the resources
   964→available to individuals approaching retirement (Hinrichs, 2021b; Karanikolos et al., 2013). Later
   965→SHARE periods were characterized mainly by weak or significant linear declines rather than abrupt
   966→retirement shocks, including a smooth decline in 2020–2022.
   967→In China, the period-specific patterns were marked by increasing post-retirement vulnerability.
   968→The 2011–2013 period showed no detectable retirement-related change, whereas 2014–2015 showed an
   969→immediate increase at retirement. In 2016–2017 and 2018–2020, depressive symptoms increased
   970→significantly after retirement, with a steeper post-retirement slope in the later period. This pattern
   971→suggests that retirement-related strain became more prolonged over time, possibly reflecting persistent
   972→inequalities in pension adequacy amid rapid demographic and institutional change (Yang, 2021). In
   973→40
   974→
   975→South Korea, retirement most often appeared as an acute transition rather than a gradual process.
   976→Significant immediate increases in depressive symptoms were evident in 2006–2007, 2008–2009, and
   977→2017–2020, suggesting that labor-force exit often produced a short-term psychological disruption. Yet
   978→this disruption was not necessarily followed by sustained deterioration. Post-retirement declines
   979→appeared in 2008–2009 and were strongest in 2017–2020. This stronger recovery in the most recent
   980→period may be consistent with a changing retirement-income environment, including the expansion of
   981→Individual Retirement Pension coverage, although the present design cannot directly identify policy
   982→mechanisms. The 2010–2012 period showed a different pattern, with depressive symptoms worsening
   983→before retirement but improving afterward, suggesting that retirement may have relieved pre-existing
   984→strain in that period.
   985→Taken together, these period-specific analyses point to a bounded form of historical contingency.
   986→Historical period was associated with trajectory components in the registered tests, yet the specific
   987→patterns offered limited support for a single orderly correspondence between historical conditions and
   988→depressive-symptom trajectories. The clearest pattern appeared in China, where later periods showed
   989→more pronounced post-retirement increases. In the other datasets, period differences were more
   990→dispersed, and many estimated trajectories involved little change. We therefore conclude cautiously that
   991→the historical period in which retirement occurs may shape when small departures from stability appear.
   992→Retirement age: selection, agency, and constraint
   993→The relatively limited and inconsistent moderation by sex, education, and retirement age should
   994→be interpreted in light of our preregistered identification strategy. In our framework, retirement age was
   995→treated as a key pre-transition attribute that anchors the retirement trajectory and captures systematic
   996→variation in when individuals leave work. Its theoretical importance lies in the fact that retirement timing
   997→41
   998→
   999→is shaped by a constellation of individual, work-related, family, and institutional factors (G. G. Fisher
  1000→et al., 2016). From a life-course perspective, individuals exercise agency in planning and negotiating
  1001→later-life work transitions, yet their choices are made within historically and institutionally structured
  1002→opportunity sets (Henkens & van Solinge, 2021).
  1003→Conditioning on retirement age therefore helps reduce confounding from pre-event selection
  1004→into retirement timing and incorporates part of the heterogeneity that might otherwise appear as
  1005→moderation by sex, education, or other individual characteristics. At the same time, retirement age
  1006→captures both agency and constraint. Early retirement may reflect health, work, or caregiving constraints,
  1007→but also financial security or voluntary exit from stressful work (Topa et al., 2018). Later retirement
  1008→may signal good health and work attachment, but also financial necessity or limited alternatives
  1009→(Brydsten & Stattin, 2025). Retirement age is therefore best understood as a life-course indicator of
  1010→accumulated advantage, constraint, and institutional timing. However, controlling for retirement age
  1011→cannot fully account for macro-level conditions that continue to shape resources after retirement. Future
  1012→studies that distinguish voluntary from involuntary retirement, full from partial retirement, and
  1013→financially secure from constrained exits would help identify more directly the agency embedded in
  1014→retirement timing.
  1015→Theoretical and practical implications
  1016→Theoretically, this study operationalizes the distinction between selection into retirement and
  1017→change around retirement. Retirement age anchors the transition and reflects prior conditions that shape
  1018→labor-force exit. Aligning depressive-symptom trajectories to retirement age therefore helps separate
  1019→psychological change surrounding retirement from earlier life-course selection into its timing. This
  1020→distinction reduces the risk of attributing to retirement itself what may instead reflect the accumulated
  1021→42
  1022→
  1023→conditions that lead people to retire earlier or later.
  1024→The same logic extends to other major life transitions. The phase-specific strategy used here,
  1025→by emphasizing event age as an anchor, may help disentangle change from selection in studies of
  1026→parenthood, migration, caregiving, or widowhood, where the timing of the event itself often reflects
  1027→prior advantage, constraint, and exposure. The study also advances life-course research by linking
  1028→individual retirement transitions to national and historical contexts. Retirement-related change is not a
  1029→stable individual phenomenon that can be averaged across societies without qualification; its meaning
  1030→is shaped by the institutional and historical conditions under which retirement takes place.
  1031→The findings also speak to the level of inference that average trajectories can support. A largely
  1032→stable average trajectory does not imply that retirement is psychologically irrelevant for every
  1033→individual, and individual cases of improvement or deterioration do not by themselves imply a strong
  1034→average effect. Interpreting a flat trajectory must therefore guard against a possible ecological fallacy.
  1035→With on average fewer than five observations per respondent, our data provide limited leverage for
  1036→characterizing within-person trajectories, and the stability we observe primarily reflects information at
  1037→the between-person level. A flat average trajectory thus describes the typical between-person pattern
  1038→around retirement; it cannot be taken as evidence against substantial within-person fluctuation (A. J.
  1039→Fisher et al., 2018). Life-course research therefore benefits from treating institutional and historical
  1040→contexts as conditions that may shape when departures from stability emerge, while keeping average
  1041→stability itself as an important empirical outcome.
  1042→Practically, the findings suggest that retirement support should be sensitive to the timing of
  1043→possible psychological risk without assuming that retirement usually produces large changes in
  1044→depression. Preparation matters most when distress begins before retirement, transition support is most
  1045→43
  1046→
  1047→relevant when symptoms rise at the point of exit, and longer-term protections are needed when
  1048→symptoms continue to worsen during the post-retirement period. Pension and labor-market reforms
  1049→should therefore be evaluated not only for fiscal sustainability, but also for the psychological conditions
  1050→they create at retirement and in later retirement years.
  1051→Limitations
  1052→Several limitations qualify our data harmonization strategy. Although we used the POMP
  1053→transformation to place depression total scores on a common 0 to 100 metric and facilitate descriptive
  1054→comparability, this procedure primarily reflects the pre-statistical phase of harmonization involving
  1055→variable recoding and rescaling rather than statistical harmonization based on latent variable models
  1056→(Neidhart et al., 2024). In principle, stronger cross-instrument harmonization would link item responses
  1057→to a shared latent depression continuum such that individuals with the same underlying trait level would
  1058→receive equivalent scores regardless of the instrument administered. Yu et al. (2025) illustrate this
  1059→rigorous approach for the CES-D by detecting cross-national differential item functioning using an item
  1060→response theory framework and deriving adjusted latent factor scores. However, the validity of such
  1061→linking hinges on the availability of an adequate set of shared and approximately invariant anchor items
  1062→to establish a common metric. In our context, these requirements are difficult to satisfy because truly
  1063→identical anchor items across all datasets are virtually nonexistent. The integration of the CES-D and
  1064→EURO-D specifically presents challenges as these instruments provide at best semantic
  1065→correspondences rather than strict item identity (see Table S1). With anchoring that is sparse and only
  1066→partially comparable, latent linking would require strong constraints and likely be highly assumption
  1067→dependent, offering limited assurance beyond the POMP transformation for our purposes.
  1068→Additional caution is warranted because the CES-D and EURO-D are overlapping but not
  1069→44
  1070→
  1071→interchangeable operationalizations of depression as prior comparisons suggest only moderate
  1072→agreement and systematic subgroup variation in discrepancies (Courtin et al., 2015). Relatedly, the
  1073→supplementary analysis based on only three overlapping items should be interpreted as a reference check
  1074→only, because this restricted index has limited content validity (Haynes et al., 1995; Smith et al., 2000)
  1075→and does not resolve cross-instrument measurement invariance concerns. Furthermore, the
  1076→measurement invariance of the EURO-D scale itself is not supported across countries which renders
  1077→strict cross-national comparisons unfeasible (Y. Zhang & Ma, 2025). Accordingly, we interpret cross-
  1078→national differences in mean levels conservatively and place primary emphasis on within-cohort
  1079→depression trajectories across the retirement transition captured by level shifts and slopes where
  1080→inference relies less on strong cross-instrument equivalence assumptions.
  1081→A further limitation is that some country- and period-specific subsamples may have been
  1082→underpowered to distinguish reliably among the candidate trajectory models. When simpler or stable
  1083→models were selected in smaller subsamples, these results should be read as limited evidence of
  1084→detectable change rather than as evidence that no change occurred, because low power reduces the
  1085→probability of detecting genuine effects. Significant estimates from these subsamples also warrant
  1086→caution. Under low-power conditions, even estimates that reach significance may be less stable, with a
  1087→greater risk that the estimated effect size is inflated, that the pattern reflects sampling fluctuation, or that
  1088→a particular trajectory model was selected because it happened to fit a small sample by chance. We
  1089→therefore treat both simpler-model selections and isolated significant findings from smaller subsamples
  1090→as provisional rather than as firm contextual evidence.
  1091→Conclusion
  1092→This study reframes retirement as a dynamic life-course transition while showing that
  1093→45
  1094→
  1095→depressive-symptom change around retirement is often modest at the average level. Across four
  1096→harmonized aging studies, many trajectories were close to stable, and detectable changes varied in
  1097→timing and direction. By distinguishing the pre-retirement phase, the immediate shift at labor-force exit,
  1098→and the post-retirement phase, it shows that retirement-related mental-health change can take multiple
  1099→temporal forms rather than following a single average trajectory. The findings also underscore the
  1100→importance of retirement age as an anchor of the transition, because the timing of labor-force exit
  1101→reflects accumulated individual, occupational, and institutional conditions. At the same time, national
  1102→institutions and historical periods help interpret some departures from stability, while the broader pattern
  1103→remains one of modest and inconsistent change, with many trajectories close to stable across the
  1104→retirement transition. Understanding retirement therefore requires attention not only to whether mental
  1105→health changes, but also to the prevalence of stability, the timing of detectable change, the conditions
  1106→under which individuals enter retirement, and the broader social context in which adjustment takes place.
