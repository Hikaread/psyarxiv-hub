    15→Abstract
    16→Experience Sampling Method (ESM) and Ecological Momentary Interventions (EMI) are
    17→promising tools for person-centered mental health care, yet evidence on prolonged
    18→transdiagnostic monitoring remains limited. This study explored service users’ and clinicians’
    19→perspectives on the usability and clinical utility of a prolonged transdiagnostic ESM protocol
    20→with on-demand EMI content. Within the DISCONNECT project, 84 service users completed
    21→debriefing questionnaires following a 30-day protocol with three daily assessments. Semi-
    22→structured interviews were conducted with 20 service users and 10 outpatient clinicians. Data
    23→were analyzed using content analysis and thematic content analysis. Both stakeholder groups
    24→perceived ESM as acceptable and clinically valuable. Participants reported increased self-
    25→awareness, improved understanding of symptom patterns, and enhanced opportunities for
    26→psychoeducation, treatment planning, and patient–clinician communication. EMI was viewed
    27→positively but used infrequently. Participants emphasized the need for personalized
    28→assessments, flexible schedules, individualized feedback, and context-sensitive interventions.
    29→Key barriers included monitoring burden, clinician time constraints, and organizational
    30→challenges. Prolonged transdiagnostic ESM monitoring appears feasible, acceptable, and
    31→clinically meaningful, although successful implementation will require greater personalization
    32→and integration into routine care.
    33→Keywords: Experience Sampling Method; Ecological Momentary Intervention;
    34→Transdiagnostic Approach; Digital Mental Health; Self-Monitoring; Qualitative Research
    35→
    36→Patient-centered care has been shown to substantially improve the quality and effectiveness of
    37→treatment for mental disorders (Boardman & Dave, 2020). It is commonly defined as care that
    38→is respectful of and responsive to individual patients’ preferences, needs, and values, ensuring
    39→that patient values guide all clinical decisions (Institute of Medicine, 2001). Core elements
    40→such as a strong therapeutic alliance and shared decision-making are consistently associated
    41→with better treatment engagement, adherence, and clinical outcomes across psychiatric
    42→populations (Khosravi et al., 2024; Marchand et al., 2019).
    43→Contemporary classification systems such as DSM and ICD prioritize between-person
    44→differences and symptom thresholds, often at the expense of within-person variability relevant
    45→for individualized care (Wright & Woods, 2020). This approach can be in many cases an
    46→obstacle for implementing a person-centered approach to treatment. Dimensional frameworks,
    47→such as the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) address some of these
    48→limitations by emphasizing continuous symptom dimensions rather than discrete diagnostic
    49→categories, thereby allowing for a more nuanced understanding of psychopathology beyond
    50→the scope of diagnostic criteria (Kotov et al., 2017). Yet they provide limited guidance for
    51→understanding short-term within-person dynamics in daily life.
    52→Digital technologies have emerged as a promising tool for strengthening personalized care in
    53→mental health. Mobile health (mHealth) tools have been explored across a range of disorders
    54→and psychological difficulties (Chan et al., 2017; Trull & Ebner-Priemer, 2009), enhancing
    55→monitoring, treatment planning, and continuity of care by capturing real-world data.
    56→Nevertheless, integration into routine practice remains limited, highlighting the importance of
    57→understanding practical, organizational, legal, ethical, and experiential factors influencing
    58→adoption (Myin-Germeys et al., 2024, 2025).
    59→
    60→A core methodological foundation for many mHealth applications is the Experience Sampling
    61→Method (ESM), which involves repeated real-time assessments of experiences, behaviors, and
    62→symptoms in natural contexts (Larson & Csikszentmihalyi, 2014). In clinical practice, brief
    63→mobile assessments capture symptom dynamics in everyday contexts while reducing recall
    64→bias, offering opportunities for monitoring change, identifying early warning signs, and
    65→supporting individualized case conceptualization (Shiffman, 2009; Kramer et al., 2014; van
    66→Os et al., 2017a; Wright & Zimmermann, 2019).
    67→Ecological Momentary Intervention (EMI) is built on ESM by delivering therapeutic content
    68→in daily life, such as reflective prompts, motivational messages, relaxation exercises, or alerts
    69→during heightened distress (Heron & Smyth, 2010). EMI outcomes can be comparable to
    70→time-intensive face-to-face interventions, especially when integrated into broader treatment
    71→frameworks (Bell et al., 2017; Dao et al., 2021), and tailored interventions using ESM data
    72→align closely with personalization and shared decision-making principles (Myin-Germeys et
    73→al., 2016).
    74→Research on ESM and EMI shows nuanced findings. Patients often report benefits including
    75→increased self-awareness, emotional differentiation, insight into symptom patterns, and
    76→improved communication of sensitive experiences (Bos et al., 2019, 2020; Folkersma et al.,
    77→2021; Palmier-Claus et al., 2013). Visualized ESM feedback is valued by both patients and
    78→clinicians for identifying patterns and supporting collaborative interpretation (Bos et al., 2019;
    79→De Thurah et al., 2025). Yet challenges such as high assessment frequency, limited
    80→personalization, poorly timed prompts, emotional burden, and digital access issues can reduce
    81→engagement, while clinicians may be ambivalent about integrating ESM data into care (Bos et
    82→al., 2020; Meglio et al., 2024; Weermeijer et al., 2023).
    83→
    84→Overall, findings point to substantial promise alongside variability in how ESM and EMI are
    85→experienced in clinical care. Literature remains fragmented, often focusing on specific
    86→diagnoses, short-term use, or single stakeholder perspectives, with a relative lack of
    87→qualitative research exploring prolonged ESM monitoring, EMI use, and individualized
    88→feedback incorporation, particularly regarding usability, feasibility, and clinical
    89→appropriateness (Jones et al., 2019; Vachon et al., 2019).
    90→The aim of this study is therefore to examine the views of patients with various psychiatric
    91→diagnoses and clinical staff from various outpatient facilities on the usability and
    92→appropriateness of using ESM and EMI through a transdiagnostic broad protocol over a one-
    93→month monitoring period, as well as individualized feedback from ESM in the field of mental
    94→health care. We aim to answer the following questions: a) What do patients and clinicians find
    95→about ESM and EMI potentially useful in clinical practice? b) What suggestions do patients
    96→and clinicians have for increasing the feasibility and usability of ESM and EMI tools in
    97→clinical practice?
    98→Transparency and reporting
    99→This qualitative study was pre-registered at the Open Science Framework (OSF) on
   100→November 4th, 2024 (Kurilla et al., 2024). In a deviation to the registered analysis plan, we
   101→supplemented the originally planned thematic content analysis of semi-structured interviews
   102→with a content analysis of the debriefings of all participating patients to capture the
   103→experiences of a larger and more diverse sample of patients.
   104→Materials and methods
   105→
   106→Study design and research paradigm
   107→This study employed a qualitative design guided by an interpretivist–pragmatic paradigm
   108→(Morgan, 2007). Data collection combined a brief written debriefing survey on participants’
   109→experiences and recommendations regarding ESM and EMI with semi-structured interviews
   110→exploring usability and clinical appropriateness. This approach allowed for an in-depth
   111→interpretation of participants’ perspectives while maintaining a focus on practical research
   112→outcomes.
   113→Setting and context
   114→This study was conducted within the DISCONNECT project, a multidisciplinary research
   115→initiative examining psychopathological and contextual factors of social isolation and
   116→loneliness in individuals with mental illness and at-risk populations (Hajdúk et al., 2025). The
   117→project includes a work package focused on the use, feasibility, and acceptability of ESM and
   118→EMI targeting distress and loneliness in daily life (Dančík et al., 2025).
   119→Data collection took place over a 30-day period using the m-Path (KU Leuven; Mestdagh et
   120→al., 2023) mobile application and completed ESM assessments three times daily. These
   121→assessments captured experiences related to sleep, daily functioning, loneliness, and HiTOP-
   122→defined psychopathology domains (somatoform, internalizing, detachment, thought disorders,
   123→and externalizing symptoms; Kotov et al., 2017). Participants also had access to a simple, on-
   124→demand EMI offering brief reflective or regulatory content (e.g., affirmations, relaxation
   125→exercises, or music). Additionally, participants could initiate situational assessments to report
   126→momentary experiences and contextual factors. The complete ESM protocol used in this study
   127→can be found as part of the project’s pre-registration on the OSF (Dančík et al., 2024).
   128→
   129→Drawing on this broader project, the present qualitative study explores how a heterogeneous
   130→group of service users and clinicians experienced prolonged ESM monitoring.
   131→Participants and sampling
   132→This study focused on individuals receiving psychiatric or psychotherapeutic treatment.
   133→Inclusion criteria for service users were: (a) age ≥ 18, (b) a clinical diagnosis within the
   134→schizophrenia spectrum, affective, anxiety, or substance use disorders, and (c) capacity to
   135→provide informed consent. Exclusion criteria included symptoms from organic causes (ICD-
   136→10 F00–F09), intellectual disability (F71–F79), severe psychological development disorders
   137→(F80–F89), medical/psychological contraindications, inability to use a smartphone, or
   138→insufficient fluency in Slovak. Debriefing data were collected from 84 participants. A
   139→subsample of 20 participants, meeting a 70% or higher ESM compliance threshold, was
   140→selected for semi-structured interviews in line with the average response rate observed in
   141→studies involving clinical samples (Jones et al., 2019; Vachon et al., 2019). Stratified random
   142→sampling was used within the group of 84 participants to select compliant individuals while
   143→ensuring balanced representation across the four diagnostic groups. Three additional
   144→participants were selected in the same manner to replace two patients who could not be
   145→reached and one who declined the interview. Additionally, 10 clinicians (psychiatrists, clinical
   146→psychologists, and psychotherapists) were recruited from collaborating outpatient services,
   147→balancing representation across gender, professional background, and clinical specialization.
   148→Inclusion criteria for clinicians were: (a) age ≥ 30, (b) ≥ 5 years of active clinical experience,
   149→and (c) working in an outpatient setting; those not meeting these criteria were excluded.
   150→Recruitment procedure
   151→Service users were recruited via purposive sampling through local clinicians in psychiatric
   152→outpatient practices from January 2025 to March 2026. Collaborating clinicians were
   153→
   154→contacted by email, and after providing informed consent, they chose either an online or in-
   155→person interview format. Recruitment of clinicians took place from February to May 2026.
   156→Data collection
   157→Shortly after the 30-day monitoring period, participants completed a brief written debriefing
   158→survey. Data collection took place from February 2025 to May 2026. Semi-structured
   159→interviews were conducted by three researchers (AK, DD, AH). All interviewers had previous
   160→training in psychology, received standardized training, and followed a common interview
   161→protocol to ensure methodological consistency. The interviewers were supervised by AK, who
   162→also monitored adherence to the study guide and provided regular feedback during data
   163→collection.
   164→The debriefing survey and all service user interviews were conducted individually and in
   165→person on the university campus. Clinicians were interviewed either on campus or via video
   166→call (MS Teams), based on preference. With participants' written and verbal consent,
   167→interviews were recorded using an encrypted audio recorder, then transcribed and coded.
   168→The analysis examined participants’ views on digital self-monitoring, notifications,
   169→questionnaire content, functionality, data visualizations, and data sharing with clinicians, or
   170→others. It also captured perceived implementation facilitators, barriers, and any additional
   171→topics regarding ESM and EMI design and usage that emerged during the interviews.
   172→We developed a debriefing questionnaire and semi-structured interview guides for data
   173→collection (Appendices 1–3). The debriefing survey explored participants' experiences using
   174→the app for ESM and EMI, including barriers, facilitators, views on questions and
   175→interventions, and suggestions for improvement. Although the questionnaire consisted of 10
   176→items, only four were selected for this analysis: 1. How would you describe your experience
   177→using a mobile app to record your daily statuses? Was there anything that was difficult for you
   178→
   179→or particularly interesting to you? 2. Did you feel that the questions in the app accurately
   180→captured your daily state and experiences? If not, which questions did you find inadequate or
   181→challenging? Which questions did you find difficult? Would you like to add any questions? 3.
   182→Have you noticed that the app would affect your daily behavior or routine? If so, how? 4.
   183→What would you recommend improving this research, whether in the use of the m-Path app or
   184→the overall organization of the study?.
   185→At the beginning of the semi-structured interviews, participants were introduced to the
   186→potential clinical uses of ESM and EMI, asked about their prior digital self-monitoring
   187→experience, and shown prototypes of their data visualizations (Supplementary material 1) to
   188→gather feedback on potential treatment implementation. The service user interview guide
   189→comprised 22 questions. Clinicians participated exclusively in semi-structured interviews (20
   190→questions) tailored to their context, which explored their clinical practice, personal self-
   191→monitoring experience, and observations of patients’ self-monitoring. They were shown
   192→questions and interventions within the app in video format as well as the data visualizations
   193→(Supplementary materials 1 and 2) to evaluate their potential integration into treatment.
   194→Data processing
   195→Written responses and interview recordings were transcribed into digital form. Preliminary
   196→data processing to derive structured items was conducted using NVivo qualitative analysis
   197→software, version 15 (Lumivero LLC). Quotes for the manuscript were translated into English
   198→using DeepL software (DeepL SE) and verified for accuracy.
   199→Data analysis
   200→Textual data were processed using frequency content analysis (Krippendorff, 2018), where
   201→responses were systematically coded based on predefined and refined categories. One
   202→
   203→researcher (RL) coded the data, adding or refining codes during team consensus meetings.
   204→Code frequencies were quantified to identify dominant patterns; codes with at least five
   205→references were included in the main results table, while those below this threshold were
   206→treated as infrequent observations and reserved for interpretation.
   207→Concurrently, inductive thematic content analysis (Dierckx De Casterlé et al., 2012) was
   208→conducted, guided by interview transcriptions, and the research-based interview guide. After
   209→pseudonymization, a codebook was compiled from the data. One researcher (AK) coded the
   210→transcripts, adding or refining codes during team consensus meetings. Finally, codes were
   211→grouped into central themes, which were validated against all transcripts and discussed within
   212→the team.
   213→Techniques to enhance trustworthiness
   214→To enhance trustworthiness, peer debriefing was carried out between two researchers (AK and
   215→RL) to ensure consistency and credibility. The coding framework was developed iteratively
   216→throughout the analytic process. Emerging codes, categories, and thematic interpretations
   217→were regularly discussed within the research team to challenge assumptions, refine
   218→interpretations, and enhance analytic rigor.
   219→Researcher reflexivity
   220→Some of the interviews were conducted by researchers who had previous collegial contact
   221→with the interviewed clinicians. This familiarity facilitated rapport and openness during the
   222→interviews but was also acknowledged as a potential source of bias. To minimize its influence,
   223→interviewers followed a standardized interview guide and adhered to reflexive practices
   224→throughout data collection.

   261→Service users
   262→The thematic content analysis identified six themes: previous experience with self-monitoring,
   263→acceptability of ESM monitoring, perceived benefits of self-monitoring, experiences with
   264→EMI, integration into mental health care, and personalization and future development. The
   265→themes, subthemes, and illustrative citations are summarized in Table 4.
   266→Previous experience with self-monitoring. Most participants reported previous experience
   267→with digital self-monitoring outside treatment, primarily through activity and sleep tracking
   268→applications. Only a minority had used self-monitoring tools within mental health care. Non-
   269→use or discontinuation was mainly attributed to low perceived usefulness, limited interest in
   270→technology, or negative experiences.
   271→
   272→Acceptability of ESM monitoring. Participants generally considered the ESM protocol
   273→acceptable. Most found the questions relevant and understandable, although some reported
   274→occasional difficulties with unclear, repetitive, or personally irrelevant items. Three
   275→assessments per day were typically viewed as manageable, and the perceived burden
   276→decreased over time. Adherence was facilitated by motivation, usability, and predictable
   277→scheduling, while interference with work duties represented the main barrier.
   278→Perceived benefits of self-monitoring. Participants described monitoring as a valuable tool
   279→for increasing self-awareness, tracking changes in symptoms, and identifying patterns in
   280→emotions, behaviors, and daily experiences. Data visualizations were perceived as useful for
   281→self-reflection and monitoring progress. Emotional responses were predominantly positive,
   282→although some participants reported discomfort when answering sensitive questions.
   283→Experience with EMI. Most participants explored the intervention content but used it only
   284→occasionally. Motivational quotations were the most frequently used and positively evaluated
   285→intervention. Limited use was mainly explained by a preference for interpersonal support, lack
   286→of perceived need, or doubts about usefulness.
   287→Integration into mental health care. Participants generally supported the integration of ESM
   288→and EMI into routine care. They expected monitoring data to improve communication with
   289→clinicians, support treatment decisions, facilitate psychoeducation, and enable earlier
   290→detection of symptom deterioration. Most participants were also open to sharing data with
   291→close others, provided they retained control over what information was shared.
   292→Personalization and future development. Participants emphasized the importance of
   293→personalization, including individualized assessment of items and tailored interventions.
   294→Frequently suggested improvements included integration with wearable devices, automated
   295→links between assessment and intervention delivery, enhanced feedback, and chatbot-assisted
   296→
   297→support. Motivation for future use was primarily driven by expectations of improved self-
   298→understanding and treatment outcomes.
   299→Clinicians
   300→The thematic content analysis identified six themes: perceived clinical value, impact on
   301→patients, integration into clinical practice, usability and design considerations, implementation
   302→requirements, and future development opportunities. The themes, subthemes, and illustrative
   303→citations are summarized in Table 4.
   304→Perceived clinical value. Clinicians emphasized the potential of self-monitoring data to
   305→enhance the objectivity of patient assessment by reducing retrospective recall bias and
   306→providing more detailed contextual information about symptoms and behavior. Self-
   307→monitoring and data visualizations were viewed as useful tools for identifying behavioral
   308→patterns, supporting patient self-reflection, facilitating psychoeducation, and strengthening
   309→patient competencies. Participants also highlighted the potential of the application to enrich
   310→psychotherapy by providing material for therapeutic discussions and supporting work between
   311→sessions.
   312→Impact on patients. Participants believed that self-monitoring could increase patients’
   313→awareness of their experiences and promote engagement in treatment. However, several
   314→clinicians also described situations in which monitoring had negative consequences,
   315→particularly when patients became preoccupied with sleep metrics or used monitoring data to
   316→reinforce negative beliefs about themselves and their condition.
   317→Integration into clinical practice. Most clinicians considered the application particularly
   318→useful during the early stages of treatment, when establishing a case formulation and
   319→treatment goals. Participants stressed the importance of adapting the monitoring protocol to
   320→individual patients and diagnoses, and some also identified potential use in relapse prevention.
   321→
   322→Although clinicians generally perceived the approach as valuable, concerns were raised
   323→regarding the additional time required to review and discuss monitoring data.
   324→Usability and design considerations. The application interface was generally perceived as
   325→intuitive and easy to use. Clinicians regarded the monitoring questions as relevant but
   326→suggested improving clarity, reducing overlap between items, and providing opportunities for
   327→personalization. Most participants recommended reducing assessment frequency to twice
   328→daily and limiting questionnaire completion time to approximately three to five minutes. Data
   329→visualizations were considered particularly valuable when interactive and customizable.
   330→Implementation requirements. The most frequently identified barriers were limited patient
   331→motivation and skills, lack of time, and organizational constraints. Participants emphasized
   332→the need for seamless integration into routine clinical workflows and highlighted uncertainties
   333→regarding reimbursement and documentation procedures. Preferred training formats combined
   334→introductory workshops with asynchronous educational materials and opportunities for
   335→follow-up consultation.
   336→Future development opportunities. Clinicians suggested expanding the application with
   337→monitoring of physical activity, nutrition, goal attainment, and AI-assisted emotional support.
   338→Additional recommendations included open-ended questions, planning functions, and a
   339→broader range of brief interventions. Most participants expressed willingness to use the
   340→application in future clinical practice, provided they received sufficient training and had
   341→adequate time to incorporate it into their work.

   344→Discussion
   345→Both service users and clinicians perceived ESM as acceptable and valuable for self-
   346→awareness and clinical care. Although EMI was viewed positively, its limited use suggests that
   347→perceived usefulness alone may not be sufficient to promote engagement. Participants also
   348→identified barriers to routine implementation.
   349→The successful integration of repeated assessments into participants' daily lives supports the
   350→feasibility and acceptability of prolonged ESM monitoring in routine outpatient care,
   351→extending previous evidence predominantly based on shorter monitoring periods (Vachon et
   352→al., 2019). Despite the intensity of the protocol, participants generally perceived monitoring as
   353→manageable, consistent with earlier studies (Bos et al., 2020; Folkersma et al., 2021). Our
   354→findings also align with research indicating that adherence to ESM is facilitated by user-
   355→friendly design, predictable assessment schedules, and the perceived relevance of collected
   356→data (Kurilla et al., 2026).
   357→Increased self-awareness confirms that ESM can function not only as an assessment method
   358→but also as a self-reflective tool (van Os et al., 2017b; Wright & Zimmermann, 2019).
   359→Individualized feedback and data visualization drive patient insight and collaborative clinical
   360→decision-making (Bos et al., 2019; De Thurah et al., 2025; Schulte-Strathaus et al., 2025).
   361→However, minor reports of participant discomfort and clinician concerns regarding the
   362→reinforcement of maladaptive preoccupations suggest that self-awareness is not universally
   363→beneficial which is consistent with previous findings (Bos et al., 2020). Instead, the impact of
   364→ESM appears contingent upon individual characteristics, symptom profiles, and how
   365→monitoring data are clinically interpreted.
   366→Crucially, both service users and clinicians strongly supported integrating ESM into routine
   367→care. Continuous monitoring and personalized feedback were perceived as facilitating
   368→
   369→communication, shared understanding, and collaborative treatment planning. (Boardman &
   370→Dave, 2020). By capturing real-time within-person dynamics, ESM may contribute to more
   371→person-centered mental health care (Myin-Germeys, 2020).
   372→In contrast to high ESM engagement, EMI utilization was infrequent and largely restricted to
   373→motivational quotations. Non-use was mainly driven by low perceived need, a preference for
   374→human interaction, and skepticism regarding utility. These cautious findings contrast with
   375→prior literature, where EMIs are typically linked to significant improvements in symptoms and
   376→emotion regulation (Bell et al., 2017; Dao et al., 2021). Low engagement may stem from the
   377→non-individualized, on-demand nature of the utilized EMIs. Literature confirms that EMI
   378→efficacy relies crucially on tailoring interventions to users' immediate contexts and clinical
   379→needs (Myin-Germeys et al., 2016; Von Lützow et al., 2025).
   380→An important contribution of the present study concerns the feasibility of applying a single
   381→ESM protocol across a heterogeneous clinical population. Participants represented diverse
   382→mental health difficulties, yet both service users and clinicians generally considered the
   383→monitoring acceptable and clinically meaningful. This finding supports the potential of
   384→transdiagnostic ESM approaches to capture common processes that cut across traditional
   385→diagnostic categories, while simultaneously providing individualized insights into each
   386→person's daily experiences. Such an approach aligns with contemporary dimensional models
   387→of psychopathology, including HiTOP (Kotov et al., 2017), and may facilitate more flexible,
   388→fine – grained, and person-centered assessment strategies in routine care, which is consistent
   389→with previous findings (Bonnier et al., 2025; Bos et al., 2020). However, some participants
   390→demanded individualized items, flexible schedules, and tailored interventions. Our findings
   391→suggest that transdiagnostic framework and personalization should not be viewed as
   392→competing approaches. While participants generally accepted a common transdiagnostic
   393→monitoring framework, they simultaneously emphasized the need for individualized items,
   394→
   395→flexible schedules, and tailored interventions. The challenge for future ESM systems may
   396→therefore be to combine broad transdiagnostic coverage with person-specific adaptation
   397→(Reininghaus et al., 2024).
   398→Despite favorable attitudes, implementation barriers persist. Service users experience
   399→monitoring burden from repetitive items and rigid scheduling, while clinicians face systemic
   400→constraints including limited time, documentation, and reimbursement uncertainty. These
   401→findings confirm that positive stakeholder perceptions do not guarantee clinical adoption
   402→(Meglio et al., 2024; Weermeijer et al., 2023), highlighting a critical discrepancy between
   403→acceptability and implementation readiness (Diel et al., 2024). Overcoming these barriers
   404→requires organizational support, clinician training, clear workflows, and viable funding
   405→models—factors as vital as technical refinement itself.
   406→Implications for future research
   407→Future research should focus on three interconnected areas to advance ESM implementation.
   408→First, shifting toward flexible, adaptive assessment protocols will allow item selection to be
   409→tailored to individual clinical presentations and contexts. Second, enhancing engagement with
   410→EMIs requires developing contextually targeted content that integrates seamlessly into
   411→broader therapeutic frameworks to complement preferred interpersonal support. Lastly,
   412→implementation science must address patient motivation and clinician workflow constraints
   413→by establishing optimal organizational models, training formats, and viable reimbursement
   414→frameworks for routine psychiatric practice.
   415→Limitations
   416→Several limitations should be considered. Service users who enrolled in the study may have
   417→been more motivated to engage with self-monitoring than the general population of mental
   418→health service users. In addition, interview participants had successfully completed a
   419→
   420→prolonged ESM protocol and therefore may have been particularly receptive to and compliant
   421→with self-monitoring. As a result, the findings may not fully reflect the perspectives of less
   422→engaged or less technologically inclined service users. Similarly, participating clinicians may
   423→have been more interested in digital innovations than clinicians in routine practice.
   424→Additionally, prior collegial contact between certain researchers and clinicians could have
   425→introduced social desirability bias, though standardized protocols and reflexive practices were
   426→employed to mitigate this risk. Finally, engagement with EMI components was relatively
   427→limited, warranting caution when interpreting conclusions regarding EMI utility.
   428→Conclusions
   429→The findings suggest that prolonged transdiagnostic ESM monitoring is feasible, acceptable,
   430→and clinically meaningful for many service users and clinicians. ESM was perceived as
   431→supporting self-awareness, psychoeducation, treatment planning, and communication,
   432→whereas limited EMI use highlighted the importance of personalized and well-integrated
   433→interventions. Although implementation challenges remain, ESM-based approaches show
   434→promise as a complement to routine mental health care. Future work should focus on
   435→balancing transdiagnostic assessment with person-specific adaptation and sustainable
   436→implementation.
