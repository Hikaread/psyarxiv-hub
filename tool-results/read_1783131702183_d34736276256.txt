    47→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 3
    48→Abstract
    49→The unsolicited reception of genital images is a prevalent phenomenon, yet the
    50→influence of online social norms and victim behavior on judgments surrounding these
    51→incidents remains underexplored. In two studies with social media users from the United
    52→Kingdom, we examined how victim behavior and acceptance of myths about cyber-
    53→sexual violence influence victim-blaming and perceptions of incident severity. Study 1
    54→(N = 131) found that participants attributed more blame to the victim and perceived the
    55→incident as less severe when the victim had previously chatted (vs. not chatted) with the
    56→perpetrator. This effect was only present among participants with higher acceptance of
    57→cyber-sexual violence myths. In Study 2 (N = 202), a similar pattern emerged, with the
    58→victim perceived as more blameworthy and the incident as less severe when she had
    59→shown sexual interest (vs. no interest) toward the perpetrator before the incident,
    60→particularly among participants with higher acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual
    61→violence. Moreover, we found an indirect effect of perceived sexual consent on this
    62→relationship. Our findings highlight the influence of online social norms on judgments
    63→of cyber-sexual violence and emphasize the importance of education on online sexual
    64→interactions.
    65→Keywords: Myths about cyber-sexual violence, dick pic, unsolicited genital image,
    66→victim blaming, social perception

    69→“It’s Just a Dick pic”: (Cyber)Rape Culture and Victim Behavior Predict
    70→Perceptions and Responses to Unsolicited Genital Images
    71→We live in a rape culture, that is, […] it is necessary to profoundly transform a culture
    72→that makes sex indistinguishable from sexual violence (Serra, 2024)
    73→Similar to offline socialization, online sexual interactions exist on a continuum,
    74→ranging from consensual flirting and playful behavior to one-sided, non-consensual,
    75→intimidating, and violating actions (Wéry & Billieux, 2016). Among the different forms
    76→of online sexual interactions, the exchange of sexually explicit materials is one of the
    77→most common practices among various age groups (Setty, 2019; Wéry & Billieux,
    78→2016). However, not all exchanges of sexual images in online settings are consensual;
    79→they can be conceptualized as sexting or as a form of cyber-sexual violence depending
    80→on prior agreement to the exchange (Henry & Powell, 2015).
    81→Unsolicited sexual images—particularly genital ones—are among the most
    82→common forms of cyber-sexual violence, especially affecting women and sexual
    83→minorities (Anonymous, 2025; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024). Beyond their frequency,
    84→these experiences have significant consequences, including feelings of humiliation,
    85→sadness, and embarrassment (Marcotte et al., 2021; Powell & Flynn, 2023). However,
    86→these incidents are frequently minimized, and victims may be blamed—particularly
    87→when preexisting beliefs held by perceivers or contextual factors (e.g., prior
    88→relationships or sexual interest) shape interpretations of the event (Bohner et al., 2023;
    89→Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012). Understanding how these perceptions form is crucial, as
    90→they can shape social responses and institutional practices. Thus, the present research
    91→aims to experimentally explore how individual (i.e., acceptance of myths about cyber-
    92→sexual violence) and situational (i.e., victim behavior) factors influence the social
    93→perceptions of unwanted genital image incidents.

    96→Emerging Forms of Sexual Violence: The Sending of ‘Dick Pics’ or Cyberflashing
    97→With the advent of new technologies, sexual interactions have changed
    98→significantly in recent years. One notable example of this shift is the increasing
    99→exchange of sexual images via social networking platforms, particularly the sending of
   100→genital images or ‘dick pics’ (Amundsen, 2021; Anonymous, 2025; Waling & Pym,
   101→2019). However, whereas requested and desired genital images may smoothly facilitate
   102→intimate exchanges and accelerate the echoes of sexual desire in various contexts, the
   103→unsolicited and/or unwanted sending of genital images can be a violent and humiliating
   104→experience (Paasonen et al., 2019).
   105→The unsolicited reception of genital images, also known as cyberflashing, is a
   106→form of unsolicited sexting that occurs when a person (often a man) sends sexually
   107→suggestive or explicit material (i.e., a picture of their genitals) to someone else (often a
   108→woman) without being asked to do so (Marcotte et al., 2021). In the context of male
   109→perpetration, this phenomenon is present in the everyday use of hookup apps by men
   110→who have sex with men—between 5% and 48% of non-heterosexual men report having
   111→received unsolicited genital images (Oswald et al., 2020; Smith, 2018)—but it is
   112→especially prevalent among women (Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024; Jeacock et al., 2024).
   113→For example, two in five women in the United Kingdom have reported receiving
   114→unsolicited genital images from men (Smith, 2018), and data from the United States
   115→indicate that 53% of women have received such images (Pew Research Center, 2017).
   116→Research shows that common self-reported reasons for sending this type of
   117→image include seeking sexual reciprocation, initiating in-person encounters, or signaling
   118→sexual interest (Martínez-Bacaicoa et al., 2023; Oswald et al., 2019). However, while
   119→the unsolicited sending of such images in non-heterosexual contexts has been associated
   120→with motivations such as excitement or boredom on the part of the sender (Dietzel,
   121→
   122→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 6
   123→2022), women who receive them in heterosexual contexts often experience them as acts
   124→of misogyny disconnected from sexual desire (Jeacock et al., 2024). In this context, a
   125→self-produced ‘dick pic’ functions as a symbol of phallic male power that diminishes
   126→women’s sense of sexual agency (Paasonen et al., 2019; Waling & Pym, 2019).
   127→Nevertheless, such images operate as a digital form of ‘catcalling’: in both scenarios,
   128→men may present their actions as compliments or expressions of sexual interest, yet they
   129→are more accurately used as tactics of control and dominance over women (Amundsen,
   130→2021; Mandau, 2020; Paasonen et al., 2019). In fact, several studies have indicated that
   131→the unsolicited sending of these images to women is associated with harassment,
   132→appearing alongside negative social media comments, revenge pornography, and
   133→gender-based hate speech (Ringrose et al., 2019; Vitis & Gilmour, 2017).
   134→Moreover, women experience unsolicited genital images as humiliating,
   135→unpleasant, and invasive (Mandau, 2020; Waling & Pym, 2019). Research indicates that
   136→women were generally bothered by their inability to consent to receiving the image, as
   137→well as by a loss of sexual agency (Amundsen, 2021), leading to feelings of anger, fear,
   138→sadness, and annoyance (Rodríguez-Domínguez et al., 2025; Salerno-Ferraro et al.,
   139→2021). However, unsolicited genital images are perceived as “just something to laugh
   140→at” by some bystanders (Amundsen, 2021; Ringrose et al., 2019). As a result, many
   141→women choose not to report these incidents—not only due to the stigma and shame
   142→associated with receiving such images, but also due to fear of being met with mocking
   143→and victim-blaming reactions when they do speak out (Flynn et al., 2022; Ringrose et
   144→al., 2019; Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024).
   145→In this context, previous qualitative studies suggest that perceptions and
   146→responses to unsolicited genital images are shaped by broader cultural frameworks—
   147→such as rape culture—that serve to normalize and legitimize such behaviors (Dietzel,
   148→
   149→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 7
   150→2022; Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Related studies in offline contexts have shown that
   151→these reactions are often underpinned by sexist beliefs and interpretive schemas
   152→commonly referred to as myths (Bohner et al., 2023).
   153→Myths about Cyber-Sexual Violence Against Women
   154→In recent years, a distinct framework of rules governing online behavior has
   155→emerged within digital environments, which includes social scripts that shape how,
   156→where, and with whom users interact across various contexts (Henry & Powell, 2015).
   157→These scripts also extend to online sexual behavior and differ based on gender
   158→(Ringrose et al., 2022). For instance, studies on online flirting behavior suggest that, for
   159→some users, it is expected that women upload photos to social media, while men engage
   160→by reacting or commenting on those images (Gill, 2023; Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2025).
   161→Other qualitative research has shown that women who share semi-nude content (e.g.,
   162→bikini photos) are more likely to be perceived as provocative or overtly sexual, whereas
   163→men posting similar content are often viewed as joking—even when their images are
   164→unsolicited (McKinlay & Lavis, 2020; Ringrose et al., 2022). As a result of these
   165→gendered scripts about online sexual behaviors, many individuals may interpret
   166→unsolicited sexual interactions or instances of cyber-sexual violence as consensual or
   167→normal expressions of sexuality.
   168→In this sense, traditional literature on offline settings has emphasized that
   169→judgments and interpretations of sexual interactions are influenced by the attitudes of
   170→perceivers, such as sexist beliefs or offline rape myths (Bohner et al., 2023). For
   171→instance, in rape cases, sexist attitudes have been associated with victim blaming and
   172→the mitigation of perpetrator responsibility (Bareket & Fiske, 2023). Prior research in
   173→offline contexts has also shown that individuals who endorse rape myths are more likely
   174→to blame victims of sexual violence (van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014), perceive such
   175→
   176→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 8
   177→incidents as less severe (Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012), and attribute greater sexual
   178→intent to victim behavior (Lofgreen et al., 2017). However, despite its relevance in the
   179→study of sexual violence, these beliefs do not account for the social context of online
   180→environments.
   181→Recently, certain attitudes toward cyber-sexual violence against women have
   182→been identified within online spaces, including statements such as: “Much of the sexual
   183→innuendo directed at women online is a result of the websites they engage with” or
   184→“Instead of focusing on women who receive a ‘dick pic’, society should pay more
   185→attention to the real victims of sexual assault” (Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024). These
   186→attitudes have been conceptualized as myths about cyber-sexual violence, encompassing
   187→“a set of descriptive or prescriptive beliefs about the conception, causes, consequences,
   188→context, victims, and perpetrators, which serve to justify, minimize, or deny cyber-
   189→sexual violence against women by men” (Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2025, p. 4).
   190→According to Attribution Theory (Heider, 1958), such beliefs may serve as
   191→frameworks for evaluating social interactions—particularly when these interactions are
   192→ambiguous (e.g., during an online conversation) or when specific factors (e.g., the
   193→victim’s behavior) must be considered. In particular, when such incidents appear as
   194→socially accepted sexual scripts—such as the sending of sexual images—they are more
   195→likely to be misperceived as consensual. This can lead to greater victim-blaming or to
   196→interpreting the situation as less serious (McCaughey & Cermele, 2022).
   197→The Role of Victim’s Behavior on the Judgement of (Cyber)Sexual Violence
   198→There is a certain consensus about the need to analyze how people perceive and
   199→judge the different forms of violence against women—particularly those that have
   200→emerged in today’s main socialization spaces, such as cyber-sexual violence (Bohner et
   201→al., 2023; Flood & Pease, 2009; Henry & Powell, 2015). It is important to recognize that
   202→
   203→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 9
   204→sexual violence against women—whether offline or online—not only involves the
   205→actions of the perpetrator but also unfolds within a broader social context that may
   206→either condemn or tolerate such behavior under certain circumstances (Flood & Pease,
   207→2009). Traditionally, some individuals have justified sexual assault by suggesting that
   208→the victim behaved inappropriately, thereby shifting the focus from the aggressor to the
   209→victim—a process known as victim blaming (Bohner et al., 2023; Megías et al., 2018).
   210→This tendency has been observed across both offline and online contexts (Romero-
   211→Sánchez et al., 2012; Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024). However, in the digital sphere,
   212→minimization has emerged as another frequent societal reaction—characterized by the
   213→denial, trivialization, or dismissal of cyber-sexual violence reported by women (Flynn et
   214→al., 2022; Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Both victim blaming and minimization reflect
   215→broader cultural norms that shift responsibility away from the perpetrator and are shaped
   216→by social expectations regarding how victims should behave and what kinds of acts are
   217→recognized as violence (Temkin & Krahé, 2008).
   218→In this sense, prior research has highlighted that women who did not fit the
   219→‘victim stereotype’ were more likely to receive judgments of blame in rape cases, and
   220→the incidents were perceived as less severe (Bohner et al., 2023; Paganini et al., 2023).
   221→Specifically, victims of offline sexual violence are often questioned and blamed due to
   222→behaviors such as having a prior relationship with the perpetrator (Mainwaring et al.,
   223→2023; van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014), having previously consumed alcohol (Romero-
   224→Sánchez et al., 2012; Temkin & Krahé, 2008), wearing provocative clothing (Bohner et
   225→al., 2023; van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014), or having previously shown sexual desire
   226→toward the perpetrator (Hills et al., 2020; Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012). Overall, these
   227→judgments may be explained by the perceived sexual consent of the victims (Bouffard &
   228→Miller, 2023; Hills et al., 2020), which increases the attribution of responsibility to them
   229→
   230→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 10
   231→and diminishes the perceived gravity of the incident—on the assumption that they were
   232→seeking out or exposing themselves to such situations (Bohner et al., 2023).
   233→In contrast to the study of judgements associated with offline sexual violence,
   234→research on cyber-sexual violence is more recent but growing. In online interactions,
   235→women frequently report experiencing cyber-sexual violence, such as receiving
   236→unsolicited genital images—often during the very first conversation in a chat or shortly
   237→after exchanging social media profiles (Gill, 2023; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024;
   238→Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Research has also shown that a shared intention of
   239→hooking up is positively associated with experiencing this form of cyber-sexual violence
   240→on dating apps (Centelles et al., 2021; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024), suggesting that
   241→such behavior may be mistakenly perceived as a normalized or expected part of the
   242→consent process in online settings.
   243→As a result, in cases of cyber-sexual violence, victim behavior, such as chatting
   244→with or previously showing sexual interest toward the perpetrator, can become factors
   245→that lead to victim-blaming and the trivialization of the incident. However, similar to
   246→offline sexual violence, such judgments could be more likely to occur among
   247→individuals who endorse stereotypical beliefs about cyber-sexual violence, its victims,
   248→and its perpetrators—that is, among those who support more myths about cyber-sexual
   249→violence (e.g., Bohner et al., 2023).
   250→The Present Research
   251→The unsolicited genital image is a prevalent and harmful form of cyber-sexual
   252→violence against women (Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024); however, victims are often
   253→blamed, and their reports are questioned (Centelles et al., 2021; Flynn et al., 2022;
   254→Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024). Research has indicated that people use prior beliefs to
   255→interpret social interactions (Bohner et al., 2023; Heider, 1958), yet it is still unknown
   256→
   257→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 11
   258→how (cyber)rape culture—a culture that normalizes, excuses, and legitimizes unwanted
   259→or non-consensual sexual behavior online, often through the misinterpretation of
   260→women’s actions as expressions of sexual consent (Reling et al., 2018; Ryan, 2011)—
   261→influences the perceptions of cyber-sexual violence. Thus, through two experimental
   262→studies, we analyzed the role of myths about cyber-sexual violence as cognitive
   263→schemas for interpreting incidents of unsolicited genital images, considering victim
   264→behavior that have been identified as influencing the perceptions of incidents of cyber-
   265→sexual violence (van der Bruggen & Grubb, 2014).
   266→To do so, we presented participants with hypothetical scenarios involving a
   267→cyber-sexual violence incident—specifically, the receipt of an unsolicited genital image.
   268→We measured levels of victim-blaming and perceived severity of the incident, while
   269→accounting for participants’ acceptance of cyber-sexual violence myths and the type of
   270→behavior displayed by the victim. In Study 1, participants judged an unsolicited genital
   271→image incident depending on the victim’s (a woman) behavior before the incident
   272→(chatted vs. not chatted with the perpetrator on Instagram). In Study 2, based on prior
   273→research linking sexual interest and harassment (Centelles et al., 2021; Gewirtz-Meydan
   274→et al., 2024), we manipulated the victim's sexual interest or attraction toward the
   275→perpetrator (interest vs. no interest) prior to the incident.
   276→All procedures adhered to the ethical guidelines outlined in the American
   277→Psychological Association [APA] Ethical Code (Principles of Psychologists and Code of
   278→Conduct) for studies involving human participants (APA, 2017), as well as broader
   279→professional standards governing research studies. This research was conducted in
   280→accordance with the ethical standards of the University's Biomedical Research Ethics
   281→Committee and received ethical approval from [blind review]. All material, measures,
   282→datasets, and the preregistration for Study 2 can be found at [blind review].

   285→Study 1
   286→The aim of Study 1 was to analyze perceptions of a fictional incident involving
   287→the receipt of an unsolicited genital image, based on the victim’s prior behavior
   288→(chatting vs. not chatting with the perpetrator before the incident) and the participants’
   289→acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual violence. Specifically, we assessed the extent to
   290→which participants believed that a woman who received such an image was responsible
   291→for the behavior (i.e., victim-blaming), as well as how serious they perceived the
   292→incident to be (i.e., perceptions of incident severity).
   293→Prior research has shown that in situations where the victim transgressed
   294→traditional gender roles—for example, by engaging in conversations with unknown
   295→men—responsibility tended to shift from the perpetrator to the victim (Grubb & Turner,
   296→2012; Mainwaring et al., 2023) and the incident was considered as less severe (Romero-
   297→Sánchez et al., 2012). Thus, we anticipated higher levels of victim-blaming (Hypothesis
   298→1a) and lower levels of perceived severity (Hypothesis 1b) in cases where the victim had
   299→chatted (vs. no chatted) with the perpetrator on a social network (i.e., Instagram).
   300→Considering prior findings (Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2025), we expected that the
   301→endorsement of myths about cyber-sexual violence would be associated with greater
   302→victim-blaming (Hypothesis 2a) and lower perceived severity (Hypothesis 2b), as well
   303→as moderate the effect of victim behavior on these perceptions. Specifically, we
   304→expected increased victim-blaming (Hypothesis 3a) and decreased perceived severity
   305→(Hypothesis 3b) when the victim had chatted (vs. not chatted) with the perpetrator,
   306→particularly among participants with higher (vs. lower) endorsement of myths about
   307→cyber-sexual violence.

   310→Method
   311→Participants
   312→Sensitivity power analysis was conducted using the pwr package in R
   313→(Champely, 2018) for a linear regression model with three predictors (main effects and
   314→interactions). Analysis revealed that 100 participants would provide 80% power to
   315→detect small-to-medium effect sizes (Cohen, 1988) with a significance level of α = .05.
   316→These effect sizes are consistent with those observed in prior research on perceptions of
   317→sexual violence (e.g., Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012). Following Simmons et al. (2013),
   318→who recommend a minimum of 50 participants per condition to ensure adequate power
   319→and reduce false positives, we aimed for at least 100 participants across the two
   320→experimental conditions.
   321→An initial sample of 173 participants completed an online survey developed
   322→using the Qualtrics software package. Participants were recruited through Prolific and
   323→each received £1 as remuneration. Following recommended guidelines for online
   324→research (Goodman & Paolacci, 2017), 42 participants were excluded for not
   325→completing the survey (n = 11), failing the manipulation check (n = 28), or being
   326→outliers in completion time (n = 2; McClelland, 2002). One additional participant who
   327→selected “other” for gender was excluded due to insufficient representation for analysis.
   328→The final sample comprised 131 residents of the United Kingdom (67 men and
   329→64 women) aged between 19 and 60 years (M = 35.57 years, SD = 10.78). All
   330→age
   331→participants were active users of social networks and were familiar the Instagram and X
   332→(Twitter) platforms. On average, they reported using Instagram 1-2 times a day (M =
   333→3.69, SD = 1.78) and X (Twitter) 1-2 times a week (M = 2.18, SD = 2.03). Most
   334→participants identified as White/European (86.3%), followed by Asian and
   335→African/Black (both 4.6%), Multiple ethnic/Racial identities (2.3%), and Other (1.5%);
   336→
   337→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 14
   338→0.8% preferred not to say. Heterosexual participants represented the majority (78.6%) of
   339→the sample, followed by bisexual (13.7%), homosexual (3.1%) and other (4.6%)
   340→participants. The educational level was as follows: 14.5% had finished secondary
   341→education, 20.6% held vocational training, 61.8% had completed higher-university
   342→education, and 3.1% had obtained doctoral studies.
   343→Procedure
   344→This study used an experimental design through the administration of an online
   345→survey via Qualtrics software. All participants received the same instructions, informing
   346→them that the survey was anonymous and aimed to assess opinions regarding online
   347→sexual interactions (estimated completion time ~7 minutes). To minimize speculation
   348→about the hypothesis and prevent potential negative reactions among some participants,
   349→we refrained from explicitly stating that the primary focus was on cyber-sexual violence
   350→against women. Participants were then informed about the voluntary nature of the study
   351→and their right to withdraw at any time. After obtaining each participant’s consent, they
   352→completed measures assessing the acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual violence and
   353→social desirability. Subsequently, participants were randomly assigned to one of two
   354→conditions: those who had previously chatted with the perpetrator on Instagram (32 men
   355→and 33 women) vs. those who had not chatted (35 men and 31 women). Within their
   356→assigned condition, participants read one scenario and then completed measures
   357→assessing victim-blaming and perceptions of the incident’s severity. Finally, participants
   358→provided sociodemographic data and were debriefed at the end of the study.
   359→Instruments and Materials
   360→Frequent Use of Social Networks. Two items were used to assess the frequency
   361→of social networks usage (Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2025). Participants first indicated if
   362→they used social networks (yes/no). Then, they reported the amount of time spent on
   363→
   364→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 15
   365→social networks on specific platforms (i.e., WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook, Twitter,
   366→Telegram, TikTok, LinkedIn, Badoo, Tinder, Twitch, YouTube). Specifically, the most
   367→widely used social networking platforms were selected according to international
   368→surveys (Kemp, 2025). Responses to the two items were rated on a 7-points Likert scale
   369→from 0 (I don’t use social media) to 6 (Continuously throughout the day).
   370→Acceptance of Myths About Cyber-Sexual Violence. The 10-items Acceptance
   371→of Myths about Cyber-Sexual Violence Against Women (AMCYS) Scale (Vizcaíno-
   372→Cuenca et al., 2025) was used to assess the acceptance of common myths of cyber-
   373→sexual violence. AMCYS items are rated along a 7-point Likert-type scale ranging from
   374→1 (Totally disagree) to 7 (Totally agree). Examples of items are “Many women lie when
   375→reporting that they suffered sexual harassment on social media” and “If an unattractive
   376→man comments on the physical appearance of a woman in social media, it is perceived
   377→as harassment, but if the man is attractive the comment is received as a compliment”.
   378→An overall mean score of AMCYS was used, with higher scores indicate greater
   379→acceptation of myths about cyber-sexual violence. The internal reliability was .81,

   490→Discussion
   491→Study 1 found that a woman who had chatted with the perpetrator prior to the
   492→incident was assigned more blame, but her behavior did not affect perceptions of
   493→severity. AMCYS was linked to victim blaming, but not severity. Importantly, an
   494→interaction effect showed that victims who had chatted with the perpetrator were blamed
   495→more, and the incident was perceived as less severe, particularly among participants
   496→with higher AMCYS scores. These results emphasize the importance of considering
   497→
   498→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 20
   499→both individual differences (e.g., myths about cyber-sexual violence) and contextual
   500→factors (e.g., victim behavior) in understanding perceptions of cyber-sexual violence.
   501→However, some of our hypotheses about main effects were not supported, which
   502→might be partially explained by potential floor or ceiling effects in our dependent
   503→measures, limiting variability and sensitivity (e.g., Kiebler & Stewart, 2023). In
   504→particular, prior research has shown that terms such as ‘blame’ and ‘guilty’ are
   505→susceptible to social desirability bias, which may contribute to a floor effect in
   506→perception measures, reducing participants’ willingness to endorse higher levels of
   507→victim-blaming (Thelan & Meadows, 2022). Additionally, prior research suggests that
   508→expressions of sexual interest or ‘hooking up’ are associated with receiving unsolicited
   509→genital images on social media. Thus, building on these findings, Study 2 aims to
   510→explore more deeply how the victim’s sexual interest and online behaviors influence
   511→perceptions of victim blaming and severity, especially among young adults (aged 18–
   512→30), who are the primary users of social media and may internalize these norms more
   513→strongly. By focusing on this demographic, Study 2 seeks to clarify the mechanisms
   514→underlying these perceptions and address the unexpected results observed in Study 1.
   515→Study 2
   516→In study 1, participants who endorsed myths about cyber-sexual violence
   517→assigned more blame to the victim and considered the incident less severe when the
   518→victim chatted (vs. did not chat) with the perpetrator before the incident. However,
   519→perceptions of online sexual harassment can also be influenced by the victim’s feelings
   520→and level of sexual interest toward the perpetrator (Marks & Zaikman, 2023). Prior
   521→research has found that when a woman expressed sexual desire—such as proposing to
   522→go to her dorm room or showing attraction—before being raped, she was more likely to
   523→
   524→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 21
   525→be blamed and the incident was trivialized (Freetly & Kane, 1995; Romero-Sánchez et
   526→al., 2012).
   527→Building on these findings, the aim of Study 2 was to examine how information
   528→about the victim’s sexual interest toward the perpetrator influenced participants’
   529→attributions of victim blame and perceptions of incident severity in the context of
   530→unsolicited genital images. We expected higher levels of victim blaming (Hypothesis
   531→1a) and lower perceptions of severity (Hypothesis 1b) when the victim had expressed
   532→interest (vs. no interest) toward the perpetrator in a prior Instagram conversation.
   533→Additionally, we hypothesized that participants with higher AMCYS scores would
   534→report greater victim blaming (Hypothesis 2a) and lower perceptions of severity
   535→(Hypothesis 2b). Finally, we expected an interaction between AMCYS scores and the
   536→victim’s behavior: specifically, increased victim blaming (Hypothesis 3a) and decreased
   537→perceptions of severity (Hypothesis 3b) when the victim expressed interest (vs. no
   538→interest) toward the perpetrator, particularly among participants with higher (vs. lower)
   539→AMCYS scores. Finally, we conducted an exploratory analysis—non-preregistered—to
   540→examine the potential indirect effect of perceived sexual consent in the relationship
   541→between the victim’s expressed sexual interest and both victim-blaming attributions and
   542→perceived severity of the incident, considering the AMCYS as a moderating variable.

   746→Discussion
   747→The results of Study 2 indicated that women who had showed sexual interest in
   748→the perpetrator during a prior chat were more likely to be blamed, and the incident was
   749→perceived as less severe. Although endorsement of myths about cyber-sexual violence
   750→(AMCYS) was not directly associated with victim-blaming, it was negatively associated
   751→with the perceived severity of the incident. Notably, an interaction effect emerged:
   752→victims who showed prior sexual interest were blamed more and their experiences were
   753→perceived as less severe—but only among participants with higher AMCYS scores.
   754→Furthermore, the findings revealed an indirect effect through perceived sexual
   755→consent. Participants with higher AMCYS scores, when exposed to a scenario in which
   756→the victim had showed sexual interest in a chat on Instagram, reported higher perceived
   757→sexual consent. In turn, this perception was linked to increased victim-blaming and
   758→decreased perceptions of the incident’s severity. These results highlight how myths
   759→about cyber-sexual violence shape interpretations of online sexual interactions,
   760→contributing to the legitimization of such violence under the guise of consent.
   761→Study 2 replicates and extends prior research on the perceptions of unsolicited
   762→genital images. To address the limitations identified in Study 1—particularly potential
   763→floor and ceiling effects in the measures of victim-blaming and perceived severity—we
   764→adapted both the vignette and the corresponding scale items to improve sensitivity.

   770→General Discussion
   771→The unsolicited genital image is a widespread form of cyber-sexual violence
   772→against women, and its occurrence is often linked to—and justified by—the victim’s
   773→prior behavior (Centelles et al., 2021; Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024). This suggests the
   774→existence of online social norms that may serve to misinterpret victim behavior as
   775→implying sexual consent, leading to the justification and perpetration of unsolicited
   776→sexual interactions. Across two experimental studies, we examined how myths about
   777→cyber-sexual violence and the victim’s behavior affect the interpretation of unsolicited
   778→genital images as normalized and consensual expressions of sexuality. Specifically, we
   779→assessed levels of victim-blaming and perceptions of the incident’s severity, and further
   780→explored the indirect role of perceived sexual consent in these relationships.
   781→Study 1 showed that the victim who had previously chatted with the perpetrator
   782→was blamed more, and the incident was perceived as less severe compared to a victim
   783→who had not chatted with him. However, this effect was only observed among men and
   784→women with higher (vs. lower) acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual violence. In
   785→Study 2, we manipulated the victim’s sexual interest and replicated prior patterns where,
   786→again, the victim who had shown prior sexual interest toward the perpetrator was
   787→blamed more, and the incident was perceived as less severe—again, particularly among
   788→participants with higher acceptance of such myths. Notably, in this second study, we
   789→also examined the indirect effect of perceived sexual consent, assessed through
   790→participants’ qualitative responses. Results revealed that participants with higher (vs.
   791→lower) acceptance of cyber-sexual violence myths perceived greater sexual consent
   792→
   793→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 31
   794→when the victim had shown prior sexual interest (vs. no interest), which in turn was
   795→associated with increased victim blaming and decreased perceptions of incident severity.
   796→Women and men are socialized into different gender roles, such that, in the
   797→social imagination, men are expected to take an active role in sexual interactions,
   798→whereas women are expected to take a passive role (Newstrom et al., 2021). This leads
   799→to the interpretation of the indirect communication associated with women’s traditional
   800→roles as signals of sexual consent (Hills et al., 2020). Supporting this, previous studies
   801→in offline settings have shown that rape victims are more likely to be blamed if they had
   802→prior contact with the perpetrator (Mainwaring et al., 2023; van der Bruggen & Grubb,
   803→2014), accepted a drink from him (Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012; Temkin & Krahé,
   804→2008), or expressed sexual interest (Hills et al., 2020; Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012).
   805→Similarly, a recent study by Pacilli et al. (2024) found that female victims of image-
   806→based sexual abuse were blamed more when they displayed greater sexual agency and
   807→had a transient relationship with the perpetrator. However, to date, no studies have
   808→explored the online dynamics in the unsolicited reception of genital images, a behavior
   809→that has often been trivialized or ridiculed on social networking platforms (Vizcaíno-
   810→Cuenca et al., 2024). In this sense, our results showed that women victims of cyber-
   811→sexual violence can be blamed and the incident (i.e., to receive a dick pic) trivialized by
   812→their behavior in social media—such as having chatted with or shown sexual interest
   813→toward the perpetrator.
   814→Nevertheless, according to our findings, not all individuals judge and perceive
   815→the unsolicited genital image incidents in the same way, but only when specific
   816→individual factors are presents. In line with prior non-experimental research (Vizcaíno-
   817→Cuenca et al., 2025), stronger acceptance of myths about cyber-sexual violence was
   818→associated with greater victim-blaming and the minimization of incidents of cyber-
   819→
   820→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 32
   821→sexual violence (i.e., receiving sexual comments on social media). Our study extends
   822→these findings to cases involving unsolicited genital images, showing that higher
   823→acceptance of these myths was positively related to victim-blaming and negatively
   824→associated with the perceived severity of the incident.
   825→These patterns are similar to those found in offline settings, in which offline rape
   826→myths (Romero-Sánchez et al., 2012) or offline myths about intimate partner violence
   827→(Megías et al., 2018) were positively associated to victim blaming. However, despite
   828→these similarities, it is essential to explore the specific dynamics that emerge in online
   829→contexts, which sustain and perpetuate sexist ideology and violence against women in
   830→these spaces (Henry & Powell, 2015). Among the potential interactions of women in
   831→online settings, their behavior in chats has been one of the most questioned in reports of
   832→cyber-sexual violence (e.g., Flynn et al., 2022; Gill, 2020). Moreover, unlike in-person
   833→sexual violence, women who reported these incidents often received reactions of
   834→trivialization (Henry & Powell, 2015; Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2024).
   835→The literature on offline sexual violence has pointed out that, in cases of rape,
   836→individuals often use their prior beliefs to shift responsibility from male perpetrators to
   837→female victims (Megías et al., 2018; Temkin & Krahé, 2008), suggesting that sexist
   838→beliefs interact with situational factors in these interactions to justify such incidents
   839→(Bohner et al., 2023). In this sense, our results showed the moderating role of myths
   840→about cyber-sexual violence on perceptions of victim behavior in a reported incident of
   841→an unsolicited sexual image. Specifically, the victim was blamed more and the incident
   842→was perceived as less severe; however, our results highlighted that these judgements
   843→may be explained by the perceived sexual consent of the victim.
   844→Supporting our results, prior research has indicated that, in cases of rape,
   845→individuals with higher levels of sexist ideology (e.g., rape myths and sexist attitudes)
   846→
   847→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 33
   848→were more likely to attribute greater sexual consent to a victim who had prior contact
   849→with the perpetrator (Lofgreen et al., 2017), to underrecognize the incident as a form of
   850→violence in ambiguous interactions (Deming et al., 2013) and to use perceived pleasure
   851→as a justification for sexual violence against women (Hills et al., 2020; Reling et al.,
   852→2018). Ryan (2011) argues that rape myths shape sexual scripts, reframing acts of sexual
   853→violence as desired and consensual encounters. In a similar vein, our research shows
   854→that myths about cyber-sexual violence operate as a cognitive framework through which
   855→women’s prior online behavior—such as chatting or engaging in a hookup—are
   856→interpreted as signals of sexual consent, thus legitimizing cyber-sexual violence against
   857→them. Thus, considering the role of online settings in our daily lives (Kemp, 2025), it is
   858→essential to challenge and transform existing myths about cyber-sexual violence against
   859→women.
   860→Theoretical and Practical Implications
   861→This research offers important theoretical implications. Two studies showed how
   862→situational factors—specifically, a victim’s prior behavior—can serve as justification for
   863→cyber-sexual violence when observers hold certain beliefs, such as myths about cyber-
   864→sexual violence. Our findings suggest that these myths function as sexual scripts,
   865→shaping how online sexual interactions are interpreted. Sexual Script Theory (Simon,
   866→2017) posits that individuals interpret sexual behavior through culturally shared
   867→narratives and social interactions. Within this framework, myths about cyber-sexual
   868→violence help shape how such behaviors are understood and how victims are perceived
   869→(Hassebrauck & Aron, 2001), influencing judgments regarding cyber-sexual violence.
   870→From a practical perspective, these findings can inform prevention and
   871→awareness programs addressing cyber-sexual violence against women (e.g.,
   872→https://www.endviolenceagainstwomen.org.uk/campaign/online-abuse/). These findings
   873→
   874→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 34
   875→highlight the pervasive influence of (cyber)rape culture, and provides a framework for
   876→developing interventions that modify myths about cyber-sexual violence, challenge
   877→established sexual scripts of consensual interactions, and help prevent revictimization.
   878→Many women refrain from reporting such incidents due to the negative reactions they
   879→receive—such as victim-blaming and the trivialization of their experiences—after being
   880→exposed to unsolicited genital images, which often leads to further revictimization.
   881→Therefore, targeted programs should be developed to address self-blame and support
   882→women in coping with these negative responses.
   883→Limitations and Future Research
   884→This work is not without limitations. First, the unsolicited reception of genital
   885→images was examined solely within a heteronormative and binary framework. However,
   886→research indicates that sexual minorities are also targets of various forms of cyber-
   887→sexual violence, and that sexist ideologies may play a role in the justification and
   888→perpetration of these experiences (Gewirtz-Meydan et al., 2024). Future studies should
   889→therefore explore how the victim’s gender identity and/or sexual orientation influence
   890→perceptions of unsolicited genital image incidents. Additionally, this research focused
   891→exclusively on the victim’s behavior; however, qualitative studies suggest that
   892→contextual factors—such as whether the incident occurs on hookup apps (e.g., Tinder,
   893→Hinge)—can also be used to justify cyber-sexual violence (Gill, 2023; Ringrose et al.,
   894→2022). Second, the sample was drawn exclusively from the United Kingdom, which
   895→may limit the generalizability of the findings. Future research should replicate these
   896→results in other cultural contexts, although prior work suggests that these beliefs are
   897→relatively consistent across cultures (e.g., Vizcaíno-Cuenca et al., 2025). Third, Study 1
   898→showed potential floor and ceiling effects in the measurement of victim-blaming and
   899→perceived severity, which may have limited the sensitivity to detect some hypothesized
   900→
   901→RESPONSES TO UNSOLICITED GENITAL IMAGES 35
   902→effects in Study 1. While Study 2 adapted measures to address these issues, the
   903→moderated mediation analyses conducted remain exploratory. Therefore, replication in
   904→future studies is necessary to confirm these effects and to deepen the understanding of
   905→the mechanisms involved, particularly regarding the interpretation of perceived sexual
   906→consent. Lastly, while our results indicate an indirect effect of perceived sexual consent,
   907→future studies should use quantitative measures to assess this construct to further
   908→explore and clarify its role in shaping judgements toward cyber-sexual violence.
   909→Conclusions
   910→This research, consisting of two experimental studies, examined how myths
   911→about cyber-sexual violence and victim behavior impact judgments of unsolicited
   912→genital images. Results showed that when the victim had prior interactions (e.g.,
   913→chatting or showing sexual interest) with the perpetrator, victim-blaming increased and
   914→the incident was perceived as less severe. However, this effect was only present among
   915→participants who accepted myths about cyber-sexual violence. Study 2 further revealed
   916→that perceived sexual consent played a role—participants who saw the victim as
   917→showing sexual interest were more likely to interpret her behavior as consent, leading to
   918→greater victim-blaming and reduced severity perceptions, especially among those
   919→endorsing these myths. These findings highlight the importance of addressing myths
   920→about cyber-sexual violence to prevent its normalization.
