    69→Results
    70→Tool evaluation cycles
    71→Four iterative cycles were necessary to establish the content validity of the developed tool, with final I-CVI values reaching 1.00 (Polit et al., 2007). The first validation cycle involved nine experts with either scientific or professional knowledge (Désormeaux-Moreau et al., 2020). Specifically, the expert panel comprised three academic researchers specialised in intellectual disability or organizational psychology, and six professionals working directly with PwID (e.g., one neuropsychologist, one professional integration coordinator at IWSS, two workplace supervisors and two special educators in IWSS). For the subsequent validation cycles, a reduced panel of five experts was formed. Experts were selected based on their satisfaction ratio from the first cycle, as well as the relevance of their expertise to the construct being assessed. This second panel included two researchers and three professionals. A detailed overview of the content validation process across the four cycles — including the evolution of clarity and representativeness scores and item modifications — is provided in Table 1 and in supplementary material on Zenodo. 
    72→Cycle 1 with nine experts
    73→At the end of the first assessment cycle, of the 97 items evaluated, 72 items (i.e., 73% of the questionnaire) achieved I-CVI scores above the 0.78 threshold for representativeness, while 61 items (i.e., 62% of the questionnaire) exceeded the acceptability threshold for clarity. Based on expert feedback regarding item representativeness, linguistic clarity, and overall exhaustiveness, several revisions were made to the initial version of the tool. Notably, three source questionnaires were replaced. Items drawn from the newly selected questionnaires were subsequently reformulated in line with the experts’ recommendations, particularly those concerning the use of simplified and accessible language tailored to PwID. 
    74→Burnout. The SMBM (Sassi & Neveu, 2010) was replaced by the Burnout Assessment Tool (BAT; Schaufeli et al., 2020). This decision was motivated by the limitations of the SMBM, which primarily focuses on exhaustion, particularly on interpersonal aspects of the emotional exhaustion subscale, thus failing to capture the broader range of burnout symptoms recognised in the literature. For the subsequent expert consultation cycles, the four core dimensions of the BAT were retained: exhaustion, mental distance, cognitive impairment and emotional impairment. All dimensions were measured using a 5-point frequency scale. For the first three dimensions, three items were selected from the short version of the BAT. Regarding the fourth dimension (i.e., emotional impairment), the research team raised concerns about the accessibility of the item wording for PwID. Consequently, all five items from the full version of the BAT were initially retained, in order to gather expert feedback during the next consultation cycle.
    75→Recovery activities. The initial questionnaire assessing recovery activities (Sonnentag & Ziljstra, 2006), was replaced by the short version of the Recovery Activity Characteristics (RAC) questionnaire (Alameer et al., 2023). The RAC employs a 5-point frequency scale to assess how often specific characteristics are present in individuals’ recovery activities. This format was considered more accessible and appropriate for PwID than asking participants to estimate the number of hours spent on each activity per week. The RAC includes seven dimensions of activity characteristics: physical, social, creative, mental, virtual, outdoor, and spiritual, each measured by two items. For the present study, the spiritual dimension was excluded, as it was considered potentially less accessible for PwID. 
    76→Physical activity. The 10 items initially selected to measure physical activity among PwID were judged inaccessible by the expert panel. Six new items were developed, drawing on the structure of the short version of the International Physical Activity Questionnaire (IPAQ), which has previously been tested on PwID (Lee et al., 2011; Lynch et al., 2024). The revised format includes two items for each level of physical activity intensity-vigorous, moderate, and light- assessing both frequency (number of days per week) and average duration per week. To further enhance accessibility, pictograms and simplified descriptions of activities were incorporated to support respondents’ understanding (see Table S4). 
    77→Subsequently, several measurement scales and items were removed to reduce the overall length and complexity of the tool. First, within the job demands section, the two dimensions "ambiguities about work" and "changes in tasks," comprising a total of six items, were eliminated. Second, in the job resources section, the dimension "independence in work," consisting of three items, was also removed. In addition, ten items measuring recovery experiences were excluded. These included the nine items from the DISQ measuring emotional, physical, and cognitive detachment (De Jonge et al., 2011), which were found to conceptually overlapped with the psychological detachment dimension already assessed through the REQ (Sonnentag & Fritz, 2007). The item inspired by the Felt Arousal Scale (Svebak & Murgatroyd, 1985) was also removed, as it was deemed inaccessible for our target population by the expert panel. All remaining items were then reformulated in accordance with expert recommendations to enhance clarity and accessibility. In response to expert feedback regarding the overall structure of the tool, the research team also revised the response formats, by converting all agreement- and frequency-based scales from 4-point to 5-point formats. An additional response option — “I don't know or I don't want to answer” — was added to each item to reduce the risk of forced responses in cases of limited comprehension. According to the experts, this adjustment was expected to improve the representation of response variability and was considered suitable for PwID, as evidenced by several studies (e.g., Akkerman et al., 2018b, 2018a; Melchiori & Church, 1997). Following these modifications, all retained items were submitted for re-evaluation in subsequent validation cycles conducted with a smaller expert panel (see table 1).
    78→Cycle 2 to 4 with five experts
    79→During the second evaluation cycle, the expert committee assessed a total of 87 items. Among these, 67 items (i.e., 77% of the questionnaire) were deemed representative (I-CVI = 1), and 73 items (i.e., 84% of the questionnaire) were considered sufficiently clear (I-CVI = 1). Based on expert feedback regarding the length and clarity of the questionnaire, the research team removed seven items. Specifically, one item was removed from the five initially selected to measure the "emotional impairment" dimension of the BAT (Schaufeli et al., 2020). Additionally, six items were removed from the RAC questionnaire (Alameer et al., 2023), resulting in the retention of a single item per dimension (i.e., physical, social, creative, mental, virtual, and outdoor). Finally, 23 items underwent minor linguistic revisions to improve clarity and accessibility for PwID (see Table S1 and S3). 
    80→During the third evaluation cycle, the expert committee reviewed the revised set of 80 items. Of these, 74 items (i.e., 93% of the questionnaire) were rated as representative (I-CVI = 1), and 73 items (i.e., 91% of the questionnaire) were considered sufficiently clear (I-CVI = 1). Following expert recommendations, nine items drawn from the burnout, recovery activities, physical activity, and recovery experiences section were slightly reformulated to further enhance clarity. During the fourth and final consultation cycle, all nine items achieved an I-CVI of 1 for both representativeness and clarity. At the conclusion of this cycle, all 80 items were fully validated by the expert committee (cf. Table 1). The final set of content-validated items is presented in Table S3 in the supplementary material.
    81→Study 2: Questionnaire accessibility test
    82→Method
    83→Ethical Approval
    84→Ethical approval for testing the accessibility of the battery of questionnaires developed for PwID was obtained from the Research Ethics Committee of the XXX (XXX).
    85→Participants and Recruitment Procedure
    86→Two IWSS located in different regions of France (“XXX” and “XXX”) were contacted. These institutions were managed by two distinct associations to ensure heterogeneity in organizational practices. The purpose and objectives of the study were presented to IWSS workers by the department heads or workplace supervisors. A total of 32 PwID at these two IWSS volunteered to participate in the study, and all met the inclusion criteria established by the research team. To be eligible for this testing phase, participants were required to (1) have a diagnosed intellectual disability, (2) be employed at least half-time in an IWSS for a minimum of one month, and (3) have experienced no work stoppage during the month preceding data collection.
    87→Data collection procedure
    88→The questionnaires were administered on-site, in a dedicated room provided by each IWSS, using LimeSurvey on tablets with secure data storage on university servers. Depending on the participants’ reading abilities and levels of autonomy, the questionnaires were administered either individually or in small groups. Participants who were able to read and respond independently completed the questionnaire on their own, whereas those requiring support were assisted by the experimenter, who remained present throughout the session to provide clarification or assistance when needed. 
    89→Informed consent was obtained from all participants, with legal guardian consent secured by institutions when required. Capacity to consent, was assessed using five questions adapted from Arscott (1997). 
    90→In addition to the items validated during the first phase, the questionnaire incorporated eight questions designed to collect demographic (i.e., sex, age, place of residence) and occupational characteristics (i.e., job tenure, work hours, workshop). Based on recommendations provided by the final expert consultation cycle, one item was added to the “mental distance” dimension of burnout and one item to the “affiliation” dimension of recovery experiences, ensuring that three items per dimension were retained. As a result, a total of 82 items assessing interpersonal and work-related variables were administrated to participants. Throughout the administration process, the experimenter systematically recorded any comments, difficulties or signs of misunderstanding expressed by the participants for each item. At the end of the session, participants were invited to provide their overall impressions regarding the accessibility of the questionnaire.
    91→Analysis
    92→All statistical analyses were performed using RStudio® software (Posit team, 2025). First, descriptive analyses were performed to characterize the demographic and occupational characteristics of the participants. Next, several analytical steps were conducted to assess the questionnaire's accessibility, based on the recommendations of Amor et al. (2023) for the development and validation of measurement tools for PwID. Specifically, means and standard deviations were calculated for each item and for each questionnaire. The number of missing responses was examined across all items, corresponding to cases in which participants selected the response option "I don't know or I don't want to answer." Response distributions were also analysed to detect potential acquiescence bias, which is known to be particularly prevalent in this population (Sigstad et al., 2014). Additionally, qualitative comments made by participants during questionnaire administration and noted by the experimenter were examined alongside missing data patterns and the distribution of responses. Finally, the average completion time was calculated for each item, each questionnaire, and for the entire survey. Completion time was considered as an important indicator of accessibility, given that PwID may experience reduced attention spans. All the material used for the data analysis is available on Zenodo.
    93→Results
    94→Characteristics of participants
    95→Among the 32 volunteers who initially participated in the study, three were unable to complete the questionnaire due to insufficient capacity to provide informed consent. The final sample therefore consisted of 29 respondents. The mean age of the participants was 34.03 years (SD = 8.15), with a slight majority being women (52%). Regarding geographic distribution, 38% of participants were from the XXX region and 62% from the XXX region. In terms of living arrangements, more than half lived independently (55%), 28% lived with their families, and 17% lived in a residential care facility. With respect to occupational characteristics, 48% of the participants reported having less than 10 years of professional experience, although 28% were unable to provide this information. Most participants were employed full-time (72%). The majority worked in conditioning workshops (79%). Details of participant characteristics are available on Table S5 on Zenodo.
    96→Questionnaire accessibility indices
    97→Descriptive data of responses
    98→Regarding negative indicators of workplace well-being, participants reported mean scores of 2.4 (SD = 0.70) for burnout and 2.07 (SD = 1.19) for need for recovery. In contrast, higher mean scores were observed for the positive indicators, with an average score of 4.08 (SD = 0.61) for vigor and 3.94 (SD = 0.95) for job satisfaction. Concerning work characteristics, participants reported mean scores of 3.40 (SD = 0.59) for job demands and 3.82 (SD = 0.74) for job resources. Regarding recovery activities, participants reported a mean score of 3.24 (SD = 1.53) for physical dimension, 3.45 (SD = 1.30) for social dimension, 2.14 (SD = 1.51) for creative dimension, 2.52 (SD = 1.43) for mental dimension, 3.52 (SD = 1.38) for virtual dimension, and 2.75 (SD = 1.29) for outdoor dimension. The physical activity assessment revealed that the majority of participants never engaged in vigorous physical activities (62%), and that nearly half never engaged in moderate physical activities (42%). More than half of the participants engaged in light physical activity one day (28%) or two days (28%) per week. However, for most respondents, this light physical activity lasted less than 30 minutes (58%). Finally, mean scores for recovery experiences ranged from 3.51 (SD = 1.20) for mastery to 4.18 (SD = 1.02) for affiliation. Positive affects experienced during the practice reached a mean score of 10 on a scale of 11 (SD = 1.44). Detailed descriptive statistics for all variables are presented in table S6 of the supplementary material.
    99→Regarding completion time, participants took an average of 28 minutes (SD = 9.19) to complete the entire questionnaire (see table S6).
   100→Missing data and data distribution
   101→Among the 82 items, 21 items (≈20%) received one or two "I don't know or I don't want to answer" responses across the 29 completed questionnaires, with six participants missing at least one item (see table S7). Concerning response distributions, the graphical representations (see Figure 1 to 3) showed variability across the full range of the response scale, from zero to five. However, participants displayed a tendency to select the two extreme response options (“0” on the blue scale and “5” on the red scale). 
   102→Participant feedback
   103→During the questionnaire administration, the researcher noted that participants occasionally requested clarification regarding the meaning of specific words or needed certain items to be repeated several times to ensure comprehension. Several participants also reported difficulties when transitioning between frequency-based response scales ("Never" to "Always") and agreement-based response scales ("Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree"). Overall, most participants considered the questionnaire length to be acceptable. However, some participants reported that the questionnaire was somewhat long and that certain questions were challenging to answer. The table summarising the participants' observations and comments is available upon request from the corresponding author.
   104→Questionnaire modifications following accessibility testing
   105→Following the evaluation of the questionnaire's accessibility and a review of expert feedback from the development phase, the items receiving the lowest approval ratings were removed from both the "mental distance" (burnout) and "affiliation" (recovery experience) dimensions. This adjustment was made to maintain a consistent structure of three items per dimension. In addition, to reduce overall completion time - which some participants perceived as excessive - the four questions assessing capacity to provide consent were removed from the final version. These questions were deemed unnecessary in the context of in-person administration, as the presence of the experimenter allows for real-time explanation of the study procedures and assessment of participants’ understanding through direct interaction. Given the difficulties reported by several participants in responding to certain items, it was determined that a member of the research team should be present during questionnaire administration to provide support and answer questions. This support must be provided without rephrasing items and while ensuring that participants’ responses are accurately recorded. Finally, based on observations during the testing phase, the research team decided to standardize the response formats to enhance usability. As most items relied on frequency-based responses, a frequency scale was adopted across the entire questionnaire to facilitate comprehension and reduce cognitive load for respondents.
   106→General discussion 
   107→The primary objective of this research was to develop a battery of questionnaires to assess workplace well-being and its associated factors, specifically tailored to PwID in sheltered employment settings, and to provide initial evidence of its content validity and accessibility. To achieve this goal, two complementary studies were conducted.
   108→The first study involved submitting items from a selection of questionnaires found in scientific literature designed to measure workplace well-being (burnout, need for recovery, vigour, job satisfaction) and its associated factors (i.e., job characteristics and recovery outside of work) to an expert committee for evaluation following the Lynn method (1986). The aim of this stage was to adapt the questionnaire to the specific cognitive and functional characteristics of PwID working in sheltered employment. Throughout the expert consultation cycles, the items were simplified primarily to enhance their clarity. These modifications align with the findings of previous studies conducted on PwID, who also employed simplified language in their questions (Akkerman et al., 2018a, 2018b; Flores et al., 2011). After four iterative cycles of consultation, the 80 items of the developed questionnaire battery were judged both conceptually representative and linguistically clear, and the feedback from the experts were deemed to be exhaustive. The expert panel, composed of both academic researchers and supporting professionals in the field, provided complementary perspectives that substantially enriched the development and refinement of the measurement tool. Expert feedback primarily addressed two key aspects: ensuring alignment with the defined concepts and theoretical framework and adapting item wording and contextual references to the specific characteristics of PwID. This combination of scientific and practical expertise is consistent with Lynn's recommendations regarding the composition of expert committees (Lynn., 1986; Desormeaux-Moreau et al., 2020). 
   109→The second study aimed to assess the accessibility and comprehensibility of the adapted questionnaires among PwID working in IWSS. The developed questionnaire was administered to 32 participants, of whom 29 completed it. Analyses of missing values and response distributions, conducted in line with the recommendations of Amor et al. (2023), yielded encouraging results. Missing responses — corresponding to items for which participants selected "I don't know or I don't want to answer" — were limited, affecting only a small proportion of participants (6 out of 29) and approximatively 20% of the items, suggesting an overall good level of understanding. Examination of response distribution was particularly important given the documented prevalence of acquiescence bias in this population (Sigstad et al., 2014). Contrary to expectations, the results did not indicate a general tendency toward acquiescence, but rather revealed a preference among some participants for extreme response options on the measurement scales (i.e., "Never" to "Always"). However, it did not characterise all participants, many of whom demonstrated nuanced response patterns. In this respect, although several previous studies assessing job satisfaction among PwID have relied on binary responses (e.g., Seltzer et al., 1984; Fornes et al., 2008; Kumin et al., 2015), the retained 5-point response scale appears both relevant and informative. This choice was further supported by participants’ positive feedback regarding the usability of the 5-point scales during questionnaire administration. Then, based on participants' comments, all response formats were subsequently standardized to frequency-based scales to reduce confusion arising from alternating between agreement-based and frequency-based scales. In addition, although participants generally reported that the questionnaire was clear and accessible, the presence of a third person during administration seems necessary. While some participants were able to complete the questionnaire independently, the majority required assistance from the experimenter, either to read the items or to navigate the tablet used to record their responses. This approach is consistent with recent studies assessing job satisfaction among PwID, which have similarly relied on assisted questionnaire administration (e.g., Akkerman et al., 2018a, 2018b; Flores et al., 2021; Lukas et al., 2018). Importantly, to minimise the risk of social desirability bias, such assistance should be provided by a neutral experimenter rather than by family members or on-site professionals as highlighted in the review by Leclerc et al. (2026).
   110→Limits and perspectives
   111→Despite the precautions taken by the research team in developing and testing this questionnaire for PwID, several limitations should be noted. First, at a theoretical level, the selection of variables and indicators was guided by the existing scientific literature, requiring decisions regarding which constructs were considered most relevant. However, other dimensions related to workplace well-being may also warrant investigation in future research. For example, constructs such as work engagement or autonomous/controlled work motivation could provide valuable complementary insights, although to date they have primarily been assessed using generic questionnaires, not specifically validated for this population (e.g., Flores et al., 2021; Di Maggio et al., 2019). 
   112→From a methodological point of view, several additional limitations should be considered. First, although the expert committee included both supporting professionals and researchers in the field, involving target population themwelves in the development process would have been valuable. Indeed, some IWSS workers are trained in the “Easy to Read” method and could have contributed their experiential expertise to improve item clarity through reformulation. Such involvement could have been implemented with the support of IWSS professionals or the experimenter. Unfortunately, it was not feasible in the present study due to limited human resources. Therefore, future efforts to develop questionnaires for this population would therefore benefit from engaging PwID from the earliest stages of questionnaire creation within a participatory research approach. Second, the questionnaire was tested on a relatively small sample of participants, most of whom worked in conditioning workshops and therefore shared specific professional characteristics. It is therefore necessary to extend the survey to people working in other types of workshops, such as food service or laundry, to ensure broader applicability. Moreover, PwID constitute a highly heterogeneous population in terms of cognitive and linguistic abilities (Finlay & Lyons, 2002; Julien-Gauthier et al., 2009), which can lead to varying levels of comprehension. This heterogeneity was evident in the present study, as three participants were unable to complete the questionnaire, whereas others were able to do so entirely and independently. The disparity in respondents' comprehension can be attributed to their varying degrees of intellectual disability (mild to moderate), a factor that is no longer recognised by the IWSS, which instead prioritizes their capabilities and needs. Consequently, this aspect was not directly accessible to the research team. To address this limitation, the research team endeavoured to develop an instrument that would be accessible to a broad range of individuals. Nevertheless, these results highlight the need to administer this questionnaire to a larger sample in order to thoroughly assess its acceptability among PwID in sheltered employment. Moreover, while the present study focused on content validity, additional forms of validity are required to fully establish the questionnaire’s psychometric soundness and operational usefulness. Future research should therefore test the battery of questionnaire on a larger and more heterogeneous sample of people working in different workshops within IWSS, in order to conduct robust psychometric analyses, particularly regarding construct validity and reliability. Finally, although the presence of the experimenter during questionnaire administration was carefully controlled (i.e., reading items without rephrasing or prompting responses), it may still have influenced participants' responses. Future developments could therefore explore more inclusive modes of administration, such as digital platforms that support greater autonomy — for example, by providing audio playback of items for participants with limited reading abilities and more accessible user interfaces.
   113→Applications 
   114→The development of this questionnaire offers promising perspectives for both the scientific community and supporting professionals in IWSS and sheltered employment more broadly. The methodological approach adopted in this study may serve as a model for other researchers seeking to develop measurement tools specifically adapted to PwID, a population that remains underrepresented in health research (Bishop et al., 2024). In the longer term, this questionnaire could provide the scientific community with a reliable measurement tool to strengthen empirical evidence on workplace well-being, a key determinant of job retention among this population (Fornes et al., 2008). From a practical perspective, its implementation by professionals in the field could facilitate the assessment of workplace well-being among PwID and support the design or adjustment of workplace interventions aimed at promoting mental health and sustainable employment. 
   115→Conclusion
   116→The development process of the battery of questionnaires designed to measure workplace well-being and associated factors among PwID established the content validity of its 80 items. These items measure burnout, need for recovery, job satisfaction, vigor, workers’ job demands and job resources, and their recovery activities and experiences. The observations and analyses conducted during the pilot phase with PwID provide initial evidence of the battery of questionnaires' accessibility. Future research should involve testing the questionnaire on a larger and more diverse sample to provide additional psychometric evidence of validity and reliability, thereby supporting its use in both research settings and practical workplace applications.
