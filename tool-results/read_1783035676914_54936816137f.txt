    23→    23→Short title: Predictions in autism: An fMRI meta-analysis
    24→    24→Keywords: autism, predictive processes, fMRI, review, meta-analysis, ALE
    25→    25→ABSTRACT
    26→    26→BACKGROUND: In autism spectrum condition (ASC), altered predictions (priors) or prediction
    27→    27→errors have been hypothesized to increase the influence of bottom-up sensory input,
    28→    28→relative to top-down prior knowledge. Such alterations could account for several autistic
    29→    29→features, but their empirical basis is unclear. In neurotypical individuals (NT), multiple
    30→    30→neuroimaging meta-analyses have aimed to outline domain-general ‘prediction networks’ of
    31→    31→the brain. However, there has not been a similar meta-analysis in autism.
    32→    32→METHODS: We searched the literature for functional magnetic resonance imaging and
    33→    33→magnetoencephalography studies with autistic participants. The contrasts that explicitly or
    34→    34→implicitly involved the processing of priors/predictions and prediction errors were selected.
    35→    35→Contrasts were divided into those that reported stronger activation in ASC compared to NT
    36→    36→(ASC > NT; 8 contrasts; 139 ASC and 150 NT participants), and vice versa (NT > ASC; 13
    37→    37→contrasts; 261 ASC and 289 NT participants). The convergence of differences between the
    38→    38→groups was then tested using activation likelihood estimation meta-analysis. We additionally
    39→    39→identified 37 contrasts without significant group differences.
    40→    40→RESULTS: ASC > NT did not result in significant convergence. NT > ASC converged in a cluster
    41→    41→in the medial frontal gyrus/cingulate gyrus.
    42→    42→1
    43→    43→
    44→    44→CONCLUSION: We found converging NT > ASC activation differences in an area associated
    45→    45→with error monitoring and uncertainty estimation. Our results are generally consistent with
    46→    46→notions of altered priors/predictions or prediction errors in autism, pointing to differences
    47→    47→at high levels of the information-processing hierarchy. However, we recommend a cautious
    48→    48→interpretation, given the limited number of available contrasts and the high proportion of
    49→    49→null results.
    50→    50→BACKGROUND
    51→    51→Autism spectrum condition (ASC) is defined by a diverse set of behavioural features in both
    52→    52→social and non-social domains, including insistence on sameness and sensory
    53→    53→hypersensitivity (1). Bayesian and predictive coding accounts of autism propose that altered
    54→    54→priors/predictions or prediction errors increase the influence of bottom-up sensory inputs,
    55→    55→relative to top-down prior beliefs (2). This ‘imbalance hypothesis’ (2) could, if correct,
    56→    56→potentially account for several autistic features in both social and non-social domains (3) (4).
    57→    57→One hypothesis of autistic perception is that of broader Bayesian priors in autism (5),
    58→    58→which could be interpreted as an increase in prior variance (6)). In Bayesian perception
    59→    59→models, the cause of noisy sensory input is inferred by multiplying the ‘likelihood’ of the
    60→    60→sensory input given each potential cause, with the ‘prior’ probability of potential causes. The
    61→    61→resulting ‘posterior’ distribution of probabilities of causes, given the sensory input, could
    62→    62→then inform perceptual decision (7) (8) (9). In autism, broader priors would shift posterior
    63→    63→distributions closer to the mean of sensory inputs (6), resulting in a (metaphorically) more
    64→    64→‘literal’ perception. Besides broader priors, it has been pointed out that instead supposing a
    65→    65→decreased variance of likelihoods – that is, less noisy sensory inputs – would have the same
    66→    66→effect (6).
    67→    67→Related accounts of autism are based on predictive coding (3) (4) (10), according to
    68→    68→which perception is generated by a hierarchical model distributed along the cerebral cortex.
    69→    69→Information generated from sensory inputs flow forward in the hierarchy, and model
    70→    70→predictions flow backward. The interpretation of sensory inputs is constrained by
    71→    71→predictions (priors), while the sensory input not explained by predictions, the prediction
    72→    72→error, is used to update them (11). The influence of prediction errors in revising predictions
    73→    73→depends on their estimated reliability (their inverse variance, or ‘precision’) (12). One
    74→    74→hypothesis is that autism is characterized by a uniformly high precision assigned to
    75→    75→prediction errors. Precise errors would increase the updating of predictions, causing them to
    76→    76→be overfitted to the sensory input (4).
    77→    77→Through different mechanisms, these examples of hypotheses of autism suppose an
    78→    78→increased influence of bottom-up sensory input, relative to top-down processing (2).
    79→    79→However, the empirical basis for such an imbalance is unclear. A recent review on autism
    80→    80→and autistic traits, covering behavioral, neuroimaging, and eye tracking evidence, found in
    81→    81→autism a tendency toward impairment in the development of new priors. It was also
    82→    82→observed, however, that overall, less than half of the reviewed findings supported an
    83→    83→imbalance (2). Another review framed in terms of a related proposal (13), that also included
    84→    84→a mix of (partly overlapping, partly different) studies, found that the evidence overall
    85→    85→suggested differences in prediction-related learning and low-level prediction errors (14).
    86→    86→Both studies decided against a meta-analysis, citing as one of the reasons the variety of the
    87→    87→kinds of reviewed evidence (2) (14). The lack of empirical clarity underlines a need to further
    88→    88→2
    89→    89→
    90→    90→investigate proposals of altered priors/predictions (from hereon, ‘predictions’) and
    91→    91→prediction errors in autism, including the neural processes supposed to underlie them.
    92→    92→Meanwhile, predictive processes in general have been studied in neurotypically
    93→    93→developing individuals (NT). Several meta-analyses have aggregated primarily functional
    94→    94→magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) results in an aim to outline domain-general1 ‘prediction
    95→    95→networks’ of the brain (15) (16) (17). Part of these analyses was to combine experiments2
    96→    96→reflecting two separate processes. First, the formation, encoding, or reinforcement of
    97→    97→predictions, and second, the violation of predictions3. Results from the combined analyses
    98→    98→differed between meta-analyses, but converged in approximately similar areas in the right
    99→    99→superior frontal gyrus (16) (17), the left (15) (16) (17)/right (15) (17) inferior frontal gyrus,
   100→   100→the left (15) (16) (17) /right (15) (17) (anterior) insula, the left superior temporal
   101→   101→sulcus/gyrus (15) (17), and the left precuneus (16) (17). Note that because (17) included all
   102→   102→experiments in (15) (16), results between them are partially dependent.
   103→   103→Results identifying ‘prediction networks’ in NTs could inform the investigation of
   104→   104→autism, as differences in associated regions could lend indirect support to the imbalance
   105→   105→hypothesis. However, there has not been a comparable meta-analysis in autism. To this aim,
   106→   106→we used a wide-ranging literature search to identify fMRI and magnetoencephalography
   107→   107→(MEG) studies with autistic participants. From these studies, we selected experiments that
   108→   108→explicitly or implicitly involve the processing of predictions/prediction errors. Then, we
   109→   109→tested the convergence of between-group activation differences (ASC > NT; NT > ASC) in two
   110→   110→activation likelihood estimation (ALE) meta-analyses.

   116→   116→Literature Search
   117→   117→The two previous reviews of the empirical evidence relating to predictions in autism (2) (14)
   118→   118→each included fewer than ten studies using fMRI or MEG. Meanwhile, the authors observed
   119→   119→that they did not necessarily capture all relevant studies, since similar paradigms can be
   120→   120→framed in different terms (2) (14). We instead used a terminology-neutral search, with the
   121→   121→aim to capture all published fMRI and MEG studies with conceptually relevant paradigms,
   122→   122→irrespective of framing.
   123→   123→We used variations on [autism] and [fMRI or MEG] and searched in Medline,
   124→   124→Embase, Web of Science, and PsycInfo for studies published between 1990 and December
   125→   125→2023 (Tables S1-S4). After deduplication, the literature search resulted in k = 6357 studies
   126→   126→(Figure 1). One study (21) outside the main search, but referenced in (14), was added. A
   127→   127→renewed search in July 2025 for more recent publications lead to the addition of one study
   128→   128→(22). Screening of titles in the reference lists of included studies did not lead to any
   129→   129→additions.

   137→   137→Selection
   138→   138→Each study was assessed according to the following criteria.
   139→   139→(a) English language, peer-reviewed, original study.
   140→   140→(b) ASC group and NT group: Of any age, and with any type of autism diagnosis, though not
   141→   141→associated with a specific genetic syndrome.
   142→   142→(c) fMRI or MEG.
   143→   143→(d) Paradigm that explicitly or implicitly involve the formation or violation of predictions:
   144→   144→Irrespective of whether these were already established or acquired within the paradigm,
   145→   145→irrespective of sensory modality, or whether there was an active task or not.
   146→   146→(e) Whole-brain, between-group experiment of two active conditions, or a parametric
   147→   147→modulator, providing sufficient contrast for either prediction formation or violation (Figure
   148→   148→2).
   149→   149→(f) Coordinates of peak activation in Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) or Talairach
   150→   150→space.
   151→   151→Criteria (a)-(d) was assessed by two authors during blind, independent title/abstract
   152→   152→screening. Decisions were intermittently unblinded to assess agreement. Criteria (e)-(f) was
   153→   153→assessed by one author during full-text screening. Decisions on the in- or exclusion of
   154→   154→paradigms and experiments were regularly discussed in a wider author group to establish
   155→   155→consensus.
   156→   156→Object recognition and conceptually similar paradigms were included, if the control
   157→   157→condition consisted of similar image elements as the object condition, and was sufficiently
   158→   158→scrambled or non-figurative (apart from single word-nonword paradigms, which were not
   159→   159→included). Cue-outcome type paradigms were included, if the cue was predictive of the
   160→   160→outcome above chance levels. Congruency-incongruency type paradigms were included, if
   161→   161→the number of congruent trials was equal or larger than incongruent trials – except for
   162→   162→paradigms in which incongruent responses could be explained by communicative intent (for
   163→   163→example irony), which were excluded.
   164→   164→Mismatch negativity effects – first observed with EEG, in oddball paradigms (23) –
   165→   165→have been interpreted in terms of both neuronal adaptation (24) and predictive coding (23).
   166→   166→We included controlled auditory and visual oddball experiments, since their structure
   167→   167→(‘deviant > standard’ or ‘infrequent > frequent’) – under a predictive coding interpretation –
   168→   168→provided sufficient contrast for prediction violation (criterion (e); Figure 2). Meanwhile, we
   169→   169→excluded repetition suppression experiments with the structure ‘early > late’ or ‘non-
   170→   170→repeating > repeating’, since they provided an insufficient corresponding contrast for
   171→   171→prediction formation under criterion (e), regardless of interpretation.

   181→   181→Guided by previous reports of mixed evidence (2), we, besides the positive evidence,

   185→   185→condition] ANOVAs without significant interaction effects, and ANOVAs where follow-up

   198→   198→about that. As a result, experiments from one study (22) were added.
   199→   199→Analysis
   200→   200→ALE analysis tests the spatial convergence across a set of peak activation coordinates (foci)
   201→   201→against a null distribution, with the associated number of individuals (observations)
   202→   202→determining the spatial uncertainty of each focus (25) (26) (27). Foci from dependent
   203→   203→experiments – that is, experiments from the same participant group – were merged if
   204→   204→contributing to the same analysis (27) (20).
   205→   205→We performed the ALE analysis in [GingerALE] v. 3.02, using the smaller
   206→   206→(conservative) gray matter mask and the recommended thresholding (20): voxel-level p <
   207→   207→.001, cluster-level p < .05 (28), family-wise error (FWE) corrected, using 1 000 permutations.
   208→   208→Talairach coordinates were converted to MNI152 in GingerALE with the icbm2tal and
   209→   209→mni2tal transforms. Results were visualized in [MRIcroGL] on the mni152 standard
   210→   210→template.
   211→   211→As null results cannot be represented in an ALE (29), we report them descriptively.
   212→   212→To complement this description, we assessed the robustness of the ALE results by an
   213→   213→adapted Fail-Safe N (FSN) analysis. Here, null results are represented by experiments with a
   214→   214→random foci distribution. The proportion of ‘null experiments’ that can be added before
   215→   215→clusters of significant convergence turn non-significant is tested. If this proportion is lower
   216→   216→than the estimated real proportion of null results, it could indicate a risk for a non-robust
   217→   217→ALE result (29).

   268→   268→DISCUSSION
   269→   269→In this study, we aggregated fMRI results in autism that explicitly or implicitly relate to the
   270→   270→processing of predictions/prediction errors. NT > ASC group activation differences
   271→   271→converged in a cluster in the medial frontal gyrus/cingulate gyrus, overlapping the dorsal
   272→   272→anterior cingulate cortex. The spatial location of differences is interesting from a theoretical
   273→   273→perspective, as dACC has been proposed to have a domain-general role in tracking goal-
   274→   274→related information such as error, conflict, and surprise (52). This information is then
   275→   275→integrated to determine the expected value of control, for example in the
   276→   276→deployment/regulation of attentional resources. This monitoring and control function
   277→   277→explains why dACC is active during a wide range of paradigms, and particularly under new
   278→   278→and complex conditions which require non-automatic control (52).
   279→   279→There are some parallels to this functional description of dACC in predictive coding
   280→   280→perspectives on the role of attention (53). Herein, attentional gain has been interpreted as a
   281→   281→situational increase in the weighting of task-relevant prediction errors (54). The influence of
   282→   282→prediction errors should increase further when unexpected information sufficiently
   283→   283→motivates an update of task-relevant predictions. Whether an outcome is considered
   284→   284→unexpected or not – that is, whether it represents changing contingencies – depends in turn
   285→   285→on the current higher-order estimates of expected uncertainty. In predictive coding
   286→   286→accounts of autism, altered volatility/uncertainty modeling has been a key process of
   287→   287→interest, in which ACC specifically is theorized to be involved (3) (4) (55).
   288→   288→What did the convergence of NT > ASC activation differences in our study mean
   289→   289→more specifically? There were too few experiments to analyze prediction formation and
   290→   290→violation separately (16) (17). Nonetheless, the structure of experiments and distribution of
   291→   291→foci implies that in NTs, there was a comparatively stronger dACC response to outcomes
   292→   292→that conflict with expectation, previous cues, or learning history. Or stated differently – that
   293→   293→under these conditions, dACC activity in autism differentiated less between unexpected and
   294→   294→expected events. This pattern conforms empirically to some previous results in autism from
   295→   295→probabilistic learning. There, behavioral and pupillometric measures indicated a reduced
   296→   296→distinction between expected/unexpected outcomes, coupled with increased estimates of
   297→   297→higher-order volatility (55).
   298→   298→Against this background, converging differences in (d)ACC could be considered
   299→   299→generally consistent with proposals of altered predictions/prediction errors in autism.
   300→   300→However, we note that activation differences alone have limited power to identify an
   301→   301→underlying mechanism. To illustrate, the NT > ASC result could be interpreted to reflect
   302→   302→uniformly precise prediction errors in autism, regardless of whether outcomes could be
   303→   303→considered expected or not. But the same observed difference could also be interpreted to
   304→   304→support broader Bayesian priors, to the extent that violations of weaker priors would
   305→   305→generate comparatively weaker prediction errors. These interpretations rest on opposite
   306→   306→assumptions of the strength of baseline error signaling, while activation differences itself
   307→   307→could be consistent with either.
   308→   308→It should also be noted that while our results show converging differences at high
   309→   309→levels of the information-processing hierarchy, they do not necessarily provide strong
   310→   310→evidence against differences in other areas, such as the sensory cortices. The reason is that
   311→   311→the domain-independent scope of our meta-analysis inherently should bias convergence to
   312→   312→7
   313→   313→
   314→   314→areas specifically with integrative functions (corresponding to high levels (11) of the
   315→   315→generative model in the predictive coding framework).
   316→   316→Given that dACC has such a general function, why then has it (or ACC generally) not
   317→   317→consistently been identified as part of a ‘prediction network’ in previous ALEs in NTs (15)
   318→   318→(16)? One explanation could be that ACC is likely generally more sensitive to many
   319→   319→prediction violation, as compared to formation, experiments. Depending on selection
   320→   320→criteria, ACC convergence could potentially have been suppressed in some combined
   321→   321→analyses. This reasoning is supported by ACC consistently being identified in other meta-
   322→   322→analyses focused only on prediction error (56) (57) (58) (59). Note however, that in (15) and
   323→   323→(16), contrast/separate analysis of prediction violation specifically did not identify ACC
   324→   324→either. These differences underline the need for sub-analyses, where in our study we were
   325→   325→restricted by the low numbers of available experiments.
   326→   326→There was no significant convergence of ASC > NT differences, possibly reflecting a
   327→   327→lack of power. Is there a systematic reason for the fewer significant ASC > NT experiments,
   328→   328→compared to NT > ASC? Given the specific sampling of prediction-related paradigms, one
   329→   329→possible interpretation is that autistic individuals were overall less sensitive to the contrasts
   330→   330→between conditions. While, again, the underlying mechanism cannot be inferred, such a
   331→   331→pattern would also be generally consistent with a reduced differentiation between
   332→   332→expected/unexpected outcomes that these and others’ results point to. Another, less
   333→   333→specific contributing factor could be a previously observed increase in variability in brain
   334→   334→activity (60)/connectivity (61) between autistic individuals. Such a pattern could also
   335→   335→indirectly decrease the probability of significant effects in ASC over NTs.
   336→   336→Guided by prior reports of mixed evidence for a predictive imbalance in autism (2),
   337→   337→we also described the counterevidence to group differences in brain activation. The share of
   338→   338→null results, approximately three-fifths, seems to at a minimum complement the overall
   339→   339→observation by (2) – though a precise comparison of figures is made difficult by differences
   340→   340→in the definition of relevant data. From this selection of studies, it appears that the fMRI
   341→   341→evidence for generally altered predictions/prediction errors in autism is, at least,
   342→   342→inconsistent. While sample sizes were relatively similar between studies with and without
   343→   343→significant findings, we cannot rule out that differences in statistical thresholding can have
   344→   344→played a systematic role in separating them. In addition to the general implications,
   345→   345→robustness analysis showed that the observed proportion of null findings warrants a careful
   346→   346→interpretation of our ALE results. Overall, this picture could be indicative of a previously
   347→   347→stated need to develop more specific hypotheses (2) (14).
   348→   348→A subset of included studies used behavioral measures that could, for our purposes,
   349→   349→strengthen confidence in the theoretical relevance of activation results. Many of the studies
   350→   350→leveraged in our analysis, however, were not designed to investigate predictions/prediction
   351→   351→errors specifically. To allow for additional validation and inference on mechanisms,
   352→   352→activation results would optimally be supplemented by a combination of behavioral
   353→   353→measures and (like previously emphasized (2)) appropriate computational modeling. To take
   354→   354→one example, (62) modelled the influence of a hierarchy of priors and prediction errors on
   355→   355→task behavior and then correlated it with brain activation results.
   356→   356→We agree with the authors of previous reviews in hoping that the field will continue
   357→   357→to develop (2) (14), so that sufficient numbers of similarly specialized studies will allow for a
   358→   358→more refined meta-analysis in the future. Relative to many of the rather indirect measures
   359→   359→of predictions/prediction errors leveraged here, such studies could potentially have more
   360→   360→8
   361→   361→
   362→   362→power to detect relevant differences. If so, it is possible that they also could yield fewer null
   363→   363→results.
   364→   364→Limitations
   365→   365→The main limitation of this meta-analysis is the small number of included experiments. First,
   366→   366→few experiments restrict power to detect differences and to do sub-analyses. Second, it
   367→   367→incurs a risk that a single or a few influential experiments drive results. Simulation work on
   368→   368→ALE analysis using FWE correction indicates that at least 17-20 experiments should be
   369→   369→included per analysis to mitigate this risk (28). After merging dependent experiments, the
   370→   370→maximum number we reached in any between-group analysis was 13. The limited amount
   371→   371→of positive evidence restricts our ability to draw strong conclusions from the results, which
   372→   372→should be replicated with a larger sample size.
   373→   373→A second important limitation is the heterogeneity of participant age groups in the
   374→   374→included studies. Restricting the study sample to only children, adolescents, or adults would
   375→   375→have limited it even further – but on the other hand, including all groups could negatively
   376→   376→affect power in different ways. For example, in autism, there are age-dependent patterns of
   377→   377→resting state connectivity differences (63), which conceivably could affect task-related brain
   378→   378→activation (64) (65). It is therefore possible that diverging differences between age cohorts
   379→   379→could have indirectly censored age-dependent ASC > NT and NT > ASC effects.
   380→   380→Conclusions
   381→   381→We have presented an ALE meta-analysis of fMRI results that explicitly or implicitly relate to
   382→   382→the processing of predictions/prediction errors in autism. Our results show converging NT >
   383→   383→ASC activation differences in ACC, a region associated with error monitoring and uncertainty
   384→   384→estimation. The structure of contributing experiments indicates that this effect could have
   385→   385→been driven by a reduced differentiation between expected and unexpected outcomes in
   386→   386→autism, at least under some conditions. Such a reduction would be consistent with
   387→   387→hypotheses of altered prediction/prediction errors generally (see for example (55)),
   388→   388→although we cannot infer by what mechanism.
   389→   389→Meanwhile, concerns over generalizability are raised by the small number of
   390→   390→available experiments – increasing the risk of chance convergence – and, like previously
   391→   391→observed (2), a high proportion of null results. For a conclusive and detailed assessment,
   392→   392→additional neuroimaging studies specifically targeting predictions/prediction errors in
   393→   393→autism are needed. Optimally, these studies should include behavioral measures and
   394→   394→computational modelling to inform the interpretation of brain activation results.
