    61‚Üí    61‚ÜíAbstract
    62‚Üí    62‚ÜíStress beliefs‚Äîgeneral expectations about the nature and consequences of stress‚Äîhave been
    63‚Üí    63‚Üílinked to health and performance, yet their role in shaping responses to everyday stress
    64‚Üí    64‚Üíremains less understood. This study examined whether stress beliefs moderate affective
    65‚Üí    65‚Üíreactivity and stress regulation strategies in response to naturally occurring stressors in daily
    66‚Üí    66‚Üílife. In a 7-day ecological momentary assessment (EMA) study, 104 university students
    67‚Üí    67‚Üícompleted thrice-daily reports (1,939 observations) on social and work-related stressors,
    68‚Üí    68‚Üípositive and negative affect, and two stress regulation strategies‚Äîsituation selection and
    69‚Üí    69‚Üíresponse modification‚Äîas proposed in the Stress Optimization Model (Crum, Jamieson, &
    70‚Üí    70‚ÜíAkinola, 2020). At baseline, stress beliefs were assessed alongside variables to characterize
    71‚Üí    71‚Üíthe sample. Negative stress beliefs moderated the association between daily stressors and
    72‚Üí    72‚Üípositive affect: individuals with high negative stress beliefs showed reduced positive affect
    73‚Üí    73‚Üíduring social stress and no increase in positive affect under higher workload. In contrast,
    74‚Üí    74‚Üíthose with low or moderate negative stress beliefs maintained positive affect during social
    75‚Üí    75‚Üístress and showed increased positive affect under higher workload‚Äîconsistent with a
    76‚Üí    76‚Üíchallenge-like appraisal. Additionally, controllability beliefs buffered increases in negative
    77‚Üí    77‚Üíaffect in response to daily social stress, with lower beliefs linked to stronger affective
    78‚Üí    78‚Üíreactivity. In contrast, stress beliefs did not moderate associations between daily stressors and
    79‚Üí    79‚Üíthe investigated stress regulation strategies. These findings suggest that stress beliefs tune
    80‚Üí    80‚Üíaffective reactivity to everyday social and work stressors, identifying them as a potential
    81‚Üí    81‚Üíintervention target given the established links between affect and long-term health. The
    82‚Üí    82‚Üígeneralizability of these findings should be confirmed across broader populations.
    83‚Üí    83‚ÜíKeywords: stress beliefs; stress mindset; affective reactivity; stress regulation; ecological
    84‚Üí    84‚Üímomentary assessment

    87‚Üí    87‚ÜíStress Beliefs Shape Daily Affective Reactivity to Social and Work Stressors: A 7-
    88‚Üí    88‚ÜíDay Ecological Momentary Assessment Study
    89‚Üí    89‚ÜíThe stress response is vital tool of adaption, enabling individuals to meet the
    90‚Üí    90‚Üímultifaceted demands imposed by the environment. When individuals experience or
    91‚Üí    91‚Üíanticipate demands in goal-related contexts‚Äîsuch as danger, conflict, or pressure (i.e.,
    92‚Üí    92‚Üístressors)‚Äîthe body responds with relatively nonspecific emotional, behavioral, and
    93‚Üí    93‚Üíphysiological changes (i.e., the stress response(Crum et al., 2020)). This stress response can
    94‚Üí    94‚Üíhelp individuals survive and thrive by mobilizing resources needed to meet current demands.
    95‚Üí    95‚ÜíAt the same time, it is well established that stress can also be harmful to mental and physical
    96‚Üí    96‚Üíhealth (McEwen, 1998; O‚ÄôConnor et al., 2021). The health impact of stress is contingent upon
    97‚Üí    97‚Üínumerous factors, including characteristics of the stressor itself‚Äîsuch as its content (e.g.,
    98‚Üí    98‚Üísocial vs. workload-related demands), intensity, and chronicity‚Äîwhich have been shown to
    99‚Üí    99‚Üíinfluence the magnitude and quality of stress responses and their longer-term health
   100‚Üí   100‚Üíconsequences (Almeida, 2005; Dickerson & Kemeny, 2004; McEwen, 1998). In addition,
   101‚Üí   101‚Üístress outcomes depend on person-related factors, including how stressors are appraised and
   102‚Üí   102‚Üíhow individuals attempt to influence or modify their stress responses (i.e., stress regulation;
   103‚Üí   103‚Üí(Crum et al., 2020; Lazarus & Folkman, 1984; McEwen, 1998).
   104‚Üí   104‚ÜíStress Beliefs: Conceptualization and Impact on Health and Performance
   105‚Üí   105‚ÜíA new perspective in explaining health and performance under stress that has
   106‚Üí   106‚Üírecently garnered attention is the concept of stress beliefs. Stress beliefs encapsulate an
   107‚Üí   107‚Üíindividual‚Äôs expectations and beliefs regarding the nature and consequences of stress
   108‚Üí   108‚Üí(Jamieson, Crum, et al., 2018; Kilby et al., 2020; Laferton et al., 2018). For instance,
   109‚Üí   109‚Üíindividuals harboring negative stress beliefs, perceive stress as deleterious to performance and
   110‚Üí   110‚Üíhealth, while others who hold positive stress beliefs, tend to view stress as a catalyst for
   111‚Üí   111‚Üíreaching one‚Äôs full potential. Such beliefs or expectations have been studied at a situation-
   112‚Üí   112‚Üí
   113‚Üí   113‚Üí6
   114‚Üí   114‚Üíspecific level, often termed stress reappraisal‚Äîthat is, appraising the acute stress response in
   115‚Üí   115‚Üía given situation as a helpful resource rather than an added demand (Jamieson et al., 2013;
   116‚Üí   116‚ÜíJamieson, Crum, et al., 2018). They have also been examined at a more generalized level,
   117‚Üí   117‚Üítermed stress beliefs or stress mindset‚Äîthe belief that stress is generally helpful or harmful
   118‚Üí   118‚Üífor health and performance (Crum et al., 2013; Fischer et al., 2016; Jamieson, Crum, et al.,
   119‚Üí   119‚Üí2018; Laferton et al., 2018). Stress beliefs have most frequently been assessed using the Stress
   120‚Üí   120‚ÜíMindset Measure (SMM; (Crum et al., 2013) or the Beliefs About Stress Scale (BASS;
   121‚Üí   121‚Üí(Laferton et al., 2018). The SMM conceptualizes stress beliefs as a largely unidimensional
   122‚Üí   122‚Üíconstruct, capturing the extent to which individuals view stress as generally enhancing versus
   123‚Üí   123‚Üídebilitating for health and performance. In contrast, the BASS differentiates stress beliefs into
   124‚Üí   124‚Üíthree interrelated facets: negative stress beliefs, positive stress beliefs, and beliefs about the
   125‚Üí   125‚Üícontrollability of stress (Laferton et al., 2018). Notably, several psychometric evaluations of
   126‚Üí   126‚Üícultural adaptations of the SMM have identified two strongly correlated but distinguishable
   127‚Üí   127‚Üífactors reflecting positive and negative aspects of stress beliefs (Florean et al., 2025; Iwamoto
   128‚Üí   128‚Üíet al., 2020; Karampas et al., 2020; Park, 2022; Tedadi et al., 2022), suggesting that these
   129‚Üí   129‚Üíbelief components may not always be fully captured by a single latent dimension across
   130‚Üí   130‚Üícontexts. This structural differentiation conceptually aligns with the separation of positive and
   131‚Üí   131‚Üínegative stress beliefs assessed by the BASS. Beyond valence-related beliefs, the BASS
   132‚Üí   132‚Üíadditionally incorporates beliefs about the controllability of stress, a dimension that is
   133‚Üí   133‚Üísupported by broader theoretical and empirical work highlighting the role of perceived control
   134‚Üí   134‚Üíand coping efficacy in shaping stress responses and outcomes (Kilby et al., 2020) and by the
   135‚Üí   135‚Üídevelopment of newer multidimensional stress belief measures that explicitly include control-
   136‚Üí   136‚Üírelated components (e.g., STRESS; (Kilby et al., 2022). Empirically supporting this
   137‚Üí   137‚Üíconceptual distinction, initial evidence from a sample of 110 university students indicated
   138‚Üí   138‚Üístrong convergent validity of the SMM with both the negative (BASS-N; r =.76) and positive
   139‚Üí   139‚Üístress belief subscales (BASS-P; r =.72) of the BASS, whereas only a moderate correlation
   140‚Üí   140‚Üí
   141‚Üí   141‚Üí7
   142‚Üí   142‚Üíemerged with the controllability subscale (BASS-C; r = .47; (Laferton & Fischer, 2021).
   143‚Üí   143‚ÜíConsequently, this pattern underscores that while BASS-P and BASS-N closely align with the
   144‚Üí   144‚Üícore dimensions of general stress mindsets, BASS-C captures a related but distinct theoretical
   145‚Üí   145‚Üífacet. Crucially, a recent large-scale validation in a representative sample of the German
   146‚Üí   146‚Üígeneral population (N = 2515; (Schmalbach et al., 2025) confirmed a bifactor structure of the
   147‚Üí   147‚ÜíBASS. This indicates that the BASS captures a strong general stress mindset factor‚Äî
   148‚Üí   148‚Üíconceptually equivalent to the unidimensional SMM‚Äîalongside more specific belief
   149‚Üí   149‚Üídimensions, such as controllability, that capture additional, theoretically meaningful variance.
   150‚Üí   150‚ÜíRecent research has uncovered persuasive evidence that stress beliefs can influence
   151‚Üí   151‚Üíhealth, well-being and performance in a diverse set of populations: In two large general
   152‚Üí   152‚Üípopulation samples, beliefs that stress is harmful was associated with increased mortality over
   153‚Üí   153‚Üíthe course of several years (Keller et al., 2012; Nabi et al., 2013). Related work, primarily
   154‚Üí   154‚Üíusing the Stress Mindset Measure, has shown that holding a stress-is-debilitating mindset is
   155‚Üí   155‚Üíassociated with higher levels of depressiveness, anxiety, somatic symptoms, and distress
   156‚Üí   156‚Üíamong adolescents (Park et al., 2018), as well as college students, employees (Casper et al.,
   157‚Üí   157‚Üí2017; Crum et al., 2013), police officers (Keech et al., 2020), and chronic pain patients
   158‚Üí   158‚Üí(Gr√ºnenwald et al., 2023). Moreover, viewing stress as debilitating has been linked to
   159‚Üí   159‚Üídecreased academic and social-evaluative performance, poorer academic outcomes in
   160‚Üí   160‚Üíundergraduate students (Keech et al., 2018), and to lower vigor and task performance in
   161‚Üí   161‚Üíemployees (Casper et al., 2017; Crum et al., 2013). Importantly, conceptually comparable
   162‚Üí   162‚Üíassociations have also been observed using the Beliefs About Stress Scale. Specifically,
   163‚Üí   163‚Üínegative stress beliefs were associated with greater somatic symptom burden and higher
   164‚Üí   164‚Üínegative affect in university students (Fischer et al., 2016; Laferton et al., 2020), as well as
   165‚Üí   165‚Üíwith poorer mental health among healthcare workers during the COVID-19 pandemic
   166‚Üí   166‚Üí(Laferton et al., 2024; Wekenborg et al., 2024). In contrast, positive stress beliefs have been
   167‚Üí   167‚Üílinked to better mental health in healthcare workers (Bai et al., 2024), better mental health in
   168‚Üí   168‚Üí
   169‚Üí   169‚Üí8
   170‚Üí   170‚Üíthe general population (Schmalbach et al., 2025), and higher vitality in employees (Digutsch
   171‚Üí   171‚Üíet al., 2022). Beyond valence-related beliefs, beliefs about the controllability of stress have
   172‚Üí   172‚Üíalso been associated with better mental health outcomes in healthcare workers (Bai et al.,
   173‚Üí   173‚Üí2024, 2025; Wekenborg et al., 2024) and in population-based samples (Schmalbach et al.,
   174‚Üí   174‚Üí2025).
   175‚Üí   175‚ÜíTaken together, accumulating evidence indicates that different components of stress
   176‚Üí   176‚Üíbeliefs‚Äîincluding negative, positive, and controllability-related beliefs‚Äîare systematically
   177‚Üí   177‚Üíassociated with health, well-being, and performance outcomes across populations. This raises
   178‚Üí   178‚Üía mechanistic question as to how stress beliefs shape individuals‚Äô experiences and actions in
   179‚Üí   179‚Üístressful situations in ways that may give rise to documented differences in mortality, mental
   180‚Üí   180‚Üíand physical symptom burden, and performance. Prior work suggests that alterations in
   181‚Üí   181‚Üíaffective stress responses and in stress regulation strategies represent two candidate pathways
   182‚Üí   182‚Üíthrough which stress beliefs may exert these effects (Crum et al., 2020; Jamieson, Crum, et
   183‚Üí   183‚Üíal., 2018; Jamieson, Hangen, et al., 2018; Yeager et al., 2022).
   184‚Üí   184‚ÜíStress beliefs and altered affective stress responses
   185‚Üí   185‚ÜíRegarding the affective stress response several experimental studies provided
   186‚Üí   186‚Üíevidence that inducing positive stress beliefs can enhance positive and reduce negative affect
   187‚Üí   187‚Üíin response to a laboratory stressor (Beltzer et al., 2014; Brooks, 2014; Crum et al., 2017).
   188‚Üí   188‚ÜíInitial experience sampling studies suggest that individuals‚Äô (unmanipulated) stress beliefs
   189‚Üí   189‚Üíinfluence the affective stress response to real world stressors. In a 10-day daily diary study,
   190‚Üí   190‚Üíuniversity students‚Äô stress beliefs prospectively moderated the affective stress response to
   191‚Üí   191‚Üídaily stressors (Laferton et al., 2020). Individuals with high negative stress beliefs responded
   192‚Üí   192‚Üíwith a stronger increase in negative affect in response to daily social stressors than students
   193‚Üí   193‚Üíwith low negative stress beliefs. Similarly, negative stress beliefs predicted poorer affect in
   194‚Üí   194‚Üíresponse to work stressors throughout a five-day daily diary assessment (Daniels et al., 2006).
   195‚Üí   195‚Üí
   196‚Üí   196‚Üí9
   197‚Üí   197‚ÜíFurthermore, affect itself was associated with altered information processing, physiological
   198‚Üí   198‚Üífunctioning, coping and health behavior (Aldao et al., 2010; DeSteno et al., 2013; Diener et
   199‚Üí   199‚Üíal., 2017; Gross et al., 2019; Pressman et al., 2018; Salovey et al., 2000; Sin, 2016). To
   200‚Üí   200‚Üísummarize, stress beliefs may alter health and performance outcomes by shaping affective
   201‚Üí   201‚Üístress reactions that operate through the above-mentioned pathways.
   202‚Üí   202‚ÜíAt the same time, it is important to note that the extent to which stress beliefs shape
   203‚Üí   203‚Üíaffective responses may depend on the nature of the stressor itself. Research indicates that
   204‚Üí   204‚Üístressor content might influences affective and physiological stress responses (Almeida,
   205‚Üí   205‚Üí2005). For example, experimental work has demonstrated that social-evaluative stressors
   206‚Üí   206‚Üíelicit stronger affective and neuroendocrine responses than non-social stressors(Dickerson &
   207‚Üí   207‚ÜíKemeny, 2004). In studies of daily stress, the most frequently reported stressors are
   208‚Üí   208‚Üíinterpersonal (social) stressors and work- or education-related stressors (Almeida et al., 2002).
   209‚Üí   209‚ÜíConsistent with experimental research, these stressor classes seem to differ in their
   210‚Üí   210‚Üípsychological impact. Several diary and experience sampling studies suggest that
   211‚Üí   211‚Üíinterpersonal stressors tend to be associated with stronger emotional reactivity than workload-
   212‚Üí   212‚Üírelated stressors, including larger increases in negative affect or distress (Bolger et al., 1989;
   213‚Üí   213‚ÜíBolger & Schilling, 1991; Kerber et al., 2026), and show more consistent associations with
   214‚Üí   214‚Üíinternalizing symptoms in daily life (Zimmermann et al., 2019). Similar differential patterns
   215‚Üí   215‚Üíhave been observed in ambulatory assessment studies comparing interpersonal and non-
   216‚Üí   216‚Üíinterpersonal stressors with respect to affective states and physical symptoms (Almeida, 2005;
   217‚Üí   217‚ÜíLeger et al., 2021).
   218‚Üí   218‚ÜíImportantly, there are also indications that the association between stressor exposure
   219‚Üí   219‚Üíand affective responses differs in the extent to which it is shaped by more time-stable
   220‚Üí   220‚Üíindividual characteristics. Prior work suggests that dispositional factors such as neuroticism
   221‚Üí   221‚Üí(Bolger & Schilling, 1991) and depressive symptomatology (Sheets & Armey, 2020) more
   222‚Üí   222‚Üístrongly moderate affective responses to interpersonal stressors than to workload-related
   223‚Üí   223‚Üí
   224‚Üí   224‚Üí10
   225‚Üí   225‚Üídemands. Moreover, initial intervention-based ambulatory evidence indicates that changes in
   226‚Üí   226‚Üístressor‚Äìaffect associations following stress-related interventions may differ between
   227‚Üí   227‚Üíinterpersonal and workload stressors (Leger et al., 2021). Taken together, these findings
   228‚Üí   228‚Üísuggest that stressor type may be particularly relevant when examining how relatively stable
   229‚Üí   229‚Üíbelief systems‚Äîsuch as stress beliefs‚Äîinteract with daily stressor exposure to shape affective
   230‚Üí   230‚Üíresponses.
   231‚Üí   231‚ÜíConsistent with this distinction, existing daily stress studies examining stress beliefs
   232‚Üí   232‚Üíand affective stress responses have focused on different stressor domains. Evidence for belief-
   233‚Üí   233‚Üírelated differences in affective reactivity has been reported in the context of work-related
   234‚Üí   234‚Üístressors (Daniels et al., 2006; Casper et al., 2017) as well as for social stressors (Laferton et
   235‚Üí   235‚Üíal., 2020). Notably, the only experience sampling study to examine both stressor types
   236‚Üí   236‚Üísimultaneously found that stress beliefs moderated affective responses to social stressors,
   237‚Üí   237‚Üíwhereas the corresponding moderation effect for workload-related stressors did not remain
   238‚Üí   238‚Üístatistically robust after correction for multiple testing (Laferton et al., 2020).
   239‚Üí   239‚ÜíStress beliefs and altered stress regulation strategies
   240‚Üí   240‚ÜíFurther, it is assumed that stress beliefs also shape stress regulation strategies; within
   241‚Üí   241‚Üíthe stress optimization model (Crum et al., 2020), two key proposed facets are situation
   242‚Üí   242‚Üíselection and response modification. Situation selection: individuals with more negative stress
   243‚Üí   243‚Üíbeliefs may be more likely to avoid stressful situations (e.g., declining challenging tasks),
   244‚Üí   244‚Üíwhich over time can lead to missed opportunities and frustration; in contrast, those with more
   245‚Üí   245‚Üípositive stress beliefs may be more inclined to approach stressful situations (e.g., seeking
   246‚Üí   246‚Üíchallenging assignments), thereby increasing opportunities for skill development and goal
   247‚Üí   247‚Üíattainment. Response modification: once in a stressful situation, stress beliefs are
   248‚Üí   248‚Üíhypothesized to guide how individuals regulate their physiological stress response; those who
   249‚Üí   249‚Üíview stress as harmful may attempt to dampen arousal, whereas those endorsing more positive
   250‚Üí   250‚Üí
   251‚Üí   251‚Üí11
   252‚Üí   252‚Üístress beliefs may try to harness their stress response to facilitate goal pursuit. However, direct
   253‚Üí   253‚Üíempirical tests of these stress regulation strategies remain relatively sparse, and much of the
   254‚Üí   254‚Üíexisting work has been conducted in laboratory settings. In support of situation selection,
   255‚Üí   255‚Üíuniversity students with more positive stress beliefs showed a greater desire for feedback
   256‚Üí   256‚Üíbefore a social-evaluative laboratory stressor (Crum et al., 2013). Outside the laboratory,
   257‚Üí   257‚Üíhowever, only a small number of studies have examined associations between stress beliefs
   258‚Üí   258‚Üíand stress regulation strategies in daily life, and these studies have relied on relatively broad
   259‚Üí   259‚Üíoperationalizations of approach- versus avoidance-oriented stress regulation strategies that do
   260‚Üí   260‚Üínot allow for a specific examination of situation selection and response modification. For
   261‚Üí   261‚Üíexample, in a five-day daily diary study, Casper et al. (2017) found that a stress is enhancing
   262‚Üí   262‚Üímindset was associated with higher approach-oriented coping among employees anticipating
   263‚Üí   263‚Üíhigh workload. Similarly, cross-sectional studies have reported associations between stress
   264‚Üí   264‚Üíbeliefs and general approach‚Äìavoidance stress regulation tendencies (Keech et al., 2018,
   265‚Üí   265‚Üí2020; Zhang et al., 2022). Taken together, existing findings provide initial evidence that stress
   266‚Üí   266‚Üíbeliefs might be related to stress regulation tendencies in general. However, beyond the
   267‚Üí   267‚Üílaboratory evidence reported by Crum et al. (2013), there is little empirical work that
   268‚Üí   268‚Üíexplicitly examines specific stress regulation strategies as conceptualized in the stress
   269‚Üí   269‚Üíoptimization model‚Äînamely situation selection and response modification. Moreover, no
   270‚Üí   270‚Üístudy so far has assessed situation selection and response modification in close temporal
   271‚Üí   271‚Üíproximity to naturally occurring daily stressors, leaving it largely unclear whether and how
   272‚Üí   272‚Üístress beliefs shape these stress regulation processes as stress unfolds in everyday life.
   273‚Üí   273‚ÜíIn conclusion, emerging evidence supports several psychological mechanisms
   274‚Üí   274‚Üílinking stress beliefs to health and performance outcomes. However, the majority of those
   275‚Üí   275‚Üístudies relied on experimentally manipulated stress beliefs and laboratory stressors, thereby
   276‚Üí   276‚Üílimiting their ecological validity. Whether these processes similarly influence health and
   277‚Üí   277‚Üíperformance when stress beliefs are naturally acquired and in the occurrence of everyday-life
   278‚Üí   278‚Üí
   279‚Üí   279‚Üí12
   280‚Üí   280‚Üístressors, remains unclear. Therefore, this study aimed to investigate how different
   281‚Üí   281‚Üícomponents of stress beliefs‚Äînegative beliefs, positive beliefs, and beliefs about the
   282‚Üí   282‚Üícontrollability of stress‚Äîare associated with affective stress responses and stress regulation
   283‚Üí   283‚Üístrategies (situation selection and response modification) in response to daily stressors using
   284‚Üí   284‚Üían ecological momentary assessment (EMA) design. Based on prior evidence linking
   285‚Üí   285‚Üínegative, positive and controllability stress beliefs to mental health and affective well-being
   286‚Üí   286‚Üíacross populations (Bai et al., 2024; Fischer et al., 2016; Laferton et al., 2024; Schmalbach et
   287‚Üí   287‚Üíal., 2025), as well as initial experience sampling studies demonstrating that stress beliefs
   288‚Üí   288‚Üímoderate affective reactivity to daily stressors (Daniels et al., 2006; Laferton et al., 2020), we
   289‚Üí   289‚Üíexpected stress beliefs to be systematically associated with affective responses to daily stress.
   290‚Üí   290‚ÜíSpecifically, higher levels of negative stress beliefs were expected to be associated with
   291‚Üí   291‚Üístronger increases in negative affect and attenuated positive affect in response to daily social
   292‚Üí   292‚Üíand workload stressors. In contrast, higher levels of positive stress beliefs and stronger beliefs
   293‚Üí   293‚Üíabout the controllability of stress were expected to be associated with lower increases in
   294‚Üí   294‚Üínegative affect and higher levels of positive affect when individuals encountered daily
   295‚Üí   295‚Üístressors.
   296‚Üí   296‚ÜíFurthermore, drawing on the stress optimization model (Crum et al., 2020) and initial
   297‚Üí   297‚Üíempirical evidence (Crum et al., 2013; Casper et al., 2017), this study examines whether stress
   298‚Üí   298‚Üíbeliefs are associated with stress regulation strategies in response to daily stressors.
   299‚Üí   299‚ÜíSpecifically, higher levels of negative stress beliefs were expected to be associated with
   300‚Üí   300‚Üígreater avoidance-based situation selection and lower attempts to utilize the stress response
   301‚Üí   301‚Üíwhen individuals encountered daily stress. In contrast, higher levels of positive stress beliefs
   302‚Üí   302‚Üíand stronger beliefs about the controllability of stress were expected to be associated with
   303‚Üí   303‚Üílower avoidance of stressful situations and greater attempts to utilize the stress response.
   304‚Üí   304‚ÜíMethod
   305‚Üí   305‚Üí
   306‚Üí   306‚Üí13
   307‚Üí   307‚ÜíProcedure
   308‚Üí   308‚ÜíUniversity students of three German universities were invited to participate in a 7-
   309‚Üí   309‚Üíday EMA study about stress perception via e-mail lists or research participation platforms
   310‚Üí   310‚Üíbetween June 2022 and July 2023. Inclusion criteria were enrollment as a university student,
   311‚Üí   311‚Üíage ‚â•18 years, absence of chronic disease, and provision of written informed consent.
   312‚Üí   312‚ÜíParticipation was voluntary and as compensation for their efforts, participants had the chance
   313‚Üí   313‚Üíto win gift certificates. Participants were invited to a baseline assessment in the lab including
   314‚Üí   314‚Üíthe baseline questionnaire and a recording of resting heart rate variability. On the following
   315‚Üí   315‚Üíday, participants underwent a 7-day EMA period. EMA was conducted via the smartphone
   316‚Üí   316‚Üíapplication m-path (www.m-path.io ).
   317‚Üí   317‚ÜíParticipants were excluded from the analysis if they did not complete the baseline
   318‚Üí   318‚Üíassessment and missed all of the daily assessments. The study was approved by the local
   319‚Üí   319‚Üíinstitutional review boards of the participating universities. All procedures performed were in
   320‚Üí   320‚Üíaccordance with the ethical standards of the institutional research committees and with the
   321‚Üí   321‚Üí1946 Helsinki declaration and its later amendments.
   322‚Üí   322‚ÜíMeasures
   323‚Üí   323‚ÜíBaseline
   324‚Üí   324‚ÜíStress beliefs were assessed using the Beliefs About Stress Scale (Laferton et al.,
   325‚Üí   325‚Üí2018). On three scales this self-report questionnaire measures negative stress beliefs (BASS-
   326‚Üí   326‚ÜíN; eight items) and positive stress beliefs (BASS-P; four items) as well as perceived control
   327‚Üí   327‚Üí(BASS-C; three items). Example items are as follows: ‚ÄòBeing stressed affects my health in the
   328‚Üí   328‚Üíshort term‚Äô (BASS-N), ‚ÄòBeing stressed enables me to work in a more focused manner‚Äô
   329‚Üí   329‚Üí(BASS-P), or ‚ÄòBeing stressed is something I am able to influence through my actions‚Äô
   330‚Üí   330‚Üí(BASS-C). Items are rated on a 4-point scale from ‚Äòcompletely disagree‚Äò (1) to ‚Äòdefinitely
   331‚Üí   331‚Üíagree‚Äò (4). The BASS has been psychometrically validated in student samples and in a
   332‚Üí   332‚Üí
   333‚Üí   333‚Üí14
   334‚Üí   334‚Üírepresentative sample of the German general population, and normative data for the general
   335‚Üí   335‚Üípopulation are available (Schmalbach et al., 2025).
   336‚Üí   336‚ÜíAt baseline, to describe the situational context of their university studies, participants
   337‚Üí   337‚Üíwere asked to report their study subject, the sum of weekly hours of courses they had signed
   338‚Üí   338‚Üíup for, whether they had a part-time job, and to provide demographic variables (age, gender).
   339‚Üí   339‚ÜíTo screen for symptoms of depression and anxiety, participants completed the depression
   340‚Üí   340‚Üí(PHQ-9) and the anxiety (GAD-7) module of the Patient Health Questionnaire (Kroenke et
   341‚Üí   341‚Üíal., 2010).
   342‚Üí   342‚ÜíEcological Momentary Assessment (EMA)
   343‚Üí   343‚ÜíDuring the 7-day EMA, an interval-contingent sampling design utilizing a
   344‚Üí   344‚Üí"coverage" approach EMA was employed (Shiffman et al., 2008; Stone et al., 2023; Vaessen
   345‚Üí   345‚Üíet al., 2023). Participants were asked to report on stressors, affect and stress regulation
   346‚Üí   346‚Üístrategies three times a day at fixed intervals (noon, 4 pm and 8 pm) with each assessment
   347‚Üí   347‚Üíreferring to the prior 4 hours.
   348‚Üí   348‚ÜíSocial Stressors & Workload Stressors. Participants‚Äô stressors were assessed using
   349‚Üí   349‚Üíitems from the situational experience module of the Personality Dynamics Diary
   350‚Üí   350‚Üí(Zimmermann et al., 2019) PDD). Three PDD items were used to assess social stress (PDD-
   351‚Üí   351‚ÜíSS; ‚ÄòI was ignored or rejected by others‚Äô; ‚ÄòI was blamed, criticized, or devalued by someone‚Äô;
   352‚Üí   352‚Üí‚ÄòI was left in the lurch by a close person‚Äô), and two items to assess workload (PDD-W; ‚ÄòI had
   353‚Üí   353‚Üíto deal with too many, or too difficult tasks‚Äô; ‚ÄòI was under high pressure to succeed while
   354‚Üí   354‚Üígetting done with my tasks‚Äô). Participants endorsed whether each item applied to them during
   355‚Üí   355‚Üíthe 4 hours preceding the EMA on a 4-point scale from 0 = very false to 3 = very true. PDD-
   356‚Üí   356‚ÜíSS and PDD-W scales were calculated using the mean of the respective items of each scale.
   357‚Üí   357‚ÜíAffective Reactivity. Daily positive and negative affect was assessed using the 10-
   358‚Üí   358‚Üíitem short form of the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (Thompson, 2007; Watson et
   359‚Üí   359‚Üí
   360‚Üí   360‚Üí15
   361‚Üí   361‚Üíal., 1988). The PANAS is one of the most widely used self-report questionnaires assessing
   362‚Üí   362‚Üípositive (PANAS-P) and negative affect (PANAS-N). Positive affect (e.g. excited, proud) and
   363‚Üí   363‚Üínegative affect (e.g. upset, afraid) are assessed on two independent five-item scales, with
   364‚Üí   364‚Üíresponses ranging from 1 = ‚Äòvery slightly or not at all‚Äô to 5 = ‚Äòextremely‚Äô. Among other time
   365‚Üí   365‚Üíframes, the PANAS has been validated to assess daily affect (Rush & Hofer, 2014).
   366‚Üí   366‚ÜíStress Regulation. Stress regulation strategies were assessed based on the stress
   367‚Üí   367‚Üíoptimization model (Crum et al., 2020). Within this model, situation selection and response
   368‚Üí   368‚Üímodulation are conceptualized as regulatory strategies that can be flexibly deployed in
   369‚Üí   369‚Üídifferent directions (e.g., avoiding vs. seeking stressful situations; minimizing vs. utilizing
   370‚Üí   370‚Üístress responses), depending on individuals‚Äô regulatory goals. To reduce participant burden
   371‚Üí   371‚Üíand ensure feasibility within the EMA design, we assessed only one option within each
   372‚Üí   372‚Üíregulatory domain. Specifically, situation selection was operationalized via avoidance of
   373‚Üí   373‚Üístressful situations (‚ÄòHow hard have you tried to avoid the stressful situation?‚Äô), and response
   374‚Üí   374‚Üímodulation was operationalized via attempts to utilize the stress response (‚ÄòHow hard have
   375‚Üí   375‚Üíyou tried to utilize your feeling of being stressed?‚Äô). Participants rated to what extent they
   376‚Üí   376‚Üíengaged in situation avoidance and utilization of the stress response regarding stressors during
   377‚Üí   377‚Üíthe preceding 4h of each EMA assessment on a 5-point scale from 0 = not at all, 1= a little, 2
   378‚Üí   378‚Üí= somewhat, 3 = considerably, 4 = extremely.
   379‚Üí   379‚ÜíParticipants
   380‚Üí   380‚Üí106 individuals provided written informed consent after receiving oral and written
   381‚Üí   381‚Üístudy information from a trained investigator. Two participants had to be excluded due to
   382‚Üí   382‚Üísmartphone or app malfunction. N = 104 provided data on baseline assessment and at least
   383‚Üí   383‚Üíone EMA assessment and were therefore included in the final analysis. The mean number of
   384‚Üí   384‚Üícompleted questionnaires (1 baseline questionnaire + 3x7 EMA questionnaires) per
   385‚Üí   385‚Üíparticipant was 19.21 (SD=3.59; Min=2; Max=22).
   386‚Üí   386‚Üí
   387‚Üí   387‚Üí16
   388‚Üí   388‚ÜíSample characteristics are shown in table 1. Participants predominantly identified as
   389‚Üí   389‚Üífemale, German and were university students from a broad range of academic disciplines. The
   390‚Üí   390‚Üímean course load per week was slightly above the average for German university students
   391‚Üí   391‚Üí(German Ministry of Education and Research, 2017) and the majority had a part-time job.
   392‚Üí   392‚ÜíNeither the number of weekly course hours nor the specific academic discipline (STEM and
   393‚Üí   393‚Üímedicine vs. other fields) was associated with baseline stress beliefs, reports of daily
   394‚Üí   394‚Üístressors, affect, or stress regulation strategies (see Supplement 2). Female participants
   395‚Üí   395‚Üíreported a higher number of weekly university course hours. Apart from this difference, no
   396‚Üí   396‚Üígender differences were observed in the study variables. The amount of participants with
   397‚Üí   397‚Üíelevated symptoms of depression and anxiety (see Table 1), is within the range of prevalence
   398‚Üí   398‚Üífound in other university student samples (Ibrahim et al., 2013). Moreover, the mean values of
   399‚Üí   399‚Üínegative, positive and controllability stress beliefs match those found in previous university
   400‚Üí   400‚Üístudent samples (Fischer et al., 2016; Laferton et al., 2020). The mean level of workload was
   401‚Üí   401‚Üícomparable to the validation student sample of the PDD (Zimmermann et al., 2019), while the
   402‚Üí   402‚Üímean level of social stress was somewhat lower in the current sample.
   403‚Üí   403‚ÜíData analysis
   404‚Üí   404‚ÜíAddressing the nested structure of the data, hierarchical linear models were estimated
   405‚Üí   405‚Üíwith repeated data entries at level 1 nested in participants at level 2. First, the fixed effects of
   406‚Üí   406‚Üístress beliefs (level 2: BASS) on each of the EMA variables (level 1: stressors, affect, stress
   407‚Üí   407‚Üíregulation strategy) were analyzed using random intercept models. To reduce
   408‚Üí   408‚Üímulticollinearity, separate random intercept models were estimated for each BASS subscale
   409‚Üí   409‚Üí(BASS-N, BASS-C, BASS-P) on each dependent variable (PDD-SS, PDD-W, PANAS-N,
   410‚Üí   410‚ÜíPANAS-P, situation selection, response modification). Second, the main hypotheses ‚Äì
   411‚Üí   411‚Üíwhether stress beliefs would influence I) affect and II) stress regulation strategies in response
   412‚Üí   412‚Üíto stressors ‚Äì was tested by adding work stress or social stress as a further variable, including
   413‚Üí   413‚Üícross-level interactions with stress beliefs. This resulted in additional random intercept
   414‚Üí   414‚Üí
   415‚Üí   415‚Üí17
   416‚Üí   416‚Üímodels with focal cross-level interaction terms of each stress belief‚Äôs subscale and work stress
   417‚Üí   417‚Üí(BASS-N* PDD-W or BASS-C* PDD-W or BASS-P* PDD-W) or social stress, respectively
   418‚Üí   418‚Üí(BASS-N* PDD-SS or BASS-C* PDD-SS or BASS-P* PDD-SS). Predictors were grand-
   419‚Üí   419‚Üímean centered, while dependent variables were not. To ensure that the cross-level interactions
   420‚Üí   420‚Üíwere due to within- rather than between-person variance in stressors, the within- and between-
   421‚Üí   421‚Üíperson variance in PDD-W and PDD-SS were included as separate predictors. Significant
   422‚Üí   422‚Üíinteractions were probed with simple slopes analyses (i.e., conditional effects at M and ¬±1 SD
   423‚Üí   423‚Üíof the moderator).
   424‚Üí   424‚ÜíFinally, to further elucidate the temporal dynamics and variability of the data, four
   425‚Üí   425‚Üísets of exploratory, non-preregistered analyses were conducted. First, to test whether stress
   426‚Üí   426‚Üíbeliefs alter the general intra-individual variability of affective responses, standard random
   427‚Üí   427‚Üíintercept models with equal residual variances were compared to models with continuous
   428‚Üí   428‚Üívariance structures. These alternative models allowed the residual variance to vary as a
   429‚Üí   429‚Üícontinuous function of stress belief scores. Differences in model fit were evaluated using
   430‚Üí   430‚Üílikelihood-ratio tests (LRT). Second, to examine prospective associations of regulatory
   431‚Üí   431‚Üístrategies within a given day, lagged hierarchical linear models were estimated. In these
   432‚Üí   432‚Üímodels, situation selection and response modification at the preceding time point (T-1)
   433‚Üí   433‚Üípredicted affect and stressors at the subsequent time point (T) within the same day. To yield
   434‚Üí   434‚Üírobust estimates, these models controlled for time of day as well as the autoregressive effect
   435‚Üí   435‚Üí(the respective outcome variable at T-1). Furthermore, the within- and between-person
   436‚Üí   436‚Üívariance in lagged predictors were included as separate predictors. Third, to investigate cross-
   437‚Üí   437‚Üíday spillover effects, day-lagged hierarchical linear models were estimated using day-level
   438‚Üí   438‚Üíaggregated variables. In these models, daily stress and affect from the previous day (Day T‚àí1)
   439‚Üí   439‚Üípredicted the choice of stress regulation strategies (i.e., avoidance and utilization) on the
   440‚Üí   440‚Üícurrent day (Day T). To account for potential systematic changes across the study period,
   441‚Üí   441‚Üíthese models included a linear time trend. Analogous to the within-day analyses, models
   442‚Üí   442‚Üí
   443‚Üí   443‚Üí18
   444‚Üí   444‚Üíadditionally controlled for the autoregressive effect (the respective regulation strategy on Day
   445‚Üí   445‚ÜíT‚àí1), thereby modeling change in the outcome variable. Again, the day-lagged predictors
   446‚Üí   446‚Üíwere disaggregated into within- and between-person variance components. Fourth, to examine
   447‚Üí   447‚Üíwhether study variables showed systematic change across the study period, exploratory
   448‚Üí   448‚Üímultilevel growth curve models were estimated using day-level aggregated variables. For
   449‚Üí   449‚Üídaily workload, social stress, affect, and stress regulation strategies, both linear and quadratic
   450‚Üí   450‚Üítrends across study days were tested. To account for potential scheduling-related variation,
   451‚Üí   451‚Üíselected models were additionally adjusted for weekday.
   452‚Üí   452‚ÜíPrior to data collection, target sample size was determined pragmatically. Because
   453‚Üí   453‚Üíclosed-form power solutions for multilevel models‚Äîparticularly for cross-level
   454‚Üí   454‚Üíinteractions‚Äîare limited and simulation-based approaches require credible variance-
   455‚Üí   455‚Üícomponent estimates that were unavailable a priori, a conventional two-sided t-test for a
   456‚Üí   456‚Üímedium effect (r = 0.50, Œ± = .05, 1‚ÄìŒ≤ = .80) was used as a planning benchmark. Assuming 20
   457‚Üí   457‚Üípercent drop-out it was aimed to include 96 participants into the study.
   458‚Üí   458‚ÜíThe level of significance was set at p <.05 per hypothesis. To account for alpha error
   459‚Üí   459‚Üíinflation the level of significance was divided by the number of tests per preregistered
   460‚Üí   460‚Üíhypothesis (Bonferroni adjustment) in accordance with disjunction hypothesis testing (Rubin,
   461‚Üí   461‚Üí2021); see Supplement 1 for constituent p-levels for each hypothesis). p-values for the
   462‚Üí   462‚Üíhierarchical linear models were estimated using Satterthwaite correction. 99%-confidence
   463‚Üí   463‚Üíintervals were estimated drawing 5000 bootstrap samples. All analyses were performed using
   464‚Üí   464‚ÜíR 4.3.1 (R Core Team, 2023).

   474‚Üí   474‚ÜíDo beliefs about stress predict daily overall levels of stressors, affect and stress
   475‚Üí   475‚Üíregulation strategies?
   476‚Üí   476‚ÜíStress beliefs were not prospectively associated with overall occurrence of social
   477‚Üí   477‚Üístress or workload, levels of positive and negative affect or levels of situation selection and
   478‚Üí   478‚Üíresponse modification (see table 2; for a detailed description of the hierarchical linear models,

   488‚Üí   488‚Üíslope = 0.38; SE=0.31; t = 1.19; p =232; see Figure 1A). Moreover, negative beliefs about

   507‚Üí   507‚ÜíDo beliefs about stress moderate daily stress regulation strategies?
   508‚Üí   508‚ÜíNone of the stress belief dimensions significantly moderated the within-person
   509‚Üí   509‚Üíassociations between daily stressors and stress regulation strategies (Table 3; for a detailed
   510‚Üí   510‚Üídescription of the hierarchical linear models see Supplement 2). Specifically, all cross-level
   511‚Üí   511‚Üíinteractions between negative stress beliefs (BASS-N), positive stress beliefs (BASS-P), and
   512‚Üí   512‚Üíbeliefs about the controllability of stress (BASS-C) and daily social or workload stressors
   513‚Üí   513‚Üípredicting avoidance-based situation selection or attempts to utilize the stress response were
   514‚Üí   514‚Üínon-significant. Thus, stress beliefs were not associated with systematic differences in daily
   515‚Üí   515‚Üístress regulation strategies in response to social or workload stressors.
   516‚Üí   516‚ÜíExploratory analyses on the temporal dynamics and variability of the data
   517‚Üí   517‚ÜíIn addition to mean daily affective stress responses, it was explored whether stress
   518‚Üí   518‚Üíbeliefs systematically alter the day-to-day fluctuations of affective responses. Comparing
   519‚Üí   519‚Üístandard models with equal residual variances to models with continuous variance structures
   520‚Üí   520‚Üí(allowing residual variance to vary as a continuous function of stress belief scores) revealed
   521‚Üí   521‚Üíthat negative stress beliefs did not significantly alter the intra-individual residual variability of
   522‚Üí   522‚Üíaffect (see Supplement 2). However, individuals holding stronger positive stress beliefs
   523‚Üí   523‚Üí(BASS-P) demonstrated significantly less intra-individual variability in daily positive affect
   524‚Üí   524‚Üí
   525‚Üí   525‚Üí21
   526‚Üí   526‚Üí(LRT = 15.81; p < .001, Œ¥ = -0.22). Similarly, higher perceived stress controllability (BASS-
   527‚Üí   527‚ÜíC) significantly predicted less intra-individual variability in daily negative affect (LRT =
   528‚Üí   528‚Üí17.60; p < .001, Œ¥ = -0.43). Thus, while negative stress beliefs did not affect the general day-
   529‚Üí   529‚Üíto-day variability of affect, positive and controllability stress beliefs were systematically
   530‚Üí   530‚Üíassociated with greater emotional stability across days.
   531‚Üí   531‚ÜíFurthermore, exploratory lagged multilevel models were estimated to investigate the
   532‚Üí   532‚Üítemporal dynamics of stressors, affect, and regulatory strategies within a given day (see
   533‚Üí   533‚ÜíSupplement 2). Across all models, strong autoregressive effects were observed, indicating that
   534‚Üí   534‚Üístressors, affective states, and regulatory strategies were highly stable within a day (all ps <
   535‚Üí   535‚Üí.001). Regarding within-day prospective associations of regulatory strategies, only avoidance-
   536‚Üí   536‚Üíbased situation selection at the within-person level predicted a prospective increase in
   537‚Üí   537‚Üínegative affect (Estimate = 0.14, p = .035). All other within-day lagged within-person effects
   538‚Üí   538‚Üíof regulatory strategies on subsequent affect, workload, or social stress were non-significant.
   539‚Üí   539‚ÜíTo investigate cross-day spillover effects, day-lagged hierarchical linear models were
   540‚Üí   540‚Üíestimated predicting current-day stress regulation strategies from prior-day stressors and
   541‚Üí   541‚Üíaffect (see Supplement 2). Across all models, significant autoregressive effects were
   542‚Üí   542‚Üíobserved, indicating moderate stability of regulatory strategies across days (all ps < .001).
   543‚Üí   543‚ÜíRegarding avoidance-based situation selection, no significant within-person prospective
   544‚Üí   544‚Üíeffects of prior-day workload, social stress, positive affect, or negative affect were observed.
   545‚Üí   545‚ÜíIn contrast, regarding attempts to utilize the stress response, higher within-person workload
   546‚Üí   546‚Üíon the previous day significantly predicted a decrease in stress utilization on the subsequent
   547‚Üí   547‚Üíday (Estimate = -0.26, p < .001). Similarly, higher prior-day positive affect predicted lower
   548‚Üí   548‚Üícurrent-day utilization (Estimate = -0.03, p = .037), whereas prior-day social stress and
   549‚Üí   549‚Üínegative affect showed no significant cross-day effects.
   550‚Üí   550‚ÜíFinally, exploratory multilevel growth curve models were estimated to examine
   551‚Üí   551‚Üísystematic change across the study period using day-level aggregated variables (see
   552‚Üí   552‚Üí
   553‚Üí   553‚Üí22
   554‚Üí   554‚ÜíSupplement 2). A significant positive linear trend was observed for workload across study
   555‚Üí   555‚Üídays. Utilization also showed an initial positive linear trend, but this effect was no longer
   556‚Üí   556‚Üísignificant after controlling for workload. No consistent linear or quadratic time trends were
   557‚Üí   557‚Üífound for the remaining variables.
   558‚Üí   558‚ÜíDiscussion
   559‚Üí   559‚ÜíFindings from this EMA study indicate that beliefs about stress shape affective
   560‚Üí   560‚Üíreactivity to daily stressors rather than global levels of stressor exposure or mean affect.
   561‚Üí   561‚ÜíStudents endorsing more negative beliefs about stress showed reduced positive affect when
   562‚Üí   562‚Üíencountering social stress and no increase in positive affect with higher workload. In contrast,
   563‚Üí   563‚Üíthose with low to moderate negative beliefs exhibited no drop in positive affect during social
   564‚Üí   564‚Üístress and demonstrated an increased positive affect under higher workload‚Äîconsistent with a
   565‚Üí   565‚Üímore challenge-like appraisal of task demands. In addition, lower controllability beliefs were
   566‚Üí   566‚Üíassociated with stronger increases in negative affect in response to social stress compared
   567‚Üí   567‚Üíwith medium and high controllability beliefs.
   568‚Üí   568‚ÜíBeyond acute affective reactivity, exploratory analyses further suggest that stress
   569‚Üí   569‚Üíbeliefs may shape the temporal dynamics of affective experiences. Specifically, positive stress
   570‚Üí   570‚Üíbeliefs were associated with reduced intra-individual variability in positive affect, while
   571‚Üí   571‚Üícontrollability beliefs were linked to reduced variability in negative affect. Importantly, these
   572‚Üí   572‚Üíeffects emerged independently of mean affect levels, suggesting that adaptive stress beliefs
   573‚Üí   573‚Üímay contribute to emotional stability rather than shifting overall affective tone. One possible
   574‚Üí   574‚Üíinterpretation is that such beliefs attenuate affective fluctuations by reducing reactivity to
   575‚Üí   575‚Üístressors (as seen with controllability beliefs) and/or facilitating more efficient recovery
   576‚Üí   576‚Üíprocesses.
   577‚Üí   577‚ÜíThese results converge with diary and EMA evidence linking stress beliefs to
   578‚Üí   578‚Üísubsequent affective reactions in university students (Laferton et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2025), in
   579‚Üí   579‚Üíworkers (Daniels et al., 2006; Harris & Daniels, 2005, 2007) and individuals under COVID-
   580‚Üí   580‚Üí
   581‚Üí   581‚Üí23
   582‚Üí   582‚Üí19 confinement (Zeng et al., 2024), as well as laboratory studies showing causal effects of
   583‚Üí   583‚Üíbelief manipulation on affective stress responses (Beltzer et al., 2014; Brooks, 2014; Crum et
   584‚Üí   584‚Üíal., 2017). Notably, prior diary research has shown that these effects persist even when
   585‚Üí   585‚Üícontrolling for neuroticism (Laferton et al., 2020). Mechanistically, such belief-driven
   586‚Üí   586‚Üímodulation of affect may help explain links between stress beliefs and long-term health
   587‚Üí   587‚Üíoutcomes, including mortality (Keller et al., 2012; Nabi et al., 2013), given that higher
   588‚Üí   588‚Üínegative affect and lower positive affect are prospectively associated with poorer health
   589‚Üí   589‚Üí(Pressman et al., 2018; Ramirez et al., 2025; Salovey et al., 2000; Sin, 2016).
   590‚Üí   590‚ÜíInterestingly, interaction effects between stress beliefs and stressor exposure were
   591‚Üí   591‚Üísomewhat more pronounced for social stressors than for workload-related demands. This
   592‚Üí   592‚Üíobservation aligns with prior experience-sampling evidence showing that belief-related
   593‚Üí   593‚Üímoderation effects were more robust for social than for workload stressors when both were
   594‚Üí   594‚Üíexamined concurrently (Laferton et al., 2020). Consistent with this, diary and ambulatory
   595‚Üí   595‚Üíresearch indicates that interpersonal stressors tend to elicit stronger affective responses and
   596‚Üí   596‚Üíare more strongly shaped by relatively stable individual characteristics. Importantly, this does
   597‚Üí   597‚Üínot imply that workload stressors are unaffected by stress beliefs. Previous experience-
   598‚Üí   598‚Üísampling studies have demonstrated belief-related moderation effects in response to work-
   599‚Üí   599‚Üírelated stress (Casper et al., 2017; Daniels et al., 2006), and experimental and applied research
   600‚Üí   600‚Üíhas documented belief-dependent effects under academic and occupational stressors
   601‚Üí   601‚Üí(e.g.,(Crum et al., 2013; Jamieson et al., 2010; Laferton et al., 2024). Taken together, the
   602‚Üí   602‚Üípresent findings suggest that the relative magnitude and consistency of belief-related
   603‚Üí   603‚Üímoderation effects may vary as a function of stressor type, with particularly robust effects
   604‚Üí   604‚Üíobserved for social stressors in everyday life. By contrast, no concurrent associations were
   605‚Üí   605‚Üíobserved between stress beliefs and two stress regulation strategies specified by the stress
   606‚Üí   606‚Üíoptimization model (Crum et al., 2020)‚Äîsituation selection (avoiding a stressful situation)
   607‚Üí   607‚Üíand response modification (utilize the stress response). There were nominal trends suggesting
   608‚Üí   608‚Üí
   609‚Üí   609‚Üí24
   610‚Üí   610‚Üíthat higher positive stress beliefs may be linked to slightly stronger utilization of the stress
   611‚Üí   611‚Üíresponse in general and under stressor exposure; however, these trends did not withstand
   612‚Üí   612‚Üímultiple-comparison control and are therefore reported for transparency only. Targeted
   613‚Üí   613‚Üíhypothesis tests in adequately powered EMA designs are warranted. To our knowledge, no
   614‚Üí   614‚Üíprior study investigating stress responses to real life stressors has assessed these two
   615‚Üí   615‚Üícomponents separately as formulated in the stress optimization model. However, there is
   616‚Üí   616‚Üíevidence for an association of stress beliefs with approach-oriented coping assessed via
   617‚Üí   617‚Üícomposite scales, which in aggregate are more comparable to situation selection than to
   618‚Üí   618‚Üíresponse utilization. In their EMA study, Casper et al. (2017) found that positive stress beliefs
   619‚Üí   619‚Üíprospectively predicted higher approach coping on days with anticipated high workload.
   620‚Üí   620‚ÜíOther cross-sectional studies report similar links between stress beliefs and proactive coping
   621‚Üí   621‚Üí(Keech et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). Moreover, students with more positive stress beliefs
   622‚Üí   622‚Üísought more feedback ahead of an anticipated social-evaluative stressor (Crum et al., 2013). A
   623‚Üí   623‚Üípossible explanation is that situation selection is predominantly an anticipatory process;
   624‚Üí   624‚Üíconsequently, our concurrent (reactive) sampling likely undercaptured it relative to the
   625‚Üí   625‚Üíanticipatory assessments used by Casper et al. (2017) and Crum et al. (2013). By contrast,
   626‚Üí   626‚Üíresponse modification processes are more immediately tied to stressor occurrence. In the
   627‚Üí   627‚Üícurrent study, the fixed schedule of three assessments per day may have lacked sensitivity to
   628‚Üí   628‚Üídetect such in-the-moment changes, as prompts could occur up to four hours after a stressor.
   629‚Üí   629‚ÜíFuture studies should employ event-related assessments to more precisely investigate the
   630‚Üí   630‚Üírelationship between stress beliefs and response modification. In addition, exploratory
   631‚Üí   631‚Üíanalyses suggested that avoidance-based situation selection was associated with higher
   632‚Üí   632‚Üínegative affect at the following within-day assessment, underlining its maladaptive nature
   633‚Üí   633‚Üíwithin the Stress Optimization Model (Crum et al., 2020). Expanding on these temporal
   634‚Üí   634‚Üídynamics, cross-day exploratory analyses further indicated that high daily workload and high
   635‚Üí   635‚Üípositive affect predicted a reduction in stress utilization on the subsequent day, suggesting a
   636‚Üí   636‚Üí
   637‚Üí   637‚Üí25
   638‚Üí   638‚Üípotential reduction in regulatory engagement following high workload and high positive
   639‚Üí   639‚Üíaffect days. Moreover, exploratory growth curve models suggested that workload increased
   640‚Üí   640‚Üíacross study days, whereas the initial increase in utilization appeared to be largely attributable
   641‚Üí   641‚Üíto this concurrent increase in workload.
   642‚Üí   642‚ÜíSome limitations and constraints on generality need to be considered when
   643‚Üí   643‚Üíinterpreting the results of the present study. First, although recruitment via university e-mail
   644‚Üí   644‚Üílists and research participation platforms may have introduced self-selection, several
   645‚Üí   645‚Üíobservations speak against substantial bias: mean stress-belief scores closely matched those
   646‚Üí   646‚Üíreported in prior student samples; course hours, perceived workload, and social stress fell
   647‚Üí   647‚Üíwithin typical student ranges; and the proportion with elevated psychological symptoms
   648‚Üí   648‚Üíapproximated rates observed in student population studies. Nevertheless, as typically seen in
   649‚Üí   649‚Üístudies using online recruitment, participants predominantly identified as female, thus limiting
   650‚Üí   650‚Üíthe ecological validity for individuals of male gender. Future studies should replicate these
   651‚Üí   651‚Üífindings in other populations and, ideally, in population-representative samples to more
   652‚Üí   652‚Üíclearly establish generalizability. Although the EMA design resulted in 1939 data points
   653‚Üí   653‚Üíamong 104 participants, considering the conservative approach to multiple testing, the power
   654‚Üí   654‚Üíof this study for detecting small effects is limited. Larger studies are needed to investigate
   655‚Üí   655‚Üísmaller potential effects between stress beliefs and daily stress regulation strategies. The main
   656‚Üí   656‚Üístrengths of the current study lie in the assessment of naturalistic stress processes on a daily
   657‚Üí   657‚Üíbasis. This design enabled the study of stress beliefs outside of the lab, while at the same time
   658‚Üí   658‚Üíreducing recall bias compared to studies assessing self-report of stress variables
   659‚Üí   659‚Üíretrospectively over several days. Specifically, the current study applied an interval contingent
   660‚Üí   660‚Üícoverage EMA approach, prompting participants to report on stressors, affect and stress
   661‚Üí   661‚Üíregulation strategies for the preceding 4 hours 3 times a day. This approach was chosen to
   662‚Üí   662‚Üíminimize the likelihood of missing stressors, which occur less frequently (a common
   663‚Üí   663‚Üílimitation of random sampling), while also avoiding the moment selection bias inherent to
   664‚Üí   664‚Üí
   665‚Üí   665‚Üí26
   666‚Üí   666‚Üíevent-contingent sampling‚Äînamely, the high likelihood of participants being unable or
   667‚Üí   667‚Üíunwilling to self-initiate a survey during acute stress (Stone et al., 2023; Vaessen et al., 2023).
   668‚Üí   668‚ÜíFurthermore, in contrast to purely event-contingent designs, this interval-based approach
   669‚Üí   669‚Üíenables the assessment of base rates for the variables of interest during non-stressor periods,
   670‚Üí   670‚Üíwhich is essential for distinguishing stress-related changes from normal daily variations
   671‚Üí   671‚Üí(Shiffman et al., 2008). However, this coverage approach introduces potential recall bias, as it
   672‚Üí   672‚Üícaptures short-term retrospective reporting of a 4-hour window rather than strictly momentary
   673‚Üí   673‚Üístates (Shiffman et al., 2008). Although coverage EMA approaches are frequently used, it
   674‚Üí   674‚Üícurrently remains unclear how participants cognitively aggregate their experiences over such
   675‚Üí   675‚Üíextended timeframes, meaning some degree of retrospective distortion cannot be completely
   676‚Üí   676‚Üíruled out (Stone et al., 2023). To address these methodological trade-offs, future research
   677‚Üí   677‚Üícould benefit from combining event-contingent sampling with an interval-based coverage
   678‚Üí   678‚Üíapproach. Such a hybrid design would allow for the immediate assessment of acute stress
   679‚Üí   679‚Üíreactions whenever feasible, while utilizing the coverage intervals as a fallback to capture
   680‚Üí   680‚Üímissed events and maintain essential base rate assessments. Finally, to minimize participant
   681‚Üí   681‚Üíburden during the high-frequency sampling protocol, two constraints regarding stressor
   682‚Üí   682‚Üímeasurement should be noted. First, the PDD assessed the extent of stressor occurrence rather
   683‚Üí   683‚Üíthan direct stressor intensity. Although prior research using the PDD indicates that these
   684‚Üí   684‚Üíoccurrence ratings are strongly associated with subjective stress intensity (Laferton et al.,
   685‚Üí   685‚Üí2020), a direct momentary measure might have provided further insights. Second, as open-
   686‚Üí   686‚Üíended qualitative data were not collected, the specific nature of the events that participants
   687‚Üí   687‚Üíclassified as social or workload stressors remains unclear. Future research could incorporate
   688‚Üí   688‚Üíbrief qualitative assessments to disentangle how specific subtypes of stressors might
   689‚Üí   689‚Üídifferentially interact with stress beliefs. Third, as an additional constraint to minimize
   690‚Üí   690‚Üíparticipant burden, only one end of the regulatory spectrum was assessed for each stress
   691‚Üí   691‚Üíregulation strategy. Specifically, situation selection was measured solely via avoidance
   692‚Üí   692‚Üí
   693‚Üí   693‚Üí27
   694‚Üí   694‚Üí(omitting approach-oriented behavior), and response modification was measured solely via
   695‚Üí   695‚Üíutilization (omitting attempts to minimize the stress response). Future studies should
   696‚Üí   696‚Üíincorporate the full spectrum of these domains, including approach and minimization
   697‚Üí   697‚Üístrategies, to capture a more comprehensive picture of stress regulation. A further limitation is
   698‚Üí   698‚Üíthe exclusive reliance on self-reported data. Although the EMA design minimizes
   699‚Üí   699‚Üíretrospective recall biases, subjective reports of stress regulation and stressor occurrence
   700‚Üí   700‚Üíremain susceptible to individual reporting biases. Future studies should therefore aim to
   701‚Üí   701‚Üíincorporate objective behavioral markers, such as digital phenotyping or physiological
   702‚Üí   702‚Üícorrelates, to corroborate these findings.
   703‚Üí   703‚ÜíIn conclusion, this seven-day EMA study shows that stress beliefs moderate affective
   704‚Üí   704‚Üíresponses to real-life work and social stressors. Together with prior evidence, these findings
   705‚Üí   705‚Üíprovide compelling convergent support that stress beliefs shape affect under stress ‚Äî
   706‚Üí   706‚Üíidentifying them as a promising intervention target, particularly given established links
   707‚Üí   707‚Üíbetween affect and long-term health. By contrast, we did not observe concurrent associations
   708‚Üí   708‚Üíwith stress regulation strategies; nevertheless, the present results warrant focused,
   709‚Üí   709‚Üípreregistered tests using EMA designs that separate anticipatory and reactive phases and
   710‚Üí   710‚Üíincorporate event-related sampling to more sensitively detect potential stress beliefs‚Äìstress
   711‚Üí   711‚Üíregulation pathways.
