    18→Abstract
    19→Emotion regulation is a central process through which people shape their emotional lives, yet
    20→much of what we know comes from studies of deliberate attempts at regulation. In everyday life,
    21→regulation may emerge spontaneously, embedded within the flow of ongoing thought. If so,
    22→patterns of spontaneous thought–both what people think about and how thoughts move between
    23→ideas–may provide a window into emotion regulation as it naturally unfolds. Across two studies,
    24→we used a Think-Aloud paradigm to capture participants’ unconstrained streams of thought and
    25→tested whether these patterns captured strategy use and predicted mental health. In Study 1,
    26→participants (N = 233) completed nearly 5,000 Think-Aloud sessions over three weeks. We
    27→trained logistic regression models to identify emotion regulation strategy signatures and tested
    28→their generalizability on held-out data. We then tested whether expression of these signatures
    29→predicted emotional outcomes in held-out data from Study 1 and in an independent sample from
    30→Study 2 (N = 780). Models identified six strategies–acceptance, rumination, savoring, expressive
    31→amplification, reappraisal, and distraction. The strongest features aligned with theory (e.g.,
    32→rumination with negative, repetitive thoughts; reappraisal with self-referential content and fewer
    33→positive-to-negative transitions). Moreover, individual differences in strategy expression
    34→predicted momentary changes in valence and both state and trait anxiety: acceptance, reappraisal,
    35→and savoring were associated with greater emotional improvement and lower anxiety, whereas
    36→rumination showed the opposite pattern. These findings reveal how emotion regulation is
    37→reflected in the content and dynamics of spontaneous thought and provide a predictive tool for
    38→identifying regulatory processes in everyday thinking.

    42→People Regulate Emotions During Spontaneous Thought Using Detectable Linguistic
    43→Signatures
    44→Emotion regulation is a central process through which people shape their emotional lives,
    45→influencing which emotions they have, when they have them, and how they experience and
    46→express them (Gross, 1998). Much of what we know about emotion regulation, however, comes
    47→from studies of deliberate, goal-directed attempts to manage emotions. Yet in everyday life,
    48→emotional experiences unfold continuously, and regulation may often occur without explicit
    49→intention, embedded within the flow of ongoing cognition (Braunstein et al., 2017). This raises a
    50→fundamental question: how does emotion regulation unfold in free-flowing thought?
    51→Understanding Emotion Regulation Beyond Deliberate Strategy Use
    52→Individuals can deploy several distinct strategies to regulate their emotions, and these strategies
    53→operate through different psychological mechanisms (Gross, 1998). For example, some strategies
    54→shape emotion by altering how individuals interpret situations: reappraisal involves changing
    55→how one construes a situation in order to alter its emotional impact, whereas acceptance involves
    56→allowing emotions to unfold without attempting to change them (Gross, 1998, 2001; Hayes et al.,
    57→1999; Lazarus & Alfert, 1964; McRae et al., 2012; Wolgast et al., 2011). Other strategies operate
    58→through attentional processes, such as rumination, which involves repeatedly focusing on one’s
    59→emotions, savoring, which involves attending to and appreciating emotional experiences, and
    60→distraction, which shifts attention away from them (Gross, 1998; Nolen-Hoeksema & Morrow,
    61→1993; Nolen-Hoeksema et al., 2008; Sheppes & Meiran, 2007; Bryant, 1989, 2003). Still others
    62→act on emotional experience or expression itself, such as expressive suppression, which involves
    63→inhibiting emotional expression, and expressive amplification which involves increasing
    64→emotional expression (Gross, 1998, 2001; Gross & John, 1998, 2003; Butler et al., 2003).
    65→Foundational research on emotion regulation strategies has largely relied on controlled
    66→experiments in which researchers instruct participants to use specific strategies and measure their
    67→immediate effects on emotional outcomes (McRae, 2013; Aldao, 2013; Burr & Samanez-Larkin,
    68→2020). Thus, the outcome of these strategies are well studied through lab experiments, but less is
    69→known about if and how these strategies operate in natural thought.
    70→Regulation in naturalistic contexts is challenging to capture. In everyday life, individuals
    71→spontaneously deploy a range of strategies, often simultaneously, and sometimes without explicit
    72→intent (Heiy & Cheavens, 2014; Brans et al., 2013; Ford et al., 2019; Mauss et al., 2007). People
    73→also demonstrate substantial variability in which strategies they use, how frequently they use
    74→them, and when they employ them within emotional episodes (Blanke et al., 2020; Grommisch et
    75→al., 2020; Kalokerinos et al., 2017; Elkjaer et al., 2022). These windows into regulation in the
    76→wild, however, often rely on self-reports of regulatory experiences, requiring individuals to
    77→recognize and report on the strategies they used over a preceding period of time. These
    78→measurement strategies can disrupt the natural flow of thoughts and emotions and may be

    82→vulnerable to biases introduced when individuals reconstruct their past experiences (Robinson &
    83→Clore, 2002). They also assume that people can accurately report on their own strategy use even
    84→though people often have limited insight into the regulatory processes they deploy, and global
    85→reports of strategy use are only weakly related to strategies observed in daily life (Koval et al.,
    86→2023; Naragon-Gailey et al., 2017; Hurlburt & Heavey, 2015; Stone et al., 2023). If emotion
    87→regulation operates spontaneously during ongoing cognition, without explicit intention, it may be
    88→difficult to accurately identify and report in real time.
    89→Spontaneous Thought Provides a Window Into Emotion Regulation as It Unfolds
    90→One promising way to address this challenge is to directly examine individuals’ spontaneous
    91→thought. Spontaneous thought refers to thoughts that arise and unfold relatively freely, without
    92→being strongly guided by deliberate cognitive control or salient external stimuli. Spontaneous
    93→thought includes experiences such as mind-wandering, imagining, planning, and remembering
    94→(Christoff et al., 2016). These thoughts occupy a substantial portion of daily life: people spend an
    95→estimated 30–50% of their waking hours engaged in this process (Killingsworth & Gilbert,
    96→2010). Spontaneous thoughts tend to center on the self, close others, and current concerns
    97→(Klinger & Cox, 1987; Kane et al., 2017; Andrews-Hanna et al., 2013; Klinger, 2013; Song &
    98→Wang, 2012). Much of this content is also emotional in nature, with affective content present in
    99→roughly 50%–67% of self-generated thoughts (Killingsworth & Gilbert, 2010; Fox, 2016; Fox et
   100→al., 2013). Given that spontaneous thought unfolds continuously and reflects individuals’ internal
   101→states, it may provide a natural medium for regulating emotion.
   102→This raises a key question: do people regulate their emotions through spontaneous thought? Prior
   103→theoretical work suggests that one of the primary functions of spontaneous thought may be to
   104→facilitate affective homeostasis (Corbani et al., 2025, 2026). If so, the flow of spontaneous
   105→thoughts should reveal how individuals deploy emotion regulation in real time. In the present
   106→work, we test the possibility that individuals spontaneously engage in distinct emotion regulation
   107→strategies during ongoing thought. To do so, we captured participants’ thoughts using a
   108→Think-Aloud task in which they narrated their unfiltered thoughts in varying contexts. We
   109→extracted features from these data to build predictive models of strategy use, and we evaluated
   110→their accuracy against participant reports. Models that can accurately predict the use of different
   111→strategies from patterns of thought would provide a novel way to examine the presence of
   112→emotion regulation as it unfolds in ongoing, undirected contexts, without requiring continual
   113→self-report.
   114→Emotion Regulation Strategies Are Reflected in What and How People Think
   115→If emotion regulation unfolds during spontaneous thought, how might it be instantiated?
   116→Regulation could be reflected in both what people think about and how their thoughts
   117→unfold (i.e., the dynamics of their thoughts over time). Prior work shows that what people
   118→think about is closely linked to emotion: self-focused, past-oriented, and negatively
   119→

   121→5
   122→valenced thoughts are associated with negative affect, whereas future-oriented and
   123→positively valenced thoughts are associated with positive affect (Andrews-Hanna et al.,
   124→2013; Raffaelli et al., 2021; Kam et al., 2024). Beyond thought content, emerging evidence
   125→suggests that the dynamics of how people think–the way the mind moves between
   126→ideas–may also play an important role in shaping emotional experience. For example,
   127→more expansive patterns of thought, characterized by flexible transitions across
   128→semantically diverse ideas, have been associated with positive affective states across
   129→theoretical, clinical, and empirical work (Corbani et al. 2025, Huppert et al., 2004;
   130→Nolen-Hoeksema et al., 2008; Raffaelli et al., 2021; Mills et al., 2021; Brunyé et al., 2013).
   131→Though we know little about how these content and dynamic features relate to emotion
   132→regulation, per se, we do know that many regulation strategies are, in fact, defined by how
   133→they shape what people think about and how their minds move. For example, rumination is
   134→characterized by repetitive negative thoughts, reflecting both constrained dynamics and
   135→negatively valenced content (Nolen-Hoeksema et al., 2008; Martin & Tesser, 1996). By
   136→contrast, cognitive reappraisal involves changing how one interprets a situation, and thus
   137→what one thinks about it, which may also be accompanied by changes in how thought
   138→unfolds (Gross, 1998; Gross & John, 2003; Speer et al., 2021; Ochsner & Gross, 2005).
   139→This raises a second question: if spontaneous thought can reveal the presence of emotion
   140→regulation as it unfolds, what features of thought content and dynamics characterize the
   141→expression of these strategies? We test how features that capture both thought content (e.g.,
   142→valence, self-focus) and thought dynamics (e.g., (dis)similarity of successive thoughts, the
   143→pace and duration of thoughts) predict the expression of distinct regulation strategies. To
   144→do so, we leveraged advances in natural language processing to test which features of
   145→spontaneous thought contributed to the prediction of each strategy. Recent work shows that
   146→emotion regulation strategies can be identified from linguistic data, including narrative
   147→accounts of emotional experiences, highlighting the promise of language-based approaches
   148→for capturing regulatory processes (Nook, 2023; Nook et al, 2017, 2024; López-Pérez et
   149→al., 2025; Pinus et al., 2025). This approach provides a comprehensive mapping of how
   150→emotion regulation strategies are expressed in ongoing cognition.
   151→Furthermore, assessing emotion regulation strategies in spontaneous thought may provide
   152→valuable insight into a person’s habitual regulation strategy use. A large body of research shows
   153→that how people tend to regulate their emotions is related to their well-being. For example,
   154→cognitive reappraisal and acceptance are generally associated with more adaptive affective
   155→responses and better long-term psychological wellbeing (Kalokerinos et al., 2015; Cutuli, 2014).
   156→In contrast, rumination or expressive suppression are typically linked to sustained or heightened
   157→negative affect and increased risk for depression and anxiety (e.g., Gross & John, 2003; Goldin et
   158→al., 2008; Cutuli 2014; Aldao et al., 2010). Does spontaneous emotion regulation using these
   159→respective strategies likewise predict psychological wellbeing? Here we test whether the extent
   160→to which people spontaneously engage in more effective regulatory strategies predicts

   165→health outcomes, including anxiety.
   166→Current Studies
   167→In the current work we analyzed spontaneous thought data collected using a Think-Aloud
   168→paradigm in which participants freely verbalized their thoughts across two datasets (Figure 1). In
   169→Study 1, participants (N = 223) completed an initial 9-minute baseline Think-Aloud session
   170→followed by three weeks of daily 5-minute sessions, a subset of which (on average 4-5 per
   171→participant) were preceded by a negative mood induction. In all, this dataset yielded almost 5,000
   172→Think-Aloud sessions. After each session, participants reported their use of emotion regulation
   173→strategies, allowing us to train models to identify strategy use from patterns of thought. In Study
   174→2, a separate sample (N = 780) completed a single 10-minute Think-Aloud session following
   175→either a positive or negative mood induction, which we used to test whether the resulting
   176→signatures related to emotional outcomes in an independent dataset. Using these data, we tested
   177→whether people spontaneously regulate their emotions during unconstrained thought, what
   178→features of thought content and thought dynamics characterize these processes, and whether they
   179→are associated with psychological wellbeing.

   347→Emotion Regulation Signatures Predict Short-Term Emotional Improvement
   348→To test how spontaneous regulation impacts momentary emotions, we fit standardized linear
   349→regression models predicting emotional improvement in each dataset from the strength of each
   350→regulation signature’s expression. Emotion improvement was computed as the change in valence
   351→from pre- to post-Think-Aloud on a 0-100 negative-to-positive valence scale, controlling for
   352→initial valence. Participants completed multiple non-induction daily-life sessions, so we
   353→computed each participant’s mean change in valence and mean signature expression across
   354→sessions and used these participant-level averages in analyses. We estimated one model per
   355→strategy so that the association between each emotion regulation signature and emotional
   356→improvement could be examined independently, without imposing competition or trade-offs
   357→among strategies. Critically, these analyses were conducted only on sessions not used to train the
   358→models. In Study 1, this included the 9-minute baseline Think-Aloud session and the daily
   359→5-minute sessions not preceded by mood induction. In Study 2, this included a single 10-minute
   360→Think-Aloud session following either a positive or negative mood induction (see
   361→condition-specific results in the Supplemental Materials).
   362→The expression of emotion regulation strategies significantly predicted emotional improvement
   363→(Figure 5A; see Supplemental Materials, Table S4 for full model coefficients). Across all three
   364→datasets, greater expression of the acceptance, reappraisal, and savoring signatures were
   365→associated with greater emotional improvement, whereas greater expression of the rumination
   366→signature was associated with reduced improvement. The expressive amplification signature was
   367→associated with reduced emotional improvement in the baseline and single-session induction
   368→datasets, but not on non-induction days. The distraction signature was not significantly
   369→associated with emotional improvement in any dataset. When considered jointly, expression of
   370→these signatures accounted for between 10% and 39% of the variance in emotional improvement
   371→across datasets.
   372→Emotion regulation signatures more consistently and robustly predicted emotional improvement
   373→than participants’ binary self-reports of strategy use. In addition, models incorporating all
   374→signatures jointly explained as much or more variance in emotional improvement than analogous
   375→models using self-reported strategy use alone (see Supplemental Materials, Figure S3 and Table
   376→S6). Together, these findings indicate that emotion regulation signatures derived from
   377→spontaneous thought reliably predict short-term emotional outcomes and, in many cases, do so
   378→more robustly than participants’ self-reported strategy use.

   382→Emotion Regulation Signatures Predict Trait Anxiety
   383→To test how emotion regulation relates to psychological wellbeing, we fit a standardized linear
   384→regression model predicting anxiety from the expression of each emotion regulation signature.
   385→As above, when participants completed multiple sessions, we tested whether their average
   386→emotion regulation signatures were associated with anxiety, which was measured at baseline
   387→using the Generalized Anxiety Disorder- 7 (GAD-7) in Study 1 and the State-Trait Anxiety
   388→Inventory (STAI) in Study 2. We estimated one model per strategy to examine each signature
   389→independently.
   390→Expression of emotion regulation signatures predicted anxiety (Figure 5B; see Supplemental
   391→Materials, Table S8 for full model coefficient). Across all three datasets, greater expression of the
   392→rumination signature was associated with higher anxiety, whereas greater expression of the
   393→acceptance, reappraisal, and savoring signatures was associated with lower anxiety. Distraction
   394→predicted higher anxiety in the non-induction and baseline datasets but not in the single-session
   395→induction data; expressive amplification was not significantly associated with anxiety in any
   396→dataset. When considered jointly, these signatures accounted for between 21% and 24% of the
   397→variance in anxiety across datasets.
   398→Emotion regulation signatures also more consistently and robustly predicted anxiety than
   399→participants’ binary self-reports of strategy use. In addition, models incorporating all signatures
   400→jointly explained substantially more variance in anxiety than analogous models using
   401→self-reported strategy use alone (see Supplemental Materials, Figure S4 and Table S10).
   402→Together, these findings indicate that emotion regulation signatures derived from spontaneous
   403→thought reliably track individual differences in mental health, and, in many cases, do so more
   404→robustly than participants’ self-reported strategy use.

   414→Discussion
   415→How does emotion regulation unfold during spontaneous thought? Here, we show that people
   416→express distinct emotion regulation strategies during spontaneous thought and that the expression
   417→of these strategies has meaningful associations with emotional wellbeing.. Across multiple
   418→datasets, we found reliable evidence that people engage in acceptance, rumination, savoring,
   419→reappraisal, distraction, and amplification. By capturing people’s thoughts during a Think-Aloud
   420→task we derived distinct linguistic signatures of each emotion regulation strategy. These
   421→signatures were characterized both by what people thought about and by how their thoughts
   422→moved through semantic space, adding insight into the psychological processes underlying these
   423→emotion regulation strategies. The degree to which individuals expressed particular strategies in
   424→spontaneous thought predicted momentary changes in valence and both state and trait anxiety.
   425→Strategies typically considered adaptive, such as acceptance, reappraisal, and savoring, were
   426→associated with greater emotional improvement and lower anxiety, whereas rumination showed
   427→the opposite pattern. Together, these findings suggest that spontaneous thought provides a
   428→window into emotion regulation processes as they naturally unfold, with important associations
   429→with emotional wellbeing.
   430→Emotion regulation reliably emerges during spontaneous thought, even in the absence of explicit
   431→instructions to regulate. Prior work has largely relied on retrospective self-report measures to
   432→assess emotion regulation in everyday life, asking individuals to reflect on the strategies they
   433→used after the fact, and has not directly examined whether or how regulation unfolds within
   434→ongoing spontaneous cognition (Blanke et al., 2020; Grommisch et al., 2020; Kalokerinos et al.,
   435→2017). The present work addresses this gap by showing that distinct regulation strategies can be
   436→identified directly from patterns of thought. In doing so, these results indicate that emotion
   437→regulation does not need to be measured solely through self-report; it can also be identified
   438→directly from the language people produce while thinking. This is particularly important given
   439→well-documented limitations of self-report measures of regulation, including concerns about
   440→retrospective bias and shared-method variance (Stone et al., 2023; Robinson & Clore, 2002;
   441→Podsakoff et al., 2003). Instead, spontaneous thought, when expressed through language, offers a
   442→powerful and minimally disruptive way to observe emotion regulation, potentially capturing both
   443→deliberate and less deliberate forms of regulation.
   444→

   446→18
   447→Regulatory strategies were distinguished both by what people thought about and by how their
   448→thoughts unfolded over time. For example, acceptance was characterized by relatively
   449→constrained thought dynamics and more positive emotional transitions, and more self-assured
   450→content. Observed patterns largely aligned with theoretical expectations for each strategy.
   451→Rumination, for instance, was characterized by constrained, repetitive engagement with
   452→negatively valenced content, consistent with its definition (Kircanski et al., 2018;
   453→Nolen-Hoeksema et al., 2008; Martin & Tesser, 1996). At the same time, our methods shed light
   454→on new psychological features that may characterize these established emotion regulation
   455→strategies. Distraction, for example, was marked by expansive thought dynamics, as expected,
   456→but its linguistic content suggested simpler, present-focused, and potentially goal-oriented
   457→thinking. Interestingly, although savoring might plausibly have involved thinking through many
   458→positive experiences, it was instead associated with relatively constrained dynamics, suggesting
   459→sustained engagement with specific positive experiences. This finding further suggests that
   460→similar thought dynamics can support very different forms of regulation and are not inherently
   461→adaptive or maladaptive in themselves (e.g., constrained thinking was observed in both savoring
   462→and rumination). Rather, the impact of thought dynamics may depend on the broader emotional
   463→and cognitive context in which they unfold, a nuance that may be important for understanding
   464→and designing interventions. By jointly considering multiple features of thought content and
   465→thought dynamics, our findings provide a comprehensive account of how emotion regulation
   466→strategies are instantiated in spontaneous thought and offer deeper insight into the processes
   467→through which regulation unfolds in real time.
   468→An important exception to this pattern was expressive suppression, for which we were not able to
   469→build a reliable signature. This may be because expressive suppression was the least frequently
   470→used strategy in our dataset, appearing on only 10.7% of days. Expressive suppression may also
   471→be difficult to identify from spontaneous thought because it involves inhibiting emotional
   472→expression rather than changing the underlying emotional experience (Gross, 1998; Gross &
   473→John, 2003). As a result, suppression may manifest less in the thoughts people express and more
   474→in what they choose to withhold. Moreover, people may achieve this regulatory goal in different
   475→ways. For example, suppression may be accompanied by shifts toward a wide range of
   476→alternative thoughts for some individuals, but by a narrowing of thought toward specific topics
   477→for others. Future research could help disentangle these possibilities by focusing more directly on
   478→expressive suppression, for example through designs that collect more data on this strategy,
   479→studies that explicitly instruct participants to suppress emotional responses, or through follow up
   480→questions that probe how expressive suppression was implemented during the task. Such
   481→approaches may even identify distinct subtypes of expressive suppression and clarify the extent
   482→to which they generalize across individuals.
   483→The expression of regulation strategies was not merely descriptive but was strongly related to
   484→emotional wellbeing, both in the moment and over longer timescales. Jointly, these signatures
   485→

   487→19
   488→accounted for meaningful variance in emotional wellbeing outcomes, explaining between 10%
   489→and 39% of the variance in emotional improvement and between 21% and 24% of the variance in
   490→anxiety across datasets. These associations emerged above and beyond participants’ self-reported
   491→strategy use, suggesting that how emotion regulation manifests in spontaneous thought is closely
   492→tied to psychological outcomes and may capture meaningful aspects of regulation not fully
   493→accessible through retrospective self-report. These findings highlight the potential of these
   494→signatures as indicators of emotional wellbeing and suggest that they provide a new way to study
   495→how patterns of spontaneous thought relate to other psychological outcomes. Some strategies
   496→were especially predictive of wellbeing. Aligning with prior findings, rumination consistently
   497→emerged as one of the strongest predictors of poorer emotional outcomes, whereas strategies
   498→such as acceptance and savoring were among the most robust predictors of emotional
   499→improvement and lower anxiety (Aldao et al., 2010; Bryan, 2003; Nolen-Hoeksema et al., 2008).
   500→These differences suggest that regulation strategies are not equally consequential when expressed
   501→in spontaneous thought, and that their signatures vary in their relevance for emotional health.
   502→These findings open several promising directions for future research. The signatures developed
   503→here could be used to examine how emotion regulation unfolds over time, allowing researchers
   504→to track when different strategies emerge and how they evolve across moments, days, or longer
   505→timescales. This approach is particularly relevant for a growing body of work seeking to identify
   506→emotion regulation as it occurs in daily life, outside of tightly controlled laboratory settings
   507→(Blanke et al., 2020; Grommisch et al., 2020; Kalokerinos et al., 2017). Future work could
   508→leverage these signatures to examine the relative contributions of different strategies across
   509→contexts, including when different strategies emerge, how multiple strategies co-occur or
   510→transition between one another, and the breadth and adaptability of individuals’ regulatory
   511→repertoires (Aldao, 2013; Ford, 2019; Kalokerinos, 2017; Blanke, 2020; Guassi Moreira, 2024).
   512→More broadly, these signatures could also potentially be applied to other forms of naturalistic
   513→data, such as conversations or diary entries. For example, applying this framework to social
   514→interactions could help reveal whether conversations themselves function as a medium for
   515→spontaneous interpersonal emotion regulation and which regulatory strategies are most effective
   516→for shaping others’ emotional experiences. Extending this approach beyond Think-Aloud
   517→paradigms may therefore provide new opportunities to study the temporal dynamics of emotion
   518→regulation in a wide variety of ecologically valid settings.
   519→Several limitations should be noted. First, although the Think-Aloud paradigm provides a
   520→valuable window into spontaneous thought, asking participants to verbalize their thoughts may
   521→itself influence the nature of those thoughts. Verbalizing may change the pace, structure, or
   522→content of thinking, making thought streams somewhat more deliberate or organized than they
   523→would be otherwise. As a result, the task captures important aspects of spontaneous thought as it
   524→unfolds, but it may not fully reflect unspoken internal thought. Second, the strategy signatures
   525→were trained using self-reported strategy use, which is itself an imperfect measure, and their
   526→
   527→EMOTION REGULATION SIGNATURES IN SPONTANEOUS THOUGHT
   528→20
   529→reports may therefore be incomplete or biased (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977). To mitigate this, we
   530→asked about strategy use using everyday language (e.g., “changed how you were thinking about a
   531→situation” for reappraisal) rather than requiring knowledge of specific strategies, aiming to
   532→capture regulation at a level participants are likely to have insight into. Even so, the labels used
   533→to train the models are likely imperfect, which may limit how precisely specific strategies can be
   534→identified.
   535→In sum, the present findings show that emotion regulation is embedded in the content and
   536→dynamics of spontaneous thought. By combining linguistic features with measures of how
   537→thoughts unfold over time, we find that people spontaneously engage in regulatory processes
   538→even when they are not explicitly instructed to regulate their emotions. Importantly, these
   539→patterns are not merely descriptive but are meaningfully related to emotional outcomes,
   540→predicting both short-term emotional improvement and longer-term mental health. Together, this
   541→work suggests that spontaneous thought provides a powerful window into how emotion
   542→regulation naturally unfolds in everyday cognition.
   543→Methods
   544→To test whether people use spontaneous thought to regulate their emotions and to identify
   545→the features of thought content and dynamics that characterize these processes, we adopted
   546→a three-step approach. First, we trained models to identify emotion regulation strategies
   547→from spontaneous thought using longitudinal data collected during negative mood
   548→inductions. Second, we evaluated model accuracy on held-out sessions from the same
   549→dataset. Finally, to examine their consequences for psychological wellbeing, we applied
   550→these models to held-out and independent data to test whether the expression of these
   551→strategies predicted mental health outcomes.
   552→Study 1
   553→In this study, participants completed an initial baseline survey followed by three weeks of
   554→ecological momentary assessment. Across both phases, participants completed
   555→Think-Aloud tasks to capture their streams of thought and reported their use of emotion
   556→regulation strategies. During the three-week period, a subset of days included a negative
   557→mood induction prior to the Think-Aloud task. We used data from these negative-induction
   558→days to train logistic regression models to predict strategy use from features of thought
   559→content and dynamics. We then evaluated model performance on the baseline and
   560→non-induction data. Accurate prediction of strategy use from these features provided
   561→evidence that people regulate their emotions spontaneously during ongoing thought. We
   562→then examined the features driving model performance to characterize how different
   563→emotion regulation strategies were expressed in spontaneous thought.

   583→Overview. Participants completed both an initial baseline survey, as well as 21
   584→days of ecological momentary assessment. In the baseline survey, participants completed a
   585→9-minute Think-Aloud task alongside a battery of self-report questionnaires. They were
   586→then enrolled in a 21-day longitudinal study. Each day, participants received a text message
   587→at a randomly selected time between 9:00 AM and 9:00 PM EST inviting them to complete

   590→Think-Aloud task was preceded by a negative mood induction (range: 1 to 9 days per

   592→task without a mood induction. After each Think-Aloud, participants self-reported their

   594→Spontaneous Thought. Spontaneous thought was assessed using a Think-Aloud
   595→task in which participants verbalized their thoughts freely and continuously (see
   596→Supplementary Materials for specific task instructions). Participants first completed a brief
   597→microphone test to confirm that audio recording was functioning properly. They were then
   598→instructed to speak aloud any thoughts that came to mind without concern for grammar or
   599→repetition. Participants initiated the task recording by pressing a button, after which a
   600→progress bar appeared around the recording button to indicate the approximate remaining
   601→time. The recording ended automatically at the conclusion of the task. This paradigm has
   602→been widely used to study spontaneous thought and has been validated to elicit thought
   603→streams that closely resemble everyday cognition, both in content and in their unprompted,

   607→uncensored flow (Raffaelli et al., 2021, 2024; Sripada & Taxali, 2020; Mildner & Tamir,

   609→Negative Mood Induction. On four or five randomly assigned days, participants
   610→completed a negative memory recall task where they were told to remember a time when
   611→they experienced deep sadness. They were asked to write at least 100 words with a focus

   618→Measures
   619→Emotion Regulation Strategy Use Self-Report. Following the Think-Aloud task,
   620→participants reported which emotion regulation strategies they used during the task.
   621→Participants were asked, “When you were thinking aloud, did you do any of the following
   622→to influence your emotions?” and could select any number of seven predefined strategies,
   623→drawn from commonly studied emotion regulation strategies (Gross, 1998; Hayes et al.,
   624→1999; Nolen-Hoeksema et al., 2008; Bryant, 2003; Webb et al., 2012): acceptance
   625→(“Accepted your emotions”), rumination (“Focused or dwelled on your emotions”),
   626→expressive amplification (“Emphasized how much you expressed your emotions”),
   627→reappraisal (“Changed how you were thinking about a situation”), savoring (“Savored your
   628→emotions”), distraction (“Distracted yourself from your emotions”), and expressive
   629→suppression (“Suppressed the expression of your emotions”). Importantly, these responses
   630→reflect participants’ retrospective descriptions of what occurred during the Think-Aloud
   631→session, rather than selections of strategies they deliberately employed. Participants could
   632→also indicate that they used no strategy (0-17.3% of sessions across datasets) or report an
   633→unlisted strategy via an open-ended response (0.8-1.3% of sessions); these responses were
   634→not included in the present analyses.

   636→two classes of variables: thought content variables, which capture what participants were
   637→thinking about, and thought dynamic variables, which capture how people think and move
   638→between thoughts (see Table S1 for definitions of all features). To extract these features

   641→transcript into sentences by splitting wherever a period, exclamation point, or question
   642→mark was followed by a space. We treated these sentences as units of thought, resulting in

   648→Thought Content. We extracted 18 linguistic features characterizing the content of
   649→participants’ spontaneous thoughts using automated text analysis. We derived measures of
   650→analytic thinking, clout, cognitive processes, insight, perception, social referents, temporal
   651→focus (past, present, future), allure-related language, function word use, pronoun use,
   652→first-person singular pronouns, common verb use, and the proportion of big words (≥7
   653→letters) used, from LIWC-22 (Boyd et al., 2022). In addition, we computed measures of
   654→linguistic distancing (based on first-person singular pronouns, present-tense verbs,
   655→discrepancy words, articles, and long words) and linguistic abstraction (descriptive action
   656→verbs, interpretive action verbs, state verbs, adjectives, and nouns) (Nook et al. 2017,
   657→Nook et al, 2022). Finally, we estimated thought valence using a pre-trained sentiment
   658→model (cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment; Barbieri et al., 2020). These features
   659→were selected using a combination of a priori theoretical considerations and data-driven
   660→feature reduction. Specifically, we retained theoretically motivated measures of thought
   661→content, including self-focus, temporal focus, valence, linguistic distancing, and
   662→abstraction, given their established links with emotion and emotion regulation
   663→(Andrews-Hanna et al., 2013; Raffaelli et al., 2021; Kam et al., 2024; Nook et al., 2024).
   664→We additionally selected LIWC categories that exhibited high variability across
   665→participants and removed redundant features to maximize interpretability. Full details of
   666→feature selection and computation are provided in the Supplement.
   667→Thought Dynamics. To characterize how thoughts unfolded over time, we
   668→extracted 20 measures from each Think-Aloud session capturing thought production,
   669→timing, valence transitions, semantic movement, and lexical diversity. These features were
   670→selected to capture a broad range of dynamic properties of spontaneous thought, including
   671→affective and semantic trajectories previously linked to emotion and wellbeing, alongside
   672→descriptive features characterizing the structure of the thought stream (Corbani et al., 2025,
   673→2026; Lux et al., 2022; Raffaelli et al., 2021; Pronin & Jacobs, 2008). These measures
   674→spanned five categories: (i) Thought production was indexed by the total number of
   675→discrete thoughts and overall word count. (ii) Temporal pacing was quantified using
   676→average thought duration and the inter-thought interval. (iii) To capture valence transitions,
   677→we estimated the valence of each thought using a pre-trained sentiment model
   678→(cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment), and we computed Markov transition
   679→probabilities between valence states (negative, neutral, positive). (iv) To characterize
   680→movement through semantic space, we computed subsequent thought similarity and
   681→thought expansiveness using cosine similarity between sentence embeddings generated
   682→with the all-mpnet-base-v2 sentence-transformer model. Subsequent thought similarity
   683→was defined as the semantic similarity between adjacent thoughts in the stream of thought.
   684→To quantify thought expansiveness, we constructed semantic thought networks in which

   695→Modeling Approach
   696→To assess whether emotion regulation strategies could be identified from spontaneous
   697→thought, we trained separate regularized logistic regression models to predict binary use of
   698→each strategy during each Think-Aloud session (used vs. not used). We standardized
   699→features indexing spontaneous thought content and dynamics by z-scoring and entered
   700→them as predictors. We chose logistic regression because it is designed to model binary
   701→outcomes and remains interpretable, allowing us to examine how different features
   702→contributed to the likelihood of strategy use.
   703→We selected model hyperparameters using grid search with five-fold stratified cross-validation,
   704→preserving the base rate of each strategy across folds. We tuned the regularization type
   705→(unregularized, L1, L2, or elastic net), regularization strength (C = 0.01 to 100), elastic net
   706→mixing parameters (which control the balance between L1 and L2 regularization), and class
   707→weighting to address imbalanced strategy use. The models output probabilities reflecting the
   708→likelihood that a strategy was used, rather than binary yes/no classifications directly. Converting
   709→these probabilities into categorical predictions requires selecting a probability threshold (e.g.,
   710→classifying sessions with predicted probabilities above 0.50 as “strategy used”), with different
   711→thresholds producing different tradeoffs between precision and recall. We therefore optimized
   712→model selection based on average precision, which summarizes model performance across all
   713→possible decision thresholds by integrating precision-recall tradeoffs across the full range of
   714→predicted probabilities and is well suited for imbalanced binary classification. We additionally
   715→report threshold-dependent performance metrics in the Supplemental Materials. The
   716→best-performing hyperparameters selected for each emotion regulation signature are also reported
   717→in the Supplemental Materials.

   871→Spontaneous Thought Features
   872→We extracted a set of features designed to characterize both the content and dynamics of
   873→participants’ spontaneous thoughts during each Think-Aloud session. Thought content features
   874→captured what participants were thinking about. Thought dynamic features captured how
   875→thoughts unfolded over time. Below, we describe the computation of each feature in detail. A
   876→complete list of features included in the emotion regulation signature models, along with brief
   877→definitions, is provided in Table S1.
   878→a. Thought Content
   879→We extracted several measures characterizing the content of participants’ spontaneous thoughts
   880→using automated text-analysis methods. As a first step, we quantified different linguistic content
   881→
   882→EMOTION REGULATION SIGNATURES IN SPONTANEOUS THOUGHT
   883→30
   884→features for each Think-Aloud session using the LIWC-22 software. LIWC-22 processes text by
   885→comparing each word in a given sample to a pre-established dictionary comprising over 12,000
   886→words, word stems, and phrases organized into theoretically motivated linguistic categories. For
   887→standard LIWC categories, scores reflect the percentage of total words in the text that match each
   888→category, yielding category-level indices of linguistic content. In addition to these standard
   889→word-count–based categories, LIWC-22 provides summary variables that are computed using
   890→proprietary algorithms developed and validated in prior research. These summary indices
   891→combine information from multiple underlying linguistic categories and are converted into
   892→percentile-based scores that indicate how a given text compares to normative language samples
   893→in terms of the targeted psychological construct.
   894→Using LIWC-22, we extracted several classes of linguistic features. Specifically, we quantified
   895→two LIWC summary indices – analytic thinking, a measure of logical and formal language use,
   896→and clout, which captures language associated with leadership, confidence, and status – as well
   897→as a set of standard word-count based categories reflecting psychological content and lexical
   898→features. These included language related to cognitive processing, insight, perception, social
   899→referents, temporal focus, indexing the relative use of past-, present-, and future-oriented
   900→language, and allure-related language which is a dimension made up of words commonly used in
   901→persuasive communication, as well as function-word based features such as overall function
   902→word use, pronoun usage, first-person singular pronoun usage, common verb use, and word
   903→length (i.e., the percentage of words containing seven or more letters).
   904→LIWC categories are hierarchically organized such that several measures are nested within
   905→broader categories (e.g., first-person singular pronouns are a subset of pronouns, which fall under
   906→function words; insight language is a subset of cognitive process language). As a result, some
   907→overlap between categories is expected by design.
   908→We selected the above-mentioned LIWC variables based on the following criteria. Although
   909→LIWC provides up to 118 categories, including all of them would introduce substantial
   910→redundancy and reduce interpretability. Accordingly, while models were allowed to determine
   911→which features were most predictive, we constrained the feature space based on variance and
   912→theoretical motivation. We first identified the 20 LIWC categories exhibiting the greatest
   913→variance in our dataset and then pruned this set to retain non-redundant variables indexing the
   914→content of participants’ thoughts. Specifically, we excluded variables reflecting form rather than
   915→content (e.g. word count), as well as highly correlated variables and broad, overarching
   916→categories, in favor of more granular features. Finally, guided by theory, we retained first-person
   917→pronoun usage and included present- and future-focused language, despite overlap with broader
   918→categories. Prior work indicates that self-focused and past-oriented thought is associated with
   919→more negative affect, whereas future-oriented thought is associated with more positive emotional
   920→states (Andrews-Hanna et al., 2013; Raffaelli et al., 2021; Kam et al., 2024).
   921→
   922→EMOTION REGULATION SIGNATURES IN SPONTANEOUS THOUGHT
   923→31
   924→We also computed measures of linguistic distancing and linguistic abstraction to capture
   925→additional psychological dimensions of spontaneous thought by adapting methods from prior
   926→work (Nook et al. 2017, Nook et al, 2022). Specifically, we measured linguistic distancing by
   927→computing the proportion of words within five LIWC categories: first-person singular pronouns,
   928→present-tense verbs, discrepancy words (e.g., would, could), articles (the, a, an), and words
   929→longer than six letters. Greater use of first-person singular pronouns, present-tense verbs, and
   930→discrepancy words indicate language that is more psychologically close, whereas greater use of
   931→articles and long words indicates language that is more psychologically distant (Pennebaker &
   932→King, 1999). After standardizing (z-scoring) these categories across all participants, we
   933→reverse-scored the psychologically close categories (first-person singular pronouns, present-tense
   934→verbs, discrepancy words), and averaged all five categories into a single linguistic distancing
   935→score. Higher scores indicate greater psychological distance from immediate experience. We
   936→quantified linguistic abstraction by classifying each word into one of five hierarchical abstraction
   937→levels: descriptive action verbs, interpretive action verbs, state verbs, adjectives, and nouns
   938→(ordered from most concrete to most abstract). Using a combination of LIWC and TreeTagger
   939→software, we counted the number of words within each abstraction level and then calculated an
   940→overall abstraction score per participant (Schmid, 1994). Scores ranged from 1 (most concrete) to
   941→5 (most abstract), with higher values indicating more abstract thought expression.
   942→Finally, we estimated the valence of each thought using a pre-trained sentiment analysis model
   943→(cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment; Barbieri et al., 2020). The model outputs probabilities
   944→for negative, neutral, and positive sentiment; we derived a continuous valence score for each
   945→thought by subtracting the probability of negative sentiment from the probability of positive
   946→sentiment, yielding a score ranging from −1 to 1. These scores were then averaged within each
   947→Think-Aloud session to obtain a session-level measure of emotional valence.
   948→b. Thought Dynamics
   949→To characterize thought dynamics, or how thoughts unfolded over time and progressed from one
   950→to the next, we extracted several complementary measures from each Think Aloud session. We
   951→first quantified thought production using the total number of discrete thoughts and overall word
   952→count. Using word-level timing from our transcription pipeline, we then derived fine-grained
   953→temporal measures indexing the rate of thought production: average thought duration (i.e., time
   954→from thought onset to offset) and the inter-thought interval (i.e., time between the end of one
   955→thought and the start of the next). Together, these measures capture the temporal pacing of
   956→thoughts.
   957→We also extracted measures capturing the likelihood of transitioning between different valence
   958→states in thought. As described above, we estimated the valence of each thought using a
   959→
   960→EMOTION REGULATION SIGNATURES IN SPONTANEOUS THOUGHT
   961→32
   962→pre-trained sentiment analysis model (cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment; Barbieri et al.,
   963→2020), which outputs probabilities for three classes (negative, neutral, positive). Each thought
   964→was assigned to the valence class with the highest probability. We then applied Markov chain
   965→analysis to the resulting valence sequences. Markov models estimate transition probabilities
   966→between states across successive observations, making them well suited for capturing how
   967→emotion dynamics evolve over time. From these models, we derived transition probabilities
   968→between all pairs of valence states (e.g., negative → neutral, neutral → positive).
   969→Next, to assess how participants moved between thoughts over time, we quantified the semantic
   970→similarity between thoughts and derived two measures that have been used in prior work to
   971→characterize thought dynamics: subsequent thought similarity and thought expansiveness. Each
   972→thought was embedded in a high-dimensional semantic space using the all-mpnet-base-v2
   973→Hugging Face sentence-transformer model, and cosine similarity between embeddings was used
   974→as the common measure of semantic relatedness, with higher values indicating greater overlap in
   975→meaning. Average subsequent thought similarity indexed local transitions in the stream of
   976→thought, defined as the mean cosine similarity between successive thoughts. Thought
   977→expansiveness indexed the overall semantic structure of participants’ thought streams, reflecting
   978→how broadly thoughts spanned semantic space across the session, as captured by network-level
   979→properties of a semantic network whose edge weights were defined by pairwise cosine similarity
   980→between thoughts (see Corbani et al., 2026 for full methodological details).
   981→Finally, to characterize the lexical diversity of participants’ thoughts, we computed two measures
   982→of lexical complexity for each thought. To focus on content-bearing language, we first removed
   983→function words. We then calculated the type–token ratio (TTR) for content words, defined as the
   984→number of unique content words divided by the total number of content words within a thought,
   985→indexing the extent to which participants used a varied vocabulary independent of function word
   986→usage. In addition, we computed lexical entropy for content words, derived from the frequency
   987→distribution of content words within a thought, which quantified the evenness of word usage.
   988→Higher entropy values indicate more even use of content words, whereas lower values reflect
   989→greater repetition of a smaller subset of words. Unlike type–token ratio, lexical entropy captures
   990→information from the full frequency distribution of content words rather than only the number of
   991→unique word types.

  1271→In the main text, we focused on average precision as our primary measure of classification performance because the models output
  1272→probabilities reflecting the likelihood that a strategy was used, rather than binary yes/no classifications directly. To compute metrics
  1273→such as accuracy, precision, recall, specificity, and F1 score, these predicted probabilities must first be converted into binary
  1274→predictions by selecting a probability threshold above which a session is classified as “strategy used” and below which it is classified
  1275→as “strategy not used.” Different thresholds produce different tradeoffs between precision and recall. Average precision avoids relying
  1276→on a single threshold by evaluating model performance across all possible probability thresholds, making it particularly informative
  1277→for imbalanced binary classification problems in which some strategies were relatively infrequent. By contrast, threshold-dependent
  1278→metrics can sometimes be misleading under class imbalance. For example, high specificity or accuracy can occur even when models
  1279→rarely classify positive cases, and precision may be undefined when no positive predictions are made. We therefore report these
  1280→additional threshold-dependent metrics primarily to provide a fuller characterization of model performance across datasets. Table S5
  1281→reports the probability threshold used to convert model output probabilities into binary predictions for each emotion regulation
  1282→signature, along with additional threshold-dependent performance metrics including ROC-AUC, accuracy, precision, recall,
  1283→specificity, and F1 score across datasets.

  1566→Emotion Regulation Signatures Predict Emotional Outcomes Above and Beyond
  1567→Self-Reported Strategy Use
  1568→To evaluate whether emotion regulation signatures captured information beyond participants’
  1569→explicit self-reports of strategy use, we compared models using signature expression to
  1570→analogous models using binary self-reported strategy use (used vs. not used) as predictors. For
  1571→emotional improvement, we fit separate standardized regression models predicting change in
  1572→valence from self-reported use of each strategy while controlling for initial valence, mirroring the
  1573→analyses conducted using signature expression. We conducted analogous analyses predicting
  1574→anxiety. Figures S3 and S4 compare the standardized regression coefficients obtained from
  1575→self-reported strategy use and signature expression across datasets, and Tables S9 and S10 report
  1576→the full regression results for the self-report models.
  1577→Overall, emotion regulation signatures more consistently predicted emotional outcomes than
  1578→self-reported strategy use. Signature expression yielded a greater number of significant
  1579→associations with both emotional improvement and anxiety across datasets, whereas self-reported
  1580→strategy use showed relatively few significant relationships. In addition, models incorporating all
  1581→emotion regulation signatures jointly explained substantially more variance in anxiety and, in
  1582→most cases, emotional improvement than analogous models using self-reported strategy use alone
  1583→(Figures S3A and S4A). The only exception was emotional improvement on non-induction days,
  1584→for which the combined self-report and signature models explained equivalent variance.
