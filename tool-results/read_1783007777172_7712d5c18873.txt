    12тЖТ    12тЖТ10 Abstract:
    13тЖТ    13тЖТ11 Obsessive thinking about a loved one (i.e., constant, involuntary, repetitive thoughts about the loved
    14тЖТ    14тЖТ12 one) is a defining feature of romantic love yet remains markedly under-researched. This article
    15тЖТ    15тЖТ13 provides the first review of obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love, organising the
    16тЖТ    16тЖТ14 disparate literature using an "Eight Perspectives" approach spanning evolutionary history,
    17тЖТ    17тЖТ15 evolutionary functions, development, mechanisms, ecology, cognitions, emotions, and behaviour. We
    18тЖТ    18тЖТ16 argue that obsessive thinking about a loved one likely emerged by co-opting mother-infant bonding
    19тЖТ    19тЖТ17 circuitry, appears across cultures and across the lifespan, and may have evolved to promote
    20тЖТ    20тЖТ18 proximity-seeking and sustained orientation toward a loved one. Contrary to long-standing claims,
    21тЖТ    21тЖТ19 the mechanisms underlying obsessive thinking in romantic love are unlikely to be shared with
    22тЖТ    22тЖТ20 obsessive-compulsive disorder. People who are in love think about their loved one for approximately
    23тЖТ    23тЖТ21 50% of their waking hours, and this thinking is associated with romantic (but not companionate)
    24тЖТ    24тЖТ22 love, distraction, proximity-seeking behaviour, and analgesia. Whether such thinking is experienced
    25тЖТ    25тЖТ23 as bothersome or enjoyable depends less on its frequency than on companionate love and
    26тЖТ    26тЖТ24 reciprocation. We conclude by identifying priorities for future research across all eight perspectives.

    36тЖТ    36тЖТ33 1 Introduction
    37тЖТ    37тЖТ34 Obsessive thinking about a loved one is a commonly cited feature of romantic love (Bajoghli et al.,
    38тЖТ    38тЖТ35 2014; Bode, 2023; Bode & Kushnick, 2021b; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007; Fisher, 2000;
    39тЖТ    39тЖТ36 Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al., 2002; Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986; Langeslag et al., 2012; Nilakantan et al.,
    40тЖТ    40тЖТ37 2014). Romantic love, generally, and obsessive thinking, in particular, is under-researched. This
    41тЖТ    41тЖТ38 article provides the first review, to our knowledge, of obsessive thinking in romantic love. It uses an
    42тЖТ    42тЖТ39 тАЬeight perspectivesтАЭ framework to outline the evolutionary history, development across the lifespan,
    43тЖТ    43тЖТ40 mechanisms, cognitions, emotions, behaviour, ecology, and evolutionary functions of obsessive
    44тЖТ    44тЖТ41 thinking about a loved one. In so doing, it provides a summary of the state of the art of research on
    45тЖТ    45тЖТ42 obsessive thinking in romantic love. We conclude by outlining areas for future research that, if
    46тЖТ    46тЖТ43 pursued, will substantially increase our understanding of this fundamental feature of romantic love.
    47тЖТ    47тЖТ44 1.1 Romantic Love
    48тЖТ    48тЖТ45 Romantic love has been defined in line with the English definition (Collins, 2025), American
    49тЖТ    49тЖТ46 Psychological Association definition (American Psychological Association, 2018), the conventions
    50тЖТ    50тЖТ47 in biopsychology (Bode & Kowal, 2023) and evolutionary social and behavioural sciences (Bode,
    51тЖТ    51тЖТ48 2026; Bode, Kavanagh, et al., 2025), and the meaning conveyed by one of the founders of
    52тЖТ    52тЖТ49 relationship science (Berscheid, 2010):
    53тЖТ    53тЖТ50 [тАж] a motivational state typically associated with a desire for long-term mating with a
    54тЖТ    54тЖТ51 particular individual. It occurs across the lifespan and is associated with distinctive cognitive,
    55тЖТ    55тЖТ52 emotional, behavioral, social, genetic, neural, and endocrine activity in both sexes. Throughout
    56тЖТ    56тЖТ53 much of the life course, it serves mate choice, courtship, sex, and pair-bonding functions. It is a
    57тЖТ    57тЖТ54 suite of adaptations and by-products that arose sometime during the recent evolutionary history
    58тЖТ    58тЖТ55 of humans (Bode & Kushnick, 2021b, p. 21).
    59тЖТ    59тЖТ56 Romantic love is commonly associated with the early stages of a romantic relationship (Bode &
    60тЖТ    60тЖТ57 Kowal, 2023) but can exist in the absence of a romantic relationship (Bringle et al., 2013; Hill et al.,
    61тЖТ    61тЖТ58 1997) or after many years or decades (Acevedo & Aron, 2009; Acevedo et al., 2012; OтАЩLeary et al.,
    62тЖТ    62тЖТ59 2011). Romantic love decreases with relationship duration and progression (Langeslag et al., 2013).
    63тЖТ    63тЖТ60 It is sometimes referred to as тАЬpassionate love,тАЭ тАЬinfatuationтАЭ, or тАЬlimerenceтАЭ by some psychologists
    64тЖТ    64тЖТ61 and as being тАЬin loveтАЭ by the general population.
    65тЖТ    65тЖТ62 Romantic love can be contrasted with companionate love (sometimes also referred to as
    66тЖТ    66тЖТ63 тАЬattachmentтАЭ). Companionate love is the comforting feeling of emotional bonding with another
    67тЖТ    67тЖТ64 person that is a much calmer state than romantic love (Berscheid, 2010; Fisher, 1998; Hatfield,
    68тЖТ    68тЖТ65 1988). It is a feature of an established pair bond. Companionate love takes time to develop, so it
    69тЖТ    69тЖТ66 increases with relationship duration and progression (Langeslag et al., 2013).
    70тЖТ    70тЖТ67 1.2 Obsessive Thinking About a Loved One
    71тЖТ    71тЖТ68 Obsessive thinking about a loved one has been identified as a common aspect of romantic love since
    72тЖТ    72тЖТ69 early work on the topic. Dorothy Tennov (Tennov, 1979), in her seminal book, Love and Limerence,
    73тЖТ    73тЖТ70 described obsessive thinking as a necessary component of intense romantic love (which she referred
    74тЖТ    74тЖТ71 to as limerence). She referred to obsessive thinking about a loved one as тАЬintrusive thinkingтАЭ and
    75тЖТ    75тЖТ72 describes it as constant, involuntary, and repetitive. The importance of obsessive thinking to
    76тЖТ    76тЖТ2
    77тЖТ    77тЖТ

    79тЖТ    79тЖТ74 of romantic love (Passionate Love Scale; (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986), early theorising (Fisher, 2000;
    80тЖТ    80тЖТ75 Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al., 2002), empirical studies (e.g., Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015;
    81тЖТ    81тЖТ76 Brand et al., 2007; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025; Langeslag et al., 2012; Nilakantan et al., 2014), and
    82тЖТ    82тЖТ77 recent work on the semantics (Bode & Kushnick, 2021b) and theory (Bode, 2023) of romantic love.
    83тЖТ    83тЖТ78 The specific cognitive features of obsessive thinking about a loved one are considered in detail
    84тЖТ    84тЖТ79 below, however, it is commonly operationalised in terms of percentage of waking hours spent

    88тЖТ    88тЖТ83 Scale (YBOCS) about thinking about a loved one as a proxy for love intensity in their studies (e.g.,
    89тЖТ    89тЖТ84 Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007).

   100тЖТ   100тЖТ95 And obsessive thinking about a loved one may not be exclusively associated with an obsessive love

   110тЖТ   110тЖТ105 1.3 тАЬEight PerspectivesтАЭ Approach
   111тЖТ   111тЖТ106 This review uses an "Eight Perspectives" approach (Bode & Kowal, 2026) to describe obsessive
   112тЖТ   112тЖТ107 thinking about a loved one in romantic love. The Eight Perspectives approach extends Tinbergen's
   113тЖТ   113тЖТ108 Four Questions (Tinbergen, 1963; i.e., evolutionary history, evolutionary functions, development
   114тЖТ   114тЖТ109 across the lifespan, and mechanisms) by adding dedicated consideration of ecology, emotions,
   115тЖТ   115тЖТ110 cognitions, and behaviour (Bode & Kowal, 2026). Together, these eight perspectives span ultimate
   116тЖТ   116тЖТ111 causes (why a phenomenon evolved), proximate causes (how it works), and phenotypic outcomes
   117тЖТ   117тЖТ112 (how it is expressed), and are aligned with the analytical traditions of ethology, ecology, psychology,
   118тЖТ   118тЖТ113 and behavioural science. The approach was designed to provide useful, simple, and flexible
   119тЖТ   119тЖТ3
   120тЖТ   120тЖТ1 Items 5, 19, and 21 of the PLS (i.e., тАЬSometimes I feel I canтАЩt control my thoughts; they are obsessively on _____.тАЭ
   121тЖТ   121тЖТтАЬ_____ always seems to be on my mind.тАЭ, and тАЬI sometimes find it difficult to concentrate on work because thoughts of
   122тЖТ   122тЖТ_____ occupy my mind.тАЭ)
   123тЖТ   123тЖТ
   124тЖТ   124тЖТ114 organisation of findings about complex human psychobehavioural phenomena, and the present
   125тЖТ   125тЖТ115 review represents its first application since its publication. As will become apparent, the approach is
   126тЖТ   126тЖТ116 well suited to obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love; it organises a disparate literature
   127тЖТ   127тЖТ117 into an interpretable structure, makes gaps in knowledge immediately visible, and invites
   128тЖТ   128тЖТ118 contributions from researchers across disciplines who might not otherwise see their work as
   129тЖТ   129тЖТ119 connected.
   130тЖТ   130тЖТ120 The eight perspectives are presented in an order that traces the causes and consequences of the
   131тЖТ   131тЖТ121 biological, ecological, psychological, and behavioural activity involved in obsessive thinking about a
   132тЖТ   132тЖТ122 loved one. Ultimate causes come first: evolutionary history situates the phenomenon in deep time,
   133тЖТ   133тЖТ123 and evolutionary functions identify the adaptive problems it may have solved. Proximate causes
   134тЖТ   134тЖТ124 follow: development describes how obsessive thinking emerges and changes across the lifespan,
   135тЖТ   135тЖТ125 biological mechanisms describe the biological and psychological processes that produce it, and
   136тЖТ   136тЖТ126 ecology describes how the surrounding environment shapes its expression. The phenotypic outcomes
   137тЖТ   137тЖТ127 are presented last: cognitions and emotions are the most direct expressions of the proximate
   138тЖТ   138тЖТ128 mechanisms, and these in turn give rise to behaviour. Figure 1 presents the eight perspectives in the
   139тЖТ   139тЖТ129 order in which they appear.

   143тЖТ   143тЖТ133 2 Evolutionary History
   144тЖТ   144тЖТ134 There is not much to say about the evolutionary history of obsessive thinking about a loved one. To
   145тЖТ   145тЖТ135 our knowledge, only one scientific text has commented on the evolution of obsessive thinking. Bode
   146тЖТ   146тЖТ136 (A. Bode, 2023), in an hypothesis and theory article, suggested that romantic love evolved by co-
   147тЖТ   147тЖТ137 opting mother-infant bonding. He suggests that three components of mother infant bonding (i.e.,
   148тЖТ   148тЖТ138 bonding attraction, obsessive thinking, and attachment) were replicated from mother-infant bonding
   149тЖТ   149тЖТ139 and took on a new function, romantic love. More recent work refines the theory by suggesting that
   150тЖТ   150тЖТ140 genes were not replicated, but rather, regulatory DNA responsible for particular neural circuitry was
   151тЖТ   151тЖТ141 co-opted (Felesina & Bode, 2026). The theory is yet to be tested, but the available evidence upon
   152тЖТ   152тЖТ142 which the theory is based is convincing. The theory suggests that processes of mutation and co-
   153тЖТ   153тЖТ143 option provide a reproductive and survival benefit to individuals expressing obsessive thinking in
   154тЖТ   154тЖТ144 romantic love and natural selection made it a ubiquitous feature of modern humans (Homo sapiens).
   155тЖТ   155тЖТ145 Although no one has directly investigated it, it appears that obsessive thinking is present in romantic
   156тЖТ   156тЖТ146 love cross-culturally and across ethnic groups. The publicly available Romantic Love Survey 2022
   157тЖТ   157тЖТ147 (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022) included data from 1,556 people self-reporting experiencing romantic love
   158тЖТ   158тЖТ4
   159тЖТ   159тЖТ
   160тЖТ   160тЖТ148 in 33 countries, mainly in North America, Western and Central Europe, and South Africa. All
   161тЖТ   161тЖТ149 indications are that obsessive thinking is present in all the countries sampled, with participants in that
   162тЖТ   162тЖТ150 survey reporting that they thought about their loved one on average 49.75% of their waking hours.
   163тЖТ   163тЖТ151 The lowest proportion of time spent thinking about their loved one was among Austrians (n = 10)
   164тЖТ   164тЖТ152 with an average of 35.10% of waking hours. While this is indicative of cross cultural and ethnic
   165тЖТ   165тЖТ153 expression, it will be necessary to demonstrate the presence of obsessive thinking about a loved one
   166тЖТ   166тЖТ154 in a greater number of ethnic groups, including in small scale societies, before we can confidently
   167тЖТ   167тЖТ155 claim it is a universal.
   168тЖТ   168тЖТ156 That obsessive thinking about a loved one appears to be a feature of romantic love cross-culturally
   169тЖТ   169тЖТ157 indicates that it almost certainly emerged prior to the emergence of modern humans at least by
   170тЖТ   170тЖТ158 300,00 years ago (Stringer, 2016). Romantic love serves the function of promoting social monogamy,
   171тЖТ   171тЖТ159 and social monogamy is associated with reduced sexual dimorphism (Alexander et al., 1979). This
   172тЖТ   172тЖТ160 occurred most drastically during the reign of Homo erectus, the commencement of which has been
   173тЖТ   173тЖТ161 dated to about 2 MYA (Ant├│n, 2003). If obsessive thinking about a loved one emerged at the same
   174тЖТ   174тЖТ162 time as the original suite of adaptations and by-products that constitute romantic love (Felesina &
   175тЖТ   175тЖТ163 Bode, 2026), we can assume it first emerged within the past 1.5-2 million years (Plavcan, 2012;
   176тЖТ   176тЖТ164 Villmoare et al., 2019).
   177тЖТ   177тЖТ165 One additional feature of obsessive thinking about a loved oneтАЩs evolutionary history relates to
   178тЖТ   178тЖТ166 sexual selection. Females have been shown to think about their partner noticeably more than males
   179тЖТ   179тЖТ167 (females = 54% of waking hours cf males = 45% (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025), and in that study,
   180тЖТ   180тЖТ168 biological sex explained 4% of the variation in how long individuals spend of their waking hours
   181тЖТ   181тЖТ169 thinking about their loved one. The authors suggest this may be the result of sexual selection,
   182тЖТ   182тЖТ170 although we canтАЩt discount it could be partially explained by sex differences in mechanisms that play
   183тЖТ   183тЖТ171 a role in mother-infant bonding. It may be that females are more suited to obsessive thinking about
   184тЖТ   184тЖТ172 their loved one because they have refined this process in relation to infants over the past 200 million
   185тЖТ   185тЖТ173 years (Grossnickle et al., 2019).
   186тЖТ   186тЖТ174 3 Evolutionary Functions
   187тЖТ   187тЖТ175 There has not been much theorising or empirical testing about the evolutionary functions of obsessive
   188тЖТ   188тЖТ176 thinking about a loved one in romantic love. Drawing on the likely evolutionary history of romantic
   189тЖТ   189тЖТ177 love, Bode (2023) highlights that obsessive thinking about an infant ensures that the mother does not
   190тЖТ   190тЖТ178 forget about her infant, and can in turn engage in the activities (i.e., caregiving) necessary to keep it
   191тЖТ   191тЖТ179 alive. The specific evolutionary functions in romantic love, however, are less clear. Individuals in
   192тЖТ   192тЖТ180 love think about their loved one about 50% of their waking hours (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025),
   193тЖТ   193тЖТ181 indicating that this intensity may have been optimally adaptive in the environment in which romantic
   194тЖТ   194тЖТ182 love emerged and evolved (Waynforth, 2023). Why we need to think about our partner that much is a
   195тЖТ   195тЖТ183 mystery when it seems feasible that thinking about them only 10% of our waking hours in a regular
   196тЖТ   196тЖТ184 manner would have a similar effect.
   197тЖТ   197тЖТ185 Tennov (Tennov, 1979) suggested obsessive thinking about a loved one maintains focus on the loved
   198тЖТ   198тЖТ186 one, sustains the emotional intensity of the loving experience, and keeps the person oriented toward
   199тЖТ   199тЖТ187 pursuing or securing reciprocation. Focussing energies on the loved one (Fisher, 1998) is also likely
   200тЖТ   200тЖТ188 to have direct benefits to reproductive fitness in circumstances when the love can be, or is,
   201тЖТ   201тЖТ189 reciprocated by promoting proximity-seeking, courtship, sex, and caregiving. Obsessive thinking
   202тЖТ   202тЖТ190 about a loved one also means that stimuli about the loved one are regularly present, in turn activating
   203тЖТ   203тЖТ191 neurobiological responses to the loved one, reinforcing romantic love. Empirical support for the
   204тЖТ   204тЖТ5
   205тЖТ   205тЖТ
   206тЖТ   206тЖТ192 proximity-seeking function comes from Valshtein et al. (2020), who found that obsessive thinking
   207тЖТ   207тЖТ193 predicted prospective proximity-seeking behaviour (see Behaviour, below). This is consistent with
   208тЖТ   208тЖТ194 the proposal that obsessive thinking promotes reproductively relevant approach behaviour toward the
   209тЖТ   209тЖТ195 loved one.
   210тЖТ   210тЖТ196 Gonzaga et al. (2008) have shown that the experience of love in romantic relationships, but not
   211тЖТ   211тЖТ197 sexual desire, facilitates the suppression of thoughts about attractive alternatives and reduces memory
   212тЖТ   212тЖТ198 for their attractiveness-related features. It is plausible that obsessive thinking about the beloved
   213тЖТ   213тЖТ199 contributes to this effect by occupying cognitive resources that would otherwise be available for
   214тЖТ   214тЖТ200 attending to rival stimuli. However, participants in that study had been in relationships for an average
   215тЖТ   215тЖТ201 of nearly three years, meaning many were unlikely to still be experiencing romantic love and its
   216тЖТ   216тЖТ202 characteristic obsessive thinking. Whether this suppression effect is stronger during the early,
   217тЖТ   217тЖТ203 obsessive-thinking-intensive phase of romantic love remains untested.

   230тЖТ   230тЖТ216 Although obsessive thinking about a loved one is most often studied in prototypical romantic love

   236тЖТ   236тЖТ222 relationship has not been systematically investigated and represents a priority for future research.
   237тЖТ   237тЖТ223 5 Biological Mechanisms
   238тЖТ   238тЖТ224 Almost nothing is known about the biological mechanisms that cause obsessive thinking about a
   239тЖТ   239тЖТ225 loved one in romantic love. One fMRI study (Fujisaki et al., 2026) suggests that the anterior insula
   240тЖТ   240тЖТ226 (aINS) may play a role: in males in the early stages of a romantic relationship, the distinctiveness of
   241тЖТ   241тЖТ227 partner-specific neural representations in the aINS was positively associated with the frequency of
   242тЖТ   242тЖТ228 intrusive thoughts about a partner measured via experience sampling. This finding awaits replication
   243тЖТ   243тЖТ229 and extension to female samples.

   246тЖТ   246тЖТ232 found in OCD. They attributed it to lower levels of serotonin during romantic love. Claimed evidence

   260тЖТ   260тЖТ244 found higher plasma and serum serotonin levels in females experiencing romantic love than female

   265тЖТ   265тЖТ249 correlational study (Bode, Kowal, et al., 2025) finding that individuals experiencing romantic love do
   266тЖТ   266тЖТ250 not differ in obsessive thinking regardless of whether they are taking selective serotonin reuptake
   267тЖТ   267тЖТ251 inhibitors (SSRIs) or not. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors are a common treatment for OCD
   268тЖТ   268тЖТ252 (Del Casale et al., 2019). If they alleviate intrusive thinking in OCD, and intrusive thinking in OCD
   269тЖТ   269тЖТ253 and obsessive thinking in romantic love are mechanistically linked, it follows that SSRIs would
   270тЖТ   270тЖТ254 reduce obsessive thinking in romantic love. Because they donтАЩt, this is strong evidence that the
   271тЖТ   271тЖТ255 mechanisms causing these two phenomena are different.
   272тЖТ   272тЖТ256 6 Ecology
   273тЖТ   273тЖТ257 There is some evidence that ecology (in the form of national-level indictors) can influence the
   274тЖТ   274тЖТ258 expression of obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love. In a cross-sectional study
   275тЖТ   275тЖТ259 investigating sex differences in romantic love using the Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode, Luoto, et
   276тЖТ   276тЖТ260 al., 2025), The United Nations Gender Inequality Index was used as a control in a hierarchical linear
   277тЖТ   277тЖТ261 regression predicting obsessive thinking about a loved one. In the final model, Gender Inequality
   278тЖТ   278тЖТ262 Index was the second largest predictor of obsessive thinking (after biological sex), indicating that

   280тЖТ   280тЖТ264 This is consistent with another study (Sorokowski et al., 2023) demonstrating associations between

   282тЖТ   282тЖТ266 relationships (i.e., intimacy, passion, and commitment). This indicates that the cultural environment
   283тЖТ   283тЖТ267 may have a causal influence on how obsessive thinking out a loved one expresses. Future studies
   284тЖТ   284тЖТ268 should investigate the association between modernisation and collectivism on obsessive thinking

   286тЖТ   286тЖТ270 7 Cognitions
   287тЖТ   287тЖТ271 According to Tennov (1979), obsessive thinking about the loved one (what she called the тАЬlimerent
   288тЖТ   288тЖТ272 object;тАЭ i.e., the person one feels limerence for) is a main component of romantic love. She relates it
   289тЖТ   289тЖТ273 to compulsive daydreaming about, preoccupation with, and uncontrollable fantasizing about the
   290тЖТ   290тЖТ274 limerent object. Likewise, Fisher (Fisher, 1998) and Fisher et al. (Fisher et al., 2002) list obsessive
   291тЖТ   291тЖТ275 thinking about a loved one as one of the characteristics of romantic love. Here, we review studies that
   292тЖТ   292тЖТ276 have assessed this cognitive aspect of romantic love.
   293тЖТ   293тЖТ7
   294тЖТ   294тЖТ
   295тЖТ   295тЖТ277 Several studies have assessed how much people think about their loved one. In a study of participants
   296тЖТ   296тЖТ278 who had ever experienced a romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), thoughts about the ex-partner
   297тЖТ   297тЖТ279 occurred several times per week on average and were fairly vivid, with nostalgia, loneliness, certain
   298тЖТ   298тЖТ280 songs, sadness, celebrations, anxiety, and boredom endorsed as common triggers. Among
   299тЖТ   299тЖТ281 participants currently in love, self-reported estimates of time spent thinking about the loved one have
   300тЖТ   300тЖТ282 ranged widely: 65% of waking hours in participants in love for 9 months or less (Langeslag et al.,
   301тЖТ   301тЖТ283 2012), 50тАУ61% in participants in a relationship for 9 months or less (Nilakantan et al., 2014), 67% in
   302тЖТ   302тЖТ284 participants who were in love (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), 50% in young adults in love for less than
   303тЖТ   303тЖТ285 two years from 33 countries (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025), and 74% (Langeslag, Submitted) and 77%
   304тЖТ   304тЖТ286 (Evans et al. 2026) in participants experiencing limerence . Lower estimates of 21% and 49% come
   305тЖТ   305тЖТ287 from experience sampling studies in male participants in relationships of 7 months or less (Fujisaki et
   306тЖТ   306тЖТ288 al., 2026) and participants experiencing limerence (Evans et al., 2026), respectively. These lower
   307тЖТ   307тЖТ289 figures may reflect reduced memory bias from capturing experiences in real-time. In sum, average
   308тЖТ   308тЖТ290 self-reported thinking about the loved one ranged from 21 to 77% of the time across studies, with
   309тЖТ   309тЖТ291 individual responses ranging from 0 to 100% indicating that, for some individuals, obsessive thinking
   310тЖТ   310тЖТ292 about a loved one can be all-consuming.
   311тЖТ   311тЖТ293 Time spent thinking about the loved one has been positively associated with romantic love intensity
   312тЖТ   312тЖТ294 (r = .25; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025) and limerence intensity (r = .37; Langeslag, Submitted and r =
   313тЖТ   313тЖТ295 .39; Evans et al., 2026) but not with companionate love intensity (r = .003; Langeslag & Philippi,
   314тЖТ   314тЖТ296 2025). This indicates that obsessive thinking about a loved one is associated with romantic love
   315тЖТ   315тЖТ297 specifically rather than companionate love, although the correlations are far from perfect, and we
   316тЖТ   316тЖТ298 caution against using obsessive thinking about a loved one as a proxy for love intensity as has been
   317тЖТ   317тЖТ299 done in some studies (i.e., Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007). Obsessive
   318тЖТ   318тЖТ300 thinking about a loved one has also been shown to increase following threat to relatedness (i.e., the
   319тЖТ   319тЖТ301 basic psychological need to feel connected to, cared for by, and accepted by others) and negative
   320тЖТ   320тЖТ302 fantasizing about the loved one across a series of experimental studies using relatedness need
   321тЖТ   321тЖТ303 manipulations and directed fantasy tasks (Valshtein et al., 2020).
   322тЖТ   322тЖТ304 People think about their loved one during cognitive tasks unrelated to the loved one. In one study
   323тЖТ   323тЖТ305 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), participants who were in love reported thinking about their loved one
   324тЖТ   324тЖТ306 on 42% of thought probes and 34% of free responses during a sustained attention response task.
   325тЖТ   325тЖТ307 Interestingly, both measures were only weakly associated with obsessive thinking about a loved one
   326тЖТ   326тЖТ308 in general (both rs < .22), suggesting these two phenomena are only somewhat coupled. In studies of
   327тЖТ   327тЖТ309 participants who had experienced a break-up and were instructed to suppress thoughts of their ex-
   328тЖТ   328тЖТ310 partner (Baird et al., 2013), participants still thought about their ex-partner 14тАУ22% of the time. High
   329тЖТ   329тЖТ311 cognitive load increased probe-caught (but not self-caught) thoughts about the ex-partner, and desire
   330тЖТ   330тЖТ312 to reconcile was positively associated with probe-caught thinking, suggesting that people who want
   331тЖТ   331тЖТ313 to reconcile think about their ex-partner more тАФ and are less aware of doing so. The behavioural
   332тЖТ   332тЖТ314 consequences of this obsessive thinking about a loved one are addressed below.
   333тЖТ   333тЖТ315 8 Emotions
   334тЖТ   334тЖТ316 Some terms analogous with "obsessive thinking about a loved one" have negative connotation, (e.g.,
   335тЖТ   335тЖТ317 "intrusive thinking", "preoccupation", and "pathological obsession" (Fisher et al., 2002; Nilakantan et
   336тЖТ   336тЖТ318 al., 2014; Tennov, 1979). Intrusive thinking implies recurrent and persistent thoughts that are at least
   337тЖТ   337тЖТ319 sometimes unwanted and distressing, and that the individual attempts to ignore or suppress
   338тЖТ   338тЖТ320 (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Indeed, in the study of participants who had experienced a
   339тЖТ   339тЖТ321 romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), participants endorsed frightened, guilty, remorseful,
   340тЖТ   340тЖТ8
   341тЖТ   341тЖТ
   342тЖТ   342тЖТ322 ashamed, anxious, and grateful as common feelings when thinking about the ex-partner. However,
   343тЖТ   343тЖТ323 thinking about a loved one is not always bothersome. In a study of participants experiencing
   344тЖТ   344тЖТ324 limerence recruited through internet limerence communities (Langeslag, Submitted), the mean score
   345тЖТ   345тЖТ325 on a slider from "it bothers me" to "I enjoy it" was 41 out of 100, significantly below the midpoint.
   346тЖТ   346тЖТ326 Thinking about the loved one bothered them. In contrast, in a study of participants who were in love
   347тЖТ   347тЖТ327 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), the mean on the same slider was 84 out of 100, significantly above the
   348тЖТ   348тЖТ328 midpoint. These participants enjoyed thinking about their loved one.
   349тЖТ   349тЖТ329 What might determine whether thinking about the loved one has positive or negative emotional
   350тЖТ   350тЖТ330 effects? Across these two studies (Langeslag, Submitted; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), enjoyment of
   351тЖТ   351тЖТ331 thinking about the loved one did not correlate positively with romantic love intensity but did
   352тЖТ   352тЖТ332 correlate positively with companionate love intensity as measured with the IAS attachment scale
   353тЖТ   353тЖТ333 (Langeslag et al., 2013). Notably, only 5% of the limerence participants were in a romantic
   354тЖТ   354тЖТ334 relationship with their loved one, compared with 92% of the in-love participants, while time spent
   355тЖТ   355тЖТ335 thinking about the loved one did not differ drastically between the two samples (74% vs. 67%). It
   356тЖТ   356тЖТ336 therefore seems that whether people enjoy thinking about their loved one or whether it bothers them
   357тЖТ   357тЖТ337 depends more on companionate love intensity and whether they are in a romantic relationship with
   358тЖТ   358тЖТ338 the loved one than on how much they are thinking about them.
   359тЖТ   359тЖТ339 The rewarding character of thinking about a loved one is also evident in its analgesic effects. In two
   360тЖТ   360тЖТ340 studies (Nilakantan et al., 2014), participants experienced less pain from thermal stimuli when
   361тЖТ   361тЖТ341 viewing pictures of their romantic partner than of an acquaintance, and this analgesia was positively
   362тЖТ   362тЖТ342 correlated with the percentage of waking hours spent thinking about their partner. The mechanism
   363тЖТ   363тЖТ343 appears to be activation of brain reward regions rather than distraction (Younger et al., 2010),
   364тЖТ   364тЖТ344 consistent with the enjoyment many people report when thinking about their loved one.
   365тЖТ   365тЖТ345 There have also been some studies linking obsessive thinking about a loved one with features
   366тЖТ   366тЖТ346 analogous to symptoms of mood disorders. In one study (Bode & Kavanagh, 2025) using the
   367тЖТ   367тЖТ347 Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022), membership of clusters associated with
   368тЖТ   368тЖТ348 higher obsessive thinking about the loved one was positively associated with hypomanic-like features
   369тЖТ   369тЖТ349 (which are closely related to elevated mood (Faurholt-Jepsen et al., 2020)). This is consistent with
   370тЖТ   370тЖТ350 previous studies (Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007) that found a similar
   371тЖТ   371тЖТ351 association and two studies finding inconsistent associations with depression symptoms (Bajoghli et
   372тЖТ   372тЖТ352 al., 2013; Bajoghli et al., 2014).
   373тЖТ   373тЖТ353 9 Behaviour
   374тЖТ   374тЖТ354 Behavioural consequences of obsessive thinking about a loved one may be that it manifests as
   375тЖТ   375тЖТ355 reduced task performance and that it may lead to proximity-seeking behaviour. Several studies have
   376тЖТ   376тЖТ356 provided evidence that love produces behavioural distraction. Love intensity as measured with the
   377тЖТ   377тЖТ357 PLS was positively correlated with the interference effect in Stroop and flanker tasks (van
   378тЖТ   378тЖТ358 Steenbergen et al., 2014), and more obsessive thinking about a loved one in general was associated
   379тЖТ   379тЖТ359 with poorer and slower performance on a short-term memory task (Langeslag & van Strien, 2020)
   380тЖТ   380тЖТ360 and with reduced ability to withhold responses in a sustained attention response task (Langeslag &
   381тЖТ   381тЖТ361 Philippi, 2025). These studies show that love and thinking about a loved one in general (rather than
   382тЖТ   382тЖТ362 during the task specifically) are associated with behavioural distraction. In addition, the more
   383тЖТ   383тЖТ363 participants thought about their loved one during the sustained attention response task as rated by
   384тЖТ   384тЖТ364 coders, the slower their responses were (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and probe-caught thinking
   385тЖТ   385тЖТ365 about an ex-partner during reading was associated with reduced reading comprehension (Baird et al.,
   386тЖТ   386тЖТ9
   387тЖТ   387тЖТ
   388тЖТ   388тЖТ366 2013). Interestingly, self-caught thinking about the ex-partner was not associated with reduced
   389тЖТ   389тЖТ367 reading comprehension (Baird et al., 2013), which suggests that thoughts about the loved one that the
   390тЖТ   390тЖТ368 person is unaware of are particularly disruptive to performance. These studies suggest that thinking
   391тЖТ   391тЖТ369 about a loved one during an unrelated task specifically is distracting. Behavioural distraction has
   392тЖТ   392тЖТ370 clear implications for workplace productivity and engagement with the non-romantic social
   393тЖТ   393тЖТ371 environment.
   394тЖТ   394тЖТ372 Romantic love is also associated with proximity-seeking behaviours that vary in severity, with
   395тЖТ   395тЖТ373 stalking as an example of a severe proximity-seeking behaviour. Valshtein et al. (2020) assessed
   396тЖТ   396тЖТ374 prospective proximity-seeking behaviour in participants in a romantic relationship using 53 items
   397тЖТ   397тЖТ375 asking how likely they would be to engage in behaviours such as showing up to the loved one's house
   398тЖТ   398тЖТ376 unannounced and sending desperate messages. Across both studies, the score on a 12-item obsessive
   399тЖТ   399тЖТ377 thinking measure was positively associated with prospective proximity-seeking behaviour. We canтАЩt
   400тЖТ   400тЖТ378 assert a causal relationship because the study was cross-sectional, but this is an avenue for future
   401тЖТ   401тЖТ379 research.

   403тЖТ   403тЖТ381 Obsessive thinking about a loved one has been identified as a core feature of romantic love for more
   404тЖТ   404тЖТ382 than four decades (Tennov, 1979), is embedded in the field's most widely used measure of romantic
   405тЖТ   405тЖТ383 love (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986), and is regularly invoked in theoretical accounts of the phenomenon
   406тЖТ   406тЖТ384 (Bode, 2023; Fisher, 1998). Yet it has never been systematically reviewed. This article provides the

   411тЖТ   411тЖТ389 emerges, however, is that our understanding of this fundamental feature of romantic love remains

   525тЖТ   525тЖТ450 Obsessive thinking about a loved one is a defining feature of romantic love, yet research on it has
   526тЖТ   526тЖТ451 accumulated in a piecemeal fashion across disciplines that rarely speak to one another. This review
   527тЖТ   527тЖТ452 has used an Eight Perspectives approach to bring that scattered literature into a single structure,
   528тЖТ   528тЖТ453 clarify what is known, and make visible what is not. The phenotypic expression of obsessive thinking
   529тЖТ   529тЖТ454 is reasonably well characterised; its mechanisms, evolutionary functions, and ecology are not.
   530тЖТ   530тЖТ455 Addressing these gaps will require contributions from the evolutionary social and behavioural
   531тЖТ   531тЖТ456 sciences, neuroscience, genetics, and cross-cultural research тАФ disciplines that have, to date, largely
   532тЖТ   532тЖТ457 overlooked this striking and ubiquitous feature of human mating psychology.
