    50‚Üí    99‚Üí    99‚Üí    99‚Üíintensify, rather than dampen, the relational tone of the interaction. Emoticons and emojis, as a form of
    51‚Üí   100‚Üí   100‚Üí   100‚Üíparalanguage, are one such substitute code: they fill the gap left by absent vocal and facial cues and
    52‚Üí   101‚Üí   101‚Üí   101‚Üíallow affect, on either side of the exchange, to be conveyed in text. Consistent with this, Coyle &
    53‚Üí   102‚Üí   102‚Üí   102‚ÜíCarmichael (2019), drawing on the IPM, found that when college students and a responder converged in
    54‚Üí   103‚Üí   103‚Üí   103‚Üítheir use of emoji during text-based self-disclosures, the responder was rated as more responsive, and it
    55‚Üí   104‚Üí   104‚Üí   104‚Üíis this perception of responsiveness that keeps the IPM cycle going.
    56‚Üí   105‚Üí   105‚Üí   105‚ÜíCommunication Accommodation Theory (CAT; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007; Giles &
    57‚Üí   106‚Üí   106‚Üí   106‚ÜíSmith, 1979) provides a wider account of how convergence works as a communicative process. CAT
    58‚Üí   107‚Üí   107‚Üí   107‚Üíproposes that interlocutors adjust their communication style to match one another, particularly when
    59‚Üí   108‚Üí   108‚Üí   108‚Üíthey perceive mutual similarities, want to build affiliation, or seek approval, and that this adjustment is
    60‚Üí   109‚Üí   109‚Üí   109‚Üíinterpreted by the other party as a signal of empathy and attunement (Giles & Ogay, 2007). CAT further
    61‚Üí   110‚Üí   110‚Üí   110‚Üíholds that this whole process is shaped by features of the dyad such as hierarchy, formality, and the
    62‚Üí   111‚Üí   111‚Üí   111‚Üírelative status of the two parties (Adams et al., 2018; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007), which raises
    63‚Üí   112‚Üí   112‚Üí   112‚Üíthe question of how convergence plays out when the two interlocutors do not occupy symmetric
    64‚Üí   113‚Üí   113‚Üí   113‚Üípositions.
    65‚Üí   114‚Üí   114‚Üí   114‚ÜíRole-asymmetric dyads and the helping context
    66‚Üí   115‚Üí   115‚Üí   115‚ÜíThe dyads in which emoticon reciprocity has been directly tested have been peer-like: peers and
    67‚Üí   116‚Üí   116‚Üí   116‚Üístrangers paired for self-disclosure (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019), dating partners and acquainted
    68‚Üí   117‚Üí   117‚Üí   117‚Üíinteraction partners (Stein, 2023), close friends in informal exchanges (Huh, 2025), and work colleagues
    69‚Üí   118‚Üí   118‚Üí   118‚Üí(Cavalheiro et al., 2024). In each of these settings, the two parties enter the conversation on broadly
    70‚Üí   119‚Üí   119‚Üí   119‚Üíequal terms and can adjust their style toward one another from comparable positions. In the present
    71‚Üí   120‚Üí   120‚Üí   120‚Üístudy, by contrast, we are looking at chat users who are seeking help from professional chat workers.
    72‚Üí   121‚Üí   121‚Üí   121‚ÜíThe user typically reaches out in distress to someone they do not know and starts the conversation by
    73‚Üí   122‚Üí   122‚Üí   122‚Üísharing what is troubling them. The chat worker, on the other side, comes into the conversation with
    74‚Üí   123‚Üí   123‚Üí   123‚Üícertain expectations attached to the role: professional ethics, training in how to respond, and a duty to
    75‚Üí   124‚Üí   124‚Üí   124‚Üísupport and affirm the user (Borgueta et al., 2018; Stricker, 2018). The user, who is seeking support, is in
    76‚Üí   125‚Üí   125‚Üí   125‚Üíthis sense the more dependent member of the dyad (Salmon & Young, 2009).
    77‚Üí   126‚Üí   126‚Üí   126‚Üí
    78‚Üí   127‚Üí   127‚Üí   127‚ÜíThe counselling literature has long argued that this role difference is consequential for what
    79‚Üí   128‚Üí   128‚Üí   128‚Üíeach party attends to and contributes. Sullivan (1954) characterised the therapist's role as that of a
    80‚Üí   129‚Üí   129‚Üí   129‚Üíparticipant observer, attending to the client's behaviour while also participating as a significant other in
    81‚Üí   130‚Üí   130‚Üí   130‚Üíthe relationship; the client's role, by contrast, is that of a full participant, more self-focused and drawing
    82‚Üí   131‚Üí   131‚Üí   131‚Üíprimarily on his or her own experience of the exchange (Kivlighan et al., 2014; Sullivan, 1954). This role
    83‚Üí   132‚Üí   132‚Üí   132‚Üídifference has two implications for cue reciprocity. First, if paralinguistic cues are subject to display rules
    84‚Üí   133‚Üí   133‚Üí   133‚Üí(Stein, 2023), the role-specific norms of professional helping should influence how chat workers use
    85‚Üí   134‚Üí   134‚Üí   134‚Üíemoticons relative to users (Cavalheiro et al., 2024). Second, in asymmetric dyads, influence may run
    86‚Üí   135‚Üí   135‚Üí   135‚Üímore strongly in one direction than the other (Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
    87‚Üí   136‚Üí   136‚Üí   136‚ÜíAsymmetric CMC dyads have themselves been studied, but this research has largely measured end-of-
    88‚Üí   137‚Üí   137‚Üí   137‚Üíinteraction outcomes such as user satisfaction, liking, or perceived competence (Adams et al., 2018;
    89‚Üí   138‚Üí   138‚Üí   138‚ÜíCavalheiro et al., 2024). What has received less attention is the turn-by-turn reciprocity inside the
    90‚Üí   139‚Üí   139‚Üí   139‚Üíinteraction itself, that is, how each party takes up the other's cues from one message to the next. This
    91‚Üí   140‚Üí   140‚Üí   140‚Üígap matters for text-based helping, where emoticons carry affective work that vocal and facial channels
    92‚Üí   141‚Üí   141‚Üí   141‚Üíwould otherwise do, and where how the two parties take up each other's cues turn by turn is itself part
    93‚Üí   142‚Üí   142‚Üí   142‚Üíof the helping process. Sequential chat data from a real helpline, of the kind analyzed here, lets us
    94‚Üí   143‚Üí   143‚Üí   143‚Üíexamine this reciprocity as it unfolds in actual help-seeking conversations.
    95‚Üí   144‚Üí   144‚Üí   144‚ÜíDecomposing reciprocity in a chat worker‚Äìuser dyad with APIM
    96‚Üí   145‚Üí   145‚Üí   145‚ÜíThe actor and partner interdependence model (APIM; Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kenny, 2018;
    97‚Üí   146‚Üí   146‚Üí   146‚ÜíKenny & Ledermann, 2010) offers a way to test this. APIM estimates two effects at once: the actor
    98‚Üí   147‚Üí   147‚Üí   147‚Üíeffect, the influence of a person's own score on their own outcome, and the partner effect, the influence
    99‚Üí   148‚Üí   148‚Üí   148‚Üíof the other person's score on that same outcome (Cook & Kenny, 2005). Reciprocity, in APIM terms, is
   100‚Üí   149‚Üí   149‚Üí   149‚Üícaptured by the partner effect, and mutual influence is shown when partner effects are present for both
   101‚Üí   150‚Üí   150‚Üí   150‚Üímembers of the dyad. In client‚Äìtherapist dyads, partner effects on relationship quality and session
   102‚Üí   151‚Üí   151‚Üí   151‚Üídepth have been found to be stronger for clients than for therapists, consistent with clients being the
   103‚Üí   152‚Üí   152‚Üí   152‚Üímore dependent member of the dyad (Jackson et al., 2012; Kivlighan, 2007; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   104‚Üí   153‚Üí   153‚Üí   153‚ÜíWhether the same asymmetry shows up in moment-to-moment emoticon use in text-based help, and in
   105‚Üí   154‚Üí   154‚Üí   154‚Üíwhich direction it runs, has not been examined.
   106‚Üí   155‚Üí   155‚Üí   155‚ÜíThe present study
   107‚Üí   156‚Üí   156‚Üí   156‚ÜíThe present study examines the following research questions using data from QuickChat, an
   108‚Üí   157‚Üí   157‚Üí   157‚Üíanonymous text-based youth chatline run by Fei Yue Community Service in Singapore, in which trained
   109‚Üí   158‚Üí   158‚Üí   158‚Üíchat workers (trained professionals and volunteers) provide brief support to users aged 12 to 25 years
   110‚Üí   159‚Üí   159‚Üí   159‚Üí(Chung et al., 2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). More information on QuickChat will be explained in the
   111‚Üí   160‚Üí   160‚Üí   160‚ÜíMethods. The study addresses three research questions, with hypotheses derived from the literature
   112‚Üí   161‚Üí   161‚Üí   161‚Üíreviewed above.
   113‚Üí   162‚Üí   162‚Üí   162‚ÜíRQ1. Do chat workers and users differ in their use of emoticons? We examine differences in the number
   114‚Üí   163‚Üí   163‚Üí   163‚Üíof emoticons used per session and the forms of emoticons (e.g., :( or :P). We hypothesized that chat
   115‚Üí   164‚Üí   164‚Üí   164‚Üíworkers use more emoticons than users in sessions (H1).
   116‚Üí   165‚Üí   165‚Üí   165‚ÜíRQ2. Are there actor and partner effects on emoticon use within sessions, and are these effects
   117‚Üí   166‚Üí   166‚Üí   166‚Üísymmetric or asymmetric across roles? Reciprocity is operationalised in two complementary ways (Stein,
   118‚Üí   167‚Üí   167‚Üí   167‚Üí2023): a binary indicator of any emoticon in a turn, and a count of how many emoticons were used. We
   119‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚Üí
   120‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚Üíhypothesized positive partner effects (reciprocity) for both chat workers and users (H2a), and a stronger
   121‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üípartner effect for the user as actor than for the chat worker as actor (H2b).
   122‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚ÜíRQ3. Do these actor and partner effects differ across sessions with higher and lower user ratings of the
   123‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üíchat worker's responsiveness and overall satisfaction? Two user-rated outcomes, I feel supported and
   124‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üíunderstood by my counsellor and I am satisfied with the counselling provided, serve as session-level
   125‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚Üímoderators. Based on the IPM, which posits that intimacy develops because of perceived
   126‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üíresponsiveness arising from reciprocal disclosure and supportive response (Reis, 2017; Reis & Shaver,
   127‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üí2018), we hypothesized that partner effects (reciprocity) are stronger in sessions rated higher on
   128‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üíresponsiveness and satisfaction (H3).
   129‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚ÜíMethods
   130‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚ÜíData and Sample
   131‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚ÜíThe data come from eC2.sg, the digital mental health platform of Fei Yue Community Service in
   132‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚ÜíSingapore, which operates the QuickChat anonymous text based chatline for youths aged 12 to 25 years.
   133‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚ÜíQuickChat provides immediate access, free synchronous chat in which users register with only an email
   134‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚Üíaddress and are assigned to an available worker on duty; the service maintains user anonymity and
   135‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚Üíoperates as a brief intervention and triage platform rather than as long term therapy (Chung et al.,
   136‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üí2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). Sessions last around 30 minutes on average, with the primary objectives
   137‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üíbeing immediate emotional support, situation assessment, and referral.
   138‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚ÜíThe corpus comprises 1,854 chat sessions from 2016 to 2020. Of these, 1,435 sessions were
   139‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üíretained for analysis after restricting to sessions with at least ten messages and complete responses on
   140‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚Üíthe two post-session items used as outcomes (perceived worker responsiveness and session
   141‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚Üísatisfaction). Each session contains turn by turn text exchanges between a user and a chat helper;
   142‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üíhelpers are professionals (social workers and counsellors) or trained volunteers. Approval for use of the
   143‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üídata was obtained from the agency, with service practitioners participating as research team members,
   144‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚Üíand research ethics approval was obtained from [Blinded for Review] University. Because the platform
   145‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚Üísupports plain text only, users and helpers compose typographic emoticons (e.g., :), :(, :D) rather than
   146‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üípictographic Unicode emojis, so all analyses below concern emoticons.
   147‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚ÜíBuilding the lagged turn level dataset
   148‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚ÜíResearch question (RQ) 2 and RQ3 both rely on a turn level dataset in which each turn carries
   149‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üíinformation about what the worker and user did in their immediately preceding turns. RQ1 does not
   150‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üírequire this lag structure and is answered from the message level data within the corpus.
   151‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚ÜíA turn is a maximal run of consecutive messages from the same speaker within a chat. Turn
   152‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚Üíboundaries come from looking at every message (with or without emoticons), based on speaker
   153‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üíchanges. With turn boundaries established, the per turn emoticon count is just the sum of the per
   154‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚Üímessage emoticon counts inside that turn. The dataset also retains the speaker role (worker or user)
   155‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üíand the turn sequence within the session. Within each session, turns alternate strictly between user and
   156‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üíworker. For any focal turn, the partner's prior turn is the immediately preceding turn (lag 1) and the
   157‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚Üíactor's own prior turn is two turns back (lag 2); turn level lagged predictors are constructed from these
   158‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚Üítwo prior turns. The final turn level analytic sample contains 86,424 turns, of which 42,945 are user
   159‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚Üíturns and 43,479 are worker turns.
   160‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üí
   161‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚ÜíMeasures
   162‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚ÜíThis section describes the emoticon predictors (used in RQ1, RQ2, and RQ3) and the session
   163‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üílevel user ratings used as moderators in RQ3.
   164‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚ÜíEmoticon identification
   165‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚ÜíEmoticons were identified by a locally hosted large language model (Open AI‚Äôs open sourced
   166‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚Üígpt-oss:20b model). For each message, the model was given the raw text, the speaker role, and up to
   167‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚Üítwo preceding messages as context, and identified each emoticon present along with a standardised
   168‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚Üíname. A few-shot prompting approach was used: the prompt included example emoticons drawn from a
   169‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üípublicly available emoticon dictionary (Sakib, 2022) and from a hand inspection of 30 randomly sampled
   170‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üíemoticon bearing messages in the present corpus. Messages with multiple emoticons produced one
   171‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üírecord per emoticon. To validate the output, 200 emoticon bearing messages were randomly sampled
   172‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚Üíand annotated by a human coder (undergraduate research assistant); agreement with the LLM labels
   173‚Üí   222‚Üí   222‚Üí   222‚Üíwas high at 99%.
   174‚Üí   223‚Üí   223‚Üí   223‚ÜíConceptualising emoticon use: binary and count forms
   175‚Üí   224‚Üí   224‚Üí   224‚ÜíFollowing prior work on emoji and emoticon reciprocity (Stein, 2023), emoticon use is
   176‚Üí   225‚Üí   225‚Üí   225‚Üíoperationalised in two complementary forms throughout RQ2 and RQ3. The binary form records
   177‚Üí   226‚Üí   226‚Üí   226‚Üíwhether a turn contains any emoticon at all (presence or absence of cue use), and the count form
   178‚Üí   227‚Üí   227‚Üí   227‚Üírecords the integer number of emoticons in the turn (intensity of cue use). Each form is also constructed
   179‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚Üífor the lagged actor and partner predictors, and is used in the corresponding models in RQ2 and RQ3.
   180‚Üí   229‚Üí   229‚Üí   229‚ÜíSession level user ratings (RQ3 moderators)
   181‚Üí   230‚Üí   230‚Üí   230‚ÜíEach session has a post session evaluation completed by the user, comprising seven items rated
   182‚Üí   231‚Üí   231‚Üí   231‚Üíon a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 3 = Slightly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree). Two items
   183‚Üí   232‚Üí   232‚Üí   232‚Üíwere used as moderators in RQ3. The first, ‚ÄúI feel supported and understood by my counsellor‚Äù, was
   184‚Üí   233‚Üí   233‚Üí   233‚Üítreated as the user's perceived responsiveness of the worker (Y1). The second, ‚ÄúI am satisfied with the
   185‚Üí   234‚Üí   234‚Üí   234‚Üícounselling provided‚Äù, tapped on overall service satisfaction (Y2). Each rating was grand mean-centered
   186‚Üí   235‚Üí   235‚Üí   235‚Üíbefore entering the model so that the simple effects of the actor and partner predictors would be
   187‚Üí   236‚Üí   236‚Üí   236‚Üíinterpretable at the average level of the rating; the centered ratings are denoted Y1 and Y2 below.

   189‚Üí   238‚Üí   238‚Üí   238‚ÜíAnalytical Methods
   190‚Üí   239‚Üí   239‚Üí   239‚ÜíThe three research questions draw on different units of analysis and different modelling
   191‚Üí   240‚Üí   240‚Üí   240‚Üístrategies. They are presented in turn.
   192‚Üí   241‚Üí   241‚Üí   241‚ÜíRQ1: Descriptive comparisons of emoticon use by role
   193‚Üí   242‚Üí   242‚Üí   242‚ÜíRQ1 uses the message level data to compare emoticon use between workers and users.
   194‚Üí   243‚Üí   243‚Üí   243‚ÜíDescriptive statistics are reported as frequencies and percentages within each role. Differences between
   195‚Üí   244‚Üí   244‚Üí   244‚Üíworkers and users are tested with Welch's two sample t test.
   196‚Üí   245‚Üí   245‚Üí   245‚ÜíRQ2: Turn level actor and partner effects on emoticon use
   197‚Üí   246‚Üí   246‚Üí   246‚Üí
   198‚Üí   247‚Üí   247‚Üí   247‚ÜíRQ2 uses the turn level lagged dataset and tests, separately for the binary and count
   199‚Üí   248‚Üí   248‚Üí   248‚Üíoperationalisations of cue use, whether emoticons in the actor's own prior turn (actor effect) and in the
   200‚Üí   249‚Üí   249‚Üí   249‚Üípartner's prior turn (partner effect) predict the actor's current emoticon use, and whether these effects
   201‚Üí   250‚Üí   250‚Üí   250‚Üídiffer between worker and user. The mixed models below account for the multilevel structure of the
   202‚Üí   251‚Üí   251‚Üí   251‚Üídata by including random intercepts for sessions and for individual speakers.
   203‚Üí   252‚Üí   252‚Üí   252‚ÜíFor each operationalisation, two models are fitted. Model 1, the pooled model, estimates a
   204‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üísingle actor and a single partner effect pooled across workers and users. This provides a picture of the
   205‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚Üíreciprocity in the dyad as a whole. Model 2, the role moderated model, interacts speaker role with both
   206‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üílagged predictors. Speaker role is treated as a factor with the chat user as the reference level. The
   207‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üíinteractions allow the actor and partner effects to differ between workers and users, and let us test for
   208‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üídifferences in actor effects across roles and in particular role asymmetry in partner effects.

   378‚Üí   427‚Üí   427‚Üí   427‚ÜíHow emoticons are reciprocated in CMC matters because, with vocal and facial channels
   379‚Üí   428‚Üí   428‚Üí   428‚Üístripped away, emoticons carry the affective work that those channels would otherwise do. Reciprocity
   380‚Üí   429‚Üí   429‚Üí   429‚Üíin emoticon use has been documented in symmetric dyads of peers, friends, and partners, but text-
   381‚Üí   430‚Üí   430‚Üí   430‚Üíbased youth chatlines present a distinct communication context: the two parties enter the exchange in a
   382‚Üí   431‚Üí   431‚Üí   431‚Üíhelping relationship, with a trained chat worker on one side and a young person seeking help on the
   383‚Üí   432‚Üí   432‚Üí   432‚Üíother. The present study examined emoticon reciprocity in this setting using APIM applied to lagged
   384‚Üí   433‚Üí   433‚Üí   433‚Üíturn-level data from a Singapore text chatline for young person seeking help. We found that emoticon
   385‚Üí   434‚Üí   434‚Üí   434‚Üíuse was routine, that chat workers and chat users differed substantially in how much and how variedly
   386‚Üí   435‚Üí   435‚Üí   435‚Üíthey used emoticons, that actor and partner effects were present in both directions but stronger from
   387‚Üí   436‚Üí   436‚Üí   436‚Üíchat worker to chat user than the reverse, and that the partner effect (i.e., reciprocity) was stronger in
   388‚Üí   437‚Üí   437‚Üí   437‚Üísessions rated higher on chat worker responsiveness and overall satisfaction".
