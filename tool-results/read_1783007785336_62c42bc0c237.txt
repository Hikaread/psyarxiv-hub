    13→    13→Abstract
    14→    14→Secrecy is a pervasive feature of everyday life. While research has predominantly focused on
    15→    15→secrets people choose to keep, the psychological mechanisms of imposed secrecy remain largely
    16→    16→unexplored. The prototypical example of imposed secrecy is a professional secret—one that is
    17→    17→organizationally mandated, with high stakes if leaked to the wrong ear, or at the wrong time. To
    18→    18→better understand the burden of imposed secrecy, we propose a framework that integrates theoretical
    19→    19→insights from multiple psychological subdisciplines and empirical evidence from applied
    20→    20→professional settings. The framework foregrounds five key aspects of imposed secrecy: the degree
    21→    21→of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation in keeping secrets (the why), secret content and appraisals (the
    22→    22→what), secrecy processes (the how), individual differences (the who) and contextual factors (the
    23→    23→where) that influence secrecy effectiveness and impact. Our framework allows for a nuanced
    24→    24→examination of distinct and interrelated impacts on the well-being of personnel handling imposed
    25→    25→secrets. This approach guides future research towards a comprehensive understanding of the costs
    26→    26→and benefits of imposed secrecy, including professional secrets across diverse industries and
    27→    27→disciplines. Ultimately, this work provides a foundation for the development of more effective ways
    28→    28→to improve the well-being of people who are required to keep secrets.
    29→    29→Keywords: secrecy; motivation; well-being

    32→    32→Most people have secrets—that is, information they intend to keep from someone else
    33→    33→(Slepian et al., 2017). Secrecy has well-known individual and relational well-being costs (Bedrov &
    34→    34→Leary, 2020; Finkenauer et al., 2009; Slepian & Moulton-Tetlock, 2019; Slepian et al., 2017, 2019),
    35→    35→yet people are often personally motivated to keep information hidden, and for very good reasons.
    36→    36→For instance, lips are kept sealed to protect relationships (Slepian & Koch, 2019), and to safeguard
    37→    37→one’s reputation (McDonald et al., 2020). But what happens when keeping information from others
    38→    38→is not purely based on a personal choice? We define this subset of information as imposed secrets,
    39→    39→and theorize that their nature and consequences for well-being are distinct from personally chosen
    40→    40→secrets (Slepian et al., 2024). Imposed secrets encompass information that individuals share with
    41→    41→others, asking those others to keep that information in confidence. While this definition conceivably
    42→    42→includes information restricted for personal reasons, it prototypically references information
    43→    43→restricted by professional or organizational obligations.
    44→    44→A vast literature exists on workplace stressors (Bhoir & Sinha, 2024; Goh et al., 2016,
    45→    45→Sonnentag et al., 2023), and yet the unique nature of professional secrecy warrants tailored
    46→    46→investigation for several reasons. First, information management processes are integral to many
    47→    47→professions, ranging from healthcare and law to finance and national security, making this a
    48→    48→widespread occupational phenomenon and thus relevant to much of the population. Despite its
    49→    49→prevalence, empirical work investigating the impact of professional secrecy is only just emerging.
    50→    50→Second, unlike other common organizational stressors such as role ambiguity or high job demands,
    51→    51→professional secrecy creates distinct challenges, which require specific skills such as selective
    52→    52→disclosure, mental compartmentalization, and ways of navigating complex ethical terrain (Costas &
    53→    53→Grey, 2016). These unique aspects necessitate tailored coping mechanisms and organizational
    54→    54→support strategies that go beyond those typically applied to other common workplace stressors.
    55→    55→Relatedly, while research into personal secrecy provides some valuable insights, those findings may
    56→    56→not directly translate to the professional context. This is primarily because the psychological
    57→    57→
    58→    58→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 4
    59→    59→dynamics involved in professional secrecy are altered by the external nature of secrecy
    60→    60→requirements imposed by the job, rather than stemming from a personal choice to keep information
    61→    61→to oneself. Finally, in addition to advancing theoretical understanding of the burden of professional
    62→    62→secrecy, developing a comprehensive model is the first step toward creating interventions aimed at
    63→    63→improving the well-being of people burdened by imposed secrecy. On an applied level, addressing
    64→    64→the potential negative impacts of secrecy on employee performance and satisfaction would
    65→    65→ultimately contribute to improved organizational outcomes across various industries.
    66→    66→In this paper, we first define secrecy and delineate it from other related constructs, before
    67→    67→highlighting key differences between personal and professional secrecy. Then, focusing on the
    68→    68→professional domain, we review the ways secrecy is enacted in work settings. Insights from
    69→    69→evidence in applied settings serve as a necessary backdrop to the final part of this paper, in which
    70→    70→we introduce a theoretical framework to articulate and explain the burden of professional secrecy.
    71→    71→Mapping Secrecy and Related Constructs
    72→    72→A key process of holding information is determining ‘who’ should know ‘what’. An
    73→    73→umbrella term for this process is information regulation, and its operation occurs along a continuum
    74→    74→ranging from withholding information from others to sharing information with others (Bingley et
    75→    75→al., 2022). On the “withhold information” end of the continuum, secrecy (Table 1, A) is sometimes
    76→    76→considered synonymous with privacy (Table 1, B). While the distinction between these constructs is
    77→    77→not always apparent, information is considered secret (vs. private) if others are believed to have a
    78→    78→right to know the information in question (Kelly, 2002). For instance, not telling one’s boss about
    79→    79→having a sexually transmitted disease would be considered within acceptable privacy boundaries,
    80→    80→whereas keeping the same information from a spouse would be considered secretive.
    81→    81→Another construct distinguishable from secrecy is concealment (Table 1, C)—the act of
    82→    82→keeping information hidden during conversation with others (Margolis, 1974; Pennebaker, 1989).
    83→    83→Intuitively, concealment could be seen as fully overlapping with secrecy, if one considers that any
    84→    84→
    85→    85→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 5
    86→    86→information actively concealed is ultimately being kept secret. However, it is possible to form an
    87→    87→intention to keep information secret, and yet never have that information come up in conversation—
    88→    88→and hence have a secret that never needs to be concealed (Slepian, 2022).
    89→    89→On the “sharing information” end of the continuum, we encounter other constructs related to
    90→    90→secrecy. First, confidentiality (Table 1, D) can be considered a special case of secrecy. While
    91→    91→secrecy involves the intention to keep information from others, confidential information is shared,
    92→    92→but with an expectation on the receiver to keep the information to themselves, or in other words, on
    93→    93→the understanding that the information will not be divulged to a third party (Cain, 1998). Confiding
    94→    94→processes (Table 1, E) can involve informal, often spontaneous sharing with a trusted person where
    95→    95→the sharer expects the recipient to maintain privacy. By contrast, confidential disclosure (Table 1, F)
    96→    96→is an intentional, formalized exchange, limited to specific trusted individuals or entities, and
    97→    97→frequently codified by organizational rules or legal agreements. Medical practitioners, for instance,
    98→    98→are trusted with sensitive health information by their patients, so that details can be used within a
    99→    99→certain scope (e.g. diagnosing and treating an ailment), but not shared beyond that scope (Australian
   100→   100→Health Practitioners Registration Association [AHPRA], 2020).
   101→   101→A second construct intertwined with sharing and secrecy processes is gossip (Table 1, G;
   102→   102→Fan & Grey, 2021). Normally, a gossiper shares information with an audience (e.g., in small, trusted
   103→   103→groups), sometimes under the implicit or explicit condition that the information should not be
   104→   104→shared beyond the group members (Michelson et al., 2010; Peters & Kashima, 2007).

   218→   218→The Nature of Personal Secrecy
   219→   219→The literature exploring the burden of personal secrecy provides initial insights into how
   220→   220→secrets impact people’s well-being. In an early investigation of 13,000 personal secrets, Slepian and
   221→   221→colleagues (2017) found that most people were keeping a secret, and that keeping secrets was
   222→   222→associated with worse well-being across a range of domains (i.e., lower relationship satisfaction,
   223→   223→feelings of inauthenticity, and poorer physical health). Following this early work, a large array of
   224→   224→studies have captured the negative impact of secrecy on cognitive, social, and affective indicators
   225→   225→(Bedrov & Leary, 2021; Maas et al., 2012; McDonald et al., 2020; Slepian et al., 2017, 2019;
   226→   226→Slepian, Kirby, & Kalokerinos, 2020). These findings suggest that personal secrecy has the capacity
   227→   227→to worsen outcomes across the main dimensions of well-being.
   228→   228→Psychological well-being has long been theorized to vary along two dimensions: hedonic
   229→   229→and eudaimonic (Kahneman & Deaton, 2010; Ryan & Deci, 2001). Hedonic well-being focuses on
   230→   230→affective experiences (i.e., aligning higher well-being with experiencing more pleasure and less
   231→   231→pain; Diener & Lucas,1999), whereas eudaimonic well-being refers to areas of personal purpose and
   232→   232→growth, and development indices. Accordingly, measures of eudaimonic well-being combine facets
   233→   233→of mastery and psychological development that extend beyond hedonic affect (Riff & Singer, 1998,
   234→   234→2000; Ryan & Deci, 2000; Waterman, 1993). In the context of secrecy, accounting for the
   235→   235→distinction between these two types of well-being is crucial, because keeping secrets could affect
   236→   236→each type of well-being differentially. For instance, keeping financial troubles hidden from one’s
   237→   237→family may be personally stressful (i.e., negatively impacting hedonic well-being), but at the same
   238→   238→time foster good family relationships (i.e., eudaimonic well-being).
   239→   239→Of course, there may be a bright side to having secrets. Indeed, a few studies shows some
   240→   240→eudaimonic advantages to keeping secrets, such as the fact that adolescents develop autonomy
   241→   241→through keeping secrets from parents (Finkenauer et al., 2002). Further, secrets can foster mutual
   242→   242→trust and closeness in family or friend contexts (Kelly, 2002; Slepian & Greenaway, 2018). People
   243→   243→
   244→   244→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 10
   245→   245→also occasionally keep personal secrets about positive life events (e.g., proposals, pregnancies, good
   246→   246→news), and these positive secrets have been shown to improve hedonic well-being (Slepian et al.,
   247→   247→2023). Nonetheless, most empirical studies support the notion that personal secrets generally carry
   248→   248→hedonic and eudaimonic costs.
   249→   249→The Nature of Professional Secrecy
   250→   250→The existing literature has amassed important insights into the ways personal secrecy can
   251→   251→harm—and in some contexts also support—well-being (Slepian, 2022). Yet, personal secrets
   252→   252→capture only part of what people keep hidden from others. For instance, people are often called on
   253→   253→to be the recipient of (and custodian to) other people’s secrets (Slepian & Greenaway, 2018), or are
   254→   254→required to regulate information by their workplace or professional associations (Slepian et al.,
   255→   255→2024).
   256→   256→Our paper focuses on this latter subset of secrets. We theorize that imposed secrets relate to
   257→   257→well-being in ways distinct from other types of secrets, and explain why this is the case. Though
   258→   258→hailing from different disciplines and literatures, empirical research offers initial insight into several
   259→   259→ways these externally imposed secrets may link to well-being. Most of this research focuses on
   260→   260→forms of professional secrets that are mandated as a result of the work that people do. Of course, not
   261→   261→all secrets kept in a professional context fall within this definition. For instance, employers may
   262→   262→secretly plan to apply for a promotion or secretly dislike working with a certain colleague. But
   263→   263→because people choose to keep that information secret, rather than the secret being required by
   264→   264→organizational or professional directives, those secrets fall outside our definition of imposed
   265→   265→professional secrecy. To further clarity, we define and provide examples of processes pertaining
   266→   266→professional secrets and other adjacent, yet distinct types of information and processes pertinent to
   267→   267→work contexts in Table 1.
   268→   268→Mapping Secrecy in Professional Contexts
   269→   269→The scholarly literature provides preliminary evidence of pathways through which imposed
   270→   270→
   271→   271→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 11
   272→   272→professional secrecy can influence well-being. In this professional context, adding to the spectrum
   273→   273→from withholding secret information to willingly sharing secret information, a new category
   274→   274→emerges of prying into other people’s secrets.
   275→   275→Withholding Professional Secrets. The most obvious way in which secrets can impact
   276→   276→professional secret-keepers is via processes that involve active concealment (Table 1, C) and
   277→   277→deception (Table 1, H). While intelligence agents may be the prototypical example of professionals
   278→   278→hiding confidential information, the practice of secret-keeping extends far beyond the realm of
   279→   279→espionage: healthcare providers are bound by strict confidentiality rules to protect patient privacy;
   280→   280→selection officers must maintain discretion about applicants and decision-making processes; used
   281→   281→car sellers and real estate agents are notorious for withholding or presenting details strategically, to
   282→   282→maximize their outcomes. These diverse examples illustrate that many professions require secrecy
   283→   283→in order to be successful, or for other ethical or legal reasons. While useful to achieve professional
   284→   284→goals, withholding, protecting, and selectively disclosing information all involve constant vigilance,
   285→   285→potential stress and moral dilemmas, resulting in cognitive load, emotional strain, and social
   286→   286→isolation (Critcher & Ferguson, 2014; Lane & Wegner, 1995; Smart & Wegner, 1999). Importantly,
   287→   287→these are internal processes, that can happen not just at times of active concealment (i.e., in
   288→   288→interpersonal contexts), but also at times when people are alone, dealing with the content of their
   289→   289→secrets (Slepian, 2022). Accordingly, our definition of secrecy (Table 1, A) aligns with modern
   290→   290→conceptualizations that go beyond information concealment, and its impact on well-being.
   291→   291→Sharing Professional Secrets. That which can be held back can also be shared. In their
   292→   292→work on secrecy in organizational life, Costas and Grey (2016) introduce the concept of “epistemic
   293→   293→architecture” to describe how secrecy operates through formal and informal processes to build
   294→   294→boundaries—figurative walls to separate people, define groups, and establish what is “outside”. Yet,
   295→   295→somewhat paradoxically, these same structures that hold and divide, are also permeable and
   296→   296→instrumental for the movement of information (Bingley et al., 2022; Costas & Grey, 2016).
   297→   297→
   298→   298→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 12
   299→   299→Interpersonal processes that facilitate information flow in professional settings vary widely. We see
   300→   300→some formal processes, such as confidential disclosure and whistleblowing, as closely relevant to
   301→   301→understanding the burden of secrets mandated by one’s job or professional affiliation (i.e.,
   302→   302→professional secrecy). In addition to these formalized practices, we also discuss some informal types
   303→   303→of information sharing, like confiding and gossiping, because understanding their nature
   304→   304→contributes, albeit indirectly, to our conceptualization of how professional secrecy might affect
   305→   305→well-being.
   306→   306→In professional spheres, it is common practice to share information confidentially with
   307→   307→colleagues. For instance, in healthcare settings, supervision and peer consultation practices provide
   308→   308→a space not just to discuss work processes, but also to offload. These practices have been linked to
   309→   309→lower burnout and higher job satisfaction (Martin et al., 2021). Yet, being confided in could be a
   310→   310→burden, particularly when there is a high degree of overlap in social networks with the secret sharer
   311→   311→(Slepian & Greenaway, 2018), as is the case in many workplaces. In addition, avenues for
   312→   312→professional disclosures are not always straightforward. Obstacles to confidential sharing can
   313→   313→include formal requirements (e.g. non-disclosure agreements; secrecy provisions), and personal
   314→   314→considerations (e.g. lack of trust in co-workers or organizational systems; personal consequences for
   315→   315→the sharer; Costas & Grey, 2016; Near & Miceli, 2016). In some contexts, individuals may therefore
   316→   316→feel trapped between moral or psychological pressures to speak out, and systemic barriers to doing
   317→   317→so safely. This tension is particularly evident when it comes to whistleblowing (Table 1, I)—a form
   318→   318→of controlled disclosure with the purpose of addressing organizational violations. While
   319→   319→organizations often encourage and formalise whistleblowing processes, research indicates that more
   320→   320→than half of public-interest whistleblowers suffer serious direct or indirect repercussions for their
   321→   321→actions (Brown et al., 2008; 2019; Near & Miceli, 1985; van der Velden et al., 2019).
   322→   322→Informal processes of sharing secret information have received little attention in the personal
   323→   323→secrecy literature to date, though preliminary evidence suggests that confiding secrets is associated
   324→   324→
   325→   325→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 13
   326→   326→with greater well-being. Confiding appears to reduce preoccupation with secrets and increase access
   327→   327→to social support, as well as perceived coping efficacy to manage secrecy (Slepian & Moulton
   328→   328→Tetlock, 2019). These findings are in line with the traditional belief that “getting things off one’s
   329→   329→chest” can be helpful for people’s hedonic well-being, offering an outlet for negative feelings (even
   330→   330→if only through expressive writing, e.g., Pennebaker, 1989). A similar advantage seems to come
   331→   331→from confidential gossiping (in fact, the content of gossip and secrets often overlaps; Dunbar, 2004;
   332→   332→Fan & Dawson, 2022; Fan & Grey, 2021; Paine, 1967; Piazza & Bering, 2010). Sharing
   333→   333→confidential gossip can generate pro-hedonic feelings, such as pleasure and entertainment (Fan et
   334→   334→al., 2021). In addition, both confidential gossip and secret sharing hold an important relational
   335→   335→function, requiring a level of vulnerability and trust between parties, and thus act as a social glue
   336→   336→(Costas & Grey, 2016). However, the sharing of secret information with third parties might backfire
   337→   337→when it comes to hedonic well-being, for instance when people fear that those who have been
   338→   338→entrusted with the information may decide to share it farther than originally intended. Therefore, the
   339→   339→uncertainty of where information might end up, and potential repercussions for the source of the
   340→   340→leak, are likely to take a toll on the original sharer.
   341→   341→Prying into Professional Secrets. Another experience with secrets in professional settings
   342→   342→involves finding out other people’s secrets. For instance, investigative journalists uncover hidden
   343→   343→truths in the public interest (e.g., Woodward & Bernstein, 1999); patent developers must be wary of
   344→   344→contenders trying to steal intellectual property (e.g. see Apple vs. Samsung, in: Costas & Grey
   345→   345→2016); law enforcing officers are routinely tasked with eliciting confessions (Table 1, J) from
   346→   346→suspects as part of criminal investigations (Gudjonsson, 1992; Kassin et al., 2010); private
   347→   347→investigators blend covert observation, information extraction, deception and ethical grey zones
   348→   348→(i.e., Maheshwari, 2023). The well-being cost of this type of prying has received limited attention.
   349→   349→A few studies have looked at the impact of long-term undercover assignments, relying on small
   350→   350→samples or retrospective case analyses (Kowalczyk & Sharpes, 2017; Miller, 2006). Those studies
   351→   351→
   352→   352→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 14
   353→   353→suggest recurrent themes of emotional strain, physical health costs, and relationship breakdown
   354→   354→(Girodo, 1991; Feinstein et al., 2014; Love et al., 2008). These early findings suggest further
   355→   355→empirical work is needed to more precisely examine what impact the process of prying has on
   356→   356→professional “secret-hunters”.
   357→   357→A Framework for Understanding the Impact of Professionally Imposed Secrecy on Well-
   358→   358→Being
   359→   359→There is currently no theoretical framework integrating relevant psychological perspectives
   360→   360→to understand and ease the burden of imposed secrecy. This paper offers one such framework,
   361→   361→building on insights from multiple psychology subdisciplines and empirical evidence in pertinent
   362→   362→applied settings. Our framework holds promise for building better ways to alleviate the burden of
   363→   363→imposed secrecy across a range of industries, given the high stakes that these types of secrets hold.
   364→   364→We theorise the burden experienced by professional secret-keepers is contingent upon five
   365→   365→interrelated factors. First, we propose that why people have secrets is likely to play a central role in
   366→   366→how secrecy impacts their well-being (Table 2, Component 1). This framework suggests that secrets
   367→   367→sit on a motivational continuum. On one side of the continuum, personal secrets are typically
   368→   368→internally motivated or generated (i.e., freely chosen based upon personal considerations or
   369→   369→interest). On the other side, secrets like those confided by others, and professional secrets, tend to be
   370→   370→more extrinsically motivated, because the decision to keep information hidden is imposed by
   371→   371→external factors, rather than left to individual discretion. Scaffolded by motivation theory, our
   372→   372→framework proposes that, all other factors being equal, extrinsically motivated secrets will have a
   373→   373→higher burden on people’s hedonic and eudaimonic well-being than intrinsically motivated ones
   374→   374→(Kasser & Ryan, 2001; Sheldon & Elliot, 1998). Where subsequent factors in our framework may
   375→   375→similarly apply to understanding the burden of personal secrecy, this first motivational factor is a
   376→   376→key dimension on which professional and personal secrets vary.
   377→   377→Second, our framework highlights the importance of distinguishing the content of what
   378→   378→
   379→   379→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 15
   380→   380→people keep secret from the processes through which they keep secrets. By disentangling the what
   381→   381→(Table 2, Component 2) from the how (Table 2, Component 3), the framework allows for these
   382→   382→features to have distinct relationships with well-being. For instance, a psychologist’s well-being
   383→   383→might be impacted by being privy to the details of a client’s secret traumatic experience. For that
   384→   384→psychologist, the most burdening aspect might be what they know about the client’s secret
   385→   385→experience, as identified by research into vicarious trauma (Molnar et al., 2017). Alternatively, the
   386→   386→most burdening aspect might be the act of keeping those details confidential (i.e., the weight of
   387→   387→holding information back from others).
   388→   388→Our framework also proposes the need to consider individual differences (the who) that
   389→   389→might make (or break) professional secret keepers (Table 2, Component 4). We hypothesize that
   390→   390→certain personal characteristics might represent a double-edged sword. For instance, John le Carré's
   391→   391→provocative description of the "perfect spy" includes a lack of conscience, feelings, scruples, and
   392→   392→fear (le Carré, 1974). Those qualities might make for an effective intelligence operative, whilst
   393→   393→increasing the person’s vulnerability to isolation, difficulty with personal relationships, and mental
   394→   394→health problems.
   395→   395→Finally, our framework considers how the professional context (the where) would influence
   396→   396→secrecy effects on well-being (Table 2, Component 5). In certain jobs, secret-keeping might be
   397→   397→required occasionally, rather than being an essential component of the work. This would be the case
   398→   398→for executive assistants, who might have access to—and be required to temporarily hold onto—
   399→   399→sensitive information, such as a merger, planned layoffs, or promotions. However, for some other
   400→   400→professions, secrecy is the core business—certainly for intelligence agencies, where secrets must be
   401→   401→kept or discovered through assignments that may span years and decades. We make the case that
   402→   402→different professional secrecy contexts are likely to result in different levels of burden on workers’
   403→   403→well-being.

   467→   467→The Why of Professional Secrets
   468→   468→Professional secrets are a prototypical example of imposed secrecy because, by definition,
   469→   469→they stem from the external requirements of one’s job. This distinguishes professional secrecy from
   470→   470→personal secrecy not only by life domain, but also by the primary motivational force behind it:
   471→   471→people keep personal secrets predominantly because of intrinsic motives, whereas professional
   472→   472→secrets are characterized by reasons tied to duties, sanctions and institutional expectations. This is
   473→   473→not to say that professional secrets can't also be intrinsically motivated; however, the key distinction
   474→   474→lies in the fact that there is always an extrinsic motivational factor at play with professional secrets.
   475→   475→This difference in motivational composition amongst types of secrets is likely to matter when
   476→   476→considering the burden of professional secrecy.
   477→   477→The motivation literature separates actions that are freely chosen and driven by personal
   478→   478→interest or enjoyment (i.e., for intrinsic reasons) from actions that are driven by external pressures or
   479→   479→rewards (i.e., for extrinsic reasons; Ryan & Deci, 2000). While intrinsically motivated and freely
   480→   480→chosen actions are beneficial for well-being, the relationship between extrinsic motivation and well-
   481→   481→being tends to be negative. Thus, extrinsically motivated actions, such as those imposed by outside
   482→   482→bodies, often come at a cost to well-being (Kasser & Ryan, 2001; Ryan & Deci, 2000; Sheldon &
   483→   483→Elliot, 1998).
   484→   484→Self-Determination Theory identifies three basic psychological needs of autonomy,
   485→   485→competence and relatedness as drivers for intrinsic motivation (Ryan & Deci, 2000). Conversely,
   486→   486→when motivation is predominantly extrinsic, some of these needs may be thwarted or absent, with
   487→   487→flow-on consequences for well-being. Indeed, imposed secrets (professional or otherwise) are likely
   488→   488→to undermine autonomy needs, when they are kept against one’s own choice. And even when an
   489→   489→individual's personal choice happens to align with a mandated secrecy requirement, the added
   490→   490→external imposition may still partially compromise autonomy (and elicit reactance; Brehm, 1966;
   491→   491→Brehm & Brehm, 1981). We therefore identify this autonomy-thwarting pathway as the principal
   492→   492→
   493→   493→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 19
   494→   494→mechanism by which mandated secrecy may be detrimental to people’s well-being. Further, we
   495→   495→theorize this pathway applies across all forms of mandated information regulation: while our
   496→   496→framework centers on restrictions of information flow (i.e., secrecy), it can also encompass
   497→   497→situations in which disclosure is mandated (i.e., mandatory reporting; Bismark et al., 2016;
   498→   498→Pietrantonio et al., 2013).
   499→   499→Secrecy research findings align with those from the broader motivation literature. In an
   500→   500→experiment, Slepian and colleagues (2023) had participants contemplate a secret they were keeping
   501→   501→because they freely chose to do so, or a secret they were keeping due to extrinsic constraints.
   502→   502→Participants reported feeling more energized by an intrinsically chosen secret than an externally
   503→   503→imposed secret. These findings suggest that professional secrets may take a particularly strong toll
   504→   504→on well-being, given that these secrets are almost always extrinsically required and enforced.
   505→   505→Testing whether motivation locus factors are indeed salient for professional secret-keepers may
   506→   506→offer a promising entry point for easing the burden of their job—leveraging the notion that over
   507→   507→time, extrinsic motivations can become internalized, and therefore more intrinsically oriented (Deci
   508→   508→& Ryan, 2000). As such, our framework paves the way for the development of interventions
   509→   509→focusing on the best ways to facilitate this process in personnel. For instance, to the extent that
   510→   510→people can be trained to focus on intrinsic reasons for professional secrecy (e.g., because it aligns
   511→   511→with personal values), well-being may benefit.
   512→   512→While we identify autonomy as key aspect linking mandated secrecy to well-being
   513→   513→outcomes, it is possible that imposed secrets may affect well-being through other channels. That is,
   514→   514→if professional secrecy is experienced as enhancing—rather than thwarting— needs for competence
   515→   515→and relatedness, well-being may benefit. We suggest that when secrecy is core to role performance
   516→   516→and fosters skill and professional pride, the need for competence may be met by information
   517→   517→regulation processes. This hypothesis is guided by existing work indicating that people must
   518→   518→actively develop and demonstrate skills in navigating secrecy requirements within organizations
   519→   519→
   520→   520→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 20
   521→   521→(Costas & Gray, 2016; Slepian et al., 2024). By engaging in these secrecy-management practices,
   522→   522→people can cultivate and demonstrate professional mastery, thereby satisfying psychological needs
   523→   523→for competence.
   524→   524→A more complex range of factors may link professional secrecy to the need for relatedness.
   525→   525→Imposed secrecy in organizational contexts may act as a glue, strengthening team cohesion and
   526→   526→mutual reliance among those “who are in the know” (Costas & Gray, 2016), and strengthen
   527→   527→identification with a professional in-group, fostering belonging, norms of reciprocal support and
   528→   528→cooperative coordination—all core elements of relatedness (Tajfel & Turner, 1979; Turner et al.,
   529→   529→1987). However, professional secrecy may simultaneously thwart relatedness with those who are
   530→   530→excluded from privileged information (outgroup members), consistent with recent evidence linking
   531→   531→occupational secrecy to isolation, stress and lower job satisfaction (Slepian et al., 2024). Taken
   532→   532→together, these dynamics indicate that professional secrecy may, under some conditions, fulfil
   533→   533→competence and relatedness needs even as it undermines autonomy. Recognizing this complex
   534→   534→balance helps explain why mandated information regulation can be experienced as both
   535→   535→professionally valuable and psychologically costly.
   536→   536→The What of Professional Secrets
   537→   537→Beyond motivations for withholding information, another key factor shaping the burden of
   538→   538→secrecy is what people are keeping secret. The ‘what’ of secrecy can refer both to the content of
   539→   539→one’s secret (i.e., what the secret is about) and to appraisals of secrets independent of their specific
   540→   540→content (i.e., what the secret means to the person keeping it).
   541→   541→The Content of Secrets. One way of understanding the burden of secrets is to categorize
   542→   542→them by content, and to determine each content category’s prevalence and impact. The first attempt
   543→   543→at building a scientific taxonomy of secret content resulted in the development of the Common
   544→   544→Secret Questionnaire—a validated instrument used to classify and study the most common
   545→   545→categories of personal secrets (Slepian et al., 2017). Capturing over 13,000 real-life secrets, Slepian
   546→   546→
   547→   547→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 21
   548→   548→and colleagues (2017) identified the 38 most common categories of personal secrets, with lying,
   549→   549→extra-relational thoughts and romantic desires being most common. Using a similar approach, a
   550→   550→more recent study surveyed 2,000 full-time employees across multiple industries and management
   551→   551→levels, to identify the most common categories of professional secrets (Slepian et al., 2024). These
   552→   552→categories encompassed secrets about clients’ details and identities, upcoming hiring and layoffs,
   553→   553→treatment of employees, future plans, finances, products, ongoing projects, unethical
   554→   554→practices/wrongdoing, and others workspace practices. However, we propose that other nuances of
   555→   555→professional secrecy escape that classification. This initial work provides valuable insight into types
   556→   556→of professional secrets, though secret content awaits formal validation with professions requiring
   557→   557→different levels of secrecy (e.g., classified, confidential, restricted), which may bring to light
   558→   558→additional professional secret categories (e.g. pertaining to collective secrets; legalities;
   559→   559→privacy/security; Slepian et al., 2024).
   560→   560→While the prevalence of specific content is a useful way to look at secrets, workers’ well-
   561→   561→being may not always be linked to how common their professional secrets are. For instance,
   562→   562→organizational policies commonly require employees to keep their salary confidential, across many
   563→   563→professional industries. Yet, outside specific instances, salary secrecy is unlikely to be the most
   564→   564→burdensome professional secret people hold. By the same logic, the rarest types of professional
   565→   565→secrets may not necessarily be the most impactful to workers’ well-being. Hence, capturing
   566→   566→characteristics of professional secrets beyond content and prevalence is necessary to develop a
   567→   567→better understanding of the burden of professional secrets.
   568→   568→The Features of Secrets. Subjective appraisals of (i.e. how one evaluates and interprets)
   569→   569→secret content can result in significant variations in the costs of secrecy, even when people hold
   570→   570→secrets with similar content (Slepian & Koch, 2021). Accordingly, secret features are distinct from
   571→   571→secret content, in that they are ultimately subjectively determined by the secret-keeper’s appraisal,
   572→   572→and are open to interpretation, rather than being an inherent property of the secret. For instance, two
   573→   573→
   574→   574→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 22
   575→   575→accountants might both be guarding information on a client’s impending bankruptcy. One might
   576→   576→view this secret as a routine confidential process in business management with minimal
   577→   577→psychological impact, while the other might experience stress and emotional turmoil, worrying for
   578→   578→the client and their employees. These divergent perspectives highlight the importance of individual
   579→   579→appraisal in determining the consequences of secret-keeping. In the professional secrecy sphere, we
   580→   580→suggest that certain appraisals may be particularly relevant to the psychological burden experienced
   581→   581→by secret-keepers.
   582→   582→First, the subjective importance of secrets is a feature consistently linked to hedonic and
   583→   583→eudaimonic well-being outcomes. There are likely to be several elements contributing to the
   584→   584→appraisal of importance—such as its potential personal, relational, organizational and societal
   585→   585→impact, ethical considerations, time-sensitivity, and emotional investment. Secrets that people rate
   586→   586→as highly important are more often on people’s minds, and take an emotional and cognitive toll,
   587→   587→independent of the content category they belong to (Bianchi et al., 2024, 2025; Lane & Wegner,
   588→   588→1995; Slepian et al., 2017; Slepian & Greenaway, 2018). Beyond personal outcomes such as
   589→   589→cognitive load, reduced authenticity, and lower life satisfaction, holding important secrets is
   590→   590→associated with poor relational outcomes, such as feelings of disconnection and lower relationship
   591→   591→quality and satisfaction (Bedrov & Gable, 2025).
   592→   592→We propose that the greater the perceived importance of a professional secret, the more it
   593→   593→will “turbocharge” the basic satisfaction of competence needs. This is because when a secret is
   594→   594→appraised as highly important, carrying and managing that information offers more opportunities to
   595→   595→demonstrate expertise, solve complex problems, and exercise professional judgement. Further, it
   596→   596→signals that an individual is trusted with consequential responsibility (Costas& Gray, 2016; Slepian
   597→   597→et al., 2024). Together, these processes are likely to foster a sense of purpose, responsibility, and
   598→   598→professional competence, potentially enhancing one's feeling of contributing meaningfully to their
   599→   599→organization or profession—key components of eudaimonic well-being.
   600→   600→
   601→   601→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 23
   602→   602→A second feature of secrets linked to well-being outcomes is valence—whether the secret is
   603→   603→experienced by the secret-keeper as positive or negative. Far from being unique to secrets,
   604→   604→negatively valenced cognitions are generally linked to poor psychological outcomes, including
   605→   605→higher anxiety, higher depression, higher distress and mental fatigue (Mathews & MacLeod, 2005).
   606→   606→Hence, research has almost exclusively focused on studying the impact of negative secrets (cf.
   607→   607→Slepian et al., 2023). This work has shown that the more negative people rate their secret, the more
   608→   608→those secrets are fatiguing (Slepian et al., 2019), isolating (Bedrov & Leary, 2021), preoccupying
   609→   609→(Slepian et al., 2016) and linked to detrimental emotion regulation strategies, such as rumination
   610→   610→and suppression (Bianchi et al., 2024). We expect the same valence-linked pattern described in the
   611→   611→personal secrecy literature to apply with mandated secrets, whether personal or professional.
   612→   612→A third feature of secrets that may influence their impact is appraisals of uncertainty (de
   613→   613→Berker et al., 2016; Moeck et al., 2024). One example comes from considering the distinction
   614→   614→between formal and informal secrets (Costas & Grey, 2016). Organizations usually provide clearly
   615→   615→defined written instructions (e.g., non-disclosure agreements) to employees required to hold formal
   616→   616→secrets. However, workers are also often entrusted with informal secrets that are less clearly
   617→   617→defined, and which require navigating uncertain boundaries on at least four dimensions. First,
   618→   618→personnel might be unclear about what exactly is to be kept secret. Second, the secret-keeper must
   619→   619→discover who already knows, who should know, and—of course—who should not know the secret
   620→   620→(Bingley et al., 2022). Third, the secret-keeper might be unsure of the time course of keeping the
   621→   621→secret. Finally, the secret-keeper may be unaware of the consequences of accidentally revealing the
   622→   622→secret (e.g., making a faux pas). One or more of these different elements likely contribute to the
   623→   623→well-being burden of professional secrecy, in ways that might not always be present in the personal
   624→   624→sphere. This is because people often have more control over—and a clearer understanding of—
   625→   625→personal secrets, whereas professional secrets may involve complex organizational dynamics and
   626→   626→shifting contexts that amplify these uncertainties.
   627→   627→
   628→   628→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 24
   629→   629→A fourth relevant feature of secrets is the degree to which people appraise them as morally
   630→   630→relevant. Some research has found that the more secrets were about wrongdoing, the more they
   631→   631→evoked feelings of shame (Slepian & Koch, 2021). Yet, this relationship was attenuated when
   632→   632→people kept the secret for professional, opposed to personal, purposes. Professional ethics
   633→   633→frequently mandate strict confidentiality, even when the information concerns unethical or illegal
   634→   634→behaviour (e.g., American Bar Association, 2020; American Psychological Association, 2017;
   635→   635→General Medical Council, 2020). While these requirements can conflict with broader societal or
   636→   636→legal norms that prioritize transparency, they are often in place for a “greater good”, and hence may
   637→   637→be considered morally defensible (Henderson, 2023). Indeed, in a recent study, the moral dimension
   638→   638→of secrets emerged as a significant predictor of emotion regulation (Bianchi et al., 2024).
   639→   639→Specifically, individuals were more likely to engage in cognitive reappraisal—a strategy involving
   640→   640→reinterpreting the meaning of a situation—when dealing with secrets they perceived as morally
   641→   641→challenging. This finding suggests that the moral appraisal of a secret influences its emotional
   642→   642→impact, and shapes how individuals manage their emotional responses to it. We therefore expect
   643→   643→that the negative association between this appraisal and hedonic well-being will be attenuated for
   644→   644→professional secrets, relative to personal secrets.
   645→   645→The literature shows that what professional secrets are about, in terms of their content and
   646→   646→how they are appraised, contributes to the burden of keeping information hidden for a living.
   647→   647→However, what secrets are about cannot fully explain how and why certain secrets weigh more
   648→   648→heavily on personnel than others. The content and features of secrets are likely to exert their
   649→   649→influence through processes that catalyze their cognitive and emotional strain. Hence, a full picture
   650→   650→of professional secrecy would be incomplete without considering the mechanisms that underpin its
   651→   651→harm.
   652→   652→The How of Professional Secrets
   653→   653→The Process Model of Having and Keeping Secrets (Slepian, 2022) is the first theoretical
   654→   654→
   655→   655→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 25
   656→   656→framework to map secrecy processes and how they unfold. This theory identifies the birth of a
   657→   657→secret with setting an intention to commit information to silence. Thus, this model decouples
   658→   658→secrecy—an intention—from concealment—an act in the service of keeping a secret. Accordingly,
   659→   659→secrecy processes can unfold in two different ways. First, they can occur in contexts in which
   660→   660→having a secret may require behavioral concealment. Second, a person can have a secret that they
   661→   661→never have to conceal in conversation. For instance, a cybersecurity analyst may be working on
   662→   662→addressing a critical system flaw, but might never need to actively conceal this information, as their
   663→   663→everyday routine interactions do not require discussing system maintenance tasks. In these contexts,
   664→   664→secrecy can still harm well-being by increasing the tendency of the mind to return to thoughts of the
   665→   665→secret (i.e., mind-wandering; Slepian, 2022). This theory explains how the consequences of having
   666→   666→a secret are distinct from the consequences of actively keeping, or concealing, a secret.
   667→   667→Concealing Secrets. At times, having a secret requires actively concealing the secret from
   668→   668→others (Bok, 1983; Margolis, 1974; Pennebaker, 1989). It is natural to consider this form of
   669→   669→deception as burdensome for professional secret-keepers. Indeed, some early studies have linked
   670→   670→concealment with negative well-being outcomes in the professional sphere. For instance, nurses and
   671→   671→healthcare workers who conceal the emotional burden of their job to a greater degree are at higher
   672→   672→risk of burnout and illness than those who conceal less (Larson, 1985, 1987). Similarly, people
   673→   673→experience worse personal and social well-being when asked to keep a secret by lying vs. omitting
   674→   674→information (Sprigings et al., 2023).
   675→   675→Although there is evidence that concealing personal secrets harms hedonic and eudaimonic
   676→   676→well-being, concealing professional secrets might carry a different weight. In some cases,
   677→   677→concealing professional secrets might feel easier than concealing personal secrets. For many
   678→   678→professional figures, such as lawyers and doctors, the simple statement “I am not able to share
   679→   679→information about that issue” might be a straightforward way to set clear boundaries with an
   680→   680→audience, and limit the need for ongoing concealment. Indeed, a mutual understanding of the
   681→   681→
   682→   682→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 26
   683→   683→requirements of the job may pave the way for clear and mutually agreed concealment within
   684→   684→professional relationships (e.g., amongst colleagues; client/attorney; doctor/patient). This is one of
   685→   685→the reasons why psychologists are trained to discuss professional boundaries and limits to
   686→   686→confidentiality in the first session with their clients (American Psychological Association, 2017;
   687→   687→Fisher, 2012).
   688→   688→Concealment is a low-hanging fruit for interventions to manage the harms of professional
   689→   689→secrecy. Indeed, there is a range of options readily available to workplaces to assist workers in
   690→   690→concealing proprietary information (i.e., training in how to protect information; organizational tools
   691→   691→such as non-disclosure agreements). However, this focus on concealment means that organizations
   692→   692→have built procedures for managing secrecy around a faulty understanding of what primarily
   693→   693→underpins the burden of secrets. Rather than concealment, research shows the main way secrets
   694→   694→harm people is through the degree to which they think about those secrets outside concealment
   695→   695→contexts. Further, the personal secrecy literature suggests that these cognitive processes might be
   696→   696→more harmful to well-being than instances of active concealment.
   697→   697→Mind-wandering to Secrets. The scientific consensus is that the process of mind-
   698→   698→wandering to secrets is more frequent than concealment of secrets (Bianchi et al., 2025; McDonald
   699→   699→et al., 2020; Slepian et al., 2017; Slepian & Greenaway, 2018; Slepian, Kirby, & Kalokerinos, 2020;
   700→   700→Slepian & Moulton-Tetlock, 2019). This may be because secrets can come to mind anywhere, any
   701→   701→time—when the secret-keeper is alone, driving, cooking, or working out. Further, people report
   702→   702→wanting to engage with thoughts about their secrets—particularly of important ones—rather than
   703→   703→suppress them (Slepian, Greenaway & Masicampo, 2020). Therefore, secrets are often ‘top of mind’
   704→   704→in that people’s minds wander to thoughts of their secrets even when there is not an immediate need
   705→   705→to conceal. In sum, mind-wandering, not concealment, is largely responsible for the mental burden
   706→   706→of secrecy (e.g., Slepian et al., 2017; Slepian et al., 2020; Slepian et al., 2019).
   707→   707→The way people mind-wander to secrets may pose a unique challenge for those grappling
   708→   708→
   709→   709→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 27
   710→   710→with secret information, though only one paper has investigated this possibility so far (Bianchi et
   711→   711→al., 2026). Aligned with the general mind-wandering literature, the authors found that when the
   712→   712→mind wanders unintentionally—rather than intentionally—to thoughts about secrets, those thoughts
   713→   713→are associated with detrimental thought content such as worries and concerns, as well as greater
   714→   714→experience of negative emotion over time. Thus, spontaneous mind-wandering to secret content
   715→   715→appears to be a key process involved in the burden of secrecy, encouraging a vicious cycle of worry
   716→   716→and rumination (Bianchi et al, 2024).
   717→   717→While the personal secrecy literature has made significant advancements by investigating
   718→   718→the well-being impact of mind-wandering to secrets, no attention has been directed so far to the
   719→   719→precise understanding of how these cognitive processes may unfold in professional secrecy. Indeed,
   720→   720→we suggest that existing findings cannot be expected to entirely generalize and account for the
   721→   721→burden of professional secrecy. For instance, what people spontaneously think about in relation to
   722→   722→professional secrets may be different from what they spontaneously think about in relation to
   723→   723→personal secrets. That is, rather than focusing on worries and concerns, professional secret keepers’
   724→   724→minds may attempt to make sense of fragmented knowledge, fill in missing context, or prepare for
   725→   725→contingencies. In this case, spontaneous mind-wandering to professional secrets may even improve
   726→   726→well-being.
   727→   727→Of course, it is equally possible that spontaneous mind-wandering to work-related secrets
   728→   728→may have similar consequences to those observed in the personal secrecy literature. As an example,
   729→   729→the process of unintentionally thinking about professional secrets may direct attention to what is not
   730→   730→known (i.e., what secrets others might have, that they are not sharing), creating strain and
   731→   731→confusion, preoccupation and frustration at being “on the outside” (i.e., not at a sufficient status to
   732→   732→be privy to the secret; Costas & Grey, 2016; Slepian et al., 2024). In addition, individuals might
   733→   733→resort to putatively maladaptive regulatory strategies, such as thought suppression, which
   734→   734→paradoxically can exacerbate the psychological costs associated with secrecy (Bianchi et al., 2024).
   735→   735→
   736→   736→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 28
   737→   737→A vicious cycle between thoughts about secrets and negative emotion would contribute to the
   738→   738→psychological burden of professional secret-keeping (Bianchi et al., 2026).
   739→   739→On the flip side, workers might benefit from deliberately letting their mind wander to their
   740→   740→professional secret, if doing so allows them to reflect on their high status, and the meaningful
   741→   741→contribution that their effort has for the organization, or the broader community (Slepian et al.,
   742→   742→2024). Further, deliberate mind-wandering to professional secrets may carry an organisational
   743→   743→benefit, in that it might lead to new insights, improved work outcomes, and higher job satisfaction,
   744→   744→as suggested by the broader mind-wandering literature (Gable et al., 2019; Zedelius & Schooler,
   745→   745→2015). For instance, deliberately pondering her company’s secret soda formula may inspire a food
   746→   746→scientist to implement a minor ingredient adjustment, leading to a successful product improvement.
   747→   747→In sum, it is possible the impact of mind-wandering processes in occupational contexts may differ
   748→   748→from their frequency and consequences in the personal sphere. Having a clearer picture of how
   749→   749→people’s minds wander to their professional secrets is a necessary step to build this understanding
   750→   750→and implement training in how to manage this component of the secret-keeping process.
   751→   751→The Who of Professional Secrets
   752→   752→Equally important to this framework is the study of individual differences among
   753→   753→professional secret-keepers, as these traits are likely to determine how well a person manages the
   754→   754→act and weight of secrecy. Although there is limited direct empirical research in this area, we
   755→   755→focus on traits that are conceptually linked with secrecy (e.g., self-concealment), or for which
   756→   756→there is indirect evidence hinting at their potential role in secrecy processes (e.g., personality). At
   757→   757→a high level, our framework posits that the qualities that preserve someone’s well-being on the
   758→   758→one hand, and suit them to being an effective secret-keeper on the other, may sometimes be
   759→   759→distinct, other times overlapping, and sometimes at odds with one another.
   760→   760→Identifying relevant individual differences serves dual purposes. First, it provides insight
   761→   761→for recruitment processes, increasing the likelihood of selecting candidates with the necessary
   762→   762→
   763→   763→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 29
   764→   764→attributes for the role (Detrick & Chibnall, 2006a, 2006b; Schmidt & Hunter, 1998). Second, it
   765→   765→informs strategies for personnel retention, and well-being management. By understanding the
   766→   766→potential psychological costs for certain people (but not others) in occupations that involve secret
   767→   767→information, organizations can implement tailored interventions to mitigate risks, and prevent
   768→   768→harm for their employees (Bartone et al., 2008; Schmidt & Hunter, 1998). Preventing individual
   769→   769→harm would have benefits for the organization itself, such as reduced occupational injury claims,
   770→   770→increased work satisfaction and effectiveness (e.g., reduced risk of information leaks).
   771→   771→Individual Differences Useful to Keep Secrets at the Cost of Well-being. When it comes
   772→   772→to recruitment, one highly relevant individual difference may be trait self-concealment: a person’s
   773→   773→tendency to deliberately hide personal information from others (Larson & Chastain, 1990).
   774→   774→Someone high on this trait might sound like the perfect candidate for an undercover assignment.
   775→   775→However, research shows that the trait of self-concealment is also a predictor of psychopathology,
   776→   776→more so than having a secret (Kelly & Yip, 2006). Indeed, existing findings link high self-
   777→   777→concealment to greater psychological distress and worse well-being outcomes, such as greater
   778→   778→symptoms of anxiety, depression, loneliness, and somatic complaints (Larson & Chastain, 1990;
   779→   779→Uysal et al., 2012). In addition, self-concealment moderates the effectiveness of emotional
   780→   780→disclosure interventions, suggesting that individuals who habitually conceal their distress may
   781→   781→benefit less from self-disclosure (Kelly & Yip, 2006). Taken together, these findings suggest that
   782→   782→recruiting someone high in self-concealment might be functional to the primary objective of
   783→   783→concealing information, yet it might be counterproductive in terms of selecting people who are at
   784→   784→higher risk of occupational psychological injury in the long run.
   785→   785→Personality traits, too, offer a logical starting point for understanding which person would
   786→   786→perform best in a role, and their capacity to manage the psychological demands of professional
   787→   787→tasks. Indeed, personality assessments tools have been developed and used for recruitment purposes
   788→   788→into professions that require secrecy, for instance within law enforcement agencies (e.g. the Inwald
   789→   789→
   790→   790→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 30
   791→   791→Personality Inventory, Inwald, 2008; the Neo-PI-R, Chibnall & Detrick, 2003; the MMPI-2,
   792→   792→Cochrane et al., 2003). Although these tools are helpful in identifying desirable personality traits for
   793→   793→those professions, such as conscientiousness (Lee et al., 2008), integrity and trustworthiness (de
   794→   794→Vries et al., 2017; Hilbig & Zettler, 2015), personality traits remain largely unexplored when it
   795→   795→comes to predicting well-being outcomes in the context of professional secrecy. For instance,
   796→   796→Girodo (1985) found that higher neuroticism was associated with poorer job performance and
   797→   797→greater psychological distress among undercover police officers. Yet, it remains unclear whether
   798→   798→secrecy plays a key role in the relationship between neuroticism and those outcomes. Some recent
   799→   799→work found that conscientiousness and neuroticism are associated with keeping more secrets, and
   800→   800→extraversion with keeping fewer secrets (Boland et al., 2024), but none of the Big Five personality
   801→   801→traits were related to secret mind-wandering to—and concealment of—personal secrets in everyday
   802→   802→life (Bianchi et al, 2025). Similarly, none of the Big Five personality traits (Soto & John, 2017)
   803→   803→predicted whistleblowing behavior (Gakhar & Mulla, 2021).
   804→   804→Individual Differences Useful to Keep Secrets and to Well-being. As with self-
   805→   805→concealment, other individual differences may be relevant to the burden of professional secret-
   806→   806→keeping, though no research to date has tested their relevance past the general recruitment (and in
   807→   807→some cases, vetting) stages (Ones et al, 1993; Australian Criminal Intelligence Commission, 2024).
   808→   808→Trait self‑control and related constructs (conscientiousness, integrity) are routinely targeted in
   809→   809→vetting because they predict reliability, rule compliance and reduced likelihood of secrecy breaches
   810→   810→(Tangney et al., 2004; Moffitt et al., 2011). High self‑control should not just reduce objective risk to
   811→   811→organizations, but also support better hedonic and eudaimonic outcomes for employees who can
   812→   812→align job demands with their individual predispositions. By influencing interpersonal flexibility and
   813→   813→deception, respectively, trait self‑monitoring (Snyder, 1974; Gangestad & Snyder, 2000) and
   814→   814→Machiavellianism (Paulhus & Williams, 2002) may give a potential advantage in
   815→   815→secrecy‑demanding roles, but also be implicated in how burdensome secrecy subsequently feels on
   816→   816→
   817→   817→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 31
   818→   818→the job. For instance, high self‑monitors may suffer less stress associated with adaptive presentation
   819→   819→and role‑switching required in undercover work, but their tendency to emphasize external
   820→   820→adjustment may erode authenticity and longer‑term eudaimonic well-being (Kernis & Goldman,
   821→   821→2006). People high in Machiavellianism might also experience aspects associated with secrecy (e.g.
   822→   822→deception) less stressful, yet pose higher organizational risks (e.g. exhibiting less loyalty, or
   823→   823→engaging in more counterproductive behaviors) and show poorer social integration and well-being
   824→   824→over time (Shaw, 2023).
   825→   825→Individual Differences Posing Potential Threats to Professional Secrecy and to Well-
   826→   826→being. Finally, moral dispositions, such as moral foundations or moral identity (Graham et al.,
   827→   827→2013; Aquino & Reed, 2002) may impact on suitability for professional secrecy requirements. For
   828→   828→instance, it is possible that the burden of keeping wrongdoing hidden within organizations is
   829→   829→particularly heavy for people who prioritise fairness, increasing their likelihood of whistleblowing
   830→   830→when encountering wrongdoing. Further, a misalignment between an individual’s moral stance and
   831→   831→their organization’s in relation to keeping certain information hidden may decrease loyalty,
   832→   832→commitment and work satisfaction in employees with a strong moral identity (Ambrose et al.,
   833→   833→2007). In sum, we suggest that this non-exhaustive list of individual differences may moderate the
   834→   834→costs and benefits of professional secrecy on hedonic and eudaimonic well-being. These
   835→   835→hypotheses, however, await further testing.
   836→   836→Social and personality psychology emphasises the importance of understanding individuals
   837→   837→within the contexts in which they operate. Having outlined person-level aspects that shape the
   838→   838→impact of mandated secrecy on well-being, we now turn to situational factors as another source of
   839→   839→variance influencing the impact of professional secrecy.
   840→   840→The Where of Professional Secrets
   841→   841→The final component of our framework turns to contextual factors that may alleviate or
   842→   842→intensify the burden of professional secrecy. One obvious distinction is between organizations
   843→   843→
   844→   844→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 32
   845→   845→that require little formal secrecy, and those whose purpose is secrecy itself (Costas & Grey,
   846→   846→2016). In the latter case, employees face a dual burden. First, the challenge of keeping a secret is
   847→   847→greater, and presumably has a higher well-being cost, in jobs that foreground secrecy. Indeed,
   848→   848→intelligence operatives report a range of issues during assignments, including cumulative stress,
   849→   849→physical symptoms (i.e., stomach issues, headaches) and burnout, trust issues and social isolation
   850→   850→(particularly at critical times, such as at the beginning and at the end of assignments; Kowalczyk
   851→   851→& Sharps, 2017). Second, the stakes if one reveals a secret are higher in jobs that are built around
   852→   852→secrecy, compared to those for which secrecy is tangential. In tightly compartmentalized work
   853→   853→environments like national intelligence communities, the risks associated with secrecy failures
   854→   854→might be high—not only nationally but also personally (e.g. requiring identity changes,
   855→   855→relocation, family separation). Therefore, in professional contexts where secrecy is ‘core
   856→   856→business’, the consequences of secrets reaching the wrong ears can be far-reaching, potentially
   857→   857→involving severe legal ramifications, safety issues for oneself and dear ones, and even security
   858→   858→implications on a much larger scale.
   859→   859→Another relevant contextual factor is the amount and quality of organizational resources
   860→   860→available to buffer the burden of professional secrecy. Organizations in which structures are in place
   861→   861→to help employees keep secrets have an advantage in reducing the psychological burden of secret-
   862→   862→keeping. Jobs in organizations centred around secrecy might be better resourced to support people’s
   863→   863→performance and healthy longevity in the role. In these settings, organizational processes and
   864→   864→structures are likely to be set up in ways that facilitate the management of secrecy. Indeed, security
   865→   865→and intelligence organizations provide specialised training and preparation to improve performance
   866→   866→in information regulation tasks (Gelles et al., 2006; Herbig, 2008). Further, a culture of secrecy has
   867→   867→the potential to provide workers with a sense of security and moral justification, and a community
   868→   868→spirit (Bok, 1989; Bingley et al., 2022; Costas & Grey, 2016). Finally, when it comes to revealing
   869→   869→secrets, organizations can support their workers in understanding the process and consequences for
   870→   870→
   871→   871→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 33
   872→   872→whistleblowing, which can improve coping and resilience among those who do choose to speak out
   873→   873→(Bjørkelo et al., 2011; Near & Miceli, 1996). In sum, organizational resources in the form of
   874→   874→structural and cultural factors might protect a professional secret-keeper’s well-being, though only
   875→   875→if they are present, well-functioning, and maintained over time.
   876→   876→The need to balance challenges inside and outside the workplace is another contextual factor
   877→   877→influencing the burden of professional secrecy. In some cases, worries and concerns related to
   878→   878→professional secrets might seep into people’s off-hours, potentially eroding psychological recovery
   879→   879→and work-life balance (Sonnentag & Fritz, 2015). In other cases, secret-keeping may become a
   880→   880→fundamental aspect of employees’ professional identity (Costas & Grey, 2016), so much so that it
   881→   881→negatively impacts personal relationships (Bedrov & Gable, 2023). Crucially, who the secret is kept
   882→   882→from is likely to matter: secrecy directed at close others (partners, family) often carries greater
   883→   883→relational and emotional cost than secrecy confined within organisational bounds, while secrecy
   884→   884→intended for the broader public raises distinct ethical and reputational stakes. Role asymmetries also
   885→   885→shape perceived legitimacy—for example, withholding information from subordinates may be seen
   886→   886→as an expected duty for senior leaders but as transgressive when practised by junior staff—and these
   887→   887→judgements influence feelings of agency, fairness and moral conflict. Recent work emphasises the
   888→   888→interpersonal consequences of decisions to reveal or conceal others’ information (Hart et al., 2024),
   889→   889→reinforcing that professional obligations to disclose or to preserve confidentiality will interact with
   890→   890→social context to determine the net psychological cost. Accordingly, we theorize that professional
   891→   891→secret-keeping is most burdensome when it spills over into personal life and when the social target
   892→   892→of secrecy (close others versus organisational insiders versus the public) renders the requirement
   893→   893→incongruent with the employee’s values or relational obligations.
   894→   894→Theoretical and Practical Implications
   895→   895→We presented a framework that articulates the challenges and costs involved in imposed
   896→   896→secrecy. The value of this model is two-fold. First, it provides a novel roadmap to identify the
   897→   897→
   898→   898→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 34
   899→   899→precise mechanisms behind the burden of secrecy people are mandated to keep for their job.
   900→   900→Second, it offers a foundation for application across diverse industries to support the well-being of
   901→   901→secret-keepers in professional settings and beyond.
   902→   902→Theoretical Advancements. This framework articulates the main elements that determine
   903→   903→the psychological costs of secrecy. In this, we distinguish the motivation behind keeping
   904→   904→professional secrets, the content of secrets from the process of keeping secrets, and person-related
   905→   905→factors from situational factors that influence the impact of secrets. Accordingly, our review
   906→   906→identified five interrelated elements that influence the burden of professional secrecy: why a secret
   907→   907→is kept; what is being kept secret; how one keeps the secret; who is keeping the secret; and the
   908→   908→context where the secret is kept. This model advances the broader secrecy literature: while existing
   909→   909→theories of personal secrecy have articulated the processes through which well-being is harmed
   910→   910→(Slepian, 2022), they have not yet incorporated the other factors highlighted in our review. In
   911→   911→developing this framework, we provide a holistic guide to the burden of secrecy. We also
   912→   912→acknowledge—and lay the groundwork for testing how—professional (or imposed) secrecy differs
   913→   913→from personal (or internal) secrecy.
   914→   914→While the lion’s share of literature to date has focused on the well-being impact of
   915→   915→information people personally choose to keep hidden (Slepian et al., 2023; cf. Slepian &
   916→   916→Greenaway, 2018), we shed light on secrets that people are professionally mandated to keep hidden.
   917→   917→That is, rather than being kept by choice, professional secrets are often imposed on individuals by
   918→   918→roles and rules, tasks associated with the nature of the job, or other organizational requirements.
   919→   919→Accordingly, our framework allows for the possibility, and articulates ways in which, professional
   920→   920→secrets may harm—or support—personnel through processes that are distinct from the ones
   921→   921→identified in the personal secrecy literature. For instance, extrinsic motivation for keeping
   922→   922→professional secrets may aid concealment processes, resulting in less of a well-being burden. This
   923→   923→may be either because the demands of concealment are lower, or resources for concealing are
   924→   924→
   925→   925→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 35
   926→   926→higher, when people have a ‘reason’ not to disclose, or when jobs train their employees in how to
   927→   927→handle concealment episodes. However, extrinsic motivation might exacerbate mind-wandering to
   928→   928→professional secrets, potentially worsening hedonic well-being. This could be the case because
   929→   929→imposed secrets induce cost-benefit analyses, weighing professional obligations against potential
   930→   930→personal or societal benefits of disclosure. That is, extrinsically motivated secrets might induce
   931→   931→cognitive dissonance when attempting to reconcile the imposed secret with one’s personal values
   932→   932→and beliefs (Festinger, 1957; Harmon-Jones & Mills, 2019). Experimental approaches manipulating
   933→   933→motivational orientations could test whether a shift towards intrinsic motivation alleviates the
   934→   934→challenges associated with keeping professional secrets.
   935→   935→Our framework challenges assumptions of what might constitute “successful” secret-
   936→   936→keeping in professional environments. Traditionally, success in professional secrecy has been
   937→   937→measured by the ability to regulate information (Costas & Grey, 2014; Hanna et al., 2015). We
   938→   938→propose a more nuanced view that adds personal well-being as an additional component of
   939→   939→successful secret-keeping. This addition is particularly important in professional settings, where the
   940→   940→stakes and long-term implications of regulating information can be higher—and more wide-
   941→   941→ranging—than in personal contexts. Our theoretical perspective outlines a range of individual
   942→   942→differences that are likely to act as moderators of the relationship between professional demands
   943→   943→and well-being outcomes. These include personality traits, social and moral dispositional
   944→   944→tendencies. Our analysis suggests that certain individual differences may facilitate secret keeping at
   945→   945→the expense of personal well-being (e.g., trait self-concealment), others may protect well-being
   946→   946→while letting information slip (e.g., extraversion, machiavellianism), and still others hold promise
   947→   947→for fulfilling secrecy and well-being needs alike (e.g., self-control; certain forms of moral identity).
   948→   948→By incorporating individual differences and their impact on secret-keeping and its psychological
   949→   949→costs, our theory suggests a paradigm shift in how organizations understand and approach
   950→   950→information regulation management.
   951→   951→
   952→   952→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 36
   953→   953→Finally, our framework outlines the burden of professional secrecy across diverse life and
   954→   954→organizational contexts. This approach allows for a more nuanced understanding of how specific
   955→   955→professional environments shape secret-keeping processes. We identified five orthogonal contextual
   956→   956→factors that may influence the burden of professional secrecy: the centrality of secrecy to a given
   957→   957→role, the consequences of revealing secrets, the resources available to enact secrecy, the degree to
   958→   958→which secrecy ‘spills over’ to influence life domains outside work, and characteristics of the target
   959→   959→of the secret. Each of these factors has implications for how professionals face unique challenges
   960→   960→related to specific job requirements involving secrecy. Further, this framework allows researchers to
   961→   961→identify relevant domains of secrecy across professions. For instance, evaluating and grouping
   962→   962→professions on these contextual factors may allow researchers to develop interventions to assist
   963→   963→secret-keeping across fields as diverse as law, healthcare, and national security, while also
   964→   964→recognizing unique challenges faced in each domain.
   965→   965→Real World Applications. Beyond its novel theoretical contributions, our framework holds
   966→   966→promise in translating research into practice, to safeguard personnel’s well-being in a wide variety
   967→   967→of settings. Empirical insights from our theoretical model could find application in how departments
   968→   968→structure and conduct selection processes for professionals handling high-stakes, long-term secret
   969→   969→information. These assessments hold promise for identifying individuals with the resilience and
   970→   970→flexibility necessary for managing, and coping with, sensitive information over extended periods.
   971→   971→These tools would be helpful when recruiting for a variety of roles, from cybersecurity experts, to
   972→   972→counter-terrorism specialists, and diplomats.
   973→   973→In many settings, the sensitive nature of the work precludes traditional ways of easing the
   974→   974→burden of secrecy and accessing support (i.e., confiding). To address these challenges, our
   975→   975→framework suggests useful targets of intervention for programs that go beyond traditional blueprints
   976→   976→for concealing information. For instance, rather than focusing solely on methods of concealment,
   977→   977→training modules could incorporate stress management, cognitive reappraisal techniques, and
   978→   978→
   979→   979→THE BURDEN OF IMPOSED SECRECY 37
   980→   980→strategies for maintaining work-life balance while protecting high-stakes secrets.
   981→   981→Finally, our model also holds value for application in settings where information regulation
   982→   982→is an everyday aspect of the role, albeit with lower stakes. Examples include human resources
   983→   983→departments, customer service representatives handling sensitive data, and product development
   984→   984→teams working on unreleased projects. In these contexts, insights stemming from investigations
   985→   985→guided by our four-pronged model could inform the development of innovative technologies such
   986→   986→as AI-driven well-being monitoring tools. These tools would afford organizations the ability to take
   987→   987→a personalized approach to the management of stress related to information regulation, rather than
   988→   988→conducting “one-size-fits-all” organization-wide interventions. Such advancements would offer
   989→   989→timely and cost-effective psychological support, while bypassing human intermediaries.
   990→   990→Conclusion
   991→   991→Secrecy is a dangerous business—and not just for James Bond and Mata Hari. We document
   992→   992→the ways in which professional secrecy may impact personnel tasked with protecting information in
   993→   993→many different professions. Expanding on existing theoretical and empirical insights from
   994→   994→literatures in a range of psychological subdisciplines, we propose a five-component framework to
   995→   995→better understand the ways imposed secrecy impacts secret-keepers. Our framework gives equal
   996→   996→weight to why information is being kept secret, what is kept secret, how secrets are kept, who is
   997→   997→doing the keeping, and the conditions where secrecy occurs. We hope that this analysis inspires
   998→   998→more targeted research investigating how to alleviate the quantum of imposed silence.
