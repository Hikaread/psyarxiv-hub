    31→Summary
    32→
    33→Background: Mental disorders affect 27% of UK women during the perinatal period (pregnancy and the first postpartum year) and are associated with increased risk of adverse maternal and child outcomes. Despite the societal burden, access to mental health services remains inconsistent, particularly for certain ethnic groups. The aim of this study was to characterise pregnant women, from an ethnically and socioeconomically diverse UK antenatal urban population, with identified risk of mental disorders in the perinatal period,  and to evaluate  the proportion in contact with mental health services We also explore potential inequity and barriers to access, as measured by referral and engagement with care.  
    34→
    35→Methods: This study utilised linked maternity and mental health records from the eLIXIR Born in South London database, comprising 67,308 pregnancies from 2018 to 2023. These records provided information about: antenatal mental health assessments through three areas of enquiry: Whooley (depression screening) questions, 2) self-reported mental health conditions, 3) family history of severe mental illness, as well as referrals to, and engagement with mental health services during pregnancy. 
    36→
    37→Findings: Among those in contact with mental health services, 38.39%, 24.73%, and 6.14% had been identified by one, two, or three areas of antenatal mental health enquiry, respectively. A positive relationship was observed between the number of questions endorsed and referral to and engagement with treatment. However, ethnic disparities were evident: Asian and Asian British women had lower odds of referral to mental health service (OR=0.51, 95% CI [0.42, 0.61]) and engagement (OR = 0.73, 95% CI [.59, .91]), compared to White women, while Black, African, Caribbean, and Black British women had higher odds of referral (OR=1.45, 95% CI [1.32, 1.60]), but lower odds of engagement (OR=0.73, 95% CI [0.59, 0.91]). Women requiring an interpreter had lower odds of referral (OR = 0.69, 95% CI [0.56, 0.82]).
    38→
    39→Interpretation: The study highlights significant disparities in identification and treatment of mental disorders in the perinatal period, perinatal mental disorders. Improved referral and care pathways, and culturally sensitive assessments are indicated to redress these disparities.

    53→Research in context:
    54→Evidence before this study
    55→
    56→Mental disorders affect up to 27% of women during pregnancy and the year following birth (the perinatal period), are associated with adverse maternal, fetal, and child outcomes, including maternal suicide, low birth weight, and long-term developmental issues. In the UK in 2016 national investment was directed towards specialist perinatal mental health services and clinical guidelines, emphasising early identification and referral, using tools such as the Whooley questions. Despite these initiatives, evidence suggests a persistent gap between identification of mental health risk in routine antenatal care and subsequent referral to, and engagement with, mental health services. Additionally, ethnic and socioeconomic disparities in mental health care access have been repeatedly observed in the literature. Prior research has rarely examined how individual-level antenatal screening responses relate to actual care engagement in real-world, ethnically diverse settings.
    57→
    58→Added value of this study
    59→
    60→This is the first large-scale population-level study in the UK to use linked maternity and mental health records to assess the relationship between antenatal screening indicators of perinatal mental health risk and actual service referral and engagement. By analysing data from over 67,000 pregnancies in a highly diverse urban population, it highlights important disparities in referral and engagement with mental health services, particularly among women from ethnic minority backgrounds and those requiring interpreters. The study also quantifies the proportion of women who, despite being flagged as at risk through screening, received no mental health care, suggesting systemic barriers in the care pathway. Although a single positive indicator should prompt further assessment and treatment, we observed a positive relationship between the number of positive mental health screening indicators and subsequent mental health service use. 
    61→
    62→Implications of all available evidence.
    63→
    64→These findings confirm and extend existing concerns about the incomplete translation of perinatal mental health screening into equitable care delivery. The study underscores the need for improved integration between maternity and mental health services, culturally responsive care models, and follow-up mechanisms that ensure screening leads to timely treatment. Given the disproportionate under-referral of women requiring interpreters and those from some ethnic minority backgrounds, our data support calls for targeted interventions to reduce structural and communication barriers to optimal perinatal mental health management. Health systems should prioritise equity-focused models that account for individual mental health risk, social context, and cultural sensitivity throughout the perinatal period.

    76→Background
    77→
    78→During pregnancy and the first twelve months following childbirth approximately 27% of women experience a mental disorder (perinatal mental disorder) (1). Untreated perinatal mental disorders are associated with several adverse outcomes, including maternal suicidality, lower birth weight, sub-optimal child growth  and prolonged child development difficulties (2). The economic burden to Society is also substantial, for both women and their children, with an estimated cost of £8.1 billion per year in the UK(3).  To mitigate the wide range of potential impacts, affected women should be accurately identified and receive timely treatment.
    79→
    80→Despite increasing investment  in perinatal mental health services in England (4), underfunding remains one of many barriers to accessing appropriate services (5, 6).  In January 2016, more than £290 million was invested to develop specialist perinatal mental health services in England (7), with further funds for community and inpatient care up to two years postpartum. Clinical guidelines reinforce the importance of  early assessment and identification of mental disorders during pregnancy (8). 
    81→
    82→At a pregnant woman’s first contact with maternity services (typically 10–12 weeks gestation), guidelines recommend asking about any past or present mental illness, previous or current treatment, and family history of mental disorder, including severe postpartum mental disorder in a first-degree relative (9). Standardised tools such as the Whooley questions for depression are integrated into a general conversation about mental health and wellbeing. The first two Whooley questions ask “During the past month, have you often been bothered by feeling down, depressed, or hopeless?”, and “During the past month, have you often been bothered by little interest or please in doing things?”  A third asks “Is this something you feel you need or want help with?”. A positive response to any of these (i.e. saying ‘yes’ to at least one) (10) should prompt further assessment and referral to appropriate services for evaluation of the type and severity of mental health problems (11). A meta-analysis of Whooley assessments has reported high sensitivity i.e. accurately identifying most negative cases of perinatal depression,  and moderate specificity, with some risk of false positives (12). 
    83→
    84→When an increased risk of a perinatal mental disorder is identified by maternity staff, further psychosocial assessment is warranted to identify the type and severity of mental disorder, and to determine subsequent treatment. Whilst previous studies  indicate that approximately 20% of women in UK antenatal care report current or historical mental health problems to their midwife at their first antenatal appointment (13), patterns of referral to, and treatment by, mental health services appear inconsistent (14).  Inequities in access to the appropriate treatment may be due to individual (e.g. ethnicity or mental health literacy) or systemic factors (e.g. patient’s postcode and the treatment available). Related to this, two sequential reviews in 2019 by the UK National Screening Committee (15) reported that whilst most women are asked about their mental health, ethnic minorities are especially vulnerable to inequitable identification and management of possible mental disorders (14, 16). 
    85→
    86→These findings emphasise, that despite investment in early identification and referral of perinatal mental disorders and supporting clinical guidance, a substantial proportion of affected women remain untreated. Limited effort has been directed towards understanding how specific responses to screening questions relate to downstream care pathways, and how sociodemographic factors may influence these relationships in real-world settings at a population level. Our analysis drew on a large data linkage of routine maternity and mental health electronic records from South London, UK. This inner-city population includes people from a wide range of ethnic and socioeconomic backgrounds, allowing exploration of those factors which may contribute to inequity in referral and engagement of perinatal mental health disorders. The study aimed to:
    87→
    88→Identify to proportion of women identified within maternity services as having/at risk of a perinatal mental disorder (i.e., positive Whooley score, self-reported mental health condition, family history of severe mental illness) and describe their sociodemographic characteristics.
    89→Identify the proportion of women at risk of perinatal mental disorder who were referred to and engaged with mental health services during pregnancy and the nature of the mental health care received. 
    90→Describe the sociodemographic, psychosocial, and clinical characteristics of those receiving a referral and engaging with mental health care.

    93→Methods
    94→
    95→Data sources
    96→
    97→This study utilised linked maternity and mental health records held within the Early Life Cross-Linkage in Research (eLIXIR, Born in South London) database. eLIXIR utilises a repository of real-time pseudonymised data from linked data sets in South London, UK. Data are extracted from the electronic health records of two acute hospitals where maternity services are provided (Guys and St Thomas and King’s College Hospital NHS Trusts) and one mental health NHS provider organisation (South London and Maudsley NHS Foundation Trust, SLaM) (17, 18). Data are obtained through opt-out consent.
    98→
    99→
   100→Ethics approval for the eLIXIR Partnership was granted by the Oxford Research Ethics Committee (approval number 23/SC/0116). The Health Research Authority Confidential Advisory Group (approval number 18/CAG/0040) provided approval under Section 251 (s251) of the NHS Act (2006) for the eLIXIR partnership to develop a pseudonymised database for secondary analysis. This research project was approved by the eLIXIR Oversight Committee in November 2020 (reference: DL018).
   101→
   102→
   103→Maternity and neonatal data from GSTT and KCH were recorded on the BadgerNet electronic patient record system (CleverMed). Data pertaining to community and hospital appointments during pregnancy are captured within the system. Linked mental health records were derived from the SLaM Clinical Records Interactive Search (CRIS) system (19). Selected variables from the SLAM CRIS dataset and BadgerNet systems, linked at the individual level as part of the eLIXIR partnership database, were extracted. Pregnancies were extracted from 1 October 2018 to 11 July 2023, which represented the entire timespan available at the time of data extraction.
   104→
   105→Antenatal maternity care variables
   106→
   107→Antenatal maternity care variables were extracted from the BadgerNet dataset. As part of women’s routine assessment, women are asked about current or past physical and mental health, alongside questions related to socioeconomic status. The following fields, recorded by the midwife at the women’s first antenatal appointment (booking appointment) were extracted:
   108→
   109→
   110→Antenatal maternity data: weeks’ gestation at booking, parity, gravidity. 
   111→
   112→Sociodemographic information: maternal age, ethnicity, Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD)(20), employment status, percentage born in the UK, percentage of those first language is English, need for an interpreter. 
   113→
   114→Antenatal mental health data: Current risk of perinatal mental disorder was determined from three areas of enquiry  and classified, for the purposes of this study, as requiring additional assessment and potentially treatment for perinatal mental disorder if they had either: 1) a Whooley positive score, 2) a self-reported prior or current mental health diagnosis, or a self-reported family history of mental disorder  and response to the following question 3) “Is this something you feel you need or want help with?” (yes/no).
   115→
   116→Mental health service variables
   117→
   118→For women in contact with mental health services from the first antenatal appointment to discharge post-delivery, the following data were extracted from CRIS records and linked with antenatal care data: 
   119→At least one referral to any mental health services during pregnancy (binary yes/no).
   120→Engagement was defined as whether a woman had attended at least one appointment for either community care, inpatient care, or NHS Talking Therapies (previously known as Improving Access to Psychological Therapies/IAPT) (binary yes/no). 
   121→
   122→Statistical analyses
   123→
   124→Data were analysed using R. Twin or triplet pregnancies were retained and considered as a single pregnancy for the purposes of analysis. For aims 1 and 2, descriptive statistics of sociodemographic, psychosocial and clinical characteristics were recorded for the cohort. To investigate aim 3, the mental health service sample (those who had a record within CRIS) alone was used. Univariate logistic regressions were conducted to assess differences in referral and engagement, and exposure of endorsing the mental health screening questions. To account for women who had more than one pregnancy during the time period of the cohort, a random intercept was included in all regression models. Odds ratios were reported with 95% confidence intervals. Data were deemed to be missing completely at random. As such, complete case analysis was employed.

Social deprivation was lower in the total sample compared to the mental health samples, with 17.95% in the most deprived category compared to 23.24% in the CRIS sample.

   146→Characteristics influencing referral to mental health care
   147→
   148→In the entire sample, several factors were significantly associated with the likelihood of referral to mental health services. In respect to the area of enquiry endorsed, women who identified as Whooley positive, in comparison to the reference group of not Whooley positive, had significantly higher odds of referral to mental health services (OR = 8.85, 95% CI [8.14, 9.62]). Those with a prior mental disorder, compared to those without, demonstrated increased odds (OR = 16.87, 95% CI [15.18, 18.80]), while having a family history of mental disorder was also associated with higher odds of referral (OR = 3.09, 95% CI [2.81, 3.39]). 
   149→Those who endorsed two areas of enquiry had significantly higher odds of referral compared to those with one (OR = 3.71, 95% CI [3.37, 4.08]), and those with three had even greater odds (OR = 6.73, 95% CI [5.78, 7.82]). IMD was also associated with service referral; women from the least deprived areas (IMD 5) had significantly lower odds of referral compared to those from the most deprived (IMD 1), with ORs decreasing progressively across IMD quintiles (IMD 2: OR = 0.99, 95% CI [.90, 1.10]; IMD 3: OR = 0.73, 95% CI [.65, .83]; IMD 4: OR = 0.42, 95% CI [.035, 0.50]; IMD 5: OR = 0.26, 95% CI [0.20, 0.34]).
   150→
   151→Black/African/Caribbean/Black British women (OR = 1.45, 95% CI [1.32, 1.60]) and Mixed/Multiple ethnic backgrounds (OR=1.90, 95%CI [1.64, 2.20]) had higher odds of referral to mental health services compared to White, and Other Asian/Asian backgrounds had lower odds (OR = 0.51, 95% CI [0.42, 0.61]). The need for an interpreter was associated with lower odds of referral (OR = 0.69, 95% CI [0.56, 0.82]), while late booking was associated with increased odds of referral (OR=1.14, 95%CI [1.03, 1.25]).

   156→Characteristics influencing engagement with mental health care
   157→The likelihood of engaging with mental health services during pregnancy was also significantly associated with positive endorsement of areas of mental health enquiry at booking appointments. Women with a positive Whooley score had an increased odds of engaging with services (OR = 3.91, 95% CI [3.53, 4.32]). Those with a prior mental health condition (OR = 3.78, 95% CI [3.40, 4.21]) and those with a family history of mental illness (OR = 1.98, 95% CI [1.77, 2.21]) were also higher odds of engaging with mental health services.
   158→When comparing women with no endorsements to those with one screening question (reference group), the odds of engaging with services were significantly lower (OR = 0.35, 95% CI [.31, .40]). Women who endorsed two questions had higher odds of engagement (OR = 2.49, 95% CI [2.22, 2.78]), and those endorsing three questions had the highest odds (OR = 3.73, 95% CI [3.09, 4.53]).
   159→Regarding social deprivation (IMD), compared to the most deprived group (reference), the odds of engaging with services were significantly higher in IMD 2 (OR = 1.14, 95% CI [1.01, 1.28) and IMD 3 (OR = 1.25, 95% CI [1.09, 1.42]). No significant differences were found in IMD 4 (OR = 0.85, 95% CI [.70, 1.03]), but IMD 5 had significantly lower odds of engagement (OR = 0.69, 95% CI [0.51, 0.93]).
   160→Engagement with mental health services also varied by ethnicity. Compared to White women (reference), Other Asian/Asian British ethnicities had lower odds of engaging with services (OR = 0.73, 95% CI [0.59, 0.91], p < .01). Women from other ethnic groups, including Black/African/Caribbean/ Black British, Any Other Ethnic Group and Mixed/Multiple ethnic groups, did not significantly differ from White women.
   161→For women who endorsed two or three mental health screening questions, the analysis revealed key differences from the entire sample. Having a prior mental health condition was not a significant predictor of service engagement in this subset (OR = 1.48, 95% CI [0.78, 2.85), in contrast to the significant association found in the full sample. Additionally, a family history of mental illness was associated with reduced odds of engaging with services (OR = 0.67, 95% CI [.58, 0.78]), differing from the higher odds observed in the entire sample. Regarding social deprivation (IMD) those in IMD 4 (or=0.59, 95%CI [0.43, 0.81]) and IMD 5 (OR=0.44, 95%CI [0.27, 0.71]) had significantly lower odds of engagement than IMD 1. While Black/African/Caribbean/Black British women had significantly higher odds of engagement with services than White women (OR=1.32, 95%CI [1.08, 1.60]).

   164→Discussion
   165→
   166→To our knowledge, this is the first study using linked routine electronic health data to examine the relationship between antenatal mental health assessments at booking and subsequent treatment engagement. We found, in this UK inner city population with a high prevalence of mental disorders a positive relationship between the number of positive responses to mental disorder assessments at booking appointment and referral to mental health care. Whooley positive endorsement and family history of mental ill health were consistently associated with referral to and engagement with care. Among women who we identified as at risk of perinatal mental disorders based on their antenatal booking appointment, the odds of being referred to and engaging with treatment increased with the number of areas of enquiry endorsed, suggesting women disclosing fewer symptoms may be overlooked, leading to under-identification and missed opportunity for early intervention. Different ethnic groups showed disparities in referral and engagement with mental health care. Other Asian/ Asian British had significantly lower odds for referral to and engagement with care than White women, while Black/African/Caribbean/Black British had higher odds for referral but lower for engagement than White women. 
   167→
   168→Overall, this study underscores the inequity in referral and treatment of women identified as at risk of perinatal mental disorders, and aligns with others reported in primary care in the US and the UK (14, 21). Our findings do not suggest any major limitations with the current methods of enquiry about risk for mental disorder in UK maternity services, given the increased odds of referral following a positive response to an identification question. However, the reliability of these methods is recognised to be dependent on mode of administration and setting, with methods involving self-report and set in primary care being more accurate (12, 22). Recommendations propose that after initial enquiry in pregnancy, follow up is undertaken by an appropriate clinical professional (11), fostering interpersonal aspects (e.g. trusting relationship, honest communication) that may  support engagement with mental healthcare (5). Furthermore, women’s mental health may deteriorate in the postnatal period and routine enquiry by maternity teams are paramount during this time to ensure ongoing access to mental health treatment.
   169→
   170→A proportion of women with a positive response to all three areas of enquiry did not engage with treatment in the setting investigated. Routine assessment by midwives of psychosocial factors is largely accepted by women (23) however our findings highlight that this does not necessarily lead to referral, acceptance and provision of effective treatment for all women (24). A potential explanation for not all women accepting a referral and/or engaging with care, may stem from them not feeling able to fully disclose psychosocial or mental health concerns to clinicians (25). We found that individuals who use interpreters had lower odds of receiving a referral. This may reflect clinician uncertainty, communication barriers, or systemic issues in accessing culturally and linguistically appropriate care. Future research should consider each of these potential explanations, including whether this reflects implicit bias, service limitations, reduced disclosure, or gaps in clinician training and confidence when working through interpreters. 
   171→
   172→This study partially aligns with previous reports describing disparities between Black, and Asian individuals in comparison to White individuals, as in both general and antenatal populations they  are less likely to be referred to mental health services for common mental disorders such as anxiety and depression (16) and more likely to be  involuntarily admitted to secondary services (26). A possible reason for those from Asian backgrounds having significantly lower rates of referral and engagement than White women may be fear of disclosure and institutional power (27) or migration status (28), and the associated combined prejudice of ethnicity and stigma.  By contrast, Black women were more likely to be referred than White women, but less likely to engage with services. Ethnic disparities in referral to mental health inpatient and community care for women during the antenatal period are well documented but the description of ethnicity using  broad ethnic groupings (16) may inhibit development of nuanced healthcare policy. Antenatal mental health services should focus on improving equity in both referral to, and engagement with care, encouraging integration of care services (e.g. interpreters, social services and community third sector organisations). Also, increasing cultural sensitivity, as organisational and sociocultural factors have been found to be barriers to care in a recent review (29). 
   173→
   174→
   175→Strengths and limitations 
   176→Level of deprivation, as assessed by IMD, is a measure of group level deprivation rather than individual; future investigations would usefully include more individual measures such as household income. An important limitation of our categorisation is the assumption that IAPT, community, and inpatient services are discrete care pathways. In reality, perinatal mental health care is often non-linear, and women may transition between service types, and our analysis may underrepresent the complexity and fluidity of these care journeys. Future research using trajectory-based methods could offer more nuanced insights into service utilisation over time.
   177→
   178→We acknowledge that statistical significance does not always equate to clinical or contextual relevance, especially in large samples where small effects can appear significant. As such our interpretation focuses on effect sizes, confidence intervals, and alignment with existing literature. While multiple comparisons increase the risk of type I error, we did not apply formal corrections to avoid inflating type II error and advise caution when interpreting findings near the null.
   179→
   180→Conclusions 
   181→
   182→In conclusion, this novel population level study of a multi-ethnic inner city antenatal population underscores the critical need to address the significant challenges in identifying perinatal mental disorders and pathways to care, in light of the disparities in referral to and engagement with mental health services. We identified significant disparities in referral and engagement among different ethnic groups. Culturally sensitive assessments and care are essential to ensure that all women, regardless of their background, receive equitable and effective mental health support. This includes integrating care services, such as interpreters and community organizations, to improve referral and engagement.
