    17→Key Points
    18→Question: Does a gold-standard autism assessment, the Autism Diagnostic Observation
    19→Schedule (ADOS), show sex bias in specific autistic traits?
    20→Findings: In 19,569 individuals across the full developmental and language spectrum, significant
    21→sex bias was observed primarily for verbally fluent adolescents and adults. Females tended to be
    22→underscored on social communication and overscored on restricted and repetitive behavior items,
    23→and the effect was small (<1 point).
    24→Meaning: Findings suggest that sex bias in the ADOS is minimal at lower ages and language
    25→levels, but assessments of verbally fluent adults might benefit from adding measures of
    26→camouflaging or sex-specific norms.
    27→Abstract
    28→Importance Autistic females are consistently underidentified relative to males. Whether item-
    29→level sex bias in the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS), the gold standard
    30→observational assessment of autism spectrum disorder, contributes to this disparity across the full
    31→developmental and language range remains unknown.
    32→Objectives To examine differential item functioning (DIF) by sex in ADOS algorithm items
    33→across all modules.
    34→Design Cross-sectional study.
    35→Setting Item-level ADOS data were drawn from the NIMH Data Archive, Simons Simplex
    36→Collection, Simons Foundation Powering Autism Research for Knowledge, and Autism Inpatient
    37→Collection.
    38→
    39→Participants Individuals with item-level ADOS or ADOS-2 data who were administered the
    40→age-appropriate module.
    41→Exposure Sex (female vs. male)
    42→Main Outcome and Measures Sex-based DIF was examined separately within each of 8
    43→module and language-level combinations, covering 112 algorithm items total. For each
    44→combination, a graded response item response theory model was fit to the item set, and DIF was
    45→evaluated using likelihood ratio test approach.
    46→Results A total of 19,569 individuals (14,807 male [75.7%]; 4,760 female [24.3%]) aged 12
    47→months to 75 years were included. Of 112 items analyzed, eleven unique items showed
    48→significant sex DIF after correcting for type I error. No significant DIF was detected in the
    49→Toddler Module, Module 1 Low Language, or Module 2 after correction. Significant DIF was
    50→detected in Module 1 High Language (3 items) and Module 3 (1 item), with Module 4 showing
    51→the greatest number of significant items (7 of 14). Overall, adult females tended to be
    52→underscored on social communication items and overscored on items concerning restricted and
    53→repetitive behaviors and interests. All effect sizes were small except one (stereotyped language,
    54→Module 1 High Language).
    55→Conclusions and Relevance These findings suggest that the ADOS is largely robust to sex-
    56→based measurement bias for younger individuals and those with less language, but significant
    57→bias exists for verbally fluent adolescents and adults. The domain-specific pattern of bias is
    58→consistent with female camouflaging of social difficulties and sex differences in the presentation
    59→of restricted, repetitive behaviors and interests. Clinician training and future instrument
    60→development should account for these differences to improve diagnostic equity across sex.
    61→
    62→Introduction
    63→The prevalence of autism spectrum disorder (“autism”) has risen substantially over the
    64→past two decades, with recent surveillance data indicating that approximately 1 in 31 children in
    65→the United States meet criteria for autism1. Across epidemiological studies and geographic
    66→regions, disparities in autism prevalence by sex represent one of the most consistently replicated
    67→findings, with males diagnosed at rates three to four times higher than females1,2. While
    68→biological factors may contribute to this sex imbalance3,4, growing evidence suggests that
    69→ascertainment and measurement biases also play a critical role5. In particular, diagnostic
    70→instruments developed and validated using predominantly male samples may be less sensitive to
    71→female presentations of autism, potentially contributing to the under-identification of autistic
    72→females6,7. Clarifying whether these instruments measure autistic traits equivalently across sexes
    73→is an important step toward understanding sex disparities in autism prevalence.
    74→Sex differences in the behavioral presentation of autism may contribute to this
    75→measurement challenge. The female presentation of autism differs from the male presentation in
    76→ways that may be unfamiliar to clinicians given the historical focus on males8,9. Specifically,
    77→autistic females tend to have milder restricted and repetitive behaviors, and their focused
    78→interests are often more similar to those of neurotypical same-sex peers in content10. Females
    79→across the lifespan are also more likely to use camouflaging strategies, which involve hiding
    80→autistic behaviors or compensating for social difficulties by mimicking others11-13. These
    81→differences are evident during diagnostic assessments, where autistic females may demonstrate
    82→greater verbal interest in social topics such as friendships14 and receive higher clinician ratings of
    83→social and emotional insight relative to males15,16. Together, these presentation differences may
    84→complicate clinical judgment during diagnostic evaluations.
    85→
    86→Given that standardized clinical instruments inform diagnostic decisions, careful
    87→evaluation of their measurement properties across sexes is essential. The Autism Diagnostic
    88→Observation Schedule (ADOS) is the most widely used observational diagnostic assessment for
    89→autism and is often regarded as the “gold standard”17,18. During administration, clinicians engage
    90→individuals in semi-structured activities designed to elicit autism-related behaviors, which are
    91→then scored on an ordinal scale. Although the ADOS has demonstrated strong reliability and
    92→validity across age groups and language levels, its development and validation relied largely on
    93→male samples17,19, raising concerns about whether items function equivalently in males and
    94→females5. Additionally, while standardized training is required for ADOS examiners, clinician
    95→experience and behavior during administration may still introduce bias20,21.
    96→Emerging evidence suggests that some ADOS items may indeed function differently for
    97→males and females, though findings have been mixed22. Factor analyses indicate that certain
    98→items, such as those assessing eye contact or unusual sensory interests, are stronger indicators of
    99→underlying autistic traits in one sex than the other23,24. Using item response theory (IRT), some
   100→studies have found no significant differential item functioning (DIF) by sex22,25. However, other
   101→studies have identified item level bias. Kalb and collegues26 identified DIF in five items across
   102→modules 1 through 3, with hand mannerisms showing significant DIF across all algorithms
   103→examined. Females were consistently underscored, meaning that females required higher levels
   104→of underlying autistic traits than males to receive equivalent scores—greater difficulty
   105→parameters for females, in IRT parlance). Yu and collegues16 reported the opposite pattern in
   106→Module 3, finding that females were overscored on two restricted and repetitive behavior items
   107→(hand mannerisms and specific interests/repetitive behaviors). With regard to social
   108→communication, three previous studies all consistently reported that females were rated as less
   109→
   110→impaired on multiple social communication items relative to males with same latent trait levels.
   111→Despite these advances, a limitation of this body of work is that most studies have examined only
   112→a small subset of items or of the population (e.g., only early childhood, or only verbally fluent
   113→children who received Module 3).
   114→The current study aimed to address inconsistencies by examining sex DIF in ADOS items
   115→in over 19,000 individuals drawn from four large research databases. We analyzed algorithm
   116→items across eight module and language level combinations, spanning the instrument’s full
   117→developmental range from toddlers through adults. Such comprehensive examination is a critical
   118→step toward understanding whether measurement bias contributes to sex disparities in autism
   119→identification. Based on prior findings, we hypothesized that specific items would exhibit DIF,
   120→with females underscored on items relative to males.
   121→Methods
   122→Participants
   123→The present study compiled data from four sources that possessed item-level ADOS data:
   124→the Simons Simplex Collection (SSC; n = 2,545), the Simons Powering Autism Research for
   125→Knowledge (SPARK; n = 2,104), the Autism Inpatient Collection (AIC; n = 1,466), and the
   126→National Institute of Mental Health Data Archive (NDA; n = 13,454) (Supplemental Methods;
   127→Table S1). Participants were included in the present study if they had item-level ADOS or
   128→ADOS-2 data available and were administered the age-appropriate module (N = 19,569; Table
   129→1). Additional variables of age, race, and diagnosis were extracted and harmonized across data
   130→sources (Table 1; Tables S2-S6).
   131→Measures
   132→
   133→The ADOS is a gold-standard assessment widely used in autism diagnostic evaluations.
   134→The ADOS is separated into five distinct modules to evaluate autistic behaviors based on age and
   135→language level (Toddler Module Low/High Language, Module 1 Low/High Language, Module 2
   136→Low/High Language, Module 3, and Module 4; Table 1 and Supplemental Methods). Module
   137→and language combinations yield eight unique item sets that were analyzed separately given that
   138→items often have different meanings across age and language; for example, the gesture item
   139→refers to conversational beat and emphatic gestures for verbally fluent adults in Module 4, and to
   140→waving ‘hi’ for a toddler without spoken language in Module 1.
   141→Each module includes 28-39 items on which trained examiners rate the participant as 0, 1,
   142→or 2 (for more details, see supplemental methods). A score of 2 reflects “definite” or “marked”
   143→atypicality, while a 1 reflects “mild” atypicality and a 0 reflects “no evidence” of atypicality18.
   144→Scores on a subset of items, called “algorithm items” (Tables S7-S11), are summed to construct a
   145→total raw score, which has empirically determined cut-offs for the classification of autism, autism
   146→spectrum, and non-spectrum18. The present analysis leveraged algorithm item scores, as these are
   147→most commonly reported in databases.
   148→The ADOS items broadly assess five domains of behavior: A) language and
   149→communication (e.g., echolalia, gestures), B) reciprocal social interaction (e.g., eye contact,
   150→facial expressions), C) imagination (e.g., creative play or comments), D) stereotyped behaviors
   151→and restricted interests (e.g., finger tensing, sensory interests in toys), and E) other behaviors
   152→(e.g., anxiety, tantrums). Multiple factor structures fit the ADOS items, although the 2-factor
   153→structure is the most widely supported model22-24, consisting of Social Communication (SC) and
   154→Restricted and Repetitive Behaviors and Interests (RRBI).
   155→Analysis
   156→
   157→Factor Structure
   158→Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) was conducted using the two-factor structure of the
   159→ADOS, with the two factors allowed to correlate with each other. Although unidimensional and
   160→three-factor models are occasionally used in the literature, the two-factor model is widely
   161→supported for DIF analysis. Furthermore, parallel analysis—a standard method for determining
   162→the number of factors in exploratory factor analysis models27,28—was conducted on the ADOS
   163→correlation matrices and identified the two-factor solution as most appropriate in 7 out of 8
   164→module/language combinations. The specific factor loading pattern for each module can be found
   165→in the supplementary material (Tables S12-S16).
   166→An IRT Graded Response Model (GRM) was fitted to the algorithm items of each
   167→module subset individually. The GRM estimates four item parameters: “a1” describes the
   168→discrimination (i.e., slope) parameter for the SC factor for a given item, “a2” describes
   169→discrimination for the RRBI factor, “b1” describes the difficulty (i.e., intercept) parameter for the
   170→first item response threshold (0 to 1), and “b2” describes the difficulty for the second threshold
   171→(1 to 2).
   172→Differential Item Functioning
   173→Sex DIF was evaluated using the IRT likelihood ratio test (LRT) approach. Two two-
   174→group models—constrained and unconstrained—were fit to all items, then compared. The
   175→constrained model set all item parameters (a1, a2, d1, d2) to be equal between the two groups
   176→(males and females), and the unconstrained model estimated all parameters freely in both groups.
   177→For each item, if the unconstrained model fit significantly better than the constrained model
   178→according to the LRT, the item was considered as functioning significantly differently between
   179→groups (i.e., males and females; evidence of DIF). Anchor items for each module subset were
   180→
   181→determined through an iterative purification procedure (see Supplementary Methods).
   182→Furthermore, given the risk of Type I error inflation due to multiple comparisons in the LRT
   183→approach, a Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate correction was applied to the p-values
   184→concerning DIF items, which maintained a familywise type I error rate of 0.05 in the subset of
   185→items post-anchor item selection (within each comparison subset). DIF effect sizes were
   186→computed including the Signed Item Difference in the Sample (SIDS), Unsigned Item Difference
   187→in the Sample (UIDS), and the Expected Score Standardized Difference (ESSD); see
   188→Supplementary Methods of explanation and interpretation of effect sizes. All analyses were
   189→implemented using R and mirt29.
   190→Results
   191→Sample Characteristics
   192→Participants included 14,809 males and 4,760 females, with ages ranging from 12 months
   193→old to 75 years old. Sample characteristics by ADOS module and language level are presented in
   194→Table 1.
   195→Differential Item Functioning
   196→Across all 8 module-language subsets, 20 of 112 items showed significant DIF before
   197→correction for multiple comparisons (Tables S17-S21), and 11 showed significant sex DIF after
   198→correction (Table 2; Figure 1, 2). The Toddler Module, Module 1 Low Language, and Module 2
   199→Low/High Language contained 0 DIF items after correction. Module 3 showed only 1 DIF item
   200→(TOPIC). Module 1 High Language showed 3 DIF items (POINT, SHOW, STEREO). Module 4
   201→contained 4 social communication DIF items and 3 RRBI DIF items between 1,752 males and
   202→731 females (EGEST, FACEE, CAFF, INSIG, USENS, OMAN, TOPIC). According to effect
   203→sizes, females tended to be underscored (i.e., negative SIDS/ESSD) on most social
   204→
   205→communication items, except for POINT in Module 1 High Language. However, females tended
   206→to be overscored (i.e., positive SIDS/ESSD) on most RRBI items, except for TOPIC in Module
   207→3. Notably, females were underscored for TOPIC in Module 3 yet overscored on TOPIC in
   208→Module 4. According to Cohen’s d guidelines for interpreting ESSD values, all observed DIF
   209→effect sizes were small except for the DIF effect of STEREO in Module 1 High Language, which
   210→was medium-sized.
   211→Discussion
   212→This study is the first to examine sex bias in autism assessment across all ages and ability
   213→levels in one of the most widely used autism diagnostic instruments, the ADOS. We pooled four
   214→national datasets to include over 19,000 individuals from ages 12 months to 75 years, with fluent
   215→language to no spoken words. Our findings suggest that while the ADOS is very robust to bias
   216→for individuals who are younger or have less language, significant sex bias exists for verbally
   217→fluent adults. The pattern differs between the social communication and RRBI traits and is
   218→consistent with reports of distinct female presentations of autism. These results raise questions
   219→about diagnostic equity across sexes and provide potential explanations for the female to male
   220→imbalance.
   221→Contrary to our hypothesis that all items with significant DIF would reflect underscoring
   222→of females (i.e., rated as more neurotypical), we observed this pattern primarily for social
   223→communication items in Module 4, which is administered to individuals over 15-years-old with
   224→fluent language. Among individuals with younger ages and/or less language, seven analyses (i.e.,
   225→Module 3, and High and Low Language levels for Modules 1, 2, and Toddler; 98 items analyzed)
   226→in the largest samples yet with adequate statistical power showed significant DIF for only 2
   227→social communication items. These two items (i.e., pointing and gesturing in Module 1 High
   228→
   229→Language) were not observed in prior studies. A broader, more coherent pattern of female
   230→underscoring emerged in four Module 4 items assessing facial expression, gestures,
   231→communication of affect, and social insight. This pattern suggests that identifying social
   232→communication difficulties in autistic females may become increasingly challenging as
   233→developmental and verbal abilities increase.
   234→One potential mechanism underlying the underscoring of females on social
   235→communication items is the greater tendency for autistic females to engage in camouflaging12,30-
   236→34. Due to gendered social expectations12,35,36, autistic females may develop compensatory
   237→strategies, including imitation or masking of autistic traits34, intentional cultivation of empathy
   238→and sociability skills36, and modification of verbal communicative behaviors37, to meet social
   239→expectations and avoid rejection. Camouflaging has also been shown to increase during
   240→adolescence and persist into adulthood38.
   241→Three recommendations emerge from these results. First, as the ADOS undergoes
   242→revisions, female-specific norms may be warranted to equate female and male autistic traits.
   243→Second, since the ADOS informs diagnostic decision-making in conjunction with other
   244→information, accuracy might be improved by assessing camouflaging after ADOS administration
   245→to reduce the likelihood of overlooking impairing social communication symptoms in females as
   246→verbal and social compensatory abilities develop. Finally, providers can reassure families of
   247→females who are younger or have less language that, despite popular opinion that autism
   248→assessment is biased against all females, their ADOS results are unlikely to show significant sex
   249→bias and are likely valid.
   250→For restricted, repetitive behaviors or interests, little evidence of sex bias was observed in
   251→earlier modules (Toddler, 1, and 2) across 98 items analyzed, excepting two items. The Module 1
   252→
   253→item on stereotyped language showed overscoring in females in this sample but was not
   254→previously reported in prior Module 1 investigations23,26 and requires replication. In contrast,
   255→females were significantly underscored in Module 3 for excessive interest in a
   256→topic/object/repetitive behavior, replicating findings from two prior studies16,39. This item has
   257→also shown better differentiation between autistic and non-autistic females than between
   258→males16,24. This robust finding is consistent with reports that female special interests tend to be
   259→more socially relevant or expressed in more socially appropriate ways10,40-42. Female-specific
   260→interpretation of this symptom may be warranted in both diagnosis and in ADOS scoring of this
   261→item (“D4”).
   262→In Module 4, three of five RRBI items showed DIF in this sample of 2,483 verbally fluent
   263→adults. No previous study of DIF has examined adults alone. Adult females were overscored (i.e.,
   264→rated as more autistic) on excessive interest in a topic compared to males with the same level of
   265→autistic latent trait, but the effect size was negligible. The effect sizes for overscoring females on
   266→hand mannerisms and sensory interests were small-to-moderate. There are a few plausible
   267→explanations for these results. First, it is possible that adult females receive higher scores because
   268→they exhibit more hand mannerisms and sensory interests than males do. This explanation seems
   269→plausible because these two items are based on the frequency of discrete occurrences and
   270→notably, among the least subjective scores. This hypothesis could be tested by coding instances
   271→of each behavior in ADOS videos of males and females.
   272→A second plausible explanation is that adult females may show equivalent frequencies of
   273→hand mannerisms and sensory interests to males, but they may be rated as showing more by
   274→clinicians due to bias. Potential mechanisms underlying this bias are speculated to be that
   275→clinicians may expect fewer mannerisms and sensory behaviors in females so the occurrences are
   276→
   277→particularly salient to clinicians, leading to overscoring; or, clinicians may be aware of research
   278→(e.g., 43) that mannerisms and sensory behaviors may present in more ‘socially acceptable’ ways
   279→in females, such as hand-wringing or hair-twirling, and score these gender-stereotype-congruent
   280→behaviors in females but not males. Both of these speculated mechanisms could be measured
   281→then evaluated as a potential mediator of the ADOS item DIF using established methods44.
   282→Limitations
   283→Several limitations of the current study should be considered. First, DIF analyses identify
   284→whether items function differently across groups but cannot determine why these differences
   285→occur. As such, the current findings cannot distinguish whether observed measurement
   286→differences reflect sex-related differences in autism presentation, biases in clinician recognition
   287→and interpretation, or both. Second, the ADOS is intended to serve as one component of a
   288→comprehensive autism evaluation that integrates multiformat interviews, a developmental
   289→history, and other standardized diagnostic assessments such as questionnaires45. In a minority of
   290→cases, the clinician’s final autism diagnostic decision contradicts the ADOS classification, as the
   291→ADOS— like all assessment tools—is not perfectly sensitive and specific18. Thus, sex disparities
   292→in autism prevalence are unlikely to be fully explained by ADOS DIF alone, nor does the
   293→absence of DIF in ADOS items guarantee equitable identification. Nevertheless, because the
   294→ADOS remains one of the most widely used autism assessment tools and is often required for
   295→access to autism-related services46, understanding and minimizing potential measurement
   296→differences remain clinically important. Third, this analysis examined ADOS algorithm items
   297→only, but non-algorithm ADOS items such as echolalia or functional play often contribute to
   298→diagnostic decision-making.
   299→
   300→Conclusions
   301→Comprehensive examination of sex-related measurement differences across all ADOS
   302→modules revealed minimal sex DIF in earlier modules, compared with a more prominent and
   303→coherent pattern among older, verbally fluent individuals in Module 4. Females tended to be
   304→underscored on social communication items and overscored on RRBI items. While camouflaging
   305→might explain the former pattern, the latter requires clarification of whether females are rated
   306→differently or truly exhibit more RRBIs during the ADOS. Future work should explore both how
   307→camouflaging autistic traits during the ADOS and how sex differences in autism presentation—
   308→particularly among older and verbally fluent individuals—might be incorporated into autism
   309→evaluation, such as by incorporating a measure of camouflaging or female-specific norms, to
   310→improve equitable diagnosis across sex and development.
