    21→Abstract
    22→Importance: Cannabis use (CU) in adolescence is linked to increased risk of developing
    23→psychotic-like experiences (PLEs) that can lead to lifelong impairment. A candidate mechanism
    24→may be disruptions in reward processing supported by dopamine but this association has not yet
    25→been tested in adolescents with CU and PLEs.
    26→Objective: Assess the interrelation between CU, PLEs, and neurobehavioral markers of reward
    27→processing, specifically behavioral reward motivation and tissue iron signal (1/nT2*; an indicator
    28→of dopamine-related neurophysiology).
    29→Design: Observational case-control cross-sectional study conducted from 2022-2025.
    30→Setting: Research site at an academic medical center.
    31→Participants: Adolescents (aged 14 - 17) within the greater Providence, RI area were recruited
    32→via electronic medical record review, social media, schools, and contact with the community.
    33→Exclusion criteria included current impairing symptoms of psychotic disorder or lifetime history
    34→of any substance other than nicotine or cannabis. Participants were characterized as either
    35→adolescents who use cannabis (>11 lifetime cannabis episodes) or controls (<5 lifetime cannabis
    36→episodes).
    37→Exposure(s) (for observational studies): CU quantity was assessed with the Daily Sessions,
    38→Frequency, Age of Onset, and Quantity of Cannabis Use Inventory. PLEs were assessed with the
    39→Achenbach Youth Self-Report Thought Problems Scale.
    40→Main Outcome(s) and Measure(s): Reward motivation (proportion of hard choices) was
    41→assessed using a behavioral task. 1/nT2* was derived from functional magnetic resonance
    42→imaging. Hypotheses, formulated during data collection as recognition grew of the risk factors of
    43→developing PLEs with CU, were: PLEs will magnify negative associations between CU and
    44→reward motivation; the positive association between greater CU and PLEs will depend on
    45→subcortical 1/nT2*.
    46→Results: 81 participants included 47 control and 34 cannabis group participants: mean [SD] age,
    47→15.53 [1.12]; 52 female (64%). There was a significant interaction between CU and PLEs on
    48→reward motivation (OR, 2.41; 95% CI, 1.10, 5.27, p=0.028), depending on reward amount and
    49→effort required. At high PLEs, the cannabis group, compared to controls, were less motivated to
    50→work for a reward when effort required was low and medium. Associations between CU quantity
    51→and PLEs depended on 1/nT2* (at high 1/nT2*, CU positively predicted PLEs). Daily
    52→concentrate hits interacted with 1/nT2* in the left putamen (β=51.58, 95% CI, 12.07, 91.09,
    53→p=0.01) and right thalamus (β=70.93, 95% CI, 12.32, 129.54, p =0.02), daily cannabis grams in
    54→the left putamen (β=73.34, 95% CI, 12.32, 129.54, p=0.02), right putamen (β=101.69, 95% CI,
    55→41.72, 161.67, p=0.001), and right thalamus (β=102.38, 95% CI, 28.00, 176.77, p=0.01).
    56→
    57→Conclusions and Relevance: Adolescent CU was associated with alterations in dopamine-
    58→relevant neural and behavioral indices as a function of PLEs. Behaviorally, this manifested as
    59→less reward motivation and strategic action. Consistent with prior research on psychosis-
    60→spectrum vulnerability and dopamine-related neurophysiology, elevated 1/nT2* was observed to
    61→moderate the link between CU and PLEs in key subcortical brain regions. Longitudinal studies
    62→are needed to understand the timing and mechanism of neurobehavioral reward processing
    63→alterations.
    64→
    65→In 2025, 17% of United States 12th graders reported past month cannabis use (CU) and
    66→over one third reported lifetime CU1. Adolescence is a critical period of brain development2–7
    67→characterized by increased vulnerability to external influences like drugs. Relatedly, adolescent
    68→CU is associated with greater risk for adverse cognitive8,9, behavioral8, and psychological10–13
    69→outcomes, including psychotic-like experiences14 (PLEs, subclinical psychosis spectrum
    70→experiences15).
    71→Associations between CU and PLEs14,16–20 are elevated with high potency products21,22
    72→which are increasingly preferred by adolescents23–25. Although PLEs are at the mildest end of the
    73→psychosis spectrum and often benign or transient, they are distressing and impairing for many
    74→adolescents. PLEs are also important transdiagnostic indicators of psychiatric vulnerability26,27,
    75→such that they predict later psychosis symptoms and other psychiatric concerns, particularly
    76→among youth with more, distressing, and persistent PLEs28,29, and among those with co-occurring
    77→CU13,30,31.
    78→The co-occurrence of CU and PLEs or psychotic symptoms may be driven by alterations
    79→in the dopaminergic system, which is implicated in reward processing32, goal-directed action33,
    80→and drug use34. Altered dopamine activity in the caudate, putamen, thalamus, and substantia
    81→nigra/ventral tegmental area (SN/VTA) in adults35–43, along with disrupted reward-based
    82→responsiveness and decision-making in adolescents and adults44–50, are associated with psychotic
    83→disorders, prodromal psychosis symptoms, psychosis risk and PLEs. In these conditions,
    84→abnormally elevated dopamine may lead to the misattribution of significance to irrelevant stimuli
    85→(aberrant salience51,52,53) manifesting in delusions, hallucinations, and altered reward
    86→responsiveness. Chronic CU also influences the dopaminergic system54,55: in adults, chronic CU
    87→is associated with both reduced striatal dopamine synthesis activity56,57 and release58–60.
    88→
    89→Adolescent research has not directly tested dopaminergic processes but behavioral indices of
    90→dopaminergic processes indicates that adolescents with regular CU exhibit alterations in reward
    91→sensitivity61,62. Neurobehavioral indicators of reward motivation63–66,32,67,68 (i.e., how much effort
    92→one is willing to exert for rewards69–71), anticipation, and learning are attenuated in adolescents
    93→who use cannabis compared to controls72–74. Notably, chronic CU in individuals prone to
    94→psychosis may be characterized by patterns of elevated, rather than attenuated, dopamine-related
    95→neurophysiology. For example, adults with CU disorder (CUD) had elevated levels of
    96→neuromelanin (characteristic of hyperdopaminergic disorders like schizophrenia) in regions
    97→implicated in psychosis (i.e., SN/VTA) compared to adults without CUD37.
    98→Whether similar interactions are evident earlier in development (i.e., adolescence) and
    99→during early precursors of psychotic disorders (which may manifest as PLEs) is unknown, but
   100→understanding these interactions is crucial given the ongoing maturation of dopaminergic
   101→processes during adolescence54,75–77. Existing methods for measuring dopaminergic processes
   102→directly (i.e., via PET) is typically restricted among adolescent populations. Moreover,
   103→dopamine-related neurophysiology is complex and can be assessed through several direct (i.e.,
   104→storage, synthesis) and indirect approaches (i.e., neuromelanin and tissue iron). Tissue iron is a
   105→necessary component of catecholamine synthesis (including dopamine)78 which can be measured
   106→non-invasively via MRI. Elevated tissue iron is associated with psychiatric and cognitive factors
   107→thought to be influenced by hyperdopaminergia, including cocaine use disorder79–82 and
   108→schizophrenia83,84. Another noninvasive approach is to assess behavior supported by
   109→dopaminergic processes via tasks balancing reward and effort32,85–88. Using these noninvasive
   110→approaches can help clarify how CU and PLEs together are associated with neurobehavioral
   111→indicators supporting dopaminergic processes in adolescence.
   112→
   113→The objective of this cross-sectional study is to evaluate the interrelation between
   114→adolescent CU, PLEs, and neurobehavioral processes related to dopamine, which were
   115→characterized by two indicators: performance on a reward motivation task, and subcortical tissue
   116→iron. First, we hypothesize that performance on a reward motivation task will depend on group
   117→(cannabis versus control), PLEs, and other task factors, such that the combination of CU and
   118→PLEs will lead to amplified negative associations between CU and reward motivation. Second,
   119→we hypothesize that the link between CU quantity and PLEs will depend on brain tissue iron
   120→(measured as a proxy, 1/nT2*), such that higher 1/nT2* will be moderate the positive
   121→associations between CU and PLEs .

   123→Participants
   124→Participants were recruited via free and paid community advertising (schools, flyers,
   125→social media) and electronic medical record review at the affiliated hospital. Eligibility criteria,
   126→chosen to maximize group differences and limit the influence of other neural confounds,
   127→included: aged 14-17; endorsed < 5 (control group) or > 11 (cannabis group) lifetime cannabis
   128→episodes and would abstain from cannabis ≥ 15 hours without withdrawal symptoms; no MRI
   129→contraindications (head injury with loss of consciousness > ten minutes, migraines, or seizures
   130→per caregiver report); no current impairing psychotic disorder symptoms (i.e., were not
   131→experiencing active hallucinations or delusions and were not receiving inpatient or intensive
   132→services to treat psychosis symptoms). Exclusion criteria included: within the past three months,
   133→scoring “high dependence” on the Electronic Cigarette Dependence Index89 or using non-vape
   134→nicotine products at least three times per week, or endorsing weekly alcohol use with average > 4
   135→drinks per episode or multiple > 4 drink episodes90; lifetime use of any other substance. The

   193→Measures
   194→Cannabis Use Quantity
   195→At the initial visit, Daily Sessions, Frequency, Age of Onset, and Quantity of Cannabis
   196→Use Inventory (DFAQ-CU)91 assessed patterns of past 3-month CU quantity by administration
   197→method (concentrate hits versus flower grams). Responses were log-transformed for skewness.
   198→Supplemental analyses examined CU frequency and CUD severity; see Supplemental Methods
   199→for details.
   200→Reward Motivation
   201→The modified EEFRT task, completed at the initial visit, requires adolescents to choose to
   202→pump up one of two balloons: easy or hard, across 72 trials. For every trial, the easy option had a
   203→fixed reward amount of 1 point and required of 20 key presses (“effort”). The hard option varied
   204→by trial but always had a higher reward amount (3, 5, or 7 points) and higher effort required (100,
   205→120, or 150 presses) compared to the easy option. Before deciding between the two options,
   206→adolescents were shown the reward amount offered (points) and effort required (number of key
   207→presses) for the easy vs. hard option, and the probability of actually earning points after trial
   208→completion (“reward probability”; 50% or 100% depending on the trial). Participants were told
   209→they would be paid a percentage of their win trials (for fairness all were paid $5 at study end).
   210→Choice on each trial was coded as 0 or 1, representing the easy or hard option, respectively.
   211→
   212→Because reward motivation is influenced by trial-level factors of reward amount offered, effort
   213→required for the hard option, and reward probability, reward motivation was the dependent
   214→variable—operationalized as proportion of trials that participants chose to complete the hard
   215→option—and the trial-level factors were predictors. Trials were excluded if reaction time (RT)
   216→occurred less than .2 seconds after choice presentation or greater than 3 standard deviations
   217→(SDs) above the average RT (4.56 seconds). Participants whose median RT was less than .3
   218→seconds were removed entirely (n=1).
   219→Psychotic-Like Experiences (PLEs)
   220→PLEs were measured by the Achenbach Youth Self-Report (YSR) Thought Problems
   221→Scale92 at the initial visit. This scale assesses behavioral and emotional symptoms including
   222→visual and auditory hallucinations and strange ideas akin to PLEs (e.g., “I hear sounds or voices
   223→that other people think aren’t there”)92. Participants reported their experiences on a Likert scale
   224→as: “not true” (0); “sometimes true” (1); “very/often true” (2). T-scores between 65-69 are
   225→considered borderline and above 70 are considered clinically significant. This scale accurately
   226→predicts psychosis-spectrum conditions (i.e., clinical high risk for psychosis or full psychosis
   227→threshold) 93,94. T-scores were z-scored for reward motivation analyses.
   228→MRI
   229→Acquisition. A Siemens 3T PRISMA scanner with a 64-channel head coil was used to
   230→acquire data. Structural MPRAGE T1 scans had the following parameters: TR/TE/TI = 1900
   231→ms/3.02 ms/ 900 ms, flip angle = 9°, FOV = 256 mm, 160 slices, Voxel size = 1mm3.Resting
   232→state T2*-weighted, single-shot, gradient-echo, echo-planar imaging (EPI) sequences were
   233→collected over nine minutes with participants’ eyes open. For pilot participants, the following
   234→parameters were used: TR = 2000 ms, TE = 30 ms; flip angle = 90°; FOV = 192 mm; 60 slices;
   235→
   236→256 measurements; voxel size 2mm3; multiband acceleration factor 3, GRAPPA in-plane
   237→acceleration factor 2. For all other participants, parameters were TR = 1600 ms, TE=30 ms; flip
   238→angle = 62°; FOV = 200 mm; 72 slices; 333 measurements; voxel size 2mm3; multiband
   239→acceleration factor 3, GRAPPA in-plane acceleration factor 2. Siemens AutoAlign tilted the slice
   240→prescription 30 degrees relative to the AP-PC line to improve orbital signal (applied in all but the
   241→five initial pilot subjects)95. Protocols differed due to sequence optimization post-piloting (e.g.,
   242→tilt). Scans were completed on a separate day from the reward motivation task.
   243→EPI preprocessing. The CONN Toolbox (version 22a) running with MATLAB (version
   244→R2023a; Natick, MA, The MathWorks, Inc.) and SPM 12 were used to preprocess functional
   245→resting state and anatomical data according to a standard pipeline (without smoothing; described
   246→in supplement). Participant data was retained if at least 170 volumes96 remained after 0.5mm
   247→movement volumes were excluded83; as a result, two subjects were excluded.
   248→Tissue Iron. Tissue iron is an indirect measure of dopamine-relevant neurophysiology, as
   249→well as other processes75,83,97,98. Tissue iron co-localizes with dopamine vesicles, is essential for
   250→dopamine synthesis, and is positively associated with presynaptic vesicular storage of
   251→dopamine75,99,100. It is also associated with dopamine-related psychiatric and cognitive factors 101
   252→96,102 83,84 79–82. Additionally, the developmental trajectory of tissue iron distribution across the
   253→brain aligns with the dopaminergic system adolescence through adulthood75,76,101,103–106. In this
   254→study, whole-brain tissue iron estimates were derived from preprocessed resting state single-echo
   255→EPI scans using AFNI. We calculated relative T2* decay per voxel compared to the whole brain
   256→average, which generated a whole-brain map of 1/nT2* signal83. This process involved: 1)
   257→normalizing of voxels at each volume (timepoint) to the mean of that volume within MNI-
   258→template brain mask; 2) computing median of voxel intensity across time; 3) calculating this
   259→
   260→value’s inverse. The output is a single-volume map where the value of each voxel represents the
   261→inverse of the normalized T2* measurement (1/nT2*). Because the T2*-weighted signal is
   262→inversely associated with tissue iron concentration98, lower 1/nT2* values are consistent with
   263→lower tissue iron. Regions of interest (ROIs) were selected based on potential roles in reward and
   264→psychosis35–37,39–42,75,76,82,83,98,103,107–109 and included the substantia nigra/VTA, left and right
   265→putamen, caudate, and thalamus.
   266→Statistical Analyses
   267→Analyses were conducted in R version 4.5.0 (see supplement for R packages).
   268→Neurobehavioral dopaminergic processes were modeled two ways: reward motivation was
   269→modeled as its behavioral phenotype that is predicted by CU and PLEs, whereas subcortical
   270→tissue iron was modeled as an individual-difference factor that may moderate the link between
   271→CU and PLEs. To examine the associations between CU, PLEs, and reward motivation (i.e.,
   272→proportion of choosing the hard option accounting for associated reward amount, effort required,
   273→and reward probability), we implemented a generalized estimating equations (GEE) model,
   274→which weights combinations of observations to evaluate correlated data and produce accurate
   275→standard errors110. A binomial distribution with a logit link function with an unstructured
   276→correlation matrix was used, given the binary nature of the response choice variable. Response
   277→choice (i.e., choosing the hard versus easy option) was treated as a nested-repeated measures
   278→dependent variable. Trial was included as a covariate to account for the learning rate. Reward
   279→amount (3, 5, or 7 points offered for the hard option), effort required (keyboard button presses
   280→required for the hard option: 100, 120, or 150), and reward probability (50% versus 100%) were
   281→trial-level independent variable. Group (control versus cannabis) was a between-subjects
   282→independent variable; PLE score was a continuous independent variable. Sex and frequency of
   283→
   284→nicotine use were tested as covariates but were not included in the final model because neither
   285→was significant.
   286→A five-way interaction between reward amount offered (for the hard option), effort
   287→required (for the hard option), reward probability, PLEs, and group was tested to account for task
   288→factors contributing to each choice. The five-way interaction was not significant but a four-way
   289→interaction without reward probability was; for parsimony, we focused on the four-way
   290→interaction as our main model with reward probability as an additional covariate. Significant
   291→interactions were probed by examining the simple slope of reward amount at levels of effort
   292→required (100, 120, and 150 keyboard presses, referred to as “low”, “medium”, and “high”) and
   293→PLEs (-1 SD, average, and +1 SD, referred to as “low”, “average”, and “high”), and cannabis
   294→versus control group.
   295→To examine whether the link between CU quantity (daily concentrate hits and daily
   296→cannabis grams) and PLEs depends on 1/nT2* in each ROI, we conducted separate multivariate
   297→linear regressions for each ROI, controlling age, sex, nicotine use, and alcohol use. Significant
   298→interactions were followed up with simple slopes analyses at -1 SD, average, and +1 SD levels of
   299→1/nT2*. Models examining CU frequency (CU sessions ratio) and problems (CUD severity) are
   300→in the supplement (Table S4).

   424→Discussion
   425→To our knowledge, this is the first study to examine the relationship between adolescent
   426→CU, PLEs, and dopamine-relevant neurobehavioral indices. We examined how CU and PLEs
   427→influence behavioral reward motivation (a construct supported by dopaminergic processes32, and
   428→whether associations between CU and PLEs depended on subcortical tissue iron (an indicator of
   429→dopamine-related neurophysiology75,78,83,97,99. Consistent with our hypotheses, we found that
   430→adolescent CU is associated with less reward motivation in the context of above average PLEs,
   431→and that 1/nT2* signal in brain regions previously implicated in psychosis spectrum disorders
   432→magnified associations between CU and PLEs.
   433→Our results advance progress towards understanding the mechanism by which CU and
   434→psychosis risk-associated phenotypes might be related, across two methods relevant to
   435→dopaminergic processes. Effortful behavior—supported by dopamine—changes when striatal
   436→dopaminergic processes are manipulated (e.g., drug administration, neuromodulation32,111), such
   437→that modifications to increase dopamine have resulted in greater reward sensitivity and effortful
   438→behavior to achieve a goal, whereas dopamine depletion led to less effort32,111. However,
   439→individuals with PLEs and psychotic disorders have shown less sensitivity to rewards (e.g.,
   440→exhibiting less effort than controls with high or guaranteed reward offered)130,131,132 and
   441→adolescents with CU have exhibited less reward motivation compared to controls (Lane et al.69;
   442→but see Lawn et al.70). In the present study, adolescents in the cannabis group, compared to
   443→controls, were less motivated by reward at high PLEs (+1 SD above average; T-score ≥ 68) and
   444→low and medium effort. These results suggest this group is exhibiting blunted reward motivation
   445→and sensitivity, highlighting the magnified deficits in reward motivation when CU and PLEs co-
   446→occur.
   447→
   448→Connecting established models of dopaminergic processes in psychosis to motivational
   449→deficits can be challenging because these deficits contradict expectations that elevated dopamine
   450→promotes effortful behavior32,33,115. This apparent paradox is addressed by several theories
   451→explaining dopaminergic dysregulation. The first theory posits that psychotic disorders like
   452→schizophrenia are characterized by decreased phasic dopamine in reference to salient stimuli
   453→(e.g., cues for a high reward) and increased tonic striatal dopamine transmission116 and
   454→spontaneous phasic dopamine, the latter of which may explain why irrelevant stimuli are deemed
   455→salient117. Second, low motivation in psychotic disorders is theorized to originate from a lifetime
   456→excess of post-synaptic striatal D2 receptors starting early in development112,118. Further, given
   457→that CU has not been associated with an excess of striatal D2 receptors58,119 it is unlikely to be
   458→the primary explanation for the growing incidence of CU and psychotic disorders30,31. Within this
   459→framework, if PLEs are also characterized by similar dopaminergic dysregulation, less reward
   460→motivation—even when effort required is low—could reflect impaired cues for salient (goal-
   461→related) stimuli rather than low tonic dopamine. Our findings that positive associations between
   462→CU and PLEs depended on higher 1/nT2* signal (in the thalamus and putamen, regions where
   463→dopaminergic functioning is associated with psychotic disorders35–39,41) further support the
   464→hypothesis that PLEs and CU together contribute to dysregulation of dopamine-relevant indices.
   465→In this context, CU and PLEs may jointly exacerbate attenuated reward sensitivity.
   466→Our findings among adolescents align with a recent study that showed CUD severity in
   467→adults is associated with elevated neuromelanin signal—a breakdown product of dopamine that
   468→binds to iron—in psychosis-related ROIs. Our results diverge with that study’s lack of evidence
   469→for an interaction between CU (including CUD severity, see Supplemental Table S4),
   470→neuromelanin, and psychosis spectrum experiences37, potentially due to methodological and
   471→
   472→developmental differences. In contrast, blunted dopamine-related neurophysiology has been
   473→associated with chronic CU in adults and animals56,59,66,119–122. Therefore, our finding that
   474→elevated 1/nT2* moderates associations between adolescent CU and PLEs may suggest
   475→dopaminergic processes implicated in psychosis-spectrum vulnerability also contribute to
   476→cannabis-related vulnerability to PLEs.
   477→Taken together, our study provides the first cross-sectional preliminary evaluation of a
   478→potential mechanism by which CU and psychosis-related symptoms co-occur using a multi-
   479→method investigation of neurobehavioral indices known to be linked to dopaminergic processes.
   480→Our methodology, like that of other studies37,83,84 is indirect. Differences in findings (i.e., Ahrens
   481→et al.37) then may be due to different methods of capturing processes supported by dopamine,
   482→developmental periods, and stages of disease. Of note, a recent study found an inverse
   483→association between tissue iron in the substantia nigra-VTA and elevated dopamine synthesis
   484→capacity in adults with schizophrenia123,124, countering previous findings that 1/nT2* is positively
   485→associated with of presynaptic vesicular storage of dopamine75,99,100. However, the applicability
   486→of these findings to our study population (i.e., adolescents with varying levels of PLEs and CU)
   487→is less clear, as dopamine system dysfunction in psychosis-spectrum experiences is highly
   488→heterogenous depending on ROI and is significantly influenced by age, sex, and other factors125.
   489→It is crucial to identify potential pathways by which adolescent CU and psychotic symptoms are
   490→related given the increasing incidence with higher potency products21,22,126, and the substantial
   491→reduction in quality of life incurred.
   492→Strengths and Limitations
   493→Strengths of the present study include measuring an indicator of tissue iron (1/nT2*),
   494→previously established as related to dopamine neurophysiology75,83,99, during its development in
   495→
   496→adolescence54,75–77. Neural confounds of other drugs did not influence our results—adolescents
   497→had no history of other illicit substance use. Additionally, our results reflected conditions of
   498→typical substance use, since CU frequently co-occurs with nicotine use127 and we included
   499→participants who used nicotine while accounting for nicotine use as a covariate.
   500→The present study had several limitations. As a cross-sectional study, the directionality of
   501→associations remain unclear. Because mesolimbic dopaminergic processes mature during
   502→adolescence54,75–77, we cannot determine whether elevated dopamine contributes to the
   503→development of CU and PLEs or if shared vulnerability underlies all three128. Longitudinal
   504→investigations are needed to determine the temporal role of 1/nT2* in the relationship between
   505→CU and PLEs. Although excluding adolescents with other illicit substance use prevented those
   506→neural confounds, this choice may have truncated the upper range of CU frequency and
   507→problems127. Similar to other studies83,99, our measure of tissue iron (1/nT2*) was indirect
   508→because it was not acquired using multi-echo MRI; replication is needed in follow-up studies
   509→using multi-echo methods like quantitative susceptibility mapping129. We did not measure
   510→positive and negative symptoms separately; future studies should characterize these symptoms to
   511→evaluate sources of functional impairment. Lastly, although the YSR Thought Problems scale is a
   512→screening tool that accurately predicts clinical high risk for psychosis and meeting psychosis
   513→threshold93,94, our study did not include a diagnostic measure of psychosis spectrum
   514→conditions130.
   515→Conclusions
   516→This cross-sectional study provides the first preliminary evidence that associations
   517→between adolescent CU and dopamine-relevant neural and behavioral indices vary as a function
   518→of PLEs. Behaviorally, this manifested as less reward motivation and strategic goal-directed
   519→
   520→action. Consistent with psychosis research on dopamine-relevant neurophysiology, greater
   521→1/nT2* was observed with higher CU and PLEs in key subcortical brain regions, including the
   522→associative striatum and thalamus. Crucial next steps include understanding the temporal
   523→ordering of these factors—whether dopamine-relevant neurophysiology was altered prior to or
   524→after CU onset—and clarifying the extent to which alterations in neurobehavioral dopamine-
   525→related processes alterations reflect broader shifts in the developmental trajectory of reward
   526→processing in adolescence, due to CU.

   956→Supplemental Methods
   957→Cannabis Use Frequency
   958→Adolescents completed the Timeline Follow-Back Interview (TLFB)1 to assess number of
   959→daily cannabis sessions, formulation used (flower, concentrate, edible), and hours high.
   960→Participants also reported on daily nicotine use (yes/no), and daily number of standard alcohol
   961→drinks. At the initial visit, the TLFB established substance use patterns 90-days prior; at the MRI
   962→visit, the TLFB characterized substance use during the time between the initial study visit and the
   963→MRI visit. This resulted in the full spectrum of substance use from 90 days prior to enrollment,
   964→through the day of the MRI. Proportion of use days and rates of use (i.e., substance use value
   965→divided by total number of measured days) were calculated for relevant TLFB variables.
   966→Cannabis sessions ratio represented CU frequency in analyses. Responses were log-transformed
   967→for skewness.
   968→Cannabis Use Disorder Criteria
   969→The Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia (KSADS)2 was used to
   970→assess past 12 month cannabis use disorder (CUD) at the initial visit. CUD severity was
   971→classified as: 0 (not present, ≤ 1 criteria met); 1 (mild, 2-3 criteria met); 2 (moderate, 4-5 criteria
   972→met); 3 (severe, 6 or more criteria met). KSADS interviews were conducted by a licensed clinical
   973→psychologist.
   974→MRI
   975→EPI preprocessing
   976→Preprocessing included: realignment with correction of susceptibility distortion
   977→interactions, slice timing correction, outlier detection, segmentation into gray matter, white
   978→matter, and CSF (CONN Toolbox “direct segmentation” option), and MNI-space normalization.
   979→
   980→The SPM realign & unwarp procedure3 was used to realign functional data. All volumes
   981→were co-registered to the first scan of the first session (used as reference image) via least squares
   982→approach and 6-parameter (rigid body) transformation 4. Data were resampled to correct for
   983→motion and magnetic susceptibility interactions, followed by SPM slice-timing correction5. ART
   984→6 was used to identify outlier scans (framewise displace threshold = 0.5mm)7,8. A mean reference
   985→BOLD image was computed using all non-outlier volumes. Anatomical and functional images
   986→were normalized to MNI-152 space. Images were segmented into: gray matter, white matter, and
   987→CSF tissues and then resampled to 2mm isotropic voxels via SPM’s unified segmentation and
   988→normalization algorithm9–11. Denoising was implemented by regressing confounding signals (5
   989→CompCor components each from white matter and CSF), motion parameters and their
   990→derivatives (12 total), outliers, session effects and their derivatives, and linear trends (2 each).
   991→Data were bandpass filtered (0.008–0.09 Hz) and despiked post-regression12.
   992→1/nT2* Supplemental Regions of Interest
   993→Supplemental exploratory analyses examined the left and right pallidum and nucleus
   994→accumbens. Per-participant 1/nT2* levels were mask-derived13: a publicly-available mask was
   995→used for the SN/VTA14 and for all other regions, estimates were made using the Harvard-Oxford
   996→atlas in FSL (version 6.0.7.7)15. Mean 1/nT2* for each ROI was calculated with AFNI’s
   997→3dmaskave.
   998→Statistical Analyses
   999→Model Fit Testing
  1000→Model fit was tested using the Quasi-likelihood Information Criterion (QIC) for
  1001→evaluating generalized estimating equations (GEEs), where lower values indicate better fit16. A
  1002→
  1003→model assuming no correlation between trials within subjects was selected based on having the
  1004→lowest QIC, which is likely explained by including trial as a covariate in the models.
  1005→Sensitivity Analyses
  1006→We conducted additional sensitivity analyses for models examining whether associations
  1007→between CU and PLEs depended on 1/nT2*. Specifically, we controlled for the influence of self-
  1008→reported psychotropic medications reported on the day of MRI. Categories of reported
  1009→medication included sleep medication, stimulant, non-stimulant, and antipsychotic. All
  1010→significant interactions remained after adjusting for medication.
