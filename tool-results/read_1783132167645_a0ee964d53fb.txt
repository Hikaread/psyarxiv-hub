    12→    12→Abstract
    13→    13→Objective
    14→    14→Self-control is an important predictor of many life outcomes across multiple domains.
    15→    15→Previous research has suggested that it is not only self-control ability, but also what
    16→    16→individuals believe about self-control that impacts outcomes. However, so far, research has
    17→    17→mapped a limited number of self-control beliefs. The present study investigates a broader set
    18→    18→of self-control beliefs in predicting various life outcomes.
    19→    19→Method
    20→    20→We used a previously derived set of self-control beliefs and identified four higher-order core
    21→    21→beliefs, namely beliefs about how important, effortless, proactive, and learnable self-control
    22→    22→is. Next, we tested the predictive value of these four higher-order beliefs on a variety of life
    23→    23→domains, including health, work or school performance, relationships, finance, and well-
    24→    24→being (N = 485).
    25→    25→Results
    26→    26→Self-control beliefs significantly contributed to the prediction of outcomes, although the
    27→    27→predictive power did not go beyond trait self-control and self-control metacognition. Notably,
    28→    28→the various self-control beliefs showed distinct associations with different life domains,
    29→    29→potentially reflecting their context-dependent nature.
    30→    30→Conclusions
    31→    31→We conclude that self-control beliefs are an overlooked factor for understanding and
    32→    32→improving self-control.
    33→    33→Keywords: Self-Control; Lay Beliefs; Life Outcomes; Network Analysis

    35→    35→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 3
    36→    36→Introduction
    37→    37→People constantly encounter self-control challenges, from maintaining a daily workout
    38→    38→or resisting tempting foods to managing personal finances or stopping scrolling to go to bed
    39→    39→on time. Some individuals exhibit more successful self-control in dealing with these
    40→    40→challenges than others. This difference is not only reflected in trait self-control (De Ridder et
    41→    41→al., 2012) but may also be influenced by individuals' beliefs about self-control. For example,
    42→    42→while many people believe self-control depends on willpower, others may consider it a
    43→    43→product of specific strategies, habits, or moral principles (e.g., Gennara et al., 2023; Job et al.,
    44→    44→2010). Indeed, people hold different beliefs about what self-control is and what determines
    45→    45→how good they are at exerting it (Gennara et al., 2023; Job et al., 2010; Li et al., 2025;
    46→    46→Vaughn & Burkins, 2023). An important question, therefore, is: do these beliefs influence
    47→    47→actual self-control outcomes? This research investigates how lay beliefs about self-control
    48→    48→shape self-control outcomes across different domains, compared to trait self-control and
    49→    49→associated concepts such as self-control metacognition.
    50→    50→Self-control is essential for achieving long-term goals, maintaining healthy habits, and
    51→    51→navigating daily responsibilities. It enables people to resist immediate temptations in favor of
    52→    52→more meaningful long-term outcomes, regardless of whether that means sticking to a diet,
    53→    53→staying focused at work, or managing personal finances effectively. Self-control refers to the
    54→    54→ability to override one’s inner responses, as well as to interrupt undesired behavioral
    55→    55→tendencies (such as impulses) and refrain from acting on them (Hofmann et al., 2014;
    56→    56→Tangney et al., 2004). Previous research has shown that higher trait self-control is
    57→    57→consistently linked to diverse domains, including better academic performance, healthier
    58→    58→lifestyles, better relationships, and greater well-being (De Ridder et al., 2012). However,
    59→    59→emerging research suggests that beyond this stable trait, people’s beliefs about self-control
    60→    60→may also play a role in shaping their self-regulatory success.
    61→    61→
    62→    62→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 4
    63→    63→Beliefs – people’s ideas and thoughts about the nature of self-control and how it
    64→    64→operates – have been shown to be key determinants for behavior in many contexts. For
    65→    65→instance, beliefs about one’s self-control capacity can influence the choice for healthy
    66→    66→behaviors (Hagger, 2025), and whether effort is believed to be enjoyable can affect the level
    67→    67→of effort individuals are willing to devote to their tasks (Mlynski et al., 2025). Beliefs about
    68→    68→self-control may influence how individuals approach self-control tasks, such as the valence
    69→    69→they associate with the task (e.g., whether positive or negative) and the emotions they
    70→    70→experience (e.g., whether pleasant or unpleasant). Additionally, self-control beliefs can
    71→    71→influence the way individuals respond to self-control challenges, such as in determining
    72→    72→which strategies to use and whether to rely on willpower of the individuals. Prior research has
    73→    73→largely focused on implicit beliefs about self-control, particularly whether people consider
    74→    74→self-control as a limited resource (versus non-limited) (Job et al., 2010) or a fixed trait (versus
    75→    75→a malleable skill) (Mukhopadhyay & Johar, 2005). These self-control beliefs have been
    76→    76→shown to be associated with greater persistence on challenging tasks, healthier behaviors,
    77→    77→higher academic achievement, lower procrastination, stronger relationships, and more pro-
    78→    78→environmental behaviors (Bernecker & Job, 2020; Di Maio et al., 2020; Francis et al., 2023;
    79→    79→Hagger et al., 2019; Jankowski & Job, 2023; Job et al., 2010, 2015; Mukhopadhyay & Johar,
    80→    80→2005).
    81→    81→Beyond Implicit Theories: A Broader Set of Self-Control Beliefs
    82→    82→Recent work has identified a broader set of lay beliefs about self-control, expanding
    83→    83→beyond the implicit limited or malleable dimensions. These include beliefs about the
    84→    84→strategic, moral, pleasant, and success-related nature of self-control (Bermúdez et al., 2023a;
    85→    85→Gennara et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025; Murray et al., 2023; Vaughn & Burkins, 2023). For
    86→    86→example, some individuals may believe self-control relies on willpower, while others
    87→    87→emphasize its strategic nature (e.g., by avoiding temptations and setting specific action plans).
    88→    88→
    89→    89→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 5
    90→    90→Again, others may view self-control as inherently effortful and requiring persistence, or
    91→    91→regard it as a source of pleasure or a reflection of morality. An experimental study comparing
    92→    92→willpower beliefs and strategy beliefs indicated that most people tend to associate self-control
    93→    93→primarily with willpower rather than strategy (Gennara et al., 2023). When describing
    94→    94→individuals with high trait self-control, people perceived them as more likely to exert
    95→    95→willpower (e.g., resisting temptation by sheer willpower) rather than as using self-control
    96→    96→strategies (e.g., bypassing a tempting shop). Moreover, these beliefs were shown to influence
    97→    97→people’s intentions to apply willpower or self-control strategies in resolving self-control
    98→    98→challenges. Participants who were prompted to endorse the strategic nature of self-control
    99→    99→intended to use strategies more than those whose beliefs about self-control as willpower were
   100→   100→highlighted (Gennara et al., 2023).
   101→   101→Another study examined people’s self-control beliefs through linguistic analysis of
   102→   102→descriptions of personal self-control experiences. People often described self-control in
   103→   103→relation to power, risk, and achievement, but far less frequently in terms of positive emotions
   104→   104→and the experience of pleasure (Vaughn & Burkins, 2023). Recently, a comprehensive
   105→   105→framework of self-control beliefs has been proposed, identifying 14 distinct components. This
   106→   106→study directly asked participants about their views on self-control and found that people
   107→   107→commonly perceive self-control as difficult and important, often requires commitment and
   108→   108→persistence to achieve, but also seems unpleasant for many (Li et al., 2025), thus confirming
   109→   109→the results of the linguistic study by Vaughn and Burkins (2023). These beliefs cover different
   110→   110→facets of people’s thoughts about self-control and may significantly impact how individuals
   111→   111→exert self-control, yet apart from studies on the belief that self-control is a limited resource,
   112→   112→no studies have examined the impact of the broader set of beliefs on life outcomes that may
   113→   113→benefit from self-control.
   114→   114→
   115→   115→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 6
   116→   116→In the present study, we will address this gap. Importantly, we will examine to what
   117→   117→extent self-control beliefs add explanatory power to the influence of self-control capacity
   118→   118→(i.e., trait self-control) and metacognition in predicting self-control outcomes. Trait self-
   119→   119→control and self-control metacognition – how individuals monitor and regulate their own self-
   120→   120→control processes and strategies (Hennecke & Bürgler, 2023) – are known to influence
   121→   121→outcomes in various domains (Bürgler & Hennecke, 2024; De Ridder et al., 2012; Hofmann
   122→   122→et al., 2014). To assess the impact of self-control beliefs, it is important to investigate the
   123→   123→predictive power of beliefs in relation to trait self-control and self-control metacognition as
   124→   124→related but distinct concepts. Previous studies attest to the notion that self-control beliefs can
   125→   125→be distinguished from trait self-control. For instance, a weak correlation was found between
   126→   126→the belief that self-control is a limited resource and trait self-control (Hagger et al., 2019),
   127→   127→implying that what people believe about self-control is relatively independent from their self-
   128→   128→control capacity. Therefore, we aim to examine the power of self-control beliefs in predicting
   129→   129→life outcomes, especially in comparison to that of trait self-control and metacognition.
   130→   130→Current Study
   131→   131→In this pre-registered study, we first aimed to reduce an initial mapping of 14 self-
   132→   132→control beliefs (Li et al., 2025) into a more comprehensive framework of core self-control
   133→   133→beliefs. The previously identified set of 14 self-control beliefs was composed by asking
   134→   134→participants about what defines self-control, capturing key aspects of self-control (Li et al.,
   135→   135→2025). However, the underlying relationships between these key aspects remained
   136→   136→unexplored. Given their conceptual associations, some components may naturally cluster into
   137→   137→broader, higher-order belief factors, resulting in a more comprehensive framework. We
   138→   138→therefore hypothesized that the 14 self-control beliefs would be reproduced (H1a) and could
   139→   139→be structured into higher-order beliefs (H1b). Furthermore, we hypothesized that self-control
   140→   140→beliefs could be distinguished from trait self-control and metacognition (H2).
   141→   141→
   142→   142→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 7
   143→   143→Finally, the ultimate objective of the current study was to examine whether self-
   144→   144→control beliefs could predict self-control outcomes across multiple life domains, next to trait
   145→   145→self-control and metacognition. Specifically, we focused on the five behavioral domains of
   146→   146→health, school or work achievement, interpersonal relationships, finance, and subjective well-
   147→   147→being as prime outcomes of self-control (De Ridder et al., 2012; Wiese et al., 2018). We
   148→   148→hypothesized that self-control beliefs would predict these outcomes, distinct from trait self-
   149→   149→control and self-control metacognition (H3). Given concerns about the social desirability bias
   150→   150→in self-reported trait self-control (Gillebaart & de Ridder, 2015; Tangney et al., 2004), we
   151→   151→included social desirability in all our models examining Hypothesis 3. This study was
   152→   152→approved by the local Ethics Review Board. Pre-registration
   153→   153→(https://osf.io/zft53/?view_only=06d145911d934896a4b0ca30893401b1), study materials,
   154→   154→and data are publicly available on OSF
   155→   155→(https://osf.io/57czm/?view_only=139425fe79af4d3686dcbedd66cab6ad). Deviations from
   156→   156→the pre-registration are described in the text and summarized in Table A1 (see Appendix A).
   157→   157→Method
   158→   158→Participants
   159→   159→As pre-registered, 500 native English-speaking participants were recruited via Prolific
   160→   160→(www.prolific.com) in return for a monetary compensation of £2.20. Fifteen participants were
   161→   161→excluded for failure to provide data due to technical issues (n = 2), not being native English
   162→   162→speakers (n = 10), or failing at least one attention check (n = 3). Consequently, the final
   163→   163→sample consists of 485 participants, with a mean age of 40.25 years old (SD = 13.13; age
   164→   164→range 18 to 78) and a balanced gender distribution (50.1% female). The majority of
   165→   165→participants (59.4%) had a higher education background.

   168→   168→Procedure
   169→   169→Participants were provided with an information letter and gave informed consent prior
   170→   170→to participation. They were then asked to complete sections measuring their beliefs about
   171→   171→self-control, trait self-control, metacognition in self-control, social desirability, and life
   172→   172→outcomes. In addition to self-reported life outcomes (e.g., health, academic/work
   173→   173→performance, relationships, finance, and subjective well-being), we included Body Mass
   174→   174→Index (BMI) as an objective measure of health-related outcomes. At the end of the study,
   175→   175→demographic information was collected, including age, gender, education level, and first
   176→   176→language. Three attention check questions were incorporated at specific points throughout the
   177→   177→study, distributed across the main sections. One example of such a question was: "Please
   178→   178→select the number 2 for this item." Upon completion, participants received the monetary
   179→   179→compensation. The average completion time was 13.4 minutes (SD = 8.5), with a range from
   180→   180→3.3 to 70.4 minutes.
   181→   181→Measures
   182→   182→Beliefs about Self-control
   183→   183→To measure individuals’ beliefs about self-control, we employed a self-report scale
   184→   184→consisting of 42 items that assess 14 components of beliefs (i.e., three items per component).
   185→   185→These components were derived from prior research that mapped beliefs by asking people
   186→   186→words to describe what self-control is (Li et al., 2025)1. The full list of items is available on
   187→   187→OSF. Participants were asked to indicate their agreement with each item on a 5-point scale (1
   188→   188→= strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree). A sample item is “Self-control is difficult.” Items
   189→   189→were presented in a randomized order to each participant. Considering the multidimensional

   191→   191→component), and tested their validity in a pilot study (N = 97). For ease of administration, we selected the three
   192→   192→items with the highest reliability (Cronbach’s α) within each component, resulting in a final scale consisting of
   193→   193→42 items.

   196→   196→nature of the scale, we assessed the reliability of each component using McDonald's ω to
   197→   197→obtain a more robust estimate (Dunn et al., 2014; Hayes & and Coutts, 2020). Given the small
   198→   198→number of three items per component, which may contribute to lower reliability, most
   199→   199→components still demonstrated an acceptable McDonald ω of over .65, with the exceptions of
   200→   200→the agency (ω = .47), morality (ω = .51), conflict (ω = .32), and mental (ω = .42)
   201→   201→components.
   202→   202→Trait Self-control
   203→   203→The Brief Self-Control Scale (Tangney et al., 2004) was used to assess participants'
   204→   204→trait self-control. This 13-item scale asks participants to rate how much each statement
   205→   205→reflects their typical behavior, on a scale from 1 (not at all) to 5 (very much). A sample item is
   206→   206→“I am good at resisting temptation.” The scale demonstrated good reliability, with a
   207→   207→Cronbach's α of .89.
   208→   208→Metacognition in Self-Control
   209→   209→Metacognition in self-control was assessed using the Metacognition in Self-Control
   210→   210→Scale (MISCS) (Bürgler et al., 2022). The MISCS consists of two subscales, each containing
   211→   211→six items, that measure metacognitive knowledge and metacognitive regulation, respectively.
   212→   212→For both subscales, participants rated their agreement with each statement on a 5-point scale
   213→   213→(1 = disagree strongly, 5 = agree strongly). A sample item is “I am aware of what strategies I
   214→   214→use to resolve self-control conflicts.” The scale demonstrated good reliability (Cronbach’s α
   215→   215→= .90).
   216→   216→Social Desirability
   217→   217→Social desirability was measured using the Balanced Inventory of Desirable
   218→   218→Responding Short Form (BIDR-16) (Hart et al., 2015). This 16-item scale assesses two
   219→   219→factors of social desirability: self-deceptive enhancement and impression management, using
   220→   220→

   222→   222→a 7-point scale (1 = strongly disagree, 7 = strongly agree). The scale showed good reliability
   223→   223→(Cronbach’s α = .82).
   224→   224→Life Outcomes
   225→   225→Life outcomes, including health situation, work or school performance, relationships,
   226→   226→and financial performance, were measured using a series of brief questions. The questions
   227→   227→included “How do you rate your health?” “How do you rate your work performance (or
   228→   228→school performance if you’re a student now)?” “How do you rate your interpersonal
   229→   229→relationships? (For example, your relationships with your partner, friends, family, colleagues,
   230→   230→or peers)”, and “How do you rate your financial behavior? (In terms of handling money,
   231→   231→avoiding unaffordable purchases, saving, and managing spending and borrowing)”.
   232→   232→Participants responded on a 5-point scale (1 = very bad, 5 = very good). Life satisfaction, as
   233→   233→an indicator of participants' subjective well-being, was assessed using Cantril’s Ladder
   234→   234→(Cantril, 1965). Participants selected from 0 (worst possible life) to 10 (best possible life) to
   235→   235→best describe how they feel their life now. Additionally, participants’ height and weight were
   236→   236→collected to calculate their Body Mass Index (BMI).
   237→   237→Finally, participants provided demographic information, including age, gender, first
   238→   238→language, and highest education level.

   251→   251→Following our pre-registered analysis plan, we first tested H1 to examine the 14 self-
   252→   252→control belief components and their potential higher-order structure. Specifically, we
   253→   253→performed a confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) using the R package lavaan version 0.6-19
   254→   254→(Rosseel, 2012) to assess the 14-component model of self-control beliefs. To validate the
   255→   255→model further, we compared it with a one-factor model, where all items loaded onto a single
   256→   256→factor. Maximum likelihood estimation was used to estimate model parameters, and a fixed
   257→   257→factor model method was employed, with the variances of latent variables set to 1 for model
   258→   258→identification. Correlations among all latent variables were allowed to be estimated. Model fit
   259→   259→was evaluated using several fit indices, including the Comparative Fit Index (CFI), the
   260→   260→Tucker-Lewis Index (TLI), the Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR), and the
   261→   261→Root Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA) (Browne & Cudeck, 1992; Hu &
   262→   262→Bentler, 1999; Kline, 2023).

   264→   264→of self-control beliefs. Network analysis is a method that effectively communicates
   265→   265→multivariate patterns of dependency, making it particularly suitable for exploring high-
   266→   266→dimensional data when there is limited prior theory regarding the relationships between
   267→   267→variables (Borsboom et al., 2021). Recently, it has been widely used in research on

   274→   274→2018), and mental health (Blanken et al., 2019; Borsboom & Cramer, 2013). A network
   275→   275→essentially consists of nodes, typically representing entities, and edges that reflect the
   276→   276→relationships between them. In the current study, the 14 self-control belief components were
   277→   277→set as nodes. To visualize the associations among them, we applied the EBICglasso model in
   278→   278→JASP, which automatically sets small connections to zero, thus reducing relatively weak
   279→   279→associations and producing a more parsimonious network (Epskamp & Fried, 2018; Friedman
   280→   280→et al., 2008). A thick and direct edge represents a strong and direct association between two
   281→   281→nodes. We examined the clustering patterns in the network to identify potential higher-order
   282→   282→belief factors (Borsboom et al., 2021; Costantini et al., 2015, 2020; Golino & Epskamp,
   283→   283→2017). Additionally, we reported node strength, betweenness, and closeness to assess the
   284→   284→centrality of the self-control belief components. Correlation stability coefficient (CS-

   288→   288→how specific items loaded onto the clusters. Factor analysis and network analysis are not
   289→   289→mutually exclusive but rather complementary; while network analysis visualizes patterns of
   290→   290→associations, factor analysis helps to identify groups of closely connected components
   291→   291→(Cramer et al., 2012). This combined approach allowed us to identify potential higher-order
   292→   292→belief factors with greater robustness and obtain more accurate scores for each factor based
   293→   293→on specific items. We used JASP’s default settings for EFA, using parallel analysis, with a
   294→   294→minimum factor loading threshold of .40. Factor rotation was set to oblique (oblimin),
   295→   295→allowing for correlations among factors.

   300→   300→previous CFA, we evaluated the model fit based on fit indices, such as CFI, TLI, SRMR, and

   303→   303→we calculated Pearson’s r correlations between the higher-order belief factors and other self-
   304→   304→control-related variables, such as trait self-control and metacognition in self-control. We also
   305→   305→conducted CFA to compare a one-factor model, in which self-control beliefs, trait self-
   306→   306→control, and metacognition were combined, with a three-factor model treating them as distinct
   307→   307→constructs. Furthermore, we employed structural equation modeling (SEM) to assess whether
   308→   308→self-control beliefs, trait self-control, and metacognition independently predicted self-control
   309→   309→outcomes. If these constructs contributed independently to the outcomes, this would support
   310→   310→our hypothesis.

   312→   312→outcomes, we conducted a hierarchical regression analysis with six life outcomes as
   313→   313→dependent variables using JASP. Higher-order belief factors and the aforementioned self-
   314→   314→control-related factors were entered sequentially as independent variables. Participants with
   315→   315→missing BMI scores were excluded from the BMI-related analyses. Social desirability was
   316→   316→controlled for in all regression models.

   340→   340→Higher-Order Model

   446→   446→Discussion
   447→   447→Self-control plays a crucial role in individuals' ability to regulate their behaviors, resist
   448→   448→temptations, and achieve long-term goals. While trait self-control has been extensively
   449→   449→studied, emerging research suggests that beliefs about the nature of self-control may also
   450→   450→influence self-control outcomes. The overall aim of this study was to examine the predictive
   451→   451→power of beliefs about self-control across various life domains. To achieve this aim, we
   452→   452→sought to reproduce a set of 14 previously identified self-control beliefs and test a higher-
   453→   453→order structure of core beliefs. As expected, our findings provided support for the 14-
   454→   454→component framework of self-control beliefs and reveal a higher-order structure of four core
   455→   455→beliefs about to what extent self-control is Important, Effortless, Proactive, and Learnable.
   456→   456→Furthermore, these four core beliefs were only weakly correlated with trait self-control and
   457→   457→
   458→   458→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 19
   459→   459→self-control metacognition, supporting the notion that beliefs are conceptually distinct
   460→   460→constructs. Finally, these beliefs showed to be small but significant predictors of the majority
   461→   461→of life outcomes (i.e., BMI, health, interpersonal relationships, finance, and well-being) even
   462→   462→though trait self-control outperformed beliefs in the majority of cases.
   463→   463→Reflecting on these main findings, we find that beliefs clearly reveal the multifaceted
   464→   464→nature of self-control as attested by the higher-order model, suggesting that these higher-order
   465→   465→belief factors capture meaningful distinctions among belief components. These factors
   466→   466→indicate that people think about self-control in terms of being important, effortless, proactive,
   467→   467→and learnable. Given that recent research emphasizes the effortless and habitual features of
   468→   468→self-control (Gillebaart & de Ridder, 2015; Stojanovic & Wood, 2024), as well as its smart
   469→   469→and strategic nature (Duckworth et al., 2016; Fujita et al., 2020), our research provides
   470→   470→evidence that these characteristics also are on top of people’s minds when thinking about self-
   471→   471→control (cf. Gennara et al., 2023). Moreover, the finding that self-control as a proactive
   472→   472→strategy defines one of the four core beliefs suggests that people, to some extent, associate
   473→   473→self-control with a strategic element. Finally, the finding that participants consider self-
   474→   474→control important matches well with scientific insights that emphasize the significant role of
   475→   475→self-control in leading happy, healthy, and successful lives.
   476→   476→Consistent with prior research, our findings demonstrate the importance of self-
   477→   477→control beliefs in various behavioral domains (Di Maio et al., 2020; Hagger et al., 2019;
   478→   478→Jankowski & Job, 2023; Mukhopadhyay & Johar, 2005). While previous studies have
   479→   479→primarily focused on the role of specific types of beliefs (e.g., self-control as a limited
   480→   480→resource and the malleability of self-control), a wide range of other self-control beliefs
   481→   481→remained underexplored until now. Our findings suggest that a broader set of self-control
   482→   482→beliefs may have a small but significant impact on behavior across several life domains,
   483→   483→including health, relationships, finance, and well-being. Although their influence did not
   484→   484→
   485→   485→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 20
   486→   486→surpass that of trait self-control, which consistently was a better predictor of all life outcomes,
   487→   487→these findings are noteworthy given that trait self-control measures are often subject to social
   488→   488→desirability bias (Gillebaart & de Ridder, 2015; Tangney et al., 2004), as also observed in the
   489→   489→present study. In contrast, self-control beliefs proved unaffected by social desirability and
   490→   490→may thus provide a better approach for understanding how thinking about self-control affects
   491→   491→outcomes. Furthermore, the beliefs did outperform metacognition in self-control, which is
   492→   492→thought to be influential in self-control exertion (Bürgler & Hennecke, 2024; Hennecke &
   493→   493→Bürgler, 2023). However, our findings suggest that self-control metacognition is not a
   494→   494→significant predictor of self-control outcomes, which again confirms that self-control beliefs
   495→   495→and metacognition are distinct concepts. These findings also imply that self-control
   496→   496→metacognition, characterized by cognitive planning, monitoring, and evaluating, may be more
   497→   497→important in dealing with specific self-control conflicts than in predicting general life
   498→   498→outcomes.
   499→   499→Among the four core beliefs, the Self-Control is Important Belief and the Self-Control
   500→   500→is Effortless Belief stood out as the most influential beliefs. These results suggest that, when
   501→   501→people believe that self-control is important and beneficial, they may be more willing to
   502→   502→invest effort in regulating their behaviors, such as maintaining a healthy diet, exercising
   503→   503→regularly, or resisting temptations, even in the absence of high trait self-control. One way in
   504→   504→which valuing self-control may enhance performance in these behavioral domains is by
   505→   505→increasing individuals’ motivation to engage in behaviors that require self-control. Future
   506→   506→research should explicitly examine this link to verify the role of beliefs in boosting
   507→   507→motivation for self-control. Aligning with recent research on metamotivation (Fujita et al.,
   508→   508→2024; Miele et al., 2020), our findings suggest that motivation directed toward self-control
   509→   509→exertion, rather than specific outcome goals, may influence outcomes in multiple life
   510→   510→domains.
   511→   511→
   512→   512→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 21
   513→   513→Similarly, believing that self-control is effortless also positively predicted better
   514→   514→health, relationships, financial outcomes, and well-being, although its impact largely
   515→   515→disappeared after controlling for trait self-control. Previous research has found that
   516→   516→individuals with higher levels of trait self-control tend to exert self-control more effortlessly,
   517→   517→for example, by identifying self-control conflicts earlier, resolving them more quickly, and
   518→   518→relying more on habits (Gillebaart & Schneider, 2024; Stojanovic & Wood, 2024). Indeed,
   519→   519→our study shows that the belief that self-control is Effortless and trait self-control were
   520→   520→moderately correlated, corroborating the notion that simply believing that self-control is
   521→   521→effortless may already motivate people toward better life outcomes (De Ridder et al., 2012).
   522→   522→Interestingly, the four core beliefs did not consistently predict outcomes in the same
   523→   523→direction, nor did they show uniform effects across different domains. For instance, the Self-
   524→   524→Control is Important belief positively predicted general health, but negatively predicted
   525→   525→finance and well-being. This pattern of differential influence suggests that the role of self-
   526→   526→control beliefs may be context-dependent. The positive association between the Self-Control
   527→   527→is Important belief and general health may indicate that strong value-based beliefs,
   528→   528→emphasizing the importance, goodness, success, and morality of self-control, support the
   529→   529→maintenance of healthy behaviors. However, these same value-driven beliefs appear to have
   530→   530→adverse effects on financial outcomes and well-being, whereas the belief that self-control is
   531→   531→effortless positively predicted both outcomes. These contrasting associations of Important and
   532→   532→Effortless beliefs with finance and well-being could mean that believing that self-control can
   533→   533→be effortless (e.g., by building habits or routines rather than emphasizing strong values and
   534→   534→motivations) may be more adaptive for sustaining financial stability and psychological well-
   535→   535→being over time. Overall, these findings highlight the subtleties of self-control beliefs
   536→   536→influence: while they may shape behavioral outcomes, different aspects of these beliefs may
   537→   537→either facilitate or hinder success depending on the domain. From this perspective, future
   538→   538→
   539→   539→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 22
   540→   540→studies could explore which specific self-control beliefs or a specific combination of self-
   541→   541→control beliefs are most predictive of different life outcomes.
   542→   542→In contrast, the Proactive and Learnable beliefs did not emerge as significant
   543→   543→predictors of life outcomes. One possible explanation is that believing in proactive self-
   544→   544→control by planning and strategizing may be more critical for managing specific self-control
   545→   545→conflicts in the moment rather than predicting broader long-term life outcomes that require
   546→   546→self-control over time. Notably, we found no evidence supporting the influence of self-control
   547→   547→beliefs on work or school performance. Previous research has revealed mixed findings
   548→   548→regarding the predictive power of beliefs about non-limited self-control resources on
   549→   549→academic outcomes (Bernecker, 2016; Job et al., 2015). Thus, together with the mixed
   550→   550→evidence in prior research, the non-significant findings in our study may imply that the
   551→   551→beliefs we identified here do not directly impact work or school performance. Alternatively, it
   552→   552→may be because the outcomes that we measured were less specific than those measured in
   553→   553→previous studies, or that academic or work performance is multifactorial and influenced by a
   554→   554→wide range of variables such as self-efficacy, grade goal, and effort regulation, which may
   555→   555→also weaken the predictive strength of self-control (and self-control beliefs) (Richardson et
   556→   556→al., 2012).
   557→   557→Strengths and Limitations
   558→   558→This study goes beyond the traditional view (which only examines a limited set of
   559→   559→belief components) (Bermúdez et al., 2023b; Gennara et al., 2023; Job et al., 2015) and
   560→   560→provides a comprehensive understanding of the impact of self-control beliefs on multiple life
   561→   561→domains, contributing to the growing body of research on self-control beliefs. Future studies
   562→   562→may consider how these beliefs can be modified to enhance self-control in target domains. As
   563→   563→beliefs are influenced by cultural, policy, and societal influences (Yanaoka et al., 2022),
   564→   564→interventions aimed at shifting self-control beliefs, alongside fostering trait self-control, may
   565→   565→
   566→   566→SELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 23
   567→   567→have promising effects, not only at the individual level but also in promoting broader societal
   568→   568→improvements in self-control behaviors (Hofmann, 2024).
   569→   569→However, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the study relied on self-
   570→   570→reported measures. Future research should incorporate more objective and specific
   571→   571→behavioral measures (e.g., tracking health behaviors over time) to validate the predictive
   572→   572→power of self-control beliefs. Self-reported outcomes may also restrict our ability to examine
   573→   573→the prediction of self-control beliefs on actual daily self-control behaviors. To address this
   574→   574→limitation, future research employing methods such as the experience sampling method may
   575→   575→provide further insights (Bürgler & Hennecke, 2024; Hofmann et al., 2012).
   576→   576→Second, while our belief measures demonstrated acceptable reliability, further
   577→   577→validation of these measures is recommended for future studies. In addition, although the
   578→   578→structure of the higher-order core beliefs was confirmed, the validation was conducted on the
   579→   579→same dataset used for their exploration. Therefore, the core belief structure still requires
   580→   580→validation in independent samples.
   581→   581→Third, the study’s reliance on an online recruitment method and a sample restricted to
   582→   582→the English-speaking population limits the generalizability of the findings. Since self-control
   583→   583→beliefs may vary across cultures (Li et al., 2025; Savani & Job, 2017; Sun et al., 2019), future
   584→   584→studies should aim to recruit more diverse samples and examine whether the prevalence and
   585→   585→predictive power of self-control beliefs on life outcomes differ across cultural and
   586→   586→demographic groups.
   587→   587→Finally, the current study remains correlational in nature. As we have explained, self-
   588→   588→control beliefs may predict life outcomes, but we cannot rule out the possibility of the
   589→   589→opposite directionality. Therefore, future studies looking into the causal effect of beliefs on
   590→   590→life outcomes may disentangle this.

   593→   593→Conclusion
   594→   594→In conclusion, this study offers novel insights into the role of self-control beliefs in
   595→   595→predicting various life outcomes across multiple domains. Our findings emphasize that self-
   596→   596→control beliefs, particularly when viewed as a multifaceted construct, may contribute to the
   597→   597→prediction of behavioral outcomes and are unaffected by social desirability bias compared to
   598→   598→trait self-control and self-control metacognition, and differ depending on the behavioral
   599→   599→domain. Together, these findings suggest that self-control beliefs may be an interesting
   600→   600→psychological mechanism for understanding and promoting self-control, with potential
   601→   601→applications for interventions targeting health, interpersonal relationships, financial
   602→   602→behaviors, and well-being.
