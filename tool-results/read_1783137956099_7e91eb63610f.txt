     8→Abstract. The embodied turn in language science has established the visible body
     9→(gesture, gaze, mouth and brow movement) as integral to how meaning is predicted and
    10→built in face-to-face conversation. We argue that this turn should extend inward, to each
    11→interlocutor's internal body, and across both comprehension and production. Self-
    12→generated visceral signals (cardiac, respiratory, gastric, and arousal-linked) act as internal
    13→control rhythms that serve three functions a theory of conversation requires: gating the
    14→uptake of incoming signals, regulating the timing of outgoing action including turn onset,
    15→and coupling across interacting individuals. These functions are not channel-symmetric,
    16→with respiration most directly tied to speech and cardiac and arousal signals setting the
    17→gain. Cast as neural gain on weighted signals, this view adds concrete, falsifiable
    18→predictions to current processing accounts, opening a largely unexplored frontier for the
    19→science of human communication.
    20→Keywords: multimodal language, face-to-face communication, internal bodily signals,
    21→brain-body interaction
    22→Introduction
    23→Over the past two decades, psycholinguistics has reconceived its object. Where production and
    24→comprehension were once modelled as isolated processes inside a single mind, language is
    25→now widely understood as a situated form of joint action, distributed across interlocutors1.
    26→Central to this shift is the recognition that the body is part of language rather than corollary
    27→to it: the visible signals of gesture, gaze, and mouth and brow movement carry semantic and
    28→pragmatic content and help interlocutors predict, coordinate, and ground meaning2. Recent
    29→syntheses characterize face-to-face conversation by features that single-mind models miss:
    30→the integral role of visual bodily signals, the continuous feedback of addressees, and the
    31→demands of multi-party interaction3.
    32→We share this conviction and aim to extend it inward, and symmetrically. The bodies
    33→in current accounts are the externally visible body (the speaker’s gestures, articulators, facial
    34→and limb movements) as carriers of predictive and pragmatic cues, and the addressee’s visible
    35→feedback. Alongside this communicative body, each interlocutor also has an internal body
    36→dynamics that is continuously active as they converse: a beating heart, a slow gastric rhythm,
    37→breathing activity, and a continuously shifting arousal state. Our proposal is that this internal
    38→body belongs in the picture too, on both the comprehension side (the listener taking in a

    62→they became irrelevant precisely when the perceptual object is another person speaking, or
    63→the action is one’s own utterance.

    79→What the internal body does: gating input, timing output, and
    80→coupling two bodies
    81→That a person’s own body modulates how they process external signals, and generate their
    82→own, is no longer speculative: reviews treat cardiac, gastric, and respiratory signals as a
    83→coordinated set of internal rhythms shaping brain dynamics and cognition4,8, consistent with
    84→predictive accounts in which the brain continuously models its own bodily state9. Rather than
    85→reviewing these channels one organ at a time, we organise the evidence around the three
    86→functions a theory of conversation needs: how the body gates incoming signals, times outgoing
    87→action, and, in principle, couples two bodies during interaction. The channels are not
    88→interchangeable. Respiration is the pivot, the rhythm most directly tied to speech and most
    89→clearly under voluntary control; the cardiac cycle and pupil-linked arousal provides the gain
    90→mechanism that sets moment-to-moment excitability; and the slower gastric rhythm
    91→contributes a broader, organism-level coupling more loosely connected to the second-by-
    92→second demands of talk. We specify throughout whether a claim is established, a plausible
    93→extension to conversation, or a speculative proposal for the agenda that follows.
    94→Gating the incoming signal
    95→The first function is gating: the probability that an external signal is registered, and how
    96→strongly, varies with the perceiver’s bodily state. The evidence is established across different
    97→bodily channels. For the cardiac cycle, one of the clearest cases concerns facial signals: fearful
    98→faces are detected more accurately and reach awareness faster at systole, when baroreceptor
    99→activity is maximal, than at diastole10,11. Cardiac phase also modulates multisensory
   100→integration and biases the reading of another’s gaze4, and reaches the processing of sound:
   101→heartbeat-synchronous sounds facilitate the neural encoding of auditory regularities even in
   102→comatose patients, predicting their recovery12. Because faces, gaze, and expressions are central
   103→multimodal signals in conversation, and speech itself is conveyed through sound, the
   104→implication is direct, though still a plausible extension: the uptake of a partner’s facial cues
   105→and speech sounds may depend partly on where they fall within the listener’s cardiac cycle.
   106→Respiration gates the same signals, and bears especially directly on communication.
   107→Across detection, discrimination, and memory tasks, performance covaries with respiratory
   108→phase13, and nasal breathing entrains cortical oscillations and improves recognition of fearful
   109→faces during inspiration14. Perceptual sensitivity to near-threshold stimuli is enhanced during
   110→inspiration, alongside respiration-modulated increases in arousal and cortical excitability15,
   111→and respiratory phase may shape the fidelity of neural representations of visual stimuli shortly
   112→before a response16. Respiration and the cardiac cycle are often best considered jointly, both
   113→modulating tactile detection within one paradigm17, and breathing helps coordinate the brain-
   114→heart interactions through which cardiac signals reach cortex18. Critically, respiration reaches

   122→The slower gastric rhythm contributes at a longer timescale and less directly. The

   128→input. Even so, the gastric contribution is the most loosely tied of the three to the demands of
   129→conversation; in our view it is best read as evidence that gating extends to the organism level,
   130→rather than as a source of immediate conversational predictions.
   131→Timing the outgoing action
   132→The same rhythms bias the timing of outgoing action, which matters because producing an
   133→utterance is a precisely timed motor act, and turn-taking requires launching it within a narrow
   134→window. Respiration provides the most direct bridge between internal bodily rhythms and
   135→conversation, since every utterance is produced on an out-breath. People initiate self-paced
   136→actions preferentially at particular respiratory phases, and the readiness potential preceding
   137→a movement is itself modulated by the respiratory cycle24. Because breathing and speech
   138→planning are tightly interleaved, speakers inhale at grammatical and motor-planning
   139→junctures, and the depth and timing of inhalation are adapted to the structure of the utterance
   140→to come, reflecting a forward model that informs respiration from upcoming content25,26,27.
   141→The reverse holds too: greater cognitive-linguistic demand reshapes speech breathing,
   142→reducing syllables per breath group28. The cardiac cycle biases action in parallel. Voluntary
   143→movements are not uniform across the cycle: sampling actions tend to coincide with systole,
   144→while freely chosen actions are withheld more often at systole, consistent with baroreceptor
   145→inhibition of motor output7, and cardiac activity directly modulates corticospinal excitability
   146→and sensorimotor alpha/beta dynamics7. Read together, these establish a two-sided pattern,
   147→that is, gating on the input side, biasing on the output side, for both respiration and the heart;
   148→what remains a plausible extension is that the same pattern governs the launching of a
   149→conversational turn, making turn-onset latency partly a bodily variable.
   150→Arousal: the common currency of gating and timing
   151→Gating and timing share a neuromodulatory thread. Brainstem nuclei controlling visceral
   152→rhythms are linked to the locus coeruleus, allowing bodily state to pace noradrenergic activity
   153→and the pupil-linked arousal that indexes it; arousal in turn modulates cortical excitability and
   154→adjusts the gain on incoming evidence and outgoing commands15. The coupling between
   155→respiration and arousal is now demonstrable directly: at rest the breathing rhythm phase-
   156→couples to spontaneous pupil-size fluctuations at ~0.2-0.3 Hz, directed-connectivity analyses
   157→point to a respiratory drive on pupillary dynamics rather than the reverse29, despite the
   158→broadband structure of the pupillary signal30. This coupling is stronger at rest than during task
   159→and tracks momentary breathing rate, suggesting the respiratory pacemaker helps set the
   160→tempo of arousal fluctuations that modulate how incoming speech, faces, and gestures are

   178→One mechanism: bodily rhythms set neural gain
   179→Across cardiac, gastric, and respiratory rhythms, the phase or state of a bodily signal
   180→modulates cortical excitability and behaviour even in neutral, non-homeostatic tasks, and for
   181→both perception and action4,7. The reframing is therefore general and two-sided: self-
   182→generated bodily signals act as internal control rhythms gating both the uptake of external
   183→cues and the planning of one’s own communicative acts. A predictive coding model gives this
   184→proposal a mechanistic home: bodily rhythms modulate local and global neural gain across
   185→the cortical hierarchy, and in doing so shape the precision assigned to predictions and
   186→prediction errors31, a logic extending from respiration to cardiac and gastric modulation9.
   187→Respiration occupies a special place because it is under partial voluntary control: controlled
   188→breathing can be framed as active inference, recontextualizing bottom-up interoceptive
   189→evidence to update higher-level predictions32. This bridges Holler and Kuhlen’s prediction
   190→account of visual bodily cues in conversation and the inner body dynamics: if a partner’s visual
   191→signals are weighted by their precision, and one’s own visceral signals help determine that
   192→precision while shaping motor output, then the heart, gut, and lungs of both interlocutors may
   193→be considered part of the machinery of conversation. These rhythms are not passive: people
   194→adjust breathing to task demands and stimulus timing, and slowed breathing reshapes pre-
   195→stimulus alpha/beta dynamics and sensitivity to faces33,34, so the body actively sets its own
   196→sampling8. Incorporating this coupling into the cognitive science of language and
   197→communication, for speakers as well as listeners, opens a largely unexplored frontier.
   198→Reading the conversational features through the internal body
   199→The evidence reviewed above comes largely from solitary tasks. Yet the processes it implicates,
   200→gating what one perceives and timing what one does, are precisely those that the study of
   201→conversation places at its centre. Holler and Kuhlen3 organise face-to-face conversation
   202→around three features: the integral role of multimodal signals, the monitoring of addressee
   203→behaviour, and the shift from dyadic to multi-party interaction. We take their synthesis as our
   204→reference framework for face-to-face multimodal communication: it consolidates the current
   205→consensus and, through its prediction-based logic, offers a natural point of contact with the

   210→internal body. Our aim is not to revise this framework, but to read each of its three features
   211→through that bodily lens. Each acquires a bodily dimension once the internal body is admitted;
   212→not merely that conversation recruits the body, but that the modulations above apply to the
   213→very signals and actions of which conversation is made.
   214→Bodily states weight the predictive value of multimodal signals
   215→Multimodal signals are integral to how meaning is composed, and a central reason is timing.
   216→The signals a speaker makes visible (with hands, head, mouth, eyes, facial expressions, and
   217→articulators) tend to lead the words, giving recipients advance, predictive information before
   218→the acoustic signal specifies it35,2. This lead is distributed across channels. Co-speech hand
   219→gestures are the clearest case: meaningful gestures begin before their lexical affiliates, and
   220→recipients exploit this to predict upcoming meaning36; even rhythmic beat gestures, carrying
   221→no referential content, facilitate lexical access37 and bias how the acoustic signal is parsed38.
   222→The visible articulators carry a comparable lead at finer grain: mouth and lip movements
   223→provide information about place and manner of articulation that precedes the acoustic signal,
   224→so visible speech speeds and sharpens neural processing of the voice rather than duplicating
   225→it39,40,41. This visible signal is itself encoded hierarchically: using temporal response functions,
   226→the cortical tracking of silent visual speech reflects not only low-level lip motion but also
   227→higher-level categorical, viseme-like articulatory features42, and visual cortex entrains to the
   228→speech carried by the lips in an intelligibility-dependent way, extracting phonological
   229→information rather than mere kinematics43. This contribution of lip movements to neural
   230→speech tracking is strongest, and most behaviourally consequential, when listening is
   231→challenging44. During naturalistic comprehension the brain even proactively predicts visual
   232→speech units beyond low-level mouth motion45. Movements of head and face supply a looser
   233→stratum, and even gaze provides cues, coupling to the unfolding speech and helping regulate
   234→the exchange46,47.
   235→Across these channels, exploiting the predictive value of a visible signal means, in
   236→predictive processing terms, assigning precision to that signal (precision-weighting). At the
   237→neural level, this precision is commonly understood to be implemented through gain, the
   238→modulation of neural responsiveness that amplifies or attenuates a signal’s influence on
   239→ongoing processing. This is the same quantity that internal bodily signals were shown above
   240→to modulate. Cardiac and respiratory cycles alter detection sensitivity and cortical excitability,
   241→while pupil-linked arousal adjusts the gain on incoming evidence. The very operation by which
   242→a gesture, brow raise, or articulatory movement is weighted may therefore be shaped by the
   243→heartbeat, breath, and arousal state. On this reading the visible channels are not weighted by
   244→a purely cognitive estimate of reliability; their precision is set, moment to moment, against a
   245→changing bodily background, so how strongly a recipient leans on what they see may partly be
   246→a function of internal state. That dependence is likely to be sharpest when the acoustic signal
   247→is degraded48,49,50.

   249→Monitoring a partner for understanding or difficulty, while comprehending and planning
   250→one’s own contribution, draws on limited resources, so comprehension can suffer under load.
   251→The internal body may bear on this not by adding another source of load, but by shaping the

   256→momentary processing capacity available to the interlocutor. Detection sensitivity, reaction
   257→times, and multisensory integration vary across the cardiac and respiratory cycles10,13, and a
   258→salient facial signal reaches awareness faster at one phase of the heartbeat than another11. A
   259→speaker must monitor subtle cues from the addressee, such as a frown, a brief gaze aversion,
   260→or a delayed nod. Because these cues belong to the class of facial and gaze signals whose uptake
   261→can be phase-dependent, monitoring efficiency is unlikely to be constant. Instead, it may rise
   262→and fall with the body’s state. The same arousal system operates more slowly: pupil-linked
   263→arousal continuously adjusts the gain on incoming evidence15, modulating the window in
   264→which a fleeting cue can register. The resource account and the bodily account are thus
   265→complementary: the former specifies that monitoring competes for limited capacity, the latter
   266→that this capacity itself fluctuates over one-to-few-second rhythms shaped by the heart,
   267→respiration, and the arousal they pace.
   268→Beyond the single body: two rhythmic systems in exchange
   269→The third feature is the step beyond the single mind, which Holler and Kuhlen identify with
   270→the move to multi-party interaction. We suggest that this outward step has a bodily
   271→counterpart that is already present in the dyad. The evidence reviewed above establishes two
   272→premises on both sides of the exchange: a perceiver’s uptake of external signals is gated by
   273→cardiac, gastric, and respiratory state, and the same rhythms bias when voluntary acts,
   274→including speech, are launched7,24. Perception and production are therefore already paced
   275→from within. It follows that two people in conversation are not simply independent processors
   276→exchanging messages. They are two oscillating bodies, each gating perception and action in
   277→time, and each producing signals, such as an utterance on an out-breath, a gaze shift, or a nod,
   278→that the other receives through their own body-dependent filter. Before interlocutors track
   279→one another’s knowledge, they are coupled at the level of their bodies: a speaker’s exhalation
   280→carries an utterance that reaches the listener at a particular point in the listener’s own bodily
   281→cycles, while the listener’s readiness to respond is shaped by where that signal falls.
   282→From solitary perception to interacting dyads: two bodies in
   283→interaction
   284→A natural question is whether evidence from solitary tasks carries over to conversation. Two
   285→considerations suggest that it should, at least in principle, and that conversation may be an
   286→especially revealing setting. First, conversation already recruits the speaker’s body in ways
   287→laboratory tasks only hint at, most clearly through respiration: in dialogue a listener’s
   288→breathing shifts to resemble speech breathing and changes in anticipation of taking the floor51,
   289→pre-turn inhalations are a recognised cue that a participant is about to speak52, and
   290→neuroimaging in unconstrained conversation shows cortical and brainstem control inhibiting
   291→the automatic respiratory rhythm so breathing can entrain to speech onset53. This coupling is
   292→not uniform global synchrony but concentrates at turn-taking events, where a prospective
   293→speaker tends to inhale at the end of the partner’s exhalation51,26. The same holds for faster
   294→signals: if cardiac and arousal state bias when a speaker launches a motor act7, turn-onset
   295→timing is partly a bodily variable.

   300→Second, if each interlocutor’s bodily state gates their own perception and biases their
   301→own production, and both entrain to a shared audiovisual stream and to each other’s visible
   302→cues, their internal rhythms should become coupled across the dyad. Predictive accounts
   303→already propose that a listener coordinates breathing with a speaker via acoustic and visible
   304→cues, aligning forward models and potentially enhancing comprehension25. Such coupling
   305→does not need to wait for language: mere visual contact between two people, with no speech
   306→and no task, is sufficient to synchronise their heart rates, pupil diameters, and, more weakly,
   307→breathing, co-occurring with the synchrony of spontaneous behaviours such as movement and
   308→smiling54. If minimal visual contact already aligns two autonomic systems, the richer
   309→signalling of conversation should do so at least as strongly. Crucially, however, dyadic coupling
   310→need not mean global in-phase synchrony. As with breathing within a single speaker,
   311→conversational coordination may involve structured phase relationships rather than uniform
   312→alignment. Turn-taking is partly anti-phasic by design: one speaker may inhale in preparation
   313→to speak as the other completes the exhalation that carries their utterance. The prediction is
   314→therefore not that interlocutors’ bodily rhythms should simply rise and fall together
   315→throughout an exchange, but that their phases should become systematically related at
   316→communicatively relevant moments, especially around turn transitions, feedback cues, and
   317→other exchange points. This yields a concrete, falsifiable reading of “alignment”: interlocutors
   318→whose visceral and arousal states are more coordinated should ground meaning faster and
   319→show tighter neural coupling, and the windows of high mutual sensitivity should be partly
   320→predictable from the dyad’s joint bodily state. The interpersonal-synchrony literature has
   321→largely treated bodily and neural coupling separately; the body-brain framework predicts they
   322→are two views of one system.
   323→An enriched picture and a new empirical agenda
   324→Processing accounts of conversation can be enriched in four ways once the internal body is
   325→included.
   326→1. An endogenous gain term, on both sides. Each cognitive process, for the
   327→comprehender (visual-signal integration, partner prediction) and the speaker (planning,
   328→articulation, turn launching), is modulated by an oscillatory gain set jointly by that
   329→person’s internal bodily signals and overall arousal.
   330→2. A production threshold, not only a perceptual gate. The model needs an explicit
   331→path from the speaker’s bodily state to the timing of their output: visceral and arousal state
   332→biases when a planned utterance is launched, so turn-onset latency is partly bodily, not
   333→purely a product of comprehension and planning.
   334→3. Active, not passive, sampling. The arrows run both ways through the body:
   335→interlocutors adjust their bodily state to align high-gain windows with anticipated
   336→informative moments (e.g., a projected turn-end, a gesture stroke), making bodily rhythm
   337→part of prediction and turn-taking rather than a backdrop.
   338→4. Coupling as a dyadic variable. The joint-action link should include inter-individual
   339→physiological coupling (cardiac and arousal synchrony, respiratory entrainment, and their
   340→relation to neural coupling) as a measurable mediator of alignment, alongside the
   341→representational parity current accounts assume.

   346→These components are not only conceptual; each yields concrete, falsifiable predictions, which

   348→Box 1. Testable predictions of the integrated body account
   349→1. Endogenous gain. A gesture, brow-raise, or other facial cue should have a larger effect on
   350→comprehension when it falls in a higher-sensitivity respiratory or cardiac phase of the listener than
   351→when it falls in a lower-sensitivity phase.
   352→2. State-dependent reliance on visual speech. Reliance on visible articulatory cues under
   353→acoustically degraded conditions should be more body-state dependent than reliance under clear-
   354→speech conditions, as the listener leans harder on the visual channel.
   355→3. Production threshold. Turn-onset latency should be predicted, in part, by the speaker’s
   356→respiratory phase, cardiac phase, and pupil-linked arousal in the moments before turn onset, over
   357→and above comprehension and planning variables.
   358→4. Coupling and alignment. Dyads with greater physiological coupling (cardiac and arousal
   359→synchrony, respiratory entrainment) should show tighter conversational alignment, fewer repair
   360→sequences, or better mutual comprehension than dyads with weaker coupling.
   361→5. Coupling as mediator. The strength of body-brain coupling should mediate the relationship
   362→between multimodal cue uptake and inter-individual neural coupling, so that bodily and neural
   363→alignment behave as two views of one system.
   364→These specific additions to the processing picture point, in turn, to a broader consequence.
   365→Perhaps the most important is not any single result but the research space it opens. Treating
   366→each interlocutor’s internal signals as part of the communicative loop turns currently separate
   367→literatures (on interoception and visceral rhythms, on multimodal language, and on
   368→interpersonal coordination) into an highly interdisciplinary and largely unexplored field of
   369→human behaviour, with its own questions, paradigms, and measures still to be defined. In
   370→broad terms the perspective invites work along several axes: how internal signals relate to the
   371→uptake of a partner’s cues and to the planning of one’s own; how this balance differs across
   372→people and develops over the lifespan; and how two bodies’ rhythms relate during live
   373→interaction. Some scaffolding exists: the ecological turn in interoception research (wearable
   374→multimodal recording, lifespan and non-WEIRD sampling, naturalistic paradigms) offers
   375→methods for the within-person and developmental questions⁸, and dynamical accounts of
   376→brain-body trajectories offer the modelling tools for inter-individual questions⁶. Realising it
   377→means bringing together the measurement of bodily signals and the study of multimodal,
   378→interactive language, traditions that rarely meet, and the principal value of this Perspective is
   379→to motivate that convergence.

   395→one illustration, autism combines atypical interoception56 and disturbed autonomic
   396→regulation with differences in social, multimodal communication; whether these co-occurring
   397→profiles are mechanistically linked rather than concurrent is a question the framework makes
   398→testable. A complementary and in some respects cleaner case is the disruption of the bodily
   399→signal most directly tied to talk, the breath: in Parkinson’s disease the normal coupling
   400→between lung-volume initiation, utterance length, and inspiratory timing is weakened57, so
   401→that the respiratory rhythm on which speech and turn-projection depend is itself disordered.
   402→Here the starting point is an impairment of a control rhythm rather than of language, and the
   403→framework predicts that such a disruption should propagate to the timing of turns and the
   404→coordination of breathing with a partner, over and above its effect on the acoustic signal. We
   405→name such avenues cautiously, as the first step toward specifying which bodily signals are
   406→worth measuring alongside the linguistic ones.
   407→Conclusion
   408→The embodied, interactive turn has shown the visible body is central to how meaning is built
   409→in conversation. We have argued this turn can be carried inward, to each interlocutor’s
   410→internal body, on both sides of the exchange and across the full family of visceral signals but
   411→with different weights. The brain that converses is housed in a body that beats, digests,
   412→breathes, and modulates its own arousal, and a growing literature shows these signals help set
   413→the gain on incoming evidence and shape the timing of one’s own acts. Treating them as part
   414→of the conversational loop offers candidate mechanisms for the prediction, the processing
   415→demands, and the coordination that the communication-as-joint-action perspective places at
   416→its centre, and enriches the idea of alignment between minds with bodies also coordinating.
   417→More importantly, it opens a largely unexplored agenda: how the body’s internal signals
   418→participate in comprehension, in production, and in their coordination across interlocutors.
   419→On this view, face-to-face conversation is a meeting of two fully embodied minds, grounded
   420→in a shared situational context and each listening and speaking through its own bodily
   421→dynamics.
