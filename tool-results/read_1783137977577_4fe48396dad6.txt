   204→   204→reference framework for face-to-face multimodal communication: it consolidates the current
   205→   205→consensus and, through its prediction-based logic, offers a natural point of contact with the

   214→   214→Bodily states weight the predictive value of multimodal signals
   215→   215→Multimodal signals are integral to how meaning is composed, and a central reason is timing.
   216→   216→The signals a speaker makes visible (with hands, head, mouth, eyes, facial expressions, and
   217→   217→articulators) tend to lead the words, giving recipients advance, predictive information before
   218→   218→the acoustic signal specifies it35,2. This lead is distributed across channels. Co-speech hand
   219→   219→gestures are the clearest case: meaningful gestures begin before their lexical affiliates, and
   220→   220→recipients exploit this to predict upcoming meaning36; even rhythmic beat gestures, carrying
   221→   221→no referential content, facilitate lexical access37 and bias how the acoustic signal is parsed38.
   222→   222→The visible articulators carry a comparable lead at finer grain: mouth and lip movements
   223→   223→provide information about place and manner of articulation that precedes the acoustic signal,
   224→   224→so visible speech speeds and sharpens neural processing of the voice rather than duplicating
   225→   225→it39,40,41. This visible signal is itself encoded hierarchically: using temporal response functions,
   226→   226→the cortical tracking of silent visual speech reflects not only low-level lip motion but also
   227→   227→higher-level categorical, viseme-like articulatory features42, and visual cortex entrains to the
   228→   228→speech carried by the lips in an intelligibility-dependent way, extracting phonological
   229→   229→information rather than mere kinematics43. This contribution of lip movements to neural
   230→   230→speech tracking is strongest, and most behaviourally consequential, when listening is
   231→   231→challenging44. During naturalistic comprehension the brain even proactively predicts visual
   232→   232→speech units beyond low-level mouth motion45. Movements of head and face supply a looser
   233→   233→stratum, and even gaze provides cues, coupling to the unfolding speech and helping regulate
   234→   234→the exchange46,47.
   235→   235→Across these channels, exploiting the predictive value of a visible signal means, in
   236→   236→predictive processing terms, assigning precision to that signal (precision-weighting). At the
   237→   237→neural level, this precision is commonly understood to be implemented through gain, the
   238→   238→modulation of neural responsiveness that amplifies or attenuates a signal’s influence on
   239→   239→ongoing processing. This is the same quantity that internal bodily signals were shown above
   240→   240→to modulate. Cardiac and respiratory cycles alter detection sensitivity and cortical excitability,
   241→   241→while pupil-linked arousal adjusts the gain on incoming evidence. The very operation by which
   242→   242→a gesture, brow raise, or articulatory movement is weighted may therefore be shaped by the
   243→   243→heartbeat, breath, and arousal state. On this reading the visible channels are not weighted by
   244→   244→a purely cognitive estimate of reliability; their precision is set, moment to moment, against a
   245→   245→changing bodily background, so how strongly a recipient leans on what they see may partly be
   246→   246→a function of internal state. That dependence is likely to be sharpest when the acoustic signal
   247→   247→is degraded48,49,50.

   249→   249→Monitoring a partner for understanding or difficulty, while comprehending and planning
   250→   250→one’s own contribution, draws on limited resources, so comprehension can suffer under load.
   251→   251→The internal body may bear on this not by adding another source of load, but by shaping the

   257→   257→times, and multisensory integration vary across the cardiac and respiratory cycles10,13, and a
   258→   258→salient facial signal reaches awareness faster at one phase of the heartbeat than another11. A
   259→   259→speaker must monitor subtle cues from the addressee, such as a frown, a brief gaze aversion,
   260→   260→or a delayed nod. Because these cues belong to the class of facial and gaze signals whose uptake
   261→   261→can be phase-dependent, monitoring efficiency is unlikely to be constant. Instead, it may rise
   262→   262→and fall with the body’s state. The same arousal system operates more slowly: pupil-linked
   263→   263→arousal continuously adjusts the gain on incoming evidence15, modulating the window in
   264→   264→which a fleeting cue can register. The resource account and the bodily account are thus
   265→   265→complementary: the former specifies that monitoring competes for limited capacity, the latter
   266→   266→that this capacity itself fluctuates over one-to-few-second rhythms shaped by the heart,
   267→   267→respiration, and the arousal they pace.
   268→   268→Beyond the single body: two rhythmic systems in exchange
   269→   269→The third feature is the step beyond the single mind, which Holler and Kuhlen identify with
   270→   270→the move to multi-party interaction. We suggest that this outward step has a bodily
   271→   271→counterpart that is already present in the dyad. The evidence reviewed above establishes two
   272→   272→premises on both sides of the exchange: a perceiver’s uptake of external signals is gated by
   273→   273→cardiac, gastric, and respiratory state, and the same rhythms bias when voluntary acts,
   274→   274→including speech, are launched7,24. Perception and production are therefore already paced
   275→   275→from within. It follows that two people in conversation are not simply independent processors
   276→   276→exchanging messages. They are two oscillating bodies, each gating perception and action in
   277→   277→time, and each producing signals, such as an utterance on an out-breath, a gaze shift, or a nod,
   278→   278→that the other receives through their own body-dependent filter. Before interlocutors track
   279→   279→one another’s knowledge, they are coupled at the level of their bodies: a speaker’s exhalation
   280→   280→carries an utterance that reaches the listener at a particular point in the listener’s own bodily
   281→   281→cycles, while the listener’s readiness to respond is shaped by where that signal falls.
   282→   282→From solitary perception to interacting dyads: two bodies in
   283→   283→interaction
   284→   284→A natural question is whether evidence from solitary tasks carries over to conversation. Two
   285→   285→considerations suggest that it should, at least in principle, and that conversation may be an
   286→   286→especially revealing setting. First, conversation already recruits the speaker’s body in ways
   287→   287→laboratory tasks only hint at, most clearly through respiration: in dialogue a listener’s
   288→   288→breathing shifts to resemble speech breathing and changes in anticipation of taking the floor51,
   289→   289→pre-turn inhalations are a recognised cue that a participant is about to speak52, and
   290→   290→neuroimaging in unconstrained conversation shows cortical and brainstem control inhibiting
   291→   291→the automatic respiratory rhythm so breathing can entrain to speech onset53. This coupling is
   292→   292→not uniform global synchrony but concentrates at turn-taking events, where a prospective
   293→   293→speaker tends to inhale at the end of the partner’s exhalation51,26. The same holds for faster
   294→   294→signals: if cardiac and arousal state bias when a speaker launches a motor act7, turn-onset
   295→   295→timing is partly a bodily variable.

   300→   300→Second, if each interlocutor’s bodily state gates their own perception and biases their
   301→   301→own production, and both entrain to a shared audiovisual stream and to each other’s visible
   302→   302→cues, their internal rhythms should become coupled across the dyad. Predictive accounts
   303→   303→already propose that a listener coordinates breathing with a speaker via acoustic and visible
   304→   304→cues, aligning forward models and potentially enhancing comprehension25. Such coupling
   305→   305→does not need to wait for language: mere visual contact between two people, with no speech
   306→   306→and no task, is sufficient to synchronise their heart rates, pupil diameters, and, more weakly,
   307→   307→breathing, co-occurring with the synchrony of spontaneous behaviours such as movement and
   308→   308→smiling54. If minimal visual contact already aligns two autonomic systems, the richer
   309→   309→signalling of conversation should do so at least as strongly. Crucially, however, dyadic coupling
   310→   310→need not mean global in-phase synchrony. As with breathing within a single speaker,
   311→   311→conversational coordination may involve structured phase relationships rather than uniform
   312→   312→alignment. Turn-taking is partly anti-phasic by design: one speaker may inhale in preparation
   313→   313→to speak as the other completes the exhalation that carries their utterance. The prediction is
   314→   314→therefore not that interlocutors’ bodily rhythms should simply rise and fall together
   315→   315→throughout an exchange, but that their phases should become systematically related at
   316→   316→communicatively relevant moments, especially around turn transitions, feedback cues, and
   317→   317→other exchange points. This yields a concrete, falsifiable reading of “alignment”: interlocutors
   318→   318→whose visceral and arousal states are more coordinated should ground meaning faster and
   319→   319→show tighter neural coupling, and the windows of high mutual sensitivity should be partly
   320→   320→predictable from the dyad’s joint bodily state. The interpersonal-synchrony literature has
   321→   321→largely treated bodily and neural coupling separately; the body-brain framework predicts they
   322→   322→are two views of one system.
   323→   323→An enriched picture and a new empirical agenda
   324→   324→Processing accounts of conversation can be enriched in four ways once the internal body is
   325→   325→included.
   326→   326→1. An endogenous gain term, on both sides. Each cognitive process, for the
   327→   327→comprehender (visual-signal integration, partner prediction) and the speaker (planning,
   328→   328→articulation, turn launching), is modulated by an oscillatory gain set jointly by that
   329→   329→person’s internal bodily signals and overall arousal.
   330→   330→2. A production threshold, not only a perceptual gate. The model needs an explicit
   331→   331→path from the speaker’s bodily state to the timing of their output: visceral and arousal state
   332→   332→biases when a planned utterance is launched, so turn-onset latency is partly bodily, not
   333→   333→purely a product of comprehension and planning.
   334→   334→3. Active, not passive, sampling. The arrows run both ways through the body:
   335→   335→interlocutors adjust their bodily state to align high-gain windows with anticipated
   336→   336→informative moments (e.g., a projected turn-end, a gesture stroke), making bodily rhythm
   337→   337→part of prediction and turn-taking rather than a backdrop.
   338→   338→4. Coupling as a dyadic variable. The joint-action link should include inter-individual
   339→   339→physiological coupling (cardiac and arousal synchrony, respiratory entrainment, and their
   340→   340→relation to neural coupling) as a measurable mediator of alignment, alongside the
   341→   341→representational parity current accounts assume.

   346→   346→These components are not only conceptual; each yields concrete, falsifiable predictions, which
   347→   347→we collect in Box 1 to make the proposal testable rather than merely plausible.
   348→   348→Box 1. Testable predictions of the integrated body account
   349→   349→1. Endogenous gain. A gesture, brow-raise, or other facial cue should have a larger effect on
   350→   350→comprehension when it falls in a higher-sensitivity respiratory or cardiac phase of the listener than
   351→   351→when it falls in a lower-sensitivity phase.
   352→   352→2. State-dependent reliance on visual speech. Reliance on visible articulatory cues under
   353→   353→acoustically degraded conditions should be more body-state dependent than reliance under clear-
   354→   354→speech conditions, as the listener leans harder on the visual channel.
   355→   355→3. Production threshold. Turn-onset latency should be predicted, in part, by the speaker’s
   356→   356→respiratory phase, cardiac phase, and pupil-linked arousal in the moments before turn onset, over
   357→   357→and above comprehension and planning variables.
   358→   358→4. Coupling and alignment. Dyads with greater physiological coupling (cardiac and arousal
   359→   359→synchrony, respiratory entrainment) should show tighter conversational alignment, fewer repair
   360→   360→sequences, or better mutual comprehension than dyads with weaker coupling.
   361→   361→5. Coupling as mediator. The strength of body-brain coupling should mediate the relationship
   362→   362→between multimodal cue uptake and inter-individual neural coupling, so that bodily and neural
   363→   363→alignment behave as two views of one system.
   364→   364→These specific additions to the processing picture point, in turn, to a broader consequence.
   365→   365→Perhaps the most important is not any single result but the research space it opens. Treating
   366→   366→each interlocutor’s internal signals as part of the communicative loop turns currently separate
   367→   367→literatures (on interoception and visceral rhythms, on multimodal language, and on
   368→   368→interpersonal coordination) into an highly interdisciplinary and largely unexplored field of
   369→   369→human behaviour, with its own questions, paradigms, and measures still to be defined. In
   370→   370→broad terms the perspective invites work along several axes: how internal signals relate to the
   371→   371→uptake of a partner’s cues and to the planning of one’s own; how this balance differs across
   372→   372→people and develops over the lifespan; and how two bodies’ rhythms relate during live
   373→   373→interaction. Some scaffolding exists: the ecological turn in interoception research (wearable
   374→   374→multimodal recording, lifespan and non-WEIRD sampling, naturalistic paradigms) offers
   375→   375→methods for the within-person and developmental questions⁸, and dynamical accounts of
   376→   376→brain-body trajectories offer the modelling tools for inter-individual questions⁶. Realising it
   377→   377→means bringing together the measurement of bodily signals and the study of multimodal,
   378→   378→interactive language, traditions that rarely meet, and the principal value of this Perspective is
   379→   379→to motivate that convergence.

   407→   407→Conclusion
   408→   408→The embodied, interactive turn has shown the visible body is central to how meaning is built
   409→   409→in conversation. We have argued this turn can be carried inward, to each interlocutor’s
   410→   410→internal body, on both sides of the exchange and across the full family of visceral signals but
   411→   411→with different weights. The brain that converses is housed in a body that beats, digests,
   412→   412→breathes, and modulates its own arousal, and a growing literature shows these signals help set
   413→   413→the gain on incoming evidence and shape the timing of one’s own acts. Treating them as part
   414→   414→of the conversational loop offers candidate mechanisms for the prediction, the processing
   415→   415→demands, and the coordination that the communication-as-joint-action perspective places at
   416→   416→its centre, and enriches the idea of alignment between minds with bodies also coordinating.
   417→   417→More importantly, it opens a largely unexplored agenda: how the body’s internal signals
   418→   418→participate in comprehension, in production, and in their coordination across interlocutors.
   419→   419→On this view, face-to-face conversation is a meeting of two fully embodied minds, grounded
   420→   420→in a shared situational context and each listening and speaking through its own bodily
   421→   421→dynamics.
