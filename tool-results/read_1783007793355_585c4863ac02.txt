   201→   201→Sixty years later, we gave the assessment of n Sex via thematic apperception another try.
   202→   202→In doing so, we aimed to benefit from lessons learned in past research by using straightforward
   203→   203→procedures for arousing sexual motivation, employing picture cues that are clearly suggestive of,
   204→   204→although not prescriptive for, sexual themes, and identifying those aspects of narrative imagery
   205→   205→that are consistently sensitive to arousal of sexual motivation.
   206→   206→We adopted an EMMIC validation approach in our work, with the acronym standing for
   207→   207→experimental manipulation, measure, indicator, and criterion (Schultheiss et al., submitted). This
   208→   208→approach is based on McClelland’s (1958, 1987) concept of validity as sensitivity of a measure
   209→   209→to variations in the thing it measures. Such a view of validity as causal validity has also re-
   210→   210→emerged in modern validity theory (Borsboom et al., 2004; Bornstein, 2011), which puts a
   211→   211→premium on the demonstration of a causal link between variations of an attribute or process and
   212→   212→its measure. This understanding of validity as sensitivity to experimental manipulation was the
   213→   213→basis and cardinal feature of all research on implicit motives, starting with Atkinson and
   214→   214→McClelland’s development of a thematic apperceptive measure of hunger motivation based on
   215→   215→variations in food deprivation in research participants. However, Schultheiss et al (submitted)
   216→   216→also suggested that for such a sensitive measure to be valid as a measure of motivation, it must be
   217→   217→demonstrated that experimentally elicited changes in thematic imagery are mediated by an
   218→   218→independent indicator of motivation. Based on a broad consensus in modern theories of
   219→   219→motivation, Schultheiss et al. argued that changes in affect may represent such a core indicator of
   220→   220→motivation. Finally, Schultheiss et al also suggested that for a measure to be useful, it should
   221→   221→predict relevant outcomes or criteria, and that the variance portion of the measure that is
   222→   222→sensitive to experimental arousal is also the one that is associated with the criterion.
   223→   223→
   224→   224→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 11
   225→   225→Thus, in our development of a valid n Sex measure, we intended to demonstrate not only
   226→   226→that the measure is sensitive to experimental arousal of sexual motivation and therefore has
   227→   227→causal validity, but that this arousal effect is mediated by changes in subjective (self-reported
   228→   228→arousal) and objective, physiological measures of affect (corrugator and zygomatic activation;
   229→   229→pupil dilation) and in turn mediates effects of experimental variations on a relevant outcome
   230→   230→measure, key pressing for extended viewing of erotic pictures. In addition, we took the following
   231→   231→issues into consideration in our research:
   232→   232→Coding system and n Sex score derivation
   233→   233→Inductive approach. As suggested by Boyatzis (1998), we used an inductive, bottom-up
   234→   234→approach to identify a broad array of narrative themes from the picture stories we collected. We
   235→   235→aimed at devising a comprehensive, specific, and differentiated coding system that captured a
   236→   236→broad range of themes in participants’ stories that could be relevant for sexual motivation and
   237→   237→thus represent potential “signal carriers” for causal effects of motivational arousal on thematic
   238→   238→apperception. In doing so, we considered both positive, approach-oriented and negative,
   239→   239→avoidance-oriented or conflictual imagery in participants’ stories, because we were interested in
   240→   240→if and how experimental manipulation of approach versus avoidance aspects of sexual motivation
   241→   241→would differentially impact story themes.
   242→   242→Complementing deductive approach. We complemented this inductive approach with a
   243→   243→deductive, top-down approach in the form of the sequence model of motivated action employed
   244→   244→in the development of many other motive coding systems. The sequence model posits a person
   245→   245→harboring a need, positive and negative expectations, and responding with positive or negative
   246→   246→affect to the process and outcome of goal pursuit. The person engages in instrumental activity
   247→   247→
   248→   248→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 12
   249→   249→aimed towards (or away from) the goal, which in the end is reached (or not). On the way to the
   250→   250→goal, the person may encounter obstacles within her- or himself or in the environment. The
   251→   251→model helped us to classify and systematize the narrative themes we identified through the
   252→   252→inductive, bottom-up approach in the stories. However, in keeping with the inductive approach,
   253→   253→we kept an open mind for identifying and describing themes that did not fit the sequence model
   254→   254→and added those to the coding system, too.
   255→   255→Intercoder reliability as prerequisite for testing effects of experimental manipulation.
   256→   256→Boyatzis (1998) emphasized that high intercoder reliability is a critical prerequisite for a
   257→   257→meaningful test of whether a given coding category is also sensitive to arousal. We therefore first
   258→   258→ensured that coders could identify all types of imagery with sufficient intercoder reliability
   259→   259→before testing individual imagery categories for experimental arousal effects.
   260→   260→Sensitivity criteria for detecting positive and negative arousal effects. We classified
   261→   261→coding categories as sensitive to experimental arousal if they (a) increased at least 1 percentage
   262→   262→point in the sexual-arousal condition and (b) this increase was at least 2 percentage points greater
   263→   263→than in the control condition (Hinzmann et al., 2023). In studies that featured a negative arousal
   264→   264→condition, we added the stipulation that the score should not decrease more than 1 percentage
   265→   265→point in the negative arousal group (Schultheiss et al., 2023). Thus, we based our classification
   266→   266→not on an inferential-statistics criterion, which would vary with sample size and face challenges
   267→   267→due to the infrequency of many types of imagery, but on the absolute criterion of a minimum
   268→   268→increase. The second criterion of a contrast with the control group was aimed to ensure that we
   269→   269→did not capture an unspecific effect (e.g., scores rising in both the arousal and control groups). At
   270→   270→the same time, we did not capitalize on scores decreasing in the control group, because
   271→   271→demonstrating an experimental manipulation effect on a measure primarily by exploiting a score
   272→   272→
   273→   273→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 13
   274→   274→reduction in the control condition would be a nonsensical and misleading criterion for measure
   275→   275→validation.
   276→   276→Emphasis on within-person changes. For the same reason, we focused on within-subject
   277→   277→changes in the sexual-motivation arousal groups as our primary estimate of the sensitivity of the
   278→   278→n Sex measure to experimental arousal of sexual motivation and did not put emphasis on a post-
   279→   279→manipulation difference between arousal and control groups or its effect size. The magnitude of
   280→   280→the latter difference may be due to other factors besides an experimental arousal effect – for
   281→   281→instance, it may reflect preexisting group differences in motivation even before the experimental
   282→   282→manipulation took place. Exploiting such differences by focusing on post-manipulation
   283→   283→differences between experimental and control groups could thus be misleading with regard to the
   284→   284→measure’s validity as conceived as its within-person sensitivity to motivational arousal.
   285→   285→Picture cue selection
   286→   286→Devising sets of high-relevance cues. We compiled two sets of 8 pictures each (X and Y; 16
   287→   287→pictures in total). All pictures were chosen so that a woman and a man were depicted in an
   288→   288→intimate situation whose outcome could be sex, but also something other than sex. All depicted
   289→   289→persons were clothed, albeit some in underwear. For instance, one picture showed a couple
   290→   290→engaging in a pillow fight in bed. Another showed a man with his hand at the zipper of the back
   291→   291→of a woman’s dress. Thus, the selected pictures correspond to what Strizver (1961) would have
   292→   292→classified as high-relevance cues. Still, we made sure that they were merely suggestive of sexual
   293→   293→activity, but not prescriptive for such a story theme. Each set also included at least one “anchor”
   294→   294→picture that had been used in previous research on motives and that was deemed to be suitable
   295→   295→for the assessment of n Sex (e.g., nightclub scene; trapeze artists; see Schultheiss & Pang, 2007).
   296→   296→
   297→   297→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 14

   299→   299→affiliation, served as a gauge with regard to how comparable in terms of general motivation the
   300→   300→samples we tested were to populations studied in earlier research.
   301→   301→Decomposing picture and arousal effects. Each picture set was used once with each of
   302→   302→three arousal techniques (see below), and each arousal method was tested in two studies, one for
   303→   303→each picture set. This resulted in a cross-study 3 (arousal method) x 2 (picture set) meta-design
   304→   304→that allowed us to decompose arousal effects that are due to method and arousal effects that are
   305→   305→due to picture suitability. The design also allowed us to test whether imagery changes found with
   306→   306→on picture set would robustly replicate for changes found with a different set, thus helping us to
   307→   307→ensure that whatever tell-tale changes in thematic apperception we identified as a function of
   308→   308→motivational arousal would not be specific to a particular set of picture cues.
   309→   309→Identification of highly sensitive picture cues. Although this remains work yet to be done,
   310→   310→our pool of 16 pictures divided into two sets will allow us to identify those pictures that are most
   311→   311→sensitive for the assessment of n Sex. We define picture sensitivity as a picture’s ability to pick
   312→   312→up motivational arousal effects (i.e., eliciting low-imagery stories under neutral and high-
   313→   313→imagery stories under arousal conditions). We assume that a picture that is more sensitive to
   314→   314→experimentally induced variations in sexual motivation will also be more sensitive in detecting
   315→   315→interindividual differences in dispositional n Sex and hence contribute, in combination with a
   316→   316→valid coding system, to the overall validity of n Sex assessment. A secondary, auxiliary criterion
   317→   317→for picture selection is the overall mean of n Sex scores it elicits, with a higher mean being
   318→   318→preferable. The ultimate goal is the identification and selection of the three to five most suitable
   319→   319→pictures into a picture story exercise (PSE) that measures n Sex with exquisite sensitivity to the
   320→   320→

   322→   322→motivational state of the story narrator and sufficient differentiation between individuals to be
   323→   323→suitable as a measure of a person’s current motive state and predictor of relevant outcomes.
   324→   324→Experimental design
   325→   325→Mixed-model design. In all studies, we used an experimental design in which effects of
   326→   326→arousal versus control conditions, varied between subjects, are measured as changes in sexual
   327→   327→imagery in picture stories from before (T1) to after the manipulation (T2) within each subject
   328→   328→(see Figure 1). This mixed-model design, first introduced into motive research by Uleman (1972)
   329→   329→and frequently employed in research since then (e.g., Schultheiss et al., 2004; Wiemers et al.,
   330→   330→2015; Wirth & Schultheiss, 2006), guards against capitalizing on initial differences in the
   331→   331→experimental groups (because they are measured and thus controlled) and allows for the
   332→   332→estimation of magnitude and direction of motivational changes separately in experimental and
   333→   333→control groups.
   334→   334→Picture-set balancing. If changes in motivation are measured within-subjects, one picture-
   335→   335→story test needs to be administered before and one after the manipulation. Administering the
   336→   336→same set of pictures twice may incur problems of boredom or unnatural responses (e.g., Lundy,
   337→   337→1985), and administering one set of pictures always before and another set of pictures always
   338→   338→after the manipulation is bound to yield pre-post differences that are simply due to between-set
   339→   339→differences in cue properties. Both issues would make a detection and/or meaningful
   340→   340→interpretation of differential T1-T2 changes in experimental and control groups difficult or even
   341→   341→impossible. We therefore split the set of 8 pictures we used in each study into 2 four-picture
   342→   342→subsets, A and B, and presented one before and one after the manipulation in balanced order, AB
   343→   343→or BA, and orthogonally to the experimental condition. This ensured that whatever differential
   344→   344→
   345→   345→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 16
   346→   346→changes in sexual imagery we observed in the arousal versus control conditions could not be
   347→   347→attributed to systematic picture set effects.
   348→   348→Validity indicators. Our focal dependent variable was the change that arousal of sexual
   349→   349→motivation elicited in various types of imagery in participants’ picture stories. Conversely, we
   350→   350→considered as valid measures of sexual motivation those imagery categories that showed the
   351→   351→requisite changes according to the criterion described above. However, this manner of
   352→   352→conceptualizing validity as sensitivity (McClelland, 1958) or causal validity (Borsboom et al.,
   353→   353→2004; Schultheiss et al., submitted) is vulnerable to overfitting and circularity. Overfitting refers
   354→   354→to the danger of selecting those imagery categories that respond sensitively to the arousal
   355→   355→conditions of a given experiment with its specific arousal method and sample of participants. It
   356→   356→may therefore not generalize to other arousal methods or samples. We countered this validity
   357→   357→threat by aiming to identify those content categories that turned out to be sensitive to
   358→   358→motivational arousal across multiple samples and arousal procedures. This is the main focus of
   359→   359→the present chapter. Circularity refers to the problem that the effect of the experimental
   360→   360→manipulation (changes in sexual imagery) is taken as proof that the manipulation had the desired
   361→   361→effect (arousing sexual motivation). However, such an approach to validity cannot rule out that
   362→   362→imagery changes are due to other effects of the experimental manipulation, such as the arousal of
   363→   363→another than the targeted motive, a change in cognitive set, an effect of language used in the
   364→   364→experimental manipulation priming similar language in picture stories, but without really altering
   365→   365→motivation, and so on.
   366→   366→To evade the circularity problem, we therefore followed the EMMIC approach and assessed
   367→   367→in all studies an independent indicator of sexual motivation: changes in (sexual) affect. We did
   368→   368→this using both self-report measures of general affect (energization, tension, hedonic
   369→   369→
   370→   370→NEED FOR SEX MEASURE 17
   371→   371→tone/valence) and specific affect (sexual arousal) as well as, in some studies, physiological
   372→   372→measures of hedonic responses (positive: zygomatic muscle changes; negative: corrugator
   373→   373→muscle changes) and arousal (pupil dilation). We thus also aimed for indicator validity; that is,
   374→   374→for the demonstration that our experimental manipulations impacted affect and that changes in
   375→   375→affect mediated the effect of the manipulation on changes in sexual imagery expressed in picture
   376→   376→stories.
   377→   377→We complemented the requirements for causal and indicator validity with a requirement for
   378→   378→criterion validity, such that changes in the n Sex measure mediate effects of the experimental
   379→   379→manipulation on a relevant criterion. The EMMIC concept of criterion validity differs from the
   380→   380→classical concept of the same name in that a mere correlation between measure and criterion is
   381→   381→not enough. It must be demonstrated through mediation analysis that the variance in the measure
   382→   382→that is sensitive to motivational arousal is the same that also predicts the outcome. In our
   383→   383→research program, we administered a key-press task (KPT) as a criterion measure. This task
   384→   384→assessed how frequently participants pressed a key to increase (or decrease) exposure to erotic
   385→   385→pictures, relative to key pressing for neutral pictures alternating with erotic pictures. Overall
   386→   386→viewing time across all picture presentations was held constant at 4 min. Thus, only the
   387→   387→proportion of erotic picture exposure relative to non-erotic picture exposure could vary, not the
   388→   388→absolute duration.
