    33‚ÜíAbstract
    34‚ÜíGambling Disorder is a significant public health and clinical issue requiring precise and reliable
    35‚Üímeasurement instruments for effective screening, assessment, diagnosis, and treatment outcome
    36‚Üíevaluation. This narrative review, incorporating a realist rapid review phase, examined and categorised
    37‚Üímeasurement tools for Gambling Disorder in adults, distinguishing their uses and appropriate contexts
    38‚Üíto provide practical guidance for clinicians, researchers, commissioners, and policy-makers. We
    39‚Üíconducted searches across PubMed, PsycInfo, Scopus, Medline, and Google Scholar, focusing on peer-
    40‚Üíreviewed studies that evaluated psychometric properties, diagnostic accuracy, and/or practical
    41‚Üíapplication of Gambling Disorder measurement instruments. Findings were categorised as relating to
    42‚Üíscreening, diagnosis, or treatment outcome (for the latter including symptom severity,
    43‚Üíprocesses/mechanisms, and recovery). We identified a range of instruments developed for different
    44‚Üípurposes and validated in diverse contexts, including brief and extensive screening tools, diagnostic
    45‚Üíinterviews, and measures of treatment response. The review highlights key considerations for
    46‚Üíinstrument selection: sensitivity and specificity vary substantially across different settings (general
    47‚Üípopulation versus enriched clinical samples), with implications for prevalence estimation and clinical
    48‚Üídecision-making. We provide evidence-based recommendations for optimal instrument choice in
    49‚Üíspecific contexts, including for: quantification of prevalence in the general population, screening in
    50‚Üíenriched settings (e.g. certain types of outpatient clinic), diagnosis, severity and treatment response.
    51‚ÜíThe review also identifies critical knowledge and methodological gaps, including limited validation
    52‚Üíacross diverse populations and lack of gold-standard comparisons, which should be addressed in future
    53‚Üíwork.
    54‚ÜíKeywords: gambling; assessment; gambling instruments; screening; measurement; reliability;
    55‚Üívalidity

    58‚ÜíIntroduction
    59‚ÜíGambling Disorder (previously known as ‚Äòpathological gambling‚Äô) is a psychiatric disorder
    60‚Üícharacterised by persistent and recurrent problematic gambling behaviour, leading to clinically
    61‚Üísignificant impairment and/or distress [1]. The prevalence of Gambling Disorder has been
    62‚Üíestimated to be 1.3% worldwide in adults, but reports range from 0.1% to 5.8% depending on
    63‚Üíthe country [2, 3]. Prevalence rates can be influenced by ecological variables such as gambling
    64‚Üíavailability and exposure (e.g. advertising), plus legislation and regulation in different
    65‚Üígeographical jurisdictions. Prevalence rates can also be influenced by measurement methods,
    66‚Üíincluding variation in the instruments used for quantification as well as any bias that can arise
    67‚Üíwhen instruments are translated across languages. In recent years, there has been growing
    68‚Üípublic health, clinical, and policy (e.g. governmental) attention towards Gambling Disorder.
    69‚ÜíFor example, Gambling Disorder is now a priority in the UK National Health Service (NHS),
    70‚Üíand a number of new clinical services have opened in England [4, 5]. Given the need to obtain
    71‚Üíaccurate prevalence rates of Gambling Disorder, and growing numbers of clinics seeking to
    72‚Üítreat Gambling Disorder globally, it is timely to review available measurement instruments for
    73‚Üíeffective identification, diagnosis, and treatment evaluation [3].
    74‚ÜíTerminology relating to gambling can be confusing and at times terms have been conflated in
    75‚Üíthe clinical and research literature. Table 1 provides an overview of commonly used terms and
    76‚Üítheir working definitions. The terms problem/harmful gambling, at-risk gambling, gambling
    77‚Üíharms, and Gambling Disorder ‚Äì although having different definitions ‚Äì are all associated with
    78‚Üídifficulties in controlling (or limiting) time spent gambling and/or money spent gambling,
    79‚Üíleading to negative consequences [6]. Problem/harmful gambling, at-risk gambling, and
    80‚ÜíGambling Disorder can include features of behavioural dependence (e.g. needing to gamble
    81‚Üíwith larger amounts of money to get the same effects; loss of control), and negative
    82‚Üíconsequences (e.g. feelings of guilt, stress or anxiety) but Gambling Disorder provides the
    83‚Üíformal diagnosis based on objective symptoms listed in formal diagnostic manuals [1, 7]. At-
    84‚Üírisk gambling most often refers to gambling meeting some but not all diagnostic criteria, and
    85‚Üíis subdivided by some researchers into lower-risk and moderate-risk gambling (though it is
    86‚Üínow known that even ‚Äòlower-risk‚Äô gambling can be linked to major impairment and many
    87‚Üícomorbidities) [8].
    88‚Üí3
    89‚Üí
    90‚ÜíIn contrast, the term ‚Äògambling-related harms‚Äô refers to any initial or exacerbated direct or
    91‚Üíindirect adverse consequence due to gambling, which includes impact on individuals who
    92‚Üígamble, but also the broader societal impact of gambling, such as effects on family members
    93‚Üíand negative economic impact [6, 9-11]. The recent UK National Institute for Health and Care
    94‚ÜíExcellence (NICE) guideline focused on ‚Äògambling-related harms‚Äô to encapsulate the broader
    95‚Üíconcept [12]. While Gambling Disorder refers to the diagnosable psychiatric disorder, it is
    96‚Üíimportant to note that a much greater number of people experience gambling-related harms. At
    97‚Üíthe same time, the advantage of focusing on Gambling Disorder is that it is a formally
    98‚Üídiagnosable disorder and that most clinical trials of interventions have examined this disorder
    99‚Üírather than broader constructs. The definition of ‚ÄòGambling Disorder‚Äô allows precision in
   100‚Üíoperationalisation and when considering prior literature and aligns directly with both mental
   101‚Üíhealth definition manuals. Therefore, the primary aim of the current paper is to consider
   102‚Üíinstruments useful in the context of Gambling Disorder.

   129‚Üíindividuals affected by gambling, it is important to use non-stigmatising and person-first

   132‚ÜíIn this narrative review, incorporating a realist rapid review phase, we examined existing
   133‚Üíliterature to address the different instruments used for screening (brief and longer screening
   134‚Üímeasures), diagnosis, and treatment outcome (symptom severity, processes of change, and
   135‚Üírecovery) for Gambling Disorder. We focused on instruments for adults (for a review of
   136‚Üíadolescent measures see [13]). We aimed to (1) distil the properties of instruments for assessing
   137‚ÜíGambling Disorder into an easy reference format to inform future decision-making; (2)
   138‚Üídescribe the currently most commonly used instruments in more detail; (3) use this information
   139‚Üíto make concrete, simple recommendations about which measures to use in particular settings;
   140‚Üíand (4) highlight directions for future research.
   141‚ÜíThe need to differentiate between instruments for screening, diagnosis, and treatment
   142‚Üíoutcomes
   143‚ÜíIn the clinical management of Gambling Disorder, the processes of screening, diagnosis, and
   144‚Üítreatment outcomes evaluation are typically carried out sequentially. Different stages of an
   145‚Üíindividual‚Äôs ‚Äúclinical journey‚Äù need different instruments [14]. Initially, individuals who may
   146‚Üíhave the disorder can be identified through formal or informal screening methods. Once
   147‚Üípotential cases are identified, a more thorough diagnostic evaluation can be conducted to
   148‚Üíconfirm the presence of Gambling Disorder. Following confirmation of any diagnosis, there
   149‚Üíshould then be assessment of treatment response in due course. It is important to be mindful of
   150‚Üíthe most appropriate instruments for each step of this process and that no one instrument suits
   151‚Üíall purposes. However, the distinction between these phases and requisite instruments is not
   152‚Üíalways clear in the Gambling Disorder literature, and indeed in clinical practice: there is
   153‚Üíconsiderable conflation between instruments used in vivo for screening, those used for
   154‚Üídiagnosis, and those used for assessing treatment response [15]. It is not uncommon for the
   155‚Üísame instruments to be used interchangeably and inappropriately, which can complicate both
   156‚Üíclinical practice and research and lead to inaccurate measurement and invalid conclusions. This
   157‚Üí5
   158‚Üí
   159‚Üíblurring of roles of different instruments underscores the need for a more structured approach
   160‚Üíin selecting and applying measurement instruments across different stages. Put differently, it
   161‚Üíis not uncommon for Gambling Disorder instruments to be used with insufficient consideration
   162‚Üíof their validity for the specific intended use ‚Äì for example, using a screening instrument to
   163‚Üímake a ‚Äòdiagnosis‚Äô in a clinical setting or an assessment instrument for ‚Äòtreatment response‚Äô
   164‚Üíwhen it was never intended or validated for this purpose.
   165‚ÜíThese complexities in measurement are further reflected in prevalence studies, where the
   166‚Üívariability in the performance of different screening and diagnostic instruments often leads to
   167‚Üísignificant challenges [2]. Research has indicated that different gambling screening
   168‚Üíinstruments can yield divergent prevalence rates even when applied to the same population [3,
   169‚Üí16]. For example, the South Oaks Gambling Screen (SOGS) has been known to produce higher
   170‚ÜíGambling Disorder prevalence rates compared to some other instruments due to its relatively
   171‚Üíhigher tendency to generate false positives (i.e. over-identify clinical cases in those who do not
   172‚Üífulfil full criteria for the disorder) [3, 17, 18]. This type of inconsistency can undermine the
   173‚Üíreliability of clinical prevalence estimates. Additionally, instruments such as the Problem
   174‚ÜíGambling Severity Index (PGSI), while effective in some enriched populations for the
   175‚Üíidentification of more severe gambling issues in a non-specific fashion, may not adequately
   176‚Üídifferentiate between milder but clinically important manifestations of gambling problems, and
   177‚Üíis not consistent with current diagnostic criteria [19, 20]. Another layer of complexity arises
   178‚Üífrom the lack of thorough psychometric validation of existing instruments in different cultural
   179‚Üícontexts (e.g. gambling behaviours differ in different cultures across the globe) and population
   180‚Üísettings (e.g. general population vs. primary care screening vs. help-seeking samples, whether
   181‚Üíor not validated for both in-person and online gambling). Variability in psychometric properties
   182‚Üíacross populations can lead to inconsistencies in classification and severity thresholds [21, 22].
   183‚ÜíMoreover, the time frame used in assessments such as past-year versus lifetime gambling can
   184‚Üíalso significantly impact the reported prevalence, with past-year rates unsurprisingly lower
   185‚Üíthan lifetime prevalence [16, 23]. Relatedly, it is important to be aware that symptoms of
   186‚ÜíGambling Disorder (including severity) can fluctuate considerably over time in a given
   187‚Üíindividual; this is why current diagnostic criteria refer to symptoms considered typically over
   188‚Üíthe preceding year [24]. These challenges highlight the need for careful selection and
   189‚Üírefinement of measurement instruments to ensure accurate and culturally sensitive assessments
   190‚Üíin Gambling Disorder prevalence studies.

   323‚ÜíMethods
   324‚ÜíThe aim of this paper was to address a critical gap in the research and clinical literature, plus
   325‚Üídevelop guidance to inform practice and policy, by conducting a narrative review incorporating
   326‚Üía realist-review phase, examining measurement tools as applied to Gambling Disorder in
   327‚Üíadults. In terms of narrative, we focused primarily on application in the context of Gambling
   328‚ÜíDisorder primarily, because this is a clinically defined condition, and most psychometric
   329‚Üíevidence comes from clinical rather than non-clinical definitions. At the same time, we also
   330‚Üítook into consideration wider literature to ensure comprehensiveness (e.g. tools mainly used in
   331‚Üíbroader literature but potentially useful in Gambling Disorder). Findings were categorised as
   332‚Üírelating to screening, diagnosis, or treatment outcome; for treatment outcome, subdivisions
   333‚Üíwere symptom severity, processes/mechanisms underlying treatment, and recovery. We also
   334‚Üítouched on broader assessments of gambling-related harms that could be relevant to measure
   335‚Üíin the context of Gambling Disorder, taking into account lived experience perspectives (see
   336‚Üílater).
   337‚ÜíConsideration of choice of instruments as Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) for clinical
   338‚Üíservices was out of scope of this paper, since in the absence of concrete evidence the most
   339‚Üíappropriate methodology to determine these is expert consensus (for example, through a
   340‚Üíscientific process such as Delphi) rather than through a rapid review. Similarly, use of
   341‚Üíinstruments to direct choice of clinical pathways at the level of individual patients (e.g. brief
   342‚Üíintervention versus longer-term intensive psychotherapy) or to assign resources, was out of
   343‚Üíscope, due to absence of clear evidence. Detection of comorbid mental and physical health
   344‚Üícomorbidities was also outside the scope of this review ‚Äì while important, detection of
   345‚Üícomorbidities is a complex area that would merit a large paper in its own right due to the
   346‚Üíbreadth of such conditions (e.g. depression, anxiety, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder,
   347‚Üíimpulsive disorders, compulsive disorders, other addictions, insomnia, other sleep disorders,
   348‚Üíand obesity, etc.) and also due to the complexity of determining interplay between different
   349‚Üíconditions.
   350‚ÜíOur review incorporated two sequential research phases: in phase 1, we conducted a narrative
   351‚Üíreview using existing systematic reviews, key handbook sources, expert knowledge, and
   352‚Üítargeted database searches across PubMed, PsycInfo, Scopus and Google Scholar to identify
   353‚Üírelevant instruments; in phase 2, we conducted a realist rapid review. The reason for this two-
   354‚Üíphase approach is that we had conducted preliminary work, but additional resources became
   355‚Üí11
   356‚Üí
   357‚Üíavailable via United Kingdom Research and Innovation (UKRI) to support rapid review
   358‚Üímethodology. The rapid review phase also ensured that Phase 1 had not overlooked any critical
   359‚Üíinstruments of relevance and in-scope.
   360‚ÜíEligibility Criteria for both Phases
   361‚ÜíStudies were included if they evaluated the psychometric properties, diagnostic accuracy, or
   362‚Üípractical application of Gambling Disorder/pathological gambling/problem gambling and
   363‚Üígambling-related harm measurement instruments. No restriction was placed on article type;
   364‚Üíprimary psychometric validation studies, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses were all
   365‚Üíconsidered eligible sources. Only peer-reviewed publications were included, with a focus on
   366‚Üíadult instruments (adolescent-specific instruments were excluded unless they had validated
   367‚Üíadult adaptations). Non-English publications were excluded. Additionally, instruments not
   368‚Üípsychometrically validated in English were excluded, irrespective of the publication language
   369‚Üíof the source article, as English-language validation was considered a prerequisite for
   370‚Üíapplicability within the scope of this review. Instruments not directly assessing Gambling
   371‚ÜíDisorder/pathological gambling/problem gambling or gambling-related harms were also
   372‚Üíexcluded. An exception was made for general quality of life and functional impairment
   373‚Üíinstruments that have been applied in Gambling Disorder research, included on the basis that
   374‚Üíno psychometrically validated gambling-specific quality of life measure meeting the above
   375‚Üícriteria exists; these instruments are discussed narratively rather than subjected to formal
   376‚Üíextraction.
   377‚ÜíSearch Strategy and Data Extraction
   378‚ÜíWe conducted an initial narrative literature review in July 2024 (Phase 1), searching and
   379‚Üíscreening across multiple databases, with no time limit, to identify relevant instruments with
   380‚Üíreported psychometric data. No formal systematic protocol was applied at this stage, consistent
   381‚Üíwith the narrative review design.
   382‚ÜíData were extracted into a structured form, capturing: instrument characteristics (purpose,
   383‚Üínumber of items, mode of administration); psychometric performance (sensitivity, specificity,
   384‚Üípredictive values, internal consistency, test-retest reliability, validity); context of validation
   385‚Üí(population type: clinical vs. general population); and practical considerations (length, ease of
   386‚Üíuse, training requirements).
   387‚Üí12
   388‚Üí
   389‚ÜíWe then conducted a realist rapid review (Phase 2) in February 2026, undertaken by a
   390‚Üíknowledge specialist with rapid review experience (KF) and a subject specialist (JeS),
   391‚Üísearching Medline, PsycInfo and Scopus, and focusing on papers published since 2014. The
   392‚Üíaim of the search was to update the literature with any relevant studies or measures not
   393‚Üíidentified in Phase 1. The knowledge specialist developed a search strategy including APA
   394‚Üícontrolled vocabulary (for PsycInfo), Medical Subject Headings (MeSH for Medline) and
   395‚Üínatural language terms (Medline, PsycInfo and Scopus).
   396‚ÜíSearch terms were validated through both testing by the knowledge specialist and submission
   397‚Üíto the wider project team for agreement from subject specialists, in accordance with Cochrane
   398‚Üírapid review guidance. The search terms for Phase 2 are shown in the Appendix. In brief, search
   399‚Üíterms explored Gambling Disorder, problem gambling, pathological gambling, gambling
   400‚Üíassessment, gambling instruments, measurement, assessment instruments, screening,
   401‚Üídiagnosis, and psychometric validation.
   402‚ÜíOut of a total of 329 references found in phase 2, 118 were screened using title, abstract and
   403‚Üíquick readings to determine the population, age group and relevance to Gambling Disorder,
   404‚Üífollowing automated and manually checked deduplication via reference management software.
   405‚ÜíSifting was done by KF initially, with JeS then cross-checking KF‚Äôs selections with his own.
   406‚ÜíAny suggested amendments to the list of included articles were discussed and agreed between
   407‚Üíboth reviewers, in accordance with current rapid review guidance.
   408‚ÜíOf the 118 screened, 13 were found relevant and put forward for full-text review by the wider
   409‚Üíproject team, with 12 possible additional measurement tools identified. After close reading by
   410‚Üíothers on the project team, it was determined that these scales fell outside the scope of the

   414‚Üíwere extracted from the Phase 2 results. This confirmed that Phase 1 had identified the relevant
   415‚Üíliterature, and that there had not been major new advances subsequently. This approach was
   416‚Üíconsistent with rapid review methodology, where targeted updating searches are used
   417‚Üípragmatically to identify any major gaps in previously identified evidence.

   459‚ÜíScreening Instruments
   460‚ÜíThis section considers instruments designed for the initial identification of possible Gambling
   461‚ÜíDisorder. We consider shorter (1-5 items) and longer (>5 items) instruments [25]. A subset of
   462‚Üíthe longer screening instruments has sometimes been used in the literature as measures of
   463‚Üíbaseline gambling severity, or in treatment evaluation studies, often with questionable validity
   464‚Üífor these purposes. Brief instruments are more useful in situations with limited time availability
   465‚Üíto screen for Gambling Disorder (e.g. where many other disorders may also be screened), such
   466‚Üías in primary care settings, as well as in multi-purpose research studies (cross-sectional and
   467‚Üílongitudinal) where very limited space is available to evaluate a particular condition. It is
   468‚Üíimportant to note that a consequence of using brief screening instruments can be a loss of
   469‚Üírelevant information, as well as a limitation of accurate detection of underlying disorder.
   470‚ÜíLonger instruments tend to be used in gambling-focused services and research [25, 29]. Note
   471‚Üíthat self-report instruments assessing diagnostic criteria were included (e.g. from DSM) in this
   472‚Üísection, rather than in the later 'Diagnosis' section, on the basis that formal diagnosis requires
   473‚Üíclinician input.
   474‚ÜíBrief instruments
   475‚ÜíThe NODS-CLiP is a brief, three-item screening instrument derived from the original 17-item
   476‚ÜíNODS [30, 31]. It was designed for use in the general population as a brief screening instrument
   477‚Üíand was validated in large general population samples through telephone and in-person surveys
   478‚Üíinvolving over 17,000 participants in the U.S. [30]. It was developed to address the challenges
   479‚Üíposed by the length and complexity of the original NODS, making it more practical for rapid
   480‚Üíscreening in clinical settings [25, 32]. The NODS-CLiP consists of three key questions related
   481‚Üíto Control, Lying, and Preoccupation. An alternative version, the NODS-PERC, was later
   482‚Üídeveloped to include an additional item (Escape), which showed good psychometric properties
   483‚Üí[33]. In situations where the base prevalence rate of Gambling Disorder is high, such as in
   484‚Üíprisons and treatment programmes for alcohol and substance use disorder, the NODS-PERC is
   485‚Üírecommended over NODS-CLiP due to its higher sensitivity [33]. In contrast, in situations
   486‚Üíwhere the base prevalence rate is low, the NODS-CLiP is preferable, as it was originally
   487‚Üídeveloped based on its performance in general population samples [30, 33]. In a meta-analysis,
   488‚Üí15
   489‚Üí
   490‚Üíthe NODS-CLiP and NODS-PERC were found to have the most substantial putative diagnostic
   491‚Üídata among the 20 screening instruments evaluated, particularly in terms of sensitivity and
   492‚Üíspecificity [25]. Both instruments demonstrated high accuracy for identifying problem and at-
   493‚Üírisk gambling, in the samples for which they were studied. While data on concurrent and
   494‚Üípredictive validity are not consistently available across all instruments, the NODS-CLiP and
   495‚ÜíNODS-PERC have been shown to have strong positive and negative predictive values in the
   496‚Üíexamined samples, as noted in Table 4 [25].
   497‚ÜíThe Brief Problem Gambling Screen (BPGS; 2-5 item versions) was developed for the purpose
   498‚Üíof screening for problem gambling in both clinical settings and population research [34]. In
   499‚Üíone study comparing nine brief screening instruments, the five-item BPGS was found to have
   500‚Üíthe highest sensitivity for identifying gambling problems across all risk levels -low-risk,
   501‚Üímoderate-risk, and problem gambling- on the PGSI, although the PGSI is not considered a gold
   502‚Üíreference standard [32]. Although the BPGS itself uses a simple binary scoring system (yes/no)
   503‚Üíto indicate problem gambling, it demonstrated strong positive predictive value in detecting
   504‚Üípatients across the full spectrum of gambling behaviour in the examined setting [32]. However,
   505‚Üíits reliance on a single response metric may limit its ability to make fine distinctions between
   506‚Üíseverity levels on its own, which should be considered when interpreting results. Another
   507‚Üísystematic review and meta-analysis, which assessed 20 different screening instruments across
   508‚Üívarious settings and age groups, identified the BPGS-2 (two-item BPGS) as one of the five
   509‚Üíinstruments with satisfactory accuracy for both Gambling Disorder and at-risk gambling (i.e.,
   510‚Üí‚Äòsub-clinical or sub-threshold cases in which individuals exhibit some symptoms of problem
   511‚Üígambling but not enough to be classified in the problem or Gambling Disorder/pathological
   512‚Üígambling categories‚Äô) [25].
   513‚ÜíLonger instruments
   514‚ÜíOne of the best known and most widely used gambling screening instruments is the South Oaks
   515‚ÜíGambling Screen (SOGS), which was originally developed by Lesieur and Blume in 1987
   516‚Üíbased on the DSM-III pathological gambling criteria to screen for pathological gambling in
   517‚Üíclinical settings [18, 35]. This 20-item instrument which assesses lifetime gambling behaviours
   518‚Üíwas designed to identify individuals with pathological gambling, with a score of 5 or more
   519‚Üíindicating a likely diagnosis. Its ease of administration, whether through interviews, self-report,
   520‚Üíor computer-administrated methods, quickly established SOGS as the dominant measure for
   521‚ÜíPPG/Gambling Disorder in various research contexts, including clinical and epidemiological
   522‚Üí16
   523‚Üí
   524‚Üístudies [36, 37]. The SOGS has also been quite often used in clinical contexts with some
   525‚Üívalidation thereof [18, 38].
   526‚ÜíDespite its popularity, the SOGS has faced criticism, particularly regarding its propensity to
   527‚Üíyield false positives, which can result in inflated prevalence rates of Gambling Disorder when
   528‚Üíused as a categorical diagnostic instrument [3, 17, 29]. This issue has persisted even under the
   529‚Üírevised DSM-5 criteria, although the incidence of false positives appears to be somewhat
   530‚Üíreduced compared to the DSM-IV criteria [36]. In efforts to address this issue, recent analyses
   531‚Üísuggest modifying the SOGS cut-off scores depending on the context of its use. Specifically, a
   532‚Üícut-off of 8 is recommended for clinical screening, while a higher cut-off of 12 is advocated
   533‚Üífor prevalence rate studies and pseudo-experimental studies to reduce the risk of false positives
   534‚Üíeven more [36], however this always comes at the cost of sensitivity of the instrument.
   535‚ÜíUndoubtedly, such issues arise from the fact that SOGS was developed to ascertain ‚Äúprobable‚Äù
   536‚ÜíPG, which is different from fulfilling DSM-5 criteria for Gambling Disorder.
   537‚ÜíInitially designed to assess lifetime gambling behaviours, the SOGS was later adapted to
   538‚Üíaccommodate various time frames in research, including past-year, past-six-month, and past-
   539‚Üíthree-month assessments [38, 39]. Research by Wulfert et al. showed that using shorter time
   540‚Üíframes, such as past-three-month or past-year, yielded better psychometric properties in respect
   541‚Üíto ascertaining a current clinical diagnosis, compared to the original lifetime assessment [23].
   542‚ÜíThese adaptations enhance the instrument's accuracy in reflecting current/recent gambling
   543‚Üíbehaviours, offering a more precise measure for specific research contexts, and helping to
   544‚Üímitigate some of the limitations associated with the lifetime SOGS. However, it also highlights
   545‚Üíthe importance of careful consideration in selecting appropriate cut-off scores and timeframes
   546‚Üíto ensure the accuracy and validity of the findings in different research and clinical
   547‚Üíapplications. It also highlights another important limitation, in that when instruments are used
   548‚Üíin variations, the variation used needs to be clearly stated and not conflated in data synthesis
   549‚Üíapproaches e.g. in pairwise or network meta-analyses or systematic reviews, as the instrument
   550‚Üíscores would have a different meaning for e.g. lifetime vs. ‚Äúlast 3 months‚Äù assessment and
   551‚Üíwould require different interpretation.
   552‚ÜíThe Problem Gambling Severity Index (PGSI), a screening instrument used to measure the
   553‚Üíseverity of gambling problems in general population research, is perhaps one of the most
   554‚Üíwidely used measures for gambling prevalence estimate studies; such widespread application
   555‚Üíhas often given little thought to validity for particular purposes [40]. The PGSI has also been
   556‚Üí17
   557‚Üí
   558‚Üíwidely used as a screening instrument [41, 42]. However, the PGSI aligns with previous public
   559‚Üíhealth perspectives of ‚Äúproblem gambling‚Äù and does not align with current DSM-5 or ICD-11
   560‚Üícriteria or provide a formal diagnosis [29, 40]. Another concern with PGSI is that it was
   561‚Üídeveloped before the advent of online gambling. It focuses on nine key items that assess
   562‚Üígambling behaviours and adverse consequences over a 12-month period [43]. Unlike the binary
   563‚Üíclassification systems of other measures, the PGSI offers putative classification into four
   564‚Üícategories across the continuum of risk: ‚Äúnon-problem gambling‚Äù, ‚Äúlow-risk gambling‚Äù,
   565‚Üí‚Äúmoderate-risk gambling‚Äù, and ‚Äúproblem gambling‚Äù [40, 43]. There is debate about the optimal
   566‚Üícut-off scores for these categories [44]. However, it is important to note that it may struggle to
   567‚Üídetect low-severity gambling problems, raising concerns about its utility, and also that it
   568‚Üíconflates to a substantial degree measurement of core Gambling Disorder features with
   569‚Üímeasurement of gambling consequences [19, 20]. Put differently, it measures some historical
   570‚Üídiagnostic criteria and some consequences but can be seen as suboptimal for the comprehensive
   571‚Üímeasurement of either. Despite being initially developed for population use, it has often been
   572‚Üíused in clinical samples but has limited utility in this context since most ‚Äì and in some clinics
   573‚Üíall ‚Äì individuals referred to those services will score in the upper category. Other cut-offs have
   574‚Üíbeen developed that can help reduce, but not entirely prevent, such issues [45].
   575‚ÜíIn a comparative analysis, despite its lower specificity (higher rate of false positives), the SOGS
   576‚Üíwas found to be the most sensitive instrument, as compared to the PGSI and the Massachusetts
   577‚ÜíGambling Screen (MAGS), for the identification of individuals with Gambling Disorder [29].
   578‚ÜíThis indicates that while the SOGS may more frequently identify false positives, it is also more
   579‚Üílikely to not miss true cases of Gambling Disorder, making it a valuable instrument in contexts
   580‚Üíwhere sensitivity is prioritised over specificity. Again, it is important to remember that
   581‚Üísensitivity and specificity can easily change as a function of threshold used and target
   582‚Üípopulation, so extra care is required to ascertain which metrics apply in each applied setting.
   583‚ÜíIn evaluating the effectiveness of Gambling Disorder screening instruments, Otto et al.'s
   584‚Üísystematic review highlighted significant limitations [29]. Of the 31 instruments identified,
   585‚Üíonly three -SOGS, PGSI, and MAGS- were found to have been validated against a semi-
   586‚Üístructured clinical interview in only three studies. Even these instruments displayed significant
   587‚Üílimitations, particularly in population-level studies. For instance, the PGSI was found to have
   588‚Üívery low Positive Predictive Values (PPVs) (as low as 0.15, extremely poor), whereas another
   589‚Üíscreening instrument (MAGS) in the same sample had much higher PPV (e.g. 0.41, but still
   590‚Üí18
   591‚Üí
   592‚Üíwould be objectively regarded as poor) to identify Gambling Disorder in general population
   593‚Üísamples using a threshold of 8 and above [29]. This low PPV suggests that many individuals
   594‚Üíidentified as having gambling problems by the PGSI would not actually meet clinical criteria
   595‚Üífor the disorder, leading to very inaccurate estimates if used for this purpose. This drawback
   596‚Üíunderscores the need for instruments with better metrics to ensure effective identification of
   597‚Üíindividuals truly at risk for Gambling Disorder. Unfortunately, many prevalence studies
   598‚Üícontinue to use the PGSI, including in the UK, partly due to it being used in the past, which
   599‚Üímeans public health, clinical services and governments do not currently have any accurate data
   600‚Üíon prevalence of Gambling Disorder nationally or in particular regions. The PGSI is likely to
   601‚Üíonly have a reasonable PPV in populations that are enriched (i.e. have a relatively high
   602‚Üíbackground level of Gambling Disorder), such as psychiatric outpatient clinics, debt
   603‚Üímanagement services, alcohol care teams in general hospitals or community substance use
   604‚Üíservices.
   605‚ÜíAnother screening instrument, the IDENT-Pro, reflects the growing need to accurately identify
   606‚Üírisk factors for gambling behaviour at an early stage [46]. Davies et al. introduced the IDENT-
   607‚ÜíPro as an instrument specifically designed to detect risk factors predictive of gambling-related
   608‚Üíharm, before they escalate into more severe problems. In the context of IDENT-Pro, the
   609‚Üíinstrument‚Äôs authors refer to issues such as chasing losses, frequency of gambling, and finding
   610‚Üígambling exciting, as ‚Äòrisk factors‚Äô (as opposed for example to deprivation, impulsivity, early
   611‚Üíexposure to gambling, etc.). The study highlighted the instrument's strong validity and
   612‚Üíreliability (shown in Table 4), particularly in identifying lower levels of gambling risk, which
   613‚Üímay be missed by more traditional measures such as the PGSI [46]. While the IDENT-Pro was
   614‚Üídesigned for use in the general population and has been validated in studies identifying early
   615‚Üírisk factors, it shows particular promise in public health and early intervention settings [46].
   616‚ÜíFurther research is necessary to validate its effectiveness across a broader range of populations
   617‚Üíand settings, ensuring its utility in diverse contexts.
   618‚ÜíThe Problem Gambling Measure (PGM) is a 15-item refined and updated version of the earlier
   619‚ÜíProblem and Pathological Gambling Measure (PPGM) [47]. The PGM, which was developed
   620‚Üífor use in the general population and has been validated in community samples, measures
   621‚Üídifferent aspects of gambling-related harm and chronic gambling [47]. The PGM incorporates
   622‚Üívariables such as the breadth of gambling involvement and the largest single-day gambling
   623‚Üíloss, which enhances its predictive accuracy across a spectrum of gambling severity. The
   624‚Üí19
   625‚Üí
   626‚Üícomplexity and length of the PGM, while contributing to its comprehensive nature, may present
   627‚Üíchallenges in settings where quick screening is necessary, such as in primary care or public
   628‚Üíhealth surveys, or busy clinics [47]. Despite these potential limitations, the PGM‚Äôs detailed
   629‚Üíassessment capabilities ensure that it provides valuable insights that more concise instruments
   630‚Üímay overlook.
   631‚ÜíThe Gambling Disorders Identification Test (GDIT) was developed based on the Banff
   632‚ÜíConsensus Agreement through a process that included an international two-round Delphi
   633‚Üímethod involving gambling experts from 10 countries [48, 49]. The stated aim was to develop
   634‚Üían instrument akin to the Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test (AUDIT) but for Gambling
   635‚ÜíDisorder, implying it to be a ‚Äòlong‚Äô assessment instrument for screening purposes. The Delphi
   636‚Üíprocess led to the selection of items for the instrument, with input from both gambling
   637‚Üíresearchers and clinicians as well as feedback from individuals with Gambling Disorder [49,
   638‚Üí50]. It comprises 14 items across three domains - gambling behaviour, gambling symptoms,
   639‚Üíand negative consequences - each selected to capture the complexity of Gambling Disorder.
   640‚ÜíAs shown in Table 4, the instrument has demonstrated excellent initial psychometric properties,
   641‚Üíincluding high internal consistency (Œ± = .94) and robust test-retest reliability (ICC= .93) [51].
   642‚ÜíAdditionally, the GDIT has proposed cut-off scores corresponding to mild, moderate, and
   643‚Üísevere levels of the disorder, consistent with the severity specifiers of the DSM diagnosis [51,
   644‚Üí52].
   645‚ÜíThe National Opinion Research Center Diagnostic Screen for Gambling Problems (NODS) is
   646‚Üíanother well-known instrument specifically designed to assess Gambling Disorder based on
   647‚ÜíDSM-IV criteria [31]. This 17-item diagnostic interview provides a thorough evaluation of
   648‚Üígambling-related behaviours and symptoms, making it a comprehensive measure for
   649‚Üídetermining the severity of the disorder. In clinical settings, the NODS has proven particularly
   650‚Üíeffective in identifying more severe cases of Gambling Disorder; however, it may be less
   651‚Üísensitive to milder cases, limiting its usefulness in differentiating between levels of severity
   652‚Üí[20]. Despite these challenges, the NODS is valued for its strong correlation with other
   653‚Üímeasures of gambling severity (r = 0.86) and its fair internal consistency (Cronbach's Œ± = 0.78),
   654‚Üíestablishing its reliability in both clinical and research contexts [53]. However, its length and
   655‚Üícomplexity may limit its practicality in time-sensitive environments or among populations
   656‚Üírequiring simpler assessments. The NODS has been validated for different time frames,
   657‚Üíincluding lifetime, past-year, and 3-month versions, allowing flexibility in assessing Gambling
   658‚Üí20
   659‚Üí
   660‚ÜíDisorder over varying periods [23]. The updated version, NODS-GD, which aligns with DSM-
   661‚Üí5 criteria, continues to demonstrate strong psychometric properties, including high internal
   662‚Üíconsistency (Cronbach's Œ± = 0.88) and test-retest reliability (ICC= 0.84), making it an essential
   663‚Üíinstrument for capturing the nuanced aspects of Gambling Disorder across diverse populations
   664‚Üí[54].
   665‚ÜíThe MIDI Gambling Disorder Module was developed and validated in people who gamble at
   666‚Üíleast occasionally and it captures all DSM-5 diagnostic items [55]. The self-report MIDI
   667‚ÜíGambling Disorder Module demonstrated a sensitivity of 0.86 and a specificity of 0.85 in
   668‚Üídetection of Gambling Disorder in people who gamble at least occasionally, as compared to
   669‚Üígold standard in-person structured clinical interviews conducted by a highly experienced
   670‚Üíclinician. It is one of very few instruments validated against a gold standard, i.e. a validated
   671‚Üístructured clinical interview (the Structured Clinical Interview for Gambling Disorder, SCI-
   672‚ÜíGD). It had test re-test reliability of 0.95, and showed good convergent and divergent validity
   673‚Üí[55]. Furthermore, the module has shown sensitivity of 100% and specificity of 98.4% in a
   674‚Üíspecialist inpatient setting, again against the gold-standard [56]. It comprises only one question
   675‚Üífor non-gamblers and 11 questions for those who do gamble, taking less than a minute to
   676‚Üícomplete. The instrument‚Äôs strong psychometric properties, consistency against the latest DSM
   677‚Üícriteria, coupled with its brevity and ease of use, make it a valuable instrument for both
   678‚Üíscreening and diagnostic purposes.
   679‚ÜíDiagnostic Instruments
   680‚ÜíThis section considers instruments designed for the formal diagnosis of Gambling Disorder.
   681‚ÜíGenerally, such instruments are suitable for use by clinicians in different settings but it is
   682‚Üíimportant to note that clinicians conducting such interviews need appropriate training and/or
   683‚Üíexpertise not only about the disorder but also the methods used to diagnose it. As such they are
   684‚Üínot suitable for use by individuals without appropriate training and/or clinical expertise.
   685‚ÜíDiagnostic instruments can form a useful part of the broader assessment which typically would
   686‚Üíalso include a clinical formulation (for example, the so-termed ‚Äú5Ps‚Äù ‚Äì presenting problem,
   687‚Üípre-disposing factors, precipitating factors, perpetuating factors, and positive/protective
   688‚Üífactors). This broader formulation provides a deeper and more nuanced understanding of how
   689‚Üíthe disorder manifests for each individual, and guides treatment targets and interventions, in
   690‚Üípartnership/collaboration with a given patient.
   691‚Üí21
   692‚Üí
   693‚ÜíThe Structured Clinical Interview for Pathological Gambling (SCI-PG) is a widely recognised,
   694‚Üíclinician-administered instrument in original form developed based on DSM-V criteria to
   695‚Üídiagnose pathological gambling [57]. It has demonstrated excellent psychometric properties,
   696‚Üíincluding high inter-rater and test-retest reliability. Its sensitivity is reported at 0.88, and
   697‚Üíspecificity at 1.00, making it a robust instrument for accurately diagnosing Gambling Disorder
   698‚Üíin clinical settings. An updated version, the Structured Clinical Interview for Gambling
   699‚ÜíDisorder (SCI-GD), consists of 10 questions (9 for inclusionary criteria and 1 for exclusionary
   700‚Üícriterion) that align with DSM-5 criteria, with a diagnosis being made if four or more
   701‚Üíinclusionary criteria are met. The SCI-GD is accurate in categorising the severity of Gambling
   702‚ÜíDisorder, with specific thresholds for mild, moderate, and severe levels [57]. There is also the
   703‚ÜíClinician-Administered form of the previously discussed MIDI Gambling Disorder Module,
   704‚Üíwhich maps closely onto the SCI-PG, thereby strengthening its convergent validity [55].
   705‚ÜíTreatment Outcome Instruments
   706‚ÜíThis section considers instruments useful in measuring outcomes from
   707‚Üíinterventions/treatments. The primary clinical focus in this area is usually gambling symptom
   708‚Üíseverity; but we also consider process/mechanistic measures, as well as measures of recovery.
   709‚ÜíOutcome measures: gambling symptom severity
   710‚ÜíThe Yale-Brown Obsessive-Compulsive Scale Modified for Pathological Gambling (PG-
   711‚ÜíYBOCS) is a clinician-administered scale specifically adapted to assess the severity of
   712‚Üígambling-related thoughts and behaviours within the past week [58]. It is structured around a
   713‚Üí10-item format, with items divided into two 5-item subscales: gambling urges/thoughts and
   714‚Üígambling behaviours. The PG-YBOCS has been recognised for its strong psychometric
   715‚Üíproperties, including excellent test-retest reliability and validity in differentiating between
   716‚Üímild, moderate, and severe cases of Gambling Disorder, making it a potentially valuable
   717‚Üíinstrument for both clinical practice and research [59, 60]. In comparative analyses, it has
   718‚Üídemonstrated a superior ability to distinguish between different levels of disorder severity
   719‚Üícompared to other measures, such as the Gambling Symptom Assessment Scale (G-SAS) [60].
   720‚ÜíThis suggests that the PG-YBOCS may be preferable (or at least worth including alongside G-
   721‚ÜíSAS) as an outcome measure in clinical trials, particularly when it is crucial to detect nuanced
   722‚Üíchanges in severity among participants. A reduction of ‚â•50% in total PG-YBOCS score has
   723‚Üíbeen identified as the optimal threshold for defining treatment response, determined through
   724‚Üí22
   725‚Üí
   726‚ÜíROC analyses conducted across data from 553 clinical trial participants [14]. It is, however,
   727‚Üílimited to clinician administration, with no self-report versions available.
   728‚ÜíThe G-SAS is a 12-item, self-administered scale designed to measure the severity of gambling
   729‚Üísymptoms over the past week. Each item is rated on a scale from 0 to 4 using various response
   730‚Üíoptions and covering various dimensions such as the intensity, frequency, and duration of
   731‚Üígambling urges and behaviours. The first four items of the G-SAS can be summed to measure
   732‚Üígambling urges. Total scores range from 0 to 48 and are classified into five severity categories:
   733‚Üíno symptoms (0‚Äì7), mild (8‚Äì20), moderate (21‚Äì30), severe (31‚Äì40), and extreme (41‚Äì48),
   734‚Üíproviding clinically meaningful thresholds that are particularly useful for monitoring treatment
   735‚Üíresponse. The G-SAS has demonstrated good reliability and validity, particularly in capturing
   736‚Üíchanges in symptoms over time, which is critical for assessing the effectiveness of
   737‚Üíinterventions [61]. A reduction of ‚â•35% in total G-SAS score has been identified as the optimal
   738‚Üíthreshold for defining treatment response [14].
   739‚ÜíOutcome measures: process/mechanism measures
   740‚ÜíWhile we consider outcome measures related to underlying mechanisms in this section, it
   741‚Üíshould be noted that this is a nascent area and much more research needs to be undertaken to
   742‚Üíidentify mechanisms of change likely to be underpinning therapeutic response; such measures
   743‚Üíare not currently commonly deployed. Choice of mechanisms to measure would be very
   744‚Üícontingent on the nature of a given treatment being deployed. For example, CBT often targets
   745‚Üíurges and gambling-related cognitions, rendering these potentially suitable mechanisms of
   746‚Üíchange to measure. As such this section gives a couple of examples of potential instruments
   747‚Üíand should not be viewed as a definitive.
   748‚ÜíThe Gambling Craving Scale (GACS) was initially validated using university student samples,
   749‚Üíestablishing its effectiveness in measuring gambling cravings in non-clinical populations [62].
   750‚ÜíMore recently, Battaglia et al. extended this work by conducting a psychometric validation of
   751‚Üíthe GACS within a treatment-seeking sample [63]. Their study provided support for the scale's
   752‚Üíreliability and validity in a clinical context, particularly in capturing the three distinct facets of
   753‚Üícraving: Anticipation, Desire, and Relief [63]. These findings suggest that the GACS can be
   754‚Üíuseful for assessing craving including in clinical settings.
   755‚ÜíThe GamCog scale is an instrument in the assessment of gambling-related cognitions within
   756‚Üíthe context of video game-related gambling [64]. Developed in response to the limitations of
   757‚Üí23
   758‚Üí
   759‚Üíthe Gambling Related Cognitions Scale (GRCS), which was found to be less suitable for video
   760‚Üígame players due to issues with model fit, convergent validity, and discriminant validity, the
   761‚ÜíGamCog incorporates additional items specifically tailored to capture the unique cognitive
   762‚Üíprocesses involved in this emerging form of gambling [64, 65]. This promising instrument
   763‚Üíunderscores the importance of adapting and refining existing measurement instruments to
   764‚Üíeffectively address the evolving landscape of gambling behaviours, particularly as they
   765‚Üíincreasingly converge with video gaming. However, while the GamCog may find useful
   766‚Üíapplications in evaluating gambling and treatment response in the context of video game-
   767‚Üírelated gambling, it is possible that may not be as ideal for more ‚Äútraditional‚Äù forms of
   768‚Üígambling outside this field, thus making it more specialised and less likely useful for general
   769‚Üípurpose.
   770‚ÜíOutcome measures: Recovery
   771‚ÜíThe concept of recovery in Gambling Disorder is increasingly recognised as a complex and
   772‚Üímultifaceted process that extends beyond merely ceasing gambling behaviours [27, 66].
   773‚ÜíAlthough consensus guidelines suggest using measures of gambling behaviour, gambling-
   774‚Üírelated harms, quality of life, and mechanisms of change [49], a systematic review
   775‚Üídemonstrated that 63 different outcome measures have been used across multiple domains,
   776‚Üísuggesting a multidimensional conceptualisation of recovery [26, 48]. Recovery can
   777‚Üíencompass a holistic improvement in various aspects of an individual's life, including
   778‚Üípsychological well-being, social relationships, and financial stability. This broader
   779‚Üíunderstanding reflects a shift that considers the individual's overall quality of life. Recovery in
   780‚Üíthis context involves not only managing and overcoming gambling but also developing insight
   781‚Üíinto the underlying factors contributing to Gambling Disorder and building a meaningful life
   782‚Üíbeyond gambling. Relevant to the notion of recovery is the concept of ‚Äòrecovery capital‚Äô which
   783‚Üírelates to all the internal and external resources an individual can access or utilise to help
   784‚Üísupport their recovery process (for a detailed discussion see [67]). The emphasis under such
   785‚Üíframeworks is often on the longer-term maintenance of these improvements, recognising that
   786‚Üírecovery is a continuous, nonlinear process that may involve setbacks such as relapses [27, 66].
   787‚ÜíIn the context of recovery, two relatively well-established types of measures in mental health
   788‚Üíare quality of life and functioning. Whilst there are few (if any) instruments that have been
   789‚Üídeveloped and validated specifically for Gambling Disorder, measures developed for other
   790‚Üíconditions may be useful to deploy.
   791‚Üí24
   792‚Üí
   793‚ÜíTo better capture this complexity, the Recovery Index for Gambling Disorder (RIGD) has been
   794‚Üídeveloped [68]. It comprises 32 items across six dimensions: gambling reduction, urge coping,
   795‚Üírecovery wisdom, life functioning, interpersonal relationships, and mental health. These
   796‚Üídimensions collectively capture both gambling-specific and broader life improvements,
   797‚Üíoffering a more holistic view of recovery. It has demonstrated strong reliability and validity
   798‚Üí(shown in Table 5), particularly in treatment-seeking populations. However, its current
   799‚Üívalidation within Australian samples suggests that further studies are needed to confirm its
   800‚Üíapplicability across different cultural contexts and among non-treatment-seeking individuals
   801‚Üí[68]. Another consideration relates to the broader concept of recovery itself. Although the
   802‚ÜíRIGD captures several important domains, including gambling reduction, wellbeing,
   803‚Üífunctioning, and interpersonal relationships, further stakeholder engagement involving
   804‚Üíclinicians, researchers, and people with lived experience may help establish greater consensus
   805‚Üíon how recovery should be conceptualised and measured in gambling disorder. Therefore,
   806‚Üídespite its strong psychometric properties, the instrument's 32-item length may limit its
   807‚Üípracticality in routine clinical and outcome-monitoring settings, making it potentially more
   808‚Üísuitable for research purposes.
   809‚ÜíQuality of life and functional impairment are increasingly recognised as important outcome
   810‚Üídomains in Gambling Disorder research in terms of recovery; however, our search identified
   811‚Üíno instruments specifically designed to measure these constructs in this population that met
   812‚Üíinclusion criteria. Notably, the only gambling-specific quality of life instrument identified - the
   813‚ÜíGambling Quality of Life Scale (GQoLS) - was developed and validated exclusively in French,
   814‚Üíprecluding its inclusion under the English-language psychometric validation criterion applied
   815‚Üíin this review [69]. This gap has been documented in the wider literature: a systematic review
   816‚Üíand meta-analysis by Bonfils et al. identified six general quality of life instruments used across
   817‚Üí35 Gambling Disorder studies, none of which were originally designed for or fully validated in
   818‚Üíthis population [70]. The most frequently used were the Medical Outcomes Study Short-Form
   819‚ÜíHealth Survey (SF-36), the Quality of Life Inventory (QOLI), and the World Health
   820‚ÜíOrganization Quality of Life Assessment Instrument (WHOQOL-BREF), with the latter
   821‚Üídemonstrating the most promising psychometric properties in Gambling Disorder samples thus
   822‚Üífar, based on the limited research conducted. The European Quality of Life Questionnaire (EQ-
   823‚Üí5D) has also been applied in this context as a standardised measure of health-related quality of
   824‚Üílife [70]. Bonfils et al. (2019) concluded that while these general instruments are capable of
   825‚Üídetecting change following intervention, they likely fail to capture the full range of quality of
   826‚Üí25
   827‚Üí
   828‚Üílife domains specifically impacted by Gambling Disorder, including financial preoccupation,
   829‚Üíloneliness, and avoidance of help-seeking [70]. In the absence of a validated gambling-specific
   830‚Üímeasure, the Work and Social Adjustment Scale (WSAS) - a brief five-item self-report measure
   831‚Üíof functional impairment across work, home management, social, and leisure domains - has
   832‚Üíbeen used in Gambling Disorder research to capture broader psychosocial burden and is
   833‚Üíincluded here as a clinically relevant measure of functioning [71, 72].
   834‚ÜíBroader assessments of gambling-related harms
   835‚ÜíWhile the above-described instruments focus on gambling symptom severity, contemporary
   836‚Üípublic health perspectives have emphasised the importance of quantifying broader gambling-
   837‚Üírelated harms. Aligning with public health perspectives, however, it is important to avoid
   838‚Üíconflating the health condition (Gambling Disorder) with the very diffuse and complex, hard
   839‚Üíto measure, but important, adverse consequences of the health condition (gambling-related
   840‚Üíharms). Measurement of broader gambling-related harms can be challenging, given the many
   841‚Üíways that they can manifest across levels of understanding (e.g. from the individual level to
   842‚Üíthe family unit or to the societal level). However, it may be useful to include measures of harm
   843‚Üíin settings involving Gambling Disorder, to provide more information about harms beyond the
   844‚Üísymptoms and their more direct consequences.
   845‚ÜíThe Gambling Harm Scale-10 (GHS-10), previously known as the Short Gambling Harm Scale
   846‚Üí(SGHS), is one of the most widely used measures for assessing gambling-related harm in many
   847‚Üícountries [73]. It emerged from development of an initial 72-item checklist, which was reduced
   848‚Üíto 10 items based on psychometric analyses. The full original format is also available but is, of
   849‚Üícourse, prohibitive in many settings due to length/burden. The short scale was developed to
   850‚Üíprovide a concise yet effective measure of gambling-related harm, with a short version (10
   851‚Üíitems) and a longer 20-item version [74]. The 20-item version has the added advantage of
   852‚Üíadditional face validity (i.e. measurement of harms across each harm domain) but may not offer
   853‚Üípsychometric advantages. Additionally, a domain-specific version has been introduced,
   854‚Üídesigned to assess specific harm domains separately, further improving its applicability across
   855‚Üídifferent contexts [75]. These variations allow for flexibility in measuring gambling harms
   856‚Üíacross diverse populations and research settings, making the GHS-10 a widely used instrument
   857‚Üífor both clinical and epidemiological studies.
   858‚Üí26
   859‚Üí
   860‚ÜíAnother tool available to measure gambling harms is the Gambling Harm Measure (GHM),
   861‚Üíwhich is a 16-item instrument developed based on classical and modern test theory analyses
   862‚Üí[76]. The GHM was developed to address conceptual and measurement limitations of earlier
   863‚Üíharm measures and it assesses harm across six life domains - financial, psychological,
   864‚Üírelationship, physical health, work/study, and legal - using dichotomously scored items that
   865‚Üícapture three increasing levels of severity within each domain: over-prioritisation of gambling,
   866‚Üípressures or strains, and serious negative consequences. Notably, the instrument avoids making
   867‚Üívalue judgments about the recreational worth of gambling and only assesses unambiguous
   868‚Üíindices of harm. A validation study by Gooding et al. (2025), drawing on three independent
   869‚Üísamples totalling 9,913 participants from the United States and Canada, supported a
   870‚Üíunidimensional factor structure that was invariant across samples, genders, and age groups.
   871‚ÜíThe GHM demonstrated high internal consistency (Œ± = 0.94‚Äì0.95) and strong convergent
   872‚Üívalidity with established measures including the PGSI harm items and the PPGM harm
   873‚Üísubscale.

   911‚ÜíFuture Research Directions
   912‚ÜíIt is important to consider gaps in the literature and ways in which future research might help
   913‚Üífurther improve how Gambling Disorder is identified and assessed. Firstly, it is essential to be
   914‚Üíclear about the purpose of a given instrument: whether for early identification, formal
   915‚Üídiagnosis, classification of disorder severity, and/or treatment outcome evaluation. Having a
   916‚Üíwell-articulated objective when choosing a given instrument (or refining an instrument) ensures
   917‚Üíthat each is applied effectively in clinical and research settings. Moreover, utilising the latest
   918‚Üígold-standard diagnostic criteria, such as self-report and clinician-administered structured
   919‚Üíclinical interviews based on diagnostic criteria, is crucial to accurately benchmark these
   920‚Üíinstruments against a gold standard. Performance metrics, including sensitivity, specificity, the
   921‚Üíarea under the receiver operator curve (ROC), and predictive values, as well as sensitivity to
   922‚Üíchange, should be used alongside various validity measures to comprehensively assess
   923‚Üíinstrument effectiveness.
   924‚ÜíAnother critical area of focus is the adaptation or creation of instruments that are culturally
   925‚Üísensitive. Ensuring that these instruments are validated across different cultural contexts will
   926‚Üíimprove their relevance and accuracy in a variety of settings. At the same time, it is not helpful
   927‚Üíto develop variants of interest that are only usable in a very specific population context, as
   928‚Üíopposed to across diverse research studies.
   929‚ÜíFuture research should explore emerging trends and innovations in the development and
   930‚Üíutilisation of measurement instruments. Advancements in technology, such as mobile apps and
   931‚Üíwearable devices, offer new opportunities for the assessment and ongoing monitoring and
   932‚Üímodification of Gambling Disorder. These technologies could provide real-time data, enhance
   933‚Üíthe accessibility of assessments, increase patient engagement, and facilitate more personalised
   934‚Üíintervention strategies.
   935‚ÜíLastly, studies are needed to assess whether these instruments not only perform well in
   936‚Üícontrolled academic settings (e.g. in Randomised Controlled Trials or academic laboratories),
   937‚Üíbut also in real-world applications such as in routine clinical practice. For example, a question
   938‚Üíthat cannot yet be addressed is ‚ÄúIs PG-YBOCS or G-SAS more sensitive to clinically
   939‚Üísignificant change in clinical gambling services (such as in the UK NHS)?‚Äù Such work will
   940‚Üíprovide critical evidence to guide future practice and the optimisation of Gambling Disorder
   941‚Üíassessment instruments.

   944‚ÜíLimitations
   945‚ÜíSeveral limitations should be considered. This was a narrative review incorporating a rapid
   946‚Üíreview component, rather than a formal systematic review of all possible instruments ‚Äì as such
   947‚Üísome instruments may have been overlooked. At the same time, the rapid review approach was
   948‚Üímost suitable because of the vast diverse literature, the need for urgent recommendations to
   949‚Üíinform research plus practice, and due to our aim of providing a practical overview and set of
   950‚Üírecommendations. This was recognised in terms of funding for the current work, which arose
   951‚Üífrom UKRI‚Äôs rapid evidence review grant call. The paper focused primarily on Gambling
   952‚ÜíDisorder due to it being clearly defined and having well-established evidence-based clinical
   953‚Üíinterventions from randomised controlled studies and subsequent meta-analysis. We do
   954‚Üíacknowledge the importance of, and need for further research in, interventions to address the
   955‚Üíbroader concept of gambling-related harms, in separate work. Lastly, this paper focused on
   956‚Üíinstruments likely to be useful in the context of Gambling Disorder; it did not focus on use of
   957‚Üítools in other settings (i.e. in contexts where there is no intention to identify or estimate rates
   958‚Üíof the clinically defined disorder).
   959‚ÜíConclusion
   960‚ÜíOverall, using the most suitable instruments in the context of Gambling Disorder can be
   961‚Üíinvaluable for several purposes: to help inform treatment decision-making and treatment
   962‚Üíprogress between therapist and individual patient; to contribute to service evaluation, audit,
   963‚Üíand quality improvement (locally and in terms of national benchmarking); and to contribute to
   964‚Üíresearch to improve the evidence-base for pathways and interventions. In the assessment and
   965‚Üítreatment of Gambling Disorder, it is crucial to recognise that multiple measures can be
   966‚Üíeffectively utilised to capture the complexity of the condition. However, the selection of
   967‚Üíappropriate instruments should be guided by a clear understanding of the specific goals of the
   968‚Üíassessment. Whether the aim is screening, diagnosis, or measuring treatment response, the
   969‚Üíchosen measures must align with the intended outcomes to ensure accuracy and relevance.
   970‚ÜíMoreover, it is essential to consider participant burden. Assessment instruments should be
   971‚Üísensitive not only to the clinical needs but also to the practical aspects of their administration,
   972‚Üíminimising discomfort and the time required from the patient. This consideration helps in
   973‚Üímaintaining patient engagement and ensures that the assessment process does not become a
   974‚Üíbarrier to effective treatment. Instruments should be used when their output(s) have utility e.g.
   975‚Üí30
   976‚Üí
   977‚Üíwould help to understand a given presentation and inform treatment decisions or address
   978‚Üíspecific research questions.
   979‚ÜíFinally, the ease of scoring, training requirement for administration, availability and cost of a
   980‚Üígiven instrument, and the potential need for follow-up assessments must be considered.
   981‚ÜíInstruments that are straightforward to score and interpret allow for more timely and accurate
   982‚Üíclinical decisions. Additionally, the practicality of conducting follow-up assessments should
   983‚Üíbe considered to ensure continuity of care and the ability to monitor progress over time.
   984‚ÜíBy keeping these considerations in mind, clinicians, researchers, and policy-makers can select
   985‚Üíand implement assessment instruments that not only provide valuable insights but also facilitate
   986‚Üía patient-centred approach to the treatment of Gambling Disorder.
