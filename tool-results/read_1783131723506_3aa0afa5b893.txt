    25→    25→ABSTRACT
    26→    26→The role of relationships for performance and well-being at work have received
    27→    27→increasing attention in the past years, but there are very few longitudinal intervention studies
    28→    28→addressing the relational dimensions of workplace well-being. We address this gap with a
    29→    29→longitudinal, control group intervention study based on the job demands-resource model that
    30→    30→explored the impact of a group coaching intervention on 32 Church of England clergy. Pre-
    31→    31→and post-measure comparisons indicated that the intervention resulted in a significant
    32→    32→decrease in participants’ relational fusion with others and an almost significant decrease in
    33→    33→interpersonal emotional cutoff. Additionally, the treatment group reported an increase in
    34→    34→supportive interpersonal relations at all levels (peer, management and congregation) compared
    35→    35→to the control group. Moreover, results indicated a positive link between these personal and
    36→    36→relational resource improvements and affective well-being, where intervention participants, in
    37→    37→contrast with the control group, experienced an almost significant decrease in their perception
    38→    38→of the severity of work demands and an increase in spiritual resources. This indicates that a
    39→    39→person-centred intervention focused on enhancing workplace relationships significantly
    40→    40→increases personal and interpersonal resources, reduces the deleterious effects of work
    41→    41→demands and promotes work-related psychological health thus offering important practical
    42→    42→implications for managing workplace relationships.

    48→    48→‘Good Work’ through Good Relationships? A Longitudinal
    49→    49→Field Intervention Study about the Effectiveness of Group Coaching on Well-Being and
    50→    50→Resilience at Work
    51→    51→“Good work” is defined as work that promotes flourishing and can improve work-
    52→    52→related psychological health (British Psychological Society, 2010), reducing levels of
    53→    53→emotional exhaustion and symptoms of burnout while encouraging work engagement. This
    54→    54→does not involve removing all negative and stressful experiences, instead, work that promotes
    55→    55→well-being is operationalized as incorporating the experience of challenging and complex
    56→    56→demands with the provision of sufficient resources (Dodge, Daly, Huyton & Sanders, 2012).
    57→    57→This interaction is articulated within the dominant account of occupational stress and well-
    58→    58→being, the job demands-resources model (JD-R, Demerouti, Bakker, Nachreiner & Schaufeli,
    59→    59→2001).
    60→    60→According to the JD-R model, job demands refer to those aspects of the job that
    61→    61→require sustained physical, social or psychological effort and evoke a health impairment
    62→    62→process that leads to strain, exhaustion and negative physical or psychological health
    63→    63→including burnout (Bakker, Demerouti & Sanz-Vergel, 2014). In contrast, job resources are
    64→    64→physical, psychological, social or organizational aspects of the job that promote work
    65→    65→engagement, reduce job demands and stimulate personal growth and development via a
    66→    66→motivational process (Bakker et al., 2014). In addition, well-being at work is recognized as
    67→    67→resulting from an interaction between the objective level of demands and resources and the
    68→    68→subjective individual appraisal of these factors (Beck, 1996; Alarcon, Eschleman & Bowling,
    69→    69→2009).
    70→    70→
    71→    71→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 4
    72→    72→Organizational psychology workplace interventions emphasize that it is possible to
    73→    73→improve a negative working environment by making adjustments to one or two specific areas,
    74→    74→as deleterious pressure is additive (Maslach, Leiter & Jackson, 2012). Research exploring the
    75→    75→distinctive contributions relational demands and resources make to the experience of “good
    76→    76→work” is expanding as it is recognized that fostering good relationships is increasingly
    77→    77→important for performance and well-being at work (Day & Leiter, 2014). Changing work
    78→    78→models emphasize flattened, team-based organizational structures that place greater emphasis
    79→    79→on high-quality, positive relationships within the work context (Colbert, 2016). In addition,
    80→    80→the increasing rate of organizational change driven by technological advances, fuels anxiety
    81→    81→within work environments (Mayton, 2011). Such stressed systems result in poorer working
    82→    82→relationships and increased bullying due to heightened emotional reactivity (Van den Brande,
    83→    83→2016).
    84→    84→Work Relationships and Workplace Well-Being
    85→    85→Research across a diverse variety of professions from the military (Brooks &
    86→    86→Greenberg, 2018), to social workers (McNicoll, 2016) have identified that interpersonal and
    87→    87→social factors are of fundamental importance to the establishment of “good work”, with such
    88→    88→relationships able to act as either exhausting demands and or enabling resources depending on
    89→    89→the quality and experience of the relationship (Day & Leiter, 2014).
    90→    90→Relational stressors include the presence of negative relational demands such as
    91→    91→conflict, bullying, criticism, presumptive expectations, social isolation and boundary
    92→    92→ambiguity (Lee, 1999; Olsen & Devor, 2015; Proeschold-Bell, Eisenberg, Legrand, Adams,
    93→    93→Smith & Wilk, 2015). Social stressors also include the absence or poor quality of potentially
    94→    94→motivational relational resources including the lack of effective leadership and social support
    95→    95→(Brooks & Greenberg, 2018; Day & Leiter, 2014; Cousins, Mackay, Clarke, Kelly, Kelly and
    96→    96→McCaig, 2004).
    97→    97→
    98→    98→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 5
    99→    99→Interpersonal supportive resources within work can be differentiated into those
   100→   100→offering instrumental support (Mathieu, Eschleman & Cheng, 2018): practical task assistance
   101→   101→linked to enhanced job satisfaction (Colbert et al., 2016) incorporating positive leadership
   102→   102→behaviours (Breevaart, Bakker, Hetland & Hetland, 2014), and those offering emotional
   103→   103→support, which encompasses affection, sympathy and understanding (Thoits, 1982). Other
   104→   104→social resources include the experience of friendship which is associated with positive
   105→   105→emotions (Colbert et al., 2016), having a sense of belonging (Brooks & Greenberg, 2018) and
   106→   106→community (McMillan and Chavis, 1986). Even the opportunity to give to others positively
   107→   107→increases the experience of meaningful work (Colbert et al., 2016).
   108→   108→Multilevel modelling of workplace well-being recognizes that individual and group
   109→   109→psychological health mutually influence each other (Halbesleben & Leon, 2014) and thus
   110→   110→individual relational resources need to be situated within a systemic model (Bowen, 1978).
   111→   111→The heightened value of supervisor support in contrast to co-worker support may be due to a
   112→   112→contagion effect, support from supervisors fostering a more overall supportive peer
   113→   113→environment (Mathieu et al., 2018), and may also be influenced by the norm of reciprocity, a
   114→   114→worker in receipt of supervisor support will experience a felt obligation to reciprocate toward
   115→   115→the organization in the form of behaviors, emotions, and commitment (Eisenberger, Armeli,
   116→   116→Rexwinkel, Lynch & Rhoades, 2001). The emotional catharsis obtained through emotional
   117→   117→venting may result in improved well-being for one employee but can leave another holding
   118→   118→the anxiety and pressure (Friedman, 1985) and unresolved management level conflict can
   119→   119→express itself in lower level employee burnout (Christian, 1982). In addition, Shoji (2015)
   120→   120→suggests that difficult team relationships may impact well-being indirectly by increasing the
   121→   121→risk of developing secondary traumatic stress from challenging client work.
   122→   122→While the focus is typically on relational interactions inside work, relational factors
   123→   123→outside of work also act as demands and resources that impact workplace well-being.
   124→   124→
   125→   125→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 6
   126→   126→Specifically, the facilitation and conflict stemming from one’s nuclear family (Wayne, Music
   127→   127→& Fleeson, 2004), relationships with wider family and friends (McMinn, Lish, Trice, Root,
   128→   128→Gilbert & Yap, 2005) and experiencing a meaningful sense of connection, belonging and
   129→   129→contribution within one’s community (Proschold-Bell et al., 2015).
   130→   130→In addition to the influence of physical relationships on well-being, the beneficial
   131→   131→impact of spiritual relatedness, an experience of connection with the sacred/transcendent, is a
   132→   132→growing area of well-being research (Miner, Dowson & Griffin, 2015). Within the Christian
   133→   133→tradition this spiritual resource is particularly pertinent for religious leaders where part of their
   134→   134→work is to model and attempt to foster this relationship among congregation members and
   135→   135→research has shown spiritual relatedness to be negatively associated with burnout (Miner &
   136→   136→Dowson & Sterland, 2010).
   137→   137→Interpersonal relations appear to impact the stress-distress continuum along a variety
   138→   138→of pathways. The stress-buffering hypothesis (Cohen & McKay, 1984) suggests that one
   139→   139→method social support benefits health is through mitigating the physiological arousal caused
   140→   140→by stress, this is supported by research that indicates the secondly stage of burnout, cynicism
   141→   141→results from a breakdown of the normative interactive co-regulation of stress using attachment
   142→   142→figures (Luyten & Van Houdenhove, 2013). However, the relationship is complex as while
   143→   143→social support can act as a social comfort reducing cardiovascular reactivity (CVR) it can also
   144→   144→increase CVR depending on the situation (Teoh & Hilmert, 2018).
   145→   145→Personal Resources, Workplace Well-Being and Work Relationships
   146→   146→Personal resources are defined as relatively stable characteristics of the self that
   147→   147→impact resilience, well-being and a person’s perceived ability to negotiate their environment
   148→   148→successfully (Bakker et al., 2014). For example, the personality traits of neuroticism and
   149→   149→extraversion have been found to be directly related to, and highly predictive of, negative and
   150→   150→positive work-related psychological health respectively (Randall, 2013). Differentiation of
   151→   151→
   152→   152→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 7
   153→   153→self, a measure of relational and emotional functioning when under pressure (Kerr & Bowen,
   154→   154→1988) has been found to be negatively correlated to stress (Salmabadi, Farshad, Salimi &
   155→   155→Alikhani, 2015), burnout (Beebe, 2007) and secondary traumatization (Halevi & Idisis, 2017),
   156→   156→and positively correlated with job satisfaction (Cavaiola, Peters, Hamdan & Lavender, 2012).
   157→   157→Personal resources also impact well-being indirectly via both a moderating and
   158→   158→mediating route. In a moderating or buffering role, differentiation of self has been shown to
   159→   159→protect individuals from the negative impact of work demands by altering their appraisal of
   160→   160→workplace stressors (Beebe, 2007; Krycak et al., 2012). Additionally, differentiation has been
   161→   161→shown to impact well-being via mediation routes including the use of healthy conflict
   162→   162→management styles (Beebe, 2007) and productive coping strategies (Murdock & Gore, 2004,
   163→   163→Krycak et al., 2012).
   164→   164→Differentiation of self has been shown to have an indirect positive impact on well-
   165→   165→being through enhancing relationships (Cochran, 2011; Beebe & Frisch, 2009). For example,
   166→   166→Krycak et al. (2012) demonstrated that both those who cut off from others or react
   167→   167→emotionally in relationships lose significant emotional support through their distancing or
   168→   168→disruptive behaviors which subsequently leads to psychological distress.
   169→   169→The proposal that a greater differentiation of self enables the activation and utilization
   170→   170→of relationships as resources thus offering a protective factor against stress and burnout
   171→   171→(Krycak et al., 2012) may overlap with the benefits afforded to individuals with a preference
   172→   172→for extraversion who tend to take a relational approach to emotionally challenging situations.
   173→   173→Extraverts engage with others and access social support more readily than introverts, this may
   174→   174→amplify perceived job resources and thus enhance well-being (Bakker, Boyd, Dollard,
   175→   175→Gillespie, Winefield & Stough., 2010).
   176→   176→Interventions to Enhance Workplace Relationships
   177→   177→
   178→   178→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 8
   179→   179→Research indicates the importance of workplace relationships in supporting work-
   180→   180→related psychological health by reducing job demands and increasing interpersonal resources.
   181→   181→However, there remain very few intervention studies focused on improving working
   182→   182→relationships (Day & Leiter, 2014). One organizational level intervention used group work
   183→   183→with co-workers to enhance team functioning through increased workplace civility (Leiter,
   184→   184→Day, Oore & Spence, 2012). Another intervention took a person-centered approach,
   185→   185→examining the impact on team relationships of developing individuals’ personal resources
   186→   186→through assertiveness workshops (Scarnera, Bosco, Soleti & Lancioni, 2009). Both these
   187→   187→interventions found improvements in participant burnout scores which were maintained over
   188→   188→time.
   189→   189→The success of these interpersonal interventions highlights the value of investing in
   190→   190→resources that enhance workplace relationships, however such approaches focus on very
   191→   191→specific elements of relational interactions and the intervention style continues to be
   192→   192→essentially individualistic, not giving consideration to the impact of complex systemic
   193→   193→relational interactions, the reenactment of historical relational patterns and the impact of
   194→   194→organizational pressure, all of which can deleteriously impact team functioning and
   195→   195→managerial relationships (Friedman, 1985).
   196→   196→Bowen Systems Coaching
   197→   197→Bowen systems coaching is a psychological intervention that does incorporate
   198→   198→systemic understanding, exploration of interpersonal patterns and intergenerational dynamics
   199→   199→alongside intrapsychic work in order to enhance both personal resources and relational
   200→   200→functioning (Bowen, 1978). Originally a first-generation family therapy approach, Bowen
   201→   201→family systems theory was subsequently applied to the organizational “family” (Friedman,
   202→   202→1985;1999) and Bowen coaching is now used within organizational consultancy (Kott, 2014),
   203→   203→
   204→   204→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 9
   205→   205→leadership development (Wilke, 2015) and work-related well-being interventions (Grosch &
   206→   206→Olsen, 1994, 2000).
   207→   207→Bowen theory, as applied to work-related psychological health, situates individuals
   208→   208→within their organizational, nuclear family and generational systems, understanding stress to
   209→   209→be less the result of a quantitative notion such as ‘overwork’ and more the effect of the
   210→   210→interaction between an individual’s position in their organization’s anxious relational system
   211→   211→and the quality of their own personal resources (Friedman, 1985). Bowen coaching’s
   212→   212→overarching goal is to increase the personal resource of differentiation of self, enabling a more
   213→   213→mature response to stressors thus promoting well-being (Kerr & Bowen, 1988). As a highly
   214→   214→practical, systemic approach, Bowen coaching engages with personal functioning at both an
   215→   215→individual and group level resulting in the development of both personal resources and
   216→   216→practical interpersonal skills (Galindo & Mills, 2016).
   217→   217→Research into the value of Bowen coaching within the workplace has primarily
   218→   218→focused on the beneficial impact of raising one’s level of differentiation on leadership
   219→   219→(Aufderhar & Flowers, 2010; Galindo & Mills, 2016) and clinical skills (Donnelly & Gosbee,
   220→   220→2009; Renshall et al., 2013). Those studies which utilized Bowen coaching for burnout
   221→   221→prevention and recuperation in psychotherapists (Grosch and Olsen, 1994) and clergy (Grosch
   222→   222→and Olsen, 2000) applied a qualitative approach.
   223→   223→Whether Bowen coaching was focused on improving psychological health or
   224→   224→enhancing workplace functioning findings reveal personal psychological benefits including
   225→   225→reduced reactivity, increased calmness and thoughtfulness under pressure and improved
   226→   226→boundary setting together with decreased levels of anxiety and self-blame (Aufderhar &
   227→   227→Flowers, 2010; Chambers, 2009; Galindo & Mills, 2016; Grosch & Olsen, 2000; Renshall et
   228→   228→al., 2013). Positive interpersonal changes were also reported, including the ability to build
   229→   229→healthier relationships, embrace and better manage challenging encounters, focus on
   230→   230→
   231→   231→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 10
   232→   232→encouraging health in organizations rather than focusing on crises (Galindo & Mills, 2016)
   233→   233→and enhanced empathy (Benyei, 1988; Donnelly & Gosbee, 2009). These relational
   234→   234→improvements infiltrated relationships beyond the workplace (Aufderhar & Flowers, 2010;
   235→   235→Galindo & Mills, 2016) and clergy also reported experiencing spiritual renewal (Grosch &
   236→   236→Olsen, 2000).
   237→   237→Currently there is only a small body of research into the impact of Bowen coaching
   238→   238→within the workplace, and all studies have employed qualitative methodology. However, the
   239→   239→results indicate the potential value of utilizing Bowen coaching as a person-centred
   240→   240→intervention to enhance work relationships both directly through improved interpersonal skills
   241→   241→and indirectly by enhancing personal resources and cross-sectional studies have developed
   242→   242→applicable quantitative measures (Beebe, 2007;2009).
   243→   243→Clergy Well-Being
   244→   244→The clerical profession has historically been seen as a group which is safe and secure
   245→   245→from stress or work pressure however, from the late 1980s a picture began to emerge that
   246→   246→clergy were not immune from the work-related burnout observed to be impacting other human
   247→   247→services professionals (e.g., Coate, 1989; Warren, 2002). A recent report into the current
   248→   248→healthcare needs for clergy identified that one third of all Church of England (CoE) clerical
   249→   249→sickness is now due to stress, anxiety or other mental health issues and these reasons were
   250→   250→cited by nearly half of those taking early retirement (St Luke’s Healthcare for the Clergy,
   251→   251→2010). Such factors not only impact clergy and their families, they also affect their
   252→   252→congregation (Rolfe, 2007) and ultimately the life and mission of the church (Burton &
   253→   253→Burton, 2009).
   254→   254→Clergy are core human service professionals and the satisfying rewards of ministry
   255→   255→continue to be inherently relational in nature however, interpersonal demands are increasing
   256→   256→alongside a decreased sense of relationships as supportive resources (Proeschold-Bell et al.,
   257→   257→
   258→   258→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 11
   259→   259→2015). Society’s increasing cultural secularization has left clergy feeling irrelevant and
   260→   260→socially isolated (Warren, 2002). The emergence of congregations-as-consumers (Miner,
   261→   261→Dowson & Sterland, 2010) has increased unrealistic expectations from parishioners and
   262→   262→heightened congregational conflict resulting in exhaustion and frustration (Berry, Francis,
   263→   263→Rolph & Rolph, 2012).
   264→   264→The negative impact of these relational demands is exacerbated by a perceived
   265→   265→absence of support from their congregation, peer ministers and senior church staff (Berry et
   266→   266→al., 2012) and clergy typically report experiences of loneliness and isolation in their roles
   267→   267→(McMinn et al., 2005). The presence of heightened relational demands with insufficient
   268→   268→resources interact to increase ministerial workloads, undermining clergy’s sense of autonomy,
   269→   269→increasing their role conflict and deteriorating their psychological health (Miner et al.,
   270→   270→2010).
   271→   271→The predominance of relational factors influencing the demands and resources of
   272→   272→ministry and the increasing impact of stress on clergy well-being suggests that a relationally
   273→   273→focused well-being intervention would be pertinent for clergy. Clergy can be reluctant to
   274→   274→engage in self-care that is not directly related to spirituality (Scott & Lovell, 2015) and
   275→   275→resistant to psychological interventions for fear of appearing to be a failure (Grosch & Olsen,
   276→   276→2000) or concerned over confidentiality; clergy support systems being intertwined with their
   277→   277→managerial church hierarchy (Berry et al., 2012). However, the explicit application of Bowen
   278→   278→theory to congregational life (Friedman, 1985) demonstrated its ministerial pertinence and
   279→   279→enhanced the acceptability of Bowen coaching for clergy.
   280→   280→Impact of Bowen Coaching on Clergy Relationships and Workplace Well-Being
   281→   281→This research study aims to quantitatively investigate the impact of Bowen coaching
   282→   282→on the well-being of Church of England clergy. The study will measure the change in personal
   283→   283→and relational resources available to participants in contrast with a control group who receive
   284→   284→
   285→   285→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 12
   286→   286→no intervention. In addition, the study will examine the impact on the subjective individual
   287→   287→appraisal of ministry demands and spiritual resources.
   288→   288→Hypotheses
   289→   289→1. Participants in the intervention group will show enhanced personal relational resources
   290→   290→relative to the control group in both interpersonal (H1a) and individual (H1b)
   291→   291→functioning.
   292→   292→2. Intervention participants will report enhanced relational resources and reduced
   293→   293→relational demands in contrast to the control group and these changes will be seen
   294→   294→across different systems. Specifically, the coaching group will report an increase in the
   295→   295→experience of senior staff support (H2a), peer support (H2b), congregational support
   296→   296→(H2c) and non-congregational support (H2d) across the time period.
   297→   297→3. At the end of the intervention there will be no change in the reported frequency of
   298→   298→relational ministry demands for either group (H3a). However, in contrast to the
   299→   299→control group, the intervention group will report a decrease in their perception of these
   300→   300→ministry demands as stressful (H3b).
   301→   301→4. Having completed the intervention, participants will show enhanced spiritual
   302→   302→resources in comparison to the control group (H4).

   344→   344→Bowen Coaching intervention
   345→   345→The coaching intervention consisted of six half-day group sessions spread out over six
   346→   346→to seven months. The intervention used the UK charity Bridge Builders’ “Leading with
   347→   347→Emotional Maturity” Bowen training course. Normally run as a three-day residential course,
   348→   348→the material was adapted into six sessions and facilitated by three experienced Bridge
   349→   349→Builders coaches. Group sizes were between 6 and 8 participants.
   350→   350→The overarching purpose of the course was to enable clergy to begin to use Bowen
   351→   351→systems theory as a lens through which to both understand the overarching shifting emotional
   352→   352→climate of a church congregation with its link to congregational behaviour and to gain insight
   353→   353→into personal patterns of emotional functioning within such systems, developing skills that
   354→   354→would enable participants to enhance their response to the emotional demands of ministry.
   355→   355→The six sessions explored and unpacked three main elements through a mixture of
   356→   356→didactic presentation, small group work, case studies and personal reflection. The first
   357→   357→element was to enable participants to establish a working understanding of Bowen theory, to
   358→   358→develop a systemic perspective and begin to understand emotional process as expressed
   359→   359→through the emotional field of relationship systems. Bowen theory incorporates eight
   360→   360→interlocking concepts: triangles, differentiation of self, nuclear family emotional system,
   361→   361→family projection process, multigenerational transmission process, emotional cutoff, sibling
   362→   362→position and societal emotional process (Kerr & Bowen, 1988). Together these concepts form
   363→   363→
   364→   364→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 15
   365→   365→a framework which can describe and interpret the complex interactions of individuals in
   366→   366→relationship systems (Galindo and Mills, 2016).
   367→   367→The second element within the course required participants to diagram their own
   368→   368→family tree (genogram) and use Bowen theory to explore the relationship patterns found there,
   369→   369→with a particular focus on identifying the participant’s own role in these repetitive
   370→   370→interactions. The third element drew the learning from both these first two elements together
   371→   371→in order to explore participants’ personally challenging ministry case studies. Bowen theory
   372→   372→and personal genograms were used to identify where clergy were replicating their own family
   373→   373→relational patterns to detrimental effect within the current congregational system. Coaching
   374→   374→then supported clergy to make adjustments to their patterns of behaviour, the impact of which
   375→   375→was reflected upon and discussed in subsequent sessions.
   376→   376→Measures
   377→   377→Personal resources. Personal resources were measured using items from three
   378→   378→subscales of the scale Differentiation of Self and Role – Clergy (DSR-C): I position, Fusion
   379→   379→with Others and Emotional Cutoff (Beebe, 2007). Responses were scored on a 6-point Likert
   380→   380→scale ranging from 1 (not very characteristic of me) to 6 (very characteristic of me). Two
   381→   381→subscales capture the interpersonal element of differentiation: The nine-item Fusion with
   382→   382→Others subscale unpacks the extent to which an individual over-identifies with others,
   383→   383→including tendencies to acquiesce to others’ expectations e.g., “I usually go along with what
   384→   384→others decide rather than ‘rock the boat’”. The eight-item Emotional Cutoff subscale assesses
   385→   385→the ability to engage with others in situations of conflict or heightened emotional
   386→   386→vulnerability, characterized by responses of distancing and denial e.g., “When there is a
   387→   387→conflict in the congregation, I often begin thinking about leaving for a new position”. The I
   388→   388→Position subscale captures the intrapsychic element of differentiation and includes 11 items
   389→   389→that assess an individual’s ability to maintain a well-defined sense of self despite pressures to
   390→   390→
   391→   391→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 16
   392→   392→conform to others’ demands or situational exigencies e.g., “When I disagree with someone, I
   393→   393→tend to encourage talking about the problem/issue”.
   394→   394→Beebe (2007) found that the DSR-C’s construct validity was supported by correlation
   395→   395→with Skowron and Friedlander’s (1998) Differentiation of Self Inventory (r = .83, p < .001),
   396→   396→good reliability was indicated (Cronbach’s alpha = .9) and there was a strong test-retest
   397→   397→coefficient (.88). Cronbach’s alpha scores for both intervention group and control group
   398→   398→participants for all research variables at both the pre- and post- time points are shown in Table
   399→   399→6 and all show acceptable levels of reliability given the number of items within each scale.

   403→   403→Relational demands and resources.Clerical experience of management support and
   404→   404→peer support were measured using two subscales from the short version of the HSE
   405→   405→Management Standards Indicators Tool (MSIT-S; Edwards & Webster, 2012) where
   406→   406→organizational pressure is evaluated on a 5-point Likert scale from 1 (never) to 5 (always).
   407→   407→The method of scoring means that a high score equates to a low level of work stressors. The 5
   408→   408→item Management Support scale includes items such as “I can rely on my line manager to help
   409→   409→me out with a work problem” and the 4 item Peer Support scale included “I get the help and
   410→   410→support I need from colleagues.” This scale was developed to remove redundant items from
   411→   411→the original 35-item measure of organizational stressors (Cousins et al., 2004) and was found
   412→   412→to be as reliable and valuable as the original (Edwards et al., 2012).
   413→   413→To ensure the tool was relevant for clergy generic business terms were changed to the
   414→   414→language of congregations: “Ministry” instead of “work”, “senior staff (i.e. Area Dean,
   415→   415→Archdeacon or Area Bishop)” replaced “line manager” and “diocese” replaced “organization”.
   416→   416→
   417→   417→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 17
   418→   418→These terms were taken from other scales used within this research and their relevance and
   419→   419→appropriateness confirmed with clergy.
   420→   420→Congregational support was measured using the Congregational Support Scale
   421→   421→-Abbreviated (CSS-A). Respondents use a 4-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly
   422→   422→disagree) to 4 (strongly agree) to respond to five questions exploring the presence of
   423→   423→guidance e.g., “I have someone in the congregation to talk to about decisions in my life”,
   424→   424→reliable alliance e.g., “There are people in the congregation who will help me if I really need
   425→   425→it”, reassurance of worth, attachment and social integration from their congregations.
   426→   426→These provisions in social relationships were identified as necessary by Cutrona and
   427→   427→Russell (1987). Lee (2010) subsequently reworded items in the first four categories to address
   428→   428→congregations as provisions of support. The fifth category was also deemed relevant for this
   429→   429→research therefore one item was taken from Caron (2013)’s abbreviated Social Provisions
   430→   430→Scale (also based on Cutrona & Russell’s research). Lee (2010)’s 15-item tool showed a high
   431→   431→internal consistency, with an alpha coefficient of .89.
   432→   432→Interpersonal resources utilized by clergy outside the congregation was assessed using
   433→   433→the non-congregational support scale (NCCS-A) which used the same five questions as the
   434→   434→CSS-A scale with adjusted wording. For example, instead of asking participants to respond to
   435→   435→the statement: “There are people in my congregation who value my skills and abilities”, it
   436→   436→asked them to indicate how much they agreed with the statement: “There are friends or
   437→   437→colleagues outside the congregation who value my skills and abilities”.
   438→   438→Work demands. The Ministry Demand Inventory (MDI) was used to measure both the
   439→   439→frequency of interpersonal congregational demands experienced by clergy and the perceived
   440→   440→severity or intrusiveness of these demands (Lee, 1999). The MDI is a 15-item self-assessed
   441→   441→scale which asks participants to indicate how often four types of ministerial social situation
   442→   442→have occurred in the past six months: Personal Criticism of the minister; Presumptive
   443→   443→
   444→   444→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 18
   445→   445→Expectations of the minister's flexibility and availability e.g., “Your sleep was interrupted by
   446→   446→a phone call from a member,”; Boundary Ambiguity e.g., “A member came by your home
   447→   447→unannounced.”, and Criticism of the minister's family. Then participants indicate how
   448→   448→intrusive they found this behavior on a four-point Likert scale from 1 (none) to 4 (high). Lee
   449→   449→(1999) employed this tool with a group of 312 American Protestant clergy and found that the
   450→   450→tool may be taken as a single scale with good internal consistency reliability for both the
   451→   451→frequency and impact measures (both Cronbach's alphas were .82).
   452→   452→Spiritual resources. The Spiritual Dryness Scale (SDS, Büssing, Günther, Baumann,
   453→   453→Frick & Jacobs, 2013) was used as a measure of the spiritual resource of spiritual relatedness.
   454→   454→The SDS is a 6-item, self-administered scale of uni-dimensional structure where individuals
   455→   455→are required to rate statements on a five-point Likert scale from 1 (not at all) to 5 (regularly),
   456→   456→for example, “I have the feeling that God has abandoned me completely”. The original
   457→   457→validation study with Roman Catholic priests found the scale had good internal consistency
   458→   458→(Cronbach’s alpha = .87) and validity (Büssing et al., 2013).

   552→   552→DISCUSSION
   553→   553→Interpersonal and social factors are fundamentally important to the establishment of
   554→   554→“good work” that enhances performance and supports well-being yet identifying the impact of
   555→   555→interventions to enhance work-place relationships continues to be an under-researched area.
   556→   556→This study employed a longitudinal, control group design to determine the impact of Bowen
   557→   557→systems coaching, a person-centred relationally-focused intervention, on the well-being of
   558→   558→CoE clergy.
   559→   559→In support of the study’s hypotheses, the intervention group when compared to the
   560→   560→control group reported a significantly lower level of relational fusion with others and an
   561→   561→almost significant lower level of interpersonal emotional cutoff, both representing medium
   562→   562→effect sizes (Richardson, 2011). The coaching group also reported higher levels of supportive
   563→   563→
   564→   564→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 22
   565→   565→interpersonal relations with senior staff, peer clergy and members of their congregation. The
   566→   566→effect size was medium for senior staff and congregational support, and small for peer
   567→   567→support.
   568→   568→The results also indicate a positive link between these personal and relational resource
   569→   569→improvements and affective well-being. Aligned with the study’s hypotheses while there was
   570→   570→no difference in the frequency of ministry demands experienced by the two groups, the
   571→   571→intervention group reported an almost significant decrease of medium effect size in the
   572→   572→perception of these demands as stressful in comparison to the control group. In addition, they
   573→   573→reported an increase in spiritual resources of medium effect size.
   574→   574→In sum, our findings demonstrate that Bowen coaching, a well-established
   575→   575→psychological therapeutic intervention can be utilized within the ministerial workplace to
   576→   576→improve relationships and promote work-related well-being. These findings lend support for
   577→   577→the Krycak et al’s (2012) proposal that proactively developing the personal resource of
   578→   578→differentiation of self enhances the awareness and utilization of relationships as resources
   579→   579→through increasing relational accessibility, expressed through a reduction in relational fusion
   580→   580→and cut-off. While the focus of the intervention was on work relationships, the results also
   581→   581→offer support for the buffering role of differentiation, protecting individuals from the negative
   582→   582→impact of work demands by altering appraisal of workplace stressors (Beebe, 2007).
   583→   583→Contrary to the hypotheses, at the end of the intervention there was no difference
   584→   584→between the two groups in the differentiation of self I position subscale which measured
   585→   585→capacity to maintain a well-defined sense of self. This was due to an increased score in both
   586→   586→coaching and control groups. Bowen theory distinguishes between two types of
   587→   587→differentiation, basic and functional and coaching aims to target the basic, average level of
   588→   588→functioning that operates irrespective of emotional conditions. In contrast, the functional level
   589→   589→is the actual level of differentiation displayed on a day-to-day basis which is negatively
   590→   590→
   591→   591→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 23
   592→   592→correlated with stress (Frost, 2014). The control group reported a decrease in stressful
   593→   593→demands at the end of the intervention thus these results may indicate that the I position
   594→   594→subscale is more vulnerable to stress than differentiation’s relational subscales.
   595→   595→The experience of non-congregational support was also in the opposite direction to
   596→   596→that hypothesised, contrasting with previous qualitative research identifying that Bowen
   597→   597→coaching within the ministerial context enhanced external relationships (Aufderhar &
   598→   598→Flowers, 2010). It may be that for this study’s participants, focusing on work relationships
   599→   599→and congregational challenges led to a reduced sense of connection with relationships outside
   600→   600→the congregation. Alternatively, the result may reflect the interpersonal dynamic that comes
   601→   601→with adjusting one’s historically established relational patterns and temporarily unsettling
   602→   602→previously established relationships (Friedman, 1985). It may also have been due to a lack of
   603→   603→clarity in the survey questions which did not specify whether the support was to be from
   604→   604→family or non-family members, the former being more important for ministerial support than
   605→   605→the latter (Scott & Lovell, 2015).
   606→   606→Limitations and Implications for Future Research
   607→   607→Practical constraints on the project limited the number of coaching groups which could
   608→   608→be run to three. The experimental design therefore sought a minimum of 20 participants in
   609→   609→both intervention and control group to ensure statistical relevance. Unfortunately, at the final
   610→   610→analysis both groups dropped below 20 and it is possible this small sample size restricted the
   611→   611→capacity to meaningfully test the study’s hypotheses. Repetition or extension of the research
   612→   612→should involve a larger sample size to make conclusions more robust. A follow up research
   613→   613→phase to identify how changes were maintained in the longer term would also be beneficial.
   614→   614→ANCOVA tests have more power in randomized studies and more bias in non-
   615→   615→randomized studies (Van Breuklen, 2006) therefore ideally participants would have been
   616→   616→randomized to generate comparable intervention groups, remove extraneous variables, prevent
   617→   617→
   618→   618→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 24
   619→   619→selection bias and minimize accidental bias (Kang, Ragan & Park, 2008). Although no
   620→   620→significant differences were found between the group groups in relation to demographic or
   621→   621→study variables the selection bias implicit in the self-selecting recruitment process could have
   622→   622→negatively impacted study results (Suresh, 2011). Future studies would significantly benefit
   623→   623→from the random allocation of participants between coaching and control groups.
   624→   624→The eligibility criteria reflected the most numerous group of clergy with the CoE and
   625→   625→gathered a broad demographic of participants by locating coaching groups across the UK.
   626→   626→This suggests that in principle the study results may be generalizable to parochially based
   627→   627→CoE clergy across the UK. Given that clerical research consistently highlights the same
   628→   628→ministry pressures and resources across different roles and denominations (e.g. Proeschold-
   629→   629→Bell et al., 2015), it suggests that the results may also be generalizable to CoE clergy
   630→   630→occupying non-parochial roles and UK clergy in other denominations. Future research to
   631→   631→explore this would be highly pertinent.
   632→   632→This longitudinal study has highlighted the potential positive correlation between three
   633→   633→types of interpersonal variables: the interpersonal aspect of the personal resource
   634→   634→differentiation of self, work relationships and spiritual relatedness. Jankowski and Vaughn’s
   635→   635→(2009) research suggests that differentiation may be related to spiritual relatedness through an
   636→   636→alteration in the interpretation of circumstances. Rather than feeling a failure and perhaps
   637→   637→withdrawing from or feeling abandoned by God when faced with challenging congregational
   638→   638→scenarios, those with higher levels of differentiation would be more likely to remain
   639→   639→connected with God as they journey through the process. Future cross-sectional research with
   640→   640→a larger sample of clergy could clarify and model these relationships, exploring their
   641→   641→relationship to overall well-being.
   642→   642→
   643→   643→Preprint: Good Work through Good Relationships? (Kissell & Moser, 2026) 25
   644→   644→Clergy are one of many human services professionals facing increasing relational
   645→   645→demands and decreasing interpersonal resources within the workplace (e.g. Rao et al., 2016;
   646→   646→McNicoll, 2016). In spite of Bowen’s original emphasis on the value of Bowen coaching to
   647→   647→improve the well-being and clinical work of therapists (Kerr & Bowen, 1988) there has been
   648→   648→relatively little research into the impact of Bowen coaching within the workplace beyond the
   649→   649→ministry context. Future research exploring the impact of Bowen coaching on other
   650→   650→professionals would therefore be pertinent.
   651→   651→CONCLUSION
   652→   652→The identification of effective, pertinent and accessible interventions to improve
   653→   653→workplace relationships is becoming increasingly important as flattened, team-based
   654→   654→organisational structures and accelerating organisational change place increasing strain on
   655→   655→relationships. The findings from this study suggest that a person-centred Bowen coaching
   656→   656→intervention can help lay the foundation for improved well-being in the long-term by
   657→   657→developing interpersonal skills, increasing the awareness and utilization of relationships as
   658→   658→resources, reducing the perception of work demands as stressful and enhancing spiritual
   659→   659→resources.
