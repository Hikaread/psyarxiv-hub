    25→    25→Abstract
    26→    26→Neuroticism is one of the most consequential personality traits for individuals and society.
    27→    27→Interventions that facilitate changes in this trait may thus have far-reaching benefits, with initial
    28→    28→evidence supporting that interventions can foster lasting neuroticism change. Most personality
    29→    29→theories posit that changes in broad, enduring traits are mediated via lower-level changes in
    30→    30→momentary states and intermediary habits. However, there are few direct tests of this theorized
    31→    31→bottom-up pathway, leaving the mechanisms, direction, and timing of effects unclear. Using
    32→    32→data from the Changing How I Live Life Study (N = 444), which evaluated a digital intervention
    33→    33→to reduce neuroticism, we examined 1) the shape and magnitude of intervention-induced
    34→    34→neuroticism change across three timescales, 2) concurrent and lagged associations among these
    35→    35→changes, and 3) whether these associations varied across treatment groups receiving strategies
    36→    36→targeting different neuroticism levels. We found that neuroticism changed nonlinearly across
    37→    37→the intervention; changes in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism measures reliably
    38→    38→predicted one another, with bottom-up effects often being larger in magnitude; and mean-level
    39→    39→changes and coupled changes in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism measures were
    40→    40→larger in the intervention groups than in the active control group. These results support the
    41→    41→conceptual and causal importance of both bottom-up and top-down (momentary, specific ⟷
    42→    42→long-term, broad) change processes. Together, this study tested key theoretical predictions about
    43→    43→the process and directionality of intervention-induced personality change to help elucidate
    44→    44→mechanisms underlying intentional personality changes and generate insights about how to
    45→    45→enhance intervention efficacy.
    46→    46→Keywords: personality; volitional change; bottom-up process; timescale; neuroticism
    47→    47→Public Significance Statement
    48→    48→A smartphone intervention successfully decreased momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism
    49→    49→measures in motivated members of the public, with groups receiving state-level interventions
    50→    50→showing stronger effects. Intervention-induced changes are driven by both bottom-up and top-
    51→    51→down processes (i.e., changes in momentary states lead to changes in more enduring traits, and
    52→    52→vice versa), highlighting many theoretical mechanisms that can be used to target neuroticism.

    55→    55→Intervention-Induced Neuroticism Change Across Multiple Timescales
    56→    56→People with high levels of neuroticism tend to be highly sensitive to threats, experience
    57→    57→frequent negative affect, and strongly react to stress (Barlow et al., 2014). Neuroticism has been
    58→    58→linked to many detrimental outcomes, including physical and mental health problems, adverse
    59→    59→life events, and premature mortality (Jokela et al., 2013; Wright & Jackson, 2023). Many people
    60→    60→would like to be less neurotic (Baranski et al., 2021), and interventions that can reduce
    61→    61→neuroticism stand to widely benefit public health and personal well-being (Bleidorn et al.,
    62→    62→2019). Growing research suggests that interventions can successfully promote lasting changes
    63→    63→in personality traits like neuroticism (Stieger et al., 2021; Wright, Haehner, et al., 2025).
    64→    64→However, the timescale and mechanisms of these intervention-induced changes remain unclear.
    65→    65→Personality can be assessed over multiple timescales and conceptualized across different
    66→    66→functional levels that range from momentary, situation-specific states; intermediate, contextual
    67→    67→habits; to broad, enduring traits (Allemand & Flückiger, 2017; Baumert et al., 2017). These
    68→    68→different levels can reciprocally influence each other over time (i.e., states ⟷ habits ⟷ traits),
    69→    69→with most theories positing that personality change is primarily driven by bottom-up processes
    70→    70→whereby changes in momentary states form into novel, intermediate habits that generalize to
    71→    71→longer-lasting, broad trait change (Geukes et al., 2018; Hudson, 2021; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).
    72→    72→Although theories converge on directional pathways across levels, there are few causal tests of
    73→    73→the link between changes in states, habits, and traits (Küchler et al., 2025; Stieger et al., 2022).
    74→    74→In this study, we investigated how intervention-induced neuroticism changes measured
    75→    75→over three timescales were related and whether their relations varied across groups receiving
    76→    76→intervention strategies targeting different levels of neuroticism. To do so, we used data from the
    77→    77→Changing How I Live Life (CHILL) Study (Haehner et al., 2025). The CHILL Study tested a
    78→    78→digital neuroticism intervention in which participants were randomized to one of four treatment
    79→    79→groups (TGs) and reported on their neuroticism during specific moments, at the end of each
    80→    80→
    81→    81→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 4
    82→    82→day, and at the end of each week before, during, and after the intervention. This allowed us to
    83→    83→test predictions from contemporary personality theories about the timescales and mechanisms
    84→    84→of intervention-induced personality change and generate ways to optimize intervention efficacy.
    85→    85→Volitional Personality Trait Changes
    86→    86→Although once thought to be highly stable, robust evidence shows that personality traits
    87→    87→can change across the lifespan (Bleidorn et al., 2022; Seifert et al., 2024; Wright, Krämer, et al.,
    88→    88→2025). Evidence of this malleability led to questions of whether people can intentionally change
    89→    89→their personality. These changes may help people achieve better career, relationship, and health
    90→    90→outcomes as downstream effects (Bleidorn et al., 2019). Neuroticism in particular is highly
    91→    91→consequential for individuals and society due to its far-reaching implications (Lahey, 2009). For
    92→    92→example, Wright and Jackson (2023) found that higher levels of and increases in neuroticism
    93→    93→prospectively predicted worse health status, more health limitations, and job loss. Neuroticism’s
    94→    94→public health costs even surpass those of mental disorders like depression and anxiety (Cuijpers
    95→    95→et al., 2010), underscoring the potential value of its volitional change efforts.
    96→    96→Initial research suggests that interventions can promote lasting changes in self-reported
    97→    97→personality traits (see Haehner et al., 2024, for a review). For example, the digital intervention
    98→    98→that we examine in the present study has been found to help motivated members of the public
    99→    99→decrease their neuroticism trait levels (|d| = 0.24 to 0.40; Wright, Haehner, et al., 2025). Similar
   100→   100→to other smartphone interventions (e.g., Stieger et al., 2021), effects emerged across self-reports
   101→   101→and some peer-reports, with intervention-induced changes exceeding normative changes and
   102→   102→demand effects during a waiting period and persisting at a 2-month follow-up. We extend this
   103→   103→work by including neuroticism measures at other timescales, testing the direction(s) over which
   104→   104→changes unfold, and examining mechanisms of these cross-timescale intentional changes.
   105→   105→Theories on Personality Trait Changes
   106→   106→Common across theories is a distinction between different levels of personality that vary
   107→   107→
   108→   108→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 5
   109→   109→in breadth and stability (Allemand & Flückiger, 2017; Wright et al., 2026). At the narrowest
   110→   110→level, personality states are momentary affects, behaviors, or cognitions that are assessed at fine
   111→   111→temporal resolutions (e.g., hourly; Fleeson & Jayawickreme, 2015). At the intermediate level,
   112→   112→habits are domain-specific tendencies that are assessed at moderate temporal resolutions (e.g.,
   113→   113→daily) to capture repeated affects/behaviors/cognitions in various contexts (Gardner et al.,
   114→   114→2012). At the broadest level, traits are dispositional patterns of affects, behaviors, and cognitions
   115→   115→that are assessed over longer time periods (e.g., monthly) or in general. In this study, we use the
   116→   116→timescales at which different measures were assessed and their instructed reference periods to
   117→   117→distinguish between levels (e.g., “right now,” “past day”, “past week”).1
   118→   118→Building on this three-level model, theories have posited that personality change occurs
   119→   119→in a bottom-up or top-down manner. The more common theoretical premise is that personality
   120→   120→change is best captured by a bottom-up process, whereby repeated changes in momentary
   121→   121→personality states lead to changes in trait-relevant habits that, over time, generalize to enduring
   122→   122→changes in people’s generalized trait self-concepts (Figure 1; Haehner et al., 2024; Hennecke et
   123→   123→al., 2014; Hudson, 2021). For example, someone wishing to become less neurotic must decrease
   124→   124→their momentary negative emotions or thoughts in specific situations, practice enacting those
   125→   125→changes frequently, maintain changes long enough for them to become automatic in or linked
   126→   126→to certain contexts, and have these habitual changes generalize into more enduring trait change.
   127→   127→Other theoretical perspectives also posit a top-down pathway of personality change that
   128→   128→originates at the trait level, shifts trait-relevant habits, then alters momentary personality states
   129→   129→(Allemand & Flückiger, 2017; Jackson & Wright, 2024). From this perspective, someone may
   130→   130→start perceiving themselves as less anxious, for example, due to new feedback or reference

   186→   186→The Present Study
   187→   187→This study aimed to advance insight into the timing, direction, and process of intentional
   188→   188→personality change. We used data from the CHILL Intervention that has been found to elicit
   189→   189→lasting changes in self- and partly peer-report trait neuroticism beyond normative changes,
   190→   190→regression-to-the-mean, and demand effects (Wright, Haehner, et al., 2025). Participants were
   191→   191→randomized to four TGs that received different combinations of strategies targeting the trait,
   192→   192→habit, and/or state levels of neuroticism (Wright et al., 2026). Participants completed
   193→   193→momentary, daily, and weekly surveys before, during, and after the 6-week intervention.
   194→   194→In line with theory (Hennecke et al., 2014; Jackson & Wright, 2024; Wrzus & Roberts,
   195→   195→2017), we expected significant declines across measures, such that the intervention would elicit
   196→   196→momentary-, daily-, and weekly-level decreases. At the between-person level, we expected
   197→   197→declines in shorter-timescale measures to be related to declines in longer-timescale measures.
   198→   198→Consistent with bottom-up views, we hypothesized that within-person changes in narrower,
   199→   199→shorter-term measures would predict within-person changes in broader, longer-term measures.
   200→   200→Finally, we expected TGs receiving strategies targeting state, habit, and/or trait neuroticism to
   201→   201→vary in amounts, patterns, and directions of cross-timescale change. Specifically, we expected
   202→   202→changes in shorter-timescale measures and bottom-up effects from lower to higher levels to be
   203→   203→more pronounced for TGs in which these lower levels were directly targeted (Table 1).

   309→   309→(Rammstedt et al., 2020; Soto & John, 2017) via weekly surveys. Six neuroticism items (e.g.,

   317→   317→All models were multilevel with timepoints (t) nested in participants (i). For RQ1-RQ2,
   318→   318→we fit generalized additive multilevel models (GAMMs) using the gamm4 R package (Wood &
   319→   319→Scheipl, 2020). For GAMMs, our significance criterion was a = .01 and we derived 99%
   320→   320→confidence intervals (CoIs). For RQ3-RQ4, we fit residual dynamic structural equation models
   321→   321→(RDSEMs) in Mplus (V9; Múthen & Múthen, 2025). For RDSEMs, we evaluated significance
   322→   322→using parameters’ 99% credible intervals (CrIs). For detailed descriptions of all main analyses,
   323→   323→exploratory analyses, and sensitivity analyses, please see File S1, Section A.
   324→   324→RQ1: Neuroticism Changes Across Timescales During the Intervention Period
   325→   325→For RQ1, we used GAMMs to explore functional forms of momentary, daily, and weekly
   326→   326→neuroticism changes. GAMMs allow trajectory shapes to be flexibly explored without needing
   327→   327→to prespecify forms (Wood, 2023). Our smoothing functions were thin plate regression splines
   328→   328→that varied across k inflection points. For weekly neuroticism, we used the highest possible k =

   339→   339→RQ2: TG-Specific Neuroticism Changes Across Timescales During the Intervention Period
   340→   340→For RQ2, we fit two GAMMs per measure that now modeled TG differences. To do so,
   341→   341→we added a TG factor variable to RQ1’s GAMMs to estimate main effects and interactions with

   351→   351→For RQ3, we fit one RDSEM per measure combination (Momentary-Daily, Momentary-
   352→   352→Weekly, Daily-Weekly). Scores decompose into stable between-person differences and within-

   354→   354→within-person temporal dynamics (e.g., momentary ® daily cross-lagged effects) from within-

   753→   753→Discussion
   754→   754→A core premise of contemporary personality change theories is that repeated changes in
   755→   755→momentary personality states lead to more enduring changes in generalized trait self-concepts
   756→   756→through bottom-up processes (Geukes et al., 2018; Hudson, 2021; Jackson & Wright, 2024;
   757→   757→Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). In this paper, we tested this hypothesis by examining intervention-
   758→   758→induced neuroticism changes across three timescales and testing their relations over time.
   759→   759→We found significant declines in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism measures
   760→   760→across the intervention. Declines in weekly neuroticism occurred more rapidly than declines in
   761→   761→daily and momentary measures, but the latter two had larger total declines that continued across
   762→   762→the intervention. Within 4 weeks, momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism changes in the
   763→   763→intervention groups significantly exceeded changes in the active control group, supporting the
   764→   764→causal utility of the intervention to elicit cross-timescale neuroticism changes. Changes in all
   765→   765→measures were also strongly related, indicating that people with greater declines in shorter-
   766→   766→
   767→   767→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 23
   768→   768→timescale measures also had greater declines in longer-timescale measures. Then, in line with
   769→   769→theory, significant bottom-up effects emerged across all timescales, with changes in shorter-
   770→   770→timescale measures predicting changes in longer-timescale measures. Although corresponding
   771→   771→top-down effects in the sample were also significant, indicating that changes in more enduring
   772→   772→neuroticism measures likewise predicted changes in more transient measures, top-down effects
   773→   773→tended to be smaller than bottom-up effects. Finally, there were some differences in the timing
   774→   774→and coupling of cross-timescale neuroticism changes associated with the different level-specific
   775→   775→strategies assigned to TGs, providing some indications on how to enhance intervention efficacy.
   776→   776→Overall, our study offers insights into the timescales over which changes in neuroticism
   777→   777→unfold, directional mechanisms of intervention-induced change, and potential ways to optimize
   778→   778→intervention efficacy by targeting different levels of personality. We discuss these points below.
   779→   779→Timing of Changes in Neuroticism Across Different Timescales
   780→   780→With 8 weeks of intensive longitudinal data with momentary, daily, and weekly surveys,
   781→   781→this study provided a fine-grained test of intervention-driven personality change across multiple
   782→   782→timescales. Neuroticism changed relatively quickly during the intervention, with 50% of all
   783→   783→declines occurring within the first 2 to 4 weeks of the intervention. This finding is in line with
   784→   784→recent research showing that most intervention-driven personality trait change occurs early in
   785→   785→interventions (Allemand et al., 2024; Roberts et al., 2017; Stieger et al., 2022), contradicting
   786→   786→former views of personality as stable, fixed characteristics (Bleidorn, 2024).
   787→   787→Further, we found that changes during the 6-week intervention plateaued around Week
   788→   788→5. This corresponds with results by Stieger et al. (2022), who found that intervention-induced
   789→   789→changes in personality states and traits plateaued after approximately 4 weeks. Generally, these
   790→   790→findings suggest that insights from clinical dose-effect research may be applied to personality
   791→   791→trait interventions, whereby rapid changes occur in the first few weeks of treatment and plateau
   792→   792→thereafter (Robinson et al., 2020). As such, we may now tentatively conclude that personality
   793→   793→
   794→   794→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 24
   795→   795→interventions lasting a few weeks are sufficient to elicit lasting personality trait changes
   796→   796→(Roberts et al., 2017; Stieger et al., 2021). However, future research on the long-term effects of
   797→   797→personality interventions, and the effects of possible booster sessions, is still needed.
   798→   798→Our study also offers novel insights into the shape of intervention-induced neuroticism
   799→   799→changes across timescales. Interestingly, we found that total declines in weekly neuroticism
   800→   800→occurred more rapidly than total declines in momentary states and intermediate, daily habits.
   801→   801→This result may be surprising as most theories highlight the relevance of bottom-up processes,
   802→   802→with changes in momentary states theorized to drive changes in broader personality traits
   803→   803→(Hennecke et al., 2014; Hudson, 2021; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). One possible explanation is
   804→   804→that rapid, early declines in the weekly measure reflect biases in self-perceptions of participants
   805→   805→who expected their neuroticism levels to change, because they were participating in an
   806→   806→intervention (Krämer et al., 2025). Alternatively, this pattern may highlight the relevance of
   807→   807→cognitive (reflective) processes in personality interventions: If people regularly think about their
   808→   808→trait levels and changes, they may perceive changes in their general trait expressions prior to
   809→   809→any corresponding enacted changes. If these self-concept changes are salient in certain
   810→   810→situations, this may influence how they think, feel, and behave in given moments, thereby
   811→   811→leading to corresponding changes in trait-relevant states and habits. To explicitly address this
   812→   812→possibility, we examined how changes in timescale-specific neuroticism levels were related.
   813→   813→Top-Down and Bottom-Up Effects of Personality Trait Interventions
   814→   814→Personality development theories posit that changes in different levels of personality are
   815→   815→related, with most theories outlining a bottom-up process whereby changes in momentary states
   816→   816→predict changes in daily, trait-relevant habits that, subsequently, predict changes in broader traits
   817→   817→(Hudson, 2021; Jackson & Wright, 2024; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). In line with these theories,
   818→   818→we found significant bottom-up effects across all timescale-specific neuroticism measures. This
   819→   819→suggests that intervention-induced changes in neuroticism states indeed broaden into novel
   820→   820→
   821→   821→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 25
   822→   822→neuroticism-related habits over time, with these intermediate habitual changes then generalizing
   823→   823→to changes in more enduring neuroticism trait levels. These results support the colloquial idea
   824→   824→of “fake it till you make it,” such that they highlight the efficacy of acting out of character in a
   825→   825→given moment to change one’s broader trait levels over time (Jackson & Wright, 2024).
   826→   826→Although our results support the presence of this bottom-up pathway, we also found the
   827→   827→reverse effect, with changes in broader, higher levels of neuroticism predicting future changes
   828→   828→in narrower, lower levels (i.e., top-down effects). In contrast to repetition and generalization
   829→   829→which are focal aspects for successful bottom-up change, our top-down effects rather point to
   830→   830→the relevance of cognitive processes (e.g., associative, reflective) in personality interventions
   831→   831→(Jackson & Wright, 2024; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). A top-down effect from a personality trait
   832→   832→to state could, for example, indicate that people must first perceive a change in their trait self-
   833→   833→concepts. Perceiving these changes may encourage someone to enact such changes in their daily
   834→   834→life or to select into different situations that then afford them greater opportunities to behave in
   835→   835→ways that align with their perceived changes. These top-down effects thus imply that “if you
   836→   836→believe it, you can achieve it” may be another mechanism to foster lasting personality changes.
   837→   837→By testing top-down and bottom-up effects in the model, this study moved beyond
   838→   838→existing research, as prior studies have typically focused on bottom-up effects as the dominating
   839→   839→mechanism (Küchler et al., 2025; Olaru et al., 2025). Overall, our findings support the causal
   840→   840→relevance of both bottom-up and top-down processes for intervention-induced personality as
   841→   841→both effects emerged over different timescales. One caveat to this conclusion, though, is that
   842→   842→bottom-up effects tended to be larger in magnitude than their corresponding top-down effects.
   843→   843→In particular, bottom-up effects originating at the state level always exceeded corresponding
   844→   844→top-down effects in our main analyses, and our sensitivity analyses indicated that all bottom-up
   845→   845→effects significantly exceeded their associated top-down effects. Nonetheless, theories and
   846→   846→future research on volitional personality change should likely incorporate both directions of
   847→   847→
   848→   848→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 26
   849→   849→effects and examine specific hypotheses on how these processes unfold over time (Jackson &
   850→   850→Wright, 2024). For example, future studies could test whether and how specific cognitive
   851→   851→mechanisms, like reflective processes, drive top-down effects (Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).
   852→   852→Treatment Group Differences
   853→   853→A key feature of the present study was examining intervention-induced neuroticism
   854→   854→changes across TGs that received distinct combinations of intervention strategies theorized to
   855→   855→target different levels of neuroticism. In contrast to the active control group that received
   856→   856→theoretically inert trait-level strategies, we found that the three intervention groups that also
   857→   857→received habit and/or state strategies had stronger, longer-lasting changes in all timescale-
   858→   858→specific measures. Specifically, mean-level trends in the joint intervention groups significantly
   859→   859→exceeded trends in the active control group across analyses and their rates of decline for
   860→   860→momentary, daily, and weekly measures surpassed declines in the active control group within 2
   861→   861→to 4 weeks. Moreover, the joint intervention groups had larger bottom-up effects from
   862→   862→momentary to daily changes, whereas the active control group had more prominent top-down
   863→   863→effects in this model. Such findings support the causal relevance of the intervention to elicit
   864→   864→mean-level changes in neuroticism across timescales and to initiate the theorized bottom-up
   865→   865→process of personality change.
   866→   866→Further, we found tentative evidence that targeting the state level of neuroticism is
   867→   867→particularly critical for intervention efficacy. The State and Habit+State Groups, which both
   868→   868→received state strategies, evidenced stronger changes than the active control group in all
   869→   869→neuroticism measures. The Habit+State Group also had stronger changes across measures than
   870→   870→the Habit Group, and the State Group similarly had stronger weekly changes than the Habit
   871→   871→Group. This suggests that targeting habits, in addition to states, may be particularly effective
   872→   872→for lasting intervention-driven personality change. Additionally, the State Group consistently
   873→   873→had more pronounced bottom-up effects than other TGs, again underscoring the utility of
   874→   874→
   875→   875→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 27
   876→   876→targeting states to initiate theorized bottom-up processes of personality trait changes. Together,
   877→   877→targeting momentary states––thereby capitalizing on bottom-up effects originating from this
   878→   878→lowest level to influence intermediate habits and broader traits––may be particularly fruitful for
   879→   879→future interventions.
   880→   880→Overall, though, interventions that targeted specific levels yielded mixed effects. Results
   881→   881→suggested that, regardless of which level is targeted, state-level change is a key component of
   882→   882→bottom-up pathways of change, with intermediate, habit-level changes being less efficacious
   883→   883→for driving trait-level change. For example, the State and Habit+State Groups (both receiving
   884→   884→state strategies) had larger bottom-up effects for momentary to daily neuroticism change than
   885→   885→the active control group and Habit Group (neither receiving state strategies), and they similarly
   886→   886→had stronger bottom-up effects from daily to weekly change than the Habit Group. One possible
   887→   887→reason for this finding is that intermediate habit changes are still mostly driven by narrow state
   888→   888→changes. Habits are formed via repeated instances of novel states (Gardner et al., 2012), so
   889→   889→trying to form habits without an equal emphasis on generating novel states may elicit less
   890→   890→change. Thus, the State Group may have had larger effects of intermediate changes predicting
   891→   891→broader changes than the Habit Group because these repeated novel states are necessary for
   892→   892→habit formation and eventual broader trait change.
   893→   893→Other potential reasons for the mixed effects are that strategies’ targeted levels may not
   894→   894→align as expected with the timescale-specific measures or that strategies may not be as specific
   895→   895→as expected regarding targeted levels. For example, a state strategy, like Physical Activity, could
   896→   896→elicit changes in both states (e.g., people go for a run) and habits (e.g., establish a new workout
   897→   897→routine). Additionally, other TG differences (e.g., required cognitive resources, targeted aspects
   898→   898→of neuroticism) may obscure effects of targeting specific levels. For instance, strategies also
   899→   899→differ in whether they primarily target affective, behavioral, and cognitive aspects of
   900→   900→neuroticism (Wright et al., 2026), and initial research suggests that targeting affective aspects
   901→   901→
   902→   902→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 28
   903→   903→may be particularly effective for changing neuroticism (Haehner et al., 2026). Future work
   904→   904→testing effects of single strategies instead of combinations of strategies is needed to better
   905→   905→understand their implications and identify the most successful strategies (Collins et al., 2024).
   906→   906→Limitations
   907→   907→First, the CHILL Study used a highly educated sample of young adults who were mostly
   908→   908→women and primarily stemmed from three Western democratic countries. As such, it remains
   909→   909→an open question whether findings generalize to other cultures or demographic groups. Second,
   910→   910→self-report measures may be subject to biases like socially desirable responding or demand
   911→   911→effects (Krämer et al., 2025; Paulhus & Vazire, 2007). Although the CHILL Study included peer
   912→   912→and implicit measures, showing effects extend beyond self-reports for generalized trait
   913→   913→neuroticism, observed changes in momentary, daily, and weekly neuroticism could be biased by
   914→   914→socially desirable responding and people’s expectation to decrease in neuroticism. Third, to
   915→   915→approximate theoretical models that distinguish states, habits, and traits, we used momentary,
   916→   916→daily, and weekly measures. However, this is an imperfect conceptualization. Personality traits
   917→   917→are typically captured using measures without specific reference periods in instructions. Among
   918→   918→the intensive longitudinal measures available in the CHILL Study, though, the weekly measure
   919→   919→does best capture neuroticism trait levels.
   920→   920→Conclusion
   921→   921→Interventions that can change consequential personality traits like neuroticism stand to
   922→   922→widely benefit public health and well-being. However, successfully changing personality traits
   923→   923→requires a comprehensive understanding of the timescales, directions, and mechanisms of
   924→   924→intervention-driven change. Contributing to this understanding, we found that neuroticism
   925→   925→assessed across different timescales changed at nonconstant rates during an intervention, with
   926→   926→most change occurring by Intervention-Week 4. Changes in momentary measures predicted
   927→   927→changes in daily and weekly measures and vice versa, highlighting the relevance of both
   928→   928→
   929→   929→TIMESCALES OF NEUROTICISM CHANGE 29
   930→   930→bottom-up and top-down processes. Interventions targeting states tended to elicit larger total
   931→   931→neuroticism changes, with this possibly being even more true when targeting states and habits.
   932→   932→Future interventions should target multiple levels of neuroticism, especially momentary and
   933→   933→intermediate levels, to elicit maximal change.
