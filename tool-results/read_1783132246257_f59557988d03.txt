    50‚Üí    99‚Üí    99‚Üí    99‚Üícomparatively underdeveloped as a deliberate intervention strategy. The framework advanced
    51‚Üí   100‚Üí   100‚Üí   100‚Üíhere is intended to complement, rather than replace, existing models by foregrounding relational
    52‚Üí   101‚Üí   101‚Üí   101‚Üívoice as an explicit, structured target.
    53‚Üí   102‚Üí   102‚Üí   102‚ÜíThe Science of Voice and Memory
    54‚Üí   103‚Üí   103‚Üí   103‚ÜíCognitive decline does not affect all neural and psychological systems uniformly. While
    55‚Üí   104‚Üí   104‚Üí   104‚Üíepisodic and declarative recall often diminish early, several lines of evidence suggest that
    56‚Üí   105‚Üí   105‚Üí   105‚Üíaffective and auditory processes associated with familiar people may remain comparatively
    57‚Üí   106‚Üí   106‚Üí   106‚Üí
    58‚Üí   107‚Üí   107‚Üí   107‚Üíaccessible. This section reviews that evidence across three areas, then carefully distinguishes the
    59‚Üí   108‚Üí   108‚Üí   108‚Üíestablished findings from the conceptual proposition this paper advances.
    60‚Üí   109‚Üí   109‚Üí   109‚ÜíAuditory Cognition and Familiar Voice Processing
    61‚Üí   110‚Üí   110‚Üí   110‚ÜíThe human voice conveys a rich array of information beyond linguistic content, including
    62‚Üí   111‚Üí   111‚Üí   111‚Üícues to a speaker's identity and emotional state, which listeners perceive with notable ease (Belin
    63‚Üí   112‚Üí   112‚Üí   112‚Üíet al., 2000). Using functional neuroimaging, Belin and colleagues identified voice-selective
    64‚Üí   113‚Üí   113‚Üí   113‚Üíregions along the superior temporal sulcus, sometimes termed the temporal voice areas, that
    65‚Üí   114‚Üí   114‚Üí   114‚Üírespond preferentially to vocal sounds relative to other complex sounds. The voice of someone
    66‚Üí   115‚Üí   115‚Üí   115‚Üíknown appears to hold a privileged status within this system: personally familiar voices elicit
    67‚Üí   116‚Üí   116‚Üí   116‚Üímore finely resolved neural response patterns than voices learned briefly in a laboratory or never
    68‚Üí   117‚Üí   117‚Üí   117‚Üíencountered before, consistent with the view that extensive and varied exposure yields richer
    69‚Üí   118‚Üí   118‚Üí   118‚Üírepresentations of the people we know (Kanber et al., 2025).
    70‚Üí   119‚Üí   119‚Üí   119‚ÜíAt the same time, voice processing is not uniformly spared in cognitive decline.
    71‚Üí   120‚Üí   120‚Üí   120‚ÜíSystematic investigation of neurodegenerative populations indicates that components of voice
    72‚Üí   121‚Üí   121‚Üí   121‚Üíperception and voice recognition can be differentially affected in dementia, including in
    73‚Üí   122‚Üí   122‚Üí   122‚ÜíAlzheimer disease (Hailstone et al., 2011). This nuance is important for the present framework,
    74‚Üí   123‚Üí   123‚Üí   123‚Üías it cautions against assuming that intact recognition of a familiar speaker is the operative
    75‚Üí   124‚Üí   124‚Üí   124‚Üímechanism. The relevant implication of this section is therefore conceptual rather than
    76‚Üí   125‚Üí   125‚Üí   125‚Üídiagnostic: the voice is not a generic auditory stimulus but a carrier of identity and affect, and the
    77‚Üí   126‚Üí   126‚Üí   126‚Üíframework's emphasis falls on the affective and relational salience of familiar voice rather than
    78‚Üí   127‚Üí   127‚Üí   127‚Üíon preserved voice-identity recognition as such. On this view, familiar relational voice may
    79‚Üí   128‚Üí   128‚Üí   128‚Üíremain a meaningful channel for engagement even as specific voice-processing abilities change.
    80‚Üí   129‚Üí   129‚Üí   129‚ÜíEmotional Encoding and Amygdala-Hippocampal Interactions
    81‚Üí   130‚Üí   130‚Üí   130‚Üí
    82‚Üí   131‚Üí   131‚Üí   131‚ÜíEmotional memory depends in part on interactions between the amygdala and
    83‚Üí   132‚Üí   132‚Üí   132‚Üíhippocampus that contribute to the encoding and retrieval of affectively charged experience. A
    84‚Üí   133‚Üí   133‚Üí   133‚Üísubstantial body of research indicates that the amygdala modulates the consolidation of
    85‚Üí   134‚Üí   134‚Üí   134‚Üímemories for emotionally arousing experiences, strengthening the retention of affectively
    86‚Üí   135‚Üí   135‚Üí   135‚Üísignificant information (McGaugh, 2004). Mechanistic work in animal models has begun to
    87‚Üí   136‚Üí   136‚Üí   136‚Üíspecify how this may occur, suggesting that coordinated amygdalo-cortical activity, including
    88‚Üí   137‚Üí   137‚Üí   137‚Üíduring sleep-dependent consolidation, enhances memory for emotionally associated material and
    89‚Üí   138‚Üí   138‚Üí   138‚Üíunderscores the role of inter-regional dialogue in prioritizing emotional memories (Saito et al.,
    90‚Üí   139‚Üí   139‚Üí   139‚Üí2025). In Alzheimer disease, the hippocampus is typically affected early, impairing declarative
    91‚Üí   140‚Üí   140‚Üí   140‚Üímemory, whereas evidence suggests that amygdala-mediated affective processing may make
    92‚Üí   141‚Üí   141‚Üí   141‚Üídistinct and partially dissociable contributions to emotion and memory (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al.,
    93‚Üí   142‚Üí   142‚Üí   142‚Üí2016). This dissociation is theoretically important: it suggests that the systems supporting factual
    94‚Üí   143‚Üí   143‚Üí   143‚Üírecall and those supporting emotional responsiveness may decline along different trajectories,
    95‚Üí   144‚Üí   144‚Üí   144‚Üíleaving affective pathways relatively engaged even as declarative detail becomes less accessible.
    96‚Üí   145‚Üí   145‚Üí   145‚ÜíRelative Preservation of Emotional Experience in Cognitive Decline
    97‚Üí   146‚Üí   146‚Üí   146‚ÜíDirect evidence for the persistence of emotion beyond memory comes from studies of
    98‚Üí   147‚Üí   147‚Üí   147‚Üípatients with hippocampal damage and with Alzheimer disease. In a study of individuals with
    99‚Üí   148‚Üí   148‚Üí   148‚Üísevere amnesia, induced feelings of sadness and happiness persisted well after participants had
   100‚Üí   149‚Üí   149‚Üí   149‚Üílost factual memory for the films that produced them (Feinstein et al., 2010). This phenomenon
   101‚Üí   150‚Üí   150‚Üí   150‚Üíwas subsequently demonstrated in Alzheimer disease: patients continued to experience
   102‚Üí   151‚Üí   151‚Üí   151‚Üíprolonged emotional states even after their declarative memory for the eliciting events had faded,
   103‚Üí   152‚Üí   152‚Üí   152‚Üíand, strikingly, weaker memory was associated with longer-lasting emotion (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et
   104‚Üí   153‚Üí   153‚Üí   153‚Üíal., 2014).
   105‚Üí   154‚Üí   154‚Üí   154‚Üí
   106‚Üí   155‚Üí   155‚Üí   155‚ÜíPersonally meaningful auditory stimuli appear especially capable of evoking such
   107‚Üí   156‚Üí   156‚Üí   156‚Üíresponses, and music has served as an instructive model case. Familiar music can cue
   108‚Üí   157‚Üí   157‚Üí   157‚Üíautobiographical memories that are engaged through a distributed neural network including
   109‚Üí   158‚Üí   158‚Üí   158‚Üímedial prefrontal cortex (Janata, 2009), and in Alzheimer disease such music-evoked
   110‚Üí   159‚Üí   159‚Üí   159‚Üíautobiographical memories may be retained and tend to carry a positive emotional tone, a pattern
   111‚Üí   160‚Üí   160‚Üí   160‚Üídescribed as a positivity effect (Cuddy et al., 2017). Brief exposure to familiar music has
   112‚Üí   161‚Üí   161‚Üí   161‚Üílikewise been shown to induce lingering emotion in individuals with Alzheimer disease
   113‚Üí   162‚Üí   162‚Üí   162‚Üí(Reschke-Hern√°ndez et al., 2020). Although these findings concern music rather than voice, they
   114‚Üí   163‚Üí   163‚Üí   163‚Üíillustrate a broader principle relevant to the present framework: personally meaningful auditory
   115‚Üí   164‚Üí   164‚Üí   164‚Üístimuli can engage emotion and autobiographical content even when declarative recall is
   116‚Üí   165‚Üí   165‚Üí   165‚Üícompromised. Relational voice is proposed here as a parallel and potentially complementary
   117‚Üí   166‚Üí   166‚Üí   166‚Üíchannel, distinguished by its direct connection to attachment relationships, a proposition that
   118‚Üí   167‚Üí   167‚Üí   167‚Üírequires direct empirical testing rather than extrapolation from the music literature alone.
   119‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚ÜíFrom Evidence to Proposition: Voice as a Cognitive Anchor
   120‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚ÜíIt is important to delineate precisely what the foregoing literature establishes and what
   121‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üíthis paper proposes. Existing research demonstrates, among other findings, that the brain
   122‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚Üícontains voice-selective processing regions (Belin et al., 2000); that affective and declarative
   123‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üísystems may decline along partially dissociable paths (Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2016); and that
   124‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üíemotional experience can persist after the memory of its cause has faded, including in Alzheimer
   125‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚Üídisease (Feinstein et al., 2010; Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2014). What this paper proposes, as its
   126‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üíprimary conceptual contribution, is an integrative interpretation: that familiar relational voice
   127‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üímay function as a cognitive anchor, an emotionally salient, identity-linked cue capable of
   128‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üísupporting emotional continuity, relational recognition, and a sense of orientation even as
   129‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚Üí
   130‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚Üíepisodic memory declines. This proposition is a synthesis of existing evidence into a new
   131‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚Üíframework; it is hypothesized rather than demonstrated, and it does not assert that voice
   132‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚Üíengagement alters neuropathology or disease progression. The conceptual leap is identified
   133‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚Üíexplicitly so that the framework's empirical status is not overstated.
   134‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚ÜíIdentity Continuity as a Clinical Priority
   135‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚ÜíIf emotionally salient relational cues remain accessible in early decline, then identity
   136‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üícontinuity, rather than recall accuracy alone, becomes a meaningful target for intervention. This
   137‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üísection develops the theoretical basis for treating identity as a clinical priority, drawing on
   138‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚Üínarrative identity, personhood and person-centered care, attachment theory, and the stabilizing
   139‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üírole of ritual.
   140‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚ÜíNarrative Identity and Self-Continuity
   141‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚ÜíContemporary psychology conceptualizes identity in substantial part as a story. Narrative
   142‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üíidentity refers to a person's internalized and evolving life story, which integrates the
   143‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üíreconstructed past and the imagined future to provide a sense of unity and purpose (McAdams &
   144‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚ÜíMcLean, 2013). On this view, selfhood is sustained not by the perfect retention of facts but by
   145‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚Üíthe ongoing integration of experience into a coherent narrative. Early cognitive decline can
   146‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üífragment this autobiographical integration, destabilizing self-concept and relational roles. The
   147‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚Üíframework proposed here suggests that reinforcing the affective and relational threads of a
   148‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚Üíperson's narrative, through familiar voice that affirms roles, relationships, and personal history,
   149‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üímay help sustain a sense of continuity even when factual recall is impaired. Continuity, in this
   150‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üísense, is conceptualized as sustained recognition of who a person is, rather than as flawless recall
   151‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚Üíof what a person did.
   152‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚ÜíPersonhood, Dignity, and Person-Centered Care
   153‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üí
   154‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚ÜíKitwood's (1997) account of personhood reoriented dementia care away from a purely
   155‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üíbiomedical model toward the recognition of the person. Personhood, in Kitwood's formulation, is
   156‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üía status conferred on one human being by others within relationship and social being, and it is
   157‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚Üísustained or undermined through the quality of interpersonal interaction (Kitwood, 1997;
   158‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚ÜíKitwood & Bredin, 1992). Among the psychological needs Kitwood identified, comfort,
   159‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚Üíattachment, identity, occupation, and inclusion are bound together by the centrality of
   160‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üírelationship. Subsequent work has elaborated person-centered care as an approach that values the
   161‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üíperson, treats individuals as unique, adopts their perspective, and provides a supportive social
   162‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üíenvironment (Brooker, 2007), and has shown that self and identity can be actively maintained
   163‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üíthrough social interaction even amid significant impairment (Sabat & Harr√©, 1992). Within this
   164‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üítradition, sustaining identity is not peripheral to care; it is the core ethical aim. The present
   165‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üíframework aligns with and operationalizes this aim by treating relational voice as a structured
   166‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚Üímeans of affirming personhood.
   167‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚ÜíRelational Identity and Attachment
   168‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚ÜíIdentity in cognitive decline is profoundly relational, and attachment theory offers a lens
   169‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üífor understanding why familiar figures carry such weight. Bowlby's (1969) account of
   170‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üíattachment as an enduring affectional bond has been extended to later life and dementia, where
   171‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üíproximity-seeking and attachment behaviors can intensify under conditions of uncertainty and
   172‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚Üíperceived insecurity (Miesen, 1993; Nelis et al., 2014). From this perspective, the familiar voice
   173‚Üí   222‚Üí   222‚Üí   222‚Üíof an attachment figure is not merely a recognizable sound but a signal of safety and connection,
   174‚Üí   223‚Üí   223‚Üí   223‚Üícapable of providing a secure base during disorientation. The framework proposed here draws on
   175‚Üí   224‚Üí   224‚Üí   224‚Üíthis insight to suggest that relational voice may stabilize emotional states in part through its
   176‚Üí   225‚Üí   225‚Üí   225‚Üíattachment significance, a possibility that warrants empirical examination.
   177‚Üí   226‚Üí   226‚Üí   226‚Üí
   178‚Üí   227‚Üí   227‚Üí   227‚ÜíRitual, Predictability, and Stabilization
   179‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚ÜíRitualized engagement introduces predictability within the context of cognitive
   180‚Üí   229‚Üí   229‚Üí   229‚Üíuncertainty. As the reliability of memory fluctuates, repeated and structured sensory experiences
   181‚Üí   230‚Üí   230‚Üí   230‚Üímay provide psychological grounding, reframing memory support from performance assessment
   182‚Üí   231‚Üí   231‚Üí   231‚Üítoward identity reinforcement. Ritual, in this sense, functions not as routine alone but as a
   183‚Üí   232‚Üí   232‚Üí   232‚Üístabilizing container through which continuity is reaffirmed. Embedding relational voice within
   184‚Üí   233‚Üí   233‚Üí   233‚Üípredictable, repeatable structures is therefore proposed to enhance its stabilizing potential, an
   185‚Üí   234‚Üí   234‚Üí   234‚Üíidea consistent with broader observations that structure and familiarity can reduce distress in
   186‚Üí   235‚Üí   235‚Üí   235‚Üídementia care.
   187‚Üí   236‚Üí   236‚Üí   236‚ÜíCommunication-Theoretic Foundations
   188‚Üí   237‚Üí   237‚Üí   237‚ÜíBecause the proposed mechanism is relational voice, the framework requires a
   189‚Üí   238‚Üí   238‚Üí   238‚Üícommunication-theoretic foundation that explains why familiar voice operates differently from
   190‚Üí   239‚Üí   239‚Üí   239‚Üígeneric auditory input. This section draws on interpersonal, relational, family systems, and
   191‚Üí   240‚Üí   240‚Üí   240‚Üíorganizational communication to articulate that distinction.
   192‚Üí   241‚Üí   241‚Üí   241‚ÜíWhy Familiar Voice Differs From Generic Auditory Stimuli
   193‚Üí   242‚Üí   242‚Üí   242‚ÜíFrom a communication standpoint, the familiar voice is a relational message, not merely
   194‚Üí   243‚Üí   243‚Üí   243‚Üían acoustic event. It carries relational history, identity attribution, and affective meaning that a
   195‚Üí   244‚Üí   244‚Üí   244‚Üíneutral or unfamiliar voice does not. Where generic auditory stimulation may engage attention or
   196‚Üí   245‚Üí   245‚Üí   245‚Üíarousal, familiar relational voice additionally signals who is speaking and what that person
   197‚Üí   246‚Üí   246‚Üí   246‚Üímeans to the listener, activating the identity and emotional associations built across a shared
   198‚Üí   247‚Üí   247‚Üí   247‚Üírelationship. This distinction is central to the framework: the proposed anchoring effect is
   199‚Üí   248‚Üí   248‚Üí   248‚Üíhypothesized to derive from relational familiarity and affective salience rather than from auditory
   200‚Üí   249‚Üí   249‚Üí   249‚Üíinput as such.
   201‚Üí   250‚Üí   250‚Üí   250‚Üí
   202‚Üí   251‚Üí   251‚Üí   251‚ÜíInterpersonal and Relational Communication
   203‚Üí   252‚Üí   252‚Üí   252‚ÜíInterpersonal and relational communication scholarship emphasizes that meaning is co-
   204‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üíconstructed and that relationships are sustained through ongoing, mutually recognized exchange.
   205‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚ÜíIn dementia care, communication strategies that affirm the person, support face and dignity, and
   206‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üíinvite reciprocity have been associated with more person-centered interactions (Ryan et al.,
   207‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üí2005; Savundranayagam & Moore-Nielsen, 2015). Relational voice can be understood as a
   208‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üívehicle for these affirming functions, conveying recognition, warmth, and continuity of
   209‚Üí   258‚Üí   258‚Üí   258‚Üírelationship through tone and cadence as much as through propositional content. Framing voice
   210‚Üí   259‚Üí   259‚Üí   259‚Üíengagement in relational terms positions it within a well-established body of communication
   211‚Üí   260‚Üí   260‚Üí   260‚Üípractice rather than treating it as a novel sensory technique.
   212‚Üí   261‚Üí   261‚Üí   261‚ÜíFamily Systems and Caregiver Communication
   213‚Üí   262‚Üí   262‚Üí   262‚ÜíFamilies operate as systems in which a change in one member reverberates throughout
   214‚Üí   263‚Üí   263‚Üí   263‚Üíthe whole. As cognitive decline alters a person's communicative capacities, established patterns
   215‚Üí   264‚Üí   264‚Üí   264‚Üíof interaction are disrupted, and roles, expectations, and emotional dynamics shift across the
   216‚Üí   265‚Üí   265‚Üí   265‚Üífamily. The experience of ambiguous loss can generate uncertainty about how to remain in
   217‚Üí   266‚Üí   266‚Üí   266‚Üírelationship with a loved one who is present yet changed (Boss, 1999, 2011). Structured
   218‚Üí   267‚Üí   267‚Üí   267‚Üírelational voice engagement may offer families an intentional means of maintaining connection,
   219‚Üí   268‚Üí   268‚Üí   268‚Üíproviding a shared practice through which identity and relationship can be affirmed despite
   220‚Üí   269‚Üí   269‚Üí   269‚Üícommunicative variability. In this sense, the framework addresses not only the person living with
   221‚Üí   270‚Üí   270‚Üí   270‚Üídecline but the relational system surrounding them.
   222‚Üí   271‚Üí   271‚Üí   271‚ÜíOrganizational Communication and Cultures of Care
   223‚Üí   272‚Üí   272‚Üí   272‚ÜíAt the level of care organizations, communication shapes culture, and culture shapes the
   224‚Üí   273‚Üí   273‚Üí   273‚Üíexperience of those receiving care. Kitwood (1997) argued that the social and organizational
   225‚Üí   274‚Üí   274‚Üí   274‚Üí
   226‚Üí   275‚Üí   275‚Üí   275‚Üíenvironment can either sustain or diminish personhood, and person-centered communication
   227‚Üí   276‚Üí   276‚Üí   276‚Üípractices have been linked to more dignity-affirming care interactions (Savundranayagam &
   228‚Üí   277‚Üí   277‚Üí   277‚ÜíMoore-Nielsen, 2015). Considered through an organizational lens, embedding relational voice
   229‚Üí   278‚Üí   278‚Üí   278‚Üíinto the routines, training, and shared practices of care teams may help align everyday
   230‚Üí   279‚Üí   279‚Üí   279‚Üícommunication with identity-preserving values. The framework thus has implications not only
   231‚Üí   280‚Üí   280‚Üí   280‚Üífor individual interactions but for how care environments structure communication at a systemic
   232‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚Üílevel.
   233‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚ÜíThe VOCES Framework
   234‚Üí   283‚Üí   283‚Üí   283‚ÜíConceptual Overview
   235‚Üí   284‚Üí   284‚Üí   284‚ÜíThe VOCES Framework (Voz Orientada a la Continuidad, Emoci√≥n y Sost√©n)
   236‚Üí   285‚Üí   285‚Üí   285‚Üíconceptualizes relational voice as a culturally embedded stabilizing mechanism in early
   237‚Üí   286‚Üí   286‚Üí   286‚Üícognitive decline. Building on evidence suggesting that affective and auditory responses to
   238‚Üí   287‚Üí   287‚Üí   287‚Üífamiliar people may be comparatively preserved, the framework positions intentional voice
   239‚Üí   288‚Üí   288‚Üí   288‚Üíengagement as a structured method for reinforcing identity continuity. Rather than treating voice
   240‚Üí   289‚Üí   289‚Üí   289‚Üías incidental sensory input, the model integrates emotionally salient auditory cues with ritualized
   241‚Üí   290‚Üí   290‚Üí   290‚Üísupport structures designed to sustain relational recognition across evolving cognitive states. The
   242‚Üí   291‚Üí   291‚Üí   291‚Üínaming of the framework reflects relational plurality and cultural responsiveness: aging and
   243‚Üí   292‚Üí   292‚Üí   292‚Üímemory are shaped by linguistic, familial, and intergenerational narratives, and intervention
   244‚Üí   293‚Üí   293‚Üí   293‚Üímodels must therefore treat cultural context as integral to identity preservation rather than as an
   245‚Üí   294‚Üí   294‚Üí   294‚Üíadjunct consideration.
   246‚Üí   295‚Üí   295‚Üí   295‚ÜíPillar I: Voz (Relational Voice)
   247‚Üí   296‚Üí   296‚Üí   296‚ÜíVoz positions relational voice as the primary emotional and cognitive anchor within the
   248‚Üí   297‚Üí   297‚Üí   297‚Üíframework. Familiar voices, particularly those associated with attachment figures, carry affective
   249‚Üí   298‚Üí   298‚Üí   298‚Üí
   250‚Üí   299‚Üí   299‚Üí   299‚Üísalience that may remain accessible despite declining episodic recall (Belin et al., 2000; Kanber
   251‚Üí   300‚Üí   300‚Üí   300‚Üíet al., 2025; Miesen, 1993). Within VOCES, voice is operationalized as an intentional
   252‚Üí   301‚Üí   301‚Üí   301‚Üíintervention mechanism rather than passive background stimulus. Structured affirmations of
   253‚Üí   302‚Üí   302‚Üí   302‚Üírelational roles, identity markers, and personal history are proposed to reinforce continuity of self
   254‚Üí   303‚Üí   303‚Üí   303‚Üíacross fluctuating cognitive states. Voz thus serves as the anchoring mechanism on which the
   255‚Üí   304‚Üí   304‚Üí   304‚Üíother pillars depend.
   256‚Üí   305‚Üí   305‚Üí   305‚ÜíPillar II: Continuidad (Identity Continuity)
   257‚Üí   306‚Üí   306‚Üí   306‚ÜíContinuidad emphasizes the preservation of narrative coherence and identity markers.
   258‚Üí   307‚Üí   307‚Üí   307‚ÜíBecause early cognitive decline often fragments autobiographical integration, destabilizing
   259‚Üí   308‚Üí   308‚Üí   308‚Üírelational roles and self-concept (McAdams & McLean, 2013), the framework prioritizes
   260‚Üí   309‚Üí   309‚Üí   309‚Üíreinforcement of identity through repeated, emotionally grounded cues that affirm personal
   261‚Üí   310‚Üí   310‚Üí   310‚Üíhistory and relational significance. Continuity is conceptualized not as perfect recall but as
   262‚Üí   311‚Üí   311‚Üí   311‚Üísustained recognition of who the individual is within evolving cognitive capacity. Continuidad
   263‚Üí   312‚Üí   312‚Üí   312‚Üítherefore defines the identity objective toward which voice engagement is directed.
   264‚Üí   313‚Üí   313‚Üí   313‚ÜíPillar III: Emoci√≥n (Emotionally Encoded Memory)
   265‚Üí   314‚Üí   314‚Üí   314‚ÜíEmoci√≥n centers the activation of emotionally encoded pathways as a stabilizing
   266‚Üí   315‚Üí   315‚Üí   315‚Üímechanism. Evidence that emotional experience can persist beyond declarative memory,
   267‚Üí   316‚Üí   316‚Üí   316‚Üíincluding in Alzheimer disease (Feinstein et al., 2010; Guzm√°n-V√©lez et al., 2014), suggests that
   268‚Üí   317‚Üí   317‚Üí   317‚Üíaffective salience embedded within familiar relational cues may facilitate recognition and
   269‚Üí   318‚Üí   318‚Üí   318‚Üíengagement even when factual retrieval diminishes. Within VOCES, emotionally meaningful
   270‚Üí   319‚Üí   319‚Üí   319‚Üívoice is intentionally structured to engage preserved affective responsiveness, reinforcing
   271‚Üí   320‚Üí   320‚Üí   320‚Üíconnection through tone, cadence, and relational familiarity rather than informational content
   272‚Üí   321‚Üí   321‚Üí   321‚Üí
   273‚Üí   322‚Üí   322‚Üí   322‚Üíalone. Emoci√≥n reframes memory engagement from cognitive testing toward affective
   274‚Üí   323‚Üí   323‚Üí   323‚Üícontinuity.
   275‚Üí   324‚Üí   324‚Üí   324‚ÜíPillar IV: Sost√©n (Structured Relational Support)
   276‚Üí   325‚Üí   325‚Üí   325‚ÜíSost√©n refers to the structured relational and tangible supports that stabilize engagement
   277‚Üí   326‚Üí   326‚Üí   326‚Üíover time. As cognitive reliability fluctuates, predictable and repeatable interaction structures
   278‚Üí   327‚Üí   327‚Üí   327‚Üímay reduce anxiety and enhance emotional grounding. Within the framework, voice-based
   279‚Üí   328‚Üí   328‚Üí   328‚Üíengagement is embedded within ritualized formats and, when applicable, supported by physical
   280‚Üí   329‚Üí   329‚Üí   329‚Üíartifacts that reinforce continuity. Sost√©n emphasizes that the value of the approach is not derived
   281‚Üí   330‚Üí   330‚Üí   330‚Üísolely from auditory input but from the consistent relational container within which voice is
   282‚Üí   331‚Üí   331‚Üí   331‚Üíexperienced; stabilization is proposed to emerge through repetition, predictability, and sustained
   283‚Üí   332‚Üí   332‚Üí   332‚Üíaffirmation of identity.
   284‚Üí   333‚Üí   333‚Üí   333‚ÜíIntegration of the Pillars
   285‚Üí   334‚Üí   334‚Üí   334‚ÜíTogether, the four pillars function as an integrated model rather than as discrete
   286‚Üí   335‚Üí   335‚Üí   335‚Üícomponents. Voz provides the anchoring mechanism, Continuidad defines the identity objective,
   287‚Üí   336‚Üí   336‚Üí   336‚ÜíEmoci√≥n engages preserved affective pathways, and Sost√©n establishes the stabilizing structure
   288‚Üí   337‚Üí   337‚Üí   337‚Üíthrough which engagement is sustained. The framework proposes that identity preservation in
   289‚Üí   338‚Üí   338‚Üí   338‚Üíearly cognitive decline requires simultaneous attention to relational voice, emotional encoding,
   290‚Üí   339‚Üí   339‚Üí   339‚Üínarrative continuity, and structured support, and that these elements are mutually reinforcing
   291‚Üí   340‚Üí   340‚Üí   340‚Üírather than independently sufficient.
   292‚Üí   341‚Üí   341‚Üí   341‚ÜíApplied Illustration: MemoriGems
   293‚Üí   342‚Üí   342‚Üí   342‚ÜíMemoriGems serves as one applied illustration of the VOCES Framework. The platform
   294‚Üí   343‚Üí   343‚Üí   343‚Üífacilitates structured relational voice recordings paired with optional sensory supports,
   295‚Üí   344‚Üí   344‚Üí   344‚Üíoperationalizing identity-based reinforcement within repeatable engagement structures. In doing
   296‚Üí   345‚Üí   345‚Üí   345‚Üí
   297‚Üí   346‚Üí   346‚Üí   346‚Üíso, it translates the theoretical components of the framework, relational voice, emotional
   298‚Üí   347‚Üí   347‚Üí   347‚Üíencoding, narrative continuity, and structured support, into a practical format accessible to
   299‚Üí   348‚Üí   348‚Üí   348‚Üífamilies navigating early cognitive change. MemoriGems does not purport to alter disease
   300‚Üí   349‚Üí   349‚Üí   349‚Üíprogression; rather, it functions as a relational support tool aligned with the framework's
   301‚Üí   350‚Üí   350‚Üí   350‚Üíemphasis on dignity, continuity, and emotionally grounded engagement. It is presented here as a
   302‚Üí   351‚Üí   351‚Üí   351‚Üísingle instantiation of the model and not as the focus of the paper, and its effectiveness, like that
   303‚Üí   352‚Üí   352‚Üí   352‚Üíof the framework more broadly, remains a matter for empirical evaluation.
   304‚Üí   353‚Üí   353‚Üí   353‚ÜíPractical Applications Across Care Contexts
   305‚Üí   354‚Üí   354‚Üí   354‚ÜíBecause relational voice requires minimal technological complexity and draws on
   306‚Üí   355‚Üí   355‚Üí   355‚Üíexisting relationships, the framework is adaptable across the settings in which early cognitive
   307‚Üí   356‚Üí   356‚Üí   356‚Üísupport occurs. The following applications are offered as conceptual possibilities to be examined
   308‚Üí   357‚Üí   357‚Üí   357‚Üíempirically rather than as established practices.
   309‚Üí   358‚Üí   358‚Üí   358‚ÜíFamily Caregiving and Home Care
   310‚Üí   359‚Üí   359‚Üí   359‚ÜíMost early cognitive support takes place at home, where informal caregivers provide the
   311‚Üí   360‚Üí   360‚Üí   360‚Üímajority of care (WHO, 2025). In these contexts, structured relational voice engagement may
   312‚Üí   361‚Üí   361‚Üí   361‚Üígive caregivers an intentional practice for maintaining connection as communication patterns
   313‚Üí   362‚Üí   362‚Üí   362‚Üíshift, allowing participation in affirmations of identity and shared history rather than reliance on
   314‚Üí   363‚Üí   363‚Üí   363‚Üíspontaneous recall. This may also offer families a shared ritual through which relationship is
   315‚Üí   364‚Üí   364‚Üí   364‚Üísustained amid ambiguous loss (Boss, 1999).
   316‚Üí   365‚Üí   365‚Üí   365‚ÜíMemory Care Communities and Adult Day Health
   317‚Üí   366‚Üí   366‚Üí   366‚ÜíIn memory care communities and adult day health programs, where staff are often
   318‚Üí   367‚Üí   367‚Üí   367‚Üínecessarily task-oriented and may have limited knowledge of residents' personal histories,
   319‚Üí   368‚Üí   368‚Üí   368‚Üírelational voice may help bring identity-affirming content into daily routines. Embedding
   320‚Üí   369‚Üí   369‚Üí   369‚Üí
   321‚Üí   370‚Üí   370‚Üí   370‚Üífamiliar voice within predictable engagement structures could complement person-centered
   322‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚Üícommunication practices and support continuity across staff and shifts (Ryan et al., 2005).
   323‚Üí   372‚Üí   372‚Üí   372‚ÜíHospitals and Acute Care
   324‚Üí   373‚Üí   373‚Üí   373‚ÜíHospitalization can be especially disorienting for people living with cognitive
   325‚Üí   374‚Üí   374‚Üí   374‚Üíimpairment, who may experience unfamiliar environments as threatening and exhibit heightened
   326‚Üí   375‚Üí   375‚Üí   375‚Üíattachment-seeking behavior (Miesen, 1993). Familiar relational voice may offer a portable
   327‚Üí   376‚Üí   376‚Üí   376‚Üísource of reassurance and orientation during acute care, potentially supporting emotional
   328‚Üí   377‚Üí   377‚Üí   377‚Üíregulation in settings where familiar people cannot always be present.
   329‚Üí   378‚Üí   378‚Üí   378‚ÜíTelehealth and Remote Support
   330‚Üí   379‚Üí   379‚Üí   379‚ÜíBecause voice can be recorded, shared, and delivered remotely, the framework is well
   331‚Üí   380‚Üí   380‚Üí   380‚Üísuited to telehealth and distributed caregiving arrangements. Remote delivery may extend
   332‚Üí   381‚Üí   381‚Üí   381‚Üíidentity-affirming engagement to families separated by geography and to care models that rely
   333‚Üí   382‚Üí   382‚Üí   382‚Üíon virtual contact, broadening access without requiring complex infrastructure.
   334‚Üí   383‚Üí   383‚Üí   383‚ÜíAdvance Care Planning
   335‚Üí   384‚Üí   384‚Üí   384‚ÜíRelational voice may also have a role in advance care planning, where capturing a
   336‚Üí   385‚Üí   385‚Üí   385‚Üíperson's voice, values, and relational priorities earlier in the trajectory could preserve identity-
   337‚Üí   386‚Üí   386‚Üí   386‚Üírelevant material for later stages. Such applications would need to be approached with attention
   338‚Üí   387‚Üí   387‚Üí   387‚Üíto consent, autonomy, and the evolving preferences of the person.
   339‚Üí   388‚Üí   388‚Üí   388‚ÜíCulturally Responsive Dementia Care
   340‚Üí   389‚Üí   389‚Üí   389‚ÜíThe framework's emphasis on linguistic and cultural responsiveness is intended to make
   341‚Üí   390‚Üí   390‚Üí   390‚Üíit adaptable across diverse communities, including multilingual families and intergenerational
   342‚Üí   391‚Üí   391‚Üí   391‚Üíhouseholds. Because the voice carries cultural and familial meaning, voice-centered approaches
   343‚Üí   392‚Üí   392‚Üí   392‚Üí
   344‚Üí   393‚Üí   393‚Üí   393‚Üímay be tailored to the languages, narratives, and relational structures that are most meaningful to
   345‚Üí   394‚Üí   394‚Üí   394‚Üía given person, supporting equitable and dignity-centered care.
   346‚Üí   395‚Üí   395‚Üí   395‚ÜíBroader Clinical and Social Implications
   347‚Üí   396‚Üí   396‚Üí   396‚ÜíCaregiver Impact
   348‚Üí   397‚Üí   397‚Üí   397‚ÜíStructured voice engagement may provide caregivers with intentional mechanisms for

   354‚Üí   403‚Üí   403‚Üí   403‚Üídirect study.

   356‚Üí   405‚Üí   405‚Üí   405‚ÜíRitualized auditory anchoring may contribute to emotional stabilization by engaging
