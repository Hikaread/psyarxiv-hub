    41→SOURCE POLICY
    42→
    43→PsyArXiv, hosted through OSF, is the primary and required discovery source.
    44→
    45→Discover papers using stable APIs wherever possible, preferably the official OSF API and PsyArXiv provider metadata. Do not rely solely on scraping a visually rendered discovery page.
    46→
    47→The canonical paper link must point directly to the individual PsyArXiv preprint, for example:
    48→
    49→https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/q73h6_v1
    50→
    51→Do not use:
    52→
    53→- The PsyArXiv or OSF home page.
    54→- A generic search-results page.
    55→- A fabricated OSF identifier.
    56→- A repository-relative resolver page when the direct link is known.
    57→- A “Find Paper Link” placeholder.
    58→- A title-based search URL.
    59→- A link copied from another paper.
    60→- A generic `https://osf.io/{id}` link when the canonical PsyArXiv preprint URL is available.
    61→- A direct PDF download URL as the paper’s primary page link.
    62→
    63→The canonical page may contain a download button instead of rendering the PDF inline. That is acceptable. The link must still lead to the exact paper.

    77→- DOI and journal citation if the paper has subsequently been peer reviewed.

   113→CURATION STANDARD
   114→
   115→Evaluate the actual paper, not merely its title.
   116→
   117→Read at least:
   118→
   119→- Abstract.
   120→- Research question.
   121→- Population and sample.
   122→- Study design.
   123→- Measures or intervention.
   124→- Main results.
   125→- Limitations.
   126→- Discussion and clinical implications.
   127→
   128→Use the full paper whenever possible. If only partial text is available, be conservative and do not invent missing methods or findings.
   129→
   130→Select only papers with substantial clinical value. A paper is valuable when it does at least one of the following:
   131→
   132→- Changes or sharpens clinical case formulation.
   133→- Identifies a mechanism that matters for assessment or psychotherapy.
   134→- Distinguishes superficially similar presentations.
   135→- Challenges a common but weak clinical assumption.
   136→- Clarifies treatment resistance, dropout, relapse, deterioration, or nonresponse.
   137→- Identifies moderators, mediators, contraindications, or population differences that matter in treatment.
   138→- Offers credible evidence about psychotherapy effectiveness or implementation.
   139→- Improves understanding of attachment, intimacy, conflict, sexuality, trauma, dissociation, suicidality, self-harm, personality organization, mood, anxiety, psychosis, addiction, eating pathology, or somatic distress.
   140→- Improves differential diagnosis or reveals shortcomings in current assessment.
   141→- Provides a clinically meaningful perspective on culture, minority stress, gender, migration, disability, or neurodivergence.
   142→- Introduces a practical therapeutic framework supported by meaningful evidence.
   143→- Synthesizes a clinically important literature rigorously enough to alter practice or conceptualization.
   144→
   145→Do not select a paper merely because it mentions therapy, mental health, depression, anxiety, trauma, or another relevant keyword.
   146→
   147→PREFERRED RESEARCH
   148→
   149→Give higher priority to:
   150→
   151→- Psychotherapy process and outcome studies.
   152→- Clinically meaningful longitudinal studies.
   153→- Ecological momentary assessment with clear clinical interpretation.
   154→- Qualitative studies that reveal important patient or therapist experiences.
   155→- Strong systematic reviews and meta-analyses.
   156→- Studies of treatment mechanisms, therapeutic alliance, rupture and repair, dropout, nonresponse, relapse, or adverse effects.
   157→- Research on complex or frequently misunderstood clinical presentations.
   158→- Research with real clinical samples.
   159→- Studies that compare plausible treatment or assessment alternatives.
   160→- Findings with actionable implications for case formulation or therapeutic stance.
   161→- Replications or null findings that meaningfully correct an influential assumption.
   162→- Work that improves culturally responsive psychotherapy without reducing culture to stereotypes.
   163→
   164→Studies do not need to prescribe a specific intervention. A paper may qualify because it produces a profound conceptual insight that changes how a therapist understands clients.
   165→
   166→EXCLUSION RULES
   167→
   168→Reject papers primarily focused on:
   169→
   170→- Pure theory without meaningful clinical evidence.
   171→- Philosophical or conceptual argument with no substantial clinical application.
   172→- Basic neuroscience, neuroanatomy, neurochemistry, genetics, biomarkers, EEG, fMRI, brain connectivity, or physiological mechanisms unless the paper also provides unusually direct and credible psychotherapy implications.
   173→- Animal models.
   174→- Laboratory perception, attention, memory, or decision-making studies without direct clinical relevance.
   175→- General social psychology.
   176→- Organizational, consumer, political, sports, forensic, or educational psychology without a strong psychotherapy connection.
   177→- Generic student stress, happiness, wellbeing, loneliness, or social-media correlations.
   178→- General population correlations with obvious conclusions and no meaningful effect on clinical understanding.
   179→- Psychometric scale construction or validation unless it materially changes clinical assessment.
   180→- Pure methodology, statistics, open-science commentary, replication debates, or measurement debates.
   181→- Epidemiology that provides prevalence estimates without deeper clinical implications.
   182→- Diagnostic classification debates with no consequence for assessment or treatment.
   183→- Protocols without results.
   184→- Registered reports before results are available.
   185→- Editorials, commentaries, posters, slide decks, corrections, supplements, or withdrawn records.
   186→- Papers whose clinical implications are generic statements added by the curator rather than supported by the results.
   187→- Tiny, severely underpowered studies making broad causal claims.
   188→- Studies whose conclusions substantially exceed their methodology.
   189→- Papers that duplicate an existing PsyHub entry without adding a meaningful revised result.
   190→- Papers that merely confirm standard textbook knowledge.

   192→DRUG-RELATED POLICY
   193→
   194→Exclude therapy papers in which the therapeutic intervention depends on administering or using psychoactive drugs, including but not limited to:
   195→
   196→- LSD.
   197→- Psilocybin or psychedelic mushrooms.
   198→- MDMA.
   199→- Ketamine or esketamine.
   200→- Ayahuasca or DMT.
   201→- Mescaline.
   202→- Ibogaine.
   203→- Cannabis-assisted psychotherapy.
   204→- Other psychedelic-assisted, entactogen-assisted, dissociative-assisted, or hallucinogen-assisted therapy.
   205→
   206→Also exclude papers mainly advocating, optimizing, comparing, or explaining drug-assisted psychotherapy.
   207→
   208→This exclusion does not apply to clinically relevant research about:
   209→
   210→- Substance-use disorders.
   211→- Addiction mechanisms.
   212→- Alcohol misuse.
   213→- Opioid, stimulant, cannabis, sedative, or behavioral addictions.
   214→- Relapse, craving, withdrawal, harm reduction, recovery, or psychotherapy for addiction.
   215→- The psychological effects of substance use when directly relevant to assessment or psychotherapy.
   216→- Treating addiction with established non-drug psychotherapeutic approaches.
   217→
   218→A paper about addiction may be included. A paper whose treatment is psychedelic-assisted or otherwise drug-assisted psychotherapy must be excluded.

   287→SCORING PROCESS
   288→
   289→Score each candidate internally before selecting it:
   290→
   291→- Direct relevance to psychotherapy practice: 0–5.
   292→- Depth of clinical insight: 0–5.
   293→- Methodological credibility: 0–5.
   294→- Novelty relative to existing PsyHub entries: 0–4.
   295→- Usefulness for formulation or treatment: 0–5.
   296→- Population relevance to the intended audience: 0–3.
   297→- Confidence in metadata and source verification: 0–3.
   298→- Risk of overinterpretation: 0 to minus 5.
   299→- Weak or indirect clinical relevance: 0 to minus 5.
   300→
   301→A high aggregate score does not override a mandatory exclusion rule.
   302→
   303→Select the strongest five to ten papers from a normal batch. Select fewer than five if the batch does not contain five genuinely strong papers. Never lower the standard to fill a quota. If none qualify, publish none and record that the batch was reviewed.

   305→WRITING REQUIREMENTS
   306→
   307→Write for an experienced clinician, not for a general wellness audience and not for an academic peer-review report.
   308→
   309→Every statement must be traceable to the paper. Clearly distinguish:
   310→
   311→- What the study directly found.
   312→- What the authors proposed.
   313→- What is a cautious clinical interpretation.
   314→- What remains uncertain.
   315→
   316→Do not:
   317→
   318→- Invent results, methods, diagnoses, sample characteristics, effect sizes, or implications.
   319→- Convert correlation into causation.
   320→- Present a preprint as established consensus.
   321→- Claim that an intervention “works” when the design cannot establish efficacy.
   322→- Give individual medical advice.
   323→- Produce promotional or sensational language.
   324→- Use generic claims such as “therapists should be aware” without explaining exactly why.
   325→- Repeat the abstract with different wording.
   326→- Overstate small samples, cross-sectional designs, uncontrolled trials, or exploratory findings.
   327→- Add recommendations unsupported by the study.
   328→- describe a paper as peer reviewed unless its journal publication is verified.
   329→
   330→Use clear, compact prose. Preserve necessary nuance and limitations.
   331→
   332→For each accepted paper, write:
   333→
   334→Summary:
   335→Describe the research question, population, sample where available, methodology, and central findings. Usually two to four substantive sentences.
   336→
   337→Clinical Insight:
   338→Explain specifically how the findings deepen, reframe, qualify, or challenge clinical understanding. Connect the result to formulation, assessment, therapeutic stance, treatment process, or a clinically meaningful decision. Usually two to four substantive sentences.
   339→
   340→Relevant For:
   341→Name the clinicians, client presentations, or treatment contexts for which the paper is most useful. Keep this concise and specific.
   342→
   343→Do not use identical boilerplate across papers.
