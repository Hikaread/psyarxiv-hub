    12→    12→Abstract
    13→    13→As global populations age, early cognitive decline increasingly affects individuals and family
    14→    14→systems navigating identity disruption and relational strain. Contemporary memory-support
    15→    15→interventions emphasize visual recall, orientation, and task-based cognitive stimulation, yet
    16→    16→emotionally salient relational voice remains underleveraged as a structured intervention
    17→    17→mechanism. Converging evidence indicates that affectively encoded emotional responses may
    18→    18→persist even after the declarative memory for the events that produced them has faded (Feinstein
    19→    19→et al., 2010; Guzmán-Vélez et al., 2014). Building on this evidence base, this paper advances a
    20→    20→conceptual contribution: familiar relational voice is proposed to function as a cognitive anchor
    21→    21→that may support emotional continuity, identity preservation, relational connection, and
    22→    22→orientation even as episodic memory weakens. The paper introduces the VOCES Framework
    23→    23→(Voz Orientada a la Continuidad, Emoción y Sostén), a culturally integrative, dignity-centered
    24→    24→model that positions relational voice, emotional encoding, narrative continuity, and ritualized
    25→    25→support as stabilizing mechanisms for identity continuity. The framework synthesizes existing
    26→    26→research across auditory neuroscience, emotional memory, narrative identity, person-centered
    27→    27→care, attachment theory, and healthcare communication; it does not assert that voice alters
    28→    28→disease processes. MemoriGems is presented as one applied illustration of the framework. The
    29→    29→paper concludes by outlining research directions, including mixed-methods, feasibility, and
    30→    30→implementation studies, necessary to empirically evaluate voice-centered approaches to identity
    31→    31→preservation in early cognitive decline.
    32→    32→Keywords: dementia, early cognitive decline, relational voice, emotional memory,
    33→    33→identity continuity, person-centered care, narrative identity, healthcare communication, VOCES

    36→    36→Voice as a Cognitive Anchor: The VOCES Framework for Identity Preservation in Early
    37→    37→Cognitive Decline
    38→    38→Early cognitive decline disrupts more than memory performance; it challenges identity
    39→    39→continuity and relational stability. As autobiographical recall weakens, individuals may
    40→    40→experience fragmentation of self-concept, while families navigate shifting communication
    41→    41→patterns and anticipatory grief (Boss, 1999). Although intervention models frequently emphasize
    42→    42→cognitive accuracy and visual recall, comparatively less attention has been given to emotionally
    43→    43→salient auditory engagement as a structured stabilizing mechanism. This paper proposes that
    44→    44→relational voice functions as a uniquely resilient pathway to emotional memory and identity
    45→    45→continuity, and it formalizes that proposition through the VOCES Framework (Voz Orientada a
    46→    46→la Continuidad, Emoción y Sostén), an intentional, culturally embedded intervention model
    47→    47→designed to reinforce dignity and continuity in early cognitive decline.
    48→    48→The argument proceeds in several stages. First, the paper situates early cognitive decline
    49→    49→within its demographic, emotional, and relational context. Second, it reviews the science of voice
    50→    50→and emotional memory, distinguishing carefully between what existing evidence demonstrates
    51→    51→and what this paper proposes. Third, it positions identity continuity as a clinically meaningful
    52→    52→target and develops the communication-theoretic foundations that explain why familiar voice
    53→    53→differs from generic auditory stimulation. The VOCES Framework is then presented as an
    54→    54→integrated model, followed by an applied illustration, a discussion of practical applications across
    55→    55→care contexts, and recommendations for empirical evaluation. Throughout, the framework is
    56→    56→offered as a conceptual synthesis grounded in existing evidence rather than as a validated clinical
    57→    57→intervention.
    58→    58→The Problem Landscape
    59→    59→
    60→    60→Demographic Context
    61→    61→Demographic aging is a worldwide phenomenon, reflecting decades of gains in life
    62→    62→expectancy and a corresponding rise in age-related cognitive conditions. The World Health
    63→    63→Organization (WHO, 2025) estimates that approximately 57 million people were living with
    64→    64→dementia worldwide in 2021, with nearly 10 million new cases each year and more than 60% of
    65→    65→affected individuals residing in low- and middle-income countries. Alzheimer disease is the most
    66→    66→common cause, contributing an estimated 60% to 70% of cases, and dementia now ranks among
    67→    67→the leading causes of death and of disability and dependency among older adults globally (WHO,
    68→    68→2025). Mild cognitive impairment, which may precede dementia, further expands the population
    69→    69→that could benefit from early, identity-preserving support.
    70→    70→These trends carry particular significance because the majority of early cognitive support
    71→    71→occurs outside formal clinical settings. The WHO (2025) estimates that informal caregivers, most
    72→    72→often family members, provide on average several hours of care per day and account for roughly
    73→    73→half of the global economic cost of dementia. Increasingly, older adults remain in home
    74→    74→environments, relying on caregivers who may lack structured psychosocial tools to support
    75→    75→identity continuity. The scale and distribution of this need motivate interest in approaches that
    76→    76→are low in technological complexity, culturally adaptable, and feasible within everyday relational
    77→    77→contexts.
    78→    78→Emotional and Relational Disruption
    79→    79→Early cognitive decline affects narrative coherence and relational stability. As
    80→    80→autobiographical memory becomes less accessible, identity markers tied to roles,
    81→    81→accomplishments, and shared experiences may weaken, and family members frequently report
    82→    82→emotional strain as familiar communication patterns shift. The disruption is therefore not solely
    83→    83→
    84→    84→neurological; it is relational. Boss (1999, 2011) characterized this experience as ambiguous loss,
    85→    85→a form of loss without closure in which a loved one is simultaneously present and
    86→    86→psychologically altered, and which is associated with sustained, unresolved grief among
    87→    87→caregivers. Recognizing the relational dimension of decline reframes the goal of early support:
    88→    88→alongside cognitive maintenance, interventions may aim to sustain connection, mutual
    89→    89→recognition, and continuity of relationship.
    90→    90→Limitations of Current Interventions
    91→    91→Existing psychosocial interventions frequently prioritize cognition and orientation.
    92→    92→Cognitive stimulation has demonstrated modest benefits for cognitive function (Aguirre et al.,
    93→    93→2013), and reminiscence therapy, which uses tangible prompts to evoke memories and stimulate
    94→    94→conversation, has shown small and somewhat inconsistent effects on cognition, mood,
    95→    95→communication, and quality of life that vary by modality and setting (Woods et al., 2018). These
    96→    96→approaches are valuable, yet they often emphasize performance outcomes rather than identity
    97→    97→preservation, and many lack a structured mechanism for reinforcing continuity of self as decline
    98→    98→progresses. Emotionally meaningful auditory engagement through familiar voices remains
    99→    99→comparatively underdeveloped as a deliberate intervention strategy. The framework advanced
   100→   100→here is intended to complement, rather than replace, existing models by foregrounding relational
   101→   101→voice as an explicit, structured target.
   102→   102→The Science of Voice and Memory
   103→   103→Cognitive decline does not affect all neural and psychological systems uniformly. While
   104→   104→episodic and declarative recall often diminish early, several lines of evidence suggest that
   105→   105→affective and auditory processes associated with familiar people may remain comparatively
   106→   106→
   107→   107→accessible. This section reviews that evidence across three areas, then carefully distinguishes the
   108→   108→established findings from the conceptual proposition this paper advances.
   109→   109→Auditory Cognition and Familiar Voice Processing
   110→   110→The human voice conveys a rich array of information beyond linguistic content, including
   111→   111→cues to a speaker's identity and emotional state, which listeners perceive with notable ease (Belin
   112→   112→et al., 2000). Using functional neuroimaging, Belin and colleagues identified voice-selective
   113→   113→regions along the superior temporal sulcus, sometimes termed the temporal voice areas, that
   114→   114→respond preferentially to vocal sounds relative to other complex sounds. The voice of someone
   115→   115→known appears to hold a privileged status within this system: personally familiar voices elicit
   116→   116→more finely resolved neural response patterns than voices learned briefly in a laboratory or never
   117→   117→encountered before, consistent with the view that extensive and varied exposure yields richer
   118→   118→representations of the people we know (Kanber et al., 2025).
   119→   119→At the same time, voice processing is not uniformly spared in cognitive decline.
   120→   120→Systematic investigation of neurodegenerative populations indicates that components of voice
   121→   121→perception and voice recognition can be differentially affected in dementia, including in
   122→   122→Alzheimer disease (Hailstone et al., 2011). This nuance is important for the present framework,
   123→   123→as it cautions against assuming that intact recognition of a familiar speaker is the operative
   124→   124→mechanism. The relevant implication of this section is therefore conceptual rather than
   125→   125→diagnostic: the voice is not a generic auditory stimulus but a carrier of identity and affect, and the
   126→   126→framework's emphasis falls on the affective and relational salience of familiar voice rather than
   127→   127→on preserved voice-identity recognition as such. On this view, familiar relational voice may
   128→   128→remain a meaningful channel for engagement even as specific voice-processing abilities change.
   129→   129→Emotional Encoding and Amygdala-Hippocampal Interactions
   130→   130→
   131→   131→Emotional memory depends in part on interactions between the amygdala and
   132→   132→hippocampus that contribute to the encoding and retrieval of affectively charged experience. A
   133→   133→substantial body of research indicates that the amygdala modulates the consolidation of
   134→   134→memories for emotionally arousing experiences, strengthening the retention of affectively
   135→   135→significant information (McGaugh, 2004). Mechanistic work in animal models has begun to
   136→   136→specify how this may occur, suggesting that coordinated amygdalo-cortical activity, including
   137→   137→during sleep-dependent consolidation, enhances memory for emotionally associated material and
   138→   138→underscores the role of inter-regional dialogue in prioritizing emotional memories (Saito et al.,
   139→   139→2025). In Alzheimer disease, the hippocampus is typically affected early, impairing declarative
   140→   140→memory, whereas evidence suggests that amygdala-mediated affective processing may make
   141→   141→distinct and partially dissociable contributions to emotion and memory (Guzmán-Vélez et al.,
   142→   142→2016). This dissociation is theoretically important: it suggests that the systems supporting factual
   143→   143→recall and those supporting emotional responsiveness may decline along different trajectories,
   144→   144→leaving affective pathways relatively engaged even as declarative detail becomes less accessible.
   145→   145→Relative Preservation of Emotional Experience in Cognitive Decline
   146→   146→Direct evidence for the persistence of emotion beyond memory comes from studies of
   147→   147→patients with hippocampal damage and with Alzheimer disease. In a study of individuals with
   148→   148→severe amnesia, induced feelings of sadness and happiness persisted well after participants had
   149→   149→lost factual memory for the films that produced them (Feinstein et al., 2010). This phenomenon
   150→   150→was subsequently demonstrated in Alzheimer disease: patients continued to experience
   151→   151→prolonged emotional states even after their declarative memory for the eliciting events had faded,
   152→   152→and, strikingly, weaker memory was associated with longer-lasting emotion (Guzmán-Vélez et
   153→   153→al., 2014).
   154→   154→
   155→   155→Personally meaningful auditory stimuli appear especially capable of evoking such
   156→   156→responses, and music has served as an instructive model case. Familiar music can cue
   157→   157→autobiographical memories that are engaged through a distributed neural network including
   158→   158→medial prefrontal cortex (Janata, 2009), and in Alzheimer disease such music-evoked
   159→   159→autobiographical memories may be retained and tend to carry a positive emotional tone, a pattern
   160→   160→described as a positivity effect (Cuddy et al., 2017). Brief exposure to familiar music has
   161→   161→likewise been shown to induce lingering emotion in individuals with Alzheimer disease
   162→   162→(Reschke-Hernández et al., 2020). Although these findings concern music rather than voice, they
   163→   163→illustrate a broader principle relevant to the present framework: personally meaningful auditory
   164→   164→stimuli can engage emotion and autobiographical content even when declarative recall is
   165→   165→compromised. Relational voice is proposed here as a parallel and potentially complementary
   166→   166→channel, distinguished by its direct connection to attachment relationships, a proposition that
   167→   167→requires direct empirical testing rather than extrapolation from the music literature alone.
   168→   168→From Evidence to Proposition: Voice as a Cognitive Anchor
   169→   169→It is important to delineate precisely what the foregoing literature establishes and what
   170→   170→this paper proposes. Existing research demonstrates, among other findings, that the brain
   171→   171→contains voice-selective processing regions (Belin et al., 2000); that affective and declarative
   172→   172→systems may decline along partially dissociable paths (Guzmán-Vélez et al., 2016); and that
   173→   173→emotional experience can persist after the memory of its cause has faded, including in Alzheimer
   174→   174→disease (Feinstein et al., 2010; Guzmán-Vélez et al., 2014). What this paper proposes, as its
   175→   175→primary conceptual contribution, is an integrative interpretation: that familiar relational voice
   176→   176→may function as a cognitive anchor, an emotionally salient, identity-linked cue capable of
   177→   177→supporting emotional continuity, relational recognition, and a sense of orientation even as
   178→   178→
   179→   179→episodic memory declines. This proposition is a synthesis of existing evidence into a new
   180→   180→framework; it is hypothesized rather than demonstrated, and it does not assert that voice
   181→   181→engagement alters neuropathology or disease progression. The conceptual leap is identified
   182→   182→explicitly so that the framework's empirical status is not overstated.
   183→   183→Identity Continuity as a Clinical Priority
   184→   184→If emotionally salient relational cues remain accessible in early decline, then identity
   185→   185→continuity, rather than recall accuracy alone, becomes a meaningful target for intervention. This
   186→   186→section develops the theoretical basis for treating identity as a clinical priority, drawing on
   187→   187→narrative identity, personhood and person-centered care, attachment theory, and the stabilizing
   188→   188→role of ritual.
   189→   189→Narrative Identity and Self-Continuity
   190→   190→Contemporary psychology conceptualizes identity in substantial part as a story. Narrative
   191→   191→identity refers to a person's internalized and evolving life story, which integrates the
   192→   192→reconstructed past and the imagined future to provide a sense of unity and purpose (McAdams &
   193→   193→McLean, 2013). On this view, selfhood is sustained not by the perfect retention of facts but by
   194→   194→the ongoing integration of experience into a coherent narrative. Early cognitive decline can
   195→   195→fragment this autobiographical integration, destabilizing self-concept and relational roles. The
   196→   196→framework proposed here suggests that reinforcing the affective and relational threads of a
   197→   197→person's narrative, through familiar voice that affirms roles, relationships, and personal history,
   198→   198→may help sustain a sense of continuity even when factual recall is impaired. Continuity, in this
   199→   199→sense, is conceptualized as sustained recognition of who a person is, rather than as flawless recall
   200→   200→of what a person did.
   201→   201→Personhood, Dignity, and Person-Centered Care
   202→   202→
   203→   203→Kitwood's (1997) account of personhood reoriented dementia care away from a purely
   204→   204→biomedical model toward the recognition of the person. Personhood, in Kitwood's formulation, is
   205→   205→a status conferred on one human being by others within relationship and social being, and it is
   206→   206→sustained or undermined through the quality of interpersonal interaction (Kitwood, 1997;
   207→   207→Kitwood & Bredin, 1992). Among the psychological needs Kitwood identified, comfort,
   208→   208→attachment, identity, occupation, and inclusion are bound together by the centrality of
   209→   209→relationship. Subsequent work has elaborated person-centered care as an approach that values the
   210→   210→person, treats individuals as unique, adopts their perspective, and provides a supportive social
   211→   211→environment (Brooker, 2007), and has shown that self and identity can be actively maintained
   212→   212→through social interaction even amid significant impairment (Sabat & Harré, 1992). Within this
   213→   213→tradition, sustaining identity is not peripheral to care; it is the core ethical aim. The present
   214→   214→framework aligns with and operationalizes this aim by treating relational voice as a structured
   215→   215→means of affirming personhood.
   216→   216→Relational Identity and Attachment
   217→   217→Identity in cognitive decline is profoundly relational, and attachment theory offers a lens
   218→   218→for understanding why familiar figures carry such weight. Bowlby's (1969) account of
   219→   219→attachment as an enduring affectional bond has been extended to later life and dementia, where
   220→   220→proximity-seeking and attachment behaviors can intensify under conditions of uncertainty and
   221→   221→perceived insecurity (Miesen, 1993; Nelis et al., 2014). From this perspective, the familiar voice
   222→   222→of an attachment figure is not merely a recognizable sound but a signal of safety and connection,
   223→   223→capable of providing a secure base during disorientation. The framework proposed here draws on
   224→   224→this insight to suggest that relational voice may stabilize emotional states in part through its
   225→   225→attachment significance, a possibility that warrants empirical examination.
   226→   226→
   227→   227→Ritual, Predictability, and Stabilization
   228→   228→Ritualized engagement introduces predictability within the context of cognitive
   229→   229→uncertainty. As the reliability of memory fluctuates, repeated and structured sensory experiences
   230→   230→may provide psychological grounding, reframing memory support from performance assessment
   231→   231→toward identity reinforcement. Ritual, in this sense, functions not as routine alone but as a
   232→   232→stabilizing container through which continuity is reaffirmed. Embedding relational voice within
   233→   233→predictable, repeatable structures is therefore proposed to enhance its stabilizing potential, an
   234→   234→idea consistent with broader observations that structure and familiarity can reduce distress in
   235→   235→dementia care.
   236→   236→Communication-Theoretic Foundations
   237→   237→Because the proposed mechanism is relational voice, the framework requires a
   238→   238→communication-theoretic foundation that explains why familiar voice operates differently from
   239→   239→generic auditory input. This section draws on interpersonal, relational, family systems, and
   240→   240→organizational communication to articulate that distinction.
   241→   241→Why Familiar Voice Differs From Generic Auditory Stimuli
   242→   242→From a communication standpoint, the familiar voice is a relational message, not merely
   243→   243→an acoustic event. It carries relational history, identity attribution, and affective meaning that a
   244→   244→neutral or unfamiliar voice does not. Where generic auditory stimulation may engage attention or
   245→   245→arousal, familiar relational voice additionally signals who is speaking and what that person
   246→   246→means to the listener, activating the identity and emotional associations built across a shared
   247→   247→relationship. This distinction is central to the framework: the proposed anchoring effect is
   248→   248→hypothesized to derive from relational familiarity and affective salience rather than from auditory
   249→   249→input as such.
   250→   250→
   251→   251→Interpersonal and Relational Communication
   252→   252→Interpersonal and relational communication scholarship emphasizes that meaning is co-
   253→   253→constructed and that relationships are sustained through ongoing, mutually recognized exchange.
   254→   254→In dementia care, communication strategies that affirm the person, support face and dignity, and
   255→   255→invite reciprocity have been associated with more person-centered interactions (Ryan et al.,
   256→   256→2005; Savundranayagam & Moore-Nielsen, 2015). Relational voice can be understood as a
   257→   257→vehicle for these affirming functions, conveying recognition, warmth, and continuity of
   258→   258→relationship through tone and cadence as much as through propositional content. Framing voice
   259→   259→engagement in relational terms positions it within a well-established body of communication
   260→   260→practice rather than treating it as a novel sensory technique.
   261→   261→Family Systems and Caregiver Communication
   262→   262→Families operate as systems in which a change in one member reverberates throughout
   263→   263→the whole. As cognitive decline alters a person's communicative capacities, established patterns
   264→   264→of interaction are disrupted, and roles, expectations, and emotional dynamics shift across the
   265→   265→family. The experience of ambiguous loss can generate uncertainty about how to remain in
   266→   266→relationship with a loved one who is present yet changed (Boss, 1999, 2011). Structured
   267→   267→relational voice engagement may offer families an intentional means of maintaining connection,
   268→   268→providing a shared practice through which identity and relationship can be affirmed despite
   269→   269→communicative variability. In this sense, the framework addresses not only the person living with
   270→   270→decline but the relational system surrounding them.
   271→   271→Organizational Communication and Cultures of Care
   272→   272→At the level of care organizations, communication shapes culture, and culture shapes the
   273→   273→experience of those receiving care. Kitwood (1997) argued that the social and organizational
   274→   274→
   275→   275→environment can either sustain or diminish personhood, and person-centered communication
   276→   276→practices have been linked to more dignity-affirming care interactions (Savundranayagam &
   277→   277→Moore-Nielsen, 2015). Considered through an organizational lens, embedding relational voice
   278→   278→into the routines, training, and shared practices of care teams may help align everyday
   279→   279→communication with identity-preserving values. The framework thus has implications not only
   280→   280→for individual interactions but for how care environments structure communication at a systemic
   281→   281→level.
   282→   282→The VOCES Framework
   283→   283→Conceptual Overview
   284→   284→The VOCES Framework (Voz Orientada a la Continuidad, Emoción y Sostén)
   285→   285→conceptualizes relational voice as a culturally embedded stabilizing mechanism in early
   286→   286→cognitive decline. Building on evidence suggesting that affective and auditory responses to
   287→   287→familiar people may be comparatively preserved, the framework positions intentional voice
   288→   288→engagement as a structured method for reinforcing identity continuity. Rather than treating voice
   289→   289→as incidental sensory input, the model integrates emotionally salient auditory cues with ritualized
   290→   290→support structures designed to sustain relational recognition across evolving cognitive states. The
   291→   291→naming of the framework reflects relational plurality and cultural responsiveness: aging and
   292→   292→memory are shaped by linguistic, familial, and intergenerational narratives, and intervention
   293→   293→models must therefore treat cultural context as integral to identity preservation rather than as an
   294→   294→adjunct consideration.
   295→   295→Pillar I: Voz (Relational Voice)
   296→   296→Voz positions relational voice as the primary emotional and cognitive anchor within the
   297→   297→framework. Familiar voices, particularly those associated with attachment figures, carry affective
   298→   298→
   299→   299→salience that may remain accessible despite declining episodic recall (Belin et al., 2000; Kanber
   300→   300→et al., 2025; Miesen, 1993). Within VOCES, voice is operationalized as an intentional
   301→   301→intervention mechanism rather than passive background stimulus. Structured affirmations of
   302→   302→relational roles, identity markers, and personal history are proposed to reinforce continuity of self
   303→   303→across fluctuating cognitive states. Voz thus serves as the anchoring mechanism on which the
   304→   304→other pillars depend.
   305→   305→Pillar II: Continuidad (Identity Continuity)
   306→   306→Continuidad emphasizes the preservation of narrative coherence and identity markers.
   307→   307→Because early cognitive decline often fragments autobiographical integration, destabilizing
   308→   308→relational roles and self-concept (McAdams & McLean, 2013), the framework prioritizes
   309→   309→reinforcement of identity through repeated, emotionally grounded cues that affirm personal
   310→   310→history and relational significance. Continuity is conceptualized not as perfect recall but as
   311→   311→sustained recognition of who the individual is within evolving cognitive capacity. Continuidad
   312→   312→therefore defines the identity objective toward which voice engagement is directed.
   313→   313→Pillar III: Emoción (Emotionally Encoded Memory)
   314→   314→Emoción centers the activation of emotionally encoded pathways as a stabilizing
   315→   315→mechanism. Evidence that emotional experience can persist beyond declarative memory,
   316→   316→including in Alzheimer disease (Feinstein et al., 2010; Guzmán-Vélez et al., 2014), suggests that
   317→   317→affective salience embedded within familiar relational cues may facilitate recognition and
   318→   318→engagement even when factual retrieval diminishes. Within VOCES, emotionally meaningful
   319→   319→voice is intentionally structured to engage preserved affective responsiveness, reinforcing
   320→   320→connection through tone, cadence, and relational familiarity rather than informational content
   321→   321→
   322→   322→alone. Emoción reframes memory engagement from cognitive testing toward affective
   323→   323→continuity.
   324→   324→Pillar IV: Sostén (Structured Relational Support)
   325→   325→Sostén refers to the structured relational and tangible supports that stabilize engagement
   326→   326→over time. As cognitive reliability fluctuates, predictable and repeatable interaction structures
   327→   327→may reduce anxiety and enhance emotional grounding. Within the framework, voice-based
   328→   328→engagement is embedded within ritualized formats and, when applicable, supported by physical
   329→   329→artifacts that reinforce continuity. Sostén emphasizes that the value of the approach is not derived
   330→   330→solely from auditory input but from the consistent relational container within which voice is
   331→   331→experienced; stabilization is proposed to emerge through repetition, predictability, and sustained
   332→   332→affirmation of identity.
   333→   333→Integration of the Pillars
   334→   334→Together, the four pillars function as an integrated model rather than as discrete
   335→   335→components. Voz provides the anchoring mechanism, Continuidad defines the identity objective,
   336→   336→Emoción engages preserved affective pathways, and Sostén establishes the stabilizing structure
   337→   337→through which engagement is sustained. The framework proposes that identity preservation in
   338→   338→early cognitive decline requires simultaneous attention to relational voice, emotional encoding,
   339→   339→narrative continuity, and structured support, and that these elements are mutually reinforcing
   340→   340→rather than independently sufficient.
   341→   341→Applied Illustration: MemoriGems
   342→   342→MemoriGems serves as one applied illustration of the VOCES Framework. The platform
   343→   343→facilitates structured relational voice recordings paired with optional sensory supports,
   344→   344→operationalizing identity-based reinforcement within repeatable engagement structures. In doing
   345→   345→
   346→   346→so, it translates the theoretical components of the framework, relational voice, emotional
   347→   347→encoding, narrative continuity, and structured support, into a practical format accessible to
   348→   348→families navigating early cognitive change. MemoriGems does not purport to alter disease
   349→   349→progression; rather, it functions as a relational support tool aligned with the framework's
   350→   350→emphasis on dignity, continuity, and emotionally grounded engagement. It is presented here as a
   351→   351→single instantiation of the model and not as the focus of the paper, and its effectiveness, like that
   352→   352→of the framework more broadly, remains a matter for empirical evaluation.
   353→   353→Practical Applications Across Care Contexts
   354→   354→Because relational voice requires minimal technological complexity and draws on
   355→   355→existing relationships, the framework is adaptable across the settings in which early cognitive
   356→   356→support occurs. The following applications are offered as conceptual possibilities to be examined
   357→   357→empirically rather than as established practices.
   358→   358→Family Caregiving and Home Care
   359→   359→Most early cognitive support takes place at home, where informal caregivers provide the
   360→   360→majority of care (WHO, 2025). In these contexts, structured relational voice engagement may
   361→   361→give caregivers an intentional practice for maintaining connection as communication patterns
   362→   362→shift, allowing participation in affirmations of identity and shared history rather than reliance on
   363→   363→spontaneous recall. This may also offer families a shared ritual through which relationship is
   364→   364→sustained amid ambiguous loss (Boss, 1999).
   365→   365→Memory Care Communities and Adult Day Health
   366→   366→In memory care communities and adult day health programs, where staff are often
   367→   367→necessarily task-oriented and may have limited knowledge of residents' personal histories,
   368→   368→relational voice may help bring identity-affirming content into daily routines. Embedding
   369→   369→
   370→   370→familiar voice within predictable engagement structures could complement person-centered
   371→   371→communication practices and support continuity across staff and shifts (Ryan et al., 2005).
   372→   372→Hospitals and Acute Care
   373→   373→Hospitalization can be especially disorienting for people living with cognitive
   374→   374→impairment, who may experience unfamiliar environments as threatening and exhibit heightened
   375→   375→attachment-seeking behavior (Miesen, 1993). Familiar relational voice may offer a portable
   376→   376→source of reassurance and orientation during acute care, potentially supporting emotional
   377→   377→regulation in settings where familiar people cannot always be present.
   378→   378→Telehealth and Remote Support
   379→   379→Because voice can be recorded, shared, and delivered remotely, the framework is well
   380→   380→suited to telehealth and distributed caregiving arrangements. Remote delivery may extend
   381→   381→identity-affirming engagement to families separated by geography and to care models that rely
   382→   382→on virtual contact, broadening access without requiring complex infrastructure.
   383→   383→Advance Care Planning
   384→   384→Relational voice may also have a role in advance care planning, where capturing a
   385→   385→person's voice, values, and relational priorities earlier in the trajectory could preserve identity-
   386→   386→relevant material for later stages. Such applications would need to be approached with attention
   387→   387→to consent, autonomy, and the evolving preferences of the person.
   388→   388→Culturally Responsive Dementia Care
   389→   389→The framework's emphasis on linguistic and cultural responsiveness is intended to make
   390→   390→it adaptable across diverse communities, including multilingual families and intergenerational
   391→   391→households. Because the voice carries cultural and familial meaning, voice-centered approaches
   392→   392→
   393→   393→may be tailored to the languages, narratives, and relational structures that are most meaningful to
   394→   394→a given person, supporting equitable and dignity-centered care.
   395→   395→Broader Clinical and Social Implications
   396→   396→Caregiver Impact
   397→   397→Structured voice engagement may provide caregivers with intentional mechanisms for
   398→   398→maintaining relational stability as communication patterns shift, reducing reliance on
   399→   399→spontaneous recall and offering a framework for continued connection despite cognitive
   400→   400→variability. Given the documented emotional burden and ambiguous loss experienced by
   401→   401→dementia caregivers (Boss, 1999, 2011), approaches that support the relationship itself, and not
   402→   402→only the person's cognition, may have value for caregiver well-being, a possibility that merits
   403→   403→direct study.
   404→   404→Emotional Regulation
   405→   405→Ritualized auditory anchoring may contribute to emotional stabilization by engaging
   406→   406→preserved affective responsiveness. Predictable and emotionally meaningful voice cues are
   407→   407→hypothesized to reduce distress associated with disorientation, particularly when embedded
   408→   408→within consistent engagement structures. While empirical validation is required, this proposal is
   409→   409→consistent with evidence that familiar, emotionally salient stimuli can elicit and sustain emotion
   410→   410→in cognitive decline (Guzmán-Vélez et al., 2014; Reschke-Hernández et al., 2020).
   411→   411→Aging in Place
   412→   412→Voice-centered approaches are adaptable to the home and community contexts in which
   413→   413→the majority of early cognitive support occurs (WHO, 2025). Because relational voice requires
   414→   414→minimal technological complexity, the framework may offer scalable support within aging-in-
   415→   415→
   416→   416→place environments that lack formal clinical infrastructure, aligning with broader goals of
   417→   417→enabling older adults to remain in familiar settings.
   418→   418→Broader Neurological Contexts
   419→   419→Although developed within the context of early cognitive decline, the conceptual
   420→   420→principles underlying the VOCES Framework may have relevance in other conditions involving
   421→   421→memory and identity disruption, such as traumatic brain injury and neurorehabilitation. The
   422→   422→potential applicability of structured relational voice in these broader neurological contexts is
   423→   423→noted as a direction for future inquiry rather than an established extension.
   424→   424→Research and Future Directions
   425→   425→As a conceptual framework, VOCES requires empirical evaluation to determine its
   426→   426→clinical relevance, feasibility, and measurable impact. Several lines of research are
   427→   427→recommended.
   428→   428→First, mixed-methods studies could examine perceived identity continuity, relational
   429→   429→connection, and emotional experience among people living with early decline and their families,
   430→   430→combining qualitative accounts with quantitative indicators of distress, engagement, and well-
   431→   431→being. Second, pilot and feasibility studies are recommended to assess acceptability, adherence,
   432→   432→and implementation consistency across home and community settings, and to identify the
   433→   433→conditions under which structured voice engagement is most usable. Third, implementation
   434→   434→science approaches could examine how the framework can be integrated into existing care
   435→   435→workflows, caregiver routines, and organizational practices, attending to barriers, facilitators, and
   436→   436→cultural adaptation.
   437→   437→Outcome measurement warrants particular attention. Future work should identify or
   438→   438→develop measures of identity continuity and personhood, alongside established measures of
   439→   439→
   440→   440→quality of life, caregiver burden, mood, and behavioral and psychological symptoms, so that the
   441→   441→constructs central to the framework can be assessed directly rather than inferred. Longitudinal
   442→   442→designs may clarify whether structured voice anchoring is associated with sustained relational
   443→   443→stability over time, and eventual controlled trials would be necessary to evaluate efficacy.
   444→   444→Throughout, interdisciplinary collaboration across gerontology, neuropsychology,
   445→   445→communication sciences, and caregiving research will be essential to determine the framework's
   446→   446→applicability and scope.
   447→   447→Limitations
   448→   448→Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, this paper is conceptual: it synthesizes
   449→   449→existing research into a new framework but does not present original empirical data, and its
   450→   450→central proposition, that familiar relational voice functions as a cognitive anchor, is hypothesized
   451→   451→rather than demonstrated. Second, the supporting evidence is drawn from related but distinct
   452→   452→literatures, including studies of emotional memory in amnesia and Alzheimer disease, voice
   453→   453→processing in healthy adults, and familiar music in dementia; extending these findings to
   454→   454→relational voice as a structured intervention is an inference that requires direct testing. Third, the
   455→   455→framework does not claim to influence neuropathology or disease progression, and care should
   456→   456→be taken not to raise unwarranted expectations. Finally, individual, cultural, and clinical
   457→   457→variability means that the approach may not be uniformly applicable, and its appropriateness,
   458→   458→including attention to consent and autonomy, must be considered in context. These limitations
   459→   459→define the agenda for the empirical work outlined above.
   460→   460→Conclusion
   461→   461→Early cognitive decline disrupts identity continuity and relational stability in ways that
   462→   462→extend beyond measurable memory performance. Drawing on evidence that emotional
   463→   463→
   464→   464→experience and responses to familiar stimuli may persist even as declarative memory declines,
   465→   465→this paper has proposed that familiar relational voice can be conceptualized as a cognitive
   466→   466→anchor, and it has formalized that proposition through the VOCES Framework. By integrating
   467→   467→relational voice, emotional encoding, narrative continuity, and ritualized support, the model
   468→   468→reframes early memory intervention from performance-based recall toward dignity-centered
   469→   469→identity reinforcement, positioning the VOCES Framework as the structured model through
   470→   470→which the concept may be operationalized. The framework is offered as a conceptual
   471→   471→contribution grounded in existing evidence; empirical investigation is necessary to determine its
   472→   472→feasibility, applicability, and measurable impact across care contexts.
