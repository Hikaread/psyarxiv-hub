    37→Abstract
    38→Background: Acute cardiac events are accompanied by multifaceted psychological responses
    39→that affect individuals' coping capacity. The construct of cardiac distress captures these
    40→responses. Although the Cardiac Distress Inventory (CDI) assesses this construct, no Italian
    41→version is available. This study aimed to translate the CDI and assess its psychometric
    42→properties.
    43→Methods: After translation and back-translation, face validity was evaluated with five patients
    44→and content validity with healthcare professionals. The CDI was administered to 293 cardiac
    45→patients who experienced an event within 12 months, recruited from two Italian centers. Missing
    46→data were handled using multiple imputation. Exploratory factor analysis (EFA; principal axis
    47→factoring and oblimin rotation) assessed factorial structure, followed by reliability analysis.
    48→Criterion validity was evaluated through correlations with Emotion Thermometers and Kessler-
    49→
    50→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 3
    51→6 (K6). ROC analysis assessed discriminative accuracy and identified the optimal cut-off score
    52→using K6 as criterion.
    53→Results: The questionnaire demonstrated good face validity and suboptimal content validity
    54→(CVI-S/Ave = .72). EFA revealed a five-factor structure explaining 37.05% of the variance,
    55→comprising "Role Change and Adaptation to Disease", "Social Isolation", "Cognitive Challenges",
    56→"Emotional Overwhelm", and "Doctor-Patient Communication". The final 42-item version (11
    57→items removed for insufficient loadings, 2 for reliability issues) showed good internal
    58→consistency (ω = .746-.879) and good discriminative accuracy (AUC = .83) with optimal cut-off
    59→of 26 (sensitivity = 81%, specificity = 74%).
    60→Conclusions: The Italian CDI represents a psychometrically sound instrument addressing a
    61→significant gap in cardiovascular psychology assessment. Its five-factor structure provides a
    62→comprehensive framework for evaluating cardiac distress and informing targeted clinical
    63→interventions.

    68→Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) represent a leading cause of mortality and morbidity
    69→worldwide (Roth et al., 2020). In Italy, prevalence is particularly high (12.87% in 2017), likely
    70→due to population aging, with CVD accounting for 34.81% of all deaths (Saglietto et al., 2021).
    71→A relevant associated issue is the bidirectional relationship between CVDs and mental
    72→health (Bueno et al., 2025). Depression occurs in 15.0% — 31.3% of cardiovascular patients,
    73→while anxiety affects 27% — 32.9% (Karami et al., 2023; Murphy et al., 2019; Rafiei et al., 2023;
    74→Storer et al., 2023). Both conditions are associated with poorer prognosis (Bueno et al., 2025).
    75→Beyond depression and anxiety, cardiac diseases and events can trigger diverse
    76→psychological responses including post-traumatic stress disorder or symptoms (Bueno et al.,
    77→
    78→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 4
    79→2025) and denial (Worcester, 2015), which serves as coping mechanism in 3.0% — 53.2% of
    80→patients (Patierno et al., 2023). Patients may also experience grief reactions related to autonomy
    81→loss and identity changes (Higgins et al., 2007; Worcester, 2015), often accompanied by feelings
    82→of anger, guilt, and subclinical depressive symptoms, collectively referred to as 'cardiac blues'
    83→(Higgins et al., 2007; Murphy et al., 2015).
    84→Contemporary research has increasingly focused on a comprehensive conceptualization
    85→of psychological experiences following cardiac events, leading to the cardiac distress construct:
    86→a persistent negative emotional state involving multiple psychosocial domains that challenges
    87→patients' capacity to cope with their condition, treatment, daily living changes, sense of self, and
    88→future orientation. Cardiac distress encompasses previously identified psychological responses
    89→and additional components, including physical symptoms perception, grief related to identity
    90→changes and functionality loss, fear of disease progression, recurrence, and death, sexual
    91→concerns, and sleep disturbances (Jackson et al., 2022b). The clinical relevance of cardiac
    92→distress assessment has been further underlined in the 2025 European Society of Cardiology
    93→Clinical Consensus Statement on Mental Health and Cardiovascular Disease (Bueno et al.,
    94→2025), which recommends its inclusion as a potential component of routine screening protocols
    95→for cardiovascular patients.
    96→Recognizing the need for comprehensive psychological assessment in cardiac
    97→populations, Jackson et al. (2022a) developed the Cardiac Distress Inventory (CDI). This 55-
    98→item instrument evaluates the presence and severity of symptoms across eight subscales. A 12-
    99→item short form (CDI-SF; Le Grande et al., 2023) was subsequently developed. Cross-cultural
   100→translations of the CDI are available in Chinese (Fong et al., 2024), Farsi (Sharif-Nia et al.,
   101→2025b), and Turkish (Afşar et al., 2025).
   102→In Italy, psychological evaluation of cardiac patients currently follows a fragmented
   103→approach. National guidelines (Consiglio Nazionale Ordine degli Psicologi, 2024) recommend
   104→the use of brief questionnaires, which are often not specifically designed for cardiac populations.
   105→
   106→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 5
   107→Commonly used instruments include the Symptoms CheckList-90-R (Prunas et al., 2012) and
   108→the Beck Depression Inventory II (Ghisi et al., 2006). While some cardiac-specific instruments
   109→are available in Italian, such as the Surgical Fear Questionnaire (Damico et al., 2024) and the
   110→HeartQoL (Fattirolli et al., 2022), none comprehensively assess cardiac distress as
   111→conceptualized above.
   112→This study aims to develop an Italian version of the CDI and evaluate its psychometric
   113→properties in a sample of patients with recent cardiac events. We hypothesize that the Italian
   114→CDI will show adequate validity and reliability for future comprehensive psychological
   115→assessment of cardiac patients across clinical and research applications.
   116→Method
   117→Procedures
   118→This study employed a sequential two-phase design: (1) translation and cross-cultural
   119→adaptation with preliminary validation, and (2) comprehensive psychometric testing in the
   120→target clinical population. Assessments were conducted by clinical psychologists with experience
   121→in cardiovascular psychology. Data collection followed standard confidentiality protocols, with
   122→prompt anonymization/pseudonymization and secure storage.
   123→The study received ethical approval from the Ethics Committees of the local health
   124→authorities (Comitato Etico Area Vasta Emilia Centro, AUSL Bologna, Italy, Ref: 155-2023-OSS-
   125→AUSLBO, 16/02/2023; Comitato Etico dell'Ospedale San Raffaele, IRCCS Ospedale San
   126→Raffaele, Italy, Ref: 119/2023, 10/05/2023) and adhered to the ethical standards of the
   127→institutional and national research committees and to the Helsinki Declaration (1975, revised
   128→2008).
   129→Phase 1: Translation and Item Pre-testing
   130→The translation process was authorized by the original authors and followed established
   131→cross-cultural adaptation guidelines (Beaton et al., 2000). Forward translation was performed
   132→by a bilingual healthcare professional, followed by back-translation by a native English speaker
   133→
   134→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 6
   135→fluent in Italian and blinded to the original questionnaire content. Translators focused on
   136→equivalence, simplicity, and culturally appropriate language. Discrepancies were resolved
   137→through a consensus meeting with the research team.
   138→Face validity was assessed through individual interviews with five patients from the
   139→target population to evaluate item comprehensibility, acceptability, and emotional impact.
   140→Content validity was evaluated by 11 cardiac healthcare professionals using a four-point
   141→scale according to Lynn's guidelines (1986).
   142→Phase 2: Item Testing
   143→Recruitment occurred between March 2023 and November 2024 at two Italian centers.
   144→The Cardiac Division of Bellaria Hospital (Bologna) recruited consecutive outpatients
   145→undergoing cardiac rehabilitation, whereas the Heart Surgery Division of IRCCS Policlinico San
   146→Donato (San Donato Milanese) recruited cardiac surgery patients before discharge.
   147→After a comprehensive explanation of the study and informed consent procedures,
   148→participants completed questionnaires either on-site or at home, with quality data checks
   149→conducted upon return. The assessment took around 35 minutes, and began with
   150→sociodemographic and clinical characteristics collection through a structured form, verified
   151→through patient interview and medical records. The primary measure was the Italian version of
   152→the CDI. Secondary measures included: Emotion Thermometers (ET) (Mitchell et al., 2012),
   153→Kessler Psychological Distress Scale-6 (K6) (Carrà et al., 2011), Patient Health Questionnaire-4
   154→(PHQ-4) (Kroenke et al., 2009), Diagnostic Criteria for Psychosomatic Research Semi-
   155→Structured Interview (DCPR-SSI) (Fava et al., 2017), a single item from the PsychoSocial Index
   156→(PSI) related to quality of life (Piolanti et al., 2016), and Morisky Medication Adherence Scale-8
   157→(MMAS-8) (Fabbrini et al., 2013). Additional clinical data, including pharmacological
   158→treatments and physiological parameters, were extracted from medical records for a participant
   159→subsample. The current analysis focuses on core psychometric validation measures.

   162→Participants
   163→Adults who had experienced a cardiac event (e.g., myocardial infarction, bypass surgery,
   164→valve surgery, arrhythmia requiring intervention) within the previous 12 months were
   165→considered eligible. Exclusion criteria included inadequate Italian language proficiency or any
   166→condition that would impair comprehension of the informed consent process or study
   167→instruments.
   168→Sample size followed the guideline of five participants per item (55 items × 5 = 275
   169→participants), providing sufficient power for Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) (Bryant and
   170→Yarnold, 1995). Recruitment aimed for at least 280 participants for site balance.
   171→Measures
   172→Sociodemographic and Clinical Data
   173→Sociodemographic and clinical data were collected using a modified version of the
   174→instrument utilized in the original CDI validation study. Variables included age, gender, marital
   175→status, living arrangements, employment status, education, financial difficulties, type of cardiac
   176→event, comorbidities, and participation in cardiac rehabilitation programs.
   177→Cardiac Distress Inventory (Jackson et al., 2022a)
   178→The CDI comprises 55 items describing post-cardiac event experiences. Respondents
   179→indicate whether each item was experienced and rate associated distress on a 4-point scale (0 =
   180→none, 3 = severe). Items not experienced are scored as 0. Total instrument score ranges from 0
   181→to 165, across 8 subscales representing core psychosocial domains relevant to cardiac
   182→populations. Established cut-offs classify moderate (≥ 25) and severe (≥ 37) cardiac-related
   183→emotional distress. The original CDI demonstrated adequate to good internal consistency across
   184→all subscales (Cronbach's α = .73–.87) and satisfactory criterion validity (Jackson et al., 2022a).
   185→Emotion Thermometers (ETs) (Mitchell et al., 2012)
   186→The ETs include four 0-10 visual analog scales assessing distress, anxiety, depression,
   187→and anger over the past week. These instruments have demonstrated good criterion validity and
   188→
   189→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 8
   190→reliability in oncological populations (Harju et al., 2019) and good clinical sensitivity for
   191→detecting psychological distress in cardiovascular patient populations (Mitchell et al., 2012). A
   192→separate thermometer assessed COVID-19-related anxiety or worry over the past week to
   193→distinguish between distress attributable to the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic and distress
   194→specifically related to participants' cardiac conditions, consistent with the original CDI
   195→validation (Jackson et al., 2022a).
   196→Kessler Psychological Distress Scale-6 (K6) (Carrà et al., 2011)
   197→The K6 is a 6-item psychological distress screening instrument validated in Italian
   198→populations. Score ranges from 6 to 30, with higher scores indicating greater distress. The
   199→Italian version demonstrated excellent internal consistency (Cronbach's α = .84) and good
   200→discriminative accuracy for distinguishing between individuals with and without severe mental
   201→health conditions (Carrà et al., 2011). In this study, the K6 exhibited good internal consistency
   202→(McDonald's ω = .84).
   203→Data Analysis
   204→All statistical analyses were performed using SPSS Statistics for Windows, version
   205→29.0.2.0, with content validity analyses conducted in Microsoft Excel for Windows, version
   206→2508. Descriptive statistics summarized sample characteristics. Distribution normality was
   207→assessed visually and with the Shapiro-Wilk test when appropriate.
   208→Pre-testing analysis
   209→Content validity indices and modified kappa coefficients were calculated according to
   210→Lynn’s guidelines (1986). Complete procedures for calculation are reported in Supplementary
   211→Material A.
   212→Missing data were handled using multiple imputation with five datasets. Detailed
   213→procedures are reported in Supplementary Material B. Complete-case analysis results are
   214→provided in Supplementary Materials D for comparison with multiple imputation solutions.
   215→While some differences between approaches are present, they are likely to reflect the reduced
   216→
   217→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 9
   218→sample size (n = 277), which is approaching the minimum threshold (n = 275). Given the modest
   219→sample size and the statistical advantages of multiple imputation (Buuren, 2018), only imputed
   220→results are presented.
   221→Exploratory Factor Analysis
   222→An Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) was performed to identify the questionnaire's
   223→underlying factor structure. Data adequacy was confirmed through Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin
   224→sampling adequacy (KMO ≥ .60; Kaiser & Rice, 1974) and Bartlett's test. Given non-normal
   225→distribution, principal axis factoring was employed, with direct oblimin rotation (δ = 0) and
   226→Kaiser normalization to allow for correlated factors. Factor retention followed the Kaiser
   227→criterion (eigenvalues > 1.0) and scree plot inspection. Items with factor loadings < .31 were
   228→systematically removed through iterative analysis until achieving a stable solution. The
   229→threshold was defined consistently with established guidelines (Tabachnick, 2014) and similarly
   230→to the original validation study (Jackson et al., 2022a). This threshold also ensured consistent
   231→results across multiple imputation datasets.
   232→Reliability
   233→Reliability was assessed using McDonald's omega (ω > .70 acceptable, > .80 good, > .90
   234→excellent). Item-total correlations and omega changes following item deletions were evaluated.
   235→Items with correlations with the total score < .30 or that increased McDonald's Omega upon
   236→deletion were considered for removal (Cristobal et al., 2007).
   237→Concurrent and Discriminant Validity
   238→Concurrent validity was assessed through Spearman's correlations between CDI and
   239→established distress measures (Distress Thermometer, K6). Discriminant validity was evaluated
   240→by examining correlations between the CDI and theoretically unrelated constructs (COVID-19
   241→concern, Anger Thermometer).

   244→Discriminant Accuracy Analysis and Cut-off Determination
   245→Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was conducted to find the optimal
   246→CDI cut-off score for high distress potentially associated with mental illness, using the K6 cut-off
   247→(≥ 7; Carrà et al., 2011) as criterion. Optimal cut-off was determined via Youden's index
   248→(Schisterman et al., 2005). Discriminative accuracy was assessed through area under the curve
   249→(AUC) values, interpreted as acceptable (> .70), good (> .80), or excellent (> .90) (Çorbacıoğlu
   250→and Aksel, 2023). The same K6-based procedure was applied to determine screening cut-offs for
   251→each subscale, identifying levels of general distress associated with each domain.
   252→Results
   253→Phase 1: Item Pre-testing
   254→Face Validity
   255→Face validity interviews were conducted with five cardiac patients (four men, one
   256→woman; median age = 76 years, IQR = 54–85). Completion required a median of 15 minutes
   257→(IQR = 10–20). Patients’ feedback showed general clarity, though items 1, 10, 47, and 54
   258→required clarification. Following research team discussion, items 47 and 54 were reworded.
   259→Additional information on the interviewed sample and their comments is reported in
   260→Supplementary Material C.
   261→Content Validity
   262→Modified Kappa coefficients were not normally distributed (W = .89, p < .001), ranging
   263→from .21 to 1.00 (Mdn = .70, IQR = .57–.81); 26 items (47%) demonstrated excellent content
   264→validity (κ ≥ .75), 11 good (κ ≥ .60), 7 fair (κ ≥ .40), and 11 poor (κ < .40). Overall CVI-S/Ave was
   265→.72, with only Physical Challenges achieving adequate validity (CVI-S/Ave = .81). Despite
   266→suboptimal indices, all items were retained for empirical testing to maintain comparability with
   267→the original instrument and enable cross-cultural comparisons. Final item retention decisions
   268→were deferred to exploratory factor analysis, which provides empirical evidence of item
   269→performance within the target population and may reveal cultural differences between expert
   270→

   272→judgment and patient experience that content validity assessment cannot capture. Complete
   273→modified Kappa coefficients, CVI-I values, and subscale CVI-S/Ave scores are presented in

   359→Discussion
   360→The purposes of the present study were to translate and assess the psychometric
   361→properties of the CDI in an Italian population with recent cardiac events. The Italian version of
   362→the CDI comprises 42 items across 5 subscales, differing from the original version. Furthermore,
   363→despite suboptimal content validity and mixed results on discriminant validity, it demonstrated
   364→good face validity, concurrent validity, reliability, and discriminant accuracy. The questionnaire
   365→and relative scoring methods are available in the supplementary materials.
   366→Face and Content Validity
   367→Face validity assessment led to revision of two items, with patients reporting clarity and
   368→comprehensibility, although the questionnaire was perceived as lengthy. While specific results
   369→regarding face validity are not reported in the original study (Jackson et al., 2022a), similar
   370→findings for clarity and comprehensibility are reported in the Farsi translation study (Sharif-Nia
   371→et al., 2025b).
   372→Content validity analysis yielded unexpected results: 26 items were identified as
   373→candidates for removal, and seven of eight subscales, as well as the whole questionnaire, showed
   374→subthreshold content validity. All items were retained to enable cross-cultural comparisons and
   375→assess divergence between expert judgment and empirical performance. This divergence was in
   376→fact later confirmed: for example, item 13 "Not knowing how my family will cope if something
   377→should happen to me", deemed less relevant by experts (κ = .34), showed high factor loading in
   378→the Emotional Overwhelm factor (.55). Conversely, item 2 "Being woken up at night by my
   379→racing heart", which was unanimously endorsed by experts (κ = 1.00), was dropped for
   380→
   381→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 15
   382→insufficient loading on any factor. A comparison of κ values with factor loadings for each item
   383→can be found in Supplementary Table A1. These findings align with the literature documenting
   384→professional-patient discrepancies regarding factors that impact disease (Dunderdale et al.,
   385→2005; Woodend et al., 1997). Unfortunately, other cross-cultural CDI validations lack content
   386→validity assessment results, precluding comparison (Afşar et al., 2025; Fong et al., 2024; Sharif-
   387→Nia et al., 2025b).
   388→Exploratory Factor Analysis Results
   389→EFA and reliability analysis led to the removal of 13 items (24% of the original),
   390→identifying five main factors: "Role Change and Adaptation to Disease", "Social Isolation",
   391→"Cognitive Challenges", "Emotional Overwhelm", and "Doctor-patient Communication." All
   392→removed items are reported in Supplementary Material G. The 42-item Italian CDI
   393→demonstrated excellent overall reliability, with the five subscales presenting adequate to good
   394→reliability.
   395→Compared to the original CDI, the removal of 13 items and the reduction to five factors
   396→may reflect cultural differences in cardiac distress conceptualization. A heatmap of the items
   397→between the two factorial structures is presented in Supplementary Table G1.
   398→"Role Change and Adaptation to Disease" represents the dominant factor in the
   399→questionnaire. The Italian CDI incorporates items from uncertainty, disconnection, depletion,
   400→and physical challenges factors into this primary factor, with the "Changes to roles and
   401→relationships" factor almost completely incorporated as well. Although this combination could
   402→appear as a statistical artifact, the individual items form a coherent pattern, addressing identity
   403→transformation ("Thinking I will never be the same again"), functional limitations ("Not being
   404→able to return to work or continue working "), physical symptoms ("Having more pain than I can
   405→deal with"), and uncertainty ("Being unable to plan for the future "). These elements could
   406→represent facets of disease adaptation challenges, and the high internal consistency of the factor
   407→suggests that this construct may better capture Italian patients’ adaptation-related distress than
   408→
   409→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 16
   410→separate discrete processes. This interpretation aligns with the literature, which identifies
   411→identity-related changes, loss, and adaptation as prevailing themes post-cardiac events (Dreyer
   412→et al., 2021; Genardini et al., 2008; Higgins et al., 2007; Murphy et al., 2022).
   413→The "Social Isolation" factor comprised mainly disconnection items (e.g., "Being isolated
   414→from friends and family") from the original "Disconnection and Hopelessness" factor,
   415→highlighting the potential relevance of this aspect for Italian cardiac patients. This finding aligns
   416→with recent research by Sharif-Nia, Jackson, Salehi, Miraghai, and Hosseini (2025a), who
   417→observed that social isolation is associated with cardiac distress through both direct and indirect
   418→pathways mediated by loneliness.
   419→The "Cognitive Challenges" factor kept almost the same structure as in the original
   420→questionnaire. The "Emotional Overwhelm" factor completely incorporated the "Death
   421→Concerns" factor plus other negative symptoms from other factors (e.g., "Being tearful more
   422→easily than before"), suggesting an integration of death-related thoughts into a broader
   423→psychological response. Finally, the "Doctor-patient communication" factor resembled the
   424→original "Health System Challenges," but focused specifically on communication difficulties with
   425→healthcare personnel. This finding aligns with evidence linking poor doctor-patient
   426→communication with higher depressive symptomatology (Schenker et al., 2009). Conversely, a
   427→study by Ballo et al. (2017) demonstrated that effective patient-doctor communication led to
   428→better mood and disease comprehension.
   429→While these findings suggest cultural differences in cardiac distress conceptualization,
   430→limited comparative literature prevents definitive conclusions. Currently, to the best of our
   431→knowledge, the only other complete cross-cultural CDI validation is the Turkish version (Afşar et
   432→al., 2025). A brief comparison reveals a similar number of dropped items (19) and an equal
   433→number of factors (5). However, the five factors in the Turkish CDI retain the same names as the
   434→original version and show some relevant differences in the maintained factors compared with
   435→
   436→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 17
   437→the Italian ones (e.g., "Disconnection and hopelessness" was dropped, while "Physical
   438→challenges" was maintained).
   439→Criterion validity and Discriminative Accuracy
   440→The Italian CDI showed good concurrent validity, with moderate to strong correlations
   441→with the Distress Thermometer (comparable to, though slightly lower than, the original study)
   442→and the K6. Discriminant validity showed mixed results: a low correlation with COVID-19
   443→concern and a significant, moderate correlation with anger. The anger correlation, which is
   444→similar to the original study (Jackson et al., 2022a), may reflect the role of anger in cardiac
   445→distress rather than poor discriminant validity, in line with the literature characterizing anger as
   446→part of cardiac events emotional response (Higgins et al., 2007). The low correlation with the
   447→COVID-19 Concern Thermometer further supports discriminant validity.
   448→The discriminative accuracy for identifying distress potentially associated with mental
   449→illness was good (sensitivity = 81%, specificity = 74%). The identified cut-off of 26 is remarkably
   450→similar to the original moderate distress threshold (25), with comparable sensitivity and
   451→specificity (78% and 81%, respectively; Jackson et al., 2022a). At the same time, all subscales
   452→demonstrated acceptable to good discriminative accuracy in identifying elevated distress levels
   453→within each domain (sensitivity = 59–78%, specificity = 65–85%).
   454→Limitations and future directions
   455→The present findings must be interpreted in light of several important limitations.
   456→Suboptimal content validity may reflect differences between professional and patient
   457→conceptualizations of cardiac distress in the Italian cultural context, potentially impacting
   458→clinical implementation if healthcare providers perceive items as less relevant.
   459→Despite standardization efforts, methodological variations across centers cannot be ruled
   460→out. The recruitment approach, while employing consecutive sampling at one site, was not
   461→designed to ensure population representativeness, thus limiting the generalizability of these
   462→findings.
   463→
   464→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 18
   465→The voluntary participation model may have introduced selection bias. Additionally,
   466→self-report measures are susceptible to social desirability bias. Future studies should consider
   467→incorporating social desirability checks, objective measures, or informant ratings.
   468→From a psychometric perspective, although the sample size met established EFA
   469→guidelines (five participants per item), contemporary recommendations suggest larger samples.
   470→The relatively low explained variance and modest communalities indicates that the five-factor
   471→structure may not fully capture the complexity of cardiac distress in Italian patients, supporting
   472→alternative structures exploration and confirmatory factor analysis in independent samples.
   473→The substantial divergence from the original eight-factor structure raises cross-cultural
   474→validity questions. Future research should conduct rigorous cross-cultural explorations to
   475→determine whether observed differences reflect cultural adaptation or psychometric limitations.
   476→Subscale cut-offs were determined using general psychological distress (K6) as a
   477→common criterion rather than domain-specific clinical measures, limiting their interpretation as
   478→diagnostic thresholds for domain-specific problems. Future studies employing domain-specific
   479→criteria would enable more precise clinical interpretation and targeted intervention planning.
   480→Future research should prioritize confirmatory factor analysis to replicate the five-factor
   481→structure, longitudinal validation to assess temporal stability, and expanded cross-cultural
   482→investigations. Furthermore, exploring associations with clinical variables (e.g., comorbidity,
   483→pharmacotherapy) and sociodemographic factors (e.g., gender, socioeconomic status) could
   484→further characterize how cardiac distress manifests. Finally, this study focused on recent cardiac
   485→events; future studies should assess construct validity and explore adaptation to chronic
   486→cardiovascular conditions, which may entail different psychological needs and coping patterns.
   487→Conclusions
   488→Despite some reported limitations, the Italian CDI shows solid psychometric properties
   489→that support its use in both clinical and research settings with Italian cardiac patients. As one of
   490→the few Italian tools specifically developed for the comprehensive psychological assessment of
   491→
   492→ITALIAN CARDIAC DISTRESS INVENTORY 19
   493→cardiac patients, its five-factor structure allows for specific characterization of patient concerns
   494→across distinct domains of disease adaptation, social isolation, cognitive challenges, emotional
   495→overwhelm, and healthcare communication. This enables targeted investigations of specific
   496→distress domains in research settings and tailored interventions in clinical practice. In
   497→conclusion, the Italian CDI addresses a significant gap in cardiac psychology assessment tools,
   498→providing healthcare professionals with a culturally appropriate instrument for a comprehensive
   499→psychological evaluation of cardiovascular patients in both clinical and research contexts.
