    32→25 Abstract
    33→26 Virtual reality (VR) can reduce experimental and clinical pain, yet it remains unclear whether
    34→27 these effects translate to naturally occurring muscle pain (NOMP) experienced during physical
    35→28 tasks. This study examined whether nature immersion presented through immersive VR
    36→29 attenuates NOMP and enhances motor performance during prescribed and self-regulated
    37→30 exercise. Two within-participant crossover studies were conducted. In Study 1, twelve healthy
    38→31 participants completed a stepped incremental cycling task to exhaustion under three
    39→32 conditions: immersive VR (nature scene), a two-dimensional sham display, and a no-
    40→33 intervention control. Pain onset, NOMP intensity, perceived effort, and time to exhaustion were
    41→34 measured. In Study 2, twenty-four participants performed intermittent isometric handgrip
    42→35 contractions at fixed light (13/100) or strong (50/100) perceived effort under the same
    43→36 conditions, with force output and NOMP intensity measured. Conditions were pseudo-
    44→37 randomised and counterbalanced. Immersive VR did not significantly delay pain onset, prolong
    45→38 time to exhaustion, reduce NOMP intensity, or lower perceived effort compared with sham or
    46→39 control conditions. A small reduction in NOMP intensity occurred early in the cycling task, but
    47→40 effects were trivial and not sustained. In Study 2, NOMP intensity, force production, and
    48→41 fatigue-related declines were similar across conditions. Exploratory analyses showed no
    49→42 moderating effects of sex, environmental presence, or fatigue. Overall, these findings
    50→43 demonstrate that nature-based VR immersion has limited effects on NOMP or motor
    51→44 performance. This likely reflects a mismatch between central mechanisms targeted by VR and
    52→45 strong peripheral drivers of NOMP. Consequently, VR hypoalgesia appears pain-type
    53→46 dependent and potentially pain-intensity dependent, with effects limited to lower pain
    54→47 intensities.

    58→48 Introduction
    59→49 Naturally occurring muscle pain (NOMP) is an inherent feature of physical tasks [1]. Emanating
    60→50 from the stimulation of metabo- and mechano-sensitive nociceptors in the working
    61→51 musculature [2,3], nociceptive signals are relayed through afferent fibres to the brain where
    62→52 they are processed into NOMP [4]. Increased exercise intensity often results in greater
    63→53 nociception and intensified NOMP [5]. Accordingly, NOMP is considered acute, transient, and
    64→54 a signal of potential tissue damage [6].
    65→55 During fixed workload tasks (e.g., constant force), individuals can maintain task performance
    66→56 in the presence of NOMP by increasing the engagement of physical and cognitive resources
    67→57 (i.e., its effort [7,8]). Therefore, task performance can be maintained but is associated with a
    68→58 higher perceived effort [9]. However, little is known about the effects of NOMP during self-
    69→59 regulated tasks such as exercising at a constant perceived effort intensity. Theoretically,
    70→60 physical outputs (e.g., force) should decrease with increasing NOMP intensity due to
    71→61 neurophysiological and cognitive effects which make the task more effortful for a given output
    72→62 [10,11]. As such, the onset and tolerance of NOMP is a strong influence on motor task
    73→63 performance regardless of task type [1,5]. Consequently, exploring ways that an individual can
    74→64 control and manipulate pain experiences to enhance task performance is an intriguing
    75→65 prospect, especially as NOMP is ever-present in daily life [6].
    76→66 Previous research has used a variety of neuromodulation techniques including electrical [12] or
    77→67 magnetic [13] brain stimulations to reduce pain intensity (hypoalgesia). However, immersive
    78→68 virtual reality could be a novel and less aversive method to reduce pain [14]. Trials exposing
    79→69 participants to nature scenes in virtual reality show higher pain thresholds (i.e., pain onset),
    80→70 increased pain tolerance (i.e., a maximal point participant[s] can deal with pain), and an overall
    81→71 hypoalgesia effect compared to no intervention [15,16]. Moreover, the more present individuals

    85→72 feel within virtual reality, the greater the hypoalgesia effect [17]. It is likely the distracting visual
    86→73 and audio cues presented in virtual reality reduce the activity of pain-related brain areas like
    87→74 the anterior cingulate cortex, insula, and thalamus from processing pain-generating signals
    88→75 [14,18,19]. Yet, whilst studies have showed that nature immersion in virtual reality can effectively
    89→76 reduce acute experimental or clinical pain [16,20], no study has assessed whether these
    90→77 interventions can have the same effect on NOMP.
    91→78 Thus, the purpose of this study was to assess whether nature scenes presented in virtual reality
    92→79 reduce the intensity of NOMP during motor tasks. Subsequently, the study aims to assess
    93→80 whether nature immersion through virtual reality enhances task outputs (e.g., time to
    94→81 exhaustion, force production) compared to sham and control conditions due to potential
    95→82 hypoalgesia. These aims were addressed in two related studies. In Study 1, participants
    96→83 completed a stepped incremental cycling task to exhaustion where their task outputs (power
    97→84 output) were prescribed. In Study 2, participants completed an intermittent isometric handgrip
    98→85 task at a constant perceived effort intensity where they could self-regulate their task outputs
    99→86 (force production). Across both studies, participants performed the tasks under three
   100→87 conditions: with immersive virtual reality projecting a 360˚ nature scene (experimental); a two-
   101→88 dimensional version of the nature scene (sham); and no nature scene (control).
   102→89 We hypothesised that time to pain onset and task exhaustion would be later in the
   103→90 experimental than the sham and control conditions and that per task iso-time (e.g., 25, 50,
   104→91 75%), pain intensity and perceived effort would be lower in the experimental than the sham and
   105→92 control conditions (Study 1). We hypothesised that force output would be higher in the
   106→93 experimental versus the sham and control conditions (Study 2). We explored whether task
   107→94 intensity, participant sex, and presence ratings impacted the interactions between NOMP and
   108→95 force production.

   143→123 Design
   144→124 This study used a pseudo-randomised, crossover, repeated measures design.
   145→125 Participants attended three sessions within the same laboratory which had a controlled
   146→126 ambient environment (temperature, humidity, noise) for all sessions. Laboratory visits were
   147→127 spaced two to seven days apart and scheduled at similar times of day (± one hour). Participants
   148→128 completed the same incremental cycling to exhaustion protocol (see Protocol) across each visit
   149→129 (Figure 1). In each visit, the incremental cycling to exhaustion protocol was completed under one
   150→130 of three conditions: (1) experimental – participants were shown nature scenes within a mounted
   151→131 virtual reality headset (Meta Quest 2: Menlo Park, USA); (2) sham – participants were shown
   152→132 the same nature scenes as within the experimental condition but in two-dimensional form on a
   153→133 screen directly in front of them; and (3) control – no intervention/nature scenes. The nature
   154→134 scenes were 4k footage of Trail of the 10 Falls (https://youtu.be/HvsBCN34dKY), a subtropical
   155→135 feature of rivers and waterfalls. In the experimental condition, the scenes were projected via
   156→136 two diamond Pentile OLED displays (1,440 × 1,600 resolution; 72 Hz refresh rate) and audio
   157→137 input via two headphones. An inbuilt tracking system provides information about the
   158→138 participants head movements, which adjusted the display environment accordingly so that
   159→139 participants could look around the scene(s) but not control their environment. In the sham
   160→140 condition, participants could only view and not interact with the scene. The scene(s) began
   161→141 playing at the start of the participants’ five-minute warm-up. The order of conditions was
   162→142 pseudo-randomised and counterbalanced between visits (see Table S1). There were six
   163→143 possible orders therefore each order was completed by two participants each.

   171→148 Protocol
   172→149 After completion of forms and an explanation of the study and scales, participants
   173→150 were fitted with a Polar H10 (Polar: London, UK) heart rate and mounted an Excalibur 3 (Lode
   174→151 bV: Groningen, The Netherlands) cycle ergometer. The heart rate monitor was synchronised
   175→152 with MetaSoft Studio (Cortex Biophysik GmbH: Leipzig, Germany) software to show a beat-by-
   176→153 beat analysis of heart rate (beats·min-1). The resistance of the ergometer was controlled from
   177→154 this software with a preset protocol.
   178→155 The protocol involved a five-minute constant power (50 Watts) warm-up. Participants were
   179→156 asked to maintain a constant cadence between 80-90 revolutions·min-1 throughout the warm-
   180→157 up and incremental cycling task. Immediately following the warm-up, the ramped incremental
   181→158 cycling task began. The task was programmed in a stepped fashion. There was a 25 Watt
   182→159 stepped increment after each two-minute stage. These steps continued until participants
   183→160 reached exhaustion. The point of exhaustion was demarcated as the point a participant

   187→161 believed they have reached their maximum and stopped cycling or if their cadence dropped
   188→162 below 60 revolutions.min-1 for more than five seconds despite strong verbal encouragement
   189→163 from the researcher. Ratings of naturally occurring muscle pain, perceived effort, and task
   190→164 motivation (see Measures) were rated verbally at the start of the first stage and then the end
   191→165 of each stage including the end of the warm-up and the point of exhaustion if this occurred
   192→166 mid-stage. Average heart rate from a 10 second window (±5 seconds from the end of each
   193→167 stage) was recorded. At task exhaustion, the time to task exhaustion (seconds) was recorded.
   194→168 Measures
   195→169 Naturally occurring muscle pain
   196→170 Naturally occurring muscle pain ratings were recorded using the category-ratio (CR)-
   197→171 100 scale [21]. Pain was defined to the participants as “the degree of hurt you perceive” [22].
   198→172 Naturally occurring muscle pain focused on the degree of hurt perceived in the working
   199→173 musculature (i.e., both legs). Lower and uppermost boundaries were predefined for each pain
   200→174 rating. For instance, the lowermost boundary of 0 ‘nothing at all’ was related to how
   201→175 participants felt during rest without any exercise. The uppermost boundary of 100 ‘maximal’
   202→176 denoted the maximal tolerable naturally occurring muscle pain level that a participant felt they
   203→177 could deal with. In relation to naturally occurring muscle pain, this would refer to the point
   204→178 where the exercise task elicited an intensity of pain that would reach/exceed the pain tolerance.
   205→179 Participants were always instructed to consider their pain ratings on a moment-by-moment
   206→180 basis.
   207→181 Participants were also instructed to alert the researcher when they first detected naturally
   208→182 occurring muscle pain within the legs. This was labelled as the participant ‘pain onset’ (seconds)
   209→183 which denoted the first point at which naturally occurring muscle pain reached a rating of ≥1
   210→184 a.u. on the CR100 scale.

   214→185 Perceived effort
   215→186 The perception of effort was defined as “how hard, heavy, and strenuous the physical
   216→187 task is” [23]. Participants rated their perceived effort according to their experience at the
   217→188 moment the question was asked. The lower and uppermost anchors of the CR100 scale were
   218→189 described according to an incremental cycling task [24]. The lowermost boundary of 0 ‘nothing
   219→190 at all’ related to when the participant was rested sitting in the saddle but not doing anything to
   220→191 turn the pedals, and the context-related uppermost boundary of 100 ‘maximal’ was related to
   221→192 when the participants considered they were cycling as hard as they could. Numbers above 100
   222→193 ‘maximal’ are present on the CR100 scale in case participants considered they had gone
   223→194 beyond what they previously thought as possible.
   224→195 Naturally occurring muscle pain and perceived effort could be rated on the same scale, as far
   225→196 as instructions given to the participant explicitly dissociate both constructs [25,26]. For
   226→197 instance, an individual could complete a very low intensity cycle for a short duration which
   227→198 would cause them to perceive some effort but involve little/no naturally occurring muscle pain
   228→199 due to the low intensity of the exercise [5].
   229→200 Task Motivation
   230→201 Task motivation was rated on a single-item scale according to “please rate how
   231→202 important it is for you to achieve your goal”. Ratings ranged from 0 ‘not at all important’ to 20
   232→203 ‘extremely important’. Prior to beginning the first stepped incremental cycling task,
   233→204 participants were asked to allocate a personal goal. A default goal was set as “to try to remain
   234→205 engaged in the cycling test for as long as possible” unless participants chose to replace this goal
   235→206 with something else.

   392→325 Throughout all visits, participants sat in an adjustable chair facing a monitor positioned
   393→326 approximately 70 cm in front of them at eye-level. Participants sat upright with their back
   394→327 resting on the chair support and forearms resting in a supinated position on the table in front
   395→328 of them with elbows at approximately 90-100° flexion. The handgrip (MLT004/ST,
   396→329 ADInstruments: Auckland, New Zealand) was placed in the dominant hand. For all handgrip
   397→330 contractions, participants were asked to squeeze the handgrip without changing the angle of
   398→331 their wrist, elbow, shoulder, or hips (i.e., leaning into the contraction). No feedback of the force
   399→332 was provided to the participants except during maximal voluntary contractions (MVC) and
   400→333 familiarisations. The handgrip dynamometer was connected to a PowerLab 16/35 system
   401→334 (ADInstruments: Auckland, New Zealand). The force signal was digitised online at a sampling
   402→335 frequency of 2 kHz using a computer and stored for analysis with commercially available
   403→336 software (LabChart8 Pro software, ADInstruments: Auckland, New Zealand).
   404→337 An intermittent structure for the handgrip task was chosen to avoid mechanical compression
   405→338 during a sustained isometric contraction, which inevitably limits the local blood flow even for
   406→339 contractions as low as 20% of MVC peak torque [27]. Such mechanical compression would
   407→340 create excessive ischaemia that would exacerbate naturally occurring muscle pain due to
   408→341 accumulation of metabolites in the muscle [28].
   409→342 Participants performed the handgrip task according to a fixed light (13) or strong (50)
   410→343 perceived effort intensity on the CR100 scale [21]. The start and end of each contraction was
   411→344 signalled to the participants via pre-programmed audio instructions using SuperLab6 software
   412→345 (Cedrus: Santa Barbara, USA). A countdown of 3 – 2 – 1 was shown on the second monitor in
   413→346 front of the participants before the first audio prompt to contract (“GO”) followed three
   414→16
   415→
   416→Draft Version #2 – 24/06/2026, submitted for peer review
   417→347 seconds later by a prompt to relax (“STOP”). During the sham and control condition,
   418→348 participants could see this display, therefore the researcher simply prompted them with
   419→349 “countdown has begun”. During the experimental condition, participants could not see this
   420→350 display, so the researcher prompted the participants by saying the countdown aloud as it was
   421→351 displayed on screen to prime them for the onset of the next repetition. All audio prompts were
   422→352 separated by three seconds until five contractions were performed. After the last “STOP”
   423→353 prompt, there was a 30-second period in which participants rested and provided verbal pain
   424→354 intensity ratings. Participants were instructed to contract and relax in relation to the audio
   425→355 prompts, by reacting as quickly as possible. During each contraction, participants only had the
   426→356 CR100 scale displayed on the second monitor (only visible during the sham and control
   427→357 conditions). No feedback of the force was provided.
   428→358 Procedure
   429→359 Warm Up
   430→360 After completion of forms and an explanation of the study, participants completed a
   431→361 brief warm up for the trial comprising 10 intermittent isometric contractions at what they
   432→362 considered to be 50% of their maximum force. Contractions and rest periods lasted
   433→363 approximately three seconds each and were prompted by the pre-recorded “GO – STOP”
   434→364 audio instructions for the start and end of each contraction. One minute after the warm-up
   435→365 finished, participants were asked to perform a MVC for three seconds. Participants were asked
   436→366 “to squeeze the handgrip as hard as you can for three seconds by going immediately to
   437→367 maximum and holding that as best you can”. Participants performed another MVC one minute
   438→368 after the first. If there was a >±10% difference between MVC peak force (Newtons), a third MVC
   439→369 was completed. The MVC peak force was noted by the researcher for the effort calibrations.

   443→370 Effort Calibrations
   444→371 Participants then completed a pyramidical effort calibration task. Participants were
   445→372 asked to squeeze the handgrip for 10 seconds at an intensity which corresponded to 5 – 15 – 30
   446→373 – 60 – 80 – 60 – 30 – 15 – 5% of their MVC peak force. Participants were shown real-time force
   447→374 data from the handgrip. Participants were instructed the force trace must remain within a
   448→375 green band (±2% of the target force percentage) for the entire 10-second period for the trial
   449→376 to be considered valid and complete. After each 10-second contraction, participants were
   450→377 asked to relax and rate their perceived effort at the end of the contraction (see Measures). This
   451→378 was followed by a 30-second rest and the next intensity of contraction. The pyramidal effort
   452→379 calibration task was completed to allow the participant to squeeze the dynamometer at
   453→380 different force levels, thus experiencing various intensities of effort during handgrip
   454→381 contractions.
   455→382 Familiarisation
   456→383 Participants were then familiarised to the intermittent isometric handgrip task at a fixed
   457→384 light (13) and strong (50) perceived effort. The fixed perceived effort intensity during the
   458→385 familiarisation corresponded to which intensity was to be completed in the main trials for that
   459→386 visit. The familiarisations involved a shortened version of the main experimental condition
   460→387 lasting approximately five minutes. That is participants completed two repetitions of five
   461→388 contractions each for the familiarisation. During the 30-second rest after each repetition,
   462→389 participants verbally rated their naturally occurring muscle pain in their dominant arm.

   464→391 (with a virtual reality headset display).

   469→393 After the familiarisation and a short five-minute rest, participants then completed the

   501→422 Data analysis of force was completed with the LabChart 8 Pro software. Force traces

   743→602 Discussion
   744→603 This two-part study aimed to assess whether nature immersion presented in virtual
   745→604 reality reduces the intensity of naturally occurring muscle pain during motor tasks versus a
   746→605 control or sham condition. We also investigated whether there were any subsequent effects to
   747→606 enhance task outputs (e.g., time to exhaustion, force production) compared to a sham or
   748→607 control condition. Contrary to our a-priori hypotheses, nature immersion through a virtual
   749→608 reality device did not delay naturally occurring muscle pain onset (Study 1), prolong task
   750→609 engagement (Study 1), or increase force production (Study 2) compared to sham or control
   751→610 conditions.
   752→611 During the incremental cycling task, pain, effort, and heart rate increased with task demand.
   753→612 During the fixed perceived effort task, force production was higher and participants reported
   754→613 more muscle pain when exercising at a strong versus light effort. Contraction times did not
   755→614 differ between intensities or conditions. These results confirm that the experimental
   756→615 manipulations were successful, and the task instructions of both studies were understood.
   757→616 Crucially, part of the rationale for this study was to examine if nature immersion in virtual reality
   758→617 could reduce NOMP in the same way studies have shown it can effectively reduce experimental
   759→618 pain (e.g., 15-20). Instead, our results are the first demonstration that pain type likely impacts
   760→619 how effectively a virtual reality intervention can modulate pain intensity. Virtual reality
   761→620 interventions can effectively attenuate experimental pain modalities by engaging descending
   762→621 inhibitory pathways and reducing the central processing of pain (18,19). Consequently, for pain
   763→622 that derives from experimental pressure (e.g., pinprick, cuff algometry), thermal (e.g., cold
   764→623 water immersion), or electrical stimulations, virtual reality interventions are likely to be effective
   765→624 because the subsequent precepts of pain are transient and distinct from the ongoing task
   766→31
   767→

   769→625 [18,19,30,31]. This means that central pain modulation can take greater effect [18] but some
   770→626 studies caveat this to conditions when the threat value is low [32].
   771→627 However, while experimental pain does allow researchers to directly control stimulation and
   772→628 pain intensities, it is not always as representative of the pain experienced in daily life [6].
   773→629 Meanwhile, NOMP is an ever-present feature of daily life for healthy and chronic populations
   774→630 [1]. It is tightly coupled with task demands [5] and the related changes in muscle homeostasis
   775→631 [3,33]. Subsequently, NOMP can deter individuals from engaging and adhering to exercise
   776→632 tasks [6], which are necessary to lead healthy lifestyles, and also impacts how efficiently we
   777→633 move when exercising [34]. Therefore, during a physical task, individuals often remain
   778→634 cognisant of their NOMP as a crucial indicator of task performance [35]. Indeed, NOMP is
   779→635 believed to factor into whether individuals’ decide to up- or down-regulate task outputs
   780→636 [2,9,35,36]. Considering that NOMP is largely driven by peripheral components [1,3] and is
   781→637 closely linked to the ongoing task, it is likely that virtual reality interventions which target the
   782→638 central pain processing mechanisms intensity [20,37] are less effective at modulating NOMP.
   783→639 To our knowledge, our study is the first to examine whether pain type does impact the
   784→640 effectiveness of virtual reality interventions for pain modulation, and we would encourage
   785→641 future studies to re-examine our results.
   786→642 Although the majority of our analysis showed null effects, in Study 1 we did observe lower NOMP
   787→643 at 25% iso-time of the cycling trial during the virtual reality condition. We maintain that virtual
   788→644 reality interventions likely have trivial effects on NOMP, however, if virtual reality does have any
   789→645 effect, it may be limited to when NOMP is at a light-moderate intensity. Indeed, at stronger
   790→646 pain intensities it is unlikely a nature immersion intervention would be salient enough to
   791→647 effectively alter the central processing of nociceptive signals [18,38]. Furthermore, previous
   792→648 studies have illustrated that effective hypoalgesia from nature immersion in virtual reality often
   793→649 occurs when stimulations elicit light-moderate pain [19,20]. As such, we speculate that virtual
   794→32

   797→650 reality interventions are not only pain type-dependent, but also potentially limited to pain
   798→651 intensities that are low-moderate. Future research could consider a meta-analytic review of
   799→652 virtual reality interventions on various pain types and pain intensities as an intriguing way to
   800→653 confirm this.
   801→654 In addition to our pre-registered analysis, we also explored other interactions to discern
   802→655 whether the null effects of virtual reality on NOMP and force production in Study 2 could be
   803→656 explained by other factors. In a series of exploratory regression analyses, we probed if
   804→657 participant sex, environmental presence ratings, and objective fatigue, affected the
   805→658 relationship between muscle pain and force production. Previous studies have showed that
   806→659 females tend to tolerate NOMP better than males [39], yet pain did not differ between sexes in
   807→660 this study and sex did not show a significant effect on the muscle pain-force interaction.
   808→661 Likewise, several studies argue that an individual’s sense of presence within virtual reality
   809→662 impacts the effectiveness of the intervention on pain [14,17], but our analyses showed no effect
   810→663 of presence ratings on the muscle pain-force interaction. Lastly, pain and fatigue operate
   811→664 through the same afferent pathways [28,33] and fatigue may impact pain effects on force
   812→665 production [40]. However, objective measures of fatigue did not impact the pain-force
   813→666 interactions we analysed. Discounting these additional confounds means that we are more
   814→667 confident in summarising that nature immersion in virtual reality has limited impact on NOMP
   815→668 and force production, but future studies ought to confirm this through a-priori study designs.
   816→669 Finally, across our two studies we explored whether the effects of a virtual reality intervention
   817→670 on motor behaviour depend on the motor task type. When completing physical tasks such as
   818→671 an incremental cycling test, participants have limited options to self-regulate their motor
   819→672 behaviour, as the sole requirement of the task is to match a given force/power [41]. While
   820→673 participants can change some aspects of their task performance (e.g., force steadiness,
   821→674 adapted movement patterns) [34,42], overall, participants are dichotomised into either a
   822→33
   823→
   824→Draft Version #2 – 24/06/2026, submitted for peer review
   825→675 ‘continue’ or ‘quit’ decision as performance is dictated by the participant meeting or falling
   826→676 short of the prescribed task demand [43]. In contrast during a motor task which allowed
   827→677 participants to freely regulate (i.e., increase or decrease) their force, as long as they
   828→678 maintained a constant perceived effort intensity, it may be possible to observe more subtle
   829→679 changes to motor behaviour with and without a virtual reality intervention [41]. However,
   830→680 NOMP intensity and time to task exhaustion were similar in most conditions in Study 1, and
   831→681 muscle pain intensity and force production were almost identical across all conditions in Study
   832→682 2. Therefore, it appears that nature immersion via a virtual reality intervention has similar null
   833→683 effects on NOMP and task performance across different types of motor task.
   834→684 Limitations and Future Research
   835→685 Throughout our discussion, we have highlighted that because virtual reality
   836→686 interventions target the descending pain modulation and central processing of pain
   837→687 [14,18,20,37], a virtual reality intervention may be less effective for pain like NOMP which has a
   838→688 strong peripheral component. Likewise, though the virtual reality condition had a high visual
   839→689 quality, the activity within the virtual reality contrasted with the participants’ movements (e.g.,
   840→690 cycling, handgrip). Therefore, this may have created a mismatch between visual input and
   841→691 physical movement, potentially reducing participants' sense of immersion/presence, and thus
   842→692 reducing the effects of virtual reality compared to other studies which have used more
   843→693 compatible virtual reality environments to the task being completed (e.g., [44]).
   844→694 Another consideration is that our studies have strictly assessed perceptual (i.e., pain intensity)
   845→695 and behavioural (i.e., power and force) changes during a virtual reality intervention. To better
   846→696 understand the definitive role of central pain modulation, future studies may benefit from
   847→697 combining perceptual and behavioural measures with neurophysiological assessments like
   848→698 conditioned pain modulation (CPM). Through CPM measures, researchers can judge how
   849→34
   850→
   851→Draft Version #2 – 24/06/2026, submitted for peer review
   852→699 effectively an individual can modulate pain via central mechanisms [45] during virtual reality
   853→700 exposure. Therefore, if virtual reality exerts its effects primarily through these central
   854→701 mechanisms, individuals with stronger CPM responses (i.e., greater endogenous inhibition)
   855→702 would be expected to show more pronounced reductions in pain [46] compared to sham or
   856→703 control conditions, confirming this mechanism.
   857→704 Lastly, we must acknowledge that a-priori calculations determined a required sample size of 17
   858→705 participants for Study 1 to detect meaningful moderate effects. Although 18 participants were
   859→706 recruited and completed the protocols, data from six participants was removed during data
   860→707 processing due to inaccurate measures by a trainee researcher. A sensitivity analysis with 12
   861→708 participants did indicate a critical 𝐹 value of 3.443. Notably, the significant, large effect on pain
   862→709 at 25% iso-time exceeded this critical value (𝐹 = 4.518), however, others which showed non-
   863→710 significant but moderate-large effect sizes like time to pain onset (𝐹 = 1.999) and time to task
   864→711 exhaustion (𝐹 = 1.711) were below this critical value. Therefore, we should acknowledge that
   865→712 Study 1 of this project is likely underpowered to detect small-medium effects, and readers
   866→713 should caution their interpretation of these results. Nevertheless, the combination of both of
   867→714 our related studies helps strengthen the overall body of evidence about the effects of a nature
   868→715 immersion exposure in virtual reality on NOMP and motor performance. Considering the
   869→716 ubiquitous nature of muscle pain and the limiting effect it has on motor task performance; we
   870→717 challenge researchers to continue seeking effective non-invasive and pharmacological
   871→718 interventions that can attenuate pain and improve performance and health (e.g., exercise
   872→719 uptake and adherence) outcomes.

   876→Perspective
   877→720
   878→721 In conclusion, nature immersion through a virtual reality intervention did not reduce
   879→722 NOMP or enhance motor performance across different task types in our studies. These findings
   880→723 suggest that the effectiveness of virtual reality for pain modulation is likely dependent on pain
   881→724 type and pain intensity. Namely, virtual reality interventions may not work for NOMP because
   882→725 of its dominant peripheral component compared to experimental pain types which are distinct
   883→726 from the ongoing task. Moreover, any potential hypoalgesia effects from virtual reality
   884→727 interventions could be restricted to low–moderate pain intensities when the processing of
   885→728 nociceptive signalling can be impacted by the distracting cues provided within virtual reality.
   886→729 Based on our findings, we advocate for future research to examine the interaction between
   887→730 central and peripheral mechanisms, incorporating neurophysiological measures to clarify
   888→731 underlying pathways and identify conditions under which virtual reality interventions may be
   889→732 most effective to reduce pain intensity. Doing so, will inform practitioners whether virtual reality
   890→733 interventions are suitable for of individual’s pain according to its type and intensity.
