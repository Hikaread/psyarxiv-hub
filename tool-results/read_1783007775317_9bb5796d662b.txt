    20→    20→Abstract
    21→    21→Background. Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) is gaining relevance in contemporary
    22→    22→pain research. However, EMA questionnaires are rarely psychometrically evaluated, potentially
    23→    23→compromising data quality. This study aimed to develop and evaluate a German item set
    24→    24→assessing psychological chronic pain processes for use in EMA, focusing on variability and
    25→    25→reliability.
    26→    26→Methods. First, the item set was piloted with three people suffering from chronic pain to gather
    27→    27→feedback on participant burden and the usability of the data. Second, an evaluation was
    28→    28→conducted with a sample of n = 68 individuals with lived experience of chronic pain who
    29→    29→completed an EMA protocol with six assessments per day over twelve days (4,236 datapoints).
    30→    30→Third, revised parts of the item set were preliminarily evaluated in n = 10 people suffering from
    31→    31→chronic pain over a 21-day EMA period with six assessments per day (1,253 datapoints).
    32→    32→Variability was examined primarily using within-person standard deviations and response
    33→    33→distributions. Reliability was assessed according to current recommendations for single-item
    34→    34→measures (test-retest reliability), two-item scales (Spearman-Brown coefficient), and multi-item
    35→    35→scales (McDonald’s Omega).
    36→    36→Results. Most items and process scales met the predefined criteria for variability and reliability.
    37→    37→However, variance was largely attributable to between-person differences. Four out of thirteen
    38→    38→scales demonstrated insufficient reliability.
    39→    39→Conclusions. This study presents the first psychometrically evaluated German EMA item set
    40→    40→capturing a broad range of clinically relevant pain processes. While the findings are promising,
    41→    41→further refinement of several scales and additional psychometric evaluation are warranted.
    42→    42→Keywords: Ecological Momentary Assessment, Chronic Pain, Psychometric Evaluation,
    43→    43→Questionnaire Development

    45→    45→1 | INTRODUCTION
    46→    46→Chronic primary pain is a complex and heterogeneous condition (Breivik et al., 2006; Sundström
    47→    47→et al., 2025), and individuals often show markedly different responses to treatment (Gómez-
    48→    48→Pérez et al., 2020; McCracken & Turk, 2002; Murillo et al., 2022). At the same time, the
    49→    49→generalisation of findings from nomothetic research to individuals has been increasingly
    50→    50→criticised for methodological reasons (Gloster et al., 2024; McCracken, 2023). Consequently,
    51→    51→personalised, process-based approaches to research and treatment are being advocated
    52→    52→(McCracken, 2023; Scholten et al., 2025; Wright & Woods, 2020), drawing on innovative
    53→    53→research methods such as Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) (May et al., 2018), a
    54→    54→Network Approach to conceptualize and study mental disorders (Åkerblom et al., 2021;
    55→    55→Borsboom, 2017; Schemer et al., 2023; Scholten et al., 2022), and Single Case Experimental
    56→    56→Design (SCED) (Jones & Hurrell, 2019; Vlaeyen et al., 2001).
    57→    57→EMA assesses longitudinal data and enables the examination of dynamic symptom fluctuations
    58→    58→essential for approaches such as individual-level network analysis (Bringmann, 2021). It has
    59→    59→become increasingly feasible with the widespread use of digital devices and can be
    60→    60→implemented in everyday life, thereby enhancing ecological validity and reducing recall bias
    61→    61→(May et al., 2018). A systematic review concluded that momentary pain measures are feasible
    62→    62→and valid and might become the new gold-standard of outcome assessment (Overton et al.,
    63→    63→2023).
    64→    64→Despite existing recommendations for the development of EMA instruments (Eisele et al., 2021;
    65→    65→Fritz et al., 2024), formal evaluation is rare, even though establishing the psychometric
    66→    66→properties of EMA items is a prerequisite for any subsequent analyses (Palmier-Claus et al.,
    67→    67→2011). Simply adapting the instructions to retrospective questionnaires – originally designed to
    68→    68→capture between-person differences – is insufficient for individual-level, longitudinal research
    69→    69→and may compromise the validity of the resulting data (Brose et al., 2020).
    70→    70→Beyond validity, reliability represents a key psychometric property and refers to the proportion of
    71→    71→observed variance attributable to true score variance rather than measurement error (Eisele et
    72→    72→al., 2021). Adequate reliability is a prerequisite for analyses based on EMA data (Bringmann,
    73→    73→
    74→    74→2021). However, no gold-standard procedures for estimating reliability in EMA currently exist
    75→    75→(Eisele et al., 2021). Moreover, commonly used reliability estimators assume multi-item scales,
    76→    76→whereas EMA research frequently relies on single-item measures (SIM) or very short scales to
    77→    77→minimise participant burden. These brief measures are particularly susceptible to measurement
    78→    78→error (Dejonckheere et al., 2022), underscoring the need for systematic psychometric evaluation
    79→    79→of newly developed EMA items and scales. Furthermore, EMA items need to be sufficiently
    80→    80→sensitive to capture meaningful temporal fluctuations (Forkmann et al., 2018). Accordingly, item
    81→    81→variability constitutes another key psychometric property of EMA measures.
    82→    82→A positive example of a thoroughly evaluated EMA questionnaire in the context of pain is the
    83→    83→Momentary Pain Catastrophizing Scale (Frumkin et al., 2023). However, no evaluated EMA
    84→    84→items are available in German that capture a broader range of pain processes. The aim of the
    85→    85→present study was therefore to develop and evaluate a German-language item set for the
    86→    86→longitudinal assessment of clinically relevant pain processes.
    87→    87→2 | METHODS
    88→    88→The new EMA instrument was named the “Prozesse Chronischer Schmerzen ErfaSSen”

    91→    91→10.17605/OSF.IO/9TUQR) and has been approved by the university’s local ethics committee
    92→    92→(#LEK-501).

   222→   222→2.2 | Pilot EMA: Pre-testing of items
   223→   223→As recommended (Eisele et al., 2021; Forkmann et al., 2018), the item set was piloted with
   224→   224→people suffering from chronic pain to evaluate the burden associated with completing the
   225→   225→assessment and to examine the comprehensibility of the item (see Figure 1, Step 5).
   226→   226→Participants were recruited from the university outpatient clinic based on the primary diagnosis
   227→   227→F45.4x (ICD-10; (Bundesinstitut für Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) im Auftrag des
   228→   228→Bundesministeriums für Gesundheit (BMG) unter Beteiligung der Arbeitsgruppe ICD des
   229→   229→Kuratoriums für Fragen der Klassifikation im Gesundheitswesen (KKG), 2020)). Exclusion
   230→   230→criteria included insufficient German language proficiency (operationalised as a score of ≥ 4 on
   231→   231→a five-point scale, with higher scores indicating lower proficiency), and acute cases of
   232→   232→psychosis, substance abuse, or suicidality.
   233→   233→Three women suffering from chronic pain participated in a 12-day EMA phase comprising six
   234→   234→assessments per day. The assessment was carried out using m-path (Mestdagh et al., 2023),
   235→   235→an app that can be installed on the participants’ smartphones. M-path required smartphones to
   236→   236→use either Android 5.1 or iOS 11.0 as their operating system. Participants received written study
   237→   237→information and gave their informed consent prior to enrolment and app installation.
   238→   238→Prior to the EMA phase, all participants attended an individual onboarding session during which
   239→   239→the complete item set (excluding distractor items) was reviewed. Participants were first asked to
   240→   240→paraphrase each item in their own words, and their responses were documented concurrently
   241→   241→by the interviewer (VEH). If a participant’s phrasing did not correspond to the definition of the
   242→   242→intended construct, the respective construct definition was provided to ensure a shared
   243→   243→understanding of each item. Each onboarding session lasted approximately one hour.
   244→   244→
   245→   245→The sampling scheme of the pilot EMA is summarised in Table 2. Items of the same construct
   246→   246→were kept together, as recommended to reduce assessment burden (Eisele et al., 2021).

   248→   248→in which they were provided with a personalised summary of their data (descriptively analysed
   249→   249→using Microsoft Excel (Excel, 2025)) and asked about their experiences during the EMA period.

   251→   251→to identify implications for participant burden. Each post interview lasted approximately one
   252→   252→hour.

   289→   289→2.3 | Main Evaluation: Testing of variability and reliability
   290→   290→Focusing on the reliability and variability as essential psychometric criteria, an EMA study with
   291→   291→participants suffering from chronic pain was implemented (preregistration:
   292→   292→https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/S4QGP).
   293→   293→In general, the study followed procedures described by Scholten and colleagues (2024)
   294→   294→regarding the evaluation of item variability, and Dejonckheere and colleagues (2022) and
   295→   295→Forkmann and colleagues (2018) for the assessment of item reliability, as will be described
   296→   296→below.
   297→   297→2.3.1 | Sample of the main evaluation
   298→   298→The objective of the recruitment process was to generate a sample of 70 valid data sets, based
   299→   299→on sample sizes of other EMA evaluation studies. As the methodological design of the current
   300→   300→evaluation study is largely based on Dejonckheere et al. (2022), the target sample size of N =
   301→   301→70 was also based on their study. It is evident that other EMA studies which employed a
   302→   302→comparable methodological design also utilized smaller sample sizes (see Ben-Zeev et al.,
   303→   303→2009 [N = 51]; Chepenik et al., 2006 [N = 57]). A systematic review of the use of EMA in chronic
   304→   304→pain research reports an average sample size of N = 62.7 (May et al., 2018).
   305→   305→The recruitment of the total sample took place in two waves (July-September 2024 and
   306→   306→January-February 2025). Recruitment was conducted on a nationwide basis in Germany, mainly
   307→   307→via online self-help groups and flyers distributed in local healthcare centres.
   308→   308→2.3.2 | Procedure for the main evaluation
   309→   309→Participants were screened for in- and exclusion criteria using an online questionnaire on SoSci
   310→   310→Survey (Leiner, 2024). Participants received written study information and provided informed
   311→   311→consent prior to data assessment and app installation within the online survey. The inclusion
   312→   312→criteria were: (1) being of age (≥ 18 years), (2) sufficient knowledge of German (native speaker
   313→   313→or self-assessment of at least 3 (moderate) on a 5-point scale (1 = very good to 5 = very poor),
   314→   314→(3) self-reported pain symptoms lasting at least six months, (4) a self-reported pain focus
   315→   315→outside the head or neck area or migraine, (5) an average pain intensity of at least 1 on an 11-
   316→   316→point scale (0 = no pain to 10 = maximally conceivable pain) in the last 14 days, and (6)
   317→   317→disability in at least one of the seven areas of life according to the Pain Disability Index (PDI;
   318→   318→
   319→   319→Dillmann et al., 2011). The PDI items were recoded dichotomously (1 = Yes, 2 = No), since no
   320→   320→cut-off to discern pathological from non-pathological disability is defined. Consequently,
   321→   321→participants whose pain focus was in the head/neck area or who reported no pain or pain-
   322→   322→related disability during the past 14 days were excluded from the study. Further exclusion
   323→   323→criteria such as acute suicidality, substance abuse, psychosis, or other circumstances
   324→   324→endangering the participant were screened in an onboarding session (see below) based on a
   325→   325→clinical screening instrument (Margraf & Cwik, 2020). We recognise that people seeking
   326→   326→treatment for chronic pain in tertiary pain clinics display heavier pain intensity scores and
   327→   327→disability ratings (Nicholas et al., 2019). The broad inclusion criteria intended to account for the
   328→   328→heterogeneity of chronic pain while ensuring that participants regularly experienced pain
   329→   329→symptoms they could report in an EMA study.
   330→   330→Prior to the EMA phase, an onboarding session was conducted via Arztkonsultation.de
   331→   331→(https://arztkonsultation.de), a digital platform specifically designed for video consultations with
   332→   332→medical practitioners and psychotherapists, in full compliance with the provisions of the General
   333→   333→Data Protection Regulation. During the onboarding process, participants could ask questions
   334→   334→and provided their informed consent again verbally prior to installing the m-path app on their
   335→   335→smartphone.
   336→   336→The recruitment was implemented in two assessment waves (see Sample). In the first wave,
   337→   337→participants provided sociodemographic information in the online screening and completed a
   338→   338→short questionnaire demonstration in m-path during the onboarding. In the second wave,
   339→   339→participants were made familiar with m-path by assessing their sociodemographic information in
   340→   340→m-path during the onboarding session. Each onboarding session took 15-20 minutes.
   341→   341→Following the onboarding session, participants completed the 12-day EMA period. Interested
   342→   342→participants received a summary of the study’s overall results via email. All participants received
   343→   343→an incentive of €30 if they completed 80 % of the questionnaire prompts.
   344→   344→2.3.3 | Sampling scheme of the main evaluation
   345→   345→The sampling scheme and item set order correspond mostly to the pilot study and is
   346→   346→summarised in Table 2: The EMA was 12 days long and included six semi-randomised prompts
   347→   347→
   348→   348→per day. The assessment was implemented using m-path (Mestdagh et al., 2023). Of the 30
   349→   349→process items, six were single-item measures (SIMs). One SIM was repeated at the end of each
   350→   350→questionnaire prompt, following protocol 1 described by Dejonckheere and colleagues (2022).
   351→   351→2.3.4 | Compliance management
   352→   352→Sufficient statistical significance can only be achieved if the number of observations is sufficient
   353→   353→for the planned analyses (Dejonckheere et al., 2022; Fritz et al., 2024). The total number of
   354→   354→observations is determined by two key factors: the sample size and the number of data points
   355→   355→generated per person. The latter is significantly influenced by the response rate.
   356→   356→Data collection in real-life situations almost inevitably leads to missed surveys (Fritz et al.,
   357→   357→2024). In order to generate valid data that reflects the temporal dynamics of the constructs
   358→   358→under investigation as realistically as possible, minimum compliance requirements can be
   359→   359→established (Trull & Ebner-Priemer, 2020). However, a gold-standard for setting such a
   360→   360→threshold in EMA studies does not yet exist. Some studies aim for a target compliance value at
   361→   361→the group level, while others set individual minimum rates for inclusion (Williams et al., 2021).
   362→   362→For group-level analyses, a target value of 80% is generally accepted (Forkmann et al., 2018).
   363→   363→Consequently, this value is adopted as the target value at the group level and was accordingly
   364→   364→incentivised.
   365→   365→On the individual level, the literature contains varied criteria, some excluding participants with a
   366→   366→response rate below 50% (e.g. Dejonckheere et al., 2019; Heininga et al., 2019; Van Der Gucht
   367→   367→et al., 2019), while others set significantly higher thresholds (e.g. 80% [Schiepek et al., 2019]).
   368→   368→In this study, an individual minimum rate of 60% is defined as an inclusion criterion for data
   369→   369→analysis. The establishment of minimum compliance requirements at both the group and
   370→   370→individual levels has been demonstrated to be a useful method of generating valid data that
   371→   371→reflects the temporal dynamics of the constructs under investigation as realistically as possible
   372→   372→(Williams et al., 2021).
   373→   373→In order to achieve this objective, several strategies were implemented with the aim of
   374→   374→increasing compliance. First, participants were informed in detail about the study design during
   375→   375→the personal onboarding interview. Secondly, approximately halfway through the 12-day survey
   376→   376→
   377→   377→period, the participants received an email containing an interim report on their response rate to
   378→   378→date. In instances where further clarification was required on technical matters, additional
   379→   379→contact was initiated via email. This approach was also employed to provide motivational
   380→   380→support, fostering a sense of engagement and empowerment among the participants. Thirdly,
   381→   381→upon attaining a compliance rate of at least 80 %, the participants received a payment of €30.
   382→   382→Participating students could choose to receive the financial incentive or course credit if they
   383→   383→completed at least 80 % of the questionnaires.
   384→   384→2.3.5 | Analysis
   385→   385→All statistical analyses were performed using the software R, version 4.5.1 (R Core Team,
   386→   386→2025). For data management, descriptive and inferential statistical analyses, functions from the
   387→   387→packages apaTables (Stanley, 2021), ggrepel (Slowikowski et al., 2024), lavaan (Rosseel,
   388→   388→2012), lme4 (Lme4 Package - RDocumentation, n.d.), mpathr (Mestdagh et al., 2023), openxlsx
   389→   389→(Schauberger et al., 2025), psych (Revelle, 2025), semTools (semTools R Package
   390→   390→[Documentation], n.d.), and tidyverse (Wickham et al., 2019) were used.
   391→   391→M-path directly gives the compliance rate per person in the app and does not export it as a
   392→   392→variable in the dataset, which is why the compliance rates reported in the Results section have
   393→   393→been averaged manually based on the compliance rates noted from m-path.
   394→   394→Variability
   395→   395→Currently, there is no standardised procedure for examining the variability of EMA items. In
   396→   396→studies in the field of time-varying network analysis, standard deviation has been used as a
   397→   397→pragmatic measure for assessing item variability (Scholten et al., 2022, 2024). Scholten, Rubel
   398→   398→et al. (2024) excluded items with a standard deviation < 0.2 (after min-max scaling) from the
   399→   399→data analysis due to insufficient variance. Following this approach, this study also defined a
   400→   400→criterion for assessing item variability.
   401→   401→In contrast to the approach of Scholten, Rubel et al. (2024), min-max scaling is not necessary in
   402→   402→this context because all items are assessed on an 11-point scale and are therefore directly
   403→   403→comparable. Instead, a within standard deviation of ≥1.0 is defined as an indicator of sufficient
   404→   404→item variability. This criterion represents a deviation from the original pre-registration
   405→   405→
   406→   406→(https://osf.io/s4rmn), in which a standard deviation greater than 0.2 was specified. The
   407→   407→deviation is due to not using scale normalisation, which makes the selected criterion more
   408→   408→stringent.
   409→   409→Additionally, an extra criterion is defined to evaluate the variability of the items, that is that no
   410→   410→single response option may comprise more than 80% of all responses across the sample
   411→   411→(Scholten et al., 2022).
   412→   412→Variability calculations
   413→   413→The standard deviation was calculated within each individual across their individual
   414→   414→measurement times. The between-person variance was analysed by calculating random
   415→   415→intercepts of an intercept-only model.
   416→   416→Additional to the preregistered analyses and following common recommendations, Intraclass
   417→   417→Correlation Coefficients (ICCs) will be reported for each item (Eisele et al., 2021; Overton et al.,
   418→   418→2023). The estimation of ICCs was achieved through the implementation of an intercept-only
   419→   419→multilevel model, which facilitated an examination of the variance structure across and within
   420→   420→individuals. ICCs quantify the proportion of the total variance of a variable that can be attributed
   421→   421→to differences between individuals and to fluctuations within an individual (Bolger & Laurenceau,
   422→   422→2013). In EMA, ICCs ranging between 0.2 - 0.4 are usual (Eisele et al., 2021), i.e. 20-40 % are
   423→   423→usually attributable to stable differences between individuals.
   424→   424→Also additional to the preregistration and following the example of other EMA evaluation studies
   425→   425→(Forkmann et al., 2018; Frumkin et al., 2023), Root Mean Squared Successive Differences
   426→   426→(RMSSDs) will be calculated on the individual level. Mean Squared Successive Differences
   427→   427→indicate the variability from timepoint to timepoint in repeated measures (Jahng et al., 2008),
   428→   428→and the RMSSD is easier to interpret since it has the same dimension as the originally used
   429→   429→scale.
   430→   430→Reliability
   431→   431→The reliability of the items is analysed using different methods, depending on the number of
   432→   432→items per construct (i.e. test-retest reliability (Dejonckheere et al., 2022), Spearman Brown
   433→   433→formula (Eisinga et al., 2013), McDonald’s Omega (ω) (Forkmann et al., 2018)). Further details
   434→   434→
   435→   435→are described in the Data Analysis section. Regardless of the method used, reliability was
   436→   436→considered sufficient if the respective reliability estimator had a value > .70 (Forkmann et al.,
   437→   437→2018; Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994).
   438→   438→Reliability calculation
   439→   439→Single-item measures (SIMs). SIMs are analysed applying a test-retest-logic based on
   440→   440→protocol 1 by Dejonckheere and colleagues (2022). In this method, the SIM is presented at two
   441→   441→timepoints within the same measurement. This is then set in relation to the total variance, which
   442→   442→is constituted by both measurements. The fundamental premise underlying this investigation is
   443→   443→that the observed discrepancy between the two measurements is exclusively attributable to
   444→   444→measurement errors, as the underlying construct has remained relatively stable over the brief
   445→   445→interval (Dejonckheere et al., 2022). This results in the following formula:
   446→   446→𝐸 (∆2)

   451→   451→𝑇𝑅𝑇

   453→   453→𝜎2 = total variance of test and retest responses,
   454→   454→𝑡𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙
   455→   455→𝜎2 = measurement error variance of test and retest responses
   456→   456→𝜀
   457→   457→In the present study, at least 22 items separated the first and repeated presentation of the SIM,
   458→   458→reducing recall effects. On average, each SIM was repeated once per day.
   459→   459→Test-retest reliability for SIMs was calculated across the sample, accordingly reflecting a
   460→   460→between-person reliability estimate.
   461→   461→Two-item measures. The estimation of reliability for scales comprising two items is calculated
   462→   462→using the Spearman-Brown formula (Eisinga et al., 2013). The formula assumes that both items
   463→   463→measure the same latent construct. It corrects for the tendency to underestimate reliability in
   464→   464→short scales (e.g., two-item constructs). This approach is also used by Forkmann et al. (2018) in
   465→   465→the evaluation of EMA items. The formula is:

   477→   477→and provides a between-person reliability estimate.
   478→   478→Multiple-item measures. As recommended (Brose et al., 2020), McDonald’s ω is calculated for
   479→   479→constructs measured with at least three items. It was calculated separately on the within and
   480→   480→between level, based on a one-factorial estimate (group-mean-centred within, person-means
   481→   481→between). A multilevel confirmatory factor analysis to extract the reliability parameters was not
   482→   482→feasible due to the limited number of items per factor.
   483→   483→2.4 | Preliminary evaluation of revised items: Testing variability and reliability
   484→   484→After the EMA evaluation, the ProCEss item set was reduced to 25 items following the results of
   485→   485→the validation procedure. Five items underwent revisions regarding their formulations, three
   486→   486→items were summarised to one item, sleep disturbances were omitted from the questionnaire
   487→   487→completely due to their different time-scale (being only assessed once per day), and one SIM
   488→   488→for negative affect was added to the item set. Therefore, the final ProCEss item set version was
   489→   489→preliminarily evaluated again, repeating the analyses of the main evaluation in a new dataset.
   490→   490→The data for this preliminary evaluation were drawn from the POINT Pain project, in which
   491→   491→personalised, network-based CBT is compared with a standardised CBT within a single-case
   492→   492→experimental design (for more details, see preregistration:
   493→   493→https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06784141). Participants are adults with chronic pain (primary
   494→   494→diagnosis: F45.4x (Bundesinstitut für Arzneimittel und Medizinprodukte (BfArM) im Auftrag des
   495→   495→Bundesministeriums für Gesundheit (BMG) unter Beteiligung der Arbeitsgruppe ICD des
   496→   496→Kuratoriums für Fragen der Klassifikation im Gesundheitswesen (KKG), 2020)) who complete
   497→   497→two EMA phases during their study participation: one prior to commencing personalised CBT
   498→   498→and one after completing the full course of therapy. For the present evaluation, only EMA data
   499→   499→collected before the personalised CBT phase were analysed, as pain experiences are expected
   500→   500→to change during treatment, potentially altering the psychometric properties of the items at the
   501→   501→
   502→   502→individual level in the post-treatment EMA. As the POINT Pain project is still ongoing, the
   503→   503→available dataset for the ProCEss evaluation was limited to N = 10 participants. It is accordingly
   504→   504→considered preliminary.

   512→   512→2.5 | Exploratory analyses
   513→   513→In order to explore the ideal timescale with which the ProCEss item set should be applied,
   514→   514→descriptive item characteristics as well as the variability indicators regarding within standard
   515→   515→deviation, random intercept, and ICCs were calculated again in three subsets: The first subset
   516→   516→included a random selection of three datapoints per day per person, the second included every

   519→   519→explore response types (e.g. generally low versus generally high variance).

   527→   527→erwachsenenalters/research/forschungsprojekt-process). Contributions from researchers
   528→   528→conducting translations or further psychometric evaluations are explicitly welcomed to foster
   529→   529→cumulative scientific progress.

   814→   814→3.4 | Explorative results
   815→   815→Three subsets including a random selection of three datapoints per day per person (2,430
   816→   816→datapoints), every second datapoint (2,136 datapoints), and one random datapoint per day per
   817→   817→person (819 datapoints) were exploratively analysed to investigate the relevance of assessment
   818→   818→timescales for the psychometric properties of the item set. The analyses revealed negligible
   819→   819→differences on all indicators. This means that means, within standard deviations, random
   820→   820→intercepts, and ICCs did not meaningfully differ from the original results. Therefore, all
   821→   821→timescales (i. e. six, three, or one assessment per day) seem to lead to similar results, and none
   822→   822→of these timescales can clearly be prioritised over the others. The results for the three subsets
   823→   823→are provided in the supplemental materials (Tables G1-G3).
   824→   824→In order to explore intraindividual variability patterns, the standard deviation was calculated per
   825→   825→participant. A visual inspection of the results implied that there are some participants who
   826→   826→display medium to high variability on most of the ProCEss item set, while others display very low
   827→   827→standard deviations on most items. Most participants, however, displayed standard deviations
   828→   828→≥ 1 across the item set. The intraindividual standard deviations are provided in the supplemental
   829→   829→materials (H1-H4).
   830→   830→4 | DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
   831→   831→The present study evaluated the ProCEss item set with regard to variability and reliability. A
   832→   832→pilot study indicated good feasibility, participant acceptance, and manageable burden. The main
   833→   833→evaluation indicated that most items showed adequate within-person variability, supporting the
   834→   834→sensitivity of the ProCEss item set to reflect temporal dynamics. However, a substantial
   835→   835→proportion of variance was attributable to between-person differences, with most ICCs
   836→   836→exceeding the typical EMA range. Reliability was good to acceptable for most ProCEss items. In
   837→   837→
   838→   838→particular, the SIMs demonstrated satisfactory performance. For multi-item measures, between-
   839→   839→person reliability was consistently higher than within-person reliability. These findings suggest
   840→   840→that, although the measure performs adequately, certain items and scales may require further
   841→   841→refinement to fully capture the intended psychological processes.
   842→   842→4.1 | Discussion of variability
   843→   843→A substantial proportion of variance was attributable to between-person differences.
   844→   844→Methodological factors may partly explain this pattern, including the semi-randomised sampling
   845→   845→scheme violating RMSSD assumptions (Jahng et al., 2008), and the cEMA model increasing
   846→   846→susceptibility to recall bias (Eisele et al., 2021; Stone et al., 2023). Similar findings have been
   847→   847→reported for the momentary Pain Catastrophising Scale (Frumkin et al., 2023). As observed in
   848→   848→the pilot study, some participants reported boredom during participation, which may contribute
   849→   849→to reduced within-person variability through careless responding that has been found to promote
   850→   850→neutral responses (Wang et al., 2024). However, response start points were randomised to
   851→   851→minimise such effects. Alternatively, lower variability may reflect psychopathology-related
   852→   852→factors in chronic pain, including potentially more rigid behavioural patterns. While psychological
   853→   853→factors are not inherently pathological in chronic pain (Eccleston, 2001), reduced flexibility as
   854→   854→observed in chronic pain patients (Krypotos et al., 2022) may limit temporal fluctuations in EMA
   855→   855→data. Blanchard and colleagues (2023) discussed a similar phenomenon in burnout syndrome,
   856→   856→where they reflected that greater psychopathology might lead to less fluctuations.
   857→   857→Determining optimal sampling frequency remains challenging, as timescales may vary across
   858→   858→processes and individuals (Frumkin et al., 2019; Wright & Woods, 2020). In the present data,
   859→   859→exploratory artificial reduction of sampling frequency revealed negligible effects on variability,
   860→   860→suggesting that sampling frequency did not explain the observed ICC patterns.
   861→   861→The findings highlight the need to further examine the sensitivity to change of the ProCEss item
   862→   862→set. Some items, such as “I was sure I could lead my life satisfactorily even though I was in
   863→   863→pain”, may reflect more stable attitudes rather than momentary states. The temporal dynamics
   864→   864→of the items should therefore be investigated both theoretically and empirically.
   865→   865→
   866→   866→4.2 | Discussion of reliability
   867→   867→Overall, most processes showed good to acceptable reliability. Between-person reliability for
   868→   868→multi-item measures was consistently higher than within-person reliability, likely due to limited
   869→   869→within-person variability and the small number of items typical of EMA scales (Nezlek, 2017).
   870→   870→Thought Suppression showed a marked decrease in between-person reliability after revision.
   871→   871→This may reflect the combination of two literal thought items (“Pull yourself together/Don’t make
   872→   872→such a fuss”) with a more abstract cognitive process. The low reliability may also indicate a
   873→   873→validity issue, as the item “I tried not to think about my pain” was adapted from the Avoidance-
   874→   874→Endurance-Questionnaire (Hasenbring et al., 2009), where it is associated with task
   875→   875→persistence. Future research should examine convergent and discriminant validity and consider
   876→   876→potential restructuring of these scales.
   877→   877→Self-Efficacy and Self-Compassion require further investigation. Items within these scales may
   878→   878→be heterogeneous, as they were intended to capture different facets of the respective
   879→   879→constructs. Both were assessed using two-item scales, for which a more liberal reliability
   880→   880→threshold of .60 has been proposed (Yang et al., 2022), yet neither scale reached this criterion.
   881→   881→Future research should examine item loadings using multilevel confirmatory factor analysis as
   882→   882→another psychometric criterion as well as to establish the expected within- and between-person
   883→   883→reliability (Yang et al., 2022).
   884→   884→Psychometric properties may change over time (Fritz et al., 2024), highlighting the need to
   885→   885→assess additional properties such as measurement invariance. Given the heterogeneous nature
   886→   886→of chronic pain (Breivik et al., 2006; Sundström et al., 2025) and the fact that the processes
   887→   887→were derived from multiple psychological models, not all items may be relevant for every
   888→   888→participant. Even in individual-level research, standardised instruments are required to enable
   889→   889→data aggregation and meta-analytic synthesis (Morley et al., 2015). If the ProCEss item set
   890→   890→were further personalised, with individually relevant items administered modularly, psychometric
   891→   891→properties may differ. Accordingly, assessing reliability of SIMs in each application may be
   892→   892→advisable, as reliability is sample-dependent and may vary across individuals and time (Brose et
   893→   893→al., 2020; Stone et al., 2023).
   894→   894→
   895→   895→4.3 | Recommendations for users of the ProCEss item set
   896→   896→We do not recommend calculating scale sum scores, as this would result in a loss of relevant
   897→   897→information (Pena Loray et al., 2025). In line with this, the processes included in the ProCEss
   898→   898→item set may not yet be fully captured by the current items and should not be considered
   899→   899→complete representations of latent constructs.
   900→   900→Sleep problems are common for chronic pain syndromes and should be considered in
   901→   901→assessment and treatment (Mathias et al., 2018). The original ProCEss item set included sleep-
   902→   902→related items, which were removed in the current version to reduce burden and due to their
   903→   903→differing time scale, which may complicate analyses. However, in the main evaluation they
   904→   904→showed good between-person and sufficient within-person reliability using a more liberal
   905→   905→criterion (> .60; Nezlek, 2017; Yang et al., 2022). We therefore encourage their use where
   906→   906→appropriate, despite their exclusion from the core item set.
   907→   907→4.4 | Strengths and Limitations
   908→   908→The ProCEss item is the first German EMA instrument to assess a broad range of clinically
   909→   909→relevant pain processes. Its development followed current best-practice recommendations
   910→   910→(Eisele et al., 2021; Fritz et al., 2024) and involved individuals with chronic pain across multiple
   911→   911→stages, from initial feedback to pilot testing and evaluation. Content validity of the item set has
   912→   912→been established (see preprint: https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/ruhdf_v1), and the main
   913→   913→evaluation included a reasonably large and diverse sample with compliance rates consistent
   914→   914→with those reported in EMA research (May et al., 2018; Ono et al., 2019). The preliminary
   915→   915→revision study showed high compliance and included patients with confirmed chronic pain
   916→   916→diagnoses. The semi-randomised sampling scheme may have reduced response bias (Stone et
   917→   917→al., 2023).
   918→   918→Overall, the findings are promising, although several issues indicate the need for further
   919→   919→refinement and evaluation of the ProCEss item set. First, especially the revised items should be
   920→   920→evaluated in a larger sample, as they were only examined in a small cohort of n = 10
   921→   921→participants. Given that sample size is generally more critical for statistical inference than the
   922→   922→number of measurement occasions (Oleson et al., 2022), the extended EMA period in the
   923→   923→
   924→   924→revised study cannot sufficiently compensate for the limited sample size. At the same time,
   925→   925→findings from psychometric evaluations are sample-dependent (Brose et al., 2020; Stone et al.,
   926→   926→2023), limiting their relevance for individual-level applications.
   927→   927→Second, the limited within-person variance requires further investigation, particularly with
   928→   928→respect to timescales and alternative sampling strategies, including fixed or momentary EMA
   929→   929→designs. A fixed sampling scheme may also reduce participant burden, as suggested by the
   930→   930→pilot phase.
   931→   931→Third, reliability could not yet be established for the Negative Affect SIM. As the target sample
   932→   932→size in the main evaluation was slightly underachieved and chronic pain status was based solely
   933→   933→on self-report, re-evaluation in a clinical sample is warranted.
   934→   934→Multilevel analyses did not converge in the main and preliminary evaluations of the ProCEss
   935→   935→item set. This is a notable limitation, as multilevel modelling is considered gold standard for
   936→   936→EMA data (Castro-Alvarez et al., 2025; Nezlek, 2017). Future research should aim to enable
   937→   937→more robust multilevel analyses of the item set.
   938→   938→4.5 | Directions for future research
   939→   939→Future research should further refine, extend, and evaluate the ProCEss item set. Future work
   940→   940→may consider estimating variability and reliability at the individual level, as has been
   941→   941→recommended due to the sample-dependency of psychometric estimators (Brose et al., 2020).
   942→   942→Similarly, if items are to be administered independently rather than as scales, future research
   943→   943→should examine the reliability of all ProCEss items at the single-item level, effectively treating
   944→   944→each as a standalone SIM.
   945→   945→Some psychometric properties remain to be examined, including sensitivity to change,
   946→   946→measurement invariance, and the item set’s capacity to capture processes dynamically over
   947→   947→time. Several processes, such as thought suppression, acceptance, or catastrophising also
   948→   948→include evaluative or regulatory components that may not be sufficiently salient in real-time
   949→   949→assessment. Although the ProCEss item set was administered using a cEMA approach,
   950→   950→whereby participants reflected on a recent time window rather than the exact moment, the
   951→   951→process labels may require refinement to better reflect their momentary operationalisation (e.g.
   952→   952→
   953→   953→“pain-related worry” instead of “catastrophising”). This raises questions regarding construct
   954→   954→validity and highlights the need for further refinement and extension of the item set.
   955→   955→4.6 | Conclusion
   956→   956→The ProCEss item set is to the best of our knowledge the only psychometrically evaluated
   957→   957→German EMA instrument assessing a broad range of chronic pain processes. Most scales
   958→   958→showed adequate reliability and variability, although a substantial proportion of variance was
   959→   959→attributable to between-person differences. Exploratory findings did not indicate a clearly
   960→   960→preferable sampling timescale. Future research should examine subgroups with differing
   961→   961→variability patterns (e.g. based on treatment experience) and further psychometric properties,
   962→   962→including sensitivity to change and measurement invariance. Individual-level psychometric
   963→   963→evaluation and a more modular structure allowing selection of personally relevant items may
   964→   964→further support individual-level applications. Overall, the ProCEss item set represents a
   965→   965→promising tool for EMA-based assessment of chronic pain processes.
