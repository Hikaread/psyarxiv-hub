    16→ABSTRACT
    17→Individuals with schizophrenia-spectrum disorders commonly exhibit debilitating negative
    18→symptoms, including avolition and anhedonia, which represent a significant barrier to
    19→engagement in goal-directed behaviors, such as physical exercise. Although exercise can
    20→significantly alleviate these symptoms, a clinical paradox arises as the symptoms themselves
    21→impede regular physical activity. This narrative review addresses this paradox by proposing
    22→an integrated neurocognitive framework centered on the proximal exercise experience,
    23→specifically exercise-related pleasure and perceived exertion. We argue that negative
    24→symptoms alter the cost-benefit analysis of exercise due to core deficits in anticipatory
    25→pleasure, effort valuation, and reinforcement learning. We propose that structured exercise
    26→acts as a corrective learning context capable of updating maladaptive internal models through
    27→prediction errors, the discrepancies between anticipated and actual experiences of effort and
    28→pleasure. We also highlight the importance of learning from both positive prediction errors
    29→(discovering exercise is less effortful or more pleasurable than expected) and carefully
    30→supported negative prediction errors, which can re-associate physical effort with mastery,
    31→competence, and control rather than threat. By focusing on these experiential and
    32→computational mechanisms, this framework provides a theoretical foundation for targeted
    33→clinical interventions designed to leverage real-time affect and post-session reflection to break
    34→the self-reinforcing cycle of inactivity and promote sustained symptom improvement.
    35→Keywords
    36→Psychosis, physical exercise, motivation, effort, pleasure, hedonic capacities

    39→1. Introduction
    40→Schizophrenia is a complex and chronic mental disorder characterized by positive, negative,
    41→disorganized, and cognitive symptoms (Owen et al., 2016). Among these dimensions,
    42→negative symptoms are particularly disabling because they affect motivation, pleasure,
    43→emotional expression, social engagement, and goal-directed behavior (Chang et al., 2020;
    44→Fulford et al., 2018; Giuliani et al., 2021; Glenthøj et al., 2020). Experiential negative
    45→symptoms, such as avolition, anhedonia, and asociality, are especially relevant for daily
    46→functioning because they directly impair the capacity to initiate and sustain meaningful
    47→activities. Despite their clinical importance, negative symptoms remain difficult to treat and
    48→show limited response to available antipsychotic medications (Correll & Schooler, 2020; Li et
    49→al., 2016). This has led to growing interest in complementary interventions capable of
    50→targeting motivational, affective, and functional impairments in schizophrenia-spectrum
    51→disorders.
    52→Against this background, physical exercise (i.e., planned, structured, and repetitive
    53→physical activity intended to improve or maintain physical fitness; Caspersen et al., 1985) has
    54→received increasing attention as a complementary intervention for schizophrenia-spectrum
    55→disorders. Reviews and meta-analyses suggest that exercise may improve several clinically
    56→relevant outcomes in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, including psychiatric symptoms,
    57→cognition, physical health, functioning, and quality of life (Dauwan et al., 2016; Gallardo-
    58→Gómez et al., 2023; Girdler et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2023; Sabe et al., 2020). Of particular
    59→relevance to the present review, exercise has also been associated with reductions in negative
    60→symptoms (Sabe et al., 2020). This raises a clinically important paradox: the very symptoms
    61→that exercise may help alleviate may also make exercise difficult to initiate, sustain, and
    62→repeat.
    63→3
    64→
    65→This paradox is especially important because individuals with schizophrenia exhibit a
    66→generally lower level of physical activity (i.e., any bodily movement produced by skeletal
    67→muscles that results in energy expenditure; Caspersen et al., 1985) than the general population
    68→(Ohi et al., 2019; Stubbs et al., 2016), and reduced physical activity appears particularly
    69→pronounced among those with more severe negative symptoms (Gorczynski et al., 2018).
    70→Negative symptoms may therefore contribute to a self-reinforcing cycle in which reduced
    71→motivation, diminished reward anticipation, and altered effort valuation decrease exercise
    72→engagement, thereby limiting opportunities to experience the affective, cognitive, social, and
    73→physiological benefits of exercise.
    74→Breaking this cycle requires moving beyond the question of whether exercise works,
    75→toward the question of how exercise is experienced. Physical exercise is not only a
    76→physiological stimulus, but also an affective and effortful experience. From the perspective of
    77→negative symptoms, two subjective dimensions appear especially important: exercise-related
    78→pleasure and perceived exertion.
    79→Here, exercise-related pleasure refers specifically to the hedonic valence of the
    80→exercise experience, that is, the degree to which a given exercise session is anticipated,
    81→experienced, or recalled as pleasant or unpleasant. This use follows the exercise-affect
    82→literature, in which pleasure-displeasure is treated as a core affective response and is
    83→commonly assessed through the Feeling Scale (Ekkekakis, 2003; Ekkekakis et al., 2011,
    84→2020; Hardy & Rejeski, 1989), rather than as synonymous with broader constructs such as
    85→enjoyment, intrinsic motivation, or affective attitudes (Kimiecik & Harris, 1996; Rhodes &
    86→Kates, 2015; Stevens et al., 2020; Teixeira et al., 2022), 1996. Perceived exertion refers to the
    87→subjective experience of how intense the effort or strain feels during physical activity (Haile
    88→et al., 2015; Robertson & Noble, 1997); see also Halperin & Emanuel, 2020) for conceptual
    89→discussion regarding its definition). Crucially, pleasure and effort dimensions are closely
    90→4
    91→
    92→linked in the broader exercise psychology literature, where higher perceived exertion is
    93→generally associated with less pleasant affective responses, a relationship captured by the
    94→Dual-Mode Theory of exercise affect (Ekkekakis, 2003).
    95→The present narrative review develops the “breaking the cycle” argument in four steps.
    96→First, we establish that physical exercise can reduce negative symptoms in schizophrenia-
    97→spectrum disorders. Second, we examine evidence that negative symptoms interfere with
    98→physical activity and exercise engagement. Third, we focus on the proximal exercise
    99→experience by reviewing studies that have examined subjective pleasure and perceived
   100→exertion experienced during exercise in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. Finally, we
   101→propose a learning-based framework, informed by the reward, effort, and prediction-error
   102→alterations summarized in Box 1, in which physical exercise may help rewire negative
   103→symptoms by updating maladaptive internal models related to effort and pleasure experiences.
   104→According to the proposed framework, individuals with schizophrenia-spectrum
   105→disorders may anticipate exercise as effortful and hard to control. However, when exercise is
   106→experienced as more manageable or more rewarding than expected, it may generate
   107→expectancy violations or prediction errors that support the updating of internal models.
   108→Concurrently, the physiological adaptations induced by physical exertion drive a potent
   109→neurometabolic and neuroprotective cascade, that may contribute to restore frontostriatal
   110→network sensitivity to catalyze this computational re-learning process. Repeated exercise
   111→experiences that violate negative expectations may gradually change how effort is anticipated,
   112→experienced, remembered, and valued. The objective of this review is therefore to articulate
   113→an integrated neurocognitive framework linking negative symptoms, exercise engagement,
   114→perceived exertion, pleasure, and prediction-error-based learning, with the aim of generating
   115→testable hypotheses for future research and intervention development.
   116→2. Effects of physical exercise on negative symptoms
   117→5
   118→
   119→At a broad level, the literature suggests that exercise can produce beneficial effects
   120→across several clinically relevant domains in individuals with schizophrenia. Studies assessing
   121→immediate responses to exercise bouts have shown that physical exercise can reduce state
   122→anxiety and psychological distress while increasing positive well-being in people with
   123→schizophrenia (Heggelund et al., 2014; Korman et al., 2026; Vancampfort et al., 2011).
   124→Exercise also shows benefits on cognitive and functional outcomes. Meta-analytic evidence
   125→suggests that aerobic exercise may improve global cognition, attention, processing speed, and
   126→functional outcomes in people with schizophrenia-spectrum disorders (Girdler et al., 2019;
   127→Korman et al., 2023; Lak et al., 2024; Vila-Barrios et al., 2023). These effects are relevant
   128→because cognitive dysfunction and negative symptoms are closely intertwined in daily
   129→functioning, particularly for goal-directed behavior, social participation, and occupational
   130→engagement.
   131→Regular moderate-to-vigorous aerobic exercise has also been associated with
   132→reductions in body weight, waist circumference, fasting blood glucose, triglycerides, and
   133→systolic blood pressure (Bredin et al., 2013; Schmitt et al., 2018; Tumiel et al., 2019;
   134→Vancampfort et al., 2019). These somatic benefits may also contribute to downregulating the
   135→chronic low-grade systemic inflammation frequently observed in schizophrenia (Furman et
   136→al., 2019; Khandaker et al., 2015). This is clinically relevant since inflammatory signaling is
   137→known to alter frontostriatal circuits, reducing ventral striatal responses to reward and
   138→lowering dopamine availability, which directly impairs motivation and motor activity (Felger
   139→& Treadway, 2017; Goldsmith & Rapaport, 2020). Systematic reviews of exercise
   140→interventions suggest that regular exercise could improve lipid metabolism and lower
   141→inflammation in this population (Gómez-Rubio & Trapero, 2019), although further studies are
   142→needed to confirm this pattern. In a recent experimental clinical trial conducted on patients
   143→6
   144→
   145→major depressive disorder (Miller et al., 2025), targeting inflammation led to a reduction in
   146→motivational deficits, reflected by an increased willingness to select high-effort options during
   147→effort-based decision-making tasks. This later finding highlights another promising avenue for
   148→future research in schizophrenia.
   149→Of particular interest for the present paper is whether physical exercise can specifically
   150→reduce negative symptoms. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses reveal that exercise
   151→interventions have been associated with improvements in general psychopathology, with some
   152→evidence for reductions in positive symptoms, such as hallucinations and delusions, and more
   153→consistent evidence for reductions in negative symptoms, including motivational and
   154→expressive dimensions such as avolition, asociality, anhedonia, alogia, and blunted affect
   155→(Gallardo-Gómez et al., 2023; Girdler et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2023; Korman et al., 2023;
   156→Mittal et al., 2017; Sabe et al., 2020; Veerman et al., 2017). The magnitude of these effects
   157→also varies across meta-analyses. Sabe et al. (2020), focusing specifically on randomized
   158→controlled trials of physical exercise for negative symptoms, reported a small effect (SMD = -
   159→0.20, 95% CI -0.37 to -0.03), mainly driven by aerobic interventions (SMD = -0.31, 95% CI -
   160→0.54 to -0.09), whereas non-aerobic interventions showed less consistent benefits. By contrast,
   161→Kim et al. (2023), which included a broader range of exercise modalities, including aerobic,
   162→multicomponent, and neuromotor forms of exercise, reported a medium effect on negative
   163→symptoms (Hedges’ g = -0.51, 95% CI -0.72 to -0.31), as well as a smaller effect on positive
   164→symptoms. Similarly, Gallardo-Gómez et al. (2023), using a multilevel meta-analytic
   165→approach across randomized controlled trials, found a medium-to-large effect on negative
   166→symptoms (g = 0.65, 95% CI 0.53 to 0.78), but no significant effect on positive symptoms (g
   167→= -0.05). These differences in effect size likely reflect several sources of heterogeneity,
   168→including the type of exercise intervention, intervention dose and number of sessions, clinical
   169→7
   170→
   171→setting, comparison condition, methodological quality, and whether studies included
   172→participants with primary or predominant negative symptoms.
   173→Importantly, Sabe et al. (2020) suggested that improvements in negative symptoms
   174→may not be fully reducible to improvements in depressive symptoms, as a subgroup analysis
   175→limited to studies excluding participants with baseline depression still showed a moderate
   176→reduction in negative symptoms. This point is important but should be interpreted cautiously:
   177→the authors also emphasized that none of the included trials specifically targeted primary or
   178→predominant negative symptoms. Thus, the current evidence does not yet establish whether
   179→exercise acts directly on core negative symptoms, or whether its effects are partly mediated by
   180→changes in mood, physical health, social engagement, or other secondary mechanisms. Further
   181→reinforcing the idea that exercise may alter the broader clinical organization of schizophrenia,
   182→Laurendeau et al. (2026) showed, using a pooled network analysis, that negative symptoms
   183→were among the most central nodes of psychopathology at baseline, and that a structured
   184→exercise intervention reorganized the overall symptom network toward a less dense and less
   185→connected architecture. Together, these findings suggest that exercise may not simply reduce
   186→isolated symptoms, but may influence broader motivational and experiential processes that are
   187→central to negative symptoms, including effort valuation, hedonic experience, and behavioral
   188→initiation.

   190→The evidence reviewed in the previous section suggests that structured physical exercise may
   191→reduce negative symptoms in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. However, the clinical
   192→relevance of this finding depends on a central paradox: the very symptoms that exercise may
   193→help improve are also likely to reduce the willingness to engage in exercise programs, as well

   198→A first line of evidence comes from cross-sectional research on motivation toward
   199→physical activity. Vancampfort et al. (2015) showed that, among inpatients with
   200→schizophrenia, higher levels of negative symptoms were associated with lower autonomous
   201→motivation to engage in physical activity. In this sense, negative symptoms are not only
   202→correlated with lower activity levels, but also with a reduced capacity to experience physical
   203→activity as personally meaningful, rewarding, or self-endorsed.
   204→Longitudinal evidence points in a broadly similar direction. In a systematic review of
   205→prospective studies in people with severe mental illness, Hassan et al. (2019) highlighted that
   206→higher negative symptom scores predicted poorer physical activity-related outcomes,
   207→including lower cardiorespiratory fitness and lower physical activity levels. This review
   208→included studies involving people with schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder, psychosis, and
   209→broader severe mental illness samples. In particular, Storch Jakobsen et al. (Storch Jakobsen
   210→et al., 2018) reported that negative symptoms predicted reduced cardiorespiratory fitness in
   211→people with schizophrenia spectrum disorders, whereas (Nyboe et al. (2016) reported that
   212→negative symptoms predicted lower physical activity over time in people with first-episode
   213→schizophrenia. Importantly, Hassan et al. also emphasized the scarcity and methodological
   214→heterogeneity of prospective studies, meaning that the temporal and potentially bidirectional
   215→relationship between negative symptoms and physical activity remains insufficiently
   216→established.

   218→refers to the physiological capacity to sustain aerobic effort, often indexed by
   219→cardiorespiratory fitness or estimated maximal oxygen uptake (Caspersen et al., 1985; Ross et
   220→al., 2016). Maurus et al. (2022), in a large cross-sectional study examining aerobic fitness,

   224→found that aerobic fitness was related to markers of brain structure, particularly hippocampal

   227→This pattern contrasts with recent digital phenotyping evidence, which provides a more
   228→direct window into everyday activity and mobility. Yang et al. (2025), using wrist wearables
   229→and smartphones in 99 recently discharged individuals with schizophrenia spectrum disorders,
   230→found that negative symptoms were among the clinical dimensions most strongly associated
   231→with passive digital markers. These markers included physical activity, GPS-derived distance
   232→travelled, sleep-related measures, phone use, and social behavior. The authors reported a
   233→negative association between physical activity, measured through step count, and negative
   234→symptom severity, especially the Brief Negative Symptom Scale’s subdomains of avolition,
   235→blunted affect, and alogia. They also found that distance travelled, messages sent, and
   236→touchscreen tapping speed yielded strong signals associated with negative symptoms,
   237→particularly experiential deficits, notably avolition and anhedonia.
   238→This latter pattern of findings suggests that digital markers, especially those related to
   239→physical activity and mobility, are particularly informative for documenting how negative
   240→symptoms are associated with reduced movement in everyday contexts. Yet these objective
   241→markers leave open a key experiential question: how is physical activity felt when it is
   242→actually performed? They indicate whether individuals move, but not whether bodily effort
   243→feels costly, tolerable, rewarding, or pleasurable. This distinction is particularly relevant in
   244→schizophrenia, because avolition and anhedonia are thought to involve impairments in reward
   245→anticipation, effort valuation, and the willingness to mobilize effort for future benefits (see
   246→Box 1 for a summary of fundamental alterations in reward, pleasure, volition, and effort-based
   247→decision-making). Applied to physical activity and exercise, these impairments may be

   250→especially important because movement often requires individuals to tolerate bodily effort
   251→before potential benefits are experienced. Accordingly, the next section focuses on two
   252→proximal experiential variables that may help explain how negative symptoms interfere with
   253→physical activity engagement: exercise-related pleasure and perceived effort.
   254→Box 1. Fundamental alterations in reward, pleasure, effort, and related
   255→neurobiology in schizophrenia
   256→Negative symptoms in schizophrenia, especially avolition and anhedonia, are closely linked to
   257→alterations in how rewards are anticipated, experienced, learned from, and weighed against effort
   258→costs (Abram et al., 2022; Correll & Schooler, 2020; Strauss & Cohen, 2017). These processes
   259→provide the psychological and neurocognitive background for understanding why beginning and then
   260→maintaining a physical exercise routine is so difficult in this population. For each process, behavioral
   261→and neural findings are presented together, followed by its implication for physical exercise.
   262→(i) Anticipatory and consummatory pleasure
   263→At the subjective level, individuals with schizophrenia typically report relatively preserved
   264→consummatory (in-the-moment) pleasure but reduced anticipatory pleasure, that is, the pleasure
   265→expected before an event (Cohen et al., 2010; Gard et al., 2007; Kring & Barch, 2014; Moran et al.,
   266→2022). This subjective picture is complemented, rather than contradicted, by neuroimaging: meta-
   267→analyses show reduced activation of the striatum and cingulate cortex (regions that are commonly
   268→triggered by expected reward; (Bartra et al., 2013; Knutson et al., 2001) during reward anticipation,
   269→whereas activation during reward consumption is more variable across studies (Wang et al., 2023;
   270→Zeng et al., 2022).
   271→Implication for physical exercise: an individual with schizophrenia may experience relief,
   272→calmness, accomplishment, or pleasure after an exercise session, yet still fail to anticipate these
   273→positive outcomes before the next one. This may help explain why a positive exercise experience
   274→does not automatically translate into spontaneous future engagement.
   275→(ii) Effort valuation and the anticipation of effort
   276→Motivated behavior depends not only on reward value but also on the effort required to obtain it
   277→(Treadway et al., 2009; Walton et al., 2018). Individuals with schizophrenia are less willing to choose
   278→high-effort options for larger rewards, particularly when motivational negative symptoms are more
   279→severe (Barch et al., 2014; Blouzard et al., 2023; Culbreth et al., 2018; Fervaha et al., 2015; Gold et
   280→al., 2013), and this partly reflects heightened sensitivity to effort cost and an altered perception of
   281→effort (Culbreth et al., 2025; Décombe et al., 2020). A recent transdiagnostic multilevel meta-analysis
   282→confirms a moderate effort-based decision-making deficit in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders (g = -
   283→0.40), with the severity of negative and positive symptoms among its significant moderators (Pillny
   284→et al., 2026). These behavioral deficits are accompanied by weaker task-related activation (measured
   285→with functional MRI) of the ventral striatum, anterior cingulate, and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, a
   286→core brain network for weighing effort against reward (Bartra et al., 2013; Prévost et al., 2010), when
   287→individuals perform effort-based decision tasks (Saleh et al., 2021). At the neurochemical level, this
   288→circuitry depends on a balance between the main excitatory neurotransmitter, glutamate, and the main
   289→inhibitory one, GABA: a glutamatergic-GABAergic imbalance in the prefrontal cortex disrupts its
   290→top-down control of subcortical dopamine circuits (dopamine being the neuromodulator that signals
   291→reward and mobilises effort; Howes et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2021). Causal animal evidence confirms
   292→this link: reducing cortical and hippocampal GABA is sufficient to produce effort-based decision-
   293→making deficits and avolition-like behaviour distinct from anhedonia, alongside attenuated effort-
   294→evoked dopamine release in the anterior cingulate cortex (Kolata et al., 2018).
   295→Implication for physical exercise: exercise may be rejected not because it lacks potential value,
   296→but because its anticipated effort cost is too salient relative to its expected reward. Even low- or
   297→11
   298→
   299→moderate-intensity exercise may then be experienced as difficult to initiate when the expected
   300→benefit is weak, delayed, or uncertain.
   301→(iii) Reward and reinforcement learning
   302→Reinforcement learning, the process of adjusting behavior based on the outcomes of past actions (Niv,
   303→2009; Rangel et al., 2008), is altered in schizophrenia, particularly the ability to acquire and use
   304→positive reward associations to guide future behavior (Dowd & Barch, 2012; Strauss, Frank, et al.,
   305→2011; Waltz et al., 2007). Deficits are most apparent when behavior requires integrating past
   306→outcomes, maintaining value representations (the brain’s internal estimates of how rewarding or
   307→worthwhile an option is;(Bartra et al., 2013), and using them to guide later decisions (Culbreth et al.,
   308→2021; Gold et al., 2013). These deficits are closely tied to negative symptoms, particularly avolition
   309→and anhedonia (Moran et al., 2019; Strauss, Wilbur, et al., 2011; Waltz & Gold, 2015).
   310→Implication for physical exercise: even when exercise produces a positive affective response, this
   311→experience may not be automatically encoded as a reason to repeat the behaviour. Interventions
   312→may therefore need to make positive outcomes explicit, repeated, and memorable, rather than
   313→assuming that pleasant experiences will spontaneously guide future engagement.
   314→(iv) Reward prediction errors and expectation updating
   315→Reward prediction errors, the discrepancies between expected and actual outcomes, signal when
   316→internal models should be updated. They are coded by dopamine activity in frontostriatal circuits (the
   317→loops linking the prefrontal cortex and the striatum that support reward valuation and goal-directed
   318→action; (Haber & Knutson, 2010), including the ventral striatum, midbrain, and orbitofrontal cortex
   319→(D’Astolfo & Rief, 2017; Papalini et al., 2020; Schultz, 2016; Steinberg et al., 2013). In
   320→schizophrenia, neural responses in these dopamine target regions during reward anticipation are
   321→attenuated, and positive prediction errors are used less effectively to guide future behaviour (Arrondo
   322→et al., 2015; Dowd & Barch, 2012; Leroy et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023).
   323→Implication for physical exercise: if an individual expects exercise to be exhausting, unpleasant,
   324→or unrewarding but the session is experienced as manageable, calming, or rewarding, this
   325→discrepancy can create a clinically useful prediction error. Because prediction-error processing
   326→may be altered, such expectancy-violating experiences may need to be explicitly identified,
   327→discussed, and repeated across sessions.
   328→(v) Modulating factors: delay discounting and prospection
   329→Several additional processes modulate this reward-effort balance. Many benefits of exercise are
   330→delayed (fitness, metabolic and cardiovascular health), yet individuals with schizophrenia show
   331→steeper delay discounting, that is, they devalue delayed rewards more rapidly than immediate ones, a
   332→tendency itself associated with negative symptoms (Bartolomeo et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2018;
   333→Heerey et al., 2011; Horan et al., 2017). Using past experience to imagine future rewards also depends
   334→on autobiographical memory and future simulation (the capacity to mentally pre-experience an
   335→event), both of which can be impaired in schizophrenia (D’Argembeau et al., 2008; Raffard et al.,
   336→2010; Schacter et al., 2007).
   337→Implication for physical exercise: interventions should not rely primarily on delayed health
   338→outcomes to motivate engagement. They should instead make immediate or near-term benefits
   339→salient (calm, a sense of achievement, or connection with others), and may benefit from post-session
   340→reflection that helps individuals register the gap between what they expected and what actually
   341→happened.
   342→4. Experienced pleasure and physical exertion during exercise in schizophrenia
   343→As acknowledged in Section 2., several studies have shown that single bouts of physical
   344→exercise can reduce state anxiety and psychological stress while increasing positive well-
   345→12
   346→
   347→being in people with schizophrenia or psychotic disorders (Heggelund et al., 2014;
   348→Vancampfort et al., 2011). However, these studies assessed psychological states before and
   349→after exercise, but not during the exercise bout itself. Only a small but relevant body of
   350→research has examined how people with schizophrenia or schizophrenia-spectrum disorders
   351→experience exercise at the subjective level. This subset of studies is important as, based on the
   352→Dual-Mode Theory (Ekkekakis, 2003), affective responses during exercise are shaped by the
   353→balance between cognitive-affective factors and interoceptive cues of bodily strain. In-
   354→moment (or retrospective) ratings of pleasure and effort may thus provide fine-grained insight
   355→into the characterization of the exercise experience, as the activity is often experienced at
   356→different levels of pleasantness and effortfulness while it is being performed (Brevers et al.,
   357→2024; Ekkekakis et al., 2011; Hardy & Rejeski, 1989).
   358→A study by Duncan et al. (2016) assessed core subjective pleasure before, during, and
   359→after a 10-min bout of moderate-intensity walking (defined as 64–76% of the calculated
   360→maximum heart rate) in outpatients with schizophrenia, using the Feeling Scale to assess
   361→pleasure-displeasure and the Felt Arousal Scale (a single-item measure of perceived
   362→activation, independent of hedonic valence; (Svebak & Murgatroyd, 1985) to assess
   363→activation. Pleasure increased after exercise and remained elevated 10 min later, but did not
   364→significantly improve during exercise itself. Perceived exertion, assessed with the Borg RPE
   365→scale, increased from baseline to the in-task assessment during exercise while remaining
   366→stable during passive sitting. Duncan et al. thus provide a particularly informative pattern:
   367→moderate-intensity walking was experienced as more effortful during the task, whereas the
   368→increase in pleasure emerged mainly after task completion.
   369→However, several limitations temper Duncan et al. (2016) findings. First, there was no
   370→healthy control group: the comparison was a passive sitting condition completed by the same
   371→participants, so the study cannot indicate whether individuals with schizophrenia differ from
   372→13
   373→
   374→healthy controls in their affective response to exercise. Second, pleasure ratings did not
   375→significantly differ between the exercise and passive sitting conditions, suggesting that the
   376→post-task increase in pleasure was not clearly specific to exercise. Third, felt arousal did not
   377→significantly change from baseline and did not differ from sitting, contrary to the authors’
   378→hypothesis: of the two affective dimensions assessed (pleasure and activation), only pleasure
   379→shifted, and only over time rather than specifically with exercise, leaving the affective
   380→response only partially characterized. Finally, most participants were overweight or obese
   381→(75%), raising the possibility that the observed pattern, namely increased perceived exertion
   382→during walking and improved pleasure mainly after completion, partly reflects obesity-related
   383→physical constraints rather than schizophrenia-specific affective dysfunction.
   384→In this respect, Pitois et al. (2025) is informative, although only indirectly related to
   385→schizophrenia. This study examined adults with severe obesity, with or without mental
   386→disorders, during two consecutive 6-min walking exercises. The psychiatric subgroup was
   387→mainly composed of individuals with mood disorders, with only one participant classified as
   388→having a psychotic disorder and no specific indication of schizophrenia. Pitois et al. found that
   389→pleasure declined over time across the walking exercises in both groups, without a significant
   390→group by time interaction. This suggests that reduced pleasure during repeated walking may
   391→be linked to obesity-related physical constraints rather than to mental disorder per se.
   392→Accordingly, the role of BMI and metabolic burden should be considered when interpreting
   393→affective responses to walking-based exercise in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. Finally,
   394→and most importantly for the present framework, the study did not examine the association
   395→between (changes in) perceived exertion and pleasure. It therefore remains unclear whether
   396→higher exertion during exercise modulated pleasure across the exercise session.

   400→
   401→increasing the distance to be covered. The authors found that perceived effort and perceived
   402→difficulty increased with task demand in both groups, indicating that individuals with
   403→schizophrenia were sensitive to changes in physical demand. However, participants with
   404→schizophrenia reported higher perceived effort and difficulty overall than healthy controls.
   405→This suggests that even relatively low-effortful forms of physical exercise may carry a higher
   406→subjective effort cost in schizophrenia. Notably, perceived effort was positively associated
   407→with BMI in the schizophrenia group, suggesting that bodily or metabolic factors may
   408→contribute to the subjective cost of physical effort.
   409→Korman et al. (2026) recently examined the immediate (acute) psychological
   410→responses to single bouts of resistance training and aerobic interval training in people with
   411→psychotic disorders receiving psychiatric rehabilitation, most of whom had schizophrenia or
   412→schizoaffective disorder. Both exercise modalities produced immediate increases in positive
   413→well-being and reductions in psychological distress. Importantly, in models adjusted for BMI,
   414→fatigue followed a modality-specific pattern: it increased after resistance training, whereas it
   415→decreased after aerobic exercise. This finding suggests that the psychological benefits of
   416→exercise may coexist with different subjective exertional costs depending on the type of
   417→activity performed. In schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, where fatigue, reduced energy,
   418→amotivation, and difficulties with behavioral initiation are major barriers to physical activity,
   419→such immediate fatigue responses may influence whether an exercise bout is experienced as
   420→tolerable, aversive, or worth repeating.
   421→Taken together, this literature suggests that people with schizophrenia-spectrum
   422→disorders can experience acute psychological benefits from exercise, but it also highlights a
   423→major gap: few studies have examined how perceived exertion, fatigue, and pleasure interact
   424→during the exercise bout itself. This gap is important because future physical activity
   425→engagement may depend not only on post-exercise well-being, but also on the experienced
   426→15
   427→
   428→and remembered balance between effort and pleasure. Understanding this balance may be
   429→particularly relevant for negative symptoms, where anticipatory pleasure, effort valuation, and
   430→behavioral initiation are often disrupted. Future studies should therefore assess pleasure,
   431→perceived exertion, fatigue, BMI, fitness level, and symptom dimensions within the same
   432→design, in order to clarify when exercise is experienced as rewarding, costly, or difficult to
   433→repeat.
   434→Beyond measuring this balance, the central question is how it can be shifted: the next
   435→section develops a learning-based framework in which physical exercise acts as an
   436→experiential context where repeated discrepancies between expected and experienced effort
   437→and pleasure progressively update the maladaptive internal models that sustain negative
   438→symptoms.
   439→5. Rewiring negative symptoms through physical exercise
   440→The previous sections suggest a central clinical paradox. Structured physical exercise may
   441→reduce negative symptoms in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, yet negative symptoms
   442→themselves may undermine the capacity to take up exercise and persist with it over repeated
   443→sessions. This paradox provides the rationale for a learning-based framework in which
   444→exercise is not viewed only as a physiological intervention, but also as an experiential context
   445→in which maladaptive expectations about effort, pleasure, reward, and control can be updated.
   446→This section builds on the fundamental alterations summarized in Box 1, including
   447→impairments in reward anticipation, hedonic experience, effort valuation, reinforcement
   448→learning, and prediction error processing. For each mechanism, Box 1 also highlights potential
   449→implications for physical exercise, which are developed further in the intervention framework
   450→below. These alterations may contribute to the maintenance of physical exercise avoidance by
   451→making exercise appear costly, unrewarding, or not worth initiating. However, they also
   452→16
   453→
   454→identify potential intervention targets. If physical exercise is repeatedly experienced as less
   455→effortful or more rewarding than expected, it may gradually update internal models of
   456→exercise and weaken the self-reinforcing cycle between negative symptoms and exercise
   457→avoidance. As illustrated in Figure 1, this framework conceptualizes physical exercise as a
   458→corrective learning context in which repeated discrepancies between expected and
   459→experienced effort and pleasure may progressively recalibrate future exercise expectations and
   460→support renewed engagement.
   461→5.1 Physical exercise as a corrective learning experience
   462→Physical effort is often perceived as costly, and individuals generally tend to minimize
   463→unnecessary effort (Cheval & Boisgontier, 2021; Inzlicht et al., 2018). From a learning
   464→perspective, physical exercise may also become associated with aversive bodily states, such as
   465→fatigue, breathlessness, or discomfort. In classical conditioning terms, exercise-related cues,
   466→such as the idea of going for a walk, entering a fitness room, wearing sport clothes, or
   467→anticipating bodily exertion, may function as conditioned stimuli if they have been repeatedly
   468→paired with aversive unconditioned stimuli such as exhaustion or physical discomfort. Over
   469→time, these cues may elicit conditioned responses, such as negative affect, anticipatory
   470→fatigue, or avoidance (Craske et al., 2014; Rescorla & Wagner, 1972). In the absence of
   471→sufficiently positive or corrective experiences, the association between exercise and aversive
   472→bodily states may remain unchanged.
   473→This learning account is particularly relevant for schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. As
   474→described in Box 1, negative symptoms are associated with reduced reward anticipation,
   475→altered effort valuation, impairments in reinforcement learning, and difficulties translating
   476→expected reward into action. As a result, individuals may assign greater weight to the
   477→immediate costs of exercise than to its delayed or uncertain benefits. The anticipated effort
   478→may be vivid and concrete, whereas the expected rewards, such as pleasure, accomplishment,
   479→17
   480→
   481→or long-term health benefits, may be weakly represented. This imbalance may bias individuals
   482→toward avoidance before the activity is even initiated.
   483→Within the present framework, physical exercise becomes clinically relevant not only
   484→because it can improve fitness or reduce symptoms, but because it can create opportunities for
   485→corrective learning. When exercise is experienced as less effortful, less aversive, more
   486→controllable, or more rewarding than expected, the discrepancy between expectation and
   487→experience can generate an expectancy violation or prediction error. Such discrepancies are
   488→central to associative learning because they signal that existing predictions no longer fit the
   489→experienced outcome and should therefore be updated (D’Astolfo & Rief, 2017; Rescorla &
   490→Wagner, 1972; Schultz et al., 1997). In anxiety disorders, exposure-based interventions rely
   491→on a similar principle: feared outcomes are challenged when threat cues are not followed by
   492→the expected aversive consequence, thereby promoting inhibitory learning and expectation
   493→change (Craske et al., 2014; Papalini et al., 2020; Rief et al., 2022).
   494→Here, we propose that a comparable logic may apply to physical exercise in
   495→schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. If an individual expects a walk, a bike ride, or a strength
   496→session to feel exhausting and beyond their control, but the activity turns out to be tolerable,
   497→manageable, or even pleasant, this experience may challenge the prior expectation and
   498→support the formation of a new internal model of exercise.
   499→5.2. Prediction errors during physical exercise: from pacing regulation to subjective
   500→learning
   501→Physical exercise regulation involves a continuous adjustment between bodily feedback,
   502→perceived exertion, and expectations about what remains to be done (Smits et al., 2014;
   503→Tucker, 2009; Ulmer, 1996). During exercise, individuals do not simply respond to current
   504→bodily sensations; they also estimate whether the ongoing level of effort is sustainable relative
   505→to the expected duration, distance, or endpoint of the task. Teleo-anticipatory and pacing
   506→18
   507→
   508→models propose that performance is regulated through this dynamic comparison between
   509→current physiological and perceptual states, anticipated task demands, and the remaining work
   510→to be completed (Noakes, 2012; St Clair Gibson et al., 2006; Tucker, 2009; Ulmer, 1996).
   511→Perceived exertion is central to this regulatory process: rather than being a passive readout of
   512→peripheral fatigue, it functions as an integrative signal that helps individuals decide whether to
   513→maintain pace, slow down, accelerate, stop, or tolerate discomfort.
   514→Thus, because exercise regulation depends on the comparison between ongoing bodily
   515→feedback and expected task demands, discrepancies may emerge between expected and
   516→experienced bodily states. If the activity feels easier than expected, the person may continue,
   517→increase intensity, or experience the situation as more manageable than anticipated. This
   518→within-exercise regulation is therefore already based on discrepancies between expected and
   519→experienced bodily states. In schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, such discrepancies may be
   520→clinically important because they do not only guide moment-to-moment pacing. They may
   521→also shape how exercise is encoded, remembered, and valued.
   522→A central aim of exercise-based interventions may therefore be to help individuals
   523→identify and learn from these prediction errors. In the context of physical exercise, prediction
   524→errors may concern perceived effort, fatigue, pleasure, or post-exercise well-being. For
   525→example, a person may expect a short walk to be exhausting but find it manageable. Another
   526→person may expect an exercise session to be unpleasant, but experience relief or
   527→accomplishment after completion. Conversely, a person may underestimate the difficulty of
   528→an activity and experience it as more effortful than anticipated. These mismatches matter
   529→because they provide information about whether exercise is really as costly or unpleasant as
   530→expected.
   531→Positive prediction errors, such as experiencing less effort than expected, are
   532→especially relevant because they may directly enhance the rewarding value of exercise. This
   533→19
   534→
   535→mechanism is consistent with recent studies (Brevers et al., 2024; Öz et al., 2026) showing
   536→that when exertion during running was lower than expected, participants reported greater
   537→pleasure. In other words, exercise was not only pleasurable because it was objectively easy,
   538→but because it was easier than anticipated. Applied to schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, this
   539→suggests that exercise interventions should not necessarily aim to reduce objective effort per
   540→se. Rather, they should be shaped around the dynamic relation between expected and
   541→experienced effort, so that individuals have opportunities to discover that exercise can be less
   542→costly or more rewarding than predicted.
   543→5.3. Learning from both positive and negative prediction errors
   544→Although positive prediction errors, where an exercise is experienced as less effortful than
   545→anticipated, are associated with increased pleasure, the intervention target should not be
   546→restricted to positive prediction errors only. First, negative prediction errors, where an
   547→exercise is experienced as more effortful than anticipated, are an inherent part of physical
   548→exercise. A session will sometimes feel more difficult, more tiring, or less pleasant than
   549→anticipated. Such experiences may reduce pleasure and reinforce avoidance if they are only
   550→interpreted as confirmation that exercise is aversive, uncontrollable, or not worth repeating. In
   551→this context, a more relevant clinical target is to help individuals become aware of the gap
   552→between anticipation and experience, and to use this gap as a source of learning.
   553→This is crucial because expectation violations do not automatically lead to belief
   554→updating. Individuals may discount, ignore, or reinterpret expectancy-violating experiences in
   555→ways that preserve prior beliefs, a process sometimes described as cognitive immunization
   556→(Kube et al., 2017). For example, after completing a walk that was easier than expected, a
   557→person may conclude that “it was only manageable today because the weather was good” or
   558→“it does not mean I could do it again.” For this reason, exercise interventions should not only
   559→generate prediction errors, but also help individuals notice, interpret, and encode them.
   560→20
   561→
   562→One way to support this process is to make predictions explicit. Before an exercise
   563→bout, individuals can be asked to predict how effortful (or unpleasant, tiring, controllable, or
   564→rewarding) the activity will be. After the bout, they can compare these predictions with their
   565→actual experience. Explicit prediction may increase the salience of prediction errors and
   566→support memory updating (Brod et al., 2022; Rief et al., 2022). This strategy may be
   567→particularly useful in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, where prediction error signaling and
   568→reinforcement learning are often altered (Arrondo et al., 2015; Leroy et al., 2020; Maia &
   569→Frank, 2017; Wang et al., 2023).
   570→When appropriately supported, negative prediction errors may become opportunities
   571→for learning. If an activity is more difficult than expected, the therapeutic focus can shift from
   572→“this was too hard” to “this was difficult, but I managed it.” In this case, the relevant update is
   573→not that exercise is easier than expected, but that difficulty can be tolerated, regulated, and
   574→overcome. This type of learning may contribute to perceived competence, mastery, and self-
   575→efficacy, which are important determinants of exercise engagement (Bandura, 1997;
   576→Hutchinson et al., 2008; McAuley et al., 2003).
   577→This dynamic is central to the present framework. The goal is to help individuals build
   578→a more flexible internal model of physical exercise: sometimes effort is lower than expected,
   579→sometimes effort is higher than expected, but in both cases the experience can provide useful
   580→information. Positive prediction errors may help re-associate exercise with pleasure, relief,
   581→and accomplishment. Negative prediction errors, when carefully supported, may help re-
   582→associate effort with mastery, competence, and control rather than threat or failure.
   583→This learning process may also be central for modifying the meaning of effort itself.
   584→Effort is typically avoided prospectively because it discounts reward value, but exerted effort
   585→can retrospectively increase the value of outcomes once they have been obtained (Inzlicht et
   586→al., 2018; Yi et al., 2020; Zhang & Zheng, 2022). In healthy participants, higher physical
   587→21
   588→
   589→effort has been associated with stronger neural responses to reward outcomes, suggesting that
   590→effort can enhance reward valuation after completion (Palidis & Gribble, 2020; Umemoto et
   591→al., 2023). This idea is consistent with learned industriousness theory, which proposes that
   592→effort can acquire secondary reinforcing properties when it is repeatedly paired with positive
   593→outcomes (Eisenberger, 1992). Applied to physical exercise, repeated experiences in which
   594→effort is followed by relief, accomplishment, pleasure, or social reinforcement may gradually
   595→reduce the aversive meaning of exertion and help re-associate effort with reward. Although
   596→these mechanisms remain to be tested directly in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, they
   597→suggest that exercise interventions should not necessarily reduce effort costs but rather help
   598→individuals reinterpret effort as a signal of accomplishment, progress, and reward.
   599→5.4. Designing exercise experiences that consolidate new internal models
   600→The practical implication of this framework is that exercise interventions should be designed
   601→to maximize tolerable expectancy violations. The therapeutic target is therefore a calibrated
   602→mismatch between expected and experienced exercise, in which the person can discover that
   603→physical exercise is more manageable, rewarding, or controllable than anticipated.
   604→This corrective learning requires repetition. Specifically, across repeated sessions,
   605→individuals can gradually build a more adaptive internal model of physical exercise: effort is
   606→not always overwhelming, discomfort can be tolerated, pleasure can emerge unexpectedly,
   607→and completion can be rewarding. Active engagement is central to this process. Continuing a
   608→walk that turns out to be less effortful than expected, completing a session despite initial
   609→doubt, or repeating the activity on subsequent days may all help consolidate a new
   610→representation of exercise. This is consistent with evidence showing that behavioral
   611→adaptation following expectation violations recruits neural circuits involved in prediction
   612→error, reward processing, and avoidance learning, including the striatum and insula (D’Astolfo
   613→& Rief, 2017).
   614→22
   615→
   616→For clinical interventions, this means that the exercise experience should be reviewed
   617→and encoded after completion. Individuals may benefit from explicitly identifying what was
   618→expected, what was experienced, and what was learned. For example: “I expected this walk to
   619→be very tiring, but it was manageable”; “I expected not to enjoy it, but I felt calmer
   620→afterward”; or “I thought I would stop after five minutes, but I completed ten.” Such
   621→reflections may strengthen autobiographical memory traces of successful exercise experiences
   622→and make them more available during future decisions to exercise. This is important because
   623→future exercise engagement may depend not only on the actual affective experience during
   624→exercise, but also on how that experience is remembered and integrated into later
   625→expectations.
   626→This focus on immediate experience is also important because many benefits of
   627→physical exercise, such as weight loss, improved physical strength, better metabolic health, or
   628→increased fitness, emerge only after repeated engagement. These delayed outcomes may be
   629→too abstract or too distant to motivate behavior when the immediate cost of effort is highly
   630→salient. Early intervention should therefore not rely primarily on long-term health outcomes.
   631→Instead, each session should be structured to produce near-term reinforcement, such as
   632→pleasure, relief, or a sense of accomplishment.
   633→One concrete way to achieve this is to let individuals regulate the intensity of exercise
   634→according to how it feels, rather than imposing a fixed prescribed intensity: people can either
   635→self-select their own intensity (self-paced or preferred-intensity exercise) or adjust it to
   636→maintain a positive value on the pleasure-displeasure dimension introduced earlier (affect-
   637→regulated exercise, typically guided by the Feeling Scale). Anchored in pleasure rather than in
   638→external performance targets, such approaches keep the session within a pleasant or at least
   639→tolerable range and have been associated with better adherence and mental-health outcomes
   640→(Callaghan et al., 2011; Carter et al., 2012; Parfitt et al., 2012).
   641→23
   642→
   643→Over time, sustained physical exercise may also improve the valuation of delayed
   644→outcomes, as suggested by evidence from healthy volunteers and animal models showing that
   645→regular exercise can reduce preference for immediate rewards and improve delay-related
   646→decision-making (Sofis et al., 2017; Strickland et al., 2016). Given that steeper delay
   647→discounting has been associated with negative symptoms in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders,
   648→this may represent another pathway through which exercise could influence motivational
   649→dysfunction (Bartolomeo et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2018; Heerey et al., 2011; Horan et al., 2017).

   658→6. Concluding discussion
   659→The framework proposed here conceptualizes physical exercise as a learning context
   660→potentially capable of updating maladaptive internal models of effort and pleasure (see Figure
   661→24
   662→
   663→1 for a graphical summary). In schizophrenia-spectrum disorders, this imbalance is
   664→particularly pronounced: anticipated effort is especially salient and immediate, whereas
   665→reward is even more uncertain, delayed, or weakly represented than in the general population,
   666→reflecting the documented alterations in effort valuation and reward anticipation summarized
   667→in Box 1. Exercise interventions may help reverse this imbalance when they repeatedly
   668→generate experiences that are easier, or more rewarding than expected: such discrepancies
   669→between expectation and experience constitute prediction errors that, encoded by
   670→dopaminergic signaling in frontostriatal reward circuits (see also Box 1), can progressively
   671→recalibrate maladaptive internal models.
   672→At the experiential level, when dysfunctional expectations, such as anticipating that
   673→exercise will be exhausting or unrewarding, are disconfirmed by a lower physical cost than
   674→anticipated, a salient positive prediction error is generated. Reinforced by other immediate
   675→rewarding aspects of exercise, such as improved mood, increased energy, or positive social
   676→interaction, these expectancy violations may gradually weaken the association between
   677→exercise and aversive experience and support renewed engagement.
   678→Overall, exercise may contribute to the reduction of negative symptoms through
   679→several complementary mechanisms. At the level of learning, it can generate positive
   680→prediction errors while also helping individuals draw useful information from negative ones.
   681→At the level of valuation, it may increase the reward value of effort, lower perceived exertion,
   682→and improve how delayed rewards are weighed. And at the experiential level, it may amplify
   683→immediate positive affect and strengthen a sense of mastery and self-efficacy. These
   684→mechanisms are intended as a heuristic framework rather than a definitive account, and they
   685→generate concrete, testable hypotheses: future trials should examine whether positive
   686→prediction errors of perceived exertion predict exercise-related pleasure, whether pleasure and
   687→fatigue during exercise predict adherence, whether changes in effort valuation mediate
   688→25
   689→
   690→improvements in negative symptoms, and whether these effects are moderated by BMI,
   691→baseline fatigue, reward-learning impairments, or the severity of avolition and anhedonia.
   692→These proposals should be read with two qualifications in mind. First, this review
   693→deliberately adopts a particular perspective: it approaches negative symptoms and exercise
   694→through a learning-based, neurocognitive lens centred on the proximal experience of effort
   695→and pleasure. This focus is complementary to, rather than a substitute for, other frameworks,
   696→including socio-cognitive accounts (for example, self-efficacy and the social and
   697→environmental determinants of physical activity), which also shape exercise engagement and
   698→which we do not develop here. Second, the proposed framework remains largely hypothetical:
   699→much of the supporting evidence is indirect, drawn from the general population, other clinical
   700→groups, or animal models, and the in-session dynamics of pleasure and perceived effort have
   701→rarely been measured directly in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. These limitations
   702→underscore the need for the dedicated empirical tests outlined above.
   703→Ultimately, breaking the cycle between negative symptoms and physical inactivity may
   704→require more than prescribing exercise; it may require designing exercise experiences that
   705→reshape how effort is predicted beforehand, felt during the activity, and recalled when the next
   706→occasion arises.
