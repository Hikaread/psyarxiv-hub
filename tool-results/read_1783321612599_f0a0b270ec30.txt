   800→within-person intervention, and preregistering the study. However, there are several limitations
   801→related to the study design that should be noted. First, we focused on college students because
   802→they are at risk for alcohol-related negative consequences and could benefit from preventative
   803→interventions that help them develop healthy habits related to alcohol. However, this means that
   804→our findings may not generalize beyond this population, which tends to be wealthier, whiter, and
   805→more highly educated than the general population (Henrich et al., 2010). Future work is needed
   806→in more diverse populations. Despite a wide range of drinking behavior in our sample, we did
   807→not recruit individuals with alcohol-use disorders. This recruitment strategy may limit the
   808→applicability of our findings to individuals with alcohol-use disorder. Finally, we employed a
   809→within-person design to enable inferences about individual-level intervention mechanisms.
   810→However, this design may have introduced demand effects (Charness et al., 2012). Future work
   811→should examine how any demand effect may be reduced by introducing regulatory choice, that
   812→is, letting participants opt into mindful distancing as a regulatory strategy on a subset of trials
   813→(Cosme et al., 2018).
   814→There are also several limitations related to the neural signature. First, it was created in
   815→the context of a task that was not designed with development or validation of neural signatures
   816→in mind. Participants used mindful distancing while viewing alcohol stimuli, which limits the
   817→potential generalizability of using this strategy in response to other internal or external stimuli.
   818→The task did not include ratings of how well participants used the strategy on any given trial,
   819→which would have enabled prediction of subjective effectiveness during strategy usage rather
   820→than merely classification of task condition. Second, the limited size and homogeneity of the
   821→sample reduces the likelihood that this signature will generalize to new samples or other
   822→populations (Baranger et al., 2025; Woo et al., 2017). Finally, the low cross-validation accuracy
   823→suggests that there was substantial variability in activation patterns across participants making it
   824→difficult to identify common patterns that reliably predict mindful distancing. Overcoming these
   825→issues by using tasks specifically designed to create neural signatures of mindful states (e.g., as
   826→described in Lewis-Peacock et al., 2025) in large and diverse samples is an important objective
   827→for future research. Once developed and validated, such signatures could be applied to new and
   828→existing data to measure mindful states in a variety of contexts.
   829→4.5 Conclusion
   830→This work employed a translational neuroscience approach to examine how mindful
   831→distancing impacts alcohol-related craving and consumption under controlled and real-world
   832→conditions. We used functional neuroimaging and machine learning to develop a predictive
   833→model of mindful distancing that enabled us to measure momentary fluctuations and individual
   834→differences in mindful distancing. Mindful distancing reduced cravings for alcohol in the
   835→18
   836→
   837→laboratory and decreased alcohol consumption in daily life, both directly and indirectly through
   838→reducing cravings. Moreover, individuals who more strongly expressed the mindful distancing
   839→brain signature—indicating greater ability to attend to alcohol mindfully at baseline—benefited
   840→the most from the intervention. Together, these findings suggest that mindful distancing can be
   841→an effective, preventative strategy for reducing alcohol consumption when successfully
   842→implemented. Furthermore, it highlights the potential of scalable, smartphone-based
   843→interventions that remind individuals to regulate their responses to alcohol (Jovanova et al.,
   844→2023).
