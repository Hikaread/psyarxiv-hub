    27→Abstract
    28→Artificial intelligence (AI) chatbots are increasingly embedded in adolescents’ daily lives,
    29→yet developmental science research struggles to keep pace with adoption. This mixed-methods
    30→study gathered developmental expert perspectives via a survey (n = 141 researchers and adjacent
    31→professionals working on issues related to youth and technology) and semi-structured interviews
    32→with a purposive subsample (n = 15) to inform a coordinated research and policy agenda on
    33→adolescent AI use. The study aims were to (1) identify priority AI use cases for future study, (2)
    34→identify priority research questions and methods, as well as barriers to future research, and (3)
    35→identify societal guardrails that developmental professionals recommend enacting now, while
    36→research is underway. Experts cautioned against vague "screen time" measures and agreed that
    37→nearly all use cases merit study but prioritized those with severe or irreversible immediate-term
    38→harms, AI literacy, AI as a social/relational actor, AI-generated (mis)information, and
    39→overreliance. No single methodological consensus emerged; participants advocated broadly for
    40→methodological pluralism and triangulation. Key barriers included the rapid pace of change in AI
    41→technology and use relative to academic timelines, limited industry transparency, funding gaps,
    42→and misaligned academic incentives. Proposed solutions included shared infrastructure,
    43→interdisciplinary consortia, and community partnerships. Experts underscored that responsibility
    44→for safe AI should not rest primarily on adolescents and families and identified three immediate
    45→guardrails: enforceable youth-centered regulation, developmentally informed product design, and
    46→multidimensional AI literacy. Findings clarify a preliminary direction for future research and
    47→advocacy and emphasize that coordination among researchers and between sectors is needed.

    50→Introduction
    51→Artificial intelligence (AI) chatbots have rapidly become nearly ubiquitous among US
    52→youth. ChatGPT launched as a consumer product in 2022, marking a turning point in public
    53→generative AI access. Today, more than 2 in 3 U.S. teens report using AI chatbots (Faverio &
    54→Sidoti, 2025; O’Neil et al., 2026) and global adoption rates continue to climb (Microsoft, 2026).
    55→Many U.S. pre-teens ages 8–12 also access generative AI chatbot applications (Maheux, Akre-
    56→Bhide, et al., 2026; O’Neil et al., 2026). AI chatbots are defined broadly as systems using large
    57→language models and image-generation models trained on vast datasets of human-generated text
    58→and images to synthesize and predict coherent output, offering human-quality language synthesis
    59→and content generation (Ronge et al., 2025). As a scientific community, we know very little about
    60→how AI may influence adolescent development and the current literature remains slow to emerge,
    61→methodologically fragmented, and limited by inconsistent terminology (Maheux, Maes, et al.,
    62→2026). Parents, teachers, clinicians, policy-makers, and adolescents themselves urgently need
    63→guidance on the developmental impacts of AI use. Rigorous developmental science research is
    64→necessary to provide that guidance, but its value will depend on coordination within and across
    65→researchers studying youth development and AI use. This includes a shared language and
    66→taxonomy of AI use cases (i.e., specific ways a user can engage with AI), a proactive and
    67→synchronized research agenda, and a set of empirically grounded protections that society can
    68→implement with confidence while longer-term research is underway.
    69→The goal of the current study was to begin addressing this gap by systematically
    70→gathering expert input from social science researchers. The study has three main aims: (1) gather
    71→insight on AI use cases among adolescents to prioritize for future research, (2) identify research
    72→questions, methods, and barriers to research that can set the stage for the future study of
    73→
    74→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 2
    75→adolescent and AI, and (3) propose expert-supported guardrails to protect young people in the
    76→interim.
    77→Adolescent Development and AI
    78→Adolescence represents a developmental period marked by change in neurobiological,
    79→social, cognitive, and emotional systems. During this time, youth experience heightened
    80→sensitivity to social information and feedback, with peer relationships becoming increasingly
    81→salient, complex, and critical for skill-building (Dahl et al., 2018; Steinberg, 2014).
    82→Simultaneously, youth undergo heightened sensitivity to reward as their behavioral and cognitive
    83→impulse control develops, creating a developmental context wherein inhibiting reward-seeking
    84→can be especially challenging (Albert et al., 2013; Crone & Dahl, 2012; Somerville, 2013). Given
    85→this drive for social engagement, reward, and novelty, adolescents and young adults are often the
    86→earliest adopters of new technology (Giedd, 2020), and AI adoption is no different. AI use has
    87→skyrocketed among adolescents, with an estimated 86% of U.S. 9–17-year-olds now using AI,
    88→including 64–67% specifically using AI chatbots and 28% using chatbots every day (Faverio &
    89→Sidoti, 2025; O’Neil et al., 2026).
    90→AI chatbots introduce novel opportunities and potential risks to an already complicated
    91→technological landscape. Many of the affordances of AI, and the downstream benefits or costs,
    92→may be uniquely salient during the adolescent developmental period (Maheux & Maes, 2026). AI
    93→chatbot use is typically a solitary activity, yet can feel like interacting with a human, potentially
    94→activating unique adolescent drives for social engagement, acceptance, and intimacy. AI chatbots
    95→can respond contingently, retain memories, reflect affect, and be personalized in ways that make
    96→exchanges feel meaningful and intimate (Maheux & Maes, 2026). Designer-modulated features
    97→such as tone, persona, and communication style further shape the character of these interactions,
    98→
    99→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 3
   100→with current models tending towards sycophancy and agreeableness. Because chatbots are non-
   101→sentient, they require nothing of the user, tolerate everything, and pass no judgment. These
   102→qualities may make AI chatbots particularly attractive to adolescents, who are particularly
   103→sensitive to peer rejection, learning to navigate mutual peer relationships, and still developing
   104→executive function skills in rewarding or arousing social contexts (American Psychological
   105→Association, 2025; Hopelab et al., 2024).
   106→How youth use AI and the implications of such use remain poorly understood. A recent
   107→systematic review of peer-reviewed research on adolescents and AI use indicates an extremely
   108→thin evidence base dominated by research on AI in academics or seeking to understand factors
   109→that give rise to AI adoption among adolescents and young adults (Maheux, Maes, et al., 2026).
   110→Nationally representative polling data suggests common uses include to search for information,
   111→help with homework, for entertainment, and, for some youth, a simulated friend (Madden et al.,
   112→2024; McClain et al., 2026; Robb & Mann, 2025). AI chatbots may serve important scaffolding
   113→functions for adolescent social, emotional, and cognitive developmental milestones—for
   114→example, by supporting learning and comprehension or providing accurate advice on navigating
   115→challenging social contexts (American Psychological Association, 2025). However, AI chatbots
   116→may also undermine aspects of adolescent development in critical ways, including displacing
   117→human relationships, reducing skill-building by outsourcing cognitive or social tasks, and
   118→promoting harmful or dysfunctional beliefs about relationships (American Psychological
   119→Association, 2025; Chen et al., 2026; Maheux & Maes, 2026). Many stakeholders are concerned
   120→that, without proper guardrails—including a strong empirical base, developmental policies,
   121→design foundations, and interventions—AI chatbots will unfold as social media did: with little
   122→
   123→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 4
   124→regard for youth well-being and potential harms among vulnerable users, and a slow roll-out of
   125→policy-relevant research lagging behind technological innovations (Davis et al., 2025).
   126→The Current Study
   127→The field of research addressing the psychosocial impacts of adolescent AI use requires
   128→consensus and coordination to keep pace with the rapid pace of technological change. Research
   129→on adolescents and technology has historically been fragmented across disciplines, with
   130→psychologists, communication scholars, public health researchers, neuroscientists, and computer
   131→scientists often working in parallel rather than in dialogue (Davis et al., 2025). More broadly,
   132→researchers often remain separate from the sectors that design, govern, and implement emerging
   133→technologies, including industry, policymakers, educators, youth-serving organizations, and
   134→young people themselves. The challenges posed by AI will be best addressed by rigorous
   135→interdisciplinary research and sustained coordination between researchers and other sectors of
   136→society.
   137→Achieving provisional consensus on which AI use cases deserve research priority, which
   138→methodological approaches are most promising, and what barriers are impeding progress requires
   139→deliberate coordination among researchers. Moreover, researchers studying adolescents and
   140→technology already know much about ideal approaches for designing tools that support positive
   141→development. Thus, scientists can also offer guidance on what society should be doing now,
   142→based on existing evidence from adjacent literatures, while longer-term research is underway.
   143→Initial consensus across these domains can provide a roadmap for future scholarship and
   144→collaborative society-wide action to support adolescent well-being in the age of AI.
   145→The current project aims to proactively address these issues. We identified individuals
   146→working for research and advocacy organizations to provide expert insight and build consensus
   147→
   148→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 5
   149→on these critical issues, leveraging both a large-scale mixed-methods survey of professionals
   150→across multiple disciplines and structured interviews with a purposive sub-sample. The goals of
   151→the current study were threefold:
   152→Aim 1: Develop a set of AI use cases among adolescents to prioritize in future research.
   153→Aim 2: Establish a research agenda by identifying priority research questions and
   154→methodological approaches and documenting perceived barriers and solutions to progress in this
   155→field.
   156→Aim 3: Identify guardrails for society to consider implementing now, based on prior
   157→research on adolescents and technology use.
   158→Methods
   159→This study employed a convergent mixed-methods design (Creswell & Clark, 2017) to
   160→characterize the current landscape of research at the intersection of adolescents and AI.
   161→Quantitative and qualitative data were collected from researchers and practitioners via two
   162→complementary methods: (1) an online survey incorporating both quantitative and open-ended
   163→responses, and (2) semi-structured interviews with a purposive subsample of survey respondents.
   164→The two data streams were analyzed separately and then integrated at the interpretation stage to
   165→produce a comprehensive account of expert perspectives on AI use case prioritization, research
   166→agenda-setting and barriers to progress, and actionable guardrails. All research activities were
   167→reviewed and considered exempt by the university Institutional Review Board (Protocol #25-
   168→2587).

   174→research laboratory. Each student independently generated a list of 15 AI use cases that they
   175→believed were both common among their peers and younger teens and that were particularly
   176→important for researchers to study. This group then met with the research team to discuss the
   177→combined responses and individually rank use cases in order of importance, after which the first
   178→author synthesized and refined the list by consolidating overlapping items and clarifying

   181→4) and via an emailed survey (n = 12), whose feedback informed further revisions to improve

   184→undergraduate advisors ranked use cases on importance again.

   257→Survey Measures
   258→Respondents were asked to assess the perceived importance of studying 20 AI use cases
   259→among adolescents, each rated on a 4-point scale from 1 (not important to study) to 4 (extremely
   260→important to study). For a list of all use cases presented to respondents, see Table 1. All
   261→participants were invited to nominate AI use cases warranting additional research attention with
   262→one open-ended question, as well as any they considered "red herrings" unlikely to yield
   263→meaningful insight. Participants were also invited to share AI use case priorities for marginalized
   264→youth—specifically LGBTQ+ youth and Black and Latine youth —given that these groups often
   265→face both unique opportunities and challenges online and report higher rates of technology use
   266→(Faverio & Sidoti, 2025; Hopelab et al., 2024).
   267→Next, active researchers completed open-ended items asking them to identify the highest-
   268→priority research questions at the intersection of youth and AI and the most promising
   269→methodological approaches for addressing those questions. Barriers to research were assessed
   270→

   272→among active researchers using a checklist of 12 items with an open-ended option to describe
   273→additional barriers not captured by the checklist.
   274→Finally, respondents indicated guardrails that society can implement now based on known
   275→research on youth development and technology. Participants selected their top five priority
   276→guardrails from a checklist of 12 items. A final open-ended item invited participants to elaborate
   277→on how they hope society will implement guardrails to ensure a positive future for young people
   278→who use AI.
   279→Interview Protocol
   280→The semi-structured interview guide was organized around the aims of the study and (for
   281→all but one participant) were conducted following the participants’ completion of the survey.
   282→Participants were first asked to elaborate on their ratings of AI use case importance and to reflect
   283→on how use cases might be conceptually organized or prioritized. They then discussed the
   284→research questions they viewed as most critical and the methodological approaches best suited to
   285→address them. Participants then discussed the barriers they perceived as most consequential.
   286→Interview flow emphasized respondents’ expertise (e.g., neuroscientists were asked to comment
   287→on the adolescent brain and implications for AI use, participants with industry experience were
   288→asked to comment on the potential role of industry-academic partnerships in addressing
   289→limitations around data access and research infrastructure). Finally, participants were asked about
   290→guardrails that could leverage existing research on youth and other technologies. Probes were
   291→used throughout to elicit concrete examples, explore points of tension or uncertainty, and provide
   292→space for additional detail.
   293→Analytic Approach
   294→

   296→Descriptive statistics were computed for all quantitative survey items. Means and
   297→standard deviations were calculated for each AI use case importance rating, barriers, and
   298→guardrails and items were rank ordered to identify areas of greatest consensus among
   299→respondents.
   300→Open-ended survey responses and interview transcripts were analyzed using reflexive
   301→thematic analysis. A coding scheme was developed deductively, organized around the three study
   302→aims, with codes refined inductively as responses were reviewed. This unified approach was
   303→applied across both data sources, with interview data treated as elaborating and contextualizing
   304→the patterns identified in the open-ended survey responses rather than as an independent data
   305→stream. Four members of the research team independently reviewed all responses and transcripts.
   306→The coding team met several times to discuss themes, revisit data, and identify key patterns.
   307→Discrepancies or disagreement in key themes were resolved through iterative discussion and
   308→consensus.
   309→Quantitative and qualitative findings were analyzed separately and then integrated at the
   310→interpretation stage using a merging strategy (Creswell & Clark, 2017). For each of the three
   311→study aims, descriptive survey findings and qualitative themes were brought together to produce
   312→a unified account of researcher and practitioner perspectives. Quotes from interviews and open-
   313→ended survey responses are used throughout the results to illustrate and contextualize the
   314→quantitative patterns.

   322→Results and Integrated Discussion
   323→Aim 1: AI Use Cases
   324→The first goal of the project was to gauge researchers’ perceptions of the relative
   325→importance of different AI use cases. An overview of the aims and themes for all aims is
   326→presented in Table 2. Within the qualitative data, participants were aligned that "screen time" or
   327→vague and decontextualized AI use measures should be avoided and replaced with emphasis on
   328→specific ways youth use AI, mirroring similar calls in research studying other technology use
   329→among youth (Maheux et al., 2025; Valkenburg, 2022). One researcher argued that “generic
   330→categories around what’s driving use (e.g., entertainment, social connection)… are not telling us
   331→the full story about use cases, what's motivating engagement, and what might lead to either over-
   332→reliance or not” (survey, researcher at a non-profit, USA).
   333→Participants rated the importance of studying 20 AI use cases among adolescents. Mean
   334→importance ratings ranged from 2.61 to 3.85 on a 4-point scale (see Table 1 and Figure 1). Five
   335→main themes emerged from both quantitative ratings and qualitative responses regarding how use
   336→cases should be prioritized and conceptualized, which we synthesize below.
   337→Theme 1: Study Everything, but Triage for Severe Cases
   338→Participants broadly endorsed the importance of studying AI use across contexts. For all
   339→20 use cases, a majority of respondents (51–96%, depending on the use case) rated them as either
   340→Important or Extremely important to study. Researchers commonly emphasized the need to be
   341→comprehensive, “I think everything should be studied, we know so little that any information
   342→would help” (survey, student advocate, USA).
   343→
   344→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 13
   345→However, quantitative and qualitative responses also revealed a triage logic: the most
   346→severe, irreversible, and immediate individual-level harms should be considered most urgent,
   347→with many arguing that “it is critical to prioritize research that could be life-saving” (survey,
   348→graduate student, USA). These included AI providing dangerous health information related to
   349→suicide, self-harm, or disordered eating; AI used as a tool for cyberbullying or harassment,
   350→including AI-generated sexual abuse material and deepfakes; and AI used for extortion or
   351→manipulation. Additionally, for marginalized youth—both Black and Latin/a/o/e and LGBTQ+
   352→populations—AI for mental health, identity, and emotional support was ranked as the top priority
   353→use case (of participants responding to these items, 94.6% [n = 105] endorsed for LGBTQ+,
   354→92.8% [n = 103] endorsed for youth of color).
   355→Notably, a subset of participants also suggested that because so little is known, and
   356→because the most immediate and acute issues may overshadow the importance of studying more
   357→subtle changes, all areas of study must be considered and addressed simultaneously. This tension
   358→reflects a broader challenge in rapid-response AI research: how to prioritize attention across risks
   359→that vary in both prevalence and severity (Saeri et al., 2026). Individual-level acute mental health
   360→outcomes, such as AI-facilitated suicide, receive disproportionate media attention (Hill, 2025),
   361→yet the prevalence of such issues remains unknown, and is likely low (Archiwaranguprok et al.,
   362→2025). Additionally, industry actors are already investing effort in identifying and mitigating
   363→acute safety risks (OpenAI, 2026), yet may have fewer incentives to focus on long-term, less
   364→sensational, or population-level risks. Focusing exclusively on acute, life-threatening harms risks
   365→overlooking a distinct category of concerns that may be less visible but nonetheless potentially
   366→consequential developmentally, including subtle effects on social development, emotional
   367→regulation, judgement, executive functioning, and emerging social norms during adolescence.
   368→
   369→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 14
   370→The case for prioritizing the most severe harms may be largely based on assumptions of
   371→limited resources, yet a narrow focus risks leaving important gaps in the evidence base that may
   372→be important for subtle, long-term, population-level changes. As one researcher put it: “in an
   373→ideal world we would have the space and the time to study everything, and design these tools to
   374→better our interactions with one another and our views towards ourselves as humans” (survey,
   375→graduate student, USA). Coordination among research teams for future AI research could ensure
   376→that all AI use cases are collectively addressed.
   377→A few participants raised a related pragmatic concern: use cases with little commercial
   378→viability may not warrant research investment, as market forces are unlikely to promote them at
   379→scale. This observation points to a broader question that may itself merit study—how commercial
   380→incentives are shaping the trajectory of AI development and, by extension, the landscape of
   381→potential AI use cases researchers should anticipate.
   382→Theme 2: AI Literacy as a Foundational and Multidimensional Skill
   383→Participants rated AI literacy as among the most important use case to study (M = 3.69,
   384→SD = 0.56). Much prior work on AI literacy has focused on workplace readiness and using AI
   385→effectively (Casal-Otero et al., 2023; Wang & Lester, 2023). However, qualitative responses
   386→consistently emphasized the multidimensionality of AI literacy—encompassing more than
   387→simply how to use AI. For example, AI literacy should include knowledge of privacy and digital
   388→footprints; critically evaluating AI-generated content especially when shared through peer
   389→networks or when content reflects biases; navigating school and institutional AI policies;
   390→developing skills of using AI in ways that support—vs. detract from—learning, relationships,
   391→and human thriving; and complex effects on labor markets, environmental costs, and intellectual
   392→
   393→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 15
   394→property. These responses reflect the extensive scope of what informed AI use requires of young
   395→people, and how researchers should conceptualize and study it accordingly.
   396→Theme 3: AI as a Social and Relational Actor
   397→Many AI interactions involve simulated sociality, and researchers largely agreed that this
   398→use case must be studied further. Use cases including companion relationships with an AI (M =
   399→3.65, SD = 0.58), romantic relationships with an AI (M = 3.43, SD = 0.80), how social skill
   400→development intersects with AI use (i.e., using AI to resolve interpersonal conflicts; seek
   401→guidance on friendships, dating, or family relationships; or practice social interactions; M = 3.36,
   402→SD = 0.71), and sexual interactions with an AI (M = 3.29, SD = 0.84) were all rated as highly
   403→important to study.
   404→Current design choices and affordances of AI chatbots were frequently cited as key to
   405→conceptualizing this use case, including the frictionless availability, responsivity, and current
   406→design choices prioritizing sycophancy in AI responses (Maheux & Maes, 2026; Raedler et al.,
   407→2025). For example, one interviewee shared: "Technology is at everyone’s fingertips, including
   408→all these kids, and if you have your best friend in your pocket or something that will constantly
   409→validate you, tell you you’re right, promote your ideas back to you and all that stuff in your
   410→pocket, then what’s your need for…over exerting yourself to make these connections in real life
   411→that seem like way too much of a barrier to overcome” (interview, graduate student, USA).
   412→Researchers emphasized that how young people conceptualize their AI chatbots—as a
   413→tool vs. a social agent with a mind, intentions, or feelings—likely shapes how those interactions
   414→affect development. Although some work has addressed AI mind perception among children and
   415→adults (S. Lee et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2025), research is needed among adolescents—a unique
   416→population actively developing mature social cognition skills in increasingly complex social
   417→
   418→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 16
   419→contexts (Dahl et al., 2018). Related to this, some researchers emphasized the "super peer"
   420→framing, a term coined to describe how prior media technologies can influence adolescent
   421→behaviors and expectations (Brown et al., 2005; Elmore et al., 2017). In the context of AI,
   422→chatbots may be seen as credible, always-available peers or role models that can shape
   423→adolescents’ understandings of norms and culture. This may have important outcomes in cases
   424→where adolescents view the chatbot as a friend or source of advice or health information.
   425→Participants also highlighted that AI use is not only an individual behavior but a socially
   426→negotiated one. Researchers noted that social norms around AI use are actively forming within
   427→peer microsystems (Center for Digital Thriving, 2025), and that these norms may exert influence
   428→on AI use that parallels the peer socialization dynamics already well documented in the
   429→technology and adolescent development literature (Navarro & Tudge, 2023). Social influences
   430→from parents may also be key to study, and participants indicated that how parenting intersects
   431→with AI use is critical to study (M = 3.42, SD = 0.73). Recent polling evidence suggests that
   432→adolescents currently report almost no guidance from parents on how to navigate the AI
   433→ecosystem safely (O’Neil et al., 2026)
   434→Theme 4: Information, Agency, and Identity in the AI Era
   435→Participants identified the importance of studying AI in adolescents’ information
   436→ecosystem—including how they seek information with AI, how they understand false AI-
   437→generated content, and how their own information may be weaponized against them. In the
   438→quantitative data, exposure to or creation of AI-generated misinformation, including hate speech,
   439→propaganda, or political media (M = 3.68, SD = 0.60), using AI to access medical and healthcare
   440→related information or advice (M = 3.68, SD = 0.60), and privacy concerns (i.e., users disclosing
   441→personal information into chatbots with unclear or misaligned [e.g., profit-driven] data use or
   442→
   443→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 17
   444→storage security policies; M = 3.60, SD = 0.63) were among the highest priority use cases.
   445→Several qualitative comments specifically mentioned deepfakes (i.e., AI-generated imagery of
   446→known others) and deepnudes (i.e., AI-generated sexual imagery of others) as key challenges in
   447→need of future study. Recent high-profile events involving AI chatbots ‘undressing’ images of
   448→people have disproportionately affected women and children (Conger et al., 2026), yet despite
   449→preliminary commentary from scholars on how to address such issues among adolescents
   450→(Alexander, 2025), systematic research on adolescents’ use of and experience with deepfakes and
   451→deepnudes remains lacking.
   452→AI interactions or exposure to misinformation may have subtle influences on self-
   453→concept, sense of agency, or orientation towards truth. One participant shared: “the
   454→sophistication of those tools [is] making it much, much harder for us to tell the difference
   455→between what is real, what is true, what has human origin, and what comes from an AI. So, there
   456→are new vulnerabilities that that creates. I'm very worried about our adolescents’ ability to
   457→understand fact…to even value fact” (interview, faculty researcher, USA).
   458→This theme was seen as particularly salient for marginalized youth, who may be
   459→disproportionately negatively impacted by biases in AI training data and more likely to use AI for
   460→identity exploration. Quantitative data suggested that access to health information, including
   461→physical, mental, and sexual (91.0%, n = 101 for LGBTQ+ youth; 94.6%, n =105 for youth of
   462→color), and exposure to racial/cultural biases for youth of color (85.6%, n = 95) and
   463→homophobic/transphobic biases for LGBTQ+ youth (85.6%, n = 95) are among the most
   464→important priorities to study for these groups. Many survey and interview respondents brought up
   465→AI biases, which have received significant attention in computational fields (Beattie et al., 2022;
   466→Iloanusi & Chun, 2024). One respondent said: “Because of the data sets it's trained on, and the
   467→
   468→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 18
   469→language it's trained on, and the cultural values it's trained on, there's a lot of little
   470→microaggressions that can happen that go under the radar” (interview, faculty researcher,
   471→Canada). AI may distort identity exploration by reflecting idealized or biased representations of
   472→identity rather than supporting authentic self-development conducted within social contexts.
   473→Critically, AI biases are not inherent in AI systems, but rather reflect the training data, “These
   474→disparities exist in digital spaces because the models are created by humans” (survey, clinician
   475→and faculty researcher, USA).
   476→However, adolescents need spaces to engage in identity exploration, and many already
   477→use technology for this purpose (Choukas-Bradley et al., 2023; Common Sense Media, 2024).
   478→For example, sexual and gender minority adolescents may be navigating identity exploration in
   479→contexts where in-person support may be limited; in such contexts, AI could serve as a relatively
   480→private or safe space for exploration. One respondent noted that “Kids are resourceful and will
   481→find the information they need, which makes it even more important to ensure that AI-enabled
   482→spaces are designed for accuracy and support for marginalized youth” (survey, advocate, USA).
   483→Thus, identifying how marginalized youth engage with AI-generated information for these
   484→purposes, and how such motivations intersect with chatbot design and adolescent developmental
   485→outcomes, are key for future research.
   486→Theme 5: The Risk of Overreliance and Cognitive Offloading
   487→Participants consistently flagged overreliance on AI and cognitive offloading to AI as a
   488→priority concern. When AI handles cognitively demanding tasks, young people may not develop
   489→the skills those tasks were meant to build (Zhai et al., 2024). Overreliance can also emerge in
   490→psychosocial contexts, including excessively relying on AI to be a social companion, provide
   491→advice, or regulate emotions. How exactly such an AI use case will be defined remains unclear,
   492→
   493→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 19
   494→and is an area that will benefit from and likely require collaborations among developmental
   495→scientists, technologists, and young people using these tools themselves. Researchers are broadly
   496→aligned that measures of quantity or frequency of use alone are unlikely to capture such a
   497→construct: “I think [studying] overreliance on AI (not just use, but excessive use) will be
   498→important” (survey, graduate student, USA).
   499→Aim 2: Research Priorities & Opportunities
   500→The second aim of the project was to begin curating a set of priority research questions,
   501→methods, barriers, and solutions. Below, we provide an overview of four themes specific to
   502→theoretical foundations and research orientation, research questions and methods, research
   503→barriers, and solutions.
   504→Theme 1: Theoretical Foundations and Research Orientation
   505→Researchers offered several overarching points that provide insight to the field as it
   506→adjusts its orientation towards the challenges of studying adolescents and AI. First, researchers
   507→agreed that a developmental lens must be applied to research on this topic—a point made in
   508→numerous survey responses and interview comments by researchers. For example, one
   509→commented, “Adolescence is a uniquely vulnerable period - not because teens are "broken," but
   510→because they're more sensitive to social feedback and rewards; Persuasive design exploits
   511→developmental traits - features like infinite scroll and "likes" specifically target how teen brains
   512→respond…Age-appropriate design isn't optional - AI for teens needs different safeguards than AI
   513→for adults” (survey, graduate student, USA). The core features of adolescence mentioned most
   514→included changes in social context and salience of peers, neurobiological development related to
   515→reward sensitivity and executive function, and identity development.
   516→
   517→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 20
   518→However, participants were not aligned on the level of urgency or concern to bring to AI-
   519→related research. Several cautioned against moral panic and reflexive negativity about AI, noting
   520→that assuming all use is harmful is a methodological and framing error akin to similar responses
   521→to social media (Orben, 2020), and researchers should instead take a balanced approach to
   522→considering both potential benefits and harms of AI. However, other respondents reported
   523→existential concerns about the nature of AI for adolescents. For example, one noted “I think that
   524→in general, kids' use of AI platforms is a brewing and very, very underappreciated and
   525→underrecognized public health problem...I've never felt in my 35 years of doing clinical research
   526→the level of urgency that I have now on needing to understand and really needing to make the
   527→public and parents aware" (interview, industry, USA). Thus, the field may not align on how to
   528→think about adolescence and AI as we study it; this may be a strength—optimistic and pessimistic
   529→viewpoints may collectively move us towards a deeper, more nuanced understanding and critical
   530→insights built on specificity rather than generalization.
   531→Third, a central conceptual tension emerged regarding whether AI indirectly or directly
   532→impacts developmental processes. The former references the displacement hypothesis, which
   533→argues that technology use may have negative impacts when it displaces healthy activities like
   534→sleep, exercise, and time socializing (Exelmans, 2020; Valkenburg & Peter, 2007). For example,
   535→one noted, “I am deeply concerned about the ways in which AI is being used socially - esp. the
   536→ways in which it displaces real human relationships. I think the outcomes are existential in
   537→nature” (survey, advocate, USA). Alternatively, direct impacts may involve AI companions
   538→undermining (or stimulating) human connection through changes in social skills and expectations
   539→(Ibrahim et al., 2026; Smith et al., 2025). Researchers advocated for keeping the developmental
   540→
   541→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 21
   542→function as the primary unit of analysis, considering how AI supports or undermines such tasks
   543→and processes, rather than considering AI use cases as a starting point.
   544→Lastly, researchers overwhelmingly emphasized that individual differences among
   545→adolescents will change how they use and respond to AI. This idea reflects the differential
   546→susceptibility to media effects model, which argues that dispositional, social, and developmental
   547→factors alter media effects (Valkenburg & Peter, 2013). Research on social media has broadly
   548→suggested a similar pattern (Maheux et al., 2025). Differential susceptibility was especially
   549→emphasized in the context of marginalized youth. Social media research emphasizes that
   550→marginalized youth may experience a “double-edged sword” wherein the benefits of online
   551→environments can compensate for offline isolation or discrimination, but harms may also be
   552→exacerbated (Choukas-Bradley et al., 2023; Common Sense Media, 2024). Adolescents
   553→experiencing discrimination, marginalization, and/or structural inequalities and resource
   554→limitations may experience both greater benefits (e.g., AI tutors mitigating educational
   555→inequities) but also greater vulnerability related to AI harms, as some preliminary work suggests
   556→(A. Lee & Culver, 2026). One researcher noted a worry that “companies are exploiting structural
   557→inequalities and in turn amplifying vulnerability (e.g. police profiling) and disempowering
   558→communities (e.g. cutting access to services)” (survey, faculty researcher, USA). Researchers
   559→also emphasized that effective AI literacy programs and supportive AI design cannot be applied
   560→to marginalized communities from the outside but must be developed in active partnership with
   561→them.
   562→Theme 2: Priority Research Questions and Methods
   563→Participants offered a range of open-ended responses identifying priority research
   564→questions. These spanned multiple dimensions: the types of AI use cases and outcome variables
   565→
   566→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 22
   567→that warrant investigation; how study design and methodology can best support this work; which
   568→AI design features should be tested or manipulated in experimental or co-design studies; and
   569→specific questions at the intersection of these key themes, measures, and methods. For instance,
   570→some researchers emphasized foundational prevalence questions—such as how many adolescents
   571→are engaging with AI across different use cases (which national polling has been able to address
   572→more quickly than traditional peer-reviewed scholarship; McClain et al., 2026; O’Neil et al.,
   573→2026). Others stressed the importance of linking specific use cases to developmental outcomes
   574→(e.g., executive functioning, social skill development) through longitudinal or causal designs.
   575→Several questions focused on individual-level processes, such as how adolescents interpret AI-
   576→generated content or respond to expressions of uncertainty and prompts to seek human support.
   577→Others took a population-level view, asking how AI-driven shifts in epistemic norms, social
   578→norms, and information environments may unfold at scale. Participants also identified outcomes
   579→that are likely to be subtle or slow-emerging—including changes in perceived agency, beliefs
   580→about human exceptionalism, and capacity for relational reciprocity—as important targets that
   581→may be missed when focusing only on severe mental health impacts. Taken together, these
   582→responses reflect no clear consensus on priority questions, underscoring the breadth of the field's
   583→empirical needs and the degree to which foundational work remains to be done across virtually
   584→all domains.
   585→Methodologically, researchers collectively argue for rigorous methodological pluralism,
   586→with many specifically noting the importance of mixed and multiple methods. Nearly all the
   587→major methods in developmental science were mentioned by at least one participant. Several
   588→mentioned large-scale surveys and population-based cohort studies are necessary for
   589→understanding prevalence and generalizability of various AI use cases. However, the rapidly
   590→
   591→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 23
   592→changing nature of AI technology, and the speed with which adolescent norms and use patterns
   593→are evolving, makes qualitative and participatory methods not merely supplementary but
   594→essential. Additionally, researchers argued for rigorous methods to capture digital behavior. AI
   595→conversational logs, passive sensing of interaction patterns, and natural language processing of
   596→chatbot exchanges offer a level of behavioral precision not available via self-report. Paired with
   597→ecological momentary assessment (EMA), these methods offer a path toward understanding
   598→specific interaction patterns—including what the user does and how the model responds—and
   599→their momentary effects.
   600→Longitudinal and experimental designs—critical for suggesting causality and for
   601→informing policy—were frequently mentioned, especially in the context of common
   602→overinterpretations of cross-sectional data. The possibility that AI effects diverge across short
   603→and long timescales (e.g., alleviating immediate loneliness or distress but exacerbating isolation
   604→or distress intolerance over time) further underscores the value of longitudinal designs (including
   605→panel designs, EMA, burst designs, and N-of-1 designs) capable of tracking individual
   606→trajectories over time. Evaluation of educational or literacy and co-designed interventions was
   607→identified as another urgent priority, especially for informing policy and opportunities to rapidly
   608→support adolescents in changing AI contexts. Experiments can also directly test how design
   609→choices influence users’ responses to chatbots. When paired with neuroimaging methods, such
   610→approaches can provide insights into neural mechanisms that drive user behavior and outcomes.
   611→In sum, no consensus on priority questions or methods emerged. To synthesize
   612→researchers’ methodological suggestions would be functionally to describe best practices across
   613→all interdisciplinary fields studying adolescents in digital contexts. The breadth of suggestions
   614→across participants signals that this phenomenon may be too multifaceted to be captured by any
   615→
   616→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 24
   617→one subfield, and progress may depend on deploying methods in combination. Triangulation—
   618→the use of multiple methods to establish convergent validity—is a cornerstone of social science
   619→research, ensuring that findings reflect the construct of interest rather than an artifact of any
   620→single method (Jick, 1979). A broad triangulation approach—across disciplines and research
   621→teams working in dialogue—may be the best approach for this field.
   622→Theme 4: Barriers to Research
   623→Participants identified several barriers to conducting rigorous research at the intersection
   624→of adolescents and AI and completed a 12-item checklist indicating the biggest barriers to
   625→research progress. Of the respondents who conduct research (other professionals were not shown
   626→the research barriers questions), most filled in at least one question on barriers (n = 114,
   627→89.76%), with quantitative data presented in Figure 2.
   628→Several barriers reflected the scale, novelty, and pace of the problem. Overwhelmingly,
   629→the most cited barrier in the quantitative data was “Rapid speed of change in AI systems and use
   630→cases (relative to time required to conduct high-quality research),” which was endorsed by 76%
   631→(n = 87). One researcher stated that the problem is: “How quickly AI changes and slow speed of
   632→publication process. It’s already outdated and still under review” (survey, faculty researcher,
   633→USA). Findings risk obsolescence before publication, and longitudinal designs face the problem
   634→of studying a moving target. This challenge is exacerbated given the opacity of how AI systems
   635→operate, and thus how design choices may influence adolescent behavior. A commonly cited
   636→barrier by 56% (n = 64) in the quantitative data was the lack of transparency on how AI
   637→platforms and AI models operate. Additionally, some participants described a form of epistemic
   638→overwhelm resulting from this pace and scale of change: "I feel utterly bewildered by where to
   639→start in a way that I haven't with any other research area. This feels enormous and existential"
   640→
   641→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 25
   642→(survey, faculty researcher, USA). Because of this speed and pressure, participants also noted a
   643→mismatch between the volume of interest in AI research and the availability of theory-driven
   644→hypotheses to anchor it, creating risk of poorly designed studies accumulating without building
   645→cumulatively.
   646→Structural barriers were equally prominent. Funding remains scarce, slow, and
   647→misaligned. Many researchers noted that lack of funding (n = 59, 51.7%) and lengthy funding
   648→application processes (n = 46, 40.36%) were key barriers. Some commented in qualitative data
   649→that most resources flow toward AI development rather than toward understanding its human
   650→consequences, and research on marginalized youth is especially underfunded. Participant
   651→recruitment for research involving minors is logistically demanding and costly, especially
   652→recruiting representative or hard-to-reach samples, and requires significant resources to invest in
   653→developing partnerships with schools and communities. Ethical review processes add further
   654→constraints, and many researchers (n = 30, 26.31%) noted IRB review challenges as a major
   655→barrier to conducting their work.
   656→Community-based work—wherein the researcher partners with community members to
   657→understand and support community needs—can be especially demanding, yet more impactful
   658→than developing interventions without careful evaluation. One researcher commented on the
   659→resources needed for this type of hands-on work with adolescents: “Doing research where kids
   660→are involved from day one is way harder than going to build some tool or some learning system
   661→and toss it out into the world and then maybe evaluate it…It's a huge time commitment. You need
   662→to fund the [graduate] students. I like to give money to the school. I have to buy myself out of
   663→teaching. So funding is always an issue, especially for a new field” (interview, faculty researcher,
   664→Canada).
   665→
   666→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 26
   667→The second most-cited barrier was the limited access to data or industry partnerships (n =
   668→65, 57.02%). Technology companies and hardware providers possess vast quantities of
   669→behavioral data that could substantially advance scientific understanding if made available
   670→through research partnerships, APIs, or participant-mediated data donation. However, data access
   671→and industry partnerships remain constrained by the defensive posture of large technology
   672→companies, “the company doesn't want you to be able to make any claims about people being
   673→depressed or not having a good time on their platform, and so they're just not gonna release the
   674→data” (interview, graduate student, USA). Researchers also cited, to a lesser degree, the lack of
   675→interdisciplinary collaborators (n = 27, 23.68%) and lack of researcher training in relevant
   676→methods (n = 26, 22.81%) as barriers. Although less frequently cited, strengthening expertise in
   677→these areas could help researchers develop alternative computational approaches that reduce
   678→reliance on direct industry partnerships and proprietary data access.
   679→Barriers specific to academic contexts were also cited as challenges. Researchers
   680→identified misaligned academic incentive structures (n = 50, 43.86%) as a prominent barrier.
   681→These include university expectations and granting agency values that sometimes disincentive
   682→high-risk research, encourage quantity of publications over quality, or prioritize independence
   683→over collaboration. More broadly, some researchers noted that the research ecosystem also
   684→threatens the quality of the emerging evidence base, including variable standards of rigor and
   685→publication bias that favors significant effects and insufficiently considers null results.
   686→Theme 5: Solutions: Coordination and Partnerships
   687→Researchers pointed to several solutions to the barriers to high-quality, rapid-response
   688→research. First, researchers were consistent that the scale and pace of AI development requires a
   689→coordinated scientific response. Fragmented individual efforts, however rigorous, will not be
   690→
   691→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 27
   692→sufficient. To support and improve the future of science on this topic, participants called for a
   693→coordinated scientific response that exceeds the capacity of individual research labs. The need is
   694→not simply for more studies but for field-level infrastructure: shared measurement standards, data
   695→repositories, interdisciplinary consortia, and multi-site cohort studies. Industry-academic
   696→partnerships, approached carefully and with appropriate transparency protections, could provide
   697→data access that individual labs cannot obtain independently.
   698→This infrastructure vision extends to research partnerships with teens, families, schools,
   699→and communities. Across all three aims, a consistent value emerged: adolescents should not be
   700→merely the subjects of research but active participants in shaping its questions, methods, and
   701→applications. This appeared as a methodological argument—participatory and co-design
   702→approaches yield more relevant research questions and more ecologically valid measurements—
   703→as well as an ethical one, grounded in the view that adolescents have a right to involvement in
   704→decisions that affect them. As one participant observed: "Youth are the experts of their own
   705→experiences, also often equally as confused/excited/scared about the future of technology as the
   706→adults in their lives" (survey, faculty researcher, USA).
   707→Several interview participants emphasized that the most pressing barrier may not be a
   708→research problem at all, but rather coordination and translation of knowledge across sectors. For
   709→example, one participant commented that the challenge may be ensuring that evidence informs
   710→public decision-making, "I actually think the issue here is one of communications and policy not
   711→so much the science parts... We need help from the political scientists on this one, because just
   712→being right on the science has not been enough to get the policy makers to listen" (survey, faculty
   713→researcher, USA). Others similarly highlighted the need to rebuild public trust in scientific
   714→expertise and orient scientific efforts toward evaluating and responding to rapidly evolving
   715→
   716→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 28
   717→technologies and policies, rather than focusing exclusively on basic research that tends to be
   718→more reactive. One commented, "I think the notion of 'scientific expertise' has been eroded in
   719→recent years, but it seems we need to convene credible, independent scientific experts through
   720→National Academies (or some body) to assemble a list of high-quality benchmarks for evaluating
   721→AI chatbots, to propose safety and quality standards, and to build a tracking system for
   722→monitoring effects of AI use on youth social/emotional wellbeing" (survey, faculty researcher,
   723→USA). These comments reframe the challenge not only as conducting science, but also as science
   724→communication and cross-sector collaboration as critical for both dissemination and ensuring
   725→research is designed to be maximally relevant to society. Engaging directly with policymakers,
   726→journalists, and communities may be central, rather than peripheral, to the research mission in a
   727→context where developmental impacts are fast-moving and potentially high stakes.
   728→Aim 3: Guardrails — What Society Can Do Now
   729→Participants selected their top five priority guardrails from a checklist of 12 items (see
   730→Figure 3). Most (n = 124, 87.9%) filled in at least one of these items. The most frequently
   731→endorsed were (1) “Effective built-in safeguards against harmful AI outputs (harassment,
   732→hallucinations, self-harm promotion, etc.)” (n = 97, 78.2%), (2) “Government regulation of AI
   733→companies requiring AI models to prioritize youth well-being” (n = 94, 75.8%), and (3) “AI
   734→literacy training in schools” (n = 91, 73.4%). Open-ended responses elaborated on these three
   735→overarching directions: regulation, design, and literacy.
   736→Theme 1: Regulation: Necessary, Urgent, and Politically Complex
   737→Participants were consistent that voluntary industry action is insufficient and that
   738→regulation is necessary. The analogy to prior historical changes—the “Big Tobacco” history and
   739→pharmaceutical development—was invoked: "AI feels like drug discovery without clinical trials!
   740→
   741→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 29
   742→Just releasing new products into the wild without study of benefits/harms/side effects" (survey,
   743→faculty researcher, Canada). The comparison to social media was equally prominent, with
   744→participants expressing concern that history is repeating itself: "Given how little care tech
   745→companies have shown for developing brains in the realm of social media, it seems absurd to
   746→expect any meaningful initiatives from tech companies when it comes to protecting teens"
   747→(survey, faculty researcher, USA).
   748→Specific regulatory priorities named by participants included strengthening data privacy
   749→protections for minors; banning targeted advertising to minors; requiring transparency about
   750→model training, algorithms, and data use; mandating safety testing before deployment to
   751→adolescent populations; and establishing independent auditing against established benchmarks.
   752→Participants described effective regulation as necessarily careful and design-focused rather than
   753→reactive: "Ideally, society will do forward looking, design focused legislation in service of
   754→healthy child development (as opposed to not regulating at all, or to letting new techs be
   755→deployed and cause harm, and THEN thinking about guardrails)" (survey, faculty researcher,
   756→USA). Notably, several participants emphasized the importance of using legislation to
   757→incentivize or mandate safe design (described below).
   758→Many cautioned as to ways legislation can go awry, arguing often that legislation
   759→involving outright bans or that penalizes the child was unlikely to be successful. In some cases,
   760→participants argued that regulation targets the wrong culprit. For example, one interviewee
   761→argued that regulating therapy bots—which are often designed with clinicians and
   762→professionals—will be less effective than regulating the general-purpose models that most users
   763→access: “Most state bills have limited the bots that are trying harder as opposed to limiting the
   764→general purpose bots. We're limiting the things that might do some good while they're a
   765→
   766→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 30
   767→minuscule part of the pie. Most of the issues are coming from these general purpose bots. So
   768→there are a lot of ways that you can fail with public policy, and I think that's a potential risk”
   769→(interview, graduate student, USA).
   770→Bans often also fail to balance potential harms against benefits of AI systems. With
   771→careful design and oversight, AI-enabled interactions might serve beneficial functions in
   772→supporting positive youth developmental. Such applications of the technology could be eroded
   773→without careful legislation and could be amplified with appropriate intervention: “We also need
   774→to look at the best case scenarios. With every horribly tragic case we read about the transcripts,
   775→how many thousands of kids were walked off a ledge? We don't know…We're doing ourselves an
   776→ethical disservice if we say…we should shut down all mental health conversation on an LLM. I
   777→think…yes, they need to be fixed and let's make sure we really understand and study what is
   778→working and how people are using these things naturally” (interview, industry, USA). Indeed,
   779→one community sample of young adult Replika users found that 3% reported the chatbot halted
   780→their suicidal ideation (Maples et al., 2024). Systematic research on the optimal design for such
   781→benefits—and the design features that undermine potential benefits—must inform regulation.
   782→Participants were equally candid about structural obstacles. Trust in institutions was
   783→identified as a prerequisite for effective regulation. One participant noted that in their own
   784→research, they find that respondents infrequently express "trust in local, state, or federal
   785→government, or in Big Tech companies, to make decisions about children and AI" (survey,
   786→research scientist, USA). Some participants flagged political funding of legislators by the
   787→technology industry as a major obstacle and many expressed skepticism in governmental
   788→regulation. Notably, most participants (81%) in the sample were from the United States, where
   789→regulation has lagged behind other jurisdictions (e.g., European Union; EU AI Act, 2024). Thus,
   790→
   791→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 31
   792→although most participants were in favor of regulations and policies promoting safe AI design,
   793→the skepticism of this coming to fruition may not be generalizable beyond the USA.
   794→Theme 2: Design as the Primary Lever
   795→Across survey and interview data, design was identified as the most powerful and
   796→underutilized potential guardrail. A exhaustive list of safety-focused design features is beyond
   797→the scope of this paper, though many of the design features enumerated elsewhere were
   798→mentioned by participants (5Rights, 2025; Collins Oguine et al., 2026; OECD, 2024; Unicef,
   799→2025). For example, participants mentioned that users must always know they are interacting
   800→with an AI system and know how their data is being collected, used, and retained, with full rights
   801→to data portability and deletion, and 47.6% (n = 59) of respondents who indicated at least one
   802→guardrail from the checklist emphasized transparency in design and data collection.
   803→Participants overwhelmingly called for AI systems to be designed intentionally with child
   804→safety as a baseline and child well-being as an aspirational standard. Participants were consistent
   805→that design must be developmentally informed, “AI tools and protections must be tailored to
   806→adolescents’ developmental needs, strengths, and vulnerabilities. This includes ensuring that AI
   807→systems support, rather than undermine, healthy cognitive, social, and emotional development”
   808→(survey, research scientist, USA). Participants also agreed that such design solutions may be best
   809→enforced via policy and regulation, with companies required to promote well-being rather than
   810→merely avoid harm: “Not just proving that you're not causing harm, but that you are adding
   811→value in supporting positive development. Because young people do not have rights in the same
   812→way, they can't advocate for themselves in the same way” (interview, faculty researcher, USA).
   813→A related theme was the importance of community participation in design. Involving
   814→teens in design and policy was endorsed in the quantitative data by 46.8% (n = 58) of
   815→
   816→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 32
   817→respondents. This theme was also mentioned frequently in qualitative responses, including the
   818→necessity of designing both with and for adolescents’ social networks “Youth, families, and
   819→educators should be involved in shaping AI policies and product design to ensure that tools are
   820→not only effective, but safe, equitable, and developmentally appropriate… AI tools should be
   821→developed to complement, but not replace, educators, mental health professionals, caregivers,
   822→and peers who provide youth with guidance, support, and valuable human connection” (survey,
   823→research scientist, USA).
   824→Participants argued that harmful design features are not technological inevitabilities but
   825→deliberate choices. Some of these, including engagement maximization, have known
   826→consequences from prior research on other technologies: “We’ve learned so much about
   827→engagement-based design features that manipulate, addict and control youth behavior. These
   828→same features are built into many of the commercial AI tools available to children. These
   829→addictive design features are not inevitable — they are a choice. And the consequences of these
   830→design choices are now well known, documented and grounded in scientific evidence" (survey,
   831→faculty researcher, US). Participants also commented that how an AI system responds (e.g.,
   832→whether it validates distress or nudges toward human support, whether it is honest or
   833→sycophantic) may be especially consequential. Sycophancy was specifically named as an
   834→underappreciated design risk: "Sycophantic AI models that reinforce or celebrate unhealthy
   835→behaviors (e.g. lack of sleep in mania, reassurance seeking in OCD) are unhelpful and may be
   836→disastrous" (survey, faculty researcher, USA).
   837→Several participants pushed explicitly against individualistic framings of AI risk, with
   838→design changes serving as a solution for a collective action problem. For example, on the topic of
   839→AI cognitive reliance, one researcher framed this as a design imperative, arguing that we should
   840→
   841→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 33
   842→"Come up with a way to force children to use mentally taxing (in a good way) strategies to
   843→engage with tasks. It shouldn't be on them to regulate themselves" (survey, graduate student,
   844→USA). The dominant policy conversation, they argued, remains too focused on personal behavior
   845→change and parental monitoring, while the more powerful determinants—corporate incentives—
   846→remain under-addressed. As one researcher stated: "The ills that technology has wrought on our
   847→youth is a social problem — not a personal problem. Asking parents to do more monitoring, or
   848→training teens to monitor their own usage can be helpful, but are ineffective at scale" (survey,
   849→faculty researcher, USA). Effective guardrails must operate at a structural level, and participants
   850→expressed concern that focusing primarily on individual literacy or family-level intervention may
   851→be less effective than holding industry to account.
   852→Theme 3: AI Literacy and Community Support
   853→AI literacy was identified as a critical proximal protective intervention, especially
   854→because regulation and design changes may be slower to unfold. Such literacy interventions were
   855→seen by most as pragmatic solutions to a reality where AI is not going away: “This technology's
   856→already here. So how can we help young people gain literacy and understanding? And also
   857→thinking about how that intersects with positive youth development and how to help young people
   858→feel like they have a sense of agency, right? That they're not just feeling like… that this
   859→technology's not just happening to them, but that they understand how to use it and they have
   860→more agency in the process itself” (interview, faculty researcher, USA). Participants were careful
   861→to frame literacy as a complement to structural change rather than a substitute for it. Placing the
   862→burden of AI safety on individual young people and their families, in the absence of regulatory
   863→and design-level protections, was seen as unfair and insufficient.
   864→
   865→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 34
   866→Similar to comments on AI literacy as a key use case for study, participants emphasized
   867→the multidimensional nature of literacy and the need to promote a diverse set of skills to equip
   868→adolescents to navigate an AI-dominated world. Effective AI literacy interventions will neither
   869→simply tell kids to avoid AI nor focus narrowly on workplace readiness: “Instead of focusing on
   870→removing harms which is impossible to accomplish 100% of the time, we need to help young
   871→people identify the limitations of AI and improve their self-awareness of how they use it,
   872→motivations for using AI, and how it can help them thrive in an ethical digital technical universe,
   873→rather than how to avoid it all costs” (survey, faculty researcher, USA). Relatedly, an aspect of
   874→AI literacy may involve supporting adolescents in not using AI and ensuring they have
   875→appropriate contexts and supports for development, “ensuring children have their psychological,
   876→social, and physical needs met will make it less likely that they'll use AI tools to fill the void”
   877→(survey, faculty researcher, USA).
   878→Structurally, respondents felt AI literacy initiatives should be comprehensive, including
   879→but not limited to AI literacy training in school curricula. In quantitative data, parent education
   880→regarding AI was the fourth most-commonly endorsed guardrail (48.4%, n = 60). As one
   881→researcher put it: “We need to raise AI-literacy not just among teens, but also with their support
   882→networks” (survey, faculty researcher, Turkey). Initiatives may include sustained teacher
   883→professional development and parent-facing literacy efforts that acknowledge adults' own
   884→uncertainty about AI. Across any intervention format, participants emphasized co-design with
   885→teens as both an ethical and a pragmatic imperative to ensure content is relevant, “AI policy,
   886→design, and initiatives are best informed by research that is co-designed by youth for youth.
   887→Inclusion of youth at all stages (including development, implementation, analysis, interpretation,
   888→dissemination, and related advocacy efforts) is the best way towards radical transformation of
   889→
   890→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 35
   891→digital spaces” (survey, clinician and faculty researcher, USA). Participants argued that
   892→deployment and rigorous evaluation of such materials should proceed concurrently—rather than
   893→waiting for an evidence base that does not yet exist: “tech moves so rapidly, we cannot wait for
   894→the perfect gold standard study to begin offering policy and parenting recommendations for this
   895→massive set of problems that may represent an existential threat” (survey response, faculty
   896→researcher, USA).
   897→A recurring concern was the lag between research results and dissemination to clinical
   898→practice, schools, and families. Standard timelines to translate science into practice are already
   899→slow and are being rapidly outpaced by the speed of AI development. Participants noted that
   900→coordinated efforts are critical to ensure AI research reaches teens and families who can benefit
   901→from it, and that researchers have a role to play in this process. For example, one participant
   902→emphasized the potential for research to simultaneously build AI literacy among adolescents, “I
   903→believe a part of the research process is to also implement action programming complimentary to
   904→the topic at hand with AI. We are hearing more and more from our middle school youth that
   905→teachers are telling them "it's okay" to use AI and they just need to know when. However,
   906→teachers are *not* telling what that "when" looks like. So the goal of AI literacy, in my
   907→perspective, should be at the forefront of all the AI and teen research moving forward” (survey,
   908→postdoctoral scholar, USA). Embedding evaluation into ongoing interventions, supporting
   909→community-based participatory models, and investing in science communication were all
   910→identified as practical steps the research community can take now, without waiting for the
   911→regulatory or funding environment to change.
   912→Conclusion
   913→
   914→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 36
   915→This study surveyed and interviewed professionals working in research or related fields to
   916→build expert consensus on adolescents and AI across three aims: (1) identifying AI use cases to
   917→prioritize for future research, (2) establishing a research agenda—including priority questions,
   918→methods, and barriers—and (3) proposing guardrails society can implement while research is
   919→underway. Experts agreed that nearly all AI use cases warrant study but prioritized severe,
   920→irreversible harms (e.g., self-harm content, exploitation) alongside AI literacy, AI as a
   921→social/relational actor, information and identity processes, and overreliance. For the research
   922→agenda, experts emphasized anchoring inquiry in developmental function rather than use case
   923→alone, attending to individual differences, and using diverse methods, though no single set of
   924→priority questions emerged. Major barriers included the pace of AI change, lack of industry
   925→transparency, and resource gaps. Proposed solutions centered on cross-disciplinary coordination
   926→and partnerships with industry, adolescents, and communities. For guardrails, experts converged
   927→on regulation, developmentally-informed design, and multidimensional AI literacy, agreeing that
   928→responsibility should not fall on teens and families alone.
   929→These findings point to an urgent need for coordination within research communities and
   930→across societal sectors. To advance research, scientists, labs, and organizations should actively
   931→share resources, data, measurement approaches, and emerging ideas rather than working in
   932→parallel silos, building the shared infrastructure and consortia to keep pace with rapid
   933→technological change. Greater collaboration and integration between academic and non-academic
   934→researchers—including non-profits, thinktanks, advocacy organizations, and trust and safety
   935→teams—can enhance our knowledge base given different strengths, barriers, and perspectives
   936→across diverse research teams. At the same time, researchers cannot work in isolation from those
   937→positioned to translate findings into action: sustained collaboration with responsible technology
   938→
   939→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 37
   940→organizations and non-profits, technologists and computational scientists, trust and safety teams,
   941→and with young people in the room as key experts and collaborators may help ensure empirical
   942→science is deployed in product design. Collaboration with science communicators, advocates, and
   943→policymakers is also necessary to ensure that empirical insights reach the public and inform
   944→policy. Finally, researchers have a role to play locally, including within their own institutions,
   945→schools, and communities, promoting AI literacy and advocating for both continued investment
   946→in rigorous research and the immediate adoption of critical guardrails. Such guardrails can
   947→continue to be refined as more rigorous research is conducted.
   948→To our knowledge, this is the first empirical study of social science researchers studying
   949→adolescents and AI and it directly builds on similar field-wide consensus projects focused on
   950→youth and social media (Davis et al., 2025). Limitations primarily relate to non-representative
   951→sampling methods that largely reflect the disciplinary knowledge and professional network of the
   952→research team. Respondents were disproportionately U.S.-based, limiting generalizability to
   953→other countries. Psychology was overrepresented relative to other relevant disciplines (e.g.,
   954→computer science, public health, communication, education), as was academia relative to
   955→industry, policy, and practitioner perspectives. Methodologically, the AI use case list—though
   956→developed collaboratively with adolescent and young adult input—reflects a finite, researcher-
   957→generated set of categories. Overall, findings should be treated as a starting point for consensus-
   958→building rather than a fully representative account of the field.
   959→This study offers an initial step toward shared priorities, research agendas, and guardrails
   960→for understanding the role of AI in adolescent development. Realizing this agenda will require
   961→sustained cross-sector coordination and iteration among researchers, communicators,
   962→technologists, policymakers, and young people themselves. Continued investment in both
   963→
   964→RESEARCH LANDSCAPE OF ADOLESCENTS AND AI 38
   965→rigorous research and timely protective measures is needed to support healthy development in
   966→the AI era.
