    13→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM)
    14→Abstract
    15→Despite decades of theoretical development proposing how personality traits, situational
    16→contingencies, and person-environment transactions jointly govern behavioral variability, there
    17→lacks a direct comparison these competing accounts within a unified empirical framework. The
    18→present study addresses this gap by introducing the Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM), a
    19→framework based on Dynamic Structural Equation Modeling that simultaneously captures
    20→within-person person-environment temporal dynamics, situation-behavior contingencies, and
    21→stable between-person differences. Using intensive longitudinal data from 306 undergraduates
    22→across 25 personality-situation combinations, we pursued three major aims: (1) identifying the
    23→most plausible theoretical model of daily behavioral dynamics; (2) identifying the effective
    24→pathways in person-environment transactions; and (3) examining individual differences and trait
    25→moderation in dynamic processes. The DCM consistently outperformed all competing models,
    26→including static trait, situational contingency models, person-situation interaction models, and
    27→traditional transaction models. Within the DCM, autoregressive effects and contemporaneous
    28→situation contingencies emerged as the dominant parameters, with cross-lagged transactions
    29→exhibiting a systematic asymmetry whereby personality states more robustly predicted
    30→subsequent situational experiences than vice versa, suggesting individuals function as active
    31→architects of their daily dynamics. Substantial individual differences in dynamic parameters were
    32→observed, yet Big Five traits explained only a modest fraction of this variability. Together, these
    33→findings establish that personality dynamics are best characterized as a multifaceted system
    34→integrating dispositional, situational, and temporal processes.
    35→Keywords: personality dynamics; person-environment transactions; experience sampling;
    36→Dynamic Structural Equation Modeling; situational contingencies; within-person variability;

    38→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 1
    39→Dynamic Transactions of Person, Situation, and Their Interactions
    40→Many promising theoretical models exist that describe how behavior is generated across
    41→time via input from persons and environment (Funder, 1991; Shiner, 2009). For example, the
    42→Cognitive-Affective Processing System (CAPS; Mischel & Shoda, 1995), the Cybernetic Big
    43→Five Theory (CB5T; DeYoung, 2015), Whole Trait Theory (WTT; Fleeson & Jayawickreme,
    44→2015), Trait Activation Theory (TAT; Tett & Burnett, 2003) and the Processual Examination of
    45→Personality in Real-Time Model (PERM; Rauthmann, 2021) all document ways that individual
    46→characteristics combine with environmental signals to generate behavior. Despite their
    47→conceptual richness, these overarching theories have seldom been tested empirically in a
    48→comprehensive manner, primarily due to the absence of a unified analytical framework capable
    49→of operationalizing core features that integrate person and environment.
    50→Most empirical investigations have proceeded along two separate methodological paths:
    51→interactional and transactional approaches. Interactional models examine how stable individual
    52→differences interact with environmental features to predict momentary behavioral expressions
    53→(Endler & Magnusson, 1976). However, these models typically neglect how states evolve
    54→continuously over time. By contrast, transactional models adopt a time-series perspective to
    55→examine the reciprocal interplay between states and environmental features across occasions,
    56→directly incorporating time (Danvers et al., 2020; Hecht et al., 2023; Sosnowska et al., 2021; Wilt
    57→& Revelle, 2023; Kocjan et al., 2025, 2024). Yet, these models often fail to explicitly examine
    58→situational contingencies (Mischel & Shoda, 1995) and do not account for individual differences
    59→in dynamic patterns (Hamaker et al., 2015). Although methodologically distinct, these two
    60→approaches offer complementary insights into the function of personality processes.
    61→
    62→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 2
    63→To date, few empirical efforts have systematically integrated these perspectives or
    64→evaluated the diverse theoretical models that aim to provide a parsimonious account of
    65→personality dynamics (Hecht et al., 2023; Sosnowska et al., 2021; Kocjan et al., 2024). As it
    66→stands, it is unclear what model of functioning best characterizes personality processes. The
    67→present study addresses this gap by introducing and examining a comprehensive analytic
    68→framework based on Dynamic Structural Equation Modeling (Asparouhov et al., 2018; Hamaker
    69→et al., 2021). This framework enables the simultaneous modeling of within-person processes and
    70→between-person variability, offering a flexible and empirically grounded platform for testing
    71→competing and complementary theoretical accounts, thereby advancing a more holistic
    72→understanding of personality dynamics. In particular, we aimed to 1) identify the most plausible
    73→theoretical models in explaining variability in daily behaviors; 2) assess the extent of individual
    74→differences in dynamic personality processes; and 3) examine personality-based moderation of
    75→daily dynamic transactions by integrating interactional and transactional approaches.
    76→Models of Behavior
    77→Behavioral states vary over time according to context, but also meaningfully differ across
    78→individuals. By conceptualizing the variability in behavioral states into density distributions
    79→(Fleeson, 2001, 2007; Xi & Jackson, 2025), the within-person variability is captured by the
    80→standard deviation (SD) of one’s personal density distribution (DDp), whereas the between-
    81→person trait differences are captured by the discrepancies at the mean levels of people’s DDp. A
    82→central aim in psychology is to explain and predict the underlying patterns governing these
    83→variabilities and differences in behavior (Funder, 2001). Traditionally, this objective was often
    84→operationalized as identifying a single factor that accounts for the largest proportion of variance
    85→observed in these behavioral states (Fleeson & Noftle, 2008; Kenrick & Funder, 1988). Later
    86→
    87→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 3
    88→development in personality dynamics emphasized the necessity to take both personal and
    89→environmental factors into consideration to gather a better understanding of state variability
    90→(Allport, 1961; 1937; Jayawickreme et al., 2021; Kuper et al., 2021; Mischel & Shoda, 1995). In
    91→Figure 1, we characterize the operationalization of major theoretical models describing the
    92→dynamics or variability of state manifestations.
    93→Singular Main Effect: Two dominant theoretical perspectives offer a single process
    94→explanation (see Figure 1, Panel Ai). First, the dispositional approach emphasizes that
    95→individuals exhibit stable patterns of behavior that are primarily driven by enduring individual
    96→differences in traits (Costa & McCrae, 1999; Funder, 1991; Roberts et al., 2006). This
    97→framework, often examined through cross-sectional analysis, uses personality traits as the single
    98→explanatory variable to predict behavioral states. As personality traits characterize between-
    99→person differences and remain stable across time, the dispositional approach makes the same
   100→prediction of state regardless of time or situation, only predicting the mean of someone’s
   101→behavioral distribution (DDp). When translated into a within-person framework, this emphasis
   102→on stable behavioral patterns implies a specific temporal process: strong auto-regressive effects
   103→such that past behavior predicts future behavior (Epstein, 1979). In this view, the "trait"
   104→manifests as high inertia, where current states strongly predict future states (See Figure 1Aii).

   133→Second, the situation contingency approach (Figure 1Aiii) emphasizes within-person
   134→processing and suggests that behavior is predominantly shaped by momentary responses to
   135→situational cues and internal cognitive processes (Bandura, 1986; Kuper et al., 2022; Mischel,
   136→1968; Shoda et al., 1993). This perspective posits that concurrent environmental factors are the
   137→dominant determinants of behavioral states, predicting strong contemporaneous situation
   138→contingencies (i.e., if…then) that overwhelm any potential between-person trait. Empirically,
   139→evidence from intensive longitudinal studies suggests that behavior is often contingent upon the
   140→
   141→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 5
   142→co-occurring characteristics of situations or environments (Huang & Ryan, 2011; Kuper et al.,
   143→2022; Shoda et al., 1993; Wood et al., 2019).
   144→The apparent conflict between these two singular effect approaches fueled the classic
   145→Person-Situation debate (e.g., Mischel, 1968). However, this debate was largely resolved by
   146→recognizing it as a false dichotomy, highlighting the joint influence of personal and
   147→environmental factors on behavioral variability across time and contexts. Despite the
   148→proliferation of sophisticated integrative theories (DeYoung, 2015; Fleeson & Jayawickreme,
   149→2015; Mischel & Shoda, 1995; Rauthmann, 2021; Revelle & Condon, 2015; Roberts & Jackson,
   150→2008), the empirical examination can be simplified to two general approaches: interactional and
   151→transactional perspectives (Caspi & Roberts, 2001; Endler & Magnusson, 1976; Sameroff, 2009).
   152→Interactionalism
   153→Interactional approaches integrate the person and environment factors, emphasizing that
   154→behavior emerges from the moderating interplay between stable individual dispositions and
   155→variable environmental inputs (Endler & Magnusson, 1976; Kenrick & Funder, 1988; Kuper et
   156→al., 2023). Statistically, interactional approaches are often operationalized as moderation, which
   157→tests if a univariate state outcome depends on the statistical interaction (or cross-product) of
   158→person and environment effects, in addition to their main effects (Figure 1, Panel B).
   159→A linear moderation pattern is embedded in numerous influential theories. For example,
   160→Trait Activation Theory (Tett & Guterman, 2000; Tett et al., 2021) proposes that traits are
   161→expressed only under conditions that afford or permit their activation. Similarly, the Motivational
   162→Trait Framework conceptualizes traits as individual differences in sensitivity to motivational
   163→affordances in the environment (Denissen & Penke, 2008). Whole Trait Theory (Fleeson &
   164→Jayawickreme, 2015; Jayawickreme et al., 2019) interprets traits as density distributions of states
   165→
   166→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 6
   167→and emphasizes that individuals differ in how their states fluctuate, contingent on which context
   168→one is in (Kuper et al., 2022; Sherman et al., 2015; Xi & Jackson, 2025). Collectively, these
   169→accounts underscore that behavior is not determined by personality or context alone, but by their
   170→joint, conditional influence.
   171→Despite the promise of interactionalism, the empirical evidence is limited. There is good
   172→evidence for the main effects of both one’s personality traits and the situational characteristics to
   173→which one is exposed, but the statistical interaction is small in magnitude and inconsistent across
   174→studies (Horstmann et al., 2021; Sherman et al., 2015; Xi & Jackson, 2025).
   175→In addition to linear moderation models, which assume that trait manifestations change
   176→proportionally with situational characteristics, nonlinear interactions have also been proposed.
   177→The Nonlinear Interaction of Person and Situation (NIPS) model (Blum et al., 2018) posits that
   178→the contingency of behavior on situational features is itself moderated by the strength of one’s
   179→personality traits. Specifically, the NIPS model theorizes a curvilinear (inverted U-shaped)
   180→association between trait level and the strength of the person factor. Individuals at either extreme
   181→of a bipolar trait dimension, very high or very low, are considered “strong persons,”
   182→characterized by rigid and consistent behavioral tendencies that are relatively impervious to
   183→environmental influence. In contrast, an individual’s behavior near the midpoint of a trait
   184→continuum is more context-sensitive and thus more likely to vary across situations.
   185→Transactionalism
   186→While interactional approaches offer valuable insights into how person and environment
   187→factors jointly predict behavioral states, they often only provide a static snapshot, typically
   188→focusing on how traits and situational characteristics concurrently influence outcomes. As a
   189→result, they do not fully capture the ongoing, evolving interplay that unfolds between individuals
   190→
   191→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 7
   192→and their environments over time. In contrast, transactional approaches shift the analytical focus
   193→towards the dynamic and temporal processes, through which individuals and their environments
   194→mutually shape each other across time (Baumert et al., 2017; Hecht et al., 2023; Shiner, 2009;
   195→Sosnowska et al., 2020; Kocjan et al., 2024). Statistically, the temporal dynamics between the
   196→two factors proposed by the transactional approaches are often operationalized through time
   197→series analyses (Hamaker et al., 2018; Jongerling et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2012). As shown in
   198→Figure 1 C, these transactional processes can be represented by path analyses examining the
   199→interplay of auto-regressive and cross-lagged effects when person (P) and environment (E)
   200→variables are measured at a minimum of two time points (t-1 and t).
   201→Transactional dynamics involves five key types of temporal effects. First, Person Stability
   202→(P→P) refers to the extent to which earlier person characteristics predict later state
   203→manifestations, reflecting continuity within the individual. Second, Environment Stability (E→E)
   204→captures the persistence of environmental features over time. Third, Person-to-Environment
   205→Influence (P→E) reflects the agentic processes of how individuals shape their surroundings
   206→through their earlier behavioral states. Fourth, Environment-to-Person Influence (E→P) denotes
   207→how prior environmental conditions affect subsequent individual states, illustrating how
   208→contextual factors can evoke, constrain, or reinforce changes within the individual. Fifth,
   209→Contemporaneous Covariance (P↔E) represents the moment-to-moment association between
   210→person and environment at the same time point, capturing how internal and external states co-
   211→vary in real time.
   212→Based on the combination of the presence or absence of these paths, Rauthmann (2020)
   213→proposed a taxonomy of 16 distinct P-E transaction patterns within the Person-Environment
   214→Relationship Model (PERM) to conceptualize the mutual impact of the two factors across time.

   216→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 8
   217→In the following section, we will review four of the proposed transactions that are the most
   218→theoretically representative, while being supported by empirical evidence from different realms
   219→of literature: bidirectional-stability transaction, bidirectional-reset transaction, person-tilt
   220→monodirectional transaction, and fully bidirectional transaction.
   221→The bidirectional-stability transaction (BS; Figure 1 Ci) represents a non-transactional
   222→dynamic in which both person and environment exhibit significant stability over time, but show
   223→minimal cross-lagged influence on each other (Rauthmann, 2020). In this pattern, an individual’s
   224→personality state at time t is primarily predicted by their prior state at t–1, while environmental
   225→conditions at t are similarly informed by previous environmental states. This aligns with
   226→traditional trait theories (e.g., Roberts & Jackson, 2008) and the environmental stability
   227→hypothesis (Bronfenbrenner, 1979; Moos, 1973), which emphasize the persistence of contextual
   228→factors. Extensive empirical evidence supports the temporal stability of both personality and
   229→environmental factors (Eid & Diener, 1999; Fraley & Roberts, 2005; Jackson & Wright, 2024;
   230→Brent W. Roberts & DelVecchio, 2000), and substantial short-term stability in intensive
   231→longitudinal designs (Fleeson, 2001; Fleeson & Gallagher, 2009; Rauthmann & Sherman, 2015).
   232→Similarly, moderate levels of environmental consistency are supported by findings across varied
   233→domains, such as cultural norms (Gelfand et al., 2011; Triandis, 2001), stable social networks
   234→(Mund & Neyer, 2014; Wrzus et al., 2013), enduring occupational roles (Wille & De Fruyt,
   235→2014), and stable physical living environments (Oishi, 2010; Stokols, 2018).
   236→The reset-bidirectional transaction (BR; Figure 1 Cii) is characterized by strong cross-
   237→lagged effects in both directions—person influencing environment and vice versa—paired with a
   238→notable absence of within-domain stability (Rauthmann, 2020). In this dynamic, an individual’s
   239→personality state at time t is shaped primarily by the prior environmental state (t–1), and vice
   240→
   241→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 9
   242→versa, rather than by each factor’s own past value. This produces a system that effectively
   243→“resets” at each time point, with current states emerging from mutual influence rather than
   244→internal continuity. Theoretical support for this pattern comes from Social Cognitive Theory
   245→(Bandura, 1986), which emphasizes reciprocal determinism among personal, behavioral, and
   246→environmental factors, and the Cybernetic Big Five Theory (DeYoung, 2015), which highlights
   247→personality regulation through feedback loops responding to discrepancies between goals and
   248→internal or external states. While empirical evidence for pure reset-bidirectional dynamics is
   249→limited, since autoregressive effects are typically stronger than cross-lagged ones (Rauthmann,
   250→2020), some studies offer suggestive findings on the reciprocal process between environment and
   251→behavior (Eaton & Funder, 2003).
   252→Building on the bi-stable pattern, the person-to-environment transaction (PT; Figure 1
   253→Ciii) reflects a unidirectional dynamic in which stable personality states shape or guide the
   254→selection and modification of environmental conditions (Rauthmann, 2020). This pattern aligns
   255→with theories emphasizing strong person-driven shaping of environments (Buss, 1987; Scarr &
   256→McCartney, 1983), such as the niche-picking principle, where individuals seek or construct
   257→contexts that reinforce their dispositional traits (Kandler et al., 2012; Specht et al., 2011).
   258→Empirical evidence across domains supports this unidirectional flow. For example, longitudinal
   259→studies show that transactional effects from person to environment tended to be stronger than the
   260→reverse (Denissen et al., 2019), with traits more reliably predicting life events than vice versa
   261→(Denissen et al., 2019; Kandler et al., 2012; Wright & Jackson, 2023a, 2023b; Wrzus et al.,
   262→2016). These findings reflect person-driven processes such as selection, evocation, and
   263→manipulation (Buss, 1987; Caspi & Roberts, 2001; Rauthmann et al., 2015), through which
   264→
   265→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 10
   266→individuals align their environments with their personality (Kandler et al., 2024; Kritzler et al.,
   267→2021; Rauthmann & Sherman, 2023; Rauthmann et al., 2015).
   268→The fully bidirectional transaction (BF; Figure 1 Civ) integrates elements of the previous
   269→transaction types, reflecting a co-evolving, mutually influential relationship in which both person
   270→and environment are stable yet responsive components of a coupled feedback system. This
   271→dynamic is well captured by the corresponsive principle (Roberts & Wood, 2006), which posits
   272→that traits lead individuals into environments that, in turn, reinforce and shape those traits. While
   273→such mechanisms can foster change, the niche-picking principle (Nye & Roberts, 2019; Roberts
   274→& Nickel, 2017) emphasizes how individuals select trait-congruent environments, reinforcing
   275→stability through sustained congruence. Bidirectional reciprocity has been widely theorized and
   276→empirically examined (Bleidorn & Denissen, 2021; Bleidorn et al., 2018; Jackson & Wright,
   277→2024). For instance, research on personality development and work experiences has shown
   278→reciprocal effects and enduring trait stability across phases such as job entry and long-term
   279→occupational engagement (Denissen et al., 2014; Roberts et al., 2003). Evidence for this pattern
   280→also emerges at shorter timescales: intensive longitudinal studies have demonstrated mutual
   281→influences between extraversion states, positive emotions, and sociable situational features,
   282→alongside meaningful stability (Hecht et al., 2023).
   283→An integrated framework: Dynamic Contingency Model
   284→While each of the theoretical models presented in Figure 1, Panels A to C, holds some
   285→empirical and theoretical validity, a comprehensive model that integrates and compares the
   286→different perspectives is necessary. First, while disposition approaches and the extending person-
   287→situation interaction approaches include between-person differences, they neglect the temporal
   288→process of state manifestations. On the opposite side, while the contingency and transactional
   289→
   290→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 11
   291→approach excels at capturing within-person processes and reciprocal influences that are not
   292→modeled in the interactional approaches, it is typically implemented through time-series analyses
   293→that either focus on a single individual (i.e., N = 1) or assume homogeneity in dynamic patterns
   294→across individuals (Beck & Jackson, 2020a; Mund & Neyer, 2014; Sameroff, 2009). As a result,
   295→the potential for explaining inter-individual differences in these processes is often overlooked.
   296→Second, standard statistical approaches to transactional models, such as the Cross-Lagged
   297→Panel Model (Selig & Little, 2012), Random Intercept CLPM (Hamaker et al., 2015), and vector
   298→autoregressive models (Lütkepohl, 2013), typically treat contemporaneous associations (Fig 1,
   299→Panel Aii) between personality states and environmental factors through the residual covariance
   300→at each time point (Hamaker et al., 2015; Lucas, 2023). Hence, these approaches typically do not
   301→capture the well-theorized and evidenced contemporaneous situational contingencies or if…
   302→then… contingencies (Fleeson, 2007; Kuper et al., 2022; Wright & Mischel, 1987).
   303→A comprehensive understanding of personality functioning requires an approach that
   304→simultaneously captures temporal transactions, contemporaneous contingencies, individual
   305→differences in these processes, and integrates between-person predictors of these processes. To
   306→address this, we adopt Dynamic Structural Equation Modeling (Asparouhov et al., 2018), a
   307→flexible multilevel time-series framework that simultaneously models temporal processes within
   308→individuals and stable individual differences in the dynamic processes. By integrating the
   309→transactional and interactional perspectives, we proposed the Dynamic Contingency Model
   310→(DCM), which compensates for the limitations of each previous practice, offering an integrated
   311→framework for understanding personality dynamics (Figure 1, Panel D).

   362→variability of state-level observations into between-person differences and within-person
   363→variability (Figure 2). At the within-person level, personality and environmental states are
   364→modeled based on various hypothesized dynamic transaction frameworks. Importantly, people
   365→can vary in each of these within-person parameters. At the between-person level, the individual

   368→differences in these dynamic processes (such as variations in transactional paths, average state
   369→levels, and process noise) can then be accounted for by dispositional traits.
   370→Current Study
   371→The integrative framework of DSEM provides a flexible model to construct, evaluate, and
   372→compare theoretical models. The present study aims to examine and identify the most plausible
   373→theoretical models that explain daily behavior. We split this general aim into three specific
   374→research aims. First, we examined the in- and out-of-sample prediction of the daily personality
   375→state of main effect models, interactional models, and transactional models. We hypothesize that
   376→our newly proposed integrated transactional model will outperform all alternative models
   377→because it most accurately captures the nuanced dynamics implied by the underlying theoretical
   378→framework. To our knowledge, this work is the first attempt to formally compare existing
   379→theoretical frameworks with empirical data under the same evaluation metrics.
   380→Secondly, we thoroughly examine all possible transactional paths in the four integrated
   381→transactional models to identify the most plausible transactional pattern in explaining the full
   382→dynamic system of both person and environment factors. Based on previous findings in
   383→longitudinal studies, we hypothesized that the fully bidirectional transaction would best represent
   384→the nature of person-environment dynamics. In doing so, the present study provides a
   385→comprehensive and explicit examination of within-person dynamic person-environment
   386→transactions, addressing a critical gap in empirical evidence from intensive longitudinal designs.
   387→Finally, building on the four transactional parameters estimated in Aim 2 (autoregressive,
   388→cross-lag, contemporaneous, residual), we examine the magnitude of individual differences in
   389→dynamic processes. Then, to integrate the interactional perspective with the transactional
   390→approach, we test whether individual differences in personality traits moderate dynamic paths
   391→
   392→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 14
   393→(i.e., cross-lag and contemporaneous). Moreover, to comprehensively investigate the individual
   394→differences in dynamic transaction, in addition to regular linear moderation, we also examine a
   395→curvilinear moderation that was theorized in the NIPS model (Blum et al., 2018). According to
   396→NIPS, we hypothesized that people with polarized trait levels will demonstrate stronger auto-
   397→regressive effect in each factor (i.e., person vs. environmental) as well as the person-to-
   398→environment transaction, but are less malleable to both lagged and contemporaneous
   399→environment-to-person transaction.

   413→The sample consisted of 361 undergraduate students at [Masked for Blind Review]. For

   421→complete an initial 30-minute lab session where they were explained the goal and procedure of

   423→on the next day of the initial lab session. ESM surveys were delivered to participants with a fixed
   424→schedule of six times per day for twenty-one days, resulting in a maximum of 126 assessments

   430→Measures
   431→Personality State Measures: Personality state measures were adapted from the BFI-2 (Soto &
   432→John, 2017), assessing three facets of each Big Five domain with a total of 60 items. To better fit
   433→the contextualized measurements nature of ESM, one inappropriately phrased question for each
   434→facet was removed. Then, a planned missingness design was practiced such that only two out of
   435→three items per facet were randomly selected. As a result, there were in total of 30 items for each
   436→survey of personality state measures. Participants rated each item on a 5-point Likert scale
   437→assessing how much they agree with the item statements (e.g., “In the past hour, you behaved
   438→outgoing and sociable.”) from 1-Strongly Disagree to 5-Strongly Agree.
   439→Situation Characteristics Measures: Participants’ subjective perceptions of situations were
   440→measured by the S8-I (Rauthmann & Sherman, 2016) based on the well-justified DIAMONDS
   441→
   442→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 16
   443→(Rauthmann et al., 2014). In this ultra-brief measure, there is one item assessing each dimension
   444→of the perception of situations: Dutiful, Intellectual, Mating, Positive, Negative, Deceptive, and
   445→Sociable. Participants rated each item on a 5-point Likert Scale indicating how well they agree
   446→with the item descriptions of the situations they have experienced (e.g., In the past hour, deep
   447→thinking was required). See the descriptive information of both personality and situation state
   448→measures in Supplementary Table 1.
   449→Personality Trait Measures: The Big Five personality trait levels of each individual were
   450→measured with the original BFI-2 (Soto & John, 2017), administered in the initial survey.
   451→Participants rate each on a 5-point Likert scale assessing how well they agree with the item
   452→statement about themselves (e.g., “I see myself as someone who is outgoing, sociable.”) from 1-
   453→Strongly Disagree to 5-Strongly Agree. See the descriptive information on the personality trait
   454→measure and its association with both personality and situation state measures in Supplementary
   455→Table 2.
   456→Analysis
   457→Data Exclusion: ESM surveys with missing responses beyond the planned missingness
   458→were excluded. All survey entries from participants who completed fewer than 40 assessments in
   459→total were also excluded. The final dataset comprised 28,740 ESM surveys from 306 participants.
   460→Data Transformation: For person-level variables, personality states were calculated as the
   461→aggregate of the ratings on items measuring a Big-Five domain at each time. The raw scores of
   462→the personality and situational states were directly used in modeling, as the DSEM model
   463→naturally separates the within-person variability and between-person variability of the state
   464→variables through latent mean centering. For trait-level variables, personality traits were
   465→calculated as the aggregated rating of items measuring each Big-Five trait domain in the initial
   466→
   467→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 17
   468→survey. The raw trait scores of individuals’ personality trait levels were then centered around the
   469→grand mean (sample mean) such that a positive rating in trait level extraversion is then indicated
   470→by how much an individual is more extraverted relative to the average of people in the sample.
   471→Model Development: The current study adopted the novel Dynamic Structural Equation
   472→Modeling (DSEM; Hamaker et al., 2018) to examine the dynamic processing of person-situation
   473→interaction. The DSEMs provide a platform to integrate the time-series analysis focusing on the
   474→dynamic processing in repeated measurement of a single case or individual (N=1) with the multi-
   475→level models differentiating within-person variations and between-person variations. Under such
   476→a framework, the observed state expressions and situation characteristics of person i and occasion
   477→t can be decomposed into between-person differences of the personal average expression and
   478→experiences and the within-person deviation of their state manifestations (e.g., behaviors,
   479→emotions, etc.) and situation experiences relative to one’s average or typical expression or
   480→experience level. Instead of independently estimating the within-person dynamic process for
   481→each individual, the DSEM partial pools individuals’ unique parameters of dynamic processing
   482→from central parameterized estimations based on the whole sample. While the individual
   483→differences in the dynamic parameters are assessed as random effects, such an integrated
   484→framework allows us to investigate further and examine the variabilities with individual
   485→difference features (e.g., personality traits) in between-person models.

   553→In experience sampling data, assessments often occur at uneven time intervals, violating
   554→the assumption of equally spaced observations in discrete time-series models. To address this, we
   555→introduce a discounting parameter (λ) that adjusts lagged effects based on the actual time elapsed
   556→between observations. Mathematically, lagged effects are scaled using an exponential decay
   557→function 𝑒,.∗0#, where Dt represents the time gap between successive observations. This
   558→transformation, commonly used in discrete time-series modeling, ensures that past influences
   559→diminish smoothly over time while maintaining the interpretability of the autoregressive
   560→coefficients. This allows shorter intervals to retain stronger dependencies, while longer intervals
   561→reflect natural decay, providing a more realistic temporal structure. By incorporating this
   562→parameter, the model avoids biases from assuming equal time intervals and offers a more flexible
   563→and accurate representation of temporal dependencies in personality states and situational
   564→experiences. All variables and associations are documented in Figure 2, and the mathematical
   565→expression of the association is shown above.

   602→For various specifications of the DCMs, the person-to-situation (PT) model simplifies the
   603→full model by removing the cross-lagged path from situational states to subsequent personality
   604→states. This reduction reflects prior longitudinal findings suggesting that environmental
   605→influences on future behavioral or experiential tendencies are generally weak. The bidirectional
   606→stable (BS) model further simplifies the full model by eliminating all cross-lagged effects
   607→between personality and situational states, thereby capturing only the autoregressive (self-
   608→stability) components of each process. In contrast, the bidirectional reset (BR) model represents
   609→the opposite conceptual reduction, retaining only the mutual cross-lagged effects between
   610→personality and situational states while removing both autoregressive paths, effectively assuming
   611→that states are fully renewed at each occasion rather than maintaining temporal stability.
   612→Model Estimation: All models were trained using a Bayesian framework through the Stan
   613→compiler via the cmdstanr interface using the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) of Hamiltonian
   614→Monte Carlo (HMC) algorithm, which are known for their efficiency in high-dimensional
   615→parameter spaces compared to traditional Gibbs sampling. Each model was executed with four
   616→parallel chains. We specified 3,000 warm-up iterations per chain, followed by 9,000 post-warm-
   617→up samples. A thinning strategy of taking every third sample was applied to reduce sampling
   618→autocorrelation and manage memory efficiency, resulting in 3,000 posterior samples for each
   619→parameter. Convergence was monitored using the R-hat (potential scale reduction factor) statistic
   620→(Gelman & Rubin, 1992); in all cases, R-hat values were smaller than 1.05. Furthermore, we
   621→ensured the sampling quality by monitoring the Effective Sample Size (ESS) for all estimated
   622→
   623→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 22
   624→parameters. Following official Stan recommendations (Vehtari et al., 2021), we verified that each
   625→parameter achieved a total bulk-ESS greater than 400 to ensure reliable posterior estimates.
   626→Statistical Inferences: Given the Bayesian estimation approach, we report the means and 95%
   627→credible intervals of the posterior distributions. Coefficients of dynamic transactions and
   628→moderations were regarded as statistically significant if the corresponding 95% credible intervals
   629→did not include zero.
   630→To achieve the two research aims of evaluating the relative performance of different
   631→theoretical frameworks and identifying the most plausible person-situation transaction system,
   632→we implemented a dual-strategy evaluation involving both in-sample and out-of-sample
   633→prediction. For Aim 1 of the theoretical model comparisons, we utilized two complementary in-
   634→sample evaluations based on Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Bayesian R-squared (R2).
   635→This choice was necessitated by the structural heterogeneity across our model set; while all
   636→models share personality states as a primary outcome, they do not share a common likelihood
   637→function. Specifically, univariate models (e.g., dispositional, contingency, and autogregressive
   638→models) estimate the likelihood of a single outcome, whereas more complex transactional
   639→frameworks (e.g., VAR(1) and Dynamic Contingency Model) are bivariate, deriving a joint
   640→system likelihood for both personality and situational states. Consequently, a direct comparison
   641→using likelihood-based out-of-sample metrics (such as LOO-IC) would be statistically biased.
   642→Utilizing RMSE and Bayesian R2 provides a complementary evaluation of model performance
   643→by simultaneously quantifying the proportion of behavioral variance explained and the absolute
   644→precision of behavioral predictions in original scale units. This dual-metric approach is
   645→particularly beneficial for establishing a "common currency" across structurally diverse models
   646→
   647→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 23
   648→with non-equivalent likelihood functions, ensuring that the selection of the most plausible
   649→framework is justified by both its substantive explanatory depth and its empirical accuracy.
   650→For Aim 2 and Aim 3, comparing person-situation transaction systems implemented
   651→within the DSEM framework, model evaluation relied primarily on the Leave-One-Out
   652→Information Criterion (LOO-IC) derived from Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOO-CV).
   653→Following established recommendations (Hollenbach & Montgomery, 2020; Johnson et al.,
   654→2022; Sivula et al., 2020; Vehtari et al., 2017), one model was considered meaningfully better if
   655→the difference in expected log predictive density (ELPD) exceeded four units and was at least
   656→two times larger than its standard error. The ELPD is an index of the model’s estimated out-of-
   657→sample prediction accuracy, as quantified via LOO-IC. Larger ELPDs are indicative of a better
   658→model, and in a model comparison, the largest value is automatically scaled to 0. Thus, the model
   659→that has a negative value for the ELPD difference and a positive value for its standard error is the
   660→inferior model.
   661→Results
   662→Aim 1: Identify the most plausible theoretical model
   663→To address our first research aim of identifying the most plausible theoretical model for
   664→explaining daily human behavior, we utilized a dual-strategy evaluation employing both Root
   665→Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Bayesian R2 as in-sample prediction metrics. A consistent
   666→general pattern emerged across all combinations of personality states and situational
   667→characteristics, with both metrics converging to identify the Dynamic Contingency Model
   668→(Figure 1D, iv) as the most superior model for explaining behavioral fluctuations. As shown in
   669→Table 1, the Dynamic Contingency Model achieved the lowest RMSE (ranging from 0.460 to
   670→0.687) and the highest Bayesian R2 (ranging from 0.294 to 0.628) across all conditions.
   671→

   673→While the single-factor static models (i.e., the trait model and the situational contingency
   674→model) generally performed worse relative to the more complex models, as expected, an
   675→interesting pattern emerged in the direct comparison between these two models. Both sample-
   676→level evaluative metrics consistently indicated that the simple trait model outperformed the
   677→situational contingency model in 17 of 25 (68%) personality-situation combinations. The
   678→situational contingency model only outperformed the simple trait model in the remaining 8 pairs.
   679→Critically, the contingency model's advantages were predominantly concentrated in personality–
   680→situation pairs that are theoretically relevant (Rauthmann, 2016). For example, Extraversion in
   681→Sociable Situation (ΔR² = +0.147), Neuroticism in Negative Situations (ΔR² = +0.100), and
   682→Conscientiousness in Dutiful Situations (ΔR² = +0.012). Taken together, these results
   683→demonstrate that, despite overlooking within-person variability entirely, the simple trait model
   684→still provided better descriptions and predictions of personality states in the majority of cases.
   685→The situational contingency model, by contrast, contributed meaningfully only when the
   686→situation activates a state manifestation that is salient to the relevant trait domain. This
   687→dissociation justifies the necessity of integrating both dispositional and situational factors to
   688→adequately capture the dynamics of personality behavior.
   689→Taking direct advantage of this integration, the "static" Person × Situation interaction
   690→models (Figure 1B) yielded Bayesian R² ranging from [0.252 to 0.586], consistently
   691→outperforming both single-factor models across all personality–situation combinations. Figure 3
   692→illustrates this pattern using a randomly selected participant's observed Extraversion state levels
   693→over time alongside model-derived predictions. In Figure 3A, the flat trajectory represents the
   694→trait model's time-invariant predictions. Notably, the predictions of the contingency model and
   695→the PxS interaction model largely followed the same trajectory, with differences emerging

   698→primarily as a mean shift, a consequence of the PxS model incorporating trait-informed state
   699→means, which the contingency model lacks. This pattern raises the question of whether the PxS
   700→model's improved performance reflects a genuine interaction between dispositional and
   701→situational factors, or simply the additive contribution of both. We address this more formally
   702→through supplementary analyses (see Supplementary Table 3), which examine the relative
   703→contributions of the main effects versus the interaction term in accounting for the observed
   704→improvement. Across all pairings, an additive main effects model performed nearly identically to
   705→the full PxS interaction model, indicating that the PxS model's apparent advantage stems
   706→primarily from the inclusion of both predictors rather than from their interaction.

   808→Aim 2: Examine the Dynamic Transaction of the Person-Environment System
   809→After identifying that the integrated Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) demonstrated
   810→superior performance in capturing an individual’s daily personality dynamics, we then
   811→specifically looked at the four different operationalizations of the integrated DCM specifications
   812→to examine the most plausible model capturing the full reciprocal dynamic system between
   813→person and situation variables. The four random-effects models representing alternative
   814→transactional patterns were first compared for each combination of personality and situational
   815→states, then the specific transactions from the superior model were explicitly examined.
   816→Model Performances. Consistent with our hypothesis, the bidirectional full transaction model

   818→Table 2). Notably, however, in more than half cases (56%; 14 out of 25 combinations), the
   819→person-to-situation (PT) transaction model exhibited fit comparable to the BF model. This

  1352→Discussion
  1353→For over five decades, personality and social sciences have debated which processes
  1354→govern behavioral variability: dispositional traits (Figure 1Ai; Costa & McCrae, 1999),
  1355→situational contingencies (Figure 1Aiii; Mischel & Shoda, 1995), person-situation interactions
  1356→(Figure 1B; Endler & Magnusson, 1976), or reciprocal person-environment transactions (Figure
  1357→1c; Caspi & Roberts, 2001; Rauthmann 2020). Despite this rich theoretical landscape spanning
  1358→decades, no study has systematically tested these competing models within a unified framework
  1359→enabling direct comparison. We conducted the first comprehensive empirical comparison,
  1360→examining these influential theoretical models under a unified framework using Dynamic
  1361→Structural Equation Modeling across 25 personality-situation combinations. Overall, our findings
  1362→suggest that daily behavioral processes cannot be adequately explained by a single set of
  1363→mechanisms proposed in prior theories. The best-fitting account of personality dynamics was
  1364→
  1365→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 41
  1366→instead a multifaceted model that combined time-dependent transactions, situational
  1367→contingencies, and extensive individual differences.
  1368→From Static to Dynamic: Comparing Theoretical Models of Behavioral Variability
  1369→A central contribution of this work is its comprehensive analytical evaluation of major
  1370→theoretical explanations for behavioral variability, testing theoretical models from single static
  1371→factors to multivariate time-series analyses. This exhaustive comparison revealed that while each
  1372→perspective captures meaningful variance, every single mechanism is insufficient on its own to
  1373→explain the complexity of behavior, in that each is capable of explaining only a single source of
  1374→fluctuation. As expected, both personality traits and environmental characteristics are important
  1375→in understanding behavioral variability; it is crucial to conceptualize and examine their impact,
  1376→taking the crucial factor of “time” into consideration (Larsen et al., 2009; Nilsson et al., 2017;
  1377→Wilt & Revelle, 2023).
  1378→Static Accounts of Personality Dynamics
  1379→The comparisons among the traditional static models, including the disposition trait,
  1380→situation contingency, and the PxS interaction models, revealed interesting and unexpected
  1381→findings. While the disposition trait model (Figure 1Ai) successfully captures between-person
  1382→differences in one’s “typical” state level, it overlooks the more prominent within-person
  1383→variability of personality states (Fleeson, 2001; Xi & Jackson, 2025). However, such a naïve
  1384→baseline still outperforms the long-believed winner from social-cognitive perspective, the
  1385→Situation Contingency Model (Mischel & Shoda, 1995), in the majority of cases. Critically, the
  1386→contingency model primarily attributes behavioral variability to individual differences in
  1387→situational reactivity rather than differences in typical behavioral levels. Without incorporating
  1388→dispositional traits, the model cannot account for systematic differences in typical behavioral
  1389→
  1390→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 42
  1391→levels and may therefore produce biased predictions based solely on contextual features (See
  1392→Figure 1A).
  1393→That said, in a small number of cases, the contingency models do show clear advantages
  1394→when situations are strongly relevant to the trait expression (e.g., sociable situations predicting
  1395→extraverted states). In highly affording situation-state pairs, the situational cues are salient
  1396→enough to override stable between-person differences and dominate behavioral expression
  1397→(Barrick & Mount, 1991; Hurtz & Donovan, 2000). Together, these findings suggest that
  1398→dispositional traits and situation affordances provide complementary utility for accurate
  1399→modeling of personality expressions. More specifically, the results offer empirical support for the
  1400→insight that personality dispositions provide person-specific baselines for understanding
  1401→behavioral processes, whereas the situation contingencies (i.e., if…then…) provide insight to
  1402→contextual nuances (Fleeson 2021; Xi & Jackson, 2025).
  1403→The person-situation (P×S) interaction approach (Figure 1B; Endler & Magnusson, 1976;
  1404→Tett & Burnett, 2003) was developed in response to the demand for integration of dispositional
  1405→and environmental influences on behavior. Although this framework improves upon single-factor
  1406→models (i.e., Trait Model and Contingency Model), our extended model comparisons confirm
  1407→previous concerns drawn based on the small magnitude of interaction effects (Sherman et al.,
  1408→2015; Horstmann et al., 2021), that these gains are driven primarily by the inclusion of the two
  1409→main effects rather than by the interaction term (See Supplementary Table 3). Previous
  1410→supporting evidence for meaningful person-situation interactions primarily focuses on situation-
  1411→trait relevant pairs (see review Tett et al., 2021) and is often based on workplace outcomes (e.g.,
  1412→job performance, counter-productive work behaviors) measured cross-sectionally in task-specific
  1413→contexts (Chen et al., 2013; Debusscher et al., 2016; Schmidt et al., 2012; Simonet et al., 2017).
  1414→
  1415→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 43
  1416→Given that the more rigorous tests through dense repeated measurements across naturally
  1417→occurring situations consistently fail to replicate necessary interaction effects (Horstmann et al.,
  1418→2021; Sherman et al., 2015; Xi & Jackson, 2025), the static interactional theories (e.g., Tett &
  1419→Burnett, 2003) are not the most effective representation of person-environment processes (Kuper
  1420→et al., 2021, 2023).
  1421→Time as a Crucial Facet of Personality Dynamics
  1422→While static models provide summary descriptions of behavioral patterns, dynamic
  1423→models inspect the temporal processes underlying daily fluctuations, yielding unique insights
  1424→into when and how each factor operates (Hamaker, 2012; Ram & Gerstorf, 2009). Personality
  1425→traits have traditionally been defined as temporally stable features distinguishing individuals
  1426→(Allport, 1937; McCrae & Costa, 1994), yet this conceptualization conflates two distinct forms
  1427→of stability: between-person rank-order consistency (who is typically higher than whom) and
  1428→within-person temporal continuity (how much current states predict subsequent states). The
  1429→Mixed Effect AR(1) model, extending from the static trait model, disentangles two forms of
  1430→stability: random intercepts capture one's typical behavioral level (between-person differences),
  1431→while autoregressive coefficients represent the within-person stability of continuity from time-to-
  1432→time (within-person temporal continuity). The evidence of formal model comparison shows that
  1433→the AR(1) model substantially outperforms the static trait model, indicating that systematic
  1434→within-person continuity captures meaningful variance beyond what is explained by stable
  1435→dispositional differences alone. In fact, autoregressive effects emerged as the most prominent
  1436→parameters in the entire dynamic system, even in more complex bivariate system models. Taken
  1437→together, these findings suggest that personality stability is not merely a static property of
  1438→
  1439→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 44
  1440→between-person differences but also a dynamic process unfolding through temporal continuity in
  1441→daily experience (Jongerling et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2012).
  1442→Beyond single-factor personality dynamics, numerous theoretical frameworks have
  1443→emphasized the dynamic interplay between persons and environments unfolding across time
  1444→(Kuper et al., 2021). Despite this theoretical richness, empirical operationalizations have been
  1445→largely constrained to VAR models or their continuous-time equivalents (i.e., stochastic
  1446→differential equation models, e.g., Hecht et al., 2023; Sosnowska et al., 2019), which by
  1447→construction restrict situational influence on lagged effects or rate of change in personality state
  1448→from time to time. This leaves one crucial dimension consistently unaddressed: the temporal
  1449→scale at which person-environment transactions operate. Do situations signal behavior
  1450→instantaneously, or do their effects accumulate and lag over time? By implicitly assuming that
  1451→situations shape behavior only after a temporal delay, existing approaches leave
  1452→contemporaneous situation-behavior linkages either unmodeled or absorbed into residual
  1453→covariance (Danvers et al., 2020; Mischel & Shoda, 1995; Revelle & Condon, 2015; Sosnowska
  1454→et al., 2019), resulting in a critical empirical gap in distinguishing the temporal scope of these
  1455→transactions (Rauthmann et al., 2016).
  1456→The present findings clarified the temporal scope of person-environment-transactions
  1457→through systematic comparison of the AR(1), VAR(1), and Dynamic Contingency Models
  1458→(DCM). Environment or situation signals function primarily as contemporaneous shocks rather
  1459→than temporally lasting influences. While adding lagged environment-to-person paths in the
  1460→VAR(1) model yielded no meaningful improvement over the AR(1) model, the Dynamic
  1461→Contingency Models (DCM), which explicitly models the well theorized contemporaneous
  1462→situational effects (Kuper et al., 2022; Mischel & Shoda, 1995) while controlling for
  1463→
  1464→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 45
  1465→autoregression and situational spillover, substantially outperformed both. This establishes the
  1466→primacy of immediate, within-moment situation-behavior linkages and reveals a critical mis-
  1467→parameterization in standard VAR approaches: when contemporaneous directed paths (i.e.,
  1468→situation contingency) are absent, their explanatory power becomes misallocated to lagged
  1469→pathways, producing distorted interpretations of environmental influence.
  1470→Person-Environment Transaction as a Reciprocal System
  1471→The Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) incorporates all necessary components of
  1472→personality dynamics: time-specific transactions across and within person and environment
  1473→factors, theoretical situational contingencies, and between-person differences in average levels
  1474→(i.e., traits). This broad set of components mirrors the multiple mechanisms proposed in dynamic
  1475→theories of personality. CAPS (Mischel & Shoda, 1995), for instance, theorizes both immediate
  1476→situation-behavior contingencies and prospective person-to-environment transactions through
  1477→situational selection and construal. CB5T (DeYoung, 2015) similarly distinguishes moment-to-
  1478→moment cybernetic adjustments to environmental demands from longer-term feedback processes
  1479→by which behavior shapes future environmental encounters. Moreover, the PERM (Rauthmann,
  1480→2020) further formalizes theoretically non-equivalent person-environment transaction types
  1481→reflecting various mechanism of dynamic personality processes. By providing temporal clarity
  1482→across both directions of the person-environment system, the DCM offers a principled empirical
  1483→framework for examining these theorized mechanisms without confounding them.
  1484→The Asymmetrical Temporal Transactions
  1485→With the temporal scope of person-environment transactions properly disambiguated, the
  1486→DCM revealed two novel findings about the structure of the dynamic system. First, the
  1487→bidirectional person-environment transactions exhibit asymmetric temporal dynamics. Across
  1488→
  1489→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 46
  1490→nearly all personality-situation combinations, PàS cross-lagged effects were found to be modest
  1491→but consistently robust, whereas SàP cross-lagged effects were notably weak and often
  1492→statistically negligible. This pattern extends well-established macro-level developmental findings
  1493→to daily timescales. Longitudinal research demonstrates that across years and decades,
  1494→personality traits predict life events more strongly than life events predict trait change (Bleidorn
  1495→& Denissen, 2021; Caspi & Roberts, 2001; Roberts et al., 2003; Wright & Jackson, 2023b). This
  1496→cross-scale correspondence suggests that person-driven dynamics are not merely macro-
  1497→developmental phenomena emerging over years, but fundamental properties of personality
  1498→systems operating continuously across different time scales.
  1499→Together, the autoregressive effects, contemporaneous situational contingencies, and
  1500→asymmetric cross-lagged transactions identified by the DCM provide empirical grounding for the
  1501→bidirectional feedback loop theorized across multiple dynamic personality frameworks (Cramer
  1502→et al., 2012; DeYoung, 2015; Mischel & Shoda, 1995; Rauthmann, 2021; Read et al., 2017;
  1503→Revelle & Condon, 2015; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017). The strong autoregressive effects reflect
  1504→stable internal anchors of consistency of the person-environment system, governed by cognitive-
  1505→affective units (Mischel & Shoda, 1995), cybernetic parameters (Carver & Scheier, 1982;
  1506→DeYoung, 2015), action tendency inertia (Revelle & Condon, 2015), or attractor forces (Danvers
  1507→et al., 2020; Sosnowska et al., 2019), which provide a stable baseline against which situational
  1508→influences operate. When situational features are encountered, they signal these internal
  1509→structures to produce immediate behavioral responses. In return, state manifestations shape
  1510→subsequent situational encounters through processes of situation selection, evocation, construal,
  1511→and modification (Buss, 1987; Rauthmann et al., 2015; Wrzus & Roberts, 2017), rendering
  1512→individuals as active drivers of their own environmental trajectories. The observed temporal
  1513→
  1514→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 47
  1515→asymmetry clarifies how this feedback loop operates: situations function as contemporaneous
  1516→triggers, while persons exert prospective, self-initiated influence on their future environments.
  1517→Content-Specific Regulatory Dynamics
  1518→Second, when inspecting the specific transaction patterns in the person-environment
  1519→feedback loops, we identified two distinct regulatory patterns almost exclusively conditioned to
  1520→different content domains. In cumulative continuity transactions, contemporaneous contingencies
  1521→and lagged effects operate in the same direction, creating cumulative continuity: personality
  1522→states predict congruent future situations, which in turn provide immediate affordances for
  1523→further state expression. In self-regulatory transactions (Revelle & Condon, 2015), these effects
  1524→operate in opposite directions, producing homeostatic regulation: situations trigger immediate
  1525→behavioral responses, but elevated states predict reduced subsequent situational exposure,
  1526→maintaining psychological equilibrium.
  1527→The cumulative continuity pattern was most prominent in models pairing personality
  1528→states with socio-emotional situation characteristics: Positivity, Negativity, and Sociality (See
  1529→Table 4). For example, higher extraversion predicted increased subsequent exposure to positive
  1530→and sociable environments while decreasing future encounters with negative situations; these
  1531→positive situations then provided concurrent affordances that further enhanced extraverted
  1532→expression, creating self-cumulative cycles of person-situation congruence (Caspi et al., 1989).
  1533→In socio-emotional domains, aligning with theories of intrinsic motivation and affect regulation
  1534→(Gross, 2024; Larsen, 2000), the cumulative continuity reflects a fundamental hedonic principle:
  1535→individuals actively construct social ecologies that maximize rewarding experiences by
  1536→maximizing positive affect and minimizing distress, creating self-reinforcing cycles where state
  1537→
  1538→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 48
  1539→manifestations both shape and are sustained by affectively congruent environments (Caspi et al.,
  1540→1989; Diener, 1984; Lyubomirsky et al., 2005).
  1541→Conversely, the self-regulatory adjustment pattern was predominantly observed in
  1542→instrumental-cognitive situations (e.g., High Duty and Intellect). Dutiful situations elicited
  1543→immediate increases in conscientious behavior to meet environmental demands, yet elevated
  1544→conscientiousness negatively predicted subsequent exposure to dutiful or intellectual situations.
  1545→These inverse temporal effects reveal a compensatory mechanism: individuals modulate state
  1546→expression and situational choices to restore baseline equilibrium following periods of intensive
  1547→cognitive demand (Baumeister et al., 2018; Carver & Scheier, 2001; Inzlicht et al., 2014). The
  1548→emergence of this self-regulatory pattern exclusively within instrumental-cognitive domains can
  1549→be attributed to the effortful nature of agentic and task-related functioning, for example
  1550→organized, diligent behavior or deep analytical thinking, require significant effortful control and
  1551→the expenditure of finite regulatory resources (Baumeister et al., 2024, 2018; Wenzel et al.,
  1552→2024). According to CB5T (DeYoung, 2007) and Resource Conservation models, personality
  1553→states function as dynamic systems that seek to minimize "entropy" or depletion. Consequently,
  1554→once a dutiful task is fulfilled through a peak in conscientious state expression, the individual
  1555→may proactively avoid further instrumental demands to allow for psychological recovery and a
  1556→return to their baseline "set point".
  1557→Individual Differences in Dynamic Processes
  1558→We observed substantial individual differences in the magnitude of dynamic transactions.
  1559→This heterogeneity was evident across all model parameters but was particularly pronounced for
  1560→cross-lagged effects. Despite the pervasiveness of individual variability, the random effects
  1561→exhibited theoretically coherent patterns between autoregressive effects and mutual impacts
  1562→
  1563→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 49
  1564→across factors. That is, individuals with greater temporal stability tended to show weaker mutual
  1565→transactions across person and situational factors (see Supplementary Table 8). Although the
  1566→theoretical patterns identified in the present study appear to characterize the sample at large, they
  1567→do not uniformly account for the behavioral dynamics of every individual, highlighting the need
  1568→for person-specific approaches to modeling dynamic person–environment systems.
  1569→Despite the magnitude of this heterogeneity, Big Five personality traits explained
  1570→observed differences in dynamic processing only modestly and inconsistently. Linear moderation
  1571→effects were sparse, emerging primarily for Neuroticism and Openness. Quadratic moderation
  1572→tests provided no support for the NIPS model's prediction that trait extremity indexes "strong
  1573→persons" with heightened autoregressive stability and reduced situational responsiveness (Blum
  1574→et al., 2018); neither the hypothesized U-shaped relationship with autoregression nor the inverted
  1575→pattern for situational contingencies was observed. Instead, the Big-Five personality traits
  1576→reliably accounted for differences in mean of personality and situation state, that is, the average
  1577→levels around which individuals’ momentary states fluctuate, as well as for overall within-person
  1578→variability (See Supplementary Table 6 & 7; Xi & Jackson, 2025). Hence, in other words, traits
  1579→provide descriptive summaries of the distribution of one’s behavioral outcomes but do not serve
  1580→as explanatory mechanisms of underlying dynamics.
  1581→However, this does not necessarily mean that individual differences in dynamic processes
  1582→are purely stochastic or untraceable. Instead, the lack of robust moderation by the Big Five
  1583→personality traits suggests that the commonly used lower-dimensional framework may lack the
  1584→granularity to capture the complexities of process-level heterogeneity in a dynamic system. This
  1585→calls for a shift toward a higher-dimensional understanding of personality processes. From
  1586→higher-dimensional trait frameworks including facets, aspects, or nuances beneath the major
  1587→
  1588→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 50
  1589→traits (Irwing et al., 2023; Mõttus et al., 2017), to person-specific organization of personality
  1590→constructs (Beck & Jackson, 2020a, 2020b), to person-specific organizations of encoded norm,
  1591→cultures, motivations, expectancies, affects, goals, regulatory strategies, and cognitive-behavioral
  1592→process revealed from one’s life narratives (McAdams et al., 2004). By moving beyond the broad
  1593→Big Five, it is more likely to uncover the deterministic features of the personality system from
  1594→which trait-level outcomes ultimately emerge.
  1595→Limitations and Future Directions
  1596→The present findings should be interpreted within the scope of several methodological
  1597→constraints that simultaneously point toward critical directions for advancing the study of
  1598→personality dynamics.
  1599→Sample representativeness. The sample consisted exclusively of undergraduate students
  1600→from an upscale private university. Although this population afforded high compliance and data
  1601→quality in an intensive longitudinal design, it necessarily limits generalizability across age,
  1602→socioeconomic status, occupational roles, and cultural contexts. University students occupy a
  1603→developmentally distinctive life stage marked by relative environmental homogeneity high social
  1604→density, and transitional life structures, which may lead to unique regulatory dynamics. Future
  1605→studies should extend these models to community samples spanning broader age ranges, life
  1606→roles, and cultural ecologies to evaluate whether the observed asymmetries and content-specific
  1607→transaction patterns generalize or vary systematically across contexts.
  1608→Measurement scope and modality. The current study operationalized daily behavioral and
  1609→environmental factors as personality states and situational characteristics assessed exclusively via
  1610→self-report using the Big Five and DIAMONDS frameworks. While these models provide
  1611→valuable nomothetic structure, they capture only subjectively construed experience and may miss
  1612→
  1613→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 51
  1614→or even bias the measurement of true behavioral or environmental processes. Future research
  1615→would benefit from multi-method approaches incorporating informant reports, physiological
  1616→indicators (e.g., cortisol, heart rate variability), and passive phone technologies (e.g., mobility,
  1617→social interaction, and communication).
  1618→Temporal Resolution and Multi-Scale Dynamics. The study employed a single timescale,
  1619→six surveys per day at approximately two-hour intervals, resulting the data largely limited to a
  1620→narrow temporal bandwidth. Personality processes, however, unfold across multiple timescales
  1621→simultaneously: moment-to-moment affective fluctuations (seconds to minutes), daily routines
  1622→and social interactions (hours), weekly patterns and goal pursuit cycles (days to weeks), and
  1623→developmental trajectories (months to years). Future research should adopt multi-scale designs
  1624→that nest fine-grained momentary assessments within longer developmental windows, with burst
  1625→designs or integrating ecological momentary assessment with longitudinal follow-ups. This
  1626→would allow examination of how micro-level transactions aggregate into macro-level patterns,
  1627→how different timescales interact (e.g., how daily dynamics shift across developmental
  1628→transitions), and how dynamic parameters themselves exhibit stability or change over extended
  1629→periods.
  1630→Conclusion
  1631→The present study introduced the Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM), a unified
  1632→analytical framework that empirically reconciles competing theories of personality dynamics
  1633→within a single multilevel system. Across 25 personality-situation combinations, the DCM
  1634→consistently outperformed dispositional, situational, interactional, and traditional transactional
  1635→alternatives, demonstrating that daily personality functioning cannot be adequately understood
  1636→
  1637→Dynamic Contingency Model (DCM) 52
  1638→through any single mechanism. Rather, advancing understanding of personality processes
  1639→requires the simultaneous incorporation of traits, context, time, and individual differences.
