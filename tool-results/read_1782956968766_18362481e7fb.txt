    23‚ÜíAbstract
    24‚ÜíBackground: Attitudes towards menstruation are shaped by cultural and social norms and can
    25‚Üíresult in misperceptions, impacting women‚Äôs health. Yet, research on cultural differences in
    26‚Üímenstrual health remains limited.
    27‚ÜíObjectives: This study aimed to examine whether South Asian and White British women living
    28‚Üíin the UK differ in menstrual health, with a focus on menstrual experiences, menstrual literacy,
    29‚Üístigma-related factors, and ethnic microaggressions in the healthcare context.
    30‚ÜíDesign: White British (n = 153) and South Asian (n = 153) menstruating women living in the UK
    31‚Üíparticipated in a preregistered cross-sectional online survey.
    32‚ÜíMethods: Menstrual experiences were assessed via menstrual distress, symptom management, and
    33‚Üíhygiene behaviors. Menstrual stigma was measured through affective internalized stigma,
    34‚Üíinternalized stigma of seeking help, secrecy, and avoidance behaviors.
    35‚ÜíResults: In addition to differences in menstrual experiences (menstrual pain, symptom
    36‚Üímanagement, hygiene behaviors), South Asian women reported lower menstrual literacy than
    37‚ÜíWhite British women. South Asians also reported greater secrecy as well as stronger avoidance
    38‚Üíbehaviors related to discussing menstruation and engaging in daily activities while menstruating,
    39‚Üíbut also more positive emotions towards menstruation. Additionally, they experienced more ethnic
    40‚Üímicroaggressions in healthcare. Secrecy and microaggressions mediated the effect of ethnic group
    41‚Üíon self-stigma of seeking help.
    42‚ÜíConclusion: Our findings suggest cultural differences in menstrual health, emphasizing the need
    43‚Üífor inclusive health education and stigma-reduction interventions. Addressing menstrual literacy
    44‚Üígaps, and tackling stigma in family and microaggressions in healthcare settings may improve
    45‚Üísupport for South Asian women in the UK.

    55‚ÜíIntroduction
    56‚ÜíMenstrual health is a key aspect of public health, and the menstrual cycle is a key
    57‚Üíindicator of women‚Äôs health (1). In line with the World Health Organization‚Äôs definition of
    58‚Üíhealth, menstrual health is ‚Äúa state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not
    59‚Üímerely the absence of disease or infirmity, in relation to the menstrual cycle‚Äù (2, p.23).
    60‚ÜíImproving menstrual health is essential for global health and aligns with the UN Sustainable
    61‚ÜíDevelopment Goals on health (SDG 3) and gender equality (SDG 5). Menstrual health impacts
    62‚Üíphysical health (e.g., reproductive tract infections), mental health (e.g., depression), and social
    63‚Üíwellbeing (e.g., attending school or work).
    64‚ÜíMenstrual stigma, rooted in broader social, economic, and structural inequalities,
    65‚Üíperpetuates and exacerbates gender inequality (3). The impacts of menstrual stigma on mental
    66‚Üíand physical health, as well as social wellbeing are particularly harmful for marginalized groups
    67‚Üísuch as people from ethnic minority backgrounds (4,5). Education is inadequate, and
    68‚Üímarginalized group members can be reluctant to seek help for menstrual pain or other symptoms
    69‚Üídue to stigma from healthcare professionals (6,7). However, menstrual health remains
    70‚Üíunderstudied in cross-cultural contexts. Most studies on menstrual stigma examine cultures in
    71‚Üíisolation or across countries, overlooking the experiences of minority groups within dominant
    72‚Üícultural settings. Little is known about how menstrual experiences, literacy, and stigma operate
    73‚Üíwhere cultural norms and marginalization intersect, limiting understanding of related health
    74‚Üíinequalities. The aim of this study therefore was to examine whether South Asian and White
    75‚ÜíBritish women living in the UK differ in menstrual health, with a focus on 1) menstrual
    76‚Üíexperiences, 2) menstrual literacy, 3) stigma-related factors, and 4) ethnic microaggressions in
    77‚Üíthe healthcare context.

    82‚ÜíMenstrual Health in Different Cultures
    83‚ÜíMenstrual Experiences
    84‚ÜíExperiences of menstrual symptoms are common, with 85% of women reporting pain and
    85‚Üí77% reporting psychological complaints (8). Yet, findings on cross-cultural differences are
    86‚Üíinconsistent: Some studies find higher levels of dysmenorrhea or premenstrual and menstrual
    87‚Üísymptoms among Western populations (e.g., Australians, Caucasians, Americans) compared to
    88‚ÜíOriental, Indian or Afro-Caribbean populations (9‚Äì11), while others report the opposite pattern
    89‚Üí(12). The substantial heterogeneity in study populations (e.g., age range, presence of menstrual
    90‚Üídisorders) and methodological approaches limits comparability across studies and conclusions
    91‚Üíthat can be drawn, requiring further research. For this reason, rather than testing hypotheses, we
    92‚Üíexplored differences in menstrual distress between White British and South Asian women.
    93‚ÜíThe findings on menstrual distress mirror research findings on ethnic differences in pain
    94‚Üísensitivity more generally, including greater pain sensitivity among minority groups (13,14),
    95‚Üíwith South Asians in the UK reporting more intense, unpleasant pain and lower thresholds than
    96‚ÜíWhite British women (15). This supports a biopsychosocial account and raises the question of
    97‚Üíwhether differences are biological or shaped by social and psychological factors. For example,
    98‚Üímenstrual pain may be more readily normalized in some Eastern contexts (9), whereas Western
    99‚Üídiscourses simultaneously medicalize and trivialize menstruation (1,16‚Äì18), framing changes as
   100‚Üísymptoms and blood as pollution, yet as mundane or ‚Äònot real‚Äô. Regarding menstrual symptoms
   101‚Üímanagement, use of pain medication and psychological therapy as well as social support might
   102‚Üíthus be greater among White British women, with mental health stigma, but also cultural views
   103‚Üíon suffering, limiting therapy use and perceived social support among South Asian women

   108‚Üí(19,20). Due to the lack of literature for the other types of menstrual symptom management, we
   109‚Üíexplored differences for these.
   110‚ÜíCultures also differ in their menstrual hygiene behaviors, with White people being more
   111‚Üílikely to use unscented tampons and menstrual cups than Black people, whereas Black people
   112‚Üíbeing more likely to use douches and wipes than White people (21,22). Systematic and avoidable
   113‚Üídifferences in menstrual health lead to negative physical (e.g. reproductive tract infections) and
   114‚Üíemotional (e.g. anxiety, distress) outcomes (23). Given the lack of research with South Asians,
   115‚Üírather than testing hypotheses, we explored differences in menstrual hygiene behaviors between
   116‚ÜíWhite British and South Asian women.
   117‚ÜíMenstrual Health Literacy
   118‚ÜíMenstrual health literacy can be seen as a subcomponent of health literacy, and
   119‚Üíunderstood as the set of cognitive and social abilities that enable individuals to access, interpret,
   120‚Üíand utilize information concerning menstruation, with the aim of promoting good menstrual
   121‚Üíhealth and well-being (24). Knowledge about menstruation remains limited in both Western and
   122‚ÜíEastern cultures, but with distinct patterns. In Eastern cultures, adolescent girls are often
   123‚Üíuninformed or misinformed before menarche, typically learning from their mothers. In Western
   124‚Üícultures, formal education often provides higher initial knowledge and preparedness, yet
   125‚Üídiscomfort in discussing menstruation continues to hinder comprehensive understanding (9,25‚Äì
   126‚Üí29). We predicted for White British women to report greater menstrual health literacy, in
   127‚Üíparticular regarding the causes of menstruation, than South Asian women (26). We explored
   128‚Üídifferences for the other menstrual literacy items, as South Asian women in the UK in theory
   129‚Üíhave similar access to education as White British women, yet educational inequalities in the UK
   130‚Üípersist (30).

   135‚ÜíMenstrual Stigma
   136‚ÜíMenstruation is a universal physiological process, yet misconceptions and negative
   137‚Üíattitudes toward it are widespread, harming the rights and quality of life of those who menstruate
   138‚Üí(7,31). Public stigma arises through social and cultural interactions that mark individuals as
   139‚Üídifferent and devalued, leading to discrimination (32). Menstrual stigma fits all three of
   140‚ÜíGoffman‚Äôs (1963) categories of stigma (16): it frames the body as unclean, the character as
   141‚Üíflawed when periods are visible, and the group identity as inferior by contrasting menstruating
   142‚Üíwomen with the normative male body. Stigma from the general public can be internalized by the
   143‚Üístigmatized individual. A core aspect of internalized menstrual stigma is concealment (33).
   144‚ÜíTerror management theory suggests that menstruation symbolizes human vulnerability,
   145‚Üíprompting existential anxiety and the urge to hide it (34). Objectification theory further explains
   146‚Üíthat women may internalize the male gaze and engage in self-objectification, motivating
   147‚Üíconcealment of menstruation (16,35). Stigma is reinforced by taboos and media messages that
   148‚Üístress secrecy to avoid embarrassment. Women who view menstruation as shameful are more
   149‚Üílikely to change behavior to withdraw from public life (36).
   150‚ÜíMenstrual stigma exacerbates gender and health inequalities, and is embedded in
   151‚Üíhistorical, cultural, and religious beliefs (7,37,38). In many Eastern cultures, menstruation is
   152‚Üíviewed positively as a natural process, yet simultaneously regarded as impure. In Western
   153‚Üícultures, patriarchal perspectives dominate, framing female bodies as inferior and menstruation
   154‚Üías dirty (9,16,26,33,38,39). Stigma manifestation takes overt and covert forms depending on
   155‚Üícultural setting. In Eastern cultures, stigma tends to be visible, expressed through formal physical
   156‚Üíseparation or restrictions on activities such as worship or household. Across South Asian
   157‚Üícontexts, menstruation is often framed as a temporary state of impurity in which women are
   158‚Üí
   159‚Üí8
   160‚Üí

   162‚Üíconsidered as ‚Äòritually unclean‚Äô, and therefore forbidden to pray or engage in sexual relations
   163‚Üíuntil after they are deemed pure, either after the menstruation has passed or after they have
   164‚Üíengaged in specific cleansing practices at the end of the menstruation. While there is religious
   165‚Üíheterogeneity and religion can play a part, South Asians tend to collectively hold such beliefs
   166‚Üí(40,41). In Western cultures, stigma is more hidden, reflected in expectations to conceal
   167‚Üímenstruation, use euphemisms, and maintain silence, resulting in the topic rarely being discussed
   168‚Üíopenly (7,16,38). Due to the physical separation in South Asian cultures, Western cultures may
   169‚Üíhave a higher tendency to rely on social support to manage their menstrual symptoms than
   170‚ÜíEastern cultures. Also, help-seeking patterns in the context of menstruation somewhat differ
   171‚Üíbetween cultures. In Eastern cultures, women often experience delayed diagnosis and undertreatment of menstrual disorders, compounded by discomfort in consulting male doctors. In
   172‚ÜíWestern cultures, delays and under-treatment also occur, though the discomfort with male
   173‚Üípractitioners is less emphasized (28). We therefore predicted for South Asian women to report
   174‚Üígreater stigma towards menstruation than White British, specifically in terms of affective
   175‚Üíinternalized stigma, internalized stigma of seeking help, secrecy, and avoidance behaviors
   176‚Üírelating to men, male health professionals, prayers, and sexual intimacy. Given menstruation is
   177‚Üíviewed more positively in South Asian cultures, we also measured positive emotions associated
   178‚Üíwith menstruation, to identify potentially distinct affective stigma profiles across cultural groups,
   179‚Üírather than assuming stigma is exclusively negative.
   180‚ÜíEthnic Microaggressions in Healthcare
   181‚ÜíIn the UK, South Asian women not only navigate cultural norms around menstruation but
   182‚Üíalso face the challenges of minority status and discrimination in healthcare. Research findings
   183‚Üíwith South Asian women in their countries of origin may not necessarily generalize to South
   184‚Üí

   188‚ÜíAsian women living in a Western dominant society like the UK (19). In such settings, South
   189‚ÜíAsian women are both an ethnic minority and embedded in a social environment with different
   190‚Üícultural norms. Consequently, they may face public stigma not only related to menstruation but
   191‚Üíalso linked to their minority status (42). For example, South Asian patients reported being
   192‚Üídiscriminated against by primary care services, and concerns about a lack of cultural awareness
   193‚Üí(43). Perceived discrimination has been shown to negatively impact both mental and physical
   194‚Üíhealth (44). Microaggressions are a more subtle, indirect but especially impactful form of
   195‚Üíprejudice that manifests in brief, everyday interactions, as microinsults, microinvalidation or
   196‚Üímicroassault. Significant harm to physical and mental health is caused due to their ambiguity and
   197‚Üírepetition (45). We predicted that South Asian women would experience greater ethnic
   198‚Üímicroaggressions in healthcare when seeking help for menstrual symptoms than White British.
   199‚ÜíThe Present Study
   200‚ÜíTaken together, research shows that menstrual health is shaped by cultural norms and
   201‚Üísocial structures, with important consequences for wellbeing and help-seeking. While studies
   202‚Üíwithin different cultures have highlighted differences in menstrual experiences, literacy, and
   203‚Üístigma, research directly comparing menstrual experiences, literacy and stigma between Eastern
   204‚Üíand Western cultures is limited, with mixed results (9‚Äì12). In addition, little is known about how
   205‚Üíthese factors manifest across cultural groups within the same national context (12,22),
   206‚Üíconsidering the group status (majority vs. minority) and discrimination associated with such
   207‚Üístatus. The current study addressees this gap by directly comparing South Asian and White
   208‚ÜíBritish women in the UK in menstrual health. Specifically, we 1) compare White British with
   209‚ÜíSouth Asian women living in the UK as a minority group in a dominant Western society, 2)
   210‚Üíexamine cultural differences in menstrual health, focusing on menstrual experiences, literacy,

   216‚Üíinsights into the process of internalized stigma.
   217‚ÜíWe assessed all three dimensions (i.e., physical, mental, social) of menstrual health (2)
   218‚Üíduring the menstruation phase. This phase was selected because of its relevance to both
   219‚Üíinternalized and public stigma: concerning the former, menstrual blood is the most concealed
   220‚Üímenstrual mark by people who menstruate (46,47); as for the latter, menstruation (i.e., bleeding
   221‚Üíphase) is the most subjected to cultural prescriptions (e.g., 43). Menstrual distress is defined as
   222‚Üíthe interference with quality of life, recreational or work activities, and social relationships
   223‚Üíresulting from menstrual cycle symptoms (49). We preregistered (50) and tested the following
   224‚Üíhypotheses for the respective research questions:
   225‚ÜíMenstrual Experiences
   226‚ÜíRQ1: Do South Asian and White British women differ in their self-reported menstrual
   227‚Üíexperiences?
   228‚ÜíHypothesis 1: South Asians and White British differ in the types of menstrual symptoms
   229‚Üímanagement. White British (vs. South Asians) report the use of social support (family, partner
   230‚Üíand friends); pain medication; and psychological therapy more often.
   231‚ÜíMenstrual Literacy
   232‚ÜíRQ2: Do South Asian and White British women differ in their menstrual literacy?
   233‚ÜíHypothesis 2: South Asians and White British differ in menstrual literacy: White British
   234‚Üíare more likely to attribute the cause of menstruation to a physiological cause than South Asians.
   235‚ÜíMenstrual Stigma
   236‚ÜíRQ3: Do South Asian and White British women differ in menstrual stigma?

   268‚ÜíParticipants
   269‚ÜíParticipants were 153 South Asian and 153 White British individuals living in the UK
   270‚Üí(Ntotal = 306) who identified as cis women, had menstruated in the past 12 months and were aged
   271‚Üíbetween 18 and 50 years. The sample was restricted to cisgender women to limit heterogeneity
   272‚Üírelated to intersecting forms of stigma (e.g., related to gender identity), which were beyond the
   273‚Üíscope of the present study. They were recruited from the general UK population via an online
   274‚Üísurvey on Qualtrics (duration: 30 minutes) which was distributed on Prolific. Informed consent
   275‚Üíwas taken in writing from the participants prior to beginning of the survey. For further
   276‚Üídemographics see Tables 1 and 2. Our target sample size of 300 participants was determined by
   277‚Üíthe available funding for Prolific (3 GBP per participant). A power analysis using G*Power (52)

   279‚Üísample size of approximately 298 participants would be required to detect a medium effect size

   429‚ÜíAnalyses were conducted using IBM SPSS Statistics 29.0 (t-tests, MANOVAs, Chisquare tests) and JASP Version 0.19.3 (mediation analyses). To examine correlations between all
   430‚Üímeasures, Pearson‚Äôs product-moment correlations were computed separately for South Asian and

   432‚Üídemographic characteristics, identity variables, menstrual literacy, internalized stigma of seeking
   433‚Üíhelp, secrecy, and ethnic microaggressions in healthcare. To test group differences in menstrual
   434‚Üíexperiences and stigma outcomes, a series of multivariate analyses of variance (MANOVAs)
   435‚Üíwere conducted, followed by univariate F tests. Specifically, MANOVAs were used to assess
   436‚Üícultural group differences in: (a) menstrual distress and symptom frequency, (b) 13 symptom
   437‚Üímanagement strategies, (c) eight menstrual hygiene behaviors, (d) affective internalized stigma
   438‚Üí(3 subscales), and (e) 11 avoidance behaviors. Chi-square tests of independence were used to test
   439‚Üígroup differences in menstrual literacy. Effect sizes were reported as partial eta squared (Œ∑¬≤p) for
   440‚ÜíMANOVAs and phi (œÜ) for chi-square tests. Mediation analyses (single mediator, two parallel

   571‚ÜíDiscussion
   572‚ÜíPrevious research has consistently evidenced that stigma surrounding menstruation is
   573‚Üíuniversal (7), yet suggests potential cultural differences. The present study sought to advance
   574‚Üíunderstanding about how menstrual health manifest across cultural groups within the same
   575‚Üínational context. By directly comparing South Asian and White British women in the UK, we
   576‚Üíinvestigated differences in menstrual experiences, literacy, stigma-related factors, and ethnic
   577‚Üímicroaggressions in healthcare, considering group status (majority vs. minority) and associated
   578‚Üídiscrimination. Our findings revealed cultural differences in how menstruation is perceived,
   579‚Üímanaged, and stigmatized across groups, with important implications for health and gender
   580‚Üíequity.
   581‚ÜíFirst, South Asian and White British women differed in their self-reported menstrual
   582‚Üíexperiences. White British women more frequently used medication (i.e., contraceptives and
   583‚Üíantidepressants), whereas South Asian women relied more on home-based (i.e., staying at home,
   584‚Üícalling in sick at work) and non-pharmacological strategies (i.e., massage and diet). This may be
   585‚Üí
   586‚Üí24
   587‚Üí
   588‚ÜíCULTURAL DIFFERENCES IN MENSTRUAL HEALTH
   589‚Üíexplained by the greater medicalization of menstruation in Western cultures (16,17). South
   590‚ÜíAsians may also prefer traditional medicine over Western medicine (63). Hygiene practices
   591‚Üídiffered as well, with White British more likely to use tampons and South Asians more likely to
   592‚Üíuse pads, douches and feminine sprays. This mirrors previous research in which White
   593‚Üíparticipants were more likely to use unscented tampons and Black participants more likely to use
   594‚Üídouches and wipes, which may relate to socio-cultural differences in body taboos and
   595‚Üíappropriate practices (22).
   596‚ÜíSouth Asian women reported greater distress from menstrual pain than White British
   597‚Üíwomen in the UK. This is in line with the mixed findings on menstrual symptoms, with some
   598‚Üístudies finding greater severity in Asian women relating to menstrual pain (12) and general pain
   599‚Üí(13,15), while other studies find greater severity of premenstrual and menstrual symptoms in
   600‚ÜíCaucasian women (9‚Äì11). Broader work shows greater pain sensitivity among minority groups
   601‚Üí(13).
   602‚ÜíSecond, South Asian women reported lower levels of menstrual health literacy, with a
   603‚Üísmall-to-moderate effect size for the full scale, and a small effect size for the physiological cause
   604‚Üíitem, in line with previous research which also found lower levels of menstrual literacy in
   605‚ÜíEastern compared to Western cultures (9,25‚Äì29). In Eastern cultures, misinformation often
   606‚Üícomes from mothers who also often have low menstrual literacy or are hesitant to openly address
   607‚Üísensitive topics (28,29). Additionally, ethnic minorities living in a Western culture may still
   608‚Üíexperience educational inequalities, limiting access to relevant information (30).
   609‚ÜíThird, South Asian women showed greater menstrual stigma than White British women.
   610‚ÜíThey reported a stronger tendency toward secrecy and avoidance behaviors around menstruation
   611‚Üícompared to White British women (see also (28,38)). Specifically, South Asian women reported
   612‚Üí
   613‚Üí25
   614‚Üí
   615‚ÜíCULTURAL DIFFERENCES IN MENSTRUAL HEALTH
   616‚Üísignificantly more avoidance of various activities while menstruating, including talking to men
   617‚Üíabout menstruation, seeking support from both male and female healthcare professionals,
   618‚Üíengaging in prayers and sexual intimacy. In South Asian cultures, menstruating women are often
   619‚Üíframed temporary impure and unclean, and therefore forbidden to engage in activities such as
   620‚Üíprayers or sexual intimacy (40,41). South Asian cultural values emphasize interdependence,
   621‚Üíemotional restraint, and the preservation of social harmony. Open discussion of taboo topics is
   622‚Üíoften discouraged, with communication tending to be indirect and nonverbal. Illness and bodily
   623‚Üímatters are frequently viewed as family concerns rather than individual issues, creating pressure
   624‚Üíto protect family reputation and prevent embarrassment. These cultural dynamics help explain
   625‚Üíwhy South Asian women in our study demonstrated greater secrecy and avoidance when it came
   626‚Üíto menstruation (64). Interestingly, while stigma was generally higher among South Asian
   627‚Üíwomen, they also reported more positive emotional responses to menstruation, in line with the
   628‚Üíliterature (38). The literature exploring positive menstrual experiences is currently limited.
   629‚ÜíFindings on menstrual joy suggest that priming women to consider positive aspects of
   630‚Üímenstruation can change their attitudes towards menstruation, reinforcing that menstrual
   631‚Üíattitudes are influenced by cognitive framing (e.g., culture, research) rather than just physical
   632‚Üíexperiences (65,66).
   633‚ÜíMenstrual literacy, secrecy and microaggressions predicated stigma-related factors,
   634‚Üíparticularly for South Asian women. Secrecy was a mediator between cultural group and
   635‚Üíavoidance behaviors, as well as affective internalized stigma, together with microaggressions as
   636‚Üíparallel mediator. Menstrual literacy emerged as a mediator between cultural group and affective
   637‚Üíinternalized stigma, secrecy, and avoidance behaviors. In line with the literature, a core
   638‚Üímanifestation of menstrual stigma is the need for concealment (33). Stigma is perpetuated
   639‚Üí
   640‚Üí26
   641‚Üí
   642‚ÜíCULTURAL DIFFERENCES IN MENSTRUAL HEALTH
   643‚Üíthrough the taboo of communication about menstruation as well as through the media, which
   644‚Üífurther emphasizes ‚Äòfeeling fresh‚Äô (scented pads), avoiding embarrassment (leaks, pads showing
   645‚Üíthrough clothing), and secrecy (using blue instead of red liquid) (16). Research shows that
   646‚Üíwomen who think that menstruation and the cycle should be kept as secrets and feel embarrassed
   647‚Üíby them reported engaging in more behavioral change aimed at isolating themselves from the
   648‚Üípublic sphere (36). In line with terror management theory, the female body, menstruation and its
   649‚Üíreproductive power are believed to represent a more visible reminder of the fragility and
   650‚Üífallibility of humankind, leading to existential angst and the need for concealment (34).
   651‚ÜíFurthermore, according to objectification theory (35), in societies that view women‚Äôs bodies as
   652‚Üísexual objects, women may internalize such views and adopt a perspective on their body through
   653‚Üíthe lens of a male gaze. Such self-objectification may motivate women to make extra efforts at
   654‚Üíhiding their periods (16). Fourth, South Asian women reported more frequent experiences of
   655‚Üíethnic microaggressions in healthcare settings, mirroring findings reporting Asian Americans
   656‚Üíreporting being less satisfied with their doctors due to them not listening or not including them in
   657‚Üídecisions concerning their health (67). Our results, which point to an intersection between ethnic
   658‚Üíand menstrual stigma in predicting help seeking behaviors, are consistent with recent findings.
   659‚ÜíSpecifically, Webster and Iyer (68) found significant relationships between perceived ethnic
   660‚Üídiscrimination during GP consultations in the UK and medication side-effects and adherence.
   661‚ÜíTogether, these findings suggest that the experience of ethnic discrimination within the
   662‚Üíhealthcare settings is preventing people from accessing adequate care, either by discouraging
   663‚Üímedication adherence or by dampening willingness to seek help, ultimately reinforcing and
   664‚Üímaintaining widespread health inequalities (69).

   669‚ÜíWe interpret the findings primarily through the lens of cultural values and norms, guided
   670‚Üíby prior research. However, alternative explanations may also account for the observed
   671‚Üídifferences, including religion, socioeconomic factors, migration background, education, and
   672‚Üíhealthcare experiences. While we measured subjective socioeconomic status, migration
   673‚Üíbackground and healthcare experiences, future research should build on these findings to
   674‚Üíexamine such additional or complementary explanations in greater detail.
   675‚ÜíLimitations and Future Research
   676‚ÜíSeveral limitations warrant consideration. First, the study relied on self-report measures,
   677‚Üíwhich are subject to social desirability bias and may not fully capture nuanced cultural
   678‚Üíexperiences. The menstrual hygiene behaviors scale used in this study includes response
   679‚Üícategories with mixed temporal frames, i.e. reporting on the frequency of use in the past year,
   680‚Üíwith response categories referring to monthly, weekly or daily use and "during my period". This
   681‚Üímay introduce ambiguity in how participants interpret and report their behaviors. Although this
   682‚Üímeasure was adopted from prior research (22) to support comparability, it may not fully capture
   683‚Üíconsistent or precise frequency of product use. Future studies would benefit from more
   684‚Üístandardized and clearly defined frequency-based measures. Similarly, the low internal validity
   685‚Üíof some menstrual distress clusters (i.e., pain) should be taken in consideration when interpreting
   686‚Üíthe results. Future studies could investigate the menstrual distress scale reliability by cluster
   687‚Üífurther, also considering the overall heterogeneity of menstrual distress items and the inherent
   688‚Üíwithin person variability in symptoms reporting across menstrual cycles.
   689‚ÜíSecond, while we targeted two broad ethnic categories, cultural heterogeneity within
   690‚Üíthese groups was not assessed. South Asian identity, for example, encompasses multiple
   691‚Üínational, religious, and linguistic backgrounds that may differentially impact menstrual health.
   692‚Üí
   693‚Üí28
   694‚Üí
   695‚ÜíCULTURAL DIFFERENCES IN MENSTRUAL HEALTH
   696‚ÜíFuture research should consider examining such nuances. Third, while our sample was
   697‚Üísufficiently powered for the planned analyses, it may not be representative of the wider UK
   698‚Üípopulation, particularly as participants were recruited online and may have differed in terms of
   699‚Üídigital literacy or openness to discussing menstruation.
   700‚ÜíFourth, we did not assess gynecological conditions which may, for certain cultural group,
   701‚Üíexacerbate menstrual symptoms distress, ultimately potentially increasing avoidance behaviors
   702‚Üí(e.g., isolation, withdrawal from social activities) and reducing help seeking behaviors. Future
   703‚Üístudies should build on our findings and conduct a culturally sensitive analysis (e.g., 63,64) of
   704‚Üíhow gynecological conditions might modulate the relationships found in our study, while also
   705‚Üíassessing relevant control variables (e.g., age at menstrual education, age at menarche, chronic
   706‚Üíhealth conditions, menstrual cycle regularity, reproductive status).
   707‚ÜíFinally, the cross-sectional design does not allow causal interpretations, especially for the
   708‚Üímediation analyses. Future research should consider longitudinal or experimental designs to
   709‚Üíestablish directionality.
   710‚ÜíImplications
   711‚ÜíMenstrual stigma needs addressing as a determinant of health and equality. Inequalities
   712‚Üírelated to menstrual health extend beyond the biological domain, affecting experiences across
   713‚Üíeducation, employment, and social life. By reducing ethnic and menstrual stigma and improving
   714‚Üímenstrual health, society also takes steps towards realization of the United Nations SDGs (3).
   715‚ÜíThis involves ensuring accurate and inclusive information is available in schools, families,
   716‚Üíhealthcare, and the media. Embedding menstrual health education into school curricula equips
   717‚Üíyoung people with knowledge that challenges stigma and builds health literacy. At the same
   718‚Üítime, health systems need to provide fair and timely diagnosis and treatment of menstrual
   719‚Üí
   720‚Üí29
   721‚Üí
   722‚ÜíCULTURAL DIFFERENCES IN MENSTRUAL HEALTH
   723‚Üíconditions, regardless of the patients‚Äô ethnic background. Women‚Äôs health remains
   724‚Üíunderrepresented in research (1). Expanding research on menstrual health and health
   725‚Üídiscrimination will provide the evidence base necessary to inform effective interventions, guide
   726‚Üípolicy development, and embed menstrual health as a central concern of global health equity.
