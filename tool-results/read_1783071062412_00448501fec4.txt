    13→Abstract 
    14→Background: Research has shown high levels of under-diagnosis of Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD), with pathways for assessment and support for DCD that are convoluted, difficult to navigate and not fit for purpose. This research therefore aimed to bring together all stakeholders involved in supporting children with motor skill difficulties to co-produce a new pathway in Bradford (UK). Methods: 13 participants attended a half-day workshop spanning lived experience, paediatrics, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, school leadership, special educational needs teaching, school nursing, physical needs specialists and reducing inequalities management. A Double Diamond approach guided participants through four phases cycling between divergence and convergence. Discussions were recorded and transcribed for thematic analysis, and participants subsequently completed an online questionnaire exploring (i) acceptability; (ii) potential barriers; and (iii) modifications. Results: Consensus was reached on a new pathway comprising four key elements: identifying and mobilising key leaders; embedding evidence-based training across sectors; a universal offer within schools and communities; and a targeted clinical offer for children requiring specialist support. Conclusion: A Double Diamond approach to co-production successfully facilitated consensus across a diverse stakeholder group, demonstrating its viability as a replicable model for redesigning neurodevelopmental pathways where top-down approaches have failed. The resulting pathway addresses long-standing structural failures in DCD provision by prioritising stakeholder mobilisation and cross-sector training before a two-tiered system of universal school-based identification and targeted clinical support. A needs-led model of this kind may reduce pressure on overstretched healthcare services while tackling the inequity of a diagnosis-led system.

    19→1. Introduction
    20→Children that have difficulties integrating sensory information to skilled action can receive a diagnosis of Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD) if these challenges persistently interfere with daily activities . Poor sensorimotor development has been linked to detrimental outcomes related to educational attainment , physical health  and socioemotional wellbeing . Research has shown that early intervention is effective at improving outcomes for children and young people with DCD . However, the UK (and other countries across the world) use a diagnosis-led model, which stipulates a need for a diagnosis before support is available, which can delay access to intervention .  
    21→There is growing parental dissatisfaction internationally, with diagnostic-led systems and the subsequent lack of support available for DCD. Research had found that wating times for assessment and diagnosis are long, difficult, frustrating and complicated, with challenges amplified by a lack of knowledge and inconsistent information provided by different healthcare professionals . Research has found that some parents do not feel that healthcare professionals can speak confidently about DCD , with as many as 43% of parents rating initial practitioner knowledge as less than sufficient when on the first stage of the pathway for a diagnosis .  Parents and carers have also reported finding the pathway stressful, and ineffective, all of which culminates in substandard post-diagnostic support . In a recent study exploring parental experiences of DCD in the UK, the average wait time from first seeking help, to gaining a diagnosis was 2.8 years . Unfortunately, long waiting times are also the case internationally for DCD, with research from Australia highlighting that 45% families waited between 2 and 4 years for diagnosis . To mitigate for these issues with the DCD pathway, there are reports of families seeking private medical care, resulting in a substantial financial and emotional burden for families . 
    22→These issues are likely, in part, due to over-stretched services that cannot keep up with demand for referrals . Recent research has shown that many of the referrals received in clinical settings are inappropriate, and represent a lack of experience / exposure, rather than clinical need . In addition, confusion around roles and responsibilities, as well as a lack of sufficient resources have also been found contribute to the inefficient and complicated pathways . It is therefore evident that DCD pathways in some localities are not working for either clinicians or families, and thus new approaches are needed to ensure timely and effective assessment and support of these children. Co-production has proven to be a useful tool in developing new approaches in healthcare and improving agency and subsequently buy-in to new initiatives . Such approaches could therefore support the development of a more effective and agreeable pathway for DCD assessment and support. This research aimed to co-produce a new pathway with all stakeholders involved in assessing and supporting children with sensorimotor difficulties, across health, education and community sectors. 
    23→2.Methods 
    24→2.1 Design
    25→The workshop was a three hour, in person session, in which participants were guided through a Double Diamond design approach  which encompassed four stages split into two sessions of divergent thinking and two sessions of convergent thinking (see figure 1). Double diamond approaches have been found to be effective in creating consensus in diverse mixed stakeholder groups, allowing expert discussions across healthcare, education, and lived experience . After the workshop, participants were sent an online questionnaire to provide feedback on the pathway developed in the session.

    29→2.2 Participants 
    30→Purposive sampling was used to identify key stakeholders involved in assessing and supporting children with DCD. Such sampling techniques have been found to be beneficial in experience-based co-production sessions . Stakeholders were based on their involvement in a previous research study  including: (i) paediatrics; (ii) occupational therapy; (iii) physiotherapy; (iv) general practitioner (GP)/ family doctor; (v) school nurses; (vi) physical needs team – a group of specialist teachers who support children with physical needs in schools; (vii) special educational needs and disability coordinators (SENDCo); (viii) educational psychology; (ix) practitioners involved in Education and Health Care Plan (EHCP) panels - as EHCPs determine whether a school receives additional funding to support the needs of a child with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND). Given the importance of ‘nothing about us without us’ , lived experience was invited. Finally, as schools play a major role in the support and care of children with SEND, school leaders were deemed important to attend. Of the 16 participants that signed consent forms, 13 attended on the day. 
    31→2.3 Materials and Procedure
    32→Participants were invited via email and signed written consent prior to attending. A young person with lived experience attended, and thus both parental and young person written consent was obtained. Prior to the workshop, the research team sent all participants two handouts: (i) official diagnostic criteria in both the DSM  and ICD  frameworks; (ii) a young person perspective on what it is like to live with DCD (see Supplementary Materials 1-2). These were circulated beforehand due to poor levels of knowledge about DCD . 
    33→The workshop took place in Bradford (UK) in January 2025. On arrival participants had the opportunity to network and generate rapport. When the workshop began, the research team presented findings from interviews with stakeholders involved in the pathway, highlighting barriers to assessment and diagnosis (; see Supplementary Material 3). After this, participants were separated into two pre-determined groups, ensuring representation from all areas of the pathway in each discussion. The handouts circulated prior to the workshop were also printed and available on each table to facilitate discussion. 
    34→In phase 1 (divergence) participants discussed they key issues from the researchers’ presentation and how these related to their job role. In phase 2, each table was tasked with developing a new ‘ideal’ pathway for DCD. In phase 3 each table presented their pathway and received feedback from the other group. For phase 4, the whole group convened to agree on a new pathway, based on those presented from phase 3. In each phase, prompts for discussion were provided via PowerPoint (see Supplementary Material 3). All discussions were recorded via tablets. Two researchers facilitated discussions, ensuring all participants were given the opportunity to contribute. All recordings were transcribed, and observational notes were taken throughout.   
    35→After the workshop, participants were sent a short online questionnaire in which they were asked (i) to rate the extent to which they thought the pathway co-produced in the session would be effective; (ii) detail what they perceive to be the strengths of the pathway; (iii) highlight potential barriers to implementation; (iv) illuminate whether their role is clearly defined; and (v) review four other DCD pathways (mix of national and international)  to establish if there was content worthy of including in the new pathway. All pathways were anonymised to ensure a lack of bias. Participants consented online, after reading an information sheet, following which they generated a unique ID to ensure anonymity and allow for withdrawal (up to 2 weeks after completion). All questions were optional, and once participants completed the questionnaire, they were guided to a debrief page. Ethical approval was granted by the University of Bradford's ethics committee (E1026).
    36→2.4 Analysis
    37→Thematic analysis  was used to analyse workshop discussions and qualitative data from the questionnaire via NVivo. The six stages of analysis were followed: (i) Familiarising yourself with the data; (ii) Generating initial codes; (iii) Searching for themes; (iv) Reviewing themes; (v) Defining and naming themes; (vi) Producing the report.
    38→A minimum of 3 members of the authorship team analysed each transcript, which allowed disagreements and contrasting interpretations to be discussed and considered until a consensus was reached. Descriptive statistics were used to explore responses to quantitative questionnaire data.

    45→In phases 1 and 2 as groups were discussing issues in the current pathway and co-producing their new pathways, there was much discussion surrounding barriers needing to be addressed. One major theme that emerged was a lack of resources within healthcare and education. Time, staffing, funding and physical space all emerged as barriers to effective support within the context of DCD. Capacity pressures within healthcare underscored the compromised the level of care afforded to children entering the DCD pathway. 
    46→We don't have the time in clinic to be able to look at all the possibilities. We can only look at what we can do within a certain time frame and with the resources we've got -Occupational Therapist
    47→There are pressures everywhere because the waiting list is getting longer - Paediatrician
    48→I think at the moment, economics are kind of driving healthcare in Bradford so there is a problem – Occupational Therapist
    49→Capacity pressures within healthcare services were identified as a key driver of long waiting times. For many families, these prolonged waits result in disengagement, with referrals becoming outdated and appointments missed, leaving them lost along the wait.
    50→I guess for us the other problem is some of it is stale news because it's like a year old by the time they've come to us…parents have forgotten why they've come for the appointment, you know they say “can you remind me?” –  Paediatrician
    51→It might be quite a while because of waiting times before diagnosis is given – Physiotherapist
    52→People [are] running out of money [at the end of the month] so they can't make it to the hospital appointment [so we have a high proportion of ‘Did Not Attend’] – Paediatrician 
    53→For those who endure the long waiting lists and receive a diagnosis for their child, for many this is merely a diagnosis without a destination. Lack of staff and time was discussed as resulting in a lack of effective intervention and support after a diagnosis, which has left professionals relying on signposting. This is particularly problematic for the population in Bradford, with the district home to a high proportion of ethnic minority groups including South Asian and Central and Eastern European communities , for whom many have English as an additional language. The district also encompasses some of the most deprived areas in the UK, with nearly a quarter of children living in poverty , which may represent a barrier to accessing healthcare information. 
    54→These kids, so they make it to the top [of the waiting list] they get a diagnosis, so what? What does that do for this kid that’s struggling to hop? We go “congratulations, you have DCD. Bye!” – Physiotherapist
    55→Post COVID especially we're seeing people [and] handing them these leaflets [and saying] ‘off you go’, and many of them can't read…the bottom line, is the statistics are about 25% of the population's reading age is about year six – Paediatrician
    56→How do families access that? Are they on an iPhone? Have they got a laptop? Do they have it at all? You know, if you give them a leaflet, are they going to read that leaflet? – Occupational Therapist
    57→Resource issues were not only seen in healthcare, a lack of time, staff and space within the education context was also rehearsed as problematic for supporting children with sensorimotor difficulties. This means, for a variety of reasons, adjustments and interventions suggested by clinical colleagues are not able to be completed: 
    58→Sometimes what’s recommended [from clinical colleagues] might just be difficult to achieve in a school setting or might conflict with a school’s approach to learning. – School Leader
    59→I think [the demand on schools] puts a lot of pressure on school staff to deliver interventions and it’s really hard because there just isn’t the staff to do it – School Nurse
    60→We can always find things that don’t work but one thing is within schools in Bradford, there’s no space… [and] if you can’t go to the park as it’s dangerous because of the needles or whatever – SENDCo
    61→A second major theme that emerged through discussions was DCD – lost in plain sight. Despite the prevalence of DCD, it appears to be a neurodevelopmental condition that is burdened by a lack of knowledge and awareness. For many participants, across both health and education, a label was seen as central to support. In the healthcare context, there was frustration surrounding strong inaccurate beliefs around diagnosis pertaining to the relationship between DCD label and support within education contexts, which leads to frustration for families:
    62→Some parents are being told by schools that we can't do anything until there's a diagnosis. And that is really very unhelpful – Paediatrician
    63→You don't get anything unless you've got a diagnosis – Adult Lived Experience
    64→It really upsets me when… parents are then coming to see me [and saying] we need this diagnosis otherwise nothing is going to happen for my child – Paediatrician 
    65→The acute lack of knowledge surrounding DCD plays a heavy role in the experience children have within the education system. Inadequate knowledge leading to negative experiences in education was rehearsed as problematic, with many teachers, including those with years of experience, being ill equipped to effectively identify and support children with sensorimotor difficulties:
    66→You know we're talking about DCD, and it isn't a term that [I’ve heard of], you know I've been teaching 20 years and I am quite knowledgeable… so I think that tells you about the kind of profile [DCD has] – School Leader
    67→So I think there's a huge bit around awareness in schools because if it's not seen as having an impact on their education or attainment then they wouldn't necessarily do a referral into our team [for intervention] – Physical Needs Team 
    68→We went to secondary school and Sam’s needs were not getting met… he got put down in lower sets for all subjects because he was struggling with certain aspects of education, and even though he’s super bright, that was obviously incredibly frustrating for all of us – Adult Lived Experience 
    69→This lack of knowledge was also apparent within healthcare settings, which translated as a sense of abandonment for families. Inadequate knowledge leading to negative experiences in healthcare was illuminated by participants, particularly in relation to primary care, which is the first port of call for many concerned parents:
    70→I’ve heard lots of families that have said they've gone to the GP and the GP says, “I don't know what to do”. You know that's a real worry, they don't even know the referral pathway let alone what this is – Adult Lived Experience
    71→Less GPs in their training are doing paediatrics – Paediatrician
    72→I'm struggling… the OT and physio reports are there, and then somebody said this percentile, and somebody said that, and then I'm thinking you know, I just want somebody to hold my hand – Paediatrician 
    73→This lack of comprehensive understanding reaffirmed DCD as a low priority compared to other neurodevelopmental conditions and disabilities, in both education and healthcare contexts. Conversely, a referral into DCD services can be seen as an easy way into the system for children with other neurodevelopmental conditions:
    74→Waiting list for DCD assessments …are low priority, because… all the other neurodisabilities take priority over that – Physiotherapist
    75→Young teachers aren’t willing to [make referrals for all neurodevelopmental conditions]… because they see that they have 30 children… so what do you do when they can’t all have an EHCP? – SENDCo
    76→So they’re potentially waiting for an autism or ADHD diagnosis, which we all know is ridiculously about two years, so then they often come to us because were often quicker – Occupational Therapist
    77→Family knowledge of childhood development was also discussed as being problematic, with participants alluding to this resulting in children not being exposed to typical developmental activities beneficial for sensorimotor development:
    78→I think not specifically DCD but more knowledge about the normal activities and things children should be doing in their developmental and formative stages because they do not [necessarily] have that experience of having a normal childhood [regarding] physical activities – Paediatrician
    79→The first question is, is your child doing anything? What are they doing? If it’s a case of coming home from school going straight to their bedroom and playing on their iPad, they’re going to have really overdeveloped thumbs, poor eyesight but not enough physical activity. So, we find that when we examine children, we look at their hands, particularly their handwriting their hands are so flaccid, there’s no muscle there. They’re not getting out, they’re not climbing trees, they’re not riding their bikes, they’re not wrestling with their mates or brothers. – Occupational Therapist
    80→If both parents have to work or if a parent is worried to let their child go to the park because of safety concerns then that’s not going to happen [physical activity]. So I think we’ve got to not lose sight of social context, and I don’t think it’s just enough for us to pressure and say “oh parent you’re going to have to go and do this” because that might not be feasible for whatever reason for that family - School Leader
    81→This lack of knowledge and prioritisation of sensorimotor development within families was also seen as a barrier, which unearthed the engagement gap when families were not taking accountability for this realm of their child’s development: 
    82→Going back to that knowledge… the main thing in terms of accountability is that parents have to think “what can I be doing” – Physiotherapist 
    83→We used to do movement groups…activity of daily living groups, life skill groups. Attendance was abysmal. We used to run them in the school holidays to …improve uptake, we used to run them after school to…improve uptake. Attendance was [still] dire, so we stopped doing them… kids need advocates - Physiotherapist

    89→If the Occupational Therapy report said… this would fit with the diagnosis of DCD, but that [diagnosis] needs to come from a Paediatrician - Paediatrician

   100→The evident lack of cohesion amongst professionals within the pathway was implicated by an ineffective pathway which is struggling to support children and families with sensorimotor difficulties.    103→I suppose the disjoint as well is you might have somebody referred from a parent into you but you don't know whether school are already doing X, Y and Z because you don't have that information [on our systems] - Paediatrician

   109→In phase 3, feedback on each of the pathways was given by the other group. On the whole, there was a large proportion of agreement between groups in terms of what needed to be carried forward in order to improve opportunities for assessment and support. When the groups came together in phases 3 and 4, discussions shifted towards building the foundations for change. Participants discussed the need to ensure success of any new pathway, this unravelled as the need to engage with key leaders within health, education and community sectors to secure buy-in and top-down investment: 
   110→I suppose really, we need to identify the movers and shakers [to make] movement and coordination matter to someone up there so that it cascades down, it’s okay the guys on the ground identifying things like this…but its identifying who is that group of people right at the top who’s going to cascade this down. I think that’s probably where generating greater knowledge comes from  - Occupational Therapist
   111→From the health side to me the word stakeholders … sounds like the people who've got the money to make the decisions, which is not us it is the Integrated Care Board. [We need to] raise the profile of conditions like this [DCD]… but it's the people who hold the money that have to decide how much importance they're going to give this – Paediatrician
   112→So I think getting in front of the right people that write the materials… [for example going] through the Education Endowment Foundation to the Department for Education – School Leader 
   113→There was consensus around the need for evidence-based training to be developed across all sectors (health, education and community) in collaboration with lived experience. Specifically, the conversations around training were largely dominated by the need for training to be embedded within education, and how that might be made feasible within already stretched schools:
   114→I think for schools it needs to be mediated… by SENDCOs and trusts because we need to figure out what's going to work in our setting. How can we match the science, the evidence, the experience with how we do things? – School Leader
   115→You know, we're trying to raise the profile of the condition, but I think at some point it needs to be a little bit less medical, the model… it's almost like, only a doctor makes this diagnosis… until that is made we can’t do anything… I was just thinking [maybe we need] a DCD champion in each school… a non-medical person? – Paediatrician
   116→The bottom line is tapping into teaching training. – SENDCo
   117→I think the training can very much be geared [to] case study examples … real pictures, real people … to put it in context. – Adult Lived Experience
   118→The need for training was not just seen within education though, with many acknowledging the need for wider community awareness raising designed to help children with DCD feel less invisible:
   119→We need to work…to develop awareness. A lot of our teachers are coming from the community, and they want to work with their own community to make Bradford better. - SENDCo
   120→I think there could be more comprehensive training [on DCD] that… could also be used in community groups etc. I think that would be really beneficial. – Adult Lived Experience
   121→Last year, we had a really good practice where … [we] organised a Teams meeting for children with a specific physical disabilities… it was really important for them to come and see that there are other people [like them], that there are that there is a community to belong to – SENDCo
   122→The next major theme that emerged from discussions was the need for a universal offer to bridge health and education. Participants were eager to ensure that children can access to resources prior to diagnosis, so that support can be accessed without the delay of long waiting lists: 
   123→What we do is identify the needs and put interventions in place. We're not waiting for a diagnosis to do that. Yeah. So… while they're sat on a waiting list for the label, this is how we’re helping. – SENDCo
   124→
   125→I know I'm a broken record, but the importance of … getting the universal offer for all children right – School Leader
   126→How much can OTs and physios in schools do to put that kind of, you know, preparation and support in place before they get that diagnosis? Because then it almost makes the diagnosis less important. At some point, you know, especially if you are going to go on to higher education or whatever, they will need a diagnosis. Actually, they could have a really healthy and kind of productive time at school if …the support is in place. – Adult Lived Experience
   127→Participants discussed positive elements in the current pathway which they were eager to retain going forwards. In particular, embedding evidence-based initiatives such as screening to facilitate triage into services. It was rehearsed that this enables the right children to be seen by the right services in a timely manner:
   128→When we found out about FUNMOVES we used that as a triage tool... Depending on the results of that would then depend if the child needed to have a Movement ABC done. Our waiting list now for DCD is down to four weeks [from 6+ months], so yeah, it’s been revolutionary – Physiotherapist
   129→FUNMOVES has really worked in our clinical settings in reducing our waiting lists – Occupational Therapist
   130→We’ve got really good resources now [as a result of collaboration with FUNMOVES] that we can also give to the children who don't meet the threshold for a [Movement] ABC, but would still benefit from doing more exercise – Physiotherapist
   131→Participants also highlighted the role universal assessment and intervention should play in school contexts, and how this information could feed into healthcare services, via improving communication and collaboration between healthcare and education: 
   132→We talked about doing FUNMOVES once, doing the intervention for those that scored poorly, Could it then be at that point that …let's get all of their parents together in one room and let's educate them what DCD is, what we found… [and] what you need to do at home. And that's how they're then linked into the process. And then it makes sense and they know what's going on…You can then say to them, right, we've reassessed your kid, they're still struggling, we're going to make a referral now to the specialist, is that okay? – Physiotherapist
   133→So FUNMOVES would be the universal offer, targeted would be [for] the ones who have scored poorly … if they're re-screened and they're still scoring poorly [after universal intervention]… it's at that point they get referred – Physiotherapist
   134→Currently FUNMOVES is covering gross motor imbalance, and a lot of issues in school to do with fine motor. Pencil skills. So, it's making sure we cover that base as well [in the new pathway].  – School Leader
   135→Following universal provision, participants stressed the importance of a seamless transition to targeted support within healthcare. Discussions surrounded responsibility for referrals and trajectory of action: 
   136→I think we certainly need gatekeepers. – Paediatrician 
   137→If you take the GP out of the mix, you're cutting a load of waiting time down. And also it's empowering parents, empowering schools, which is really important. – Adult Lived Experience
   138→Kids need to be seeing an OT and physio before they see us… - Paediatrician
   139→The final major theme that emerged was a universal offer not a uniform one, in which participants alluded to the need for flexibility within the pathway to ensure no child falls through the cracks. Participants agreed that the universal offer should sit both within schools and communities. In relation to the school context, discussions were had about embedding the universal offer within existing infrastructure to ensure feasibility:
   140→For our schools, our capacity to offer interventions is probably quite limited…but our capacity to change what we do universally or our willingness to do that is probably limitless if we can … have the conversations with the experts. – School Leader
   141→So that these kids aren't being singled out, actually it's [universal offer] integrated into kind of day-to-day learning. Some really quick and easy wins… that can make a huge difference – Adult Lived Experience
   142→More physical learning activities instead of just…looking at the board, then writing… you’d have more active learning… [For example] designing things on a computer or … using actual objects and things instead of writing… it’s definitely helped me, but probably [would help] others as well. – Young Person Lived Experience

   144→There were also discussions about what happens when a school cannot participate in the universal offer, and how to avoid missing children with additional needs from these schools. The group agreed that there is a need for community hubs to mitigate disadvantage associated with the postcode lottery: 
   145→If a school doesn't have the capacity to engage in FUNMOVES, [What's the option], what’s the pathway? – School leader
   146→Can we also have that kind of hub… which will be a one stop shop for practitioners and education team to call in and say, I don't understand this…what do you think about it? It's almost like a helpline, but it isn't a helpline. It's a place to go to find [help] and to pose questions – SENDCo
   147→I think that space to go for parents, that really clear, you know, what should parents do if they've got concerns. Like, like what you said about the hub – Adult Lived Experience
   148→Participants also recognised the need for universal approaches to have local solutions for local needs to ensure they align with the needs of the community, and pre-existing health and education resources to avoid duplicated effort:
   149→What works for one family and one school, one community, does not work for the others. Cultural differences, the approaches, the understandings, it needs to be more [of a] level playing field - SENDCo
   150→And we're forgetting the local offer, everything's supposed to be on the local offer. So…we signpost there rather than try and create something else, just utilise what we’ve already got - Physiotherapist

   156→Of the 17 invited to take part, 10 participants completed the questionnaire, comprising Occupational Therapy (n=1), Physiotherapy (n=2), SENDCo (n=2), Inclusion Consultant (n=1), School Leader (n=1), Paediatrics (n=1), Physical Needs Team (n=1) and Adult Lived Experience (n=1).
   157→Participants were asked to rate on a scale of 1-5 to what extent the pathway developed in the workshop would be an effective way to identify and support DCD in Bradford (not at all –a great extent). Preliminary feedback suggested broad support for the pathway, with a mean score of 4.4 (SD=.52). When asked about the strengths of the pathway, participants appreciated (i) the inclusion of all key stakeholders, (ii) greater communication between services; (iii) better definition of roles and responsibilities and (iv) more defined referral routes. Many participants also applauded the two-tiered approach to diagnosis, with universal in schools prior to targeted offers within healthcare. 
   158→When asked if participants could foresee any potential barriers to implementing the new pathway, 8 responded ‘yes’ and 2 responded ‘no’. Of those that responded ‘yes’ concerns were raised regarding the need for greater engagement amongst schools, healthcare and families who often do not prioritise DCD. Participants also highlighted resource constraints, particularly in relation to schools, where time is limited and staff workloads are high. 
   159→Participants were asked whether they felt their role was sufficiently clear within the pathway. Of the ten participants, 8 responded ‘yes’ and 2 responded ‘no’. Participants that responded no highlighted that their roles were difficult to represent on the pathway as they support schools externally.
   160→Participants were then asked to review other national and international pathways. Participants were first presented with the Partnering for Change model ; see Supplementary Material 4). Eight participants concluded there was nothing in this model they would like to see added. Two participants liked the simplicity of the pathway, and the inclusion of relationship building to ensure the communication between families, healthcare and education is improved. Participants were then presented with a current pathway from a Yorkshire NHS Trust (see Supplementary Material 5). Eight participants concluded there was nothing in this model they would like to see added. Two participants liked the emphasis on parental involvement, including the need for parents to feed into assessment, and support. Participants were then shown a pathway from EACD Guidelines ; see Supplementary Material 6). According to participants there was nothing worthwhile adding from this pathway. Finally, participants were shown the pathway recommended by Dyspraxia DCD Ireland . Two participants highlighted this pathway was easier to understand, but also acknowledged that there was a lack of detail in places.
   161→3.3 Final pathway
   162→The pathway developed in the workshop was amended to reflect feedback in the questionnaire. This included improving clarity and ease of navigation, as well as ensuring roles that participants felt were not reflected, were included where possible (see figure 5). In addition, as this pathway is comprehensive, infographics were made to disseminate to each individual role within the pathway (including families).

   166→4. Discussion 
   167→This research aimed to co-produce a new pathway with all stakeholders involved in assessing and supporting children with sensorimotor difficulties, across health, education and community sectors. The current analysis illuminated the journey from discontent with the current pathway, to harmony amongst all key stakeholders on the best course of action for assessing and supporting children with sensorimotor difficulties. Participants identified four key stages to the pathway: (i) identifying and mobilising key leaders; (ii) evidence-based training; (iii) universal offer within schools and communities (iv) targeted offer within clinical services 
   168→4.1 Identifying and mobilising key leaders 
   169→In order to facilitate the success of a new pathway, participants highlighted the need to mobilise key leaders within both healthcare and education sectors, due to a perceived lack prioritisation at present. This is perhaps not surprising given research consistently shows low levels of knowledge and understanding about DCD across sectors , without knowledge of the condition, DCD will not become a priority. However, many with lived experience of DCD are concerned the problem is not always a lack of knowledge, but an evident disinterest in DCD more broadly within the context of neurodivergence . This is particularly frustrating, as over 13 years ago, it was reported that only 27% of respondents (doctors, paediatricians, school teachers and parents) were either ‘very familiar’ or ‘somewhat familiar’ with DCD, compared to 87% for ADHD and 81% for Autism . Given the lack of knowledge and prioritisation of DCD , it is therefore unlikely that schools will use the limited budget they do have  to support these children. This is made even more challenging by evidence from a recent parliamentary report highlighting that 99% of school leaders suggest that the funding they receive for SEND is not sufficient to effectively support them .  To ensure the new pathway is embedded it is therefore crucial to mobilise key stakeholders across both health and education including Integrated Care Boards and the Department of Education.  Research has shown that in complex systems, mobilising such stakeholders can be helpful to establish shared goals, and ensure adequate resources are provided to new initiatives including tools, expertise, skills and funding . 
   170→4.2 Evidence-based training
   171→Improving the skillset of all involved in the DCD pathway was also highlighted by participants as being central to success. Specifically, there was a demand for evidence-based training which utilises lived experience to generate holistic knowledge of DCD that encompasses not only sensorimotor development, but also the wider challenges associated with DCD, including cognition , and the detrimental impact on education outcomes , physical health  and mental health . The importance of including lived experience in designing this training cannot be underestimated. The voice of insiders is central to ensuring equitable and meaningful solutions, as this mitigates the potential for othering within such research and systems within which they are beholden . 
   172→Importantly, participants recognised that education and healthcare were not the only stakeholders to have insufficient knowledge. Family knowledge of childhood development was also found to be problematic with regards to awareness and understanding surrounding the role of the family in developing sensorimotor skills. There was an evident engagement gap which reflected a stark difference between families who possessed greater knowledge and those who underestimated the importance of physical activity (e.g. riding a bike, playing with friends), and activities of daily living (e.g. self-care skills such as toothbrushing, toileting, dressing) for wider childhood development . It could also be the case that families are inhibited in their practices by the social environment in which they live. For example, previous research in Bradford has highlighted that many feel unsafe accessing public spaces for physical activity , yet they also lack appropriate indoor and outdoor space at home to engage in meaningful activity . It is therefore important that more research is done to understand the social context behind this lack of knowledge and/or engagement with activities to support childhood development.
   173→4.3. Universal offer within schools and communities
   174→Prior to accessing clinical services, participants were eager to ensure that there was a comprehensive universal offer, which provides equitable opportunities to be identified and supported. Such approaches have the potential to reduce healthcare inequalities as the first point of access is no longer barriered . Universal approaches embedded within school settings have shown promise in relation to mental health, with results finding improved accessibility by removing barriers to learning . 
   175→Participants highlighted that there are valid, reliable and feasible tools for assessing gross motor skills in schools, such as FUNMOVES  which allows children with sensorimotor difficulties to be identified earlier and facilitates greater information sharing between health and education. Participants did, however, highlight that there are no such resources for fine motor or activities of daily living available for use in schools. Participants were eager to ensure that all facets of sensorimotor development are included within the new universal offer. Despite the plentiful options for assessing fine motor skills , participants were cautious of potential constraints within schools such as time, staff and funding . Later discussions with clinical professionals identified teacher-reported DCD-Q  as an interim solution to identify children who have difficulties with fine motor skills and activities of daily living. Participants were, however, wary of the subjective nature of such proxy-report measures 
   176→Participants also highlighted the need for universal intervention to be embedded within schools and communities to avoid children and families being lost along the wait for a DCD diagnosis. Universal intervention has the potential to reduce inappropriate referrals into clinical services, such as children who have lacked exposure to motor skills through play and physical activity . By improving the quality of referrals into clinical services, it is likely that capacity pressures within healthcare  will be reduced, thus creating a more efficient system. It is, however, important to consider that by giving schools a defined role, it may increase pressures on workforce with a well-documented staffing crisis , and a lack of specialist knowledge . Given this, participants were eager to ensure that community initiatives support schools to deliver the universal offer. Previous research has shown that schools and communities working together to improve outcomes has been particularly effective when using place-based approaches . 
   177→4.4. Targeted offer within clinical services
   178→It was, however noted that targeted provision in clinical settings was still important, to enable children who cannot be effectively supported through schools and community settings to access timely diagnosis and intervention. Importantly, it was noted that this two-tiered system, would help reduce the number of inappropriate referrals entering the system, which slow down the process for those that need support . This in turn may reduce waiting times, which have been consistently long for families awaiting assessment for DCD across time . By freeing up capacity within healthcare services, there is also the potential to improve intervention beyond signposting, which unfortunately is the default for families at present which they find ineffective and insufficient 
   179→Participants did however express the need for defined roles for each healthcare profession within the DCD pathway, due to varying levels of knowledge and experience with the condition .  Specifically, participants highlighted that Occupational Therapy and Physiotherapy have the greatest expertise, but that the burden of responsibility is on paediatricians to diagnose, when they have comparatively less specialist knowledge . It was therefore suggested that Occupational Therapy and Physiotherapy should provide clear recommendations to paediatricians as to whether a diagnosis may be suitable if there are no other mitigating health factors. This will allow diagnosis by exclusion for paediatricians, which may help streamline the trajectory to diagnosis. By having clearly defined roles within the pathway, it will also ensure greater accountability across professions.
   180→4.1 Limitations
   181→There was some uneven stakeholder representation across the two tables. For example, only Table 1 had lived experience (both adult and young person) – this was however deemed necessary due to the dynamic being parent and child. Similarly, only Table 2 had a SENDCo present.  In addition, two job roles were not able to be present on the day (Educational Psychologist and GP), and instead sent representation, for example the GP sent a representative from the Reducing Inequalities Alliance, who work closely with family doctors to reduce the impact of health inequalities in the district. Having representation from all key stakeholders would have potentially benefitted the development of the new pathway. To mitigate their absence, all participants were asked to consider roles that were not able to be present. Despite a relatively small sample of 13 participants, this was considered a success given the nature of the workshop being in person (3 hours), and the important role participants play in delivering health and education services.  Finally, as with all group discussions, power dynamics played a role. Although there were dominant voices within each group, non-verbal consensus was observed throughout the workshop (i.e. nodding, smiling) to indicate shared agreement. 
   182→5. Conclusion
   183→A Double Diamond approach to co-production was effective for redesigning the DCD pathway, facilitating consensus across a diverse group of stakeholders spanning health, education and lived experience in a half-day workshop. The co-produced pathway addresses long-standing structural failures in DCD provision by prioritising stakeholder mobilisation and embedding cross-sector training prior to a two-tiered system of universal school-based identification and targeted clinical support. Crucially, by replacing the existing diagnosis-led model with a needs-led approach, children will be able to access support when difficulties first present, rather than having to wait for a diagnosis. This has the potential to improve outcomes for children and reduce capacity pressures on overstretched healthcare services, whilst also addressing the inequalities in accessing healthcare services. Given the unprecedented demand placed on services by autism and ADHD pathways  co-production of this type offers a scalable and transferable model for redesigning neurodevelopmental provision more broadly.
