   208→2.2 Measures
   209→2.2.1 Life Events Checklist (LEC-5)
   210→The LEC-5 (Weathers, Blake et al., 2013) was used to record participants' traumatic
   211→events, covering 16 different types of events (item 17: “Any other very stressful event or
   212→experience” was not included to ensure that the included traumatic events fulfilled the
   213→inclusion criteria). Specifically, participants were asked if they had experienced any of the
   214→https://osf.io/7xbcz/overview?view_only=4c6ce3197d5e493aa03c1355d1d1cea2
   215→Preprint v3.0 02.07.2026

   218→listed 16 events during the last 18 months and, if so, whether they had experienced it
   219→personally, witnessed it, learnt that it happened to a close one, or were repeatedly confronted
   220→with details of such an event in the course of their job. If they had experienced more than one
   221→traumatic event, participants had to specify which event caused the most distress and this
   222→event was used as the index event during the survey. For the purpose of the present study, the
   223→LEC-5 items were retrospectively categorized as interpersonal or non-interpersonal trauma
   224→(see Table 1), based on a pre-defined categorization system that was already used in previous
   225→studies (Cloitre et al., 2018; Hyland et al., 2019; Rácz et al., 2023).
   226→2.2.2 Coping Orientation to Problems Experienced Inventory (Brief-COPE)
   227→The Brief-COPE (German translation by Knoll et al., 2005) was used to evaluate
   228→participants’ coping style in response to their traumatic event. The questionnaire consists of
   229→28 items, with two items each representing 14 different coping strategies. Participants were
   230→asked to rate items retrospectively on a scale from 1 (not doing at all) to 4 (doing a lot), with
   231→reference to the first month following the traumatic event. In line with previous research,
   232→three subscales were generated, i.e., problem-focused coping, emotion-focused coping, and
   233→avoidant coping (see e.g., Poulus et al., 2020; Chudzicka-Czupała et al., 2023; Milam et al.,
   234→2024; Xu et al., 2024). The problem-focused coping subscale included active coping,
   235→planning, positive reframing and instrumental support; the emotion-focused coping subscale
   236→comprised acceptance, emotional support, humour, self-blame, religion and venting; the
   237→avoidant coping subscale consisted of behavioral disengagement, denial, self-distraction, and
   238→substance use. Adequate internal consistencies were reported for all three subscales (Milam et
   239→al., 2024). In the present study, internal consistencies were as follows: problem-focused
   240→coping: Cronbach's α = .79; emotion-focused coping: Cronbach's α = .58; avoidant coping:
   241→Cronbach's α = .68.

   246→2.2.3 Posttraumatic Stress Disorder Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5)
   247→The PCL-5 (German translation by Krüger-Gottschalk et al., 2017) was used to assess
   248→participants' current posttraumatic stress symptoms. This 20-item self-report measure assesses
   249→the severity of DSM-5-defined PTSD symptoms, with participants indicating how frequently
   250→each symptom caused distress over the past month. Items are rated from 0 (not at all) to 4
   251→(extremely). High internal consistency was reported for the total sum score (Krüger-
   252→Gottschalk et al., 2017). For the present sample, the total score demonstrated excellent
   253→internal consistency, with Cronbach's α = .95.
   254→2.2.4 Depression Anxiety Stress Scale (DASS-21)
   255→The DASS-21 (German translation by Nilges & Essau, 2021) was used to evaluate
   256→participants' current symptoms of depression, anxiety, and stress. It consists of 21 items
   257→assessing the frequency of depressive, anxious, and stress-related symptoms experienced
   258→during the past week, rated on a scale from 0 (did not apply to me at all) to 3 (applied to me
   259→very much or most of the time). The subscales include seven items per scale. Good to
   260→excellent internal consistency have been reported for each scale (Henry & Crawford, 2005). In
   261→this sample, the DASS-21 demonstrated good to excellent internal consistency: Depression:
   262→Cronbach's α = .92; Anxiety: Cronbach's α = .84; Stress: Cronbach's α = .90.
   263→2.2.5 Satisfaction with Life Scale (SWLS)
   264→Current life satisfaction was assessed using SWLS (German translation by Glaesmer et
   265→al., 2011). This five-item self-report instrument measures global life satisfaction over the past
   266→month. Participants were asked to answer each item on a scale from 1 (strongly disagree) to 7
   267→(strongly agree). Excellent internal consistency was reported for the total sum score
   268→(Glaesmer et al., 2011). In the present sample, the total score also demonstrated excellent
   269→internal consistency, with Cronbach's α = .90.

   274→2.3 Data analyses
   275→RStudio (R Core Team, 2024) was used for data analyses. Pearson correlations were
   276→used to examine the relationships between coping styles and current posttraumatic stress
   277→symptoms, depression, anxiety, stress, and current life satisfaction, separately for each trauma
   278→type. To investigate group differences in symptomatology and coping, independent-samples t-
   279→tests were conducted, with trauma type serving as independent variable. Because t-tests are
   280→robust to violations of the normality assumption and the sample size was sufficiently large,
   281→parametric tests were used for the primary analyses even when this assumption was not fully
   282→met. When the assumption of homogeneity of variances was violated, the Welch’s t-test was
   283→used. To examine the predictive validity of the different coping styles, two hierarchical
   284→multiple regression analyses were conducted, one for each trauma type. In the first step of
   285→each analysis, current posttraumatic stress symptoms were predicted from age and gender, and
   286→the three coping styles were added as predictors in the second step. Additionally, a multiple
   287→hierarchical regression analysis was performed including the total sample, predicting current
   288→posttraumatic stress symptoms using age and gender as predictors in the first step, followed
   289→by adding trauma type as predictor in the second step. For the regression analyses, all
   290→variables (criterion and predictors) were z-standardized prior to analyses. Finally, despite the
   291→retrospective study design, an exploratory mediation analysis was conducted using the R
   292→mediation package (Tingley et al., 2014) to further examine avoidant coping as a statistical
   293→mediator of the association between trauma type and posttraumatic stress symptoms. Given
   294→the retrospective nature of the data, the analysis was intended to explore statistical
   295→associations rather than test causal pathways. Here, bootstrapping was applied with 5,000
   296→simulations. Data and analysis scripts are available at
   297→https://osf.io/7xbcz/overview?view_only=4c6ce3197d5e493aa03c1355d1d1cea2.

   379→4. Discussion
   380→This online study examined the relationship between trauma type, coping style, and
   381→trauma-related symptomatology, including a sample of participants who had experienced
   382→different trauma types, i.e., interpersonal or non-interpersonal trauma. We expected positive
   383→associations between avoidant coping and trauma-related symptomatology, as well as higher
   384→levels of posttraumatic stress symptoms, depression, anxiety, and stress, lower life
   385→satisfaction, and greater use of maladaptive coping in the interpersonal trauma group
   386→compared to non-interpersonal trauma group. We further expected coping style to explain a
   387→significant proportion of the variance of posttraumatic stress symptoms within both trauma
   388→groups and trauma type account for significant unique variance in posttraumatic stress
   389→symptoms across the sample. Via exploratory analyses, we expected avoidant coping to be a
   390→significant statistical mediator of the association between trauma type and posttraumatic stress
   391→symptoms. In line with our hypotheses, avoidant coping was positively correlated with
   392→trauma-related symptoms within both trauma groups, whereas the correlation between
   393→avoidant coping and life satisfaction was not significant. Group-based comparisons revealed
   394→significantly higher levels of posttraumatic stress symptoms, depression, anxiety, and stress,
   395→https://osf.io/7xbcz/overview?view_only=4c6ce3197d5e493aa03c1355d1d1cea2
   396→Preprint v3.0 02.07.2026
   397→
   398→PREPRINT: TRAUMA TYPE, COPING, AND SYMPTOMATOLOGY 15
   399→as well as lower life satisfaction, in the interpersonal trauma group compared to the non-
   400→interpersonal trauma group, supporting the notion that interpersonal trauma is associated with
   401→greater negative psychological consequences. Furthermore, and also in line with our
   402→hypotheses, individuals who experienced interpersonal trauma reported significantly higher
   403→levels of avoidant coping and lower levels of problem-focused coping, suggesting that these
   404→individuals were more likely to engage in coping strategies associated with negative trauma
   405→outcomes. However, despite the statistically significant group differences, the observed effect
   406→sizes were small to medium, indicating that the findings should be interpreted with
   407→appropriate caution. There was no significant group difference in the use of emotion-focused
   408→coping, consistent with the mixed findings regarding its relationship with trauma outcomes.
   409→However, the results should be interpreted with caution dues to the weak internal consistency
   410→of this subscale. Within both trauma groups, avoidant coping explained a significant
   411→proportion of variance in posttraumatic stress symptoms, highlighting the expected relevance
   412→of these factors in explaining variability in posttraumatic stress responses. Across trauma
   413→groups, trauma type itself accounted for significant unique variance in posttraumatic stress
   414→symptoms. However, the explained variance remained low, indicating that trauma type was
   415→associated with only a limited amount of variability in posttraumatic stress symptoms. Finally,
   416→the results of our exploratory mediation analysis were consistent with a statistical mediation
   417→model in which avoidant coping partially accounted for the association between trauma type
   418→and posttraumatic stress symptoms. This finding extends previous evidence by indicating that
   419→avoidant coping may statistically account for part of the association between interpersonal
   420→trauma and posttraumatic stress symptoms. However, given the retrospective design of the
   421→study and the exploratory nature of this analysis, these results do not allow strong causal
   422→conclusions and should be interpreted with caution.
   423→While previous studies have examined posttraumatic symptoms, trauma type, and
   424→coping style separately, this study integrates these lines of research by examining group
   425→https://osf.io/7xbcz/overview?view_only=4c6ce3197d5e493aa03c1355d1d1cea2
   426→Preprint v3.0 02.07.2026

   429→differences in coping style across trauma types and the interrelations among trauma type,
   430→coping style, and posttraumatic symptomatology. Specifically, the potential of reducing
   431→avoidant coping as a preventive target should be further examined, especially for individuals
   432→who have experienced interpersonal trauma. Future research could investigate whether
   433→interventions targeting avoidant coping are associated with more improved posttraumatic
   434→adjustment, particularly in prevention and early intervention settings. More generally,
   435→addressing avoidant coping is a central target in therapy, as previous studies have shown that
   436→cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) for PTSD can effectively reduce avoidant coping
   437→strategies (Bourdon et al., 2019; Zang et al., 2017).
   438→However, our results raise the question of why individuals tend to rely more on
   439→maladaptive coping strategies following interpersonal trauma. One possible explanation
   440→involves post-trauma cognitive processing styles: interpersonal trauma may lead to more
   441→pronounced negative changes in cognitions, which are among the core diagnostic criteria of
   442→PTSD (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). These altered cognitions could, in turn,
   443→increase reliance on maladaptive coping strategies, such as avoidance, to manage distressing
   444→thoughts and emotions. In this context, Sheerin et al. (2018) investigated the association
   445→between trauma-related cognitions, coping, and PTSD symptoms following combat trauma.
   446→Results showed that avoidant coping did not longer predict PTSD symptoms significantly
   447→when trauma-related cognitions were added to the model, supporting the notion that cognitive
   448→changes may underlie the relationship between avoidant coping and PTSD symptoms.
   449→Accordingly, future research should examine trauma type, posttraumatic stress symptoms,
   450→trauma-related cognitions, and coping style within a single framework to more thoroughly
   451→disentangle their potential relationships. Furthermore, the effects of interventions directly
   452→targeting dysfunctional trauma-related cognitions, such as Cognitive Bias Modification-
   453→Appraisal training (CBM-App, e.g., de Kleine et al., 2019; Woud et al., 2021; Woud et al.,
   454→2018) on coping styles across different trauma types should be investigated to examine

   459→whether modifying maladaptive cognitions leads to more adaptive coping patterns and,
   460→consequently, reduced symptom severity.
   461→4.1 Limitations
   462→This study is not without limitations. First, the sample characteristics limit the
   463→generalizability of the findings. Specifically, the sample was relatively young, more than three
   464→quarters of participants were female, and participation required fluency in German, limiting
   465→cultural diversity within the sample. Consequently, the findings can only be generalized to
   466→populations that differ from these characteristics to a limited extent. Further research using
   467→more diverse and representative samples is therefore needed. Furthermore, these analyses
   468→focused exclusively on participants’ most distressing traumatic life event within the past 18
   469→months. This provides a clear reference point for the study, but also constitutes a limitation as
   470→it does not account for the full trauma history. To more comprehensively examine the
   471→relationships between trauma characteristics and various outcomes, future research should
   472→consider factors such as the total number of traumatic experiences and the diversity of
   473→traumatic events within a trauma category. A further limitation concerns the Brief-COPE,
   474→which relies on a 14-factor structure, and for which the literature on alternative subscale
   475→constructions remains inconclusive (Solberg et al., 2022). Although the scales used in the
   476→present analyses have been applied in previous studies, there is no factorial evidence
   477→supporting this approach, and internal consistency was weak, particularly for the emotion
   478→focused subscale. Additionally, posttraumatic coping was assessed retrospectively, whereas
   479→symptoms were assessed at the current time point of the study. Given that the traumatic events
   480→occurred up to 18 months earlier, reports of initial coping may be influenced by current
   481→symptom levels as well as other processes such as autobiographical memory and post-hoc
   482→reconstruction. This limitation should be considered when interpreting the observed
   483→association between coping and current symptom level. Future longitudinal research is needed
   484→to address this issue more directly. Furthermore, traumatic events were retrospectively
   485→https://osf.io/7xbcz/overview?view_only=4c6ce3197d5e493aa03c1355d1d1cea2
   486→Preprint v3.0 02.07.2026

   489→classified into interpersonal and non-interpersonal categories. Although an established LEC-5
   490→categorization system was used, some items may have been difficult to assign unambiguously
   491→within this broad distinction. Furthermore, it cannot be ruled out entirely that individuals who
   492→experienced traumatic event meeting the DSM-5 criteria were excluded because their trauma
   493→was not adequately captured by the LEC-5. It is therefore recommended that future studies
   494→supplement the LEC-5 with an open item allowing participants to specify their traumatic
   495→experience, thereby enabling eligibility decisions to be made on an individual basis.
   496→Additionally, although the study was pre-registered, the present analyses report secondary
   497→analyses and the mediation analysis was not pre-registered.
   498→4.2. Conclusion
   499→Overall, our results indicate a higher level of avoidant coping among participants who
   500→experienced interpersonal trauma, as well as a strong association between avoidant coping,
   501→trauma type, and posttraumatic symptomatology. These findings suggest that avoidant coping
   502→may be relevant to symptom differences across trauma types. However, given the
   503→retrospective design of the study and the methodological limitations, particularly those related
   504→to trauma-type classification, the psychometric properties of the Brief-COPE, and the
   505→limitations of conducting mediation analyses in a cross-sectional design, these findings should
   506→be regarded as preliminary and interpreted with caution. Future studies should employ
   507→prospective longitudinal designs and examine additional factors, including other trauma-
   508→relevant cognitive processes, to draw firmer conclusions.
