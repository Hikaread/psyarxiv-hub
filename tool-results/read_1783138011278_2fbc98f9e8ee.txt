    20→    20→The Internet is flooded with short news, feature articles, videos, and podcasts extolling
    21→    21→the goodness of physical exercise. For a few years now, these claims have
    22→    22→increasingly emphasized the cognitive benefits of practicing physical exercise
    23→    23→regularly, statements and beliefs that are seemingly supported by hundreds of
    24→    24→randomized controlled trials (RCTs), dozens of meta-analyses synthesizing this
    25→    25→empirical work, and even endorsements from the World Health Organization (WHO).
    26→    26→This consensus, however, deserves closer scrutiny.
    27→    27→Over the past decade, a number of reviews and meta-analyses on the cognitive
    28→    28→benefits of physical exercise reported null findings (e.g., Singh et al., 2019; Verburg et
    29→    29→al., 2013; Young et al., 2015). In addition, even the most recent individual studies
    30→    30→continue to exhibit the same limitations previously identified as obstacles to cumulative
    31→    31→scientific progress, such as insufficient statistical power, a lack of adequate control
    32→    32→
    33→    33→groups, and insufficient adjustment for baseline differences (see Beedie et al., 2018;
    34→    34→Ciria et al., 2023; Diamond & Ling, 2019a; Gavelin et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2016;
    35→    35→Lindheimer et al., 2015; Román-Caballero et al., 2023). Unsurprisingly, the 2020 WHO
    36→    36→guidelines on physical activity determined that most randomized controlled trials
    37→    37→(RCTs) provided low or very low certainty of evidence. One would expect that,
    38→    38→following these reports, the self-correcting nature of science should spark serious
    39→    39→debates on the assumption that regular exercise improves cognition. However, we see
    40→    40→no signs that existing assumptions are being challenged, methods and procedures are
    41→    41→being reconsidered, or that there is a genuine effort to approach the issue from a more
    42→    42→rigorous angle. Despite nearly 50 years since the first randomized controlled trial on
    43→    43→this topic (Young, 1979), the field continues to revisit the same questions from largely
    44→    44→unchanged perspectives.
    45→    45→Taking a closer look at the methodological issues hampering progress in the field, we
    46→    46→notice that influential meta-analyses and umbrella analyses (e.g., Singh et al., 2025)
    47→    47→often rely on suboptimal analytical choices and fail to evaluate between-study
    48→    48→heterogeneity. Publication bias is often assessed through visual inspection of funnel
    49→    49→plots and effect sizes adjusted using methods with suboptimal performance, such as
    50→    50→the trim-and-fill method, because they provide high false-positive rates and
    51→    51→undercorrection (Carter et al., 2019). For instance, a recent state-of-the-art study-level
    52→    52→meta-analysis of 2,239 estimates (a reanalysis of the data from Singh et al., 2025)
    53→    53→revealed that, despite previous claims of consistent evidence of benefits across all
    54→    54→populations, the between-study heterogeneity and publication bias make statements
    55→    55→about average effects meaningless (Bartoš et al., 2025). The majority of outcome,
    56→    56→population, and exercise specific subgroup analysis showed that the available
    57→    57→evidence is compatible with both beneficial effects and null effects of exercise on
    58→    58→cognitive outcomes. For instance, while 150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical
    59→    59→activity per week for 24 weeks showed benefit of in older adults with memory issues
    60→    60→(Lautenschlager et al., 2008), a similar 24-months physical activity intervention did not
    61→    61→show any cognitive effect in sedentary older adults (Sink et al., 2015). Certainly, not
    62→    62→all exercise interventions exert the same cardiovascular or metabolic effects, which
    63→    63→might in turn vary according to contextual factors and individual characteristics. A
    64→    64→similar principle may apply to cognitive (and neural) outcomes. However, the existing
    65→    65→empirical literature suffers from methodological shortcomings and publication bias that
    66→    66→preclude drawing firm conclusions about any potential moderators.
    67→    67→Unsurprisingly, researchers' prior beliefs bias not only how they interpret their own
    68→    68→findings, but also how they seek out and attend to available evidence. Critical studies
    69→    69→exposing these methodological limitations are often criticized (see Dupuy et al., 2024,
    70→    70→and later Ciria et al.’s, 2024 reply; or Hillman et al., 2019, and later Diamond & Ling’s,
    71→    71→2019b, reply). For instance, Dupuy et al. (2024) main criticisms of Ciria et al. (2023)
    72→    72→were the lack of certain key moderators in the analyses (e.g., exercise type, intensity,
    73→    73→dose), that the outcome might be the result of a statistical artifact, that the use of
    74→    74→baseline execution was incorrect, and a lack of transparency. In their reply to Dupuy
    75→    75→et al.’s commentary, Ciria et al. (2024) reported that including all those moderators did
    76→    76→not affect the outcome reported in the original paper, showed once more the
    77→    77→robustness of their analyses (e.g., using different methods to address publication bias,
    78→    78→reporting frequentist and bayesian analyses), and responded to the criticism for a lack
    79→    79→of transparency, reminding that all materials used in their umbrella analysis are public
    80→    80→(https://osf.io/e9zqf/), while peer reviews of their manuscript are also available at the
    81→    81→
    82→    82→journal’s website (https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-023-01554-4#peer-
    83→    83→review). Dupuy et al. also maintained that human and animal models offer mechanistic
    84→    84→insights into exercise-induced cognitive changes, but they also noted that “the dose-
    85→    85→response relationship between physical exercise, underlying mechanisms and
    86→    86→cognitive outcomes is complex, and it is important to consider each specific aspect
    87→    87→before drawing any firm conclusions”. Ciria et al. countered by pointing out the
    88→    88→apparent contradiction between this stated complexity and the unearned certainty
    89→    89→often attributed to the cognitive benefits of regular exercise. Ultimately, even if
    90→    90→mechanistic accounts exist, the empirical evaluations by Ciria et al. and others (e.g.,
    91→    91→Bartoš et al., 2025) preclude definitive conclusions. Moreover, as discussed below,
    92→    92→these mechanistic frameworks are frequently limited by conceptual flaws.
    93→    93→These critical studies are also frequently neglected by those who firmly endorse the
    94→    94→positive effects of exercise on cognition. A good illustration of this selective attention
    95→    95→can be found in the reception of a recent umbrella review (Ciria et al., 2023), which
    96→    96→initially attracted considerable attention in the field and continues to be frequently cited.
    97→    97→However, despite its cautious conclusions, a large proportion of these citations distort
    98→    98→its message. In fact, from 114 citations (as of April 2026) in Scopus, 31 (37%) are
    99→    99→miscitations (e.g., claiming positive effects; Hamm et al., 2025). Not surprisingly,
   100→   100→Dupuy et al.’s (2024) critical commentary has 30 citations whereas Ciria et al.’s (2024)
   101→   101→reply has received only 8.
   102→   102→Inconclusive empirical evidence does not necessarily undermine the hypotheses
   103→   103→about the existence of true phenomena. Thus, whether physical exercise has a
   104→   104→specific and idiosyncratic effect on the brain, or it is just another daily activity which,
   105→   105→when performed in a structured and regular manner, shapes individuals' cognitive
   106→   106→repertoire, is, of course, an interesting matter that deserves to be studied from a basic
   107→   107→research point of view. It however warrants more and better research (e.g., registered
   108→   108→reports, multilab collaborations and well-conducted longitudinal studies), and surely
   109→   109→not more underpowered RCTs, reviews or meta-analyses based on the same pool of
   110→   110→individual underpowered RCTs. Indeed, although meta-analyses typically achieve
   111→   111→higher statistical power than individual studies, meta-analyses based primarily on
   112→   112→RCTs with small sample sizes may still provide low-quality estimates of the underlying
   113→   113→effects (Lakens & Evers, 2014).
   114→   114→In addition, any mechanistic account of the effects of regular exercise should carefully
   115→   115→consider several conceptual pitfalls. First, the problem of reverse inference (Poldrack,
   116→   116→2006), which arises when changes in a particular brain region are taken as direct
   117→   117→evidence of changes in a specific cognitive function typically associated with that
   118→   118→region. Because most brain areas support multiple processes, such inferences are not
   119→   119→logically warranted. For instance, the hippocampus supports a wide array of functions
   120→   120→beyond declarative memory, including spatial navigation and cognitive mapping
   121→   121→(Epstein et al., 2017; Maguire et al., 2000; O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978), scene construction
   122→   122→and imagination (Hassabis et al., 2007), future-oriented thinking and prospection
   123→   123→(Schacter et al., 2007), and stress and affective processing (Fanselow & Dong, 2010).
   124→   124→A volumetric change in this structure therefore does not, on its own, license inferences
   125→   125→about memory per se. In fact, one of the most cited articles in the exercise-brain-
   126→   126→cognition literature (Erickson et al., 2011) showed increased hippocampal volume after
   127→   127→aerobic training, with respect to the control group (which showed a reduction in
   128→   128→
   129→   129→hippocampal volume in the post evaluation), while both groups showed the same
   130→   130→memory improvement.
   131→   131→Second, the so-called “brain as a muscle” logic (Simons et al., 2016; Von Bastian et
   132→   132→al., 2022) assumes that repeatedly engaging a cognitive process strengthens the
   133→   133→underlying capacity in much the same way that resistance training strengthens a
   134→   134→muscle. However, as Simons et al. (2016) concluded in their comprehensive review of
   135→   135→brain-training interventions, improvements are typically confined to the trained task
   136→   136→itself and rarely generalize to untrained abilities. That is, far transfer appears to be the
   137→   137→exception rather than the rule, contrary to what the muscle analogy would predict. Von
   138→   138→Bastian et al. (2022) further argued that most training-related gains are better
   139→   139→explained by factors such as strategy acquisition, task automatization, or changes in
   140→   140→motivation and engagement, rather than by any genuine expansion of cognitive
   141→   141→capacity. From this perspective, the regular practice of exercise may simply increase
   142→   142→individuals’ motivation or engagement with tasks, much like other habitual activities
   143→   143→(e.g., maintaining a regular work or study routine), without involving any mechanism
   144→   144→intrinsic to physical exercise itself that would account for improvements in cognitive
   145→   145→capacities, if such improvements occur.
   146→   146→Third, findings from contemporary neuroscience that challenge the notion of one-to-
   147→   147→one mappings between brain regions and cognitive functions (Westlin et al., 2023).
   148→   148→Under the principle of degeneracy (i.e., the capacity of structurally different elements
   149→   149→to perform the same function or yield the same output; Edelman & Gally, 2001; Price
   150→   150→& Friston, 2002), the same cognitive outcome can emerge from multiple, functionally
   151→   151→equivalent neural configurations. This applies both between and within individuals,
   152→   152→since different people solving the same memory or executive-function task can recruit
   153→   153→partially non-overlapping networks (Seghier & Price, 2018). Even within a single
   154→   154→individual the same task can be accomplished on different trials by markedly different
   155→   155→brain activation patterns. For instance, Nakuci et al. (2025) recently identified, across
   156→   156→three perceptual decision-making experiments, multiple distinct yet stable trial
   157→   157→subtypes each associated with a different configuration of whole-brain activity.
   158→   158→Consistently, patients with markedly different lesion profiles can likewise display
   159→   159→indistinguishable behavioral performance (Noppeney et al., 2004). Together, these
   160→   160→observations undermine the assumption that any single neural marker (e.g.,
   161→   161→hippocampal volume, gray-matter density in a given region, or activation in a specific
   162→   162→network) can be uniquely diagnostic of a cognitive benefit attributable to physical
   163→   163→exercise.
   164→   164→Last, but not least, pervasive brain-based explanations might also overshadow or
   165→   165→prevent other hypotheses, which could well be more plausible and offer better
   166→   166→explanations for the observed effects (if any), from being thoroughly considered or
   167→   167→explored. For example, regular exercise may cultivate learned industriousness
   168→   168→(Eisenberger, 1992), the acquired tendency to find effortful activity itself rewarding, so
   169→   169→that the apparent cognitive benefits observed after chronic exercise interventions
   170→   170→could, at least in part, reflect a broader increase in participants' willingness to engage
   171→   171→with and persist at effortful cognitive tasks rather than any change in cognitive capacity
   172→   172→per se (see Laurence et al., 2015, for evidence in rats). From a complementary
   173→   173→perspective, Pesce et al. (2023), in a meta-review of RCTs of chronic physical activity,
   174→   174→argued that the effects on cognition are best understood through context-mechanism-
   175→   175→outcome configurations. In this framework, the cognitive, motor, and social enrichment
   176→   176→
   177→   177→embedded in the activity, rather than the physical exertion itself, is the likely active
   178→   178→ingredient. This effectively recasts regular physical activity as a form of cognitive
   179→   179→training when it is sufficiently complex, engaging, and varied.
   180→   180→Exercise RCTs, particularly those involving previously sedentary participants, may
   181→   181→also function as broad behavior-change interventions rather than isolated
   182→   182→physiological manipulations. The adoption of a structured exercise program often
   183→   183→coincides with a range of concomitant lifestyle changes, such as enhanced sleep
   184→   184→quality and regularity (Chennaoui et al., 2015), greater attention to diet (Meeusen,
   185→   185→2014), and increased self-efficacy (Wang et al., 2026). In group-based settings, these
   186→   186→interventions further provide significant social interaction and support. While these
   187→   187→factors represent genuine holistic benefits of exercise participation, they introduce
   188→   188→additional, often uncontrolled, confounding variables that complicate the attribution of
   189→   189→cognitive gains to narrow mechanistic pathways (e.g., neurotrophic factors,
   190→   190→angiogenesis, or cardiovascular adaptations) as opposed to these wider psychosocial
   191→   191→and lifestyle shifts. This underscores the premise that while the global value of exercise
   192→   192→is undisputed, more sophisticated experimental designs are required to disentangle
   193→   193→these broader lifestyle drivers from the purported direct effects of physical exertion on
   194→   194→the brain.
   195→   195→The future will determine whether regular physical exercise exerts a genuine effect on
   196→   196→cognition, the magnitude of such an effect, the mechanisms through which it might
   197→   197→operate, the moderators involved, and its practical significance. In our view,
   198→   198→addressing these fundamental questions requires a paradigm shift. This shift begins
   199→   199→with a collective acknowledgment, by basic researchers, practitioners, and funders
   200→   200→alike, of the substantial methodological limitations, statistical pitfalls, and pervasive
   201→   201→publication bias that characterize much of the existing literature. It also requires
   202→   202→recognizing the complexity of the individual context involved in adopting and
   203→   203→maintaining physically active behavior. In line with recent critical appraisals (Román-
   204→   204→Caballero et al., 2023), these limitations concern, among others, the robustness of the
   205→   205→observed effects (often compromised by small sample sizes and low statistical power)
   206→   206→as well as the difficulty of establishing causality, given the frequent lack of appropriate
   207→   207→control conditions to rule out placebo-like and expectancy effects and the inadequate
   208→   208→control of baseline differences and confounding variables. Furthermore, the scarcity
   209→   209→of long-term interventions and longitudinal designs limits our ability to capture the
   210→   210→dynamics and persistence of potential cognitive changes and to formally test causal
   211→   211→models. Importantly, many of these shortcomings reflect not only methodological
   212→   212→choices but also structural and practical constraints, including the high economic and
   213→   213→logistical costs of large-scale interventions, participant adherence over extended
   214→   214→periods, and the inherent challenges of implementing ecologically valid yet well-
   215→   215→controlled designs.
   216→   216→These persistent issues underscore the need for a more nuanced and evidence-based
   217→   217→perspective on which physical activity interventions work, for whom, and under what
   218→   218→contextual circumstances (Pesce et al., 2023). This shift should be accompanied by
   219→   219→the development and testing of novel hypotheses, likely demanding more sensitive
   220→   220→and appropriate tools to detect any cognitive effect of chronic physical exercise, as
   221→   221→well as multilab collaborative studies. At the same time, it is essential that stakeholders
   222→   222→do not lose sight of the robust and well-established evidence supporting the many
   223→   223→
   224→   224→other benefits of physical exercise, which more than justify recommendations for its
   225→   225→regular practice, irrespective of any potential cognitive gains.
