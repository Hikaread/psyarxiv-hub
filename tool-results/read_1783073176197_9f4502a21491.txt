    14→Abstract
    15→Traditional Functional Behavior Assessments (FBAs) excel at isolating the environmental
    16→contingencies that maintain challenging behaviors over time. However, by focusing primarily on
    17→external consequences, the standard operant paradigm often overlooks the biological, interoceptive,
    18→and neurodevelopmental mechanics that lower behavioral thresholds and drive initial behavioral
    19→activation. This paper introduces the Dual Lens Model of Behavior (DLM), a theoretical framework
    20→designed to conceptually unify operant learning histories with internal nervous system states. The
    21→DLM posits that behavioral topographies emerge from the reciprocal interaction between external
    22→reinforcement variables (Lens 1) and internal organismic state-regulation profiles (Lens 2). By
    23→integrating theoretical insights from radical behaviorism, interoceptive neuroscience, allostatic
    24→predictive processing, and trauma-informed care, the model establishes a structured methodology to
    25→distinguish among reinforcement-driven, state-driven, and interaction-driven behavioral
    26→phenotypes. Furthermore, this synthesis offers specific, falsifiable predictions for intervention
    27→outcomes, provides a systematic four-step clinical assessment workflow, and presents a hypothetical
    28→conceptual illustration demonstrating the framework's practical utility. The DLM outlines a
    29→common scientific taxonomy to bridge the historic divide between behavioral analysts and
    30→neurobiological clinicians, with the objective of minimizing iatrogenic harm in therapeutic and
    31→educational environments.
    32→Keywords: functional behavior assessment, interoception, allostasis, behavior analysis, autism,
    33→trauma-informed care, nervous system regulation, clinical workflow

    38→Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) and experimental functional analysis represent a
    39→historical milestone in clinical psychology and applied behavior analysis (ABA), establishing a
    40→robust framework for identifying environmental contingencies that maintain challenging behaviors
    41→(Iwata et al., 1982/1994; Hanley et al., 2003). The standard four-function taxonomy—encompassing
    42→attention, escape, tangible access, and automatic reinforcement—has demonstrated utility in
    43→designing effective, consequence-based interventions across diverse clinical populations. Yet, a
    44→clear conceptual gap persists within contemporary practice: traditional FBA methodologies are
    45→designed to track behavioral maintenance rather than the neurobiological and internal conditions
    46→governing behavioral activation or threshold reduction.
    47→In educational and therapeutic settings, practitioners encounter behavioral episodes that defy
    48→traditional operant explanations. These outbursts often manifest in the absence of clear external
    49→antecedents or immediate maintaining reinforcers. Alternatively, they occur when an organism is
    50→subjected to internal physiological or psychological load—such as chronic pain, sub-clinical
    51→inflammation, sensory overload, neurodevelopmental exhaustion, or systemic autonomic arousal.
    52→When traditional operant interventions, such as extinction or strict differential reinforcement, are
    53→applied uniformly to these phenotypes, clinicians document limited efficacy or a paradoxical,
    54→dangerous escalation in behavioral severity. This clinical challenge exposes a conceptual boundary
    55→in standard applied behavior sciences: the failure to formally account for the organism's baseline
    56→physiological state during the assessment and formulation process.
    57→Concurrently, advancements in interoceptive neuroscience and predictive processing models
    58→of allostasis demonstrate that the central nervous system continuously monitors viscero-somatic
    59→signals to alter affective valence and dictate behavioral output (Craig, 2002; Sterling, 2012).
    60→Behavioral topographies may, in some contexts, function as allostatic strategies utilized by the brain
    61→to anticipate metabolic demands or down-regulate internal physiological distress. Similarly, trauma-
    62→informed and neurodevelopmental frameworks show that chronic stress, altered sensory gating

    65→filters, and communication breakdowns elevate an individual's autonomic baseline, causing neutral
    66→environmental stimuli to be registered as immediate, neuroceptive threats (SAMHSA, 2014; Miller
    67→et al., 2007; Pellicano & Burr, 2012). To resolve this theoretical fragmentation, a unified model is
    68→required to synthesize external operant mechanics and internal neurobiological states into an
    69→actionable diagnostic framework.
    70→The Dual Lens Model Defined
    71→The Dual Lens Model of Behavior (DLM) provides a structured conceptual framework
    72→designed to evaluate behavior as an emergent property of a dynamic organism interacting
    73→continuously with its environment. Rather than viewing operant conditioning and neurobiology as
    74→competing paradigms, the DLM asserts that comprehensive behavioral analysis requires the
    75→simultaneous application of two distinct, interacting causal levels of analysis:
    76→• Lens 1: Behavioral Reinforcement. This lens addresses the external, observable operant
    77→mechanics operating within the environment. It answers the fundamental clinical
    78→question: What environmental consequence is reinforcing and maintaining the behavior
    79→over time? This involves isolating standard environmental functions such as social
    80→positive, social negative, material positive, or automatic sensory maintenance (Iwata et
    81→al., 1982/1994).
    82→• Lens 2: Nervous System State. This lens addresses the internal physiological,
    83→neurodevelopmental, and cognitive variables that dictate an individual's structural
    84→threshold. It answers the critical question: What internal condition is driving the nervous
    85→system toward behavioral activation? This focus evaluates the internal baseline, moving
    86→away from a purely exteroceptive focus toward an integrated axis that honors
    87→interoceptive and antecedent variables.
    88→By shifting the focus from an exteroceptive and consequence-focused axis to an integrated
    89→axis that honors interoceptive and antecedent variables, the DLM redefines the clinical objective.
    90→Interventions must look beyond standard operant extinction to incorporate allostatic stabilization,

   108→Core Nervous System Driver Domains
   109→To operationalize the internal vulnerability axis (Lens 2), the DLM organizes internal
   110→variables into five core Nervous System Driver Domains:
   111→1. Internal Body State (Interoception): Encompasses internal physiological disruptions
   112→that deplete allostatic reserves, generate negative affective valence, and function as
   113→potent unconditioned motivating operations (e.g., gastrointestinal distress, dental
   114→abscesses, hypoglycemia, or sleep deprivation).
   115→2. Sensory Processing: Evaluates structural features in the modulation, habituation, and
   116→integration of environmental and somatic sensory stimuli (e.g., auditory overload or
   117→vestibular seeking; Miller et al., 2007).

   120→3. Control and Predictability: Represents the neurological need for environmental
   121→stability, cognitive certainty, and autonomy to maintain down-regulated autonomic states
   122→(e.g., transition stress and routine rigidity).
   123→4. Emotional State: Assesses acute affective dysregulation where internal emotional states
   124→overwhelm top-down cognitive control mechanisms (e.g., autonomic flooding and
   125→prolonged neurobiological recovery cycles; Garfinkel & Critchley, 2013).
   126→5. Neurodevelopmental Load: Quantifies the immediate cognitive and communicative
   127→demands placed upon an individual relative to their structural neurological capacity (e.g.,

   141→Relationship to Existing Theoretical Frameworks
   142→The DLM does not seek to replace contemporary behavioral or neurobiological paradigms;
   143→rather, it serves as a conceptual bridge that extends and synthesizes them into a bi-directional
   144→framework.

   147→Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) & Operant Contingencies
   148→Traditional FBA operates on a consequence-focused axis, tracking how behaviors are
   149→maintained via environmental reinforcement (Iwata et al., 1982/1994). The DLM preserves this
   150→methodology under Lens 1 but shifts it from an exhaustive explanation to a conditional one. Under
   151→the DLM, the operant functions identified via an FBA may, in some cases, represent the structural
   152→forms through which an underlying nervous system state is expressed.
   153→Motivating Operations (MOs)
   154→In behavior analysis, Motivating Operations (comprising Establishing Operations [EOs] and
   155→Abolishing Operations [AOs]) alter the momentary value of a reinforcer and the frequency of the
   156→behavior maintained by that reinforcer (Michael, 1993). The DLM expands the interpretation of
   157→EOs by formally mapping Lens 2 driver domains as chronic or acute physiological EOs. For
   158→example, internal body state alterations (e.g., neuroinflammation or sleep loss) act as unconditioned
   159→motivating operations that magnify the aversiveness of standard environmental demands, thereby
   160→lowering the behavioral activation threshold for escape.
   161→The Biopsychosocial Perspective & Trauma-Informed Care
   162→While the biopsychosocial model acknowledges that biological, psychological, and social
   163→factors interact, it often lacks an operationalized mechanism for behavior-analytic implementation.
   164→The DLM translates this perspective into behavioral terms by matching autonomic dysregulation
   165→with operant topographies. Similarly, it incorporates trauma-informed concepts—such as
   166→neuroception (Porges, 2004)—by demonstrating how an elevated autonomic baseline causes neutral
   167→external antecedent stimuli to be registered as unconditioned threats, driving survival-based
   168→behavioral topographies independently of active operant consequences (SAMHSA, 2014).
   169→Interoceptive Neuroscience & Allostatic Predictive Processing
   170→Predictive processing models suggest that the brain functions as an allostatic organ,
   171→continuously predicting metabolic demands and regulating the internal milieu via visceral feedback

   177→what appears to be an arbitrary or maladaptive operant topography may represent a logical
   178→behavioral accommodation to an altered internal state.

   224→Empirical Predictions and Hypotheses
   225→To establish its scientific utility and support future empirical testing, the Dual Lens Model
   226→generates four distinct, falsifiable hypotheses. By cross-referencing an individual's operant profile
   227→with their nervous system driver screening, behavior can be categorized into clear archetypes that
   228→yield divergent intervention trajectories:
   229→• Hypothesis 1 (Reinforcement-Driven Topographies): Behaviors presenting with
   230→minimal baseline vulnerabilities across the five Lens 2 domains will respond to
   231→traditional operant interventions, including differential reinforcement and planned
   232→extinction. Conversely, introducing sensory or physiological modifications to these
   233→specific phenotypes will cause no significant change in the baseline rate of the behavior.
   234→• Hypothesis 2 (State-Driven Topographies): Behaviors initiated during acute states of
   235→autonomic flooding, somatic pain, or sensory overload will demonstrate an immediate
   236→reduction in frequency following targeted state-regulation interventions (e.g.,
   237→pharmacological pain management, sensory gating accommodations), irrespective of
   238→whether external reinforcement contingencies remain completely active.
   239→• Hypothesis 3 (Interaction-Driven Topographies / Enhanced MOs): Internal nervous
   240→system dysregulation operates as an unconditioned Motivating Operation (MO) that
   241→magnifies the value of environmental reinforcers. Specifically, an elevated autonomic
   242→baseline will lower the structural threshold for escape-maintained behavior, rendering
   243→standard instructional demands significantly more aversive than they are at physiological
   244→baseline.
   245→• Hypothesis 4 (Iatrogenic Escalation): Implementing strict operant extinction protocols
   246→(such as physical or escape containment) on a state-driven or survival-based behavioral
   247→phenotype will provoke a physiological cascade, characterized by prolonged autonomic
   248→recovery cycles, elevated cortisol markers, and a high probability of iatrogenic
   249→behavioral bursts.

   252→Clinical Application: The Dual Lens Assessment Process
   253→The DLM operationalizes these theoretical concepts into an empirical four-step clinical
   254→assessment workflow designed to systematically reduce subjective bias.
   255→Step 1: Functional Behavior Assessment (The Reinforcement Lens)
   256→The clinician executes standard, empirical FBA procedures comprising indirect assessments,
   257→descriptive ABC data collection, and, when clinically indicated, experimental functional analyses
   258→(Iwata et al., 1982/1994; Hanley et al., 2003). This step isolates the potential environmental
   259→consequences maintaining the behavior across standard conditions: Attention, Escape, Tangible,
   260→Alone/Automatic, or Play/Control.
   261→Step 2: Nervous System State Screening (The Activation Lens)
   262→Concurrently, the clinician administers the Dual Lens Behavior Assessment Checklist (DL-
   263→BAC) to quantify vulnerabilities across the five core Nervous System Driver Domains (Internal
   264→Body State, Sensory Processing, Control/Predictability, Emotional State, and Neurodevelopmental
   265→Load).
   266→Step 3: State Manipulation Probes
   267→To determine whether an identified internal variable is actively driving behavioral
   268→manifestation, the clinician conducts systematic State Manipulation Probes. In these probes,
   269→targeted state-regulation supports are introduced while external environmental reinforcement
   270→contingencies are held completely constant. For example, if an individual exhibits escape-
   271→maintained behavior during academic tasks and presents with high auditory sensitivities on the DL-
   272→BAC, high-fidelity noise-canceling headphones are introduced during tasks while keeping demand
   273→sequences identical.
   274→The Dual Lens Model of Behavior 11
   275→
   276→Step 4: Interaction Analysis and Classification
   277→Based on the data collected during the state manipulation probes, the behavior is formally
   278→classified into one of four distinct archetypes: Reinforcement-Driven, State-Driven, Interaction-
   279→Driven, or Indeterminate. This classification guides the final design of the intervention protocol,
   280→restricting the clinical use of extinction to purely reinforcement-driven phenotypes.
   281→Hypothetical Conceptual Illustration
   282→The following scenario is entirely hypothetical and is intended solely to illustrate the
   283→conceptual logic of the proposed framework rather than represent empirical evidence.
   284→The Standard Operant Formulation (Lens 1)
   285→Consider an illustrative scenario where an 8-year-old student is referred for physical
   286→aggression within an educational setting. A traditional FBA is initiated. Scatterplot logs and
   287→descriptive ABC data demonstrate that aggression occurs primarily during intensive academic
   288→demands or transitions away from preferred electronic items. When aggression manifests, staff
   289→implement a standard escape or tangible removal sequence, allowing the student to take a break or
   290→returning the device to ensure immediate physical safety. Experimental functional analysis
   291→conditions confirm an apparent dual-function operant profile: social negative reinforcement (escape
   292→from tasks) and material positive reinforcement (access to tangibles; Hanley et al., 2003).
   293→Following a standard operant framework, a representative intervention plan is developed
   294→utilizing escape extinction (guided compliance following task refusal), differential reinforcement of
   295→alternative behavior (DRA; prompting the use of a communication icon), and a response-cost
   296→schedule for electronic access. Under a purely consequence-focused model, the student
   297→demonstrates an escalation in aggression. The behavior spreads into previously neutral baseline
   298→periods and culminates in a behavioral burst requiring crisis containment.

   301→The Dual Lens Formulation (Lens 1 + Lens 2)
   302→When evaluated through the Dual Lens Model, the clinician maintains the FBA data but
   303→concurrently analyzes a conceptual screening of the five core driver domains. The assessment
   304→highlights baseline vulnerabilities across two domains: (1) Internal Body State, uncovering a
   305→medical history of chronic gastrointestinal reflux and sub-clinical inflammation; and (2) Sensory
   306→Processing, detailing hypersensitivity to fluorescent light flickering and high-amplitude acoustic
   307→environments (Miller et al., 2007).
   308→State manipulation probes are introduced to isolate these variables conceptually. First, a
   309→medical protocol is utilized to address the gastrointestinal inflammation. Second, environmental
   310→sensory modifications are established: ambient fluorescent lighting is replaced with indirect warm
   311→LEDs, and sound-dampening acoustic panels are installed in the workspace. Crucially, to validate
   312→the theoretical model, academic task demands and electronic access contingencies are held
   313→completely unchanged from baseline levels during these probes.
   314→Under this conceptual framework, the model predicts that the introduction of these state-
   315→regulation supports results in a substantial decrease in aggressive topographies, independent of any
   316→operant consequence modifications. The remaining low-level behavioral episodes are then
   317→addressed through standard DRA skill-acquisition protocols without invoking extinction bursts. The
   318→dual lens analysis demonstrates that the phenotype was strictly Interaction-Driven: gastrointestinal
   319→pain and sensory overload acted as somatic Establishing Operations, elevating the student's
   320→autonomic nervous system baseline and rendering normal instructional demands intolerable.
   321→Resolving the internal state vulnerabilities effectively lowers the neurological load, raising the
   322→behavioral threshold and restoring the efficacy of standard, non-coercive behavior-analytic teaching
   323→protocols.

   326→Discussion
   327→The Dual Lens Model offers a synthesis of external environmental mechanics and internal
   328→neurobiological variables, yet several conceptual boundaries and limitations require formal
   329→acknowledgement to guide future research.
   330→Conceptual Boundaries and Domain Overlap
   331→A primary conceptual limitation of the model lies in the potential boundary overlap among
   332→the five Lens 2 domains. For instance, a neurodevelopmental communication breakdown
   333→(Neurodevelopmental Load) can immediately trigger acute autonomic flooding (Emotional State) or
   334→alter an individual's interoceptive tolerance for somatic discomfort (Internal Body State).
   335→Disentangling these variables requires sensitive, distinct operational definitions within the DL-BAC
   336→to ensure that clinicians do not rely on subjective, confounded inferences. Furthermore, in non-
   337→verbal or highly impaired clinical populations, distinguishing between an unmapped sub-clinical
   338→physiological pain state and an automatic operant reinforcement history remains a diagnostic
   339→challenge.
   340→Methodological Challenges in Naturalistic Settings
   341→The execution of Step 3 (State Manipulation Probes) demands a high degree of
   342→environmental control. In complex, naturalistic settings such as public school classrooms or multi-
   343→generational households, holding external reinforcement contingencies completely static while
   344→systematically isolating a single nervous system variable is logistically difficult. Confounding
   345→environmental variables may blend with state modifications, challenging the internal validity of the
   346→clinician's classification data.
   347→Directives for Future Empirical Testing and Falsifiability
   348→To transition the DLM from a conceptual framework to an empirically validated model,
   349→future research must focus on operationalizing and validating the psychometric reliability of the
   350→Dual Lens Behavior Assessment Checklist (DL-BAC) across diverse settings. Additionally, the

   353→integration of non-invasive biometric tracking devices—such as real-time heart rate variability
   354→(HRV) monitors and galvanic skin response (GSR) sensors—presents a promising avenue to
   355→directly quantify autonomic baseline shifts before, during, and after behavioral episodes. By pairing
   356→real-time biometric tracking with standard descriptive ABC data, future studies can empirically test
   357→the predictive boundaries of interaction-driven phenotypes, moving toward a compassionate,
   358→precise, and scientifically unified behavioral science.
   359→Conclusion
   360→Human behavior is an emergent outcome driven by a continuous, bi-directional feedback
   361→loop between an organism's internal neurobiological state and external environmental consequences
   362→(Skinner, 1953). The Dual Lens Model is intended to provide a conceptual framework for
   363→integrating historically separate perspectives on behavioral function and nervous system state. By
   364→evaluating both what is maintaining a behavior (Lens 1) and what is driving the nervous system
   365→toward activation (Lens 2), clinicians can move beyond reactive, consequence-focused
   366→management. This dual analysis is intended to support the design of conceptually systematic and
   367→testable intervention approaches that can be evaluated empirically.
