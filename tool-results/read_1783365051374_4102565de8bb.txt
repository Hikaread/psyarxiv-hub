    13→Abstract
    14→Background. Drinking intentions consistently predict alcohol consumption, but findings for
    15→behavioral measures such as inhibitory control are mixed, particularly in earlier stages of use.
    16→Methods. 142 recreational alcohol users (40 males, 102 females; M = 33.30 years, SD = 14.76)
    17→completed a four-day EMA study with three daily prompts assessing alcohol use, craving,
    18→perceived control, and planned use, directly followed by a mobile Go/No-Go task assessing
    19→inhibitory control. Bayesian multilevel models examined whether inhibition errors, planned use,
    20→craving, and perceived control predicted drinking at momentary and daily timescales.
    21→Results. Momentary evidence indicated a positive association between planned use and drinking
    22→between (β = 0.18, 95% CrI [0.03, 0.31]) and within-persons (β = 0.31, 95% CrI [0.20, 0.42]),
    23→while negative within-person effects emerged for craving (β = -0.12, 95% CrI [-0.23, -0.02]) and
    24→perceived control (β = -0.41, 95% CrI [-0.52, -0.28]). Daily evidence found that planned use was
    25→positively associated with daily drinking (β = 0.84, 95% CrI [0.44, 1.24]), whereas perceived
    26→control was negatively associated with drinking (β = -0.78, 95% CrI [-1.21, -0.36]). No credible
    27→evidence was found for within-person effects on daily alcohol use.
    28→Conclusions. Findings suggest that alcohol use in recreational drinkers, may be driven by
    29→intentions and self-regulatory beliefs, as opposed to momentary or daily lapses with inhibition. A
    30→negative within-person association between momentary craving and alcohol use, despite a
    31→positive linear study trend, may reflect a mismatch between the reference windows measuring
    32→craving (right now) and drinking (night before, last two hours).
    33→Keywords: Ecological momentary assessment, alcohol use, planned alcohol use, inhibitory
    34→control, Go/No-Go task

    36→Introduction
    37→Individuals characterized by higher levels of impulsivity tend to drink more on average
    38→while also showing greater variability in their day-to-day alcohol use patterns, suggesting
    39→heightened sensitivity to contextual cues and proximal influences that may drive shifts in
    40→drinking from one situation to the next (de Wit, 2009; Koob & Volkow, 2016; Yücel et al.,
    41→2019). A pressing question is therefore not only who is at risk of problematic drinking but also
    42→when individuals are most vulnerable to alcohol use. Mounting evidence suggests that self-
    43→regulatory processes fluctuate dynamically across time and context, and that these momentary
    44→changes are linked to greater alcohol consumption, poorer treatment outcomes, and higher
    45→relapse risk (see e.g. Smith et al., 2014 for a meta-analysis). Understanding these micro-level
    46→dynamics may be critical for designing more responsive prevention and intervention strategies.
    47→To better understand these fluctuating daily processes, researchers have increasingly
    48→examined within-person associations between impulsivity and alcohol using ecological
    49→momentary assessment (EMA; Halvorson et al., 2021; McCarthy et al., 2018; Pedersen et al.,
    50→2019; Racine et al., 2024; Simons et al., 2010; Stamates & Lau-Barraco, 2020; Stevens et al.,
    51→2020), which captures self-regulation in real time and provides an ecologically valid view of how
    52→momentary lapses in inhibitory control relate to drinking in everyday life (Shiffman et al., 2008;
    53→Wray et al., 2014). However, while self-reported impulsivity captures individuals' perceived
    54→tendencies to act rashly, it does not directly assess the underlying cognitive control processes that
    55→regulate behavior in the moment. One related but distinct mechanism thought to underlie
    56→impulsive drinking, and believed to contribute to individual differences in addictive behaviors, is
    57→inhibitory control, defined as the ability to suppress automatic or prepotent responses in favor of
    58→1
    59→
    60→goal-directed behavior (Boog & Franken, 2024; Field et al., 2010; Field & Jones, 2017; Jones et
    61→al., 2013a; Koob & Volkow, 2016).
    62→Inhibitory control has traditionally been studied in laboratory settings using well-known
    63→behavioral tasks, most notably the Stop-Signal Task (SST) and the Go/No-Go task (Faßbender et
    64→al., 2023; Hu et al., 2016; Reichl et al., 2023; Sánchez-Kuhn et al., 2017; Smith et al., 2014;
    65→Weidacker et al., 2017; Wright et al., 2014). The SST requires participants to inhibit an already-
    66→initiated response following the presentation of an unexpected stop signal and is considered a
    67→gold-standard measure of response inhibition (Logan & Cowan, 1984; Verbruggen & Logan,
    68→2008), whereas the Go/No-Go task assesses response withholding (Donders, 1969; Wessel,
    69→2018). During a Go/No-Go task, respondents respond to frequent go stimuli and withhold
    70→responses to no-go stimuli, capturing the point at which drinking can often be prevented most
    71→effectively, before the action is initiated at all. In contrast, response cancellation reflects a later,
    72→emergency form of control, stopping an already initiated action.
    73→Parallel to the rise in EMA studies examining momentary impulsivity, a growing subset
    74→of research has begun using SST and Go/No-Go tasks to assess momentary inhibitory control
    75→(Hachenberger et al., 2024). However, relatively few studies have focused specifically on
    76→inhibitory control in relation to alcohol use, documenting within-person associations between
    77→momentary inhibitory control and alcohol use or craving (Burton & Jones, 2025; Jones et al.,
    78→2013b). This limited body of research is likely due to methodological constraints, as inhibitory
    79→control requires millisecond level accuracy that has only recently become feasible on
    80→smartphones (Schatz et al., 2015). Although some studies suggest that declines in inhibitory
    81→control predict heavier same-day drinking, particularly among heavier drinkers (Jones et al.,
    82→2
    83→
    84→2018), other EMA studies incorporating smartphone-based Go/No-Go or stop-signal tasks tend
    85→to find modest predictive effects when examined in isolation (Zech et al., 2023).
    86→The Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) is a widely accepted framework for predicting
    87→behavior based on behavioral intentions, which are influenced by attitudes, subjective norms, and
    88→perceived self-control (Ajzen, 1991). The TPB framework has been widely applied to health-
    89→related behaviors, including alcohol use, where intentions have been strongly linked to
    90→consumption (Cooke et al., 2016). The mixed findings across EMA studies examining alcohol
    91→use are consistent with theoretical models of addiction, which posit that neurocognitive changes
    92→in reward, stress, and control systems progressively diminish voluntary regulation as drinking
    93→levels increase (Koob & Volkow, 2016; Yang et al., 2022). Recreational drinkers, by contrast,
    94→generally retain greater capacity for intentional control, making this population particularly
    95→informative for examining early self-regulatory processes prior to the onset of dependence. Self-
    96→report EMA studies have likewise demonstrated robust predictive effects of perceived control,
    97→most notably in the work by Remmerswaal et al. (2019), who found that lower perceived control
    98→and higher craving prospectively preceded drinking episodes and predicted consumption volume
    99→using both random and self-initiated assessments in a recreational drinking sample. However, the
   100→role of inhibitory control in recreational drinkers remains unclear. Moreover, meta-analytic
   101→evidence supports a dose–response relationship, with greater alcohol exposure linked to more
   102→pronounced inhibitory impairments (López-Caneda et al., 2014; Smith et al., 2014). Thus,
   103→examining inhibitory control in recreational samples may help clarify whether momentary or
   104→daily fluctuations in inhibitory control are meaningfully linked to alcohol use at earlier stages of
   105→drinking.

   107→
   108→Taken together, the existing literature highlights several key gaps. First, it remains
   109→unclear whether momentary fluctuations in inhibitory control are concurrently associated with
   110→drinking in recreational users, above and beyond well-established self-regulation indicators of
   111→drinking (e.g., perceived control, craving, and planned use). Second, the direction and magnitude
   112→of momentary self-regulation–alcohol associations vary considerably across studies,
   113→underscoring the need for clearer characterization of within-person processes. Third, few EMA
   114→studies have directly compared behavioral and self-report measures of self-regulation when
   115→examined simultaneously within the same model or across multiple timescales. The present study
   116→aimed to assess these gaps using a four-day mobile EMA study administering random self-report
   117→assessments followed by an inhibitory control behavioral task. Using Bayesian multilevel
   118→models, we evaluated both within- and between-person associations across random prompts
   119→(momentary model) and daily aggregates (daily model) to examine the role of perceived control,
   120→craving, planned use, and inhibitory control errors (measured by the Go/No-Go task) in
   121→predicting alcohol consumption in a large sample of regular (weekly minimum) drinkers.
   122→At the within-person level, we hypothesized that moments characterized by poorer
   123→inhibitory control (i.e., more inhibition errors on the Go/No-Go task at a given prompt; Burton &
   124→Jones, 2025), lower perceived control, higher craving, and higher daily planned drinking would
   125→be associated with greater alcohol consumption reported during concurrent random assessments
   126→(RAs; Remmerswaal et al., 2019; Waters et al., 2020). At the between-person level, we expected
   127→similar patterns, with individuals showing on average poorer inhibitory control, higher craving,
   128→and higher planned use reporting higher consumption, though we anticipated these effects might
   129→be weaker given prior EMA evidence that between-person effects are often less robust than
   130→within-person ones (Pedersen et al., 2019; Stevens et al., 2020; Tomko et al., 2014). In addition,
   131→4
   132→
   133→we examined whether within- and between-person associations differed across momentary and
   134→daily time scales; these analyses were exploratory.
   135→Method
   136→Participants. The present study included a convenience sample of 142 participants (40
   137→males, 102 females; M = 33.30 years, SD = 14.76, range = 18–77). Eligibility criteria included:
   138→legal drinking age (18+), self-reported regular alcohol use (defined as more than one alcoholic
   139→drink per week), Dutch language proficiency, and possession of a smartphone. Participants were
   140→recruited through the researchers' social networks and invited via email. Those interested were
   141→provided with a detailed information letter including instructions on how to download the study
   142→applications to their personal smartphone devices. Participants were informed of the voluntary
   143→nature of the study, including the right to withdraw at any time, and confidentiality regarding the
   144→researchers’ use of their personal data. Once they joined the study, participants had the opportunity
   145→to earn 400 euros (2x 100 euros and 4x 50 euros) in raffle prize money. This study was approved
   146→by the Erasmus University Rotterdam Faculty Ethics Committee of the Erasmus School of Social
   147→and Behavioral Sciences.
   148→Procedure. Participants downloaded two applications to their smartphone devices: the
   149→RealLife Exp application (LifeData app) (www.lifedatacorp.com) for surveys and the Inquisit
   150→Player application (Milliseconds app) for the Go/No-Go task. Informed consent was provided via
   151→the LifeData app, followed by a baseline survey on demographics, alcohol use, and impulsivity,
   152→which participants also filled out through the app. Over four consecutive days, participants
   153→received three random EMA prompts per day, from 10 in the morning until 10 in the evening, in
   154→which they were given a maximum of 90-minutes to complete the survey, after which the survey
   155→5
   156→
   157→would disappear. The random prompt was followed by a link to complete a Go/No-Go behavioral
   158→task in the Milliseconds app.
   159→Baseline Measure: Alcohol Use Problems. The Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test
   160→(AUDIT; Babor et al., 2001; Saunders et al., 1993; Dutch translation: Schippers & Broekman,
   161→2010) was used to evaluate the severity of alcohol use. In the current sample, the AUDIT showed
   162→good internal consistency (Cronbach's α = .84; Tavakol & Dennick, 2011), which aligns with
   163→previous reports that also used the AUDIT to assess hazardous drinking (α ≈ .84; Babor et al.,
   164→2001; Burton & Jones, 2025). Detailed item content and scoring are reported in Supplementary
   165→Materials (S.1.1 Methods).
   166→Daily Measure: Craving. Participants rated their craving for alcohol at the current moment
   167→on a 0 to 100-point Visual Analogue Scale (VAS), ranging from 0 (no urge) to 100 (strongest urge),
   168→both during randomly prompted and self-initiated assessments.
   169→Daily Measure: Perceived Control. Participants rated their perceived ability to control
   170→their drinking with a single-item measure: "How much control do you feel you have over your
   171→drinking?" Responses were recorded on a VAS scale of 0 (no control) to 100 (a lot of control).
   172→Daily Measure: Alcohol Consumption. During morning random assessments (RAs),
   173→participants were asked to report the number of alcoholic beverages they had consumed the day
   174→before, which were followed by afternoon and evening RAs on alcohol use within the past two
   175→hours.
   176→Daily Measure: Planned Use. During the morning's RAs, participants were asked to
   177→estimate the total amount of drinks they were planning to consume for the upcoming day.
   178→Daily Measure: The cued Go/No-Go Task. Participants were instructed to complete a five-
   179→minute (100 trial) adapted mobile Go/No-Go task (Fillmore et al., 2006; Figure 1) using the
   180→6
   181→
   182→Inquisit Player application (Milliseconds app) on their smartphone, in which they needed to press
   183→the screen for green (go) targets and refrain from pressing for blue (no-go) targets. Vertical cues
   184→were followed by green (go) targets in 80% of cases, while horizontal cues were followed by blue
   185→(no-go) targets in 80% of cases. In line with common practice in Go/No-Go and EMA quality
   186→control, we excluded blocks with accuracy below 60% (slightly above chance, 50%), indicating
   187→unreliable engagement with the task (Jones et al., 2018; Marhe et al., 2013). Additional task
   188→exclusion criteria are reported in Supplementary Materials (S.1.2 Methods).
   189→Figure 1. Cued Go/No-Go task. A fixation cross was presented on the screen for 800 ms, followed by a
   190→blank screen for 500 ms. Next, a white rectangle cue was positioned vertically or horizontally in the center
   191→of the screen for 100-500 ms, followed by either a green go target or a blue (no-go) target. In 80% of trials,
   192→vertical cues predicted green (go) targets and horizontal cues predicted blue (no-go) targets.
   193→Statistical Analysis. Before fitting our daily and momentary multilevel models examining
   194→fluctuations in alcohol use, we first examined how each variable of interest changed on average
   195→across the four-day EMA study. We ran a series of Bayesian mixed-effects models using Markov
   196→chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling in R version 4.5.3 (R Core Team, 2026) using the brms
   197→package (Bürkner, 2017), in which each dependent variable (momentary alcohol use, craving,
   198→perceived control, and planned alcohol use) was predicted from elapsed time since the first RA of
   199→the study, with random intercepts for participant. Inhibitory control was not included in these linear
   200→7
   201→
   202→time trend models because Go/No-Go task performance across prompts was already examined
   203→separately (see Figure 2), and no meaningful linear trend was observed.
   204→Next, we ran two Bayesian multilevel models at the momentary (prompt) level and the
   205→daily level. Before fitting them, we examined patterns of missing data, which indicated the data
   206→were not missing completely at random (i.e., not MCAR; see Supplementary S.1.3). We therefore
   207→accounted for the missingness using multiple imputation (MICE), which assumes data are missing
   208→at random (MAR). One participant was dropped from the momentary model due to poor Go/No-
   209→Go task performance, resulting in a momentary sample of 141 participants (3,148 assessments). In
   210→the daily model, 28 participants were excluded because they did not have complete RA data,
   211→resulting in a final sample of 114 participants contributing between 1 and 4 days each (295
   212→aggregated person-days). In both models, time-varying predictors were decomposed into within-
   213→person (state) and between-person (trait) components using person-mean centering. The
   214→momentary model added an autoregressive (AR₁) structure to account for residual temporal
   215→dependence between consecutive EMA prompts (Jongerling et al., 2015), while the daily model
   216→used aggregated momentary variables to examine average day-level consumption. Full prior
   217→specifications, sampler settings, missingness rates and imputation procedure, and rationale for the
   218→random-effects structure are reported in Supplementary Materials (S.1.3-1.5 Methods).
   219→Convergence was assessed using trace plots, Gelman-Rubin statistics (R̂ ), and inspection
   220→of divergent transitions on representative single-imputation fits; parameter estimates were pooled
   221→across all 30 imputations using Rubin's rules (Bürkner, 2017; Gelman et al., 1996). For both
   222→models, trace plots indicated good mixing and stationarity of the chains, per-imputation Gelman-
   223→Rubin parameters were within acceptable limits (R̂ ≤ 1.05), and no divergent transitions or sampler
   224→warnings were observed. Posterior predictive checks indicated reasonable daily model fit; however,
   225→8
   226→
   227→the Gaussian likelihood did not fully capture the right-skewed distribution of momentary alcohol
   228→use. The primary models used a Gaussian likelihood; a sensitivity analysis using a negative
   229→binomial distribution, which is designed for widely dispersed data, yielded similar results, with the
   230→exception of the small within-person craving effect, which was no longer credible under the
   231→negative binomial specification (Supplementary Materials 2.0 Tables S1-S3). Model parameters
   232→were interpreted based on posterior means and 95% credible intervals; intervals excluding zero
   233→were regarded to demonstrate evidence of a credible effect, whereas intervals including zero lacked
   234→credible evidence (Kruschke, 2018; Kruschke, 2021).

   324→Discussion
   325→The current study examined whether the combined use of a behavioral inhibition task and
   326→self-report measures of planned use, perceived control, and craving, would explain unique
   327→within- and between-person variance in alcohol use across momentary and daily time scales. In
   328→both the momentary and daily models, there was evidence of a credible positive association
   329→between planned use and alcohol consumption, such that individuals who reported higher
   330→14
   331→
   332→average morning planned use consumed more alcohol overall, and days characterized by higher
   333→planned use were associated with higher alcohol use across that day's EMA prompts. There were
   334→two unexpected findings based on our initial hypotheses. First, there was no evidence of a
   335→credible association between inhibitory control (measured by errors on the Go/No-Go task) with
   336→alcohol consumption at either the momentary or daily level. And second, the momentary model
   337→demonstrated a small yet credible negative within-person association between craving and
   338→alcohol use, whereas no evidence of an effect was found at the daily level, despite clear within-
   339→person fluctuations across the study as demonstrated by the linear time trend analyses.
   340→The evidence supporting planned use in driving momentary and daily alcohol use echoes
   341→the well-supported framework of TPB and suggests that planned use reflects intentionality or
   342→anticipatory commitment, structuring the day’s drinking behavior and increasing the likelihood
   343→that drinking plans are realized (Ajzen, 1991; Cooke et al., 2016). However, an intention-
   344→behavior gap often exists, in which momentary self-regulatory and situational factors help to
   345→determine whether intentions translate into behavior, a finding reflected across previous EMA
   346→studies highlighting the central role of intentionality across recreational and heavier drinking
   347→populations (Jones et al., 2018; Simons et al., 2010; Stevens et al., 2020). Conceptually, the
   348→current study results align with widely accepted models of addiction that emphasize top-down,
   349→goal-directed processes during earlier stages of alcohol use, when drinking is often guided by
   350→planned intentions and expectancies rather than automatic or impulsive responses (Volkow et al.,
   351→2010).
   352→Similarly, perceived control emerged as a robust within-person predictor of momentary
   353→alcohol consumption, such that lower perceived control at a given prompt was associated with
   354→higher drinking at that time. Within the TPB framework, perceived behavioral control is
   355→15
   356→
   357→considered a key determinant of both intentions and behavior, reflecting individuals' perceived
   358→ability to regulate their behavior (Ajzen, 1991; Cooke et al., 2016). In line with this framework,
   359→the present findings suggest that momentary fluctuations in perceived control, can act as a
   360→proximal “brake” on drinking, such that even when drinking is planned, moments of reduced
   361→perceived control increase the likelihood of consumption. These results are consistent with
   362→Remmerswaal et al. (2019), who reported that lower perceived control predicted greater alcohol
   363→use in a recreational drinking sample, and conceptually support the notion that self-regulation
   364→shapes drinking behavior at both trait and state levels. Planned use reflects the anticipatory and
   365→intentional aspects of drinking that determine whether drinking is likely to occur across the day,
   366→whereas perceived control captures the momentary ability to regulate behavior in response to
   367→situational demands, helping to explain why perceived control emerged alongside planned use as
   368→a proximal predictor of momentary alcohol use. It is also important to note that self-report and
   369→behavioral measures of inhibition and self-regulation are often only weakly associated, as they
   370→appear to capture different aspects of self-regulatory functioning (Bernoster et al., 2019). Thus,
   371→the evidence of a credible effect found for planned use and perceived control in predicting
   372→alcohol use, but not for inhibitory control and craving, may reflect differences in what these
   373→measures assess rather than the relative importance of these constructs for drinking behavior.
   374→The analysis examining inhibitory control, measured by inhibitory (commission) errors
   375→on the Go/No-Go task, did not provide credible evidence for an effect to predict alcohol
   376→consumption at either the momentary or daily level. This finding may be due to the following
   377→reasons. First, accuracy scores indicate that the Go/No-Go task was relatively easy to perform
   378→(average accuracy ~98%), which may have resulted in a ceiling effect and hence in restricted
   379→variance to inform predictions of alcohol use and detect individual differences in inhibitory
   380→16
   381→
   382→control (Burton & Jones, 2025; Chirokoff et al., 2024). Another possibility is that problems with
   383→momentary inhibitory control may be less salient among recreational drinkers than in heavier or
   384→dependent drinkers because their inhibitory control capacity is not chronically impaired (as is
   385→often observed in heavier/dependent use) and therefore, are less likely to perform poorly on
   386→momentary Go/No-Go tasks (Abroms et al., 2006; de Wit, 2009; Koob & Volkow, 2016; Smith
   387→et al., 2014). However, future studies should explore whether this is also true for more general
   388→trait levels of inhibitory control among recreational drinkers. Furthermore, overall compliance
   389→across the inhibitory control task was moderate (~41%), which implies that completing a
   390→cognitive behavioral task such as the Go/No-Go, may add additional participant burden on top of
   391→a daily EMA survey even for recreational users. This drop in compliance may be a result of users
   392→needing to leave the EMA app to use a separate platform to complete the Go/No-Go task.
   393→The unexpected negative association between craving and alcohol consumption in the
   394→momentary model may reflect the timing of random prompts rather than a true suppression
   395→effect. While most EMA literature supports a positive association between craving and alcohol
   396→use, this discrepancy likely reflects a fundamental difference in assessment design.
   397→Remmerswaal et al. (2019) demonstrated that craving co-occurs with alcohol use at self-initiated,
   398→but not random, assessments among recreational drinkers, indicating that assessment type likely
   399→plays a role. More specifically, craving was captured specifically at the moment of assessment,
   400→while alcohol use was measured over a broader reference window (the previous night at morning
   401→prompts; the past two hours at afternoon and evening prompts). Because a participant’s craving
   402→level at the moment of assessment may not reflect their craving state throughout the full window
   403→over which drinking was reported. A participant who drank earlier in the two-hour window may
   404→therefore report elevated alcohol use alongside reduced current craving (a post-consumption
   405→17
   406→
   407→state), while a participant whose craving had not yet translated into drinking may report elevated
   408→current craving alongside lower recent use (an anticipatory state). Consistent with this, Burton
   409→and Jones (2025) found that greater craving intensity was associated with lower concurrent
   410→alcohol consumption (β = -0.04, 95% CI [-0.06, -0.01]), while still predicting subsequent use,
   411→suggesting a timing-related negative association. Together, these findings may help explain the
   412→confounding positive linear time trend for both craving and alcohol in the current study. More
   413→broadly, existing literature supports a dose–response relationship between craving and drinking
   414→severity, suggesting that craving may act as a proximal driver of alcohol use in heavier use or
   415→alcohol-dependent populations (Kulkarni et al., 2026; Shiffman et al., 2008; Smith et al., 2014).
   416→The small credible negative within-person craving effect observed in the primary Gaussian
   417→model was no longer credible under the negative binomial specification (see Supplementary
   418→Table S3), further supporting the interpretation that this association is likely attributable to
   419→temporal misalignment.
   420→Strengths, Limitations, and Future Directions. The present study is part of a limited
   421→number of daily assessment studies that successfully integrated the use of an adapted behavioral
   422→phone task. Using an EMA design, we were able to capture more ecologically valid data
   423→involving alcohol use in the everyday life of recreational drinkers, while simultaneously limiting
   424→recall bias by assessing behaviors as they occur in real-time or shortly afterwards (Shiffman et
   425→al., 2008). Nevertheless, certain limitations should be taken into consideration with the current
   426→findings. First, the slight mismatch between the time reference used to measure craving and
   427→alcohol may have influenced the negative association that emerged at the momentary level.
   428→Although participants ranged in age from 18 to 77, the sample mean of 33.30 years suggests
   429→findings may be most generalizable to younger recreational drinkers, and caution should be
   430→18
   431→
   432→exercised when extrapolating to older populations. Another possible limitation was the four-day
   433→study duration, a relatively short time to fully capture the fluctuations between momentary and
   434→daily predictors of alcohol use that might have emerged over a longer study period, especially
   435→among infrequent users. Moreover, the use of a mobile Go/No-Go task in predicting real-world
   436→outcomes compared to the strength of previous findings exhibited by self-report questionnaires
   437→(Eisenberg et al., 2019; Zech et al., 2023). Lastly, while planned use was the strongest predictor
   438→in the momentary and daily models predicting alcohol use, it also had the highest rate of missing
   439→data (82.9%) prior to MI given it was only reported once during morning assessments. Therefore,
   440→most values were imputed in the momentary model, and the credible association with momentary
   441→alcohol use should be assessed with some caution.
   442→To conclude, the present study highlights new findings on the role of momentary and
   443→daily predictors of alcohol use among recreational drinkers, adding to a growing body of
   444→research examining within- and between-person fluctuations in self-regulatory (e.g., planned use,
   445→perceived control, and craving) or inhibitory processes on the effects of daily drinking (Burton &
   446→Jones, 2025; Jones et al., 2018; Remmerswaal et al., 2019; Stevens et al., 2020). Together, the
   447→current findings suggest that in recreational drinkers, planned use and perceived control may be
   448→more influential in predicting daily drinking patterns, whereas no credible evidence was found
   449→for an effect of automatic mechanisms (i.e., inhibitory control, craving) underlying drinking on
   450→the consumption in the current sample. These findings may inform future digital health
   451→interventions, such as just-in-time-adaptive interventions (JITAIs; Nahum-Shani et al., 2018;
   452→Waddell et al., 2025) aimed at preventing hazardous drinking among recreational users by
   453→optimizing intervention timing during periods of elevated drinking intentions or reduced
   454→perceived control.

   850→Momentary Cognitive and Self-Regulatory Predictors of Daily Alcohol Use: Intent vs.

   852→1.0 Methods
   853→1.1 AUDIT Item Content and Scoring
   854→The AUDIT is comprised of 10 items broken down into three separate domains aimed at
   855→assessing the severity of one’s drinking behavior: 1) alcohol consumption (items 1-3), 2)
   856→drinking habits (items 4-7), and 3) consequences of alcohol use (items 8-10). The first 8
   857→items are measured on a 5-point Likert scale (0 = never used to 4 = almost daily use),
   858→whereas items 9 and 10 are scored on a 3-point Likert scale (0 = no; 2 = yes, but not in
   859→the last year; 4 = yes, within the last year). Scores can range from 0-40 on the AUDIT,
   860→with a score of 40 indicating the highest level of alcohol use severity one can attain.
   861→However, a score of 8 or higher is enough to indicate problematic alcohol use.
   862→1.2 Go/No-Go Task Data Quality Criteria
   863→We also excluded blocks with implausible mean reaction times (<200 ms), a standard
   864→approach to remove anticipatory or excessively delayed responses (Ratcliff, 1993;
   865→Whelan, 2008). Participants with more than 50% of their blocks flagged as invalid (due to
   866→low accuracy or implausible reaction times) were excluded from further analyses. In total,
   867→21 blocks were flagged as invalid across all participants, leading to the exclusion of one
   868→participant who exceeded the 50% threshold (proportion invalid = 0.67).
   869→1.3 Missingness and Multiple Imputation
   870→Missingness was examined at the prompt-, person-, and item-levels, and associations
   871→between missingness and observed values were tested using Wilcoxon rank-sum tests
   872→with false discovery rate (FDR) correction. Missingness in several prompt-level variables
   873→showed systematic associations with other study variables (e.g., craving, perceived
   874→control). Planned alcohol use (82.9% missing) was administered at morning prompts
   875→only, so its high missingness is missing by design, and its apparent association with
   876→observed alcohol use reflects time-of-day differences in drinking rather than informative
   877→missingness. By contrast, missingness in inhibition errors (60.3% missing), which was
   878→administered after every random assessment, showed systematic associations with
   879→observed values (e.g., craving), suggesting that data are not missing completely at
   880→random (i.e., not MCAR). Momentary self-report items (alcohol use, craving, perceived
   881→control) showed approximately 36% missingness. We also assessed for careless
   882→responding including invariant responses such as identical VAS scores across the board
   883→(i.e., “straightlined” responses). Approximately 0.8% of RAs contained evidence of
   884→
   885→careless responding, a finding considered to be negligible. Thus, no cases were excluded
   886→based on careless or inattentive responding.
   887→Because MICE assumes data are missing at random (MAR), these associations are
   888→accounted for in the imputation model; however, missing not at random (MNAR) cannot
   889→be ruled out from the observed data alone. Only 211 complete data rows across 84
   890→participants were available before multiple imputation. Missingness was handled through
   891→multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE; van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn,
   892→2011) using predictive mean matching (PMM), generating and pooling 30 imputed
   893→datasets. The imputation model included alcohol use, inhibition error, perceived control,
   894→craving, planned use, age, gender, participant, time since the first random assessment, and
   895→cumulative time since the first random assessment.
   896→1.4 Random-Effects Structure Rationale
   897→Both models included random intercepts for participants, and all decomposed predictors
   898→were standardized prior to estimation. Because assessments were irregularly spaced, time
   899→since the previous random assessment was included as a within-person predictor, and
   900→time since the first random assessment was used as the time index for the AR₁ structure.
   901→A random slope for time since previous assessment was tested but removed due to poor
   902→identifiability (45 divergent transitions). Random slopes for the substantive within-person
   903→predictors (craving, perceived control, inhibition errors, planned use) were not included,
   904→as the limited number of within-person observations (a maximum of 12 RAs per
   905→participant over four days) was insufficient to reliably estimate separate slopes for each
   906→predictor per participant. In the daily model, 19 participants contributed only a single day
   907→and therefore could not inform within-person daily estimates.
   908→1.5 Prior Specifications and Sampler Settings
   909→Weakly informative priors were specified for all parameters to improve model stability
   910→and regularize estimates. Regression coefficients were assigned normal priors centered at
   911→zero (Normal(0, 0.25)), with slightly wider priors for the daily model (Normal(0, 0.5)).
   912→Intercepts were assigned Student-t priors (df = 3, μ = 0, σ = 2.0). Random effect standard
   913→deviations were assigned Cauchy priors (0, 0.3), and residual standard deviations (σ)
   914→were assigned Student-t priors (df = 4, μ = 0, σ = 1.5). These priors constrain extreme
   915→parameter values while allowing the data to primarily inform the posterior estimates. For
   916→each imputed dataset, the momentary model was estimated using three chains with 3,000
   917→iterations per chain (1,500 warm-up), and the daily model was estimated using four
   918→chains with 4,000 iterations per chain (1,500 warm-up).
   919→2.0 Sensitivity Analysis: Negative Binomial Distribution
   920→
   921→The posterior predictive checks indicated that the momentary model did not adequately
   922→fit with the Gaussian distribution. The initial decision to use a Gaussian likelihood was
   923→based on previous common practice among EMA studies to use a Gaussian likelihood to
   924→examine alcohol use. To determine the robustness of the model findings, we ran
   925→additional sensitivity checks by fitting a negative binomial distribution to both the
   926→momentary and daily models see if there were any noticeable differences to the initial
   927→results. The negative binomial family (log link) extends the Poisson to allow for
   928→overdispersion and is designed for count data such as number of drinks. The negative
   929→binomial accommodates the right-skewed distribution of alcohol use that the Gaussian
   930→likelihood did not fully capture. The main results in the paper are still based on the
   931→Gaussian models, and the negative binomial results are provided here as supplementary
   932→material to show that the findings hold across both specifications. Coefficients are on the
   933→log scale and are not directly comparable in magnitude to the Gaussian estimates; the
   934→comparison focuses on which predictors remain credible (95% CrI excluding zero) and
   935→the direction of effects. Priors were adjusted for the log link: regression coefficients
   936→Normal(0, 0.5), intercept Student-t(3, 0, 2.5), random effect SD Cauchy(0, 0.3), and
   937→shape parameter Gamma(0.01, 0.01).
