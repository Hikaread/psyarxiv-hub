    25→Abstract
    26→Objective: Although psychological harm among animal care workers is well documented,
    27→whether specific occupational exposures predict distinct conditions remains untested. This study
    28→examined whether specific occupational exposures differentially predict moral injury, secondary
    29→traumatic stress (STS), burnout, and depression, and additionally tested two predictions specific
    30→to moral injury. Method: A cross-sectional sample of 291 U.S. animal care workers across six
    31→sectors completed a 12-item author-constructed exposure frequency checklist, the Moral Injury
    32→Outcome Scale, Professional Quality of Life Scale, and Patient Health Questionnaire-9. Bivariate
    33→and hierarchical regressions, partial correlations, and caseness-group comparisons tested
    34→differential prediction. Results: Exposure types activated distinct conditions. Total exposure
    35→dose predicted STS, burnout, and depression but not moral injury. Euthanasia exposure, assessed
    36→separately as decision-making and administration, predicted burnout and secondary traumatic
    37→stress, but not moral injury. Handling deceased animal remains predicted moral injury and trust
    38→violations after controlling for both euthanasia decision-making and administration, paralleling
    39→military aftermath-of-war findings. Public threats and moral accusations predicted moral injury
    40→across both shame and trust violation subvariants and were the only exposure predicting all four
    41→conditions; verbal aggression most strongly predicted burnout and depression; physical
    42→aggression most strongly predicted STS. Depression was the least differentiated outcome.
    43→Clinical moral injury was characterized by elevated interpersonal threat exposure, not by
    44→elevated animal care task exposure. Conclusions: Different exposure types activate different
    45→psychological conditions. Findings challenge current euthanasia-centric conceptualizations of
    46→psychological harm in animal care and support differentiated condition-specific assessment and
    47→intervention.
    48→
    49→Keywords: moral injury, secondary traumatic stress, burnout, animal care workers, occupational
    50→exposure
    51→
    52→Animal care workers, including those in animal sheltering, animal control, veterinary medicine,
    53→rescue, and volunteer roles, are routinely exposed to occupational stressors that can erode well-
    54→being. These demands have been linked to a set of related but separable conditions: burnout,
    55→secondary traumatic stress (STS), compassion fatigue, depression, and moral injury. Despite
    56→some disparity of terminology, these constructs can be broadly defined. Burnout is a state of
    57→emotional exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced efficacy arising from chronic workplace demands
    58→(Maslach & Leiter, 2016); STS is the posttraumatic symptoms that follow indirect exposure to
    59→others' suffering (Bride, 2004); compassion fatigue is an empathic-distress state widely
    60→conceptualized as the combination of burnout and STS (Stamm, 2010); depression is marked by
    61→persistent low mood, anhedonia, and impaired functioning; and, more recently, moral injury is
    62→defined as a syndrome resulting from exposure to events that violate deeply held moral beliefs,
    63→commonly assessed through two subvariants, shame (i.e., internalized self-blame) and trust
    64→violations (i.e., loss of faith in others and institutions; Litz et al., 2009, 2022, 2025; Shay, 2014).
    65→Each has been documented in animal care populations, where a growing body of evidence points
    66→to substantial well-being concerns overall (Andrukonis & Protopopova, 2020; Scotney et al.,
    67→2019; Scotney et al., 2015; Trevathan-Minnis et al., 2025; Volk et al., 2022; Wolf et al., 2024).
    68→Although frequently co-occurring, these are empirically distinct constructs with different
    69→etiologies, symptom profiles, and treatment implications for animal care workers (McNally,
    70→2026a). Despite increased recognition of these issues, critical gaps persist in the literature, and
    71→more work is needed to understand how these distinct conditions develop in animal care settings,
    72→including whether different exposure types predict different psychological conditions in this
    73→population.
    74→Occupational Exposures in Animal Care Work
    75→
    76→In animal care settings, workers are likely to encounter a range of potentially difficult stressors
    77→such as direct animal care tasks (i.e., treating severe wounds and illness), making euthanasia
    78→decisions, administering euthanasia, events involving the separation of animals and owners (i.e.,
    79→receiving surrendered animals, removing animals from neglect or abuse, interacting with
    80→distressed owners), witnessing or discovering animal abuse, handling deceased animal remains,
    81→and various forms of public hostility including verbal aggression, physical aggression, and
    82→threats and moral accusations. These exposure types differ in sensory intensity, moral content,
    83→relational context, and institutional embeddedness, dimensions that may determine which
    84→psychological condition develops.
    85→Given the broad range of possible exposures, the wide variation across animal care settings, and
    86→the conceptual confusion introduced by years of overlapping and conflated terminology,
    87→cohesive and systematic study of animal care worker well-being has been challenging. Research
    88→has nonetheless expanded: broad well-being surveys document overall distress (Scotney et al.,
    89→2019; Volk et al., 2022; Wolf et al., 2024), euthanasia-related strain has been examined in
    90→relation to factors such as live release rate and euthanasia practices (Andrukonis & Protopopova,
    91→2020; Rohlf & Bennett, 2005), and quantitative work has begun to link general job demands
    92→(Monaghan et al., 2024), as well an animal-care-specific exposures to psychological outcomes
    93→(Andrukonis et al., 2020; Trevathan-Minnis et al., 2025). This expansion matters, because
    94→collapsing distinct forms of psychological harm into a single, broad explanation yields
    95→interventions that inherit the same lack of distinction, leaving workers' condition-specific needs
    96→unmet and directing organizational resources too narrowly.
    97→Continued progress therefore depends on furthering this literature to determine whether
    98→particular exposures leave workers especially susceptible to particular outcomes, mapping which
    99→
   100→exposures produce which conditions. Research on euthanasia's impact must also continue,
   101→informed by broader multidisciplinary frameworks rather than examined in isolation or without
   102→theoretical grounding. Distinguishing which exposures produce which conditions is, ultimately, a
   103→precondition for accurate assessment and tailored intervention.
   104→Two A Priori Predictions for Moral Injury
   105→Building on this need to identify which exposures drive which conditions, the present study gives
   106→particular attention to moral injury. While STS, burnout, and compassion fatigue have been
   107→recognized in animal care for some time, moral distress and moral injury have only more
   108→recently been acknowledged as relevant to this population, with moral injury increasingly
   109→identified as an occupational hazard (Baysal et al., 2024; Williamson et al., 2023) and recent
   110→prevalence estimates indicating that 17.4% of animal care workers may experience clinical moral
   111→injury and an additional 21.0% of workers may carry subclinical moral distress (McNally,
   112→2026b). Two a priori predictions specific to moral injury are therefore advanced, each developed
   113→below before the study's full aims are presented.
   114→Public Hostility As A Moral Stressor
   115→A central a priori prediction concerns public hostility, specifically the threats and moral
   116→accusations animal care workers encounter from the public. Moral injury theory holds that injury
   117→arises not only from one's own perceived transgressions but from the acts of others and from
   118→betrayal (Litz et al., 2009; Shay, 2014). Yet accounts of moral injury in animal care have
   119→centered almost entirely on exposures involving animals, while the morally injurious potential of
   120→human-sourced, interpersonal exposures has gone largely unexamined. Animal care workers are
   121→routinely criticized for their decisions and for undesired outcomes; many find themselves on the
   122→receiving end of unsolicited messages of public scorn, for example being labeled "killers" or
   123→
   124→called uncaring. These are accusations that strike at moral identity and often directly contradict
   125→workers' own understanding of their work as compassionate and animal-centered. This gap
   126→between external condemnation and internal moral commitment is itself a candidate pathway to
   127→moral injury as it has the potential to be experienced as a relational, identity-based assault rather
   128→than a depleting or fear-based stressor. A full theoretical treatment is beyond the scope of this
   129→article, yet these predictions draw on a moral-identity framework grounded in established
   130→literature on moral-emotion and social-sanction processes (Aquino & Reed, 2002; Bandura,
   131→1991; McNally, 2026c; Tangney et al., 2007). The present study therefore predicts that public
   132→threats and moral accusations — distinct from generalized verbal or physical aggression — will
   133→predict moral injury across both its shame and trust-violation subvariants.
   134→The Aftermath Hypothesis: Body Handling as Moral Residue
   135→A second a priori prediction — derived not from the animal care literature but from military
   136→trauma research — concerns the handling of deceased animal remains. In military contexts,
   137→mortuary affairs (i.e., the recovery, processing, and transport of human remains) has been
   138→associated with elevated PTSD and depression independent of combat exposure (McCarroll et
   139→al., 1995; Sutker et al., 1994), and Flynn, McCarroll, and Biggs (2015) documented
   140→psychological stress specific to military mortuary workers and distinct from combat-related
   141→stress. This literature framed remains handling primarily within a trauma/PTSD paradigm. The
   142→present study advances a different prediction: that in animal care, the aftermath of death
   143→constitutes a morally injurious exposure, distinct from the trauma and depletion of the euthanasia
   144→act itself.
   145→Across cultures and history, mortuary and memorial practices (e.g., burial, ceremony, the
   146→marking and honoring of remains) have conferred meaning on death, affirmed the moral worth of
   147→
   148→the deceased, and allowed survivors to integrate loss into a coherent moral order (Burrell &
   149→Selman, 2022; Hidalgo et al., 2021; Neimeyer, 2019). The handling of deceased animal remains
   150→in many animal care settings involves processes such as placement in plastic bags, freezing, and
   151→procedural storage that strips death of this ritual scaffolding, confronting workers with the body
   152→and the system's disposition of it absent any meaning-conferring acknowledgment. This exposure
   153→is distinct from the resource-driven decision-making that precedes euthanasia and from the active
   154→witnessing of suffering and death during administration. Where those stages engage the worker
   155→as decision-maker or witness, the aftermath engages the worker as the one who must reconcile
   156→what the institution does with the body once life has ended. The manner of handling may itself
   157→function as an institutional betrayal (Smith & Freyd, 2014) consistent with the prediction that
   158→body handling is associated specifically with trust violations rather than shame alone. Many
   159→cultural grief practices are also communal, underscoring the role of social support in integrating
   160→loss; in animal care, by contrast, workers are often left to absorb repeated deaths alone, without
   161→institutional recognition or ritual.
   162→The Present Study
   163→This study examined whether specific occupational exposure types differentially predict moral
   164→injury, STS, burnout, and depression. Two a priori predictions specific to moral injury outcomes
   165→were also examined: that public threats and moral accusations, and the handling of deceased
   166→animal remains, would each predict moral injury outcomes. The following hypotheses were
   167→tested:
   168→Hypothesis 1. Different exposure types will show distinct patterns of association with the four
   169→outcomes, such that no single exposure predicts all conditions uniformly and outcomes are
   170→differentiated by exposure type.
   171→
   172→Hypothesis 2. Public threats and moral accusations will predict moral injury across both its
   173→shame and trust-violation subvariants, distinct from generalized verbal or physical aggression.
   174→Hypothesis 3. Euthanasia exposure (decision-making and administration) will predict STS,
   175→burnout, and moral injury.
   176→Hypothesis 4. Handling deceased animal remains will uniquely predict moral injury outcomes
   177→after controlling for euthanasia decision-making and administration.
   178→Method
   179→Participants
   180→Participants were recruited through professional networks, social media, and animal care
   181→organizations across the United States. Out of 393 individuals who accessed the survey, a final
   182→sample of 291 animal care workers resulted consisting of those who provided the required
   183→electronic informed consent, met inclusion criteria (i.e., 18 years or older and currently working
   184→or volunteering in animal care), and completed an adequate percentage of the survey for proper
   185→data analysis. The sample was predominantly female (64.9%, n = 189), White (81.4%, n = 237),
   186→and ranged in age from 18 to 80 years (M = 36.8, SD = 10.12). Participants represented six
   187→primary sectors: animal control (18.2%, n = 53), sheltering (22.7%, n = 66), veterinary medicine
   188→(10.7%, n = 31), rescue (29.9%, n = 87), volunteer/foster (10.3%, n = 30), and other (8.2%, n =
   189→24). A majority of participants had worked or volunteered in animal care for 3 or more years
   190→(89.3%, n =259), with substantial weekly engagement: 62.4% n = 181 working or volunteering
   191→40 or more hours per week and 80.0% n = 232 reporting 30 or more hours per week.
   192→Measures
   193→
   194→Occupational Exposure Frequency Checklist.
   195→A 12-item author-constructed checklist assessed the frequency of specific occupational
   196→exposures in animal care work. Items were developed based on clinical experience, existing
   197→literature, professional experience with animal care professionals, direct experience in animal
   198→care, and theoretical frameworks that informed hypotheses. Nine items assessed animal-related
   199→exposures (i.e., receiving surrendered animals, removing animals from abuse/neglect, interacting
   200→with owners of animals being surrendered/removed, euthanasia decision-making, administering
   201→euthanasia, tending to severe wounds, treating severe illness, seeing animal abuse, and handling
   202→deceased animal remains) and three items assessed public hostility exposures (i.e., verbal
   203→aggression, physical aggression, and receiving threats/accusations from the public). Items were
   204→rated on a 6-point frequency scale from 1 (neither deal with nor hear about) to 6 (every day). The
   205→12-item scale demonstrated good internal consistency (α = .89, N = 281).
   206→Moral Injury Outcome Scale (MIOS).
   207→The MIOS (Litz et al., 2022) is a 22-item measure assessing moral injury outcomes. Fourteen
   208→Impact items (rated 0–4) yield a total impact score (range 0–56) and two subscale scores: Shame
   209→(7 items, range 0–28) and Trust Violations (7 items, range 0–28). Eight Functional Impairment
   210→items assess interference with daily functioning. Clinical caseness thresholds (Litz et al., 2025)
   211→classify respondents as no moral injury, moral distress (subclinical), or moral injury (clinical
   212→syndrome). Internal consistency was excellent (α = .91 for Impact Total, .88 for Shame, and .84
   213→for Trust Violations).
   214→Professional Quality of Life Scale (ProQOL-5).
   215→
   216→The ProQOL-5 (Stamm, 2010) assesses compassion satisfaction, burnout, and secondary
   217→traumatic stress in helping professionals. The present findings analyzed the 10-item Burnout (α =
   218→.73) and 10-item STS (α = .86) subscales, each rated 1–5 and summed (range 10–50).
   219→Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9).
   220→The PHQ-9 (Kroenke et al., 2001) is a 9-item measure of depression severity rated 0–3 (range 0–
   221→27). Internal consistency was good (α = .89).
   222→Procedure
   223→This study was reviewed by Pearl IRB and received an exempt research determination according
   224→to 45 CFR 46.104(d)(2) on June 11, 2024 (IRB ID 2024-0249). Participants provided electronic
   225→informed consent and completed an anonymous online survey distributed through professional
   226→animal care networks, social media platforms, and direct outreach to animal care organizations.
   227→The survey was structured in two parts: Part 1 (N = 291) included the MIOS, ProQOL-5, PHQ-9,
   228→the 12-item exposure checklist, and demographic items. Part 2 (n = 151) was optional and
   229→included additional measures not reported here.
   230→Data Analysis
   231→Valid sample sizes ranged from 278 to 290 across analyses due to missing data on individual
   232→measures. Ten participants had incomplete MIOS responses. Analyses proceeded in four stages.
   233→First, bivariate regressions tested each of the 12 exposure items as an individual predictor of each
   234→of the six outcomes (i.e., Moral Injury Total, Shame, Trust, depression, STS, burnout), yielding a
   235→12 × 6 differential prediction matrix. Second, hierarchical regressions tested whether the three
   236→public hostility items added significant variance beyond the nine animal care items in predicting
   237→moral injury outcomes. Third, partial correlations tested whether handling deceased animal
   238→
   239→remains uniquely predicted moral injury outcomes after controlling for euthanasia decision-
   240→making and euthanasia administration. Fourth, one-way ANOVAs compared exposure
   241→frequencies across moral injury caseness levels. A PMIE cross-validation compared exposure
   242→frequencies between workers who did and did not endorse a morally injurious event on the
   243→MIOS.
   244→Results
   245→Descriptive Statistics
   246→Means, standard deviations, intercorrelations, and internal consistencies for all study variables
   247→are presented in Table 1. Moral injury impact scores averaged 21.49 (SD = 10.45, range 0–56),
   248→with trust violations (M = 11.88, SD = 5.51) exceeding shame (M = 9.62, SD = 6.11). ProQOL
   249→subscale scores fell within the average range per ProQOL-5 interpretive guidelines (Stamm,
   250→2010): burnout (M = 27.07, SD = 6.22) and STS (M = 29.35, SD = 7.59). Depression scores (M
   251→= 10.96, SD = 6.27) fell at the PHQ-9 threshold for moderate depression (≥ 10; Kroenke et al.,
   252→2001). The 12-item exposure composite averaged 3.45 (SD = 0.96) on the 1–6 frequency scale.
   253→The four outcome constructs were moderately to strongly intercorrelated (e.g., moral injury with
   254→burnout, r = .57; with STS, r = .41; with depression, r = .62; all p < .001), consistent with their
   255→status as related but distinct conditions
   256→A dissociation between exposure dose and type was evident at the composite level. The 12-item
   257→exposure composite correlated significantly with STS (r = .220, p < .001), burnout (r = .126, p =
   258→.032), and depression (r = .138, p = .019), but not with Moral Injury Total (r = .062, p = .304),
   259→Shame (r = .071, p = .234), or Trust Violations (r = .038, p = .528). The sheer quantity of
   260→occupational exposure a worker accrued predicted their secondary traumatic stress, burnout, and
   261→
   262→depression, but not their moral injury. Moral injury, in contrast to the other three conditions, was
   263→unrelated to total exposure dose.
   264→Differential Prediction of Psychological Outcomes
   265→Table 2 presents the standardized coefficients from bivariate regressions of each exposure item
   266→on each outcome. Several patterns supported differential prediction.
   267→Trust violations showed the most selective profile: only three of 12 items reached significance
   268→— handling deceased animal remains (β = .130, p = .029), verbal aggression (β = .155, p = .010),
   269→and threats/accusations (β = .140, p = .019). No animal care task predicted trust violations. STS
   270→showed the broadest profile, with 10 of 12 items reaching significance or trend.
   271→Three items were non-significant across all six outcomes: receiving surrendered animals,
   272→interacting with owners of animals being surrendered/removed, and treating severe illness.
   273→Threats and accusations from the public was the only item that significantly predicted all six
   274→outcomes. By contrast, handling deceased animal remains showed a selective moral injury
   275→profile — significant for Moral Injury Total and Trust Violations, trending for Shame and
   276→burnout, and non-significant for depression.
   277→The Euthanasia Null Finding
   278→Euthanasia decision-making did not significantly predict Moral Injury Total (β = -.006, p =
   279→.924), Shame (β = -.022, p = .718), or Trust Violations (β = .013, p = .826). Administering
   280→euthanasia showed a similar pattern (Moral Injury Total β = .064, p = .289; Trust β = .002, p =
   281→.979), with only a trend for Shame (β = .108, p = .072). Neither euthanasia variable predicted
   282→depression (decision-making β = .049, p = .404; administration β = .112, p = .058). By contrast,
   283→euthanasia decision-making significantly predicted STS (β = .157, p = .008) and burnout (β =
   284→
   285→.142, p = .016), and administering euthanasia predicted STS (β = .144, p = .014). This pattern —
   286→euthanasia predicting STS and burnout but not moral injury — was consistent across bivariate
   287→regression, partial correlation, and caseness comparison.
   288→Differential Prediction of Depression
   289→In bivariate regressions, depression was predicted by verbal aggression (β = .217, p < .001),
   290→threats/accusations (β = .171, p = .004), physical aggression (β = .207, p < .001), and seeing
   291→abuse (β = .199, p < .001) — the same exposures that predicted STS — with no exposure
   292→uniquely predicting depression beyond this shared set. By contrast, handling deceased animal
   293→remains did not predict depression (β = .084, p = .154), and this weak association was further
   294→attenuated in partial correlations. Every significant depression predictor was also a significant
   295→STS predictor: depression's exposure signature was a subset of STS's broader profile, with no
   296→distinct etiology of its own.
   297→Hierarchical Regression: Public Hostility Beyond Animal Care Tasks
   298→Hierarchical regressions added the three public hostility items (Block 2) to the nine animal care
   299→items (Block 1) for each moral injury outcome (Table 3). The public hostility cluster added
   300→significant variance for every moral injury outcome: Moral Injury Total (ΔR² = .039, Fchange(3,
   301→259) = 3.74, p = .012), Shame (ΔR² = .033, Fchange(3, 259) = 3.25, p = .023), and Trust
   302→Violations (ΔR² = .039, Fchange(3, 259) = 3.62, p = .014). For Trust Violations, the animal care
   303→block alone was non-significant (Block 1 R² = .042, p = .243) and became significant only with
   304→the addition of public hostility.
   305→In the full (Block 2) models, the unique predictors of moral injury localized to two exposures.
   306→For Moral Injury Total, only handling deceased remains (β = .188, p = .022) and
   307→
   308→threats/accusations (β = .188, p = .015) were significant. For Trust Violations, the same two
   309→items were significant — handling remains (β = .202, p = .015) and threats (β = .173, p = .028).
   310→For Shame, only threats reached significance (β = .165, p = .031), with seeing abuse and body
   311→handling trending. Body handling and public threats converged as the only unique moral injury
   312→predictors in saturated 12-item models, surviving alongside all competing exposures.
   313→The hierarchical models also recovered the same condition-specific signatures seen bivariately.
   314→In the full models, burnout was uniquely predicted by verbal aggression (β = .212, p = .003) and
   315→threats (β = .151, p = .047); STS by physical aggression (β = .200, p = .014) and seeing abuse (β
   316→= .182, p = .036); and depression by verbal aggression (β = .183, p = .012).
   317→Body Handling as Unique Predictor of Moral Injury
   318→Partial correlations controlling for both euthanasia decision-making and administration revealed
   319→that handling deceased animal remains remained a significant predictor of Moral Injury Total (r
   320→= .146, p = .015) and Trust Violations (r = .166, p = .006), but not Shame (r = .100, p = .098),
   321→STS (r = .057, p = .345), or depression (r = .053, p = .386). By contrast, euthanasia decision-
   322→making (Moral Injury Total r = -.082, p = .177) and administering euthanasia (Moral Injury Total
   323→r = -.010, p = .866) were non-significant when controlling for body handling. Body handling was
   324→the unique predictor; euthanasia was not. Notably, body handling's bivariate associations with
   325→STS (r = .146) and depression (r = .084) dropped to non-significance once euthanasia was
   326→controlled, indicating that those associations were shared with euthanasia exposure, whereas the
   327→moral injury association was independent of it. Body handling thus survived as a unique moral
   328→injury predictor across three convergent tests — the bivariate regression, this partial correlation,
   329→
   330→and the saturated 12-item regression (β = .188 for Moral Injury Total, β = .202 for Trust
   331→Violations).
   332→Relational Contact as a Protective Exposure
   333→One exposure operated in the protective direction. In the full hierarchical models, interacting
   334→with owners of animals being surrendered/removed was a significant negative predictor of STS
   335→(β = -.283, p < .001) and depression (β = -.232, p = .004), indicating that workers reporting more
   336→frequent owner contact reported lower STS and depression.
   337→Caseness Analysis
   338→One-way ANOVAs compared exposure frequencies across moral injury caseness levels (no
   339→moral injury, n = 172–173; moral distress, n = 58–59; clinical moral injury, n = 49). Significant
   340→differences emerged for seeing abuse, F(2, 277) = 4.879, p = .008; verbal aggression, F(2, 277) =
   341→5.350, p = .005; threats/accusations, F(2, 277) = 5.646, p = .004; and physical aggression, F(2,
   342→278) = 3.496, p = .032. For each of these four interpersonal exposures, Tukey HSD contrasts
   343→confirmed that the clinical moral injury group reported significantly higher exposure than the no-
   344→moral-injury group (seeing abuse mean difference = 0.68, p = .007; verbal aggression = 0.67, p =
   345→.004; threats = 0.72, p = .003; physical aggression = 0.51, p = .038), and homogeneous-subset
   346→tests placed the clinical group in its own band for seeing abuse, verbal aggression, and threats.
   347→Euthanasia decision-making, F(2, 275) = 1.953, p = .144, and administering euthanasia, F(2,
   348→276) = 1.624, p = .199, did not differ across caseness levels. The clinical moral injury group was
   349→distinguished by its elevated interpersonal-threat profile, not by its exposure to animal care tasks
   350→or euthanasia (Table 4).
   351→
   352→Handling deceased remains also differed across caseness groups, F(2, 278) = 3.720, p = .025, but
   353→in a pattern distinct from the interpersonal exposures: the elevation was carried by the moral
   354→distress group (M = 3.80) rather than the clinical group (M = 3.67), and the only significant
   355→Tukey contrast was moral distress > no moral injury (mean difference = 0.56, p = .041); the
   356→clinical-versus-no-injury contrast was non-significant (p = .182), and the homogeneous-subset
   357→test did not separate the groups (p = .079). Thus, although body handling discriminates caseness
   358→groups overall, it does not single out the clinical moral injury group the way the interpersonal
   359→exposures do.
   360→PMIE Cross-Validation
   361→A planned cross-validation compared exposure frequencies between workers who endorsed at
   362→least one potentially morally injurious event (PMIE) on the MIOS (n ≈ 243) and those who did
   363→not (n ≈ 47), using independent-samples t-tests across all 12 exposure items. On a two-sided test
   364→with the appropriate variance correction, no exposure item significantly distinguished PMIE
   365→endorsers from non-endorsers. The largest difference was for threats and accusations from the
   366→public, which endorsers reported more frequently at a trend level (Welch t = -1.876, df = 58.89, p
   367→= .066; under an equal-variances assumption t(287) = -2.129, p = .034, but Levene's test
   368→indicated unequal variances, F = 5.49, p = .020, favoring the Welch estimate). All effect sizes
   369→were small (|d| ≤ 0.35), with confidence intervals spanning zero. That exposure frequency largely
   370→fails to distinguish PMIE endorsers from non-endorsers indicates that PMIE endorsement is not a
   371→simple function of how much exposure a worker accrues. This converges with the composite-
   372→level finding that total exposure dose predicted STS, burnout, and depression but not moral
   373→injury.
   374→
   375→Discussion
   376→This study examined whether specific occupational exposures in animal care work differentially
   377→predict moral injury, secondary traumatic stress, burnout, and depression. Several principal
   378→findings emerge.
   379→Tasks Non-Significant for Measurable Injury
   380→In bivariate analysis, three exposures were non-significant across all four conditions: receiving
   381→surrendered animals, interacting with owners of animals being surrendered/removed, and treating
   382→severe illness. These routine animal care tasks, while potentially stressful, did not produce
   383→measurable psychological injury on any assessed outcome, suggesting that distress in animal care
   384→is concentrated in specifically charged exposures rather than distributed evenly across the work.
   385→It is worth noting, however, that in the full hierarchical model, interacting with owners of
   386→animals being surrendered/removed was a significant negative predictor of STS and depression
   387→once other exposures were held constant, despite being unrelated to either outcome at the
   388→bivariate level. This suppression pattern suggests that direct contact with the people responsible
   389→for an animal may buffer against traumatic stress and demoralization, perhaps by affording
   390→workers an agentic role — helping a person access treatment, connect with resources, or make an
   391→informed decision in the animal's interest — rather than positioning them as passive witnesses to
   392→suffering. This finding should be interpreted with caution and become an area of future research.
   393→Dose Versus Type: Moral Injury Is Not a Function of Exposure Amount
   394→A striking pattern emerged at the composite level: the total amount of occupational exposure a
   395→worker reported predicted secondary traumatic stress, burnout, and depression, but was entirely
   396→unrelated to moral injury. STS, burnout, and depression behaved partly as dose-dependent
   397→conditions — accumulating with the sheer quantity of difficult exposure — whereas moral injury
   398→
   399→did not. Moral injury is not produced by how much a worker is exposed to but by what kind of
   400→exposure they encounter: the relational, moral, and institutional character of specific events, not
   401→their frequency or volume. This is consistent with the conceptual distinction between trauma-
   402→load models, in which harm scales with dose, and moral injury, in which harm follows from the
   403→violation of moral expectation regardless of frequency. It also cautions against exposure-count
   404→screening as a means of identifying morally injured workers, since the workers most at risk for
   405→moral injury are not necessarily those reporting the most exposure.
   406→Public Hostility and Moral Accusation as Moral Injury Drivers
   407→The three public hostility items were among the strongest predictors across outcomes, and
   408→threats/accusations was the only item to predict all four conditions. Critically, the public hostility
   409→items showed distinct signatures: verbal aggression was the strongest predictor of burnout and
   410→depression (i.e., a dejection pathway), physical aggression was the strongest predictor of STS
   411→(i.e., a fear/arousal pathway), and threats/accusations was the strongest predictor of moral injury
   412→across both subvariants (i.e., a moral-identity-threat pathway). The public hostility cluster added
   413→significant variance for moral injury beyond the nine animal care items.
   414→This differential pattern within public hostility has implications for measurement and
   415→intervention. Being verbally berated is depleting and dejecting but does not specifically produce
   416→moral injury; being morally accused — called an animal killer, murderer, or uncaring — strikes
   417→at moral identity and activates the shame and trust-violation pathways that define moral injury.
   418→Collapsing types of public hostility into a single construct obscures these distinct effects.
   419→Euthanasia Is Not the Primary Driver of Moral Injury
   420→
   421→Contrary to Hypothesis 3, neither euthanasia decision-making nor administration predicted moral
   422→injury, despite the prominence of euthanasia in accounts of distress in animal care work. Both
   423→predicted STS and burnout, but the moral injury signal was carried solely by handling deceased
   424→remains. Hypotheses anticipated that remains handling would carry the greatest moral weight,
   425→but not that deciding and administering would be non-significant for moral injury altogether.
   426→While Hypotheses 4 was supported, Hypotheses 3 was not. Disaggregating euthanasia revealed
   427→that three exposures routinely grouped under a single heading — deciding, administering, and
   428→handling remains afterward — are psychologically distinct: the decision and the act operated
   429→through depletion and traumatic-stress pathways, while the aftermath operated through moral
   430→injury. Because prior work has treated euthanasia participation as a unitary construct, any moral
   431→injury signal localized to remains handling would have been masked.
   432→This finding carries immediate practical implications. Organizational resources directed toward
   433→euthanasia-related support — while potentially helpful for STS and burnout — may fail to
   434→address the exposures that actually produce moral injury. A more targeted approach would
   435→distinguish euthanasia support (e.g., addressing trauma and depletion) from moral injury
   436→prevention (e.g., addressing the relational and moral-meaning dimensions of exposure, including
   437→how remains are handled).
   438→The Aftermath, Not the Act: Body Handling as Moral Residue
   439→Handling deceased animal remains uniquely predicted moral injury — specifically Moral Injury
   440→Total and Trust Violations — after controlling for both euthanasia decision-making and
   441→administration, and neither euthanasia variable retained significance once body handling was
   442→controlled. This association appears dimensional rather than categorical when considering that,
   443→although remains handling predicted moral injury continuously across the sample, it did not
   444→
   445→sharply distinguish the clinical moral injury group at the caseness level, where its elevation was
   446→carried by the moral distress group rather than the clinical one.
   447→That handling remains would load onto moral injury was an a priori prediction (Hypothesis 4)
   448→derived from the military aftermath of war findings and mortuary-affairs literature, in which
   449→remains handling has been associated with psychological distress independent of combat
   450→exposure (Flynn et al., 2015; McCarroll et al., 1995; Sutker et al., 1994).The present results
   451→extend that literature in two ways. First, they demonstrate the analogous dissociation in a civilian
   452→animal care population: the aftermath of death is psychologically distinct from the act that
   453→precedes it. Second, where the military literature framed remains handling within a trauma/PTSD
   454→paradigm, the present pattern situates it within a moral paradigm — body handling predicted
   455→trust violations and moral injury specifically, not merely STS. This pattern is consistent with the
   456→interpretation that the way deceased animals are handled may itself constitute a morally injurious
   457→event. The absence of meaningful death rituals may leave workers to absorb the moral residue of
   458→death without the scaffolding that ordinarily makes it bearable.
   459→This interpretation also locates the violation in the systemic conditions that structure this
   460→exposure. The infrastructures of contemporary animal care render certain realities procedurally
   461→"necessary", including that animals are often euthanized at volume, and their bodies handled and
   462→stored with the efficiency the system demands. The injury may be activated at either point of the
   463→violation, or the intersection of the two conditions: confronting the bodily aftermath of death, and
   464→doing so within a system that both houses those “necessary” realities and then furthermore
   465→provides no ritual, mourning, or acknowledgment to mark it.
   466→
   467→This is consistent with the present finding that body handling predicted trust violations rather
   468→than shame: the injury registers as a rupture in the moral order maintained by others and by the
   469→institution — a betrayal-based moral injury in Shay's (2014) sense — rather than as self-
   470→condemnation. Where the system makes undignified disposal routine and supplies no
   471→countervailing ritual, the absence of honoring is itself the morally injurious condition.
   472→Additionally, at the organizational level for locus of repair: the relevant intervention is not only
   473→clinical treatment of the injured worker but structural reform of how institutions acknowledge
   474→animal death.
   475→Because this disaggregated euthanasia pattern is, to the author's knowledge, the first of its kind in
   476→animal care populations, it requires replication across independent samples and sectors before
   477→strong conclusions are drawn. The claim is not that deciding or administering euthanasia is
   478→benign — both predicted STS and burnout — but that their relationship to moral injury
   479→specifically may be weaker than the field has assumed.
   480→Depression as the Least Differentiated Outcome
   481→Depression's exposure signature was the least distinct of the four conditions. It was predicted by
   482→the dejecting interpersonal exposures it shares with burnout (e.g., verbal aggression and threats)
   483→rather than by a unique class of exposure. This is consistent with depression's transdiagnostic
   484→standing: it functions less as a condition with its own exposure etiology and more as a
   485→downstream consequence of chronic conditions. Where moral injury was predicted specifically
   486→by interpersonal/moral threat and the institutional aftermath of death, and STS was predicted
   487→broadly by emotional and sensory exposure to suffering, depression was predicted non-
   488→specifically, positioning itself as a condition that accrues across the demands of the work rather
   489→than arising from any particular exposure.
   490→
   491→Limitations and Future Directions
   492→Several limitations warrant consideration. The cross-sectional design precludes causal inference;
   493→longitudinal research is needed to establish temporal ordering among exposure and outcome. The
   494→12-item exposure checklist was author-constructed and, while demonstrating acceptable
   495→reliability (α = .89), has not undergone formal psychometric validation; future research should
   496→develop and validate a standardized measure of occupational exposures in animal care, ideally
   497→distinguishing exposure frequency from perceived moral violation. Two items in the public
   498→hostility cluster — verbal aggression (in person) and threats/accusations (in person or online) —
   499→are confounded on the online dimension, preventing clean differentiation of whether the moral
   500→content or the online visibility of hostility drives moral injury. The sample was recruited through
   501→professional networks and social media, introducing potential self-selection bias.
   502→Future research should disaggregate euthanasia-related exposure rather than measuring it as a
   503→single construct. Given the novelty of the disaggregated pattern and the body-handling finding,
   504→replication across independent samples and sectors is a priority. Future work should also test
   505→organizational-level interventions (e.g., improved remains-handling protocols, structured
   506→debriefing after public hostility incidents) and examine whether specific exposure types activate
   507→different conditions or moral injury subvariants.
   508→Conclusion
   509→The psychological cost of animal care work is not a single burden but a set of distinct injuries.
   510→Different exposure types activate different psychological conditions, where moral injury was
   511→predicted by the relational and moral-meaning dimensions of exposure, was the most exposure-
   512→specific of the four conditions, and was the only one unrelated to total exposure dose. STS was
   513→
   514→predicted broadly by direct emotional and sensory exposure to animal suffering while burnout
   515→was most strongly predicted by chronic interpersonal hostility and, bivariately, by the cognitive
   516→burden of euthanasia decision-making, consistent with a depletion pathway. Depression was the
   517→least differentiated and shared its predictors with STS. Routine relational contact with owners
   518→was, if anything, protective. Disaggregating euthanasia into its components revealed that the
   519→handling of remains, rather than the decision or the act, uniquely predicted moral injury — a
   520→finding obscured when euthanasia is treated as a single exposure. These findings challenge the
   521→field's euthanasia-centric understanding of psychological harm in animal care. Protecting animal
   522→care workers will require moving beyond a single narrative of harm to a more differentiated
   523→accounting that allows for assessment and intervention matched to the source of each condition.
