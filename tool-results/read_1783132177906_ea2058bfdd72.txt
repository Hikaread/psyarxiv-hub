    50ŌåÆ    50ŌåÆ    50ŌåÆWhether the same reciprocity dynamics carry over to CMC dyads in which the two parties enter the
    51ŌåÆ    51ŌåÆ    51ŌåÆexchange in clearly different roles, and whether reciprocal emoticon use in those dyads predicts how
    52ŌåÆ    52ŌåÆ    52ŌåÆthe interaction is perceived by the help-seeking party, has not been examined. The present study takes
    53ŌåÆ    53ŌåÆ    53ŌåÆup this question in a text-only helpline setting, namely an anonymous youth chatline in Singapore in
    54ŌåÆ    54ŌåÆ    54ŌåÆwhich a trained chat worker provides brief support to a help-seeking young person. The setting is of
    55ŌåÆ    55ŌåÆ    55ŌåÆinterest in its own right, given young people's growing use of text-based services for mental health
    56ŌåÆ    56ŌåÆ    56ŌåÆsupport (Gatti et al., 2016; Navarro et al., 2019; Nesmith, 2018). It also provides a useful case for asking
    57ŌåÆ    57ŌåÆ    57ŌåÆhow emoticon reciprocity operates when the two communicators occupy structurally different
    58ŌåÆ    58ŌåÆ    58ŌåÆpositions, with implications for other CMC dyads marked by role, training, and status asymmetry
    59ŌåÆ    59ŌåÆ    59ŌåÆText-based chat as a help-seeking context for young people
    60ŌåÆ    60ŌåÆ    60ŌåÆText-based chat (TBC) has become a key modality for delivering mental health support to young
    61ŌåÆ    61ŌåÆ    61ŌåÆpeople. Young people aged 10 to 24 years are at high risk for mental health problems yet are among the
    62ŌåÆ    62ŌåÆ    62ŌåÆleast likely to seek professional treatment, citing privacy, stigma, and discomfort with face-to-face
    63ŌåÆ    63ŌåÆ    63ŌåÆsettings (Navarro et al., 2020). For these reasons they increasingly prefer anonymous online chat, which
    64ŌåÆ    64ŌåÆ    64ŌåÆthey experience as less stigmatising, more accessible, and, for those with social anxiety, easier for
    65ŌåÆ    65ŌåÆ    65ŌåÆorganising and expressing thoughts (Gatti et al., 2016; Haner & Pepler, 2016; Navarro et al., 2019;
    66ŌåÆ    66ŌåÆ    66ŌåÆNesmith, 2018). Yet, the same features that make TBC appealing (anonymity, text-only interaction,
    67ŌåÆ    67ŌåÆ    67ŌåÆabsence of face-to-face contact) also strip away the nonverbal channels through which warmth and
    68ŌåÆ    68ŌåÆ    68ŌåÆunderstanding are conventionally communicated in helping work. Tone of voice, facial expression, and
    69ŌåÆ    69ŌåÆ    69ŌåÆgesture are unavailable, leaving the chat worker with typed text as the primary means of conveying
    70ŌåÆ    70ŌåÆ    70ŌåÆsupport. In this context, Chung & Lim (2025) found that emoticon use was the strongest predictor of
    71ŌåÆ    71ŌåÆ    71ŌåÆusers' perceptions of service usefulness in a Singapore youth chatline, which they interpret as evidence
    72ŌåÆ    72ŌåÆ    72ŌåÆthat emoticons convey affective tone in ways that words alone do not easily replicate in typed text.
    73ŌåÆ    73ŌåÆ    73ŌåÆIf emoticon use by chat workers matters in this way, how chat worker and user emoticon use
    74ŌåÆ    74ŌåÆ    74ŌåÆinfluence each other across a session becomes a question worth asking. Yet this question has not been
    75ŌåÆ    75ŌåÆ    75ŌåÆexamined in role-asymmetric helping dyads, that is, dyads such as the one in the present study, in which
    76ŌåÆ    76ŌåÆ    76ŌåÆa trained chat worker is in role to provide support to a young person seeking help. The two enter the
    77ŌåÆ    77ŌåÆ    77ŌåÆchat in different positions and with different jobs to do, and the chat worker, unlike the user, is
    78ŌåÆ    78ŌåÆ    78ŌåÆexpected to keep to a professional manner.
    79ŌåÆ    79ŌåÆ    79ŌåÆEmoticons and reciprocity in CMC
    80ŌåÆ    80ŌåÆ    80ŌåÆReciprocity in cue use is consequential for how communication partners perceive one another.
    81ŌåÆ    81ŌåÆ    81ŌåÆCoyle & Carmichael (2019) found that interlocutors were rated as more responsive when both used
    82ŌåÆ    82ŌåÆ    82ŌåÆemoji than when only one did so, and Cavalheiro et al. (2024) reported similar results on warmth and
    83ŌåÆ    83ŌåÆ    83ŌåÆ
    84ŌåÆ    84ŌåÆ    84ŌåÆcommunication positivity. Stein (2023) showed experimentally that participants used emoji more often
    85ŌåÆ    85ŌåÆ    85ŌåÆwhen replying to messages that contained emoji, particularly with closer interlocutors. Huh (2025)
    86ŌåÆ    86ŌåÆ    86ŌåÆextended these findings, showing that the influence of partner responsiveness, enhanced through emoji
    87ŌåÆ    87ŌåÆ    87ŌåÆuse, extends from face-to-face interactions into digital ones.
    88ŌåÆ    88ŌåÆ    88ŌåÆTwo complementary theoretical accounts make sense of these findings. The first is the
    89ŌåÆ    89ŌåÆ    89ŌåÆInterpersonal Process Model of Intimacy (IPM; Laurenceau et al., 2004; Laurenceau et al., 1998; Reis,
    90ŌåÆ    90ŌåÆ    90ŌåÆ2017; Reis & Shaver, 2018), which describes intimacy as the outcome of an ongoing transactional cycle:
    91ŌåÆ    91ŌåÆ    91ŌåÆone person reveals personal feelings or information, the other responds in ways that convey
    92ŌåÆ    92ŌåÆ    92ŌåÆunderstanding, validation, and caring, and if the discloser perceives the response as responsive, the
    93ŌåÆ    93ŌåÆ    93ŌåÆcycle continues, with roles often reversing across the interaction. Face-to-face, alongside the words
    94ŌåÆ    94ŌåÆ    94ŌåÆspoken, nonverbal cues such as tone, gesture, and facial expression play a key role in conveying this
    95ŌåÆ    95ŌåÆ    95ŌåÆresponsiveness (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019). In CMC, those cues are unavailable, which threatens the
    96ŌåÆ    96ŌåÆ    96ŌåÆcycle. Social Information Processing theory (Walther, 1992) and its extension, the hyperpersonal model
    97ŌåÆ    97ŌåÆ    97ŌåÆ(Walther & Whitty, 2021), hold that communicators redirect socioemotional expression through
    98ŌåÆ    98ŌåÆ    98ŌåÆwhatever code systems the medium affords, and that reciprocal exchange of these substitute cues can
    99ŌåÆ    99ŌåÆ    99ŌåÆintensify, rather than dampen, the relational tone of the interaction. Emoticons and emojis, as a form of
   100ŌåÆ   100ŌåÆ   100ŌåÆparalanguage, are one such substitute code: they fill the gap left by absent vocal and facial cues and
   101ŌåÆ   101ŌåÆ   101ŌåÆallow affect, on either side of the exchange, to be conveyed in text. Consistent with this, Coyle &
   102ŌåÆ   102ŌåÆ   102ŌåÆCarmichael (2019), drawing on the IPM, found that when college students and a responder converged in
   103ŌåÆ   103ŌåÆ   103ŌåÆtheir use of emoji during text-based self-disclosures, the responder was rated as more responsive, and it
   104ŌåÆ   104ŌåÆ   104ŌåÆis this perception of responsiveness that keeps the IPM cycle going.
   105ŌåÆ   105ŌåÆ   105ŌåÆCommunication Accommodation Theory (CAT; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007; Giles &
   106ŌåÆ   106ŌåÆ   106ŌåÆSmith, 1979) provides a wider account of how convergence works as a communicative process. CAT
   107ŌåÆ   107ŌåÆ   107ŌåÆproposes that interlocutors adjust their communication style to match one another, particularly when
   108ŌåÆ   108ŌåÆ   108ŌåÆthey perceive mutual similarities, want to build affiliation, or seek approval, and that this adjustment is
   109ŌåÆ   109ŌåÆ   109ŌåÆinterpreted by the other party as a signal of empathy and attunement (Giles & Ogay, 2007). CAT further
   110ŌåÆ   110ŌåÆ   110ŌåÆholds that this whole process is shaped by features of the dyad such as hierarchy, formality, and the
   111ŌåÆ   111ŌåÆ   111ŌåÆrelative status of the two parties (Adams et al., 2018; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007), which raises
   112ŌåÆ   112ŌåÆ   112ŌåÆthe question of how convergence plays out when the two interlocutors do not occupy symmetric
   113ŌåÆ   113ŌåÆ   113ŌåÆpositions.
   114ŌåÆ   114ŌåÆ   114ŌåÆRole-asymmetric dyads and the helping context
   115ŌåÆ   115ŌåÆ   115ŌåÆThe dyads in which emoticon reciprocity has been directly tested have been peer-like: peers and
   116ŌåÆ   116ŌåÆ   116ŌåÆstrangers paired for self-disclosure (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019), dating partners and acquainted
   117ŌåÆ   117ŌåÆ   117ŌåÆinteraction partners (Stein, 2023), close friends in informal exchanges (Huh, 2025), and work colleagues
   118ŌåÆ   118ŌåÆ   118ŌåÆ(Cavalheiro et al., 2024). In each of these settings, the two parties enter the conversation on broadly
   119ŌåÆ   119ŌåÆ   119ŌåÆequal terms and can adjust their style toward one another from comparable positions. In the present
   120ŌåÆ   120ŌåÆ   120ŌåÆstudy, by contrast, we are looking at chat users who are seeking help from professional chat workers.
   121ŌåÆ   121ŌåÆ   121ŌåÆThe user typically reaches out in distress to someone they do not know and starts the conversation by
   122ŌåÆ   122ŌåÆ   122ŌåÆsharing what is troubling them. The chat worker, on the other side, comes into the conversation with
   123ŌåÆ   123ŌåÆ   123ŌåÆcertain expectations attached to the role: professional ethics, training in how to respond, and a duty to
   124ŌåÆ   124ŌåÆ   124ŌåÆsupport and affirm the user (Borgueta et al., 2018; Stricker, 2018). The user, who is seeking support, is in
   125ŌåÆ   125ŌåÆ   125ŌåÆthis sense the more dependent member of the dyad (Salmon & Young, 2009).
   126ŌåÆ   126ŌåÆ   126ŌåÆ
   127ŌåÆ   127ŌåÆ   127ŌåÆThe counselling literature has long argued that this role difference is consequential for what
   128ŌåÆ   128ŌåÆ   128ŌåÆeach party attends to and contributes. Sullivan (1954) characterised the therapist's role as that of a
   129ŌåÆ   129ŌåÆ   129ŌåÆparticipant observer, attending to the client's behaviour while also participating as a significant other in
   130ŌåÆ   130ŌåÆ   130ŌåÆthe relationship; the client's role, by contrast, is that of a full participant, more self-focused and drawing
   131ŌåÆ   131ŌåÆ   131ŌåÆprimarily on his or her own experience of the exchange (Kivlighan et al., 2014; Sullivan, 1954). This role
   132ŌåÆ   132ŌåÆ   132ŌåÆdifference has two implications for cue reciprocity. First, if paralinguistic cues are subject to display rules
   133ŌåÆ   133ŌåÆ   133ŌåÆ(Stein, 2023), the role-specific norms of professional helping should influence how chat workers use
   134ŌåÆ   134ŌåÆ   134ŌåÆemoticons relative to users (Cavalheiro et al., 2024). Second, in asymmetric dyads, influence may run
   135ŌåÆ   135ŌåÆ   135ŌåÆmore strongly in one direction than the other (Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   136ŌåÆ   136ŌåÆ   136ŌåÆAsymmetric CMC dyads have themselves been studied, but this research has largely measured end-of-
   137ŌåÆ   137ŌåÆ   137ŌåÆinteraction outcomes such as user satisfaction, liking, or perceived competence (Adams et al., 2018;
   138ŌåÆ   138ŌåÆ   138ŌåÆCavalheiro et al., 2024). What has received less attention is the turn-by-turn reciprocity inside the
   139ŌåÆ   139ŌåÆ   139ŌåÆinteraction itself, that is, how each party takes up the other's cues from one message to the next. This
   140ŌåÆ   140ŌåÆ   140ŌåÆgap matters for text-based helping, where emoticons carry affective work that vocal and facial channels
   141ŌåÆ   141ŌåÆ   141ŌåÆwould otherwise do, and where how the two parties take up each other's cues turn by turn is itself part
   142ŌåÆ   142ŌåÆ   142ŌåÆof the helping process. Sequential chat data from a real helpline, of the kind analyzed here, lets us
   143ŌåÆ   143ŌåÆ   143ŌåÆexamine this reciprocity as it unfolds in actual help-seeking conversations.
   144ŌåÆ   144ŌåÆ   144ŌåÆDecomposing reciprocity in a chat workerŌĆōuser dyad with APIM
   145ŌåÆ   145ŌåÆ   145ŌåÆThe actor and partner interdependence model (APIM; Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kenny, 2018;
   146ŌåÆ   146ŌåÆ   146ŌåÆKenny & Ledermann, 2010) offers a way to test this. APIM estimates two effects at once: the actor
   147ŌåÆ   147ŌåÆ   147ŌåÆeffect, the influence of a person's own score on their own outcome, and the partner effect, the influence
   148ŌåÆ   148ŌåÆ   148ŌåÆof the other person's score on that same outcome (Cook & Kenny, 2005). Reciprocity, in APIM terms, is
   149ŌåÆ   149ŌåÆ   149ŌåÆcaptured by the partner effect, and mutual influence is shown when partner effects are present for both
   150ŌåÆ   150ŌåÆ   150ŌåÆmembers of the dyad. In clientŌĆōtherapist dyads, partner effects on relationship quality and session
   151ŌåÆ   151ŌåÆ   151ŌåÆdepth have been found to be stronger for clients than for therapists, consistent with clients being the
   152ŌåÆ   152ŌåÆ   152ŌåÆmore dependent member of the dyad (Jackson et al., 2012; Kivlighan, 2007; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   153ŌåÆ   153ŌåÆ   153ŌåÆWhether the same asymmetry shows up in moment-to-moment emoticon use in text-based help, and in
   154ŌåÆ   154ŌåÆ   154ŌåÆwhich direction it runs, has not been examined.
   155ŌåÆ   155ŌåÆ   155ŌåÆThe present study
   156ŌåÆ   156ŌåÆ   156ŌåÆThe present study examines the following research questions using data from QuickChat, an
   157ŌåÆ   157ŌåÆ   157ŌåÆanonymous text-based youth chatline run by Fei Yue Community Service in Singapore, in which trained
   158ŌåÆ   158ŌåÆ   158ŌåÆchat workers (trained professionals and volunteers) provide brief support to users aged 12 to 25 years
   159ŌåÆ   159ŌåÆ   159ŌåÆ(Chung et al., 2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). More information on QuickChat will be explained in the
   160ŌåÆ   160ŌåÆ   160ŌåÆMethods. The study addresses three research questions, with hypotheses derived from the literature
   161ŌåÆ   161ŌåÆ   161ŌåÆreviewed above.
   162ŌåÆ   162ŌåÆ   162ŌåÆRQ1. Do chat workers and users differ in their use of emoticons? We examine differences in the number
   163ŌåÆ   163ŌåÆ   163ŌåÆof emoticons used per session and the forms of emoticons (e.g., :( or :P). We hypothesized that chat
   164ŌåÆ   164ŌåÆ   164ŌåÆworkers use more emoticons than users in sessions (H1).
   165ŌåÆ   165ŌåÆ   165ŌåÆRQ2. Are there actor and partner effects on emoticon use within sessions, and are these effects
   166ŌåÆ   166ŌåÆ   166ŌåÆsymmetric or asymmetric across roles? Reciprocity is operationalised in two complementary ways (Stein,
   167ŌåÆ   167ŌåÆ   167ŌåÆ2023): a binary indicator of any emoticon in a turn, and a count of how many emoticons were used. We
   168ŌåÆ   168ŌåÆ   168ŌåÆ
   169ŌåÆ   169ŌåÆ   169ŌåÆhypothesized positive partner effects (reciprocity) for both chat workers and users (H2a), and a stronger
   170ŌåÆ   170ŌåÆ   170ŌåÆpartner effect for the user as actor than for the chat worker as actor (H2b).
   171ŌåÆ   171ŌåÆ   171ŌåÆRQ3. Do these actor and partner effects differ across sessions with higher and lower user ratings of the
   172ŌåÆ   172ŌåÆ   172ŌåÆchat worker's responsiveness and overall satisfaction? Two user-rated outcomes, I feel supported and
   173ŌåÆ   173ŌåÆ   173ŌåÆunderstood by my counsellor and I am satisfied with the counselling provided, serve as session-level
   174ŌåÆ   174ŌåÆ   174ŌåÆmoderators. Based on the IPM, which posits that intimacy develops because of perceived
   175ŌåÆ   175ŌåÆ   175ŌåÆresponsiveness arising from reciprocal disclosure and supportive response (Reis, 2017; Reis & Shaver,
   176ŌåÆ   176ŌåÆ   176ŌåÆ2018), we hypothesized that partner effects (reciprocity) are stronger in sessions rated higher on
   177ŌåÆ   177ŌåÆ   177ŌåÆresponsiveness and satisfaction (H3).
   178ŌåÆ   178ŌåÆ   178ŌåÆMethods
   179ŌåÆ   179ŌåÆ   179ŌåÆData and Sample
   180ŌåÆ   180ŌåÆ   180ŌåÆThe data come from eC2.sg, the digital mental health platform of Fei Yue Community Service in
   181ŌåÆ   181ŌåÆ   181ŌåÆSingapore, which operates the QuickChat anonymous text based chatline for youths aged 12 to 25 years.
   182ŌåÆ   182ŌåÆ   182ŌåÆQuickChat provides immediate access, free synchronous chat in which users register with only an email
   183ŌåÆ   183ŌåÆ   183ŌåÆaddress and are assigned to an available worker on duty; the service maintains user anonymity and
   184ŌåÆ   184ŌåÆ   184ŌåÆoperates as a brief intervention and triage platform rather than as long term therapy (Chung et al.,
   185ŌåÆ   185ŌåÆ   185ŌåÆ2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). Sessions last around 30 minutes on average, with the primary objectives
   186ŌåÆ   186ŌåÆ   186ŌåÆbeing immediate emotional support, situation assessment, and referral.
   187ŌåÆ   187ŌåÆ   187ŌåÆThe corpus comprises 1,854 chat sessions from 2016 to 2020. Of these, 1,435 sessions were
   188ŌåÆ   188ŌåÆ   188ŌåÆretained for analysis after restricting to sessions with at least ten messages and complete responses on
   189ŌåÆ   189ŌåÆ   189ŌåÆthe two post-session items used as outcomes (perceived worker responsiveness and session
   190ŌåÆ   190ŌåÆ   190ŌåÆsatisfaction). Each session contains turn by turn text exchanges between a user and a chat helper;
   191ŌåÆ   191ŌåÆ   191ŌåÆhelpers are professionals (social workers and counsellors) or trained volunteers. Approval for use of the
   192ŌåÆ   192ŌåÆ   192ŌåÆdata was obtained from the agency, with service practitioners participating as research team members,
   193ŌåÆ   193ŌåÆ   193ŌåÆand research ethics approval was obtained from [Blinded for Review] University. Because the platform
   194ŌåÆ   194ŌåÆ   194ŌåÆsupports plain text only, users and helpers compose typographic emoticons (e.g., :), :(, :D) rather than
   195ŌåÆ   195ŌåÆ   195ŌåÆpictographic Unicode emojis, so all analyses below concern emoticons.
   196ŌåÆ   196ŌåÆ   196ŌåÆBuilding the lagged turn level dataset
   197ŌåÆ   197ŌåÆ   197ŌåÆResearch question (RQ) 2 and RQ3 both rely on a turn level dataset in which each turn carries
   198ŌåÆ   198ŌåÆ   198ŌåÆinformation about what the worker and user did in their immediately preceding turns. RQ1 does not
   199ŌåÆ   199ŌåÆ   199ŌåÆrequire this lag structure and is answered from the message level data within the corpus.
   200ŌåÆ   200ŌåÆ   200ŌåÆA turn is a maximal run of consecutive messages from the same speaker within a chat. Turn
   201ŌåÆ   201ŌåÆ   201ŌåÆboundaries come from looking at every message (with or without emoticons), based on speaker
   202ŌåÆ   202ŌåÆ   202ŌåÆchanges. With turn boundaries established, the per turn emoticon count is just the sum of the per
   203ŌåÆ   203ŌåÆ   203ŌåÆmessage emoticon counts inside that turn. The dataset also retains the speaker role (worker or user)
   204ŌåÆ   204ŌåÆ   204ŌåÆand the turn sequence within the session. Within each session, turns alternate strictly between user and
   205ŌåÆ   205ŌåÆ   205ŌåÆworker. For any focal turn, the partner's prior turn is the immediately preceding turn (lag 1) and the
   206ŌåÆ   206ŌåÆ   206ŌåÆactor's own prior turn is two turns back (lag 2); turn level lagged predictors are constructed from these
   207ŌåÆ   207ŌåÆ   207ŌåÆtwo prior turns. The final turn level analytic sample contains 86,424 turns, of which 42,945 are user
   208ŌåÆ   208ŌåÆ   208ŌåÆturns and 43,479 are worker turns.
   209ŌåÆ   209ŌåÆ   209ŌåÆ
   210ŌåÆ   210ŌåÆ   210ŌåÆMeasures
   211ŌåÆ   211ŌåÆ   211ŌåÆThis section describes the emoticon predictors (used in RQ1, RQ2, and RQ3) and the session
   212ŌåÆ   212ŌåÆ   212ŌåÆlevel user ratings used as moderators in RQ3.
   213ŌåÆ   213ŌåÆ   213ŌåÆEmoticon identification
   214ŌåÆ   214ŌåÆ   214ŌåÆEmoticons were identified by a locally hosted large language model (Open AIŌĆÖs open sourced
   215ŌåÆ   215ŌåÆ   215ŌåÆgpt-oss:20b model). For each message, the model was given the raw text, the speaker role, and up to
   216ŌåÆ   216ŌåÆ   216ŌåÆtwo preceding messages as context, and identified each emoticon present along with a standardised
   217ŌåÆ   217ŌåÆ   217ŌåÆname. A few-shot prompting approach was used: the prompt included example emoticons drawn from a
   218ŌåÆ   218ŌåÆ   218ŌåÆpublicly available emoticon dictionary (Sakib, 2022) and from a hand inspection of 30 randomly sampled
   219ŌåÆ   219ŌåÆ   219ŌåÆemoticon bearing messages in the present corpus. Messages with multiple emoticons produced one
   220ŌåÆ   220ŌåÆ   220ŌåÆrecord per emoticon. To validate the output, 200 emoticon bearing messages were randomly sampled
   221ŌåÆ   221ŌåÆ   221ŌåÆand annotated by a human coder (undergraduate research assistant); agreement with the LLM labels
   222ŌåÆ   222ŌåÆ   222ŌåÆwas high at 99%.
   223ŌåÆ   223ŌåÆ   223ŌåÆConceptualising emoticon use: binary and count forms
   224ŌåÆ   224ŌåÆ   224ŌåÆFollowing prior work on emoji and emoticon reciprocity (Stein, 2023), emoticon use is
   225ŌåÆ   225ŌåÆ   225ŌåÆoperationalised in two complementary forms throughout RQ2 and RQ3. The binary form records
   226ŌåÆ   226ŌåÆ   226ŌåÆwhether a turn contains any emoticon at all (presence or absence of cue use), and the count form
   227ŌåÆ   227ŌåÆ   227ŌåÆrecords the integer number of emoticons in the turn (intensity of cue use). Each form is also constructed
   228ŌåÆ   228ŌåÆ   228ŌåÆfor the lagged actor and partner predictors, and is used in the corresponding models in RQ2 and RQ3.
   229ŌåÆ   229ŌåÆ   229ŌåÆSession level user ratings (RQ3 moderators)
   230ŌåÆ   230ŌåÆ   230ŌåÆEach session has a post session evaluation completed by the user, comprising seven items rated
   231ŌåÆ   231ŌåÆ   231ŌåÆon a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 3 = Slightly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree). Two items
   232ŌåÆ   232ŌåÆ   232ŌåÆwere used as moderators in RQ3. The first, ŌĆ£I feel supported and understood by my counsellorŌĆØ, was
   233ŌåÆ   233ŌåÆ   233ŌåÆtreated as the user's perceived responsiveness of the worker (Y1). The second, ŌĆ£I am satisfied with the
   234ŌåÆ   234ŌåÆ   234ŌåÆcounselling providedŌĆØ, tapped on overall service satisfaction (Y2). Each rating was grand mean-centered
   235ŌåÆ   235ŌåÆ   235ŌåÆbefore entering the model so that the simple effects of the actor and partner predictors would be
   236ŌåÆ   236ŌåÆ   236ŌåÆinterpretable at the average level of the rating; the centered ratings are denoted Y1 and Y2 below.

   238ŌåÆ   238ŌåÆ   238ŌåÆAnalytical Methods
   239ŌåÆ   239ŌåÆ   239ŌåÆThe three research questions draw on different units of analysis and different modelling
   240ŌåÆ   240ŌåÆ   240ŌåÆstrategies. They are presented in turn.
   241ŌåÆ   241ŌåÆ   241ŌåÆRQ1: Descriptive comparisons of emoticon use by role
   242ŌåÆ   242ŌåÆ   242ŌåÆRQ1 uses the message level data to compare emoticon use between workers and users.
