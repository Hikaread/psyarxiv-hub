     7→Sleep-related out-of-body experiences (OBEs) occupy an ambiguous position between sleep
     8→paralysis and lucid dreaming. OBEs are often considered part of the vestibular-motor component
     9→of sleep paralysis, but they also share features with lucid dreaming, including vivid internally
    10→generated experience, heightened awareness, and perceived agency. Here, we examined whether
    11→the emotional composition of sleep-related OBEs is closer to sleep paralysis, closer to lucid
    12→dreaming, or distinguishable from both.
    13→We analysed 666 participant-by-experience-type emotional reports corresponding to OBEs,
    14→sleep paralysis, and lucid dreams. Emotional variables were coded as binary presence/absence
    15→indicators. The main multivariate analyses focused on reports containing at least one discrete
    16→emotion, yielding 658 reports: 184 OBEs, 210 sleep paralysis reports, and 264 lucid dream reports.
    17→Emotional composition was assessed using Jaccard dissimilarity, non-metric multidimensional
    18→scaling, PERMANOVA, multivariate dispersion, and emotion-by-group association analyses.
    19→Responses indicating absence of emotion were analysed separately.
    20→Emotional composition differed significantly across OBEs, sleep paralysis, and lucid dreams.
    21→Descriptively, the OBE–lucid dream contrast showed the smallest observed pairwise effect size,
    22→whereas contrasts involving sleep paralysis showed larger observed effects. Sleep paralysis was
    23→associated with high-arousal negative emotions, particularly fear, terror, and anxiety, and
    24→showed the most homogeneous emotional profile. Lucid dreams were associated with happiness,
    25→love, hope, and sexual energy. OBEs were distinguished by peace/tranquility and, to a lesser
    26→extent, euphoria. Absence-of-emotion responses were rare and did not account for the
    27→multivariate pattern.
    28→Sleep-related OBEs were not emotionally reducible to sleep paralysis. Their emotional profile
    29→was consistent with greater affective similarity to lucid dreams than to sleep paralysis, while
    30→remaining distinguishable from both. These findings suggest that emotional composition
    31→provides an informative axis for characterizing sleep-related altered states of consciousness.
    32→Keywords: out-of-body experience; sleep paralysis; lucid dreaming; dream emotions; altered
    33→states of consciousness.
