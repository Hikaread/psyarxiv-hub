    21→Abstract:
    22→Research on computer-mediated communication has shown that reciprocal emoticon and emoji
    23→use predicts how warm, responsive, and engaging a text exchange is perceived to be. This work,
    24→however, has been conducted almost entirely in symmetric dyads, such as peers, friends, dating
    25→partners, or colleagues, in which both parties enter the exchange on equal terms. We extend this
    26→literature to a role-asymmetric helping dyad, examining emoticon reciprocity between trained chat
    27→workers and help-seeking young people in a Singapore youth chatline. Drawing on the Interpersonal
    28→Process Model of Intimacy and Communication Accommodation Theory, we analysed 86,424 lagged
    29→turns nested within 1,435 sessions using the Actor–Partner Interdependence Model, operationalising
    30→emoticon use in both binary (presence) and count (intensity) forms. Three findings emerged. First,
    31→emoticons were a routine feature of the channel (87% of sessions), with chat workers using more and a
    32→narrower affirming repertoire than users. Second, positive actor and partner effects were present in
    33→both directions, but the partner effect from chat worker to user was substantially larger than the
    34→reverse, indicating asymmetric reciprocity tracking the role and power asymmetry of the dyad. Third,
    35→partner effects were stronger in sessions rated higher on worker responsiveness and session
    36→satisfaction; actor effects were also moderated by both ratings, though in opposing directions
    37→depending on operationalisation. What matters in text-based help, our results suggest, is not either
    38→party's emoticon use considered alone but the reciprocal exchange between them.
    39→Key words: Emoticons; Emojis; Reciprocity; Communication Accommodation Theory;
    40→Interpersonal Process Model of Intimacy; Text Engagement; Text Chats

    42→Introduction
    43→Paralinguistic cues such as emoticons and emojis have become a stable feature of computer-
    44→mediated communication (CMC), and a growing body of work has shown that reciprocal use of these
    45→cues, rather than use by either party in isolation, predicts how warm, responsive, and engaging a text
    46→exchange is judged to be (Cavalheiro et al., 2024; Coyle & Carmichael, 2019; Huh, 2025; Stein, 2023). The
    47→studies that have directly tested this reciprocity have done so in peer-like dyads: strangers paired for
    48→self-disclosure (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019), dating partners and acquainted interaction partners (Stein,
    49→2023), close friends in informal exchanges (Huh, 2025), and work colleagues (Cavalheiro et al., 2024).
    50→Whether the same reciprocity dynamics carry over to CMC dyads in which the two parties enter the
    51→exchange in clearly different roles, and whether reciprocal emoticon use in those dyads predicts how
    52→the interaction is perceived by the help-seeking party, has not been examined. The present study takes
    53→up this question in a text-only helpline setting, namely an anonymous youth chatline in Singapore in
    54→which a trained chat worker provides brief support to a help-seeking young person. The setting is of
    55→interest in its own right, given young people's growing use of text-based services for mental health
    56→support (Gatti et al., 2016; Navarro et al., 2019; Nesmith, 2018). It also provides a useful case for asking
    57→how emoticon reciprocity operates when the two communicators occupy structurally different
    58→positions, with implications for other CMC dyads marked by role, training, and status asymmetry
    59→Text-based chat as a help-seeking context for young people
    60→Text-based chat (TBC) has become a key modality for delivering mental health support to young
    61→people. Young people aged 10 to 24 years are at high risk for mental health problems yet are among the
    62→least likely to seek professional treatment, citing privacy, stigma, and discomfort with face-to-face
    63→settings (Navarro et al., 2020). For these reasons they increasingly prefer anonymous online chat, which
    64→they experience as less stigmatising, more accessible, and, for those with social anxiety, easier for
    65→organising and expressing thoughts (Gatti et al., 2016; Haner & Pepler, 2016; Navarro et al., 2019;
    66→Nesmith, 2018). Yet, the same features that make TBC appealing (anonymity, text-only interaction,
    67→absence of face-to-face contact) also strip away the nonverbal channels through which warmth and
    68→understanding are conventionally communicated in helping work. Tone of voice, facial expression, and
    69→gesture are unavailable, leaving the chat worker with typed text as the primary means of conveying
    70→support. In this context, Chung & Lim (2025) found that emoticon use was the strongest predictor of
    71→users' perceptions of service usefulness in a Singapore youth chatline, which they interpret as evidence
    72→that emoticons convey affective tone in ways that words alone do not easily replicate in typed text.
    73→If emoticon use by chat workers matters in this way, how chat worker and user emoticon use
    74→influence each other across a session becomes a question worth asking. Yet this question has not been
    75→examined in role-asymmetric helping dyads, that is, dyads such as the one in the present study, in which
    76→a trained chat worker is in role to provide support to a young person seeking help. The two enter the
    77→chat in different positions and with different jobs to do, and the chat worker, unlike the user, is
    78→expected to keep to a professional manner.
    79→Emoticons and reciprocity in CMC
    80→Reciprocity in cue use is consequential for how communication partners perceive one another.
    81→Coyle & Carmichael (2019) found that interlocutors were rated as more responsive when both used
    82→emoji than when only one did so, and Cavalheiro et al. (2024) reported similar results on warmth and
    83→
    84→communication positivity. Stein (2023) showed experimentally that participants used emoji more often
    85→when replying to messages that contained emoji, particularly with closer interlocutors. Huh (2025)
    86→extended these findings, showing that the influence of partner responsiveness, enhanced through emoji
    87→use, extends from face-to-face interactions into digital ones.
    88→Two complementary theoretical accounts make sense of these findings. The first is the
    89→Interpersonal Process Model of Intimacy (IPM; Laurenceau et al., 2004; Laurenceau et al., 1998; Reis,
    90→2017; Reis & Shaver, 2018), which describes intimacy as the outcome of an ongoing transactional cycle:
    91→one person reveals personal feelings or information, the other responds in ways that convey
    92→understanding, validation, and caring, and if the discloser perceives the response as responsive, the
    93→cycle continues, with roles often reversing across the interaction. Face-to-face, alongside the words
    94→spoken, nonverbal cues such as tone, gesture, and facial expression play a key role in conveying this
    95→responsiveness (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019). In CMC, those cues are unavailable, which threatens the
    96→cycle. Social Information Processing theory (Walther, 1992) and its extension, the hyperpersonal model
    97→(Walther & Whitty, 2021), hold that communicators redirect socioemotional expression through
    98→whatever code systems the medium affords, and that reciprocal exchange of these substitute cues can
    99→intensify, rather than dampen, the relational tone of the interaction. Emoticons and emojis, as a form of
   100→paralanguage, are one such substitute code: they fill the gap left by absent vocal and facial cues and
   101→allow affect, on either side of the exchange, to be conveyed in text. Consistent with this, Coyle &
   102→Carmichael (2019), drawing on the IPM, found that when college students and a responder converged in
   103→their use of emoji during text-based self-disclosures, the responder was rated as more responsive, and it
   104→is this perception of responsiveness that keeps the IPM cycle going.
   105→Communication Accommodation Theory (CAT; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007; Giles &
   106→Smith, 1979) provides a wider account of how convergence works as a communicative process. CAT
   107→proposes that interlocutors adjust their communication style to match one another, particularly when
   108→they perceive mutual similarities, want to build affiliation, or seek approval, and that this adjustment is
   109→interpreted by the other party as a signal of empathy and attunement (Giles & Ogay, 2007). CAT further
   110→holds that this whole process is shaped by features of the dyad such as hierarchy, formality, and the
   111→relative status of the two parties (Adams et al., 2018; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007), which raises
   112→the question of how convergence plays out when the two interlocutors do not occupy symmetric
   113→positions.
   114→Role-asymmetric dyads and the helping context
   115→The dyads in which emoticon reciprocity has been directly tested have been peer-like: peers and
   116→strangers paired for self-disclosure (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019), dating partners and acquainted
   117→interaction partners (Stein, 2023), close friends in informal exchanges (Huh, 2025), and work colleagues
   118→(Cavalheiro et al., 2024). In each of these settings, the two parties enter the conversation on broadly
   119→equal terms and can adjust their style toward one another from comparable positions. In the present
   120→study, by contrast, we are looking at chat users who are seeking help from professional chat workers.
   121→The user typically reaches out in distress to someone they do not know and starts the conversation by
   122→sharing what is troubling them. The chat worker, on the other side, comes into the conversation with
   123→certain expectations attached to the role: professional ethics, training in how to respond, and a duty to
   124→support and affirm the user (Borgueta et al., 2018; Stricker, 2018). The user, who is seeking support, is in
   125→this sense the more dependent member of the dyad (Salmon & Young, 2009).
   126→
   127→The counselling literature has long argued that this role difference is consequential for what
   128→each party attends to and contributes. Sullivan (1954) characterised the therapist's role as that of a
   129→participant observer, attending to the client's behaviour while also participating as a significant other in
   130→the relationship; the client's role, by contrast, is that of a full participant, more self-focused and drawing
   131→primarily on his or her own experience of the exchange (Kivlighan et al., 2014; Sullivan, 1954). This role
   132→difference has two implications for cue reciprocity. First, if paralinguistic cues are subject to display rules
   133→(Stein, 2023), the role-specific norms of professional helping should influence how chat workers use
   134→emoticons relative to users (Cavalheiro et al., 2024). Second, in asymmetric dyads, influence may run
   135→more strongly in one direction than the other (Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   136→Asymmetric CMC dyads have themselves been studied, but this research has largely measured end-of-
   137→interaction outcomes such as user satisfaction, liking, or perceived competence (Adams et al., 2018;
   138→Cavalheiro et al., 2024). What has received less attention is the turn-by-turn reciprocity inside the
   139→interaction itself, that is, how each party takes up the other's cues from one message to the next. This
   140→gap matters for text-based helping, where emoticons carry affective work that vocal and facial channels
   141→would otherwise do, and where how the two parties take up each other's cues turn by turn is itself part
   142→of the helping process. Sequential chat data from a real helpline, of the kind analyzed here, lets us
   143→examine this reciprocity as it unfolds in actual help-seeking conversations.
   144→Decomposing reciprocity in a chat worker–user dyad with APIM
   145→The actor and partner interdependence model (APIM; Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kenny, 2018;
   146→Kenny & Ledermann, 2010) offers a way to test this. APIM estimates two effects at once: the actor
   147→effect, the influence of a person's own score on their own outcome, and the partner effect, the influence
   148→of the other person's score on that same outcome (Cook & Kenny, 2005). Reciprocity, in APIM terms, is
   149→captured by the partner effect, and mutual influence is shown when partner effects are present for both
   150→members of the dyad. In client–therapist dyads, partner effects on relationship quality and session
   151→depth have been found to be stronger for clients than for therapists, consistent with clients being the
   152→more dependent member of the dyad (Jackson et al., 2012; Kivlighan, 2007; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   153→Whether the same asymmetry shows up in moment-to-moment emoticon use in text-based help, and in
   154→which direction it runs, has not been examined.
   155→The present study
   156→The present study examines the following research questions using data from QuickChat, an
   157→anonymous text-based youth chatline run by Fei Yue Community Service in Singapore, in which trained
   158→chat workers (trained professionals and volunteers) provide brief support to users aged 12 to 25 years
   159→(Chung et al., 2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). More information on QuickChat will be explained in the
   160→Methods. The study addresses three research questions, with hypotheses derived from the literature
   161→reviewed above.
   162→RQ1. Do chat workers and users differ in their use of emoticons? We examine differences in the number
   163→of emoticons used per session and the forms of emoticons (e.g., :( or :P). We hypothesized that chat
   164→workers use more emoticons than users in sessions (H1).
   165→RQ2. Are there actor and partner effects on emoticon use within sessions, and are these effects
   166→symmetric or asymmetric across roles? Reciprocity is operationalised in two complementary ways (Stein,
   167→2023): a binary indicator of any emoticon in a turn, and a count of how many emoticons were used. We
   168→
   169→hypothesized positive partner effects (reciprocity) for both chat workers and users (H2a), and a stronger
   170→partner effect for the user as actor than for the chat worker as actor (H2b).
   171→RQ3. Do these actor and partner effects differ across sessions with higher and lower user ratings of the
   172→chat worker's responsiveness and overall satisfaction? Two user-rated outcomes, I feel supported and
   173→understood by my counsellor and I am satisfied with the counselling provided, serve as session-level
   174→moderators. Based on the IPM, which posits that intimacy develops because of perceived
   175→responsiveness arising from reciprocal disclosure and supportive response (Reis, 2017; Reis & Shaver,
   176→2018), we hypothesized that partner effects (reciprocity) are stronger in sessions rated higher on
   177→responsiveness and satisfaction (H3).
   178→Methods
   179→Data and Sample
   180→The data come from eC2.sg, the digital mental health platform of Fei Yue Community Service in
   181→Singapore, which operates the QuickChat anonymous text based chatline for youths aged 12 to 25 years.
   182→QuickChat provides immediate access, free synchronous chat in which users register with only an email
   183→address and are assigned to an available worker on duty; the service maintains user anonymity and
   184→operates as a brief intervention and triage platform rather than as long term therapy (Chung et al.,
   185→2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). Sessions last around 30 minutes on average, with the primary objectives
   186→being immediate emotional support, situation assessment, and referral.
   187→The corpus comprises 1,854 chat sessions from 2016 to 2020. Of these, 1,435 sessions were
   188→retained for analysis after restricting to sessions with at least ten messages and complete responses on
   189→the two post-session items used as outcomes (perceived worker responsiveness and session
   190→satisfaction). Each session contains turn by turn text exchanges between a user and a chat helper;
   191→helpers are professionals (social workers and counsellors) or trained volunteers. Approval for use of the
   192→data was obtained from the agency, with service practitioners participating as research team members,
   193→and research ethics approval was obtained from [Blinded for Review] University. Because the platform
   194→supports plain text only, users and helpers compose typographic emoticons (e.g., :), :(, :D) rather than
   195→pictographic Unicode emojis, so all analyses below concern emoticons.
   196→Building the lagged turn level dataset
   197→Research question (RQ) 2 and RQ3 both rely on a turn level dataset in which each turn carries
   198→information about what the worker and user did in their immediately preceding turns. RQ1 does not
   199→require this lag structure and is answered from the message level data within the corpus.
   200→A turn is a maximal run of consecutive messages from the same speaker within a chat. Turn
   201→boundaries come from looking at every message (with or without emoticons), based on speaker
   202→changes. With turn boundaries established, the per turn emoticon count is just the sum of the per
   203→message emoticon counts inside that turn. The dataset also retains the speaker role (worker or user)
   204→and the turn sequence within the session. Within each session, turns alternate strictly between user and
   205→worker. For any focal turn, the partner's prior turn is the immediately preceding turn (lag 1) and the
   206→actor's own prior turn is two turns back (lag 2); turn level lagged predictors are constructed from these
   207→two prior turns. The final turn level analytic sample contains 86,424 turns, of which 42,945 are user
   208→turns and 43,479 are worker turns.

   210→Measures
   211→This section describes the emoticon predictors (used in RQ1, RQ2, and RQ3) and the session
   212→level user ratings used as moderators in RQ3.
   213→Emoticon identification
   214→Emoticons were identified by a locally hosted large language model (Open AI’s open sourced
   215→gpt-oss:20b model). For each message, the model was given the raw text, the speaker role, and up to
   216→two preceding messages as context, and identified each emoticon present along with a standardised
   217→name. A few-shot prompting approach was used: the prompt included example emoticons drawn from a
   218→publicly available emoticon dictionary (Sakib, 2022) and from a hand inspection of 30 randomly sampled
   219→emoticon bearing messages in the present corpus. Messages with multiple emoticons produced one
   220→record per emoticon. To validate the output, 200 emoticon bearing messages were randomly sampled
   221→and annotated by a human coder (undergraduate research assistant); agreement with the LLM labels
   222→was high at 99%.
   223→Conceptualising emoticon use: binary and count forms
   224→Following prior work on emoji and emoticon reciprocity (Stein, 2023), emoticon use is
   225→operationalised in two complementary forms throughout RQ2 and RQ3. The binary form records
   226→whether a turn contains any emoticon at all (presence or absence of cue use), and the count form
   227→records the integer number of emoticons in the turn (intensity of cue use). Each form is also constructed
   228→for the lagged actor and partner predictors, and is used in the corresponding models in RQ2 and RQ3.
   229→Session level user ratings (RQ3 moderators)
   230→Each session has a post session evaluation completed by the user, comprising seven items rated
   231→on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 3 = Slightly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree). Two items
   232→were used as moderators in RQ3. The first, “I feel supported and understood by my counsellor”, was
   233→treated as the user's perceived responsiveness of the worker (Y1). The second, “I am satisfied with the
   234→counselling provided”, tapped on overall service satisfaction (Y2). Each rating was grand mean-centered
   235→before entering the model so that the simple effects of the actor and partner predictors would be
   236→interpretable at the average level of the rating; the centered ratings are denoted Y1 and Y2 below.

   240→strategies. They are presented in turn.

   242→RQ1 uses the message level data to compare emoticon use between workers and users.
   243→Descriptive statistics are reported as frequencies and percentages within each role. Differences between
   244→workers and users are tested with Welch's two sample t test.

   312→Results
   313→RQ1: Differences in worker and user emoticon use
   314→RQ1 examines whether workers and users differ in emoticon use along two dimensions: volume
   315→and the forms used. Analyses drew on 1,435 sessions, comprising 179,267 messages, of which 9,811
   316→(5.5%) contained at least one emoticon. Emoticon use was common across sessions: 87.0% (n = 1,249) of
   317→sessions had at least one emoticon. Workers sent more of these messages (n = 6,998, 71.3%) than users
   318→(n = 2,813, 28.7%). See Table 1.
   319→[Table 1 here]
   320→Number of Emoticons Per Session
   321→Workers sent more emoticons per session than users (worker M = 4.98, SD = 5.66; user M =
   322→2.13, SD = 6.85; Table C2). A Welch two sample t test confirmed this difference (t(2769.8) = 12.18, p <
   323→.001, 95% CI [2.40, 3.32]).
   324→Forms of emoticons used
   325→Worker emoticon use was concentrated on two forms: smiley :) (74.2%, n = 5,307) and
   326→frown/sad/pouting :( (18.4%, n = 1,313). The next most frequent worker forms, :D (laughing, big grin or
   327→laugh with glasses; 2.5%, n = 181) and :| (straight face; 1.9%, n = 139), each contributed less than 3%.
   328→
   329→Users drew on a wider set covering a broader affective range: :) (31.5%, n = 960), :( (30.9%, n = 944), :'(
   330→(crying; 15.6%, n = 475), :D (4.6%, n = 139), and :/ (skeptical, annoyed or uneasy; 4.0%, n = 122).
   331→The RQ1 analyses are consistent with H1. Workers used emoticons more frequently than users
   332→(volume) and concentrated on two affirming forms dominated by :) (forms used), whereas users drew
   333→on a wider, more affectively varied repertoire.
   334→RQ2: Actor and partner effects on emoticon use within sessions
   335→RQ2 asks whether emoticons in the actor's own prior turn (actor effect) and in the partner's
   336→prior turn (partner effect) predict the actor's current emoticon use, and whether these effects are
   337→symmetric or asymmetric between workers and users. The analytic sample comprised 86,424 turns
   338→nested within 1,435 sessions and 926 unique authors.
   339→Modelling approach
   340→As described in the Methods, two operationalisations of emoticon use were modelled in
   341→parallel: a binary indicator (presence vs absence) and a count (intensity). For each, a pooled model
   342→(Model 1) and a role moderated model (Model 2) were fitted, with crossed random intercepts for

   426→Discussion
   427→How emoticons are reciprocated in CMC matters because, with vocal and facial channels
   428→stripped away, emoticons carry the affective work that those channels would otherwise do. Reciprocity
   429→in emoticon use has been documented in symmetric dyads of peers, friends, and partners, but text-
   430→based youth chatlines present a distinct communication context: the two parties enter the exchange in a
   431→helping relationship, with a trained chat worker on one side and a young person seeking help on the
   432→other. The present study examined emoticon reciprocity in this setting using APIM applied to lagged
   433→turn-level data from a Singapore text chatline for young person seeking help. We found that emoticon
   434→use was routine, that chat workers and chat users differed substantially in how much and how variedly
   435→they used emoticons, that actor and partner effects were present in both directions but stronger from
   436→chat worker to chat user than the reverse, and that the partner effect (i.e., reciprocity) was stronger in
   437→sessions rated higher on chat worker responsiveness and overall satisfaction".
   438→Emoticons as a routine feature of text-based help (RQ1)
   439→Almost nine in ten sessions contained at least one emoticon. In a service where the only
   440→available channel is typed text, this rate of use suggests that emoticons are commonly used to
   441→communicate affect in this setting. This pattern aligns with the predictions of Social Information
   442→Processing theory and the Hyperpersonal model (Walther, 1992; Walther & Whitty, 2021), which hold
   443→
   444→that communicators redirect socioemotional expression through whatever cues the medium affords
   445→when richer vocal and facial channels are unavailable. The rate is also consistent with prior CMC
   446→research documenting widespread emoticon and emoji use in everyday text-based exchanges
   447→(Cavalheiro et al., 2024; Stein, 2023), and the present results extend that pattern to a helping context.
   448→Chat workers sent considerably more emoticons per session than chat users, and the great
   449→majority of emoticon-bearing messages came from chat workers. This volume gap is unlikely to appear
   450→in a friend-to-friend exchange of the kind studied by Stein (2023) or Cavalheiro et al. (2024), where
   451→neither party is bound by a professional role. It is consistent with the role asymmetry argued in the
   452→Introduction: chat workers carry a responsibility to support, affirm, and elicit, and in a channel stripped
   453→of vocal and facial cues, emoticons are one of the few textual resources available for doing that work
   454→(Chung & Lim, 2025). Chat workers' repertoire was also narrower than chat users', concentrated on a
   455→small number of affirming forms, whereas chat users drew on a wider affective range. Chat workers, in
   456→other words, do more of the cue work but in a more restricted set of forms, which we return to as a
   457→limitation below.
   458→Reciprocity in a role-asymmetric dyad (RQ2)
   459→That chat workers do more of the cue work descriptively raises the question of how the two
   460→parties' cues influence one another turn by turn. The pooled APIM models returned a positive actor
   461→effect and a positive partner effect in both the binary and count operationalisations, supporting H2a:
   462→emoticon use in this dyad is reciprocal. This places the chat worker–user dyad in the same broad pattern
   463→as the symmetric dyads studied by Coyle & Carmichael (2019), Stein (2023), Cavalheiro et al. (2024), and
   464→Huh (2025), and offers the first APIM-level evidence we are aware of that the reciprocity documented in
   465→those settings carries over into a role-asymmetric helping context. The actor effects, present alongside
   466→the partner effects, are best read as turn-to-turn consistency: each party tends to carry their own cue
   467→use forward from one turn to the next, independent of what the partner does. This is a feature of how
   468→individuals remain consistent in their own cue use within a session, rather than a finding about
   469→reciprocity.
   470→Reciprocity, then, is present in this dyad. But it is not symmetric. The partner effect from chat
   471→worker to chat user was substantially larger than that from chat user to chat worker, in both
   472→operationalisations, supporting H2b. We read this finding through both theoretical accounts in the
   473→Introduction, with the helping context giving each a specific role. From the IPM perspective, the chat
   474→worker is positioned as the party who initiates and sustains the responsiveness cycle on the chat user's
   475→behalf. The IPM holds that intimacy develops when one party communicates personally relevant feelings
   476→and the other responds in ways that convey understanding, validation, and caring, and that emotional
   477→disclosures, rather than factual ones, are the disclosures most strongly tied to the experience of
   478→intimacy (Laurenceau et al., 2004). The medium itself supports this, since anonymity and reduced cues in
   479→in text-based exchange can make people more willing to disclose and feel more intimate than they
   480→would face-to-face (Walther, 1996). In a chatline session, the chat user enters the exchange already in a
   481→disclosing position, often with content that is affectively loaded. According to the IPM, the chat worker
   482→is trained to respond in a way that the chat user perceives as understanding and caring, and emoticons
   483→are one of the few textual resources available to do so once vocal and facial channels are absent. A chat
   484→worker's :) placed alongside a difficult message can soften, validate, and invite further disclosure.
   485→Because perceived partner responsiveness mediates the link between disclosure and intimacy
   486→
   487→(Laurenceau et al., 1998, 2004), the chat worker's emoticon use is how the chat user comes to sense the
   488→chat worker's support and understanding.
   489→CAT addresses the complementary question: why the chat user's cue use moves with the chat
   490→worker's rather than the reverse. CAT holds that interlocutors converge on each other's communicative
   491→style as a way of signalling affiliation, seeking approval, and reducing uncertainty, and that this
   492→convergence is modulated by hierarchy, formality, and the relative status of the two parties (Adams et
   493→al., 2018; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007). All three modulators are present in the chatline dyad.
   494→The chat worker is trained, in role, and holds institutional standing as the helping professional; the chat
   495→user has initiated the encounter from a position of seeking support and has comparatively less
   496→situational power. Adams et al. (2018) found in a controlled experiment that the lower-power party in a
   497→text exchange used more textisms after viewing many textisms from the higher-power partner, and
   498→Stein (2023) showed that participants used more emojis when replying to messages that contained
   499→emojis, with this reciprocation strongest when interaction partners were closer or held more standing.
   500→The chat worker → chat user partner effect estimated here is what these findings would predict for the
   501→chatline, now observed in a real helping service: chat users accommodate to the chat worker's emoticon
   502→use more than the chat worker accommodates to theirs, and the asymmetry tracks the power and role
   503→asymmetry of the dyad.
   504→The chat user → chat worker partner effect, although smaller, was still positive: chat workers
   505→did adjust their emoticon use in response to chat users' prior turns. We read this as the chat worker
   506→reciprocating enough to sustain the responsiveness cycle. If the chat worker did not reciprocate at all,
   507→the cycle would not sustain itself, since responsiveness on one side alone does not produce the
   508→perceived mutuality on which intimacy depends. The cycle is led but not one-sided, and this two-way
   509→movement appears to be part of how the helping work proceeds in a text helpline.
   510→Reciprocity and how chat users experience the session (RQ3)
   511→If this asymmetric reciprocity is the communicative shape of the helping dyad, the next question
   512→is whether it matters for how chat users experience the session. The RQ3 analyses indicate that it does.
   513→The partner effect was stronger in sessions where chat users rated the chat worker as more responsive
   514→(Y1) and where users reported greater satisfaction with the chat sessions (Y2), across both
   515→operationalisations and both moderators, supporting H3. The IPM says that responsiveness builds when
   516→both parties take up each other's cues — emoticons, in this setting — and that this reciprocal exchange
   517→is what produces the perception of being understood and cared for (Laurenceau et al., 2004; Laurenceau
   518→et al., 1998; Reis, 2017). Sessions in which the emoticon exchange between chat worker and chat user
   519→moved together most closely were also the sessions in which chat users later rated the chat worker as
   520→more responsive and reported greater satisfaction with the service.
   521→Taken with findings for RQ2, it is not chat workers' emoticon use on its own that predicts chat
   522→users' experience of the service, but the reciprocal uptake of emoticons by chat users, and the chat
   523→worker's reciprocal uptake of chat users' emoticons in return. The chatline operates well when the
   524→emoticon exchange flows in both directions, and the user-rating data suggest that chat users themselves
   525→perceive the difference between sessions in which it does and those in which it does not."
   526→Limitations and scope
   527→
   528→Three limitations bound the present findings. First, we measured emoticons as presence and
   529→count, not as meaning. The same form can serve different functions, including expressing sentiment,
   530→adjusting tone, or signalling irony (Hu et al., 2017), and the sender's intended meaning may not match
   531→how the partner reads it. A smiley face may convey warmth in one turn and sarcasm in another. The
   532→partner effects here aggregate over these possibilities, and future work linking specific cues to their
   533→intended and perceived meaning would give a clearer picture of what is being reciprocated.
   534→Second, the design is observational. The lagged turn structure of APIM gives the predictor
   535→temporal precedence over the outcome, which rules out reverse causation at the turn level, but the
   536→partner effects estimated here remain associations rather than causal estimates.Third, the corpus is
   537→limited to typographic emoticons, not pictographic Unicode emojis. The QuickChat interface supports
   538→typed text only, so chat users and chat workers compose forms such as :) and :( from keyboard
   539→characters, and the richer inventory of pictographic emojis is not part of the channel under study. How
   540→far the present findings transfer to services that support full emoji input, or to other cultural and clinical
   541→settings, is an open question.
   542→Within those bounds, the present findings extend the CMC literature on emoticon reciprocity
   543→from peer-like dyads into a role-asymmetric helping setting, and they suggest that what matters in text-
   544→based help is not either party's emoticon use considered alone but the reciprocal exchange between the
   545→two. The chat worker–user pairing is one instance of a broader group of dyads in which the two parties
   546→occupy structurally different positions, and the asymmetric reciprocity observed here may be a useful
   547→starting point for examining cue uptake in other role-differentiated text-based exchanges.
