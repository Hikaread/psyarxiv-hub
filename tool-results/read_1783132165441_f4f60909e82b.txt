    21тЖТ    21тЖТAbstract:
    22тЖТ    22тЖТResearch on computer-mediated communication has shown that reciprocal emoticon and emoji
    23тЖТ    23тЖТuse predicts how warm, responsive, and engaging a text exchange is perceived to be. This work,
    24тЖТ    24тЖТhowever, has been conducted almost entirely in symmetric dyads, such as peers, friends, dating
    25тЖТ    25тЖТpartners, or colleagues, in which both parties enter the exchange on equal terms. We extend this
    26тЖТ    26тЖТliterature to a role-asymmetric helping dyad, examining emoticon reciprocity between trained chat
    27тЖТ    27тЖТworkers and help-seeking young people in a Singapore youth chatline. Drawing on the Interpersonal
    28тЖТ    28тЖТProcess Model of Intimacy and Communication Accommodation Theory, we analysed 86,424 lagged
    29тЖТ    29тЖТturns nested within 1,435 sessions using the ActorтАУPartner Interdependence Model, operationalising
    30тЖТ    30тЖТemoticon use in both binary (presence) and count (intensity) forms. Three findings emerged. First,
    31тЖТ    31тЖТemoticons were a routine feature of the channel (87% of sessions), with chat workers using more and a
    32тЖТ    32тЖТnarrower affirming repertoire than users. Second, positive actor and partner effects were present in
    33тЖТ    33тЖТboth directions, but the partner effect from chat worker to user was substantially larger than the
    34тЖТ    34тЖТreverse, indicating asymmetric reciprocity tracking the role and power asymmetry of the dyad. Third,
    35тЖТ    35тЖТpartner effects were stronger in sessions rated higher on worker responsiveness and session
    36тЖТ    36тЖТsatisfaction; actor effects were also moderated by both ratings, though in opposing directions
    37тЖТ    37тЖТdepending on operationalisation. What matters in text-based help, our results suggest, is not either
    38тЖТ    38тЖТparty's emoticon use considered alone but the reciprocal exchange between them.
    39тЖТ    39тЖТKey words: Emoticons; Emojis; Reciprocity; Communication Accommodation Theory;
    40тЖТ    40тЖТInterpersonal Process Model of Intimacy; Text Engagement; Text Chats

    42тЖТ    42тЖТIntroduction
    43тЖТ    43тЖТParalinguistic cues such as emoticons and emojis have become a stable feature of computer-
    44тЖТ    44тЖТmediated communication (CMC), and a growing body of work has shown that reciprocal use of these
    45тЖТ    45тЖТcues, rather than use by either party in isolation, predicts how warm, responsive, and engaging a text
    46тЖТ    46тЖТexchange is judged to be (Cavalheiro et al., 2024; Coyle & Carmichael, 2019; Huh, 2025; Stein, 2023). The
    47тЖТ    47тЖТstudies that have directly tested this reciprocity have done so in peer-like dyads: strangers paired for
    48тЖТ    48тЖТself-disclosure (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019), dating partners and acquainted interaction partners (Stein,
    49тЖТ    49тЖТ2023), close friends in informal exchanges (Huh, 2025), and work colleagues (Cavalheiro et al., 2024).
    50тЖТ    50тЖТWhether the same reciprocity dynamics carry over to CMC dyads in which the two parties enter the
    51тЖТ    51тЖТexchange in clearly different roles, and whether reciprocal emoticon use in those dyads predicts how
    52тЖТ    52тЖТthe interaction is perceived by the help-seeking party, has not been examined. The present study takes
    53тЖТ    53тЖТup this question in a text-only helpline setting, namely an anonymous youth chatline in Singapore in
    54тЖТ    54тЖТwhich a trained chat worker provides brief support to a help-seeking young person. The setting is of
    55тЖТ    55тЖТinterest in its own right, given young people's growing use of text-based services for mental health
    56тЖТ    56тЖТsupport (Gatti et al., 2016; Navarro et al., 2019; Nesmith, 2018). It also provides a useful case for asking
    57тЖТ    57тЖТhow emoticon reciprocity operates when the two communicators occupy structurally different
    58тЖТ    58тЖТpositions, with implications for other CMC dyads marked by role, training, and status asymmetry
    59тЖТ    59тЖТText-based chat as a help-seeking context for young people
    60тЖТ    60тЖТText-based chat (TBC) has become a key modality for delivering mental health support to young
    61тЖТ    61тЖТpeople. Young people aged 10 to 24 years are at high risk for mental health problems yet are among the
    62тЖТ    62тЖТleast likely to seek professional treatment, citing privacy, stigma, and discomfort with face-to-face
    63тЖТ    63тЖТsettings (Navarro et al., 2020). For these reasons they increasingly prefer anonymous online chat, which
    64тЖТ    64тЖТthey experience as less stigmatising, more accessible, and, for those with social anxiety, easier for
    65тЖТ    65тЖТorganising and expressing thoughts (Gatti et al., 2016; Haner & Pepler, 2016; Navarro et al., 2019;
    66тЖТ    66тЖТNesmith, 2018). Yet, the same features that make TBC appealing (anonymity, text-only interaction,
    67тЖТ    67тЖТabsence of face-to-face contact) also strip away the nonverbal channels through which warmth and
    68тЖТ    68тЖТunderstanding are conventionally communicated in helping work. Tone of voice, facial expression, and
    69тЖТ    69тЖТgesture are unavailable, leaving the chat worker with typed text as the primary means of conveying
    70тЖТ    70тЖТsupport. In this context, Chung & Lim (2025) found that emoticon use was the strongest predictor of
    71тЖТ    71тЖТusers' perceptions of service usefulness in a Singapore youth chatline, which they interpret as evidence
    72тЖТ    72тЖТthat emoticons convey affective tone in ways that words alone do not easily replicate in typed text.
    73тЖТ    73тЖТIf emoticon use by chat workers matters in this way, how chat worker and user emoticon use
    74тЖТ    74тЖТinfluence each other across a session becomes a question worth asking. Yet this question has not been
    75тЖТ    75тЖТexamined in role-asymmetric helping dyads, that is, dyads such as the one in the present study, in which
    76тЖТ    76тЖТa trained chat worker is in role to provide support to a young person seeking help. The two enter the
    77тЖТ    77тЖТchat in different positions and with different jobs to do, and the chat worker, unlike the user, is
    78тЖТ    78тЖТexpected to keep to a professional manner.
    79тЖТ    79тЖТEmoticons and reciprocity in CMC
    80тЖТ    80тЖТReciprocity in cue use is consequential for how communication partners perceive one another.
    81тЖТ    81тЖТCoyle & Carmichael (2019) found that interlocutors were rated as more responsive when both used
    82тЖТ    82тЖТemoji than when only one did so, and Cavalheiro et al. (2024) reported similar results on warmth and
    83тЖТ    83тЖТ
    84тЖТ    84тЖТcommunication positivity. Stein (2023) showed experimentally that participants used emoji more often
    85тЖТ    85тЖТwhen replying to messages that contained emoji, particularly with closer interlocutors. Huh (2025)
    86тЖТ    86тЖТextended these findings, showing that the influence of partner responsiveness, enhanced through emoji
    87тЖТ    87тЖТuse, extends from face-to-face interactions into digital ones.
    88тЖТ    88тЖТTwo complementary theoretical accounts make sense of these findings. The first is the
    89тЖТ    89тЖТInterpersonal Process Model of Intimacy (IPM; Laurenceau et al., 2004; Laurenceau et al., 1998; Reis,
    90тЖТ    90тЖТ2017; Reis & Shaver, 2018), which describes intimacy as the outcome of an ongoing transactional cycle:
    91тЖТ    91тЖТone person reveals personal feelings or information, the other responds in ways that convey
    92тЖТ    92тЖТunderstanding, validation, and caring, and if the discloser perceives the response as responsive, the
    93тЖТ    93тЖТcycle continues, with roles often reversing across the interaction. Face-to-face, alongside the words
    94тЖТ    94тЖТspoken, nonverbal cues such as tone, gesture, and facial expression play a key role in conveying this
    95тЖТ    95тЖТresponsiveness (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019). In CMC, those cues are unavailable, which threatens the
    96тЖТ    96тЖТcycle. Social Information Processing theory (Walther, 1992) and its extension, the hyperpersonal model
    97тЖТ    97тЖТ(Walther & Whitty, 2021), hold that communicators redirect socioemotional expression through
    98тЖТ    98тЖТwhatever code systems the medium affords, and that reciprocal exchange of these substitute cues can
    99тЖТ    99тЖТintensify, rather than dampen, the relational tone of the interaction. Emoticons and emojis, as a form of
   100тЖТ   100тЖТparalanguage, are one such substitute code: they fill the gap left by absent vocal and facial cues and
   101тЖТ   101тЖТallow affect, on either side of the exchange, to be conveyed in text. Consistent with this, Coyle &
   102тЖТ   102тЖТCarmichael (2019), drawing on the IPM, found that when college students and a responder converged in
   103тЖТ   103тЖТtheir use of emoji during text-based self-disclosures, the responder was rated as more responsive, and it
   104тЖТ   104тЖТis this perception of responsiveness that keeps the IPM cycle going.
   105тЖТ   105тЖТCommunication Accommodation Theory (CAT; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007; Giles &
   106тЖТ   106тЖТSmith, 1979) provides a wider account of how convergence works as a communicative process. CAT
   107тЖТ   107тЖТproposes that interlocutors adjust their communication style to match one another, particularly when
   108тЖТ   108тЖТthey perceive mutual similarities, want to build affiliation, or seek approval, and that this adjustment is
   109тЖТ   109тЖТinterpreted by the other party as a signal of empathy and attunement (Giles & Ogay, 2007). CAT further
   110тЖТ   110тЖТholds that this whole process is shaped by features of the dyad such as hierarchy, formality, and the
   111тЖТ   111тЖТrelative status of the two parties (Adams et al., 2018; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007), which raises
   112тЖТ   112тЖТthe question of how convergence plays out when the two interlocutors do not occupy symmetric
   113тЖТ   113тЖТpositions.
   114тЖТ   114тЖТRole-asymmetric dyads and the helping context
   115тЖТ   115тЖТThe dyads in which emoticon reciprocity has been directly tested have been peer-like: peers and
   116тЖТ   116тЖТstrangers paired for self-disclosure (Coyle & Carmichael, 2019), dating partners and acquainted
   117тЖТ   117тЖТinteraction partners (Stein, 2023), close friends in informal exchanges (Huh, 2025), and work colleagues
   118тЖТ   118тЖТ(Cavalheiro et al., 2024). In each of these settings, the two parties enter the conversation on broadly
   119тЖТ   119тЖТequal terms and can adjust their style toward one another from comparable positions. In the present
   120тЖТ   120тЖТstudy, by contrast, we are looking at chat users who are seeking help from professional chat workers.
   121тЖТ   121тЖТThe user typically reaches out in distress to someone they do not know and starts the conversation by
   122тЖТ   122тЖТsharing what is troubling them. The chat worker, on the other side, comes into the conversation with
   123тЖТ   123тЖТcertain expectations attached to the role: professional ethics, training in how to respond, and a duty to
   124тЖТ   124тЖТsupport and affirm the user (Borgueta et al., 2018; Stricker, 2018). The user, who is seeking support, is in
   125тЖТ   125тЖТthis sense the more dependent member of the dyad (Salmon & Young, 2009).
   126тЖТ   126тЖТ
   127тЖТ   127тЖТThe counselling literature has long argued that this role difference is consequential for what
   128тЖТ   128тЖТeach party attends to and contributes. Sullivan (1954) characterised the therapist's role as that of a
   129тЖТ   129тЖТparticipant observer, attending to the client's behaviour while also participating as a significant other in
   130тЖТ   130тЖТthe relationship; the client's role, by contrast, is that of a full participant, more self-focused and drawing
   131тЖТ   131тЖТprimarily on his or her own experience of the exchange (Kivlighan et al., 2014; Sullivan, 1954). This role
   132тЖТ   132тЖТdifference has two implications for cue reciprocity. First, if paralinguistic cues are subject to display rules
   133тЖТ   133тЖТ(Stein, 2023), the role-specific norms of professional helping should influence how chat workers use
   134тЖТ   134тЖТemoticons relative to users (Cavalheiro et al., 2024). Second, in asymmetric dyads, influence may run
   135тЖТ   135тЖТmore strongly in one direction than the other (Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   136тЖТ   136тЖТAsymmetric CMC dyads have themselves been studied, but this research has largely measured end-of-
   137тЖТ   137тЖТinteraction outcomes such as user satisfaction, liking, or perceived competence (Adams et al., 2018;
   138тЖТ   138тЖТCavalheiro et al., 2024). What has received less attention is the turn-by-turn reciprocity inside the
   139тЖТ   139тЖТinteraction itself, that is, how each party takes up the other's cues from one message to the next. This
   140тЖТ   140тЖТgap matters for text-based helping, where emoticons carry affective work that vocal and facial channels
   141тЖТ   141тЖТwould otherwise do, and where how the two parties take up each other's cues turn by turn is itself part
   142тЖТ   142тЖТof the helping process. Sequential chat data from a real helpline, of the kind analyzed here, lets us
   143тЖТ   143тЖТexamine this reciprocity as it unfolds in actual help-seeking conversations.
   144тЖТ   144тЖТDecomposing reciprocity in a chat workerтАУuser dyad with APIM
   145тЖТ   145тЖТThe actor and partner interdependence model (APIM; Campbell & Stanton, 2015; Kenny, 2018;
   146тЖТ   146тЖТKenny & Ledermann, 2010) offers a way to test this. APIM estimates two effects at once: the actor
   147тЖТ   147тЖТeffect, the influence of a person's own score on their own outcome, and the partner effect, the influence
   148тЖТ   148тЖТof the other person's score on that same outcome (Cook & Kenny, 2005). Reciprocity, in APIM terms, is
   149тЖТ   149тЖТcaptured by the partner effect, and mutual influence is shown when partner effects are present for both
   150тЖТ   150тЖТmembers of the dyad. In clientтАУtherapist dyads, partner effects on relationship quality and session
   151тЖТ   151тЖТdepth have been found to be stronger for clients than for therapists, consistent with clients being the
   152тЖТ   152тЖТmore dependent member of the dyad (Jackson et al., 2012; Kivlighan, 2007; Kivlighan et al., 2014).
   153тЖТ   153тЖТWhether the same asymmetry shows up in moment-to-moment emoticon use in text-based help, and in
   154тЖТ   154тЖТwhich direction it runs, has not been examined.
   155тЖТ   155тЖТThe present study
   156тЖТ   156тЖТThe present study examines the following research questions using data from QuickChat, an
   157тЖТ   157тЖТanonymous text-based youth chatline run by Fei Yue Community Service in Singapore, in which trained
   158тЖТ   158тЖТchat workers (trained professionals and volunteers) provide brief support to users aged 12 to 25 years
   159тЖТ   159тЖТ(Chung et al., 2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). More information on QuickChat will be explained in the
   160тЖТ   160тЖТMethods. The study addresses three research questions, with hypotheses derived from the literature
   161тЖТ   161тЖТreviewed above.
   162тЖТ   162тЖТRQ1. Do chat workers and users differ in their use of emoticons? We examine differences in the number
   163тЖТ   163тЖТof emoticons used per session and the forms of emoticons (e.g., :( or :P). We hypothesized that chat
   164тЖТ   164тЖТworkers use more emoticons than users in sessions (H1).
   165тЖТ   165тЖТRQ2. Are there actor and partner effects on emoticon use within sessions, and are these effects
   166тЖТ   166тЖТsymmetric or asymmetric across roles? Reciprocity is operationalised in two complementary ways (Stein,
   167тЖТ   167тЖТ2023): a binary indicator of any emoticon in a turn, and a count of how many emoticons were used. We
   168тЖТ   168тЖТ
   169тЖТ   169тЖТhypothesized positive partner effects (reciprocity) for both chat workers and users (H2a), and a stronger
   170тЖТ   170тЖТpartner effect for the user as actor than for the chat worker as actor (H2b).
   171тЖТ   171тЖТRQ3. Do these actor and partner effects differ across sessions with higher and lower user ratings of the
   172тЖТ   172тЖТchat worker's responsiveness and overall satisfaction? Two user-rated outcomes, I feel supported and
   173тЖТ   173тЖТunderstood by my counsellor and I am satisfied with the counselling provided, serve as session-level
   174тЖТ   174тЖТmoderators. Based on the IPM, which posits that intimacy develops because of perceived
   175тЖТ   175тЖТresponsiveness arising from reciprocal disclosure and supportive response (Reis, 2017; Reis & Shaver,
   176тЖТ   176тЖТ2018), we hypothesized that partner effects (reciprocity) are stronger in sessions rated higher on
   177тЖТ   177тЖТresponsiveness and satisfaction (H3).
   178тЖТ   178тЖТMethods
   179тЖТ   179тЖТData and Sample
   180тЖТ   180тЖТThe data come from eC2.sg, the digital mental health platform of Fei Yue Community Service in
   181тЖТ   181тЖТSingapore, which operates the QuickChat anonymous text based chatline for youths aged 12 to 25 years.
   182тЖТ   182тЖТQuickChat provides immediate access, free synchronous chat in which users register with only an email
   183тЖТ   183тЖТaddress and are assigned to an available worker on duty; the service maintains user anonymity and
   184тЖТ   184тЖТoperates as a brief intervention and triage platform rather than as long term therapy (Chung et al.,
   185тЖТ   185тЖТ2026; Chung & Lim, 2025). Sessions last around 30 minutes on average, with the primary objectives
   186тЖТ   186тЖТbeing immediate emotional support, situation assessment, and referral.
   187тЖТ   187тЖТThe corpus comprises 1,854 chat sessions from 2016 to 2020. Of these, 1,435 sessions were
   188тЖТ   188тЖТretained for analysis after restricting to sessions with at least ten messages and complete responses on
   189тЖТ   189тЖТthe two post-session items used as outcomes (perceived worker responsiveness and session
   190тЖТ   190тЖТsatisfaction). Each session contains turn by turn text exchanges between a user and a chat helper;
   191тЖТ   191тЖТhelpers are professionals (social workers and counsellors) or trained volunteers. Approval for use of the
   192тЖТ   192тЖТdata was obtained from the agency, with service practitioners participating as research team members,
   193тЖТ   193тЖТand research ethics approval was obtained from [Blinded for Review] University. Because the platform
   194тЖТ   194тЖТsupports plain text only, users and helpers compose typographic emoticons (e.g., :), :(, :D) rather than
   195тЖТ   195тЖТpictographic Unicode emojis, so all analyses below concern emoticons.
   196тЖТ   196тЖТBuilding the lagged turn level dataset
   197тЖТ   197тЖТResearch question (RQ) 2 and RQ3 both rely on a turn level dataset in which each turn carries
   198тЖТ   198тЖТinformation about what the worker and user did in their immediately preceding turns. RQ1 does not
   199тЖТ   199тЖТrequire this lag structure and is answered from the message level data within the corpus.
   200тЖТ   200тЖТA turn is a maximal run of consecutive messages from the same speaker within a chat. Turn
   201тЖТ   201тЖТboundaries come from looking at every message (with or without emoticons), based on speaker
   202тЖТ   202тЖТchanges. With turn boundaries established, the per turn emoticon count is just the sum of the per
   203тЖТ   203тЖТmessage emoticon counts inside that turn. The dataset also retains the speaker role (worker or user)
   204тЖТ   204тЖТand the turn sequence within the session. Within each session, turns alternate strictly between user and
   205тЖТ   205тЖТworker. For any focal turn, the partner's prior turn is the immediately preceding turn (lag 1) and the
   206тЖТ   206тЖТactor's own prior turn is two turns back (lag 2); turn level lagged predictors are constructed from these
   207тЖТ   207тЖТtwo prior turns. The final turn level analytic sample contains 86,424 turns, of which 42,945 are user
   208тЖТ   208тЖТturns and 43,479 are worker turns.
   209тЖТ   209тЖТ
   210тЖТ   210тЖТMeasures
   211тЖТ   211тЖТThis section describes the emoticon predictors (used in RQ1, RQ2, and RQ3) and the session
   212тЖТ   212тЖТlevel user ratings used as moderators in RQ3.
   213тЖТ   213тЖТEmoticon identification
   214тЖТ   214тЖТEmoticons were identified by a locally hosted large language model (Open AIтАЩs open sourced
   215тЖТ   215тЖТgpt-oss:20b model). For each message, the model was given the raw text, the speaker role, and up to
   216тЖТ   216тЖТtwo preceding messages as context, and identified each emoticon present along with a standardised
   217тЖТ   217тЖТname. A few-shot prompting approach was used: the prompt included example emoticons drawn from a
   218тЖТ   218тЖТpublicly available emoticon dictionary (Sakib, 2022) and from a hand inspection of 30 randomly sampled
   219тЖТ   219тЖТemoticon bearing messages in the present corpus. Messages with multiple emoticons produced one
   220тЖТ   220тЖТrecord per emoticon. To validate the output, 200 emoticon bearing messages were randomly sampled
   221тЖТ   221тЖТand annotated by a human coder (undergraduate research assistant); agreement with the LLM labels
   222тЖТ   222тЖТwas high at 99%.
   223тЖТ   223тЖТConceptualising emoticon use: binary and count forms
   224тЖТ   224тЖТFollowing prior work on emoji and emoticon reciprocity (Stein, 2023), emoticon use is
   225тЖТ   225тЖТoperationalised in two complementary forms throughout RQ2 and RQ3. The binary form records
   226тЖТ   226тЖТwhether a turn contains any emoticon at all (presence or absence of cue use), and the count form
   227тЖТ   227тЖТrecords the integer number of emoticons in the turn (intensity of cue use). Each form is also constructed
   228тЖТ   228тЖТfor the lagged actor and partner predictors, and is used in the corresponding models in RQ2 and RQ3.
   229тЖТ   229тЖТSession level user ratings (RQ3 moderators)
   230тЖТ   230тЖТEach session has a post session evaluation completed by the user, comprising seven items rated
   231тЖТ   231тЖТon a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 3 = Slightly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree). Two items
   232тЖТ   232тЖТwere used as moderators in RQ3. The first, тАЬI feel supported and understood by my counsellorтАЭ, was
   233тЖТ   233тЖТtreated as the user's perceived responsiveness of the worker (Y1). The second, тАЬI am satisfied with the
   234тЖТ   234тЖТcounselling providedтАЭ, tapped on overall service satisfaction (Y2). Each rating was grand mean-centered
   235тЖТ   235тЖТbefore entering the model so that the simple effects of the actor and partner predictors would be
   236тЖТ   236тЖТinterpretable at the average level of the rating; the centered ratings are denoted Y1 and Y2 below.

   238тЖТ   238тЖТAnalytical Methods
   239тЖТ   239тЖТThe three research questions draw on different units of analysis and different modelling
   240тЖТ   240тЖТstrategies. They are presented in turn.
   241тЖТ   241тЖТRQ1: Descriptive comparisons of emoticon use by role
   242тЖТ   242тЖТRQ1 uses the message level data to compare emoticon use between workers and users.
   243тЖТ   243тЖТDescriptive statistics are reported as frequencies and percentages within each role. Differences between
   244тЖТ   244тЖТworkers and users are tested with Welch's two sample t test.
   245тЖТ   245тЖТRQ2: Turn level actor and partner effects on emoticon use
   246тЖТ   246тЖТ
   247тЖТ   247тЖТRQ2 uses the turn level lagged dataset and tests, separately for the binary and count
   248тЖТ   248тЖТoperationalisations of cue use, whether emoticons in the actor's own prior turn (actor effect) and in the
   249тЖТ   249тЖТpartner's prior turn (partner effect) predict the actor's current emoticon use, and whether these effects
   250тЖТ   250тЖТdiffer between worker and user. The mixed models below account for the multilevel structure of the
   251тЖТ   251тЖТdata by including random intercepts for sessions and for individual speakers.
   252тЖТ   252тЖТFor each operationalisation, two models are fitted. Model 1, the pooled model, estimates a
   253тЖТ   253тЖТsingle actor and a single partner effect pooled across workers and users. This provides a picture of the

   426тЖТ   426тЖТDiscussion
   427тЖТ   427тЖТHow emoticons are reciprocated in CMC matters because, with vocal and facial channels
   428тЖТ   428тЖТstripped away, emoticons carry the affective work that those channels would otherwise do. Reciprocity
   429тЖТ   429тЖТin emoticon use has been documented in symmetric dyads of peers, friends, and partners, but text-
   430тЖТ   430тЖТbased youth chatlines present a distinct communication context: the two parties enter the exchange in a
   431тЖТ   431тЖТhelping relationship, with a trained chat worker on one side and a young person seeking help on the
   432тЖТ   432тЖТother. The present study examined emoticon reciprocity in this setting using APIM applied to lagged
   433тЖТ   433тЖТturn-level data from a Singapore text chatline for young person seeking help. We found that emoticon
   434тЖТ   434тЖТuse was routine, that chat workers and chat users differed substantially in how much and how variedly
   435тЖТ   435тЖТthey used emoticons, that actor and partner effects were present in both directions but stronger from
   436тЖТ   436тЖТchat worker to chat user than the reverse, and that the partner effect (i.e., reciprocity) was stronger in
   437тЖТ   437тЖТsessions rated higher on chat worker responsiveness and overall satisfaction".
   438тЖТ   438тЖТEmoticons as a routine feature of text-based help (RQ1)
   439тЖТ   439тЖТAlmost nine in ten sessions contained at least one emoticon. In a service where the only
   440тЖТ   440тЖТavailable channel is typed text, this rate of use suggests that emoticons are commonly used to
   441тЖТ   441тЖТcommunicate affect in this setting. This pattern aligns with the predictions of Social Information
   442тЖТ   442тЖТProcessing theory and the Hyperpersonal model (Walther, 1992; Walther & Whitty, 2021), which hold
   443тЖТ   443тЖТ
   444тЖТ   444тЖТthat communicators redirect socioemotional expression through whatever cues the medium affords
   445тЖТ   445тЖТwhen richer vocal and facial channels are unavailable. The rate is also consistent with prior CMC
   446тЖТ   446тЖТresearch documenting widespread emoticon and emoji use in everyday text-based exchanges
   447тЖТ   447тЖТ(Cavalheiro et al., 2024; Stein, 2023), and the present results extend that pattern to a helping context.
   448тЖТ   448тЖТChat workers sent considerably more emoticons per session than chat users, and the great
   449тЖТ   449тЖТmajority of emoticon-bearing messages came from chat workers. This volume gap is unlikely to appear
   450тЖТ   450тЖТin a friend-to-friend exchange of the kind studied by Stein (2023) or Cavalheiro et al. (2024), where
   451тЖТ   451тЖТneither party is bound by a professional role. It is consistent with the role asymmetry argued in the
   452тЖТ   452тЖТIntroduction: chat workers carry a responsibility to support, affirm, and elicit, and in a channel stripped
   453тЖТ   453тЖТof vocal and facial cues, emoticons are one of the few textual resources available for doing that work
   454тЖТ   454тЖТ(Chung & Lim, 2025). Chat workers' repertoire was also narrower than chat users', concentrated on a
   455тЖТ   455тЖТsmall number of affirming forms, whereas chat users drew on a wider affective range. Chat workers, in
   456тЖТ   456тЖТother words, do more of the cue work but in a more restricted set of forms, which we return to as a
   457тЖТ   457тЖТlimitation below.
   458тЖТ   458тЖТReciprocity in a role-asymmetric dyad (RQ2)
   459тЖТ   459тЖТThat chat workers do more of the cue work descriptively raises the question of how the two
   460тЖТ   460тЖТparties' cues influence one another turn by turn. The pooled APIM models returned a positive actor
   461тЖТ   461тЖТeffect and a positive partner effect in both the binary and count operationalisations, supporting H2a:
   462тЖТ   462тЖТemoticon use in this dyad is reciprocal. This places the chat workerтАУuser dyad in the same broad pattern
   463тЖТ   463тЖТas the symmetric dyads studied by Coyle & Carmichael (2019), Stein (2023), Cavalheiro et al. (2024), and
   464тЖТ   464тЖТHuh (2025), and offers the first APIM-level evidence we are aware of that the reciprocity documented in
   465тЖТ   465тЖТthose settings carries over into a role-asymmetric helping context. The actor effects, present alongside
   466тЖТ   466тЖТthe partner effects, are best read as turn-to-turn consistency: each party tends to carry their own cue
   467тЖТ   467тЖТuse forward from one turn to the next, independent of what the partner does. This is a feature of how
   468тЖТ   468тЖТindividuals remain consistent in their own cue use within a session, rather than a finding about
   469тЖТ   469тЖТreciprocity.
   470тЖТ   470тЖТReciprocity, then, is present in this dyad. But it is not symmetric. The partner effect from chat
   471тЖТ   471тЖТworker to chat user was substantially larger than that from chat user to chat worker, in both
   472тЖТ   472тЖТoperationalisations, supporting H2b. We read this finding through both theoretical accounts in the
   473тЖТ   473тЖТIntroduction, with the helping context giving each a specific role. From the IPM perspective, the chat
   474тЖТ   474тЖТworker is positioned as the party who initiates and sustains the responsiveness cycle on the chat user's
   475тЖТ   475тЖТbehalf. The IPM holds that intimacy develops when one party communicates personally relevant feelings
   476тЖТ   476тЖТand the other responds in ways that convey understanding, validation, and caring, and that emotional
   477тЖТ   477тЖТdisclosures, rather than factual ones, are the disclosures most strongly tied to the experience of
   478тЖТ   478тЖТintimacy (Laurenceau et al., 2004). The medium itself supports this, since anonymity and reduced cues in
   479тЖТ   479тЖТin text-based exchange can make people more willing to disclose and feel more intimate than they
   480тЖТ   480тЖТwould face-to-face (Walther, 1996). In a chatline session, the chat user enters the exchange already in a
   481тЖТ   481тЖТdisclosing position, often with content that is affectively loaded. According to the IPM, the chat worker
   482тЖТ   482тЖТis trained to respond in a way that the chat user perceives as understanding and caring, and emoticons
   483тЖТ   483тЖТare one of the few textual resources available to do so once vocal and facial channels are absent. A chat
   484тЖТ   484тЖТworker's :) placed alongside a difficult message can soften, validate, and invite further disclosure.
   485тЖТ   485тЖТBecause perceived partner responsiveness mediates the link between disclosure and intimacy
   486тЖТ   486тЖТ
   487тЖТ   487тЖТ(Laurenceau et al., 1998, 2004), the chat worker's emoticon use is how the chat user comes to sense the
   488тЖТ   488тЖТchat worker's support and understanding.
   489тЖТ   489тЖТCAT addresses the complementary question: why the chat user's cue use moves with the chat
   490тЖТ   490тЖТworker's rather than the reverse. CAT holds that interlocutors converge on each other's communicative
   491тЖТ   491тЖТstyle as a way of signalling affiliation, seeking approval, and reducing uncertainty, and that this
   492тЖТ   492тЖТconvergence is modulated by hierarchy, formality, and the relative status of the two parties (Adams et
   493тЖТ   493тЖТal., 2018; Giles et al., 2023; Giles & Ogay, 2007). All three modulators are present in the chatline dyad.
   494тЖТ   494тЖТThe chat worker is trained, in role, and holds institutional standing as the helping professional; the chat
   495тЖТ   495тЖТuser has initiated the encounter from a position of seeking support and has comparatively less
   496тЖТ   496тЖТsituational power. Adams et al. (2018) found in a controlled experiment that the lower-power party in a
   497тЖТ   497тЖТtext exchange used more textisms after viewing many textisms from the higher-power partner, and
   498тЖТ   498тЖТStein (2023) showed that participants used more emojis when replying to messages that contained
   499тЖТ   499тЖТemojis, with this reciprocation strongest when interaction partners were closer or held more standing.
   500тЖТ   500тЖТThe chat worker тЖТ chat user partner effect estimated here is what these findings would predict for the
   501тЖТ   501тЖТchatline, now observed in a real helping service: chat users accommodate to the chat worker's emoticon
   502тЖТ   502тЖТuse more than the chat worker accommodates to theirs, and the asymmetry tracks the power and role
   503тЖТ   503тЖТasymmetry of the dyad.
   504тЖТ   504тЖТThe chat user тЖТ chat worker partner effect, although smaller, was still positive: chat workers
   505тЖТ   505тЖТdid adjust their emoticon use in response to chat users' prior turns. We read this as the chat worker
   506тЖТ   506тЖТreciprocating enough to sustain the responsiveness cycle. If the chat worker did not reciprocate at all,
   507тЖТ   507тЖТthe cycle would not sustain itself, since responsiveness on one side alone does not produce the
   508тЖТ   508тЖТperceived mutuality on which intimacy depends. The cycle is led but not one-sided, and this two-way
   509тЖТ   509тЖТmovement appears to be part of how the helping work proceeds in a text helpline.
   510тЖТ   510тЖТReciprocity and how chat users experience the session (RQ3)
   511тЖТ   511тЖТIf this asymmetric reciprocity is the communicative shape of the helping dyad, the next question
   512тЖТ   512тЖТis whether it matters for how chat users experience the session. The RQ3 analyses indicate that it does.
   513тЖТ   513тЖТThe partner effect was stronger in sessions where chat users rated the chat worker as more responsive
   514тЖТ   514тЖТ(Y1) and where users reported greater satisfaction with the chat sessions (Y2), across both
   515тЖТ   515тЖТoperationalisations and both moderators, supporting H3. The IPM says that responsiveness builds when
   516тЖТ   516тЖТboth parties take up each other's cues тАФ emoticons, in this setting тАФ and that this reciprocal exchange
   517тЖТ   517тЖТis what produces the perception of being understood and cared for (Laurenceau et al., 2004; Laurenceau
   518тЖТ   518тЖТet al., 1998; Reis, 2017). Sessions in which the emoticon exchange between chat worker and chat user
   519тЖТ   519тЖТmoved together most closely were also the sessions in which chat users later rated the chat worker as
   520тЖТ   520тЖТmore responsive and reported greater satisfaction with the service.
   521тЖТ   521тЖТTaken with findings for RQ2, it is not chat workers' emoticon use on its own that predicts chat
   522тЖТ   522тЖТusers' experience of the service, but the reciprocal uptake of emoticons by chat users, and the chat
   523тЖТ   523тЖТworker's reciprocal uptake of chat users' emoticons in return. The chatline operates well when the
   524тЖТ   524тЖТemoticon exchange flows in both directions, and the user-rating data suggest that chat users themselves
   525тЖТ   525тЖТperceive the difference between sessions in which it does and those in which it does not."
   526тЖТ   526тЖТLimitations and scope
   527тЖТ   527тЖТ
   528тЖТ   528тЖТThree limitations bound the present findings. First, we measured emoticons as presence and
   529тЖТ   529тЖТcount, not as meaning. The same form can serve different functions, including expressing sentiment,
   530тЖТ   530тЖТadjusting tone, or signalling irony (Hu et al., 2017), and the sender's intended meaning may not match
   531тЖТ   531тЖТhow the partner reads it. A smiley face may convey warmth in one turn and sarcasm in another. The
   532тЖТ   532тЖТpartner effects here aggregate over these possibilities, and future work linking specific cues to their
   533тЖТ   533тЖТintended and perceived meaning would give a clearer picture of what is being reciprocated.
   534тЖТ   534тЖТSecond, the design is observational. The lagged turn structure of APIM gives the predictor
   535тЖТ   535тЖТtemporal precedence over the outcome, which rules out reverse causation at the turn level, but the
   536тЖТ   536тЖТpartner effects estimated here remain associations rather than causal estimates.Third, the corpus is
   537тЖТ   537тЖТlimited to typographic emoticons, not pictographic Unicode emojis. The QuickChat interface supports
   538тЖТ   538тЖТtyped text only, so chat users and chat workers compose forms such as :) and :( from keyboard
   539тЖТ   539тЖТcharacters, and the richer inventory of pictographic emojis is not part of the channel under study. How
   540тЖТ   540тЖТfar the present findings transfer to services that support full emoji input, or to other cultural and clinical
   541тЖТ   541тЖТsettings, is an open question.
   542тЖТ   542тЖТWithin those bounds, the present findings extend the CMC literature on emoticon reciprocity
   543тЖТ   543тЖТfrom peer-like dyads into a role-asymmetric helping setting, and they suggest that what matters in text-
   544тЖТ   544тЖТbased help is not either party's emoticon use considered alone but the reciprocal exchange between the
   545тЖТ   545тЖТtwo. The chat workerтАУuser pairing is one instance of a broader group of dyads in which the two parties
   546тЖТ   546тЖТoccupy structurally different positions, and the asymmetric reciprocity observed here may be a useful
   547тЖТ   547тЖТstarting point for examining cue uptake in other role-differentiated text-based exchanges.
