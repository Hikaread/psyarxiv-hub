     1→   800→within-person intervention, and preregistering the study. However, there are several limitations
     2→   801→related to the study design that should be noted. First, we focused on college students because
     3→   802→they are at risk for alcohol-related negative consequences and could benefit from preventative
     4→   803→interventions that help them develop healthy habits related to alcohol. However, this means that
     5→   804→our findings may not generalize beyond this population, which tends to be wealthier, whiter, and
     6→   805→more highly educated than the general population (Henrich et al., 2010). Future work is needed
     7→   806→in more diverse populations. Despite a wide range of drinking behavior in our sample, we did
     8→   807→not recruit individuals with alcohol-use disorders. This recruitment strategy may limit the
     9→   808→applicability of our findings to individuals with alcohol-use disorder. Finally, we employed a
    10→   809→within-person design to enable inferences about individual-level intervention mechanisms.
    11→   810→However, this design may have introduced demand effects (Charness et al., 2012). Future work
    12→   811→should examine how any demand effect may be reduced by introducing regulatory choice, that
    13→   812→is, letting participants opt into mindful distancing as a regulatory strategy on a subset of trials
    14→   813→(Cosme et al., 2018).
    15→   814→There are also several limitations related to the neural signature. First, it was created in
    16→   815→the context of a task that was not designed with development or validation of neural signatures
    17→   816→in mind. Participants used mindful distancing while viewing alcohol stimuli, which limits the
    18→   817→potential generalizability of using this strategy in response to other internal or external stimuli.
    19→   818→The task did not include ratings of how well participants used the strategy on any given trial,
    20→   819→which would have enabled prediction of subjective effectiveness during strategy usage rather
    21→   820→than merely classification of task condition. Second, the limited size and homogeneity of the
    22→   821→sample reduces the likelihood that this signature will generalize to new samples or other
    23→   822→populations (Baranger et al., 2025; Woo et al., 2017). Finally, the low cross-validation accuracy
    24→   823→suggests that there was substantial variability in activation patterns across participants making it
    25→   824→difficult to identify common patterns that reliably predict mindful distancing. Overcoming these
    26→   825→issues by using tasks specifically designed to create neural signatures of mindful states (e.g., as
    27→   826→described in Lewis-Peacock et al., 2025) in large and diverse samples is an important objective
    28→   827→for future research. Once developed and validated, such signatures could be applied to new and
    29→   828→existing data to measure mindful states in a variety of contexts.
    30→   829→4.5 Conclusion
    31→   830→This work employed a translational neuroscience approach to examine how mindful
    32→   831→distancing impacts alcohol-related craving and consumption under controlled and real-world
    33→   832→conditions. We used functional neuroimaging and machine learning to develop a predictive
    34→   833→model of mindful distancing that enabled us to measure momentary fluctuations and individual
    35→   834→differences in mindful distancing. Mindful distancing reduced cravings for alcohol in the
    36→   835→18
    37→   836→
    38→   837→laboratory and decreased alcohol consumption in daily life, both directly and indirectly through
    39→   838→reducing cravings. Moreover, individuals who more strongly expressed the mindful distancing
    40→   839→brain signature—indicating greater ability to attend to alcohol mindfully at baseline—benefited
    41→   840→the most from the intervention. Together, these findings suggest that mindful distancing can be
    42→   841→an effective, preventative strategy for reducing alcohol consumption when successfully
    43→   842→implemented. Furthermore, it highlights the potential of scalable, smartphone-based
    44→   843→interventions that remind individuals to regulate their responses to alcohol (Jovanova et al.,
    45→   844→2023).

    66→   865→factors. To help us quantify our efforts on gender, we used the Citation Gap Illuminator
    67→   866→(https://dcosme.shinyapps.io/citation-gap-illuminator) and obtained the predicted gender of
    68→   867→authors in each reference by using databases that store the probability of a first name being
    69→   868→carried by a woman (Caplar et al., 2017; Dion et al., 2018; Dworkin et al., 2020; Maliniak et al.,
    70→   869→2013; Mitchell et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2022). By this measure, our citations contain 42%
    71→   870→women and 56% men (2% of names could not be classified) across all authors from

    73→   872→considering only first and last authors from non-software references; and 100% men across
    74→   873→software references. This method is limited in that a) names, pronouns, and social media
    75→   874→profiles used to construct the databases may not, in every case, be indicative of gender identity
