    61→Abstract
    62→Stress beliefs—general expectations about the nature and consequences of stress—have been
    63→linked to health and performance, yet their role in shaping responses to everyday stress
    64→remains less understood. This study examined whether stress beliefs moderate affective
    65→reactivity and stress regulation strategies in response to naturally occurring stressors in daily
    66→life. In a 7-day ecological momentary assessment (EMA) study, 104 university students
    67→completed thrice-daily reports (1,939 observations) on social and work-related stressors,
    68→positive and negative affect, and two stress regulation strategies—situation selection and
    69→response modification—as proposed in the Stress Optimization Model (Crum, Jamieson, &
    70→Akinola, 2020). At baseline, stress beliefs were assessed alongside variables to characterize
    71→the sample. Negative stress beliefs moderated the association between daily stressors and
    72→positive affect: individuals with high negative stress beliefs showed reduced positive affect
    73→during social stress and no increase in positive affect under higher workload. In contrast,
    74→those with low or moderate negative stress beliefs maintained positive affect during social
    75→stress and showed increased positive affect under higher workload—consistent with a
    76→challenge-like appraisal. Additionally, controllability beliefs buffered increases in negative
    77→affect in response to daily social stress, with lower beliefs linked to stronger affective
    78→reactivity. In contrast, stress beliefs did not moderate associations between daily stressors and
    79→the investigated stress regulation strategies. These findings suggest that stress beliefs tune
    80→affective reactivity to everyday social and work stressors, identifying them as a potential
    81→intervention target given the established links between affect and long-term health. The
    82→generalizability of these findings should be confirmed across broader populations.
    83→Keywords: stress beliefs; stress mindset; affective reactivity; stress regulation; ecological
    84→momentary assessment

    87→Stress Beliefs Shape Daily Affective Reactivity to Social and Work Stressors: A 7-

    89→The stress response is vital tool of adaption, enabling individuals to meet the
    90→multifaceted demands imposed by the environment. When individuals experience or
    91→anticipate demands in goal-related contexts—such as danger, conflict, or pressure (i.e.,
    92→stressors)—the body responds with relatively nonspecific emotional, behavioral, and
    93→physiological changes (i.e., the stress response(Crum et al., 2020)). This stress response can
    94→help individuals survive and thrive by mobilizing resources needed to meet current demands.
    95→At the same time, it is well established that stress can also be harmful to mental and physical
    96→health (McEwen, 1998; O’Connor et al., 2021). The health impact of stress is contingent upon
    97→numerous factors, including characteristics of the stressor itself—such as its content (e.g.,
    98→social vs. workload-related demands), intensity, and chronicity—which have been shown to
    99→influence the magnitude and quality of stress responses and their longer-term health
   100→consequences (Almeida, 2005; Dickerson & Kemeny, 2004; McEwen, 1998). In addition,
   101→stress outcomes depend on person-related factors, including how stressors are appraised and
   102→how individuals attempt to influence or modify their stress responses (i.e., stress regulation;
   103→(Crum et al., 2020; Lazarus & Folkman, 1984; McEwen, 1998).
   104→Stress Beliefs: Conceptualization and Impact on Health and Performance
   105→A new perspective in explaining health and performance under stress that has
   106→recently garnered attention is the concept of stress beliefs. Stress beliefs encapsulate an
   107→individual’s expectations and beliefs regarding the nature and consequences of stress
   108→(Jamieson, Crum, et al., 2018; Kilby et al., 2020; Laferton et al., 2018). For instance,
   109→individuals harboring negative stress beliefs, perceive stress as deleterious to performance and
   110→health, while others who hold positive stress beliefs, tend to view stress as a catalyst for
   111→reaching one’s full potential. Such beliefs or expectations have been studied at a situation-
   112→
   113→6
   114→specific level, often termed stress reappraisal—that is, appraising the acute stress response in
   115→a given situation as a helpful resource rather than an added demand (Jamieson et al., 2013;
   116→Jamieson, Crum, et al., 2018). They have also been examined at a more generalized level,
   117→termed stress beliefs or stress mindset—the belief that stress is generally helpful or harmful
   118→for health and performance (Crum et al., 2013; Fischer et al., 2016; Jamieson, Crum, et al.,
   119→2018; Laferton et al., 2018). Stress beliefs have most frequently been assessed using the Stress
   120→Mindset Measure (SMM; (Crum et al., 2013) or the Beliefs About Stress Scale (BASS;
   121→(Laferton et al., 2018). The SMM conceptualizes stress beliefs as a largely unidimensional
   122→construct, capturing the extent to which individuals view stress as generally enhancing versus
   123→debilitating for health and performance. In contrast, the BASS differentiates stress beliefs into
   124→three interrelated facets: negative stress beliefs, positive stress beliefs, and beliefs about the
   125→controllability of stress (Laferton et al., 2018). Notably, several psychometric evaluations of
   126→cultural adaptations of the SMM have identified two strongly correlated but distinguishable
   127→factors reflecting positive and negative aspects of stress beliefs (Florean et al., 2025; Iwamoto
   128→et al., 2020; Karampas et al., 2020; Park, 2022; Tedadi et al., 2022), suggesting that these
   129→belief components may not always be fully captured by a single latent dimension across
   130→contexts. This structural differentiation conceptually aligns with the separation of positive and
   131→negative stress beliefs assessed by the BASS. Beyond valence-related beliefs, the BASS
   132→additionally incorporates beliefs about the controllability of stress, a dimension that is
   133→supported by broader theoretical and empirical work highlighting the role of perceived control
   134→and coping efficacy in shaping stress responses and outcomes (Kilby et al., 2020) and by the
   135→development of newer multidimensional stress belief measures that explicitly include control-
   136→related components (e.g., STRESS; (Kilby et al., 2022). Empirically supporting this
   137→conceptual distinction, initial evidence from a sample of 110 university students indicated
   138→strong convergent validity of the SMM with both the negative (BASS-N; r =.76) and positive
   139→stress belief subscales (BASS-P; r =.72) of the BASS, whereas only a moderate correlation
   140→
   141→7
   142→emerged with the controllability subscale (BASS-C; r = .47; (Laferton & Fischer, 2021).
   143→Consequently, this pattern underscores that while BASS-P and BASS-N closely align with the
   144→core dimensions of general stress mindsets, BASS-C captures a related but distinct theoretical
   145→facet. Crucially, a recent large-scale validation in a representative sample of the German
   146→general population (N = 2515; (Schmalbach et al., 2025) confirmed a bifactor structure of the
   147→BASS. This indicates that the BASS captures a strong general stress mindset factor—
   148→conceptually equivalent to the unidimensional SMM—alongside more specific belief
   149→dimensions, such as controllability, that capture additional, theoretically meaningful variance.
   150→Recent research has uncovered persuasive evidence that stress beliefs can influence
   151→health, well-being and performance in a diverse set of populations: In two large general
   152→population samples, beliefs that stress is harmful was associated with increased mortality over
   153→the course of several years (Keller et al., 2012; Nabi et al., 2013). Related work, primarily
   154→using the Stress Mindset Measure, has shown that holding a stress-is-debilitating mindset is
   155→associated with higher levels of depressiveness, anxiety, somatic symptoms, and distress
   156→among adolescents (Park et al., 2018), as well as college students, employees (Casper et al.,
   157→2017; Crum et al., 2013), police officers (Keech et al., 2020), and chronic pain patients
   158→(Grünenwald et al., 2023). Moreover, viewing stress as debilitating has been linked to
   159→decreased academic and social-evaluative performance, poorer academic outcomes in
   160→undergraduate students (Keech et al., 2018), and to lower vigor and task performance in
   161→employees (Casper et al., 2017; Crum et al., 2013). Importantly, conceptually comparable
   162→associations have also been observed using the Beliefs About Stress Scale. Specifically,
   163→negative stress beliefs were associated with greater somatic symptom burden and higher
   164→negative affect in university students (Fischer et al., 2016; Laferton et al., 2020), as well as
   165→with poorer mental health among healthcare workers during the COVID-19 pandemic
   166→(Laferton et al., 2024; Wekenborg et al., 2024). In contrast, positive stress beliefs have been
   167→linked to better mental health in healthcare workers (Bai et al., 2024), better mental health in
   168→
   169→8
   170→the general population (Schmalbach et al., 2025), and higher vitality in employees (Digutsch
   171→et al., 2022). Beyond valence-related beliefs, beliefs about the controllability of stress have
   172→also been associated with better mental health outcomes in healthcare workers (Bai et al.,
   173→2024, 2025; Wekenborg et al., 2024) and in population-based samples (Schmalbach et al.,
   174→2025).
   175→Taken together, accumulating evidence indicates that different components of stress
   176→beliefs—including negative, positive, and controllability-related beliefs—are systematically
   177→associated with health, well-being, and performance outcomes across populations. This raises
   178→a mechanistic question as to how stress beliefs shape individuals’ experiences and actions in
   179→stressful situations in ways that may give rise to documented differences in mortality, mental
   180→and physical symptom burden, and performance. Prior work suggests that alterations in
   181→affective stress responses and in stress regulation strategies represent two candidate pathways
   182→through which stress beliefs may exert these effects (Crum et al., 2020; Jamieson, Crum, et
   183→al., 2018; Jamieson, Hangen, et al., 2018; Yeager et al., 2022).
   184→Stress beliefs and altered affective stress responses
   185→Regarding the affective stress response several experimental studies provided
   186→evidence that inducing positive stress beliefs can enhance positive and reduce negative affect
   187→in response to a laboratory stressor (Beltzer et al., 2014; Brooks, 2014; Crum et al., 2017).
   188→Initial experience sampling studies suggest that individuals’ (unmanipulated) stress beliefs
   189→influence the affective stress response to real world stressors. In a 10-day daily diary study,
   190→university students’ stress beliefs prospectively moderated the affective stress response to
   191→daily stressors (Laferton et al., 2020). Individuals with high negative stress beliefs responded
   192→with a stronger increase in negative affect in response to daily social stressors than students
   193→with low negative stress beliefs. Similarly, negative stress beliefs predicted poorer affect in
   194→response to work stressors throughout a five-day daily diary assessment (Daniels et al., 2006).
   195→
   196→9
   197→Furthermore, affect itself was associated with altered information processing, physiological
   198→functioning, coping and health behavior (Aldao et al., 2010; DeSteno et al., 2013; Diener et
   199→al., 2017; Gross et al., 2019; Pressman et al., 2018; Salovey et al., 2000; Sin, 2016). To
   200→summarize, stress beliefs may alter health and performance outcomes by shaping affective
   201→stress reactions that operate through the above-mentioned pathways.
   202→At the same time, it is important to note that the extent to which stress beliefs shape
   203→affective responses may depend on the nature of the stressor itself. Research indicates that
   204→stressor content might influences affective and physiological stress responses (Almeida,
   205→2005). For example, experimental work has demonstrated that social-evaluative stressors
   206→elicit stronger affective and neuroendocrine responses than non-social stressors(Dickerson &
   207→Kemeny, 2004). In studies of daily stress, the most frequently reported stressors are
   208→interpersonal (social) stressors and work- or education-related stressors (Almeida et al., 2002).
   209→Consistent with experimental research, these stressor classes seem to differ in their
   210→psychological impact. Several diary and experience sampling studies suggest that
   211→interpersonal stressors tend to be associated with stronger emotional reactivity than workload-
   212→related stressors, including larger increases in negative affect or distress (Bolger et al., 1989;
   213→Bolger & Schilling, 1991; Kerber et al., 2026), and show more consistent associations with
   214→internalizing symptoms in daily life (Zimmermann et al., 2019). Similar differential patterns
   215→have been observed in ambulatory assessment studies comparing interpersonal and non-
   216→interpersonal stressors with respect to affective states and physical symptoms (Almeida, 2005;
   217→Leger et al., 2021).
   218→Importantly, there are also indications that the association between stressor exposure
   219→and affective responses differs in the extent to which it is shaped by more time-stable
   220→individual characteristics. Prior work suggests that dispositional factors such as neuroticism
   221→(Bolger & Schilling, 1991) and depressive symptomatology (Sheets & Armey, 2020) more
   222→strongly moderate affective responses to interpersonal stressors than to workload-related
   223→
   224→10
   225→demands. Moreover, initial intervention-based ambulatory evidence indicates that changes in
   226→stressor–affect associations following stress-related interventions may differ between
   227→interpersonal and workload stressors (Leger et al., 2021). Taken together, these findings
   228→suggest that stressor type may be particularly relevant when examining how relatively stable
   229→belief systems—such as stress beliefs—interact with daily stressor exposure to shape affective
   230→responses.
   231→Consistent with this distinction, existing daily stress studies examining stress beliefs
   232→and affective stress responses have focused on different stressor domains. Evidence for belief-
   233→related differences in affective reactivity has been reported in the context of work-related
   234→stressors (Daniels et al., 2006; Casper et al., 2017) as well as for social stressors (Laferton et
   235→al., 2020). Notably, the only experience sampling study to examine both stressor types
   236→simultaneously found that stress beliefs moderated affective responses to social stressors,
   237→whereas the corresponding moderation effect for workload-related stressors did not remain
   238→statistically robust after correction for multiple testing (Laferton et al., 2020).
   239→Stress beliefs and altered stress regulation strategies
   240→Further, it is assumed that stress beliefs also shape stress regulation strategies; within
   241→the stress optimization model (Crum et al., 2020), two key proposed facets are situation
   242→selection and response modification. Situation selection: individuals with more negative stress
   243→beliefs may be more likely to avoid stressful situations (e.g., declining challenging tasks),
   244→which over time can lead to missed opportunities and frustration; in contrast, those with more
   245→positive stress beliefs may be more inclined to approach stressful situations (e.g., seeking
   246→challenging assignments), thereby increasing opportunities for skill development and goal
   247→attainment. Response modification: once in a stressful situation, stress beliefs are
   248→hypothesized to guide how individuals regulate their physiological stress response; those who
   249→view stress as harmful may attempt to dampen arousal, whereas those endorsing more positive
   250→
   251→11
   252→stress beliefs may try to harness their stress response to facilitate goal pursuit. However, direct
   253→empirical tests of these stress regulation strategies remain relatively sparse, and much of the
   254→existing work has been conducted in laboratory settings. In support of situation selection,
   255→university students with more positive stress beliefs showed a greater desire for feedback
   256→before a social-evaluative laboratory stressor (Crum et al., 2013). Outside the laboratory,
   257→however, only a small number of studies have examined associations between stress beliefs
   258→and stress regulation strategies in daily life, and these studies have relied on relatively broad
   259→operationalizations of approach- versus avoidance-oriented stress regulation strategies that do
   260→not allow for a specific examination of situation selection and response modification. For
   261→example, in a five-day daily diary study, Casper et al. (2017) found that a stress is enhancing
   262→mindset was associated with higher approach-oriented coping among employees anticipating
   263→high workload. Similarly, cross-sectional studies have reported associations between stress
   264→beliefs and general approach–avoidance stress regulation tendencies (Keech et al., 2018,
   265→2020; Zhang et al., 2022). Taken together, existing findings provide initial evidence that stress
   266→beliefs might be related to stress regulation tendencies in general. However, beyond the
   267→laboratory evidence reported by Crum et al. (2013), there is little empirical work that
   268→explicitly examines specific stress regulation strategies as conceptualized in the stress
   269→optimization model—namely situation selection and response modification. Moreover, no
   270→study so far has assessed situation selection and response modification in close temporal
   271→proximity to naturally occurring daily stressors, leaving it largely unclear whether and how
   272→stress beliefs shape these stress regulation processes as stress unfolds in everyday life.
   273→In conclusion, emerging evidence supports several psychological mechanisms
   274→linking stress beliefs to health and performance outcomes. However, the majority of those
   275→studies relied on experimentally manipulated stress beliefs and laboratory stressors, thereby
   276→limiting their ecological validity. Whether these processes similarly influence health and
   277→performance when stress beliefs are naturally acquired and in the occurrence of everyday-life
   278→
   279→12
   280→stressors, remains unclear. Therefore, this study aimed to investigate how different
   281→components of stress beliefs—negative beliefs, positive beliefs, and beliefs about the
   282→controllability of stress—are associated with affective stress responses and stress regulation
   283→strategies (situation selection and response modification) in response to daily stressors using
   284→an ecological momentary assessment (EMA) design. Based on prior evidence linking
   285→negative, positive and controllability stress beliefs to mental health and affective well-being
   286→across populations (Bai et al., 2024; Fischer et al., 2016; Laferton et al., 2024; Schmalbach et
   287→al., 2025), as well as initial experience sampling studies demonstrating that stress beliefs
   288→moderate affective reactivity to daily stressors (Daniels et al., 2006; Laferton et al., 2020), we
   289→expected stress beliefs to be systematically associated with affective responses to daily stress.
   290→Specifically, higher levels of negative stress beliefs were expected to be associated with
   291→stronger increases in negative affect and attenuated positive affect in response to daily social
   292→and workload stressors. In contrast, higher levels of positive stress beliefs and stronger beliefs
   293→about the controllability of stress were expected to be associated with lower increases in
   294→negative affect and higher levels of positive affect when individuals encountered daily
   295→stressors.
   296→Furthermore, drawing on the stress optimization model (Crum et al., 2020) and initial
   297→empirical evidence (Crum et al., 2013; Casper et al., 2017), this study examines whether stress
   298→beliefs are associated with stress regulation strategies in response to daily stressors.
   299→Specifically, higher levels of negative stress beliefs were expected to be associated with
   300→greater avoidance-based situation selection and lower attempts to utilize the stress response
   301→when individuals encountered daily stress. In contrast, higher levels of positive stress beliefs
   302→and stronger beliefs about the controllability of stress were expected to be associated with
   303→lower avoidance of stressful situations and greater attempts to utilize the stress response.

   307→Procedure
   308→University students of three German universities were invited to participate in a 7-
   309→day EMA study about stress perception via e-mail lists or research participation platforms
   310→between June 2022 and July 2023. Inclusion criteria were enrollment as a university student,
   311→age ≥18 years, absence of chronic disease, and provision of written informed consent.
   312→Participation was voluntary and as compensation for their efforts, participants had the chance
   313→to win gift certificates. Participants were invited to a baseline assessment in the lab including
   314→the baseline questionnaire and a recording of resting heart rate variability. On the following
   315→day, participants underwent a 7-day EMA period. EMA was conducted via the smartphone
   316→application m-path (www.m-path.io ).
   317→Participants were excluded from the analysis if they did not complete the baseline
   318→assessment and missed all of the daily assessments. The study was approved by the local
   319→institutional review boards of the participating universities. All procedures performed were in
   320→accordance with the ethical standards of the institutional research committees and with the
   321→1946 Helsinki declaration and its later amendments.
   322→Measures
   323→Baseline
   324→Stress beliefs were assessed using the Beliefs About Stress Scale (Laferton et al.,
   325→2018). On three scales this self-report questionnaire measures negative stress beliefs (BASS-
   326→N; eight items) and positive stress beliefs (BASS-P; four items) as well as perceived control
   327→(BASS-C; three items). Example items are as follows: ‘Being stressed affects my health in the
   328→short term’ (BASS-N), ‘Being stressed enables me to work in a more focused manner’
   329→(BASS-P), or ‘Being stressed is something I am able to influence through my actions’
   330→(BASS-C). Items are rated on a 4-point scale from ‘completely disagree‘ (1) to ‘definitely
   331→agree‘ (4). The BASS has been psychometrically validated in student samples and in a
   332→
   333→14
   334→representative sample of the German general population, and normative data for the general
   335→population are available (Schmalbach et al., 2025).
   336→At baseline, to describe the situational context of their university studies, participants
   337→were asked to report their study subject, the sum of weekly hours of courses they had signed
   338→up for, whether they had a part-time job, and to provide demographic variables (age, gender).
   339→To screen for symptoms of depression and anxiety, participants completed the depression
   340→(PHQ-9) and the anxiety (GAD-7) module of the Patient Health Questionnaire (Kroenke et
   341→al., 2010).
   342→Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA)
   343→During the 7-day EMA, an interval-contingent sampling design utilizing a
   344→"coverage" approach EMA was employed (Shiffman et al., 2008; Stone et al., 2023; Vaessen
   345→et al., 2023). Participants were asked to report on stressors, affect and stress regulation
   346→strategies three times a day at fixed intervals (noon, 4 pm and 8 pm) with each assessment
   347→referring to the prior 4 hours.
   348→Social Stressors & Workload Stressors. Participants’ stressors were assessed using
   349→items from the situational experience module of the Personality Dynamics Diary
   350→(Zimmermann et al., 2019) PDD). Three PDD items were used to assess social stress (PDD-
   351→SS; ‘I was ignored or rejected by others’; ‘I was blamed, criticized, or devalued by someone’;
   352→‘I was left in the lurch by a close person’), and two items to assess workload (PDD-W; ‘I had
   353→to deal with too many, or too difficult tasks’; ‘I was under high pressure to succeed while
   354→getting done with my tasks’). Participants endorsed whether each item applied to them during
   355→the 4 hours preceding the EMA on a 4-point scale from 0 = very false to 3 = very true. PDD-
   356→SS and PDD-W scales were calculated using the mean of the respective items of each scale.
   357→Affective Reactivity. Daily positive and negative affect was assessed using the 10-
   358→item short form of the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (Thompson, 2007; Watson et
   359→
   360→15
   361→al., 1988). The PANAS is one of the most widely used self-report questionnaires assessing
   362→positive (PANAS-P) and negative affect (PANAS-N). Positive affect (e.g. excited, proud) and
   363→negative affect (e.g. upset, afraid) are assessed on two independent five-item scales, with
   364→responses ranging from 1 = ‘very slightly or not at all’ to 5 = ‘extremely’. Among other time
   365→frames, the PANAS has been validated to assess daily affect (Rush & Hofer, 2014).
   366→Stress Regulation. Stress regulation strategies were assessed based on the stress
   367→optimization model (Crum et al., 2020). Within this model, situation selection and response
   368→modulation are conceptualized as regulatory strategies that can be flexibly deployed in
   369→different directions (e.g., avoiding vs. seeking stressful situations; minimizing vs. utilizing
   370→stress responses), depending on individuals’ regulatory goals. To reduce participant burden
   371→and ensure feasibility within the EMA design, we assessed only one option within each
   372→regulatory domain. Specifically, situation selection was operationalized via avoidance of
   373→stressful situations (‘How hard have you tried to avoid the stressful situation?’), and response
   374→modulation was operationalized via attempts to utilize the stress response (‘How hard have
   375→you tried to utilize your feeling of being stressed?’). Participants rated to what extent they
   376→engaged in situation avoidance and utilization of the stress response regarding stressors during
   377→the preceding 4h of each EMA assessment on a 5-point scale from 0 = not at all, 1= a little, 2
   378→= somewhat, 3 = considerably, 4 = extremely.
   379→Participants
   380→106 individuals provided written informed consent after receiving oral and written
   381→study information from a trained investigator. Two participants had to be excluded due to
   382→smartphone or app malfunction. N = 104 provided data on baseline assessment and at least
   383→one EMA assessment and were therefore included in the final analysis. The mean number of
   384→completed questionnaires (1 baseline questionnaire + 3x7 EMA questionnaires) per
   385→participant was 19.21 (SD=3.59; Min=2; Max=22).

   388→Sample characteristics are shown in table 1. Participants predominantly identified as
   389→female, German and were university students from a broad range of academic disciplines. The
   390→mean course load per week was slightly above the average for German university students
   391→(German Ministry of Education and Research, 2017) and the majority had a part-time job.
   392→Neither the number of weekly course hours nor the specific academic discipline (STEM and
   393→medicine vs. other fields) was associated with baseline stress beliefs, reports of daily
   394→stressors, affect, or stress regulation strategies (see Supplement 2). Female participants
   395→reported a higher number of weekly university course hours. Apart from this difference, no
   396→gender differences were observed in the study variables. The amount of participants with
   397→elevated symptoms of depression and anxiety (see Table 1), is within the range of prevalence
   398→found in other university student samples (Ibrahim et al., 2013). Moreover, the mean values of
   399→negative, positive and controllability stress beliefs match those found in previous university
   400→student samples (Fischer et al., 2016; Laferton et al., 2020). The mean level of workload was
   401→comparable to the validation student sample of the PDD (Zimmermann et al., 2019), while the
   402→mean level of social stress was somewhat lower in the current sample.
   403→Data analysis
   404→Addressing the nested structure of the data, hierarchical linear models were estimated
   405→with repeated data entries at level 1 nested in participants at level 2. First, the fixed effects of
   406→stress beliefs (level 2: BASS) on each of the EMA variables (level 1: stressors, affect, stress
   407→regulation strategy) were analyzed using random intercept models. To reduce
   408→multicollinearity, separate random intercept models were estimated for each BASS subscale
   409→(BASS-N, BASS-C, BASS-P) on each dependent variable (PDD-SS, PDD-W, PANAS-N,
   410→PANAS-P, situation selection, response modification). Second, the main hypotheses –
   411→whether stress beliefs would influence I) affect and II) stress regulation strategies in response
   412→to stressors – was tested by adding work stress or social stress as a further variable, including
   413→cross-level interactions with stress beliefs. This resulted in additional random intercept
   414→
   415→17
   416→models with focal cross-level interaction terms of each stress belief’s subscale and work stress
   417→(BASS-N* PDD-W or BASS-C* PDD-W or BASS-P* PDD-W) or social stress, respectively
   418→(BASS-N* PDD-SS or BASS-C* PDD-SS or BASS-P* PDD-SS). Predictors were grand-
   419→mean centered, while dependent variables were not. To ensure that the cross-level interactions
   420→were due to within- rather than between-person variance in stressors, the within- and between-
   421→person variance in PDD-W and PDD-SS were included as separate predictors. Significant
   422→interactions were probed with simple slopes analyses (i.e., conditional effects at M and ±1 SD
   423→of the moderator).
   424→Finally, to further elucidate the temporal dynamics and variability of the data, four
   425→sets of exploratory, non-preregistered analyses were conducted. First, to test whether stress
   426→beliefs alter the general intra-individual variability of affective responses, standard random
   427→intercept models with equal residual variances were compared to models with continuous
   428→variance structures. These alternative models allowed the residual variance to vary as a
   429→continuous function of stress belief scores. Differences in model fit were evaluated using
   430→likelihood-ratio tests (LRT). Second, to examine prospective associations of regulatory
   431→strategies within a given day, lagged hierarchical linear models were estimated. In these
   432→models, situation selection and response modification at the preceding time point (T-1)
   433→predicted affect and stressors at the subsequent time point (T) within the same day. To yield
   434→robust estimates, these models controlled for time of day as well as the autoregressive effect
   435→(the respective outcome variable at T-1). Furthermore, the within- and between-person
   436→variance in lagged predictors were included as separate predictors. Third, to investigate cross-
   437→day spillover effects, day-lagged hierarchical linear models were estimated using day-level
   438→aggregated variables. In these models, daily stress and affect from the previous day (Day T−1)
   439→predicted the choice of stress regulation strategies (i.e., avoidance and utilization) on the
   440→current day (Day T). To account for potential systematic changes across the study period,
   441→these models included a linear time trend. Analogous to the within-day analyses, models
   442→
   443→18
   444→additionally controlled for the autoregressive effect (the respective regulation strategy on Day
   445→T−1), thereby modeling change in the outcome variable. Again, the day-lagged predictors
   446→were disaggregated into within- and between-person variance components. Fourth, to examine
   447→whether study variables showed systematic change across the study period, exploratory
   448→multilevel growth curve models were estimated using day-level aggregated variables. For
   449→daily workload, social stress, affect, and stress regulation strategies, both linear and quadratic
   450→trends across study days were tested. To account for potential scheduling-related variation,
   451→selected models were additionally adjusted for weekday.
   452→Prior to data collection, target sample size was determined pragmatically. Because
   453→closed-form power solutions for multilevel models—particularly for cross-level
   454→interactions—are limited and simulation-based approaches require credible variance-
   455→component estimates that were unavailable a priori, a conventional two-sided t-test for a
   456→medium effect (r = 0.50, α = .05, 1–β = .80) was used as a planning benchmark. Assuming 20
   457→percent drop-out it was aimed to include 96 participants into the study.
   458→The level of significance was set at p <.05 per hypothesis. To account for alpha error
   459→inflation the level of significance was divided by the number of tests per preregistered
   460→hypothesis (Bonferroni adjustment) in accordance with disjunction hypothesis testing (Rubin,
   461→2021); see Supplement 1 for constituent p-levels for each hypothesis). p-values for the
   462→hierarchical linear models were estimated using Satterthwaite correction. 99%-confidence
   463→intervals were estimated drawing 5000 bootstrap samples. All analyses were performed using
   464→R 4.3.1 (R Core Team, 2023).

   473→Results
   474→Do beliefs about stress predict daily overall levels of stressors, affect and stress
   475→regulation strategies?
   476→Stress beliefs were not prospectively associated with overall occurrence of social
   477→stress or workload, levels of positive and negative affect or levels of situation selection and
   478→response modification (see table 2; for a detailed description of the hierarchical linear models,
   479→see Supplement 2).
   480→Do beliefs about stress moderate the daily affective stress response?
   481→Negative beliefs about stress significantly moderated the within-person association of
   482→daily social stress and positive affect (table 3; for a detailed description of the hierarchical
   483→linear models see Supplement 2): When experiencing social stress, participants who held high
   484→negative beliefs about stress had lower daily positive affect (BASS-N +1SD: simple slope = -
   485→0.82; SE=0.29; t = 2.83, p < .005), whereas for participants who held medium or low negative
   486→beliefs about stress, there was no association between daily social stress and daily positive
   487→affect (BASS-N M: simple slope = -0.22; SE=0.22; t = 0.99; p =.319; BASS-N -1SD: simple
   488→slope = 0.38; SE=0.31; t = 1.19; p =232; see Figure 1A). Moreover, negative beliefs about
   489→stress significantly moderated the within-person association of daily workload and positive
   490→affect (table 3; for a detailed description of the hierarchical linear models see Supplement 2):
   491→When experiencing higher workload, participants who held low or medium negative beliefs
   492→about stress had higher daily positive affect (BASS-N -1SD: simple slope = 0.60; SE=0.17; t =
   493→3.64 p < .003; BASS-N M: simple slope = 0.30; SE=0.11; t = 2.71 p < .007), whereas for
   494→participants with high negative beliefs about stress, there was no association between daily
   495→workload and daily positive affect (BASS-N +1: simple slope = -0.00; SE=0.16; t = 0.03; p
   496→

   498→=.979; see Figure 1B). Further, controllability beliefs (BASS-C) significantly moderated the
   499→within-person association of daily social stress and negative affect (table 3; for a detailed
   500→description of the hierarchical linear models see Supplement 2). When experiencing social
   501→stress, participants with low controllability beliefs had stronger increases in daily negative
   502→affect (BASS-C -1SD: simple slope = 2.73; SE=0.18; t = 14.93 p < .001) than participants
   503→with medium controllability beliefs (BASS-C M: simple slope = 2.17; SE=0.12; t = 18.52 p <
   504→.001) and with high controllability beliefs (BASS-C +1: simple slope = 1.61; SE=0.17; t =

   506→the associations of perceived stressors and affective responses were not significant.
   507→Do beliefs about stress moderate daily stress regulation strategies?
   508→None of the stress belief dimensions significantly moderated the within-person
   509→associations between daily stressors and stress regulation strategies (Table 3; for a detailed
   510→description of the hierarchical linear models see Supplement 2). Specifically, all cross-level
   511→interactions between negative stress beliefs (BASS-N), positive stress beliefs (BASS-P), and
   512→beliefs about the controllability of stress (BASS-C) and daily social or workload stressors
   513→predicting avoidance-based situation selection or attempts to utilize the stress response were
   514→non-significant. Thus, stress beliefs were not associated with systematic differences in daily
   515→stress regulation strategies in response to social or workload stressors.
   516→Exploratory analyses on the temporal dynamics and variability of the data
   517→In addition to mean daily affective stress responses, it was explored whether stress
   518→beliefs systematically alter the day-to-day fluctuations of affective responses. Comparing
   519→standard models with equal residual variances to models with continuous variance structures
   520→(allowing residual variance to vary as a continuous function of stress belief scores) revealed
   521→that negative stress beliefs did not significantly alter the intra-individual residual variability of
   522→affect (see Supplement 2). However, individuals holding stronger positive stress beliefs
   523→(BASS-P) demonstrated significantly less intra-individual variability in daily positive affect
   524→
   525→21
   526→(LRT = 15.81; p < .001, δ = -0.22). Similarly, higher perceived stress controllability (BASS-
   527→C) significantly predicted less intra-individual variability in daily negative affect (LRT =
   528→17.60; p < .001, δ = -0.43). Thus, while negative stress beliefs did not affect the general day-
   529→to-day variability of affect, positive and controllability stress beliefs were systematically
   530→associated with greater emotional stability across days.
   531→Furthermore, exploratory lagged multilevel models were estimated to investigate the
   532→temporal dynamics of stressors, affect, and regulatory strategies within a given day (see
   533→Supplement 2). Across all models, strong autoregressive effects were observed, indicating that
   534→stressors, affective states, and regulatory strategies were highly stable within a day (all ps <
   535→.001). Regarding within-day prospective associations of regulatory strategies, only avoidance-
   536→based situation selection at the within-person level predicted a prospective increase in
   537→negative affect (Estimate = 0.14, p = .035). All other within-day lagged within-person effects
   538→of regulatory strategies on subsequent affect, workload, or social stress were non-significant.
   539→To investigate cross-day spillover effects, day-lagged hierarchical linear models were
   540→estimated predicting current-day stress regulation strategies from prior-day stressors and
   541→affect (see Supplement 2). Across all models, significant autoregressive effects were
   542→observed, indicating moderate stability of regulatory strategies across days (all ps < .001).
   543→Regarding avoidance-based situation selection, no significant within-person prospective
   544→effects of prior-day workload, social stress, positive affect, or negative affect were observed.
   545→In contrast, regarding attempts to utilize the stress response, higher within-person workload
   546→on the previous day significantly predicted a decrease in stress utilization on the subsequent
   547→day (Estimate = -0.26, p < .001). Similarly, higher prior-day positive affect predicted lower
   548→current-day utilization (Estimate = -0.03, p = .037), whereas prior-day social stress and
   549→negative affect showed no significant cross-day effects.

   551→systematic change across the study period using day-level aggregated variables (see
   552→

   554→Supplement 2). A significant positive linear trend was observed for workload across study
   555→days. Utilization also showed an initial positive linear trend, but this effect was no longer
   556→significant after controlling for workload. No consistent linear or quadratic time trends were
   557→found for the remaining variables.
   558→Discussion
   559→Findings from this EMA study indicate that beliefs about stress shape affective
   560→reactivity to daily stressors rather than global levels of stressor exposure or mean affect.
   561→Students endorsing more negative beliefs about stress showed reduced positive affect when
   562→encountering social stress and no increase in positive affect with higher workload. In contrast,
   563→those with low to moderate negative beliefs exhibited no drop in positive affect during social
   564→stress and demonstrated an increased positive affect under higher workload—consistent with a
   565→more challenge-like appraisal of task demands. In addition, lower controllability beliefs were
   566→associated with stronger increases in negative affect in response to social stress compared
   567→with medium and high controllability beliefs.
   568→Beyond acute affective reactivity, exploratory analyses further suggest that stress
   569→beliefs may shape the temporal dynamics of affective experiences. Specifically, positive stress
   570→beliefs were associated with reduced intra-individual variability in positive affect, while
   571→controllability beliefs were linked to reduced variability in negative affect. Importantly, these
   572→effects emerged independently of mean affect levels, suggesting that adaptive stress beliefs
   573→may contribute to emotional stability rather than shifting overall affective tone. One possible
   574→interpretation is that such beliefs attenuate affective fluctuations by reducing reactivity to
   575→stressors (as seen with controllability beliefs) and/or facilitating more efficient recovery
   576→processes.
   577→These results converge with diary and EMA evidence linking stress beliefs to
   578→subsequent affective reactions in university students (Laferton et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2025), in
   579→workers (Daniels et al., 2006; Harris & Daniels, 2005, 2007) and individuals under COVID-
   580→
   581→23
   582→19 confinement (Zeng et al., 2024), as well as laboratory studies showing causal effects of
   583→belief manipulation on affective stress responses (Beltzer et al., 2014; Brooks, 2014; Crum et
   584→al., 2017). Notably, prior diary research has shown that these effects persist even when
   585→controlling for neuroticism (Laferton et al., 2020). Mechanistically, such belief-driven
   586→modulation of affect may help explain links between stress beliefs and long-term health
   587→outcomes, including mortality (Keller et al., 2012; Nabi et al., 2013), given that higher
   588→negative affect and lower positive affect are prospectively associated with poorer health
   589→(Pressman et al., 2018; Ramirez et al., 2025; Salovey et al., 2000; Sin, 2016).
   590→Interestingly, interaction effects between stress beliefs and stressor exposure were
   591→somewhat more pronounced for social stressors than for workload-related demands. This
   592→observation aligns with prior experience-sampling evidence showing that belief-related
   593→moderation effects were more robust for social than for workload stressors when both were
   594→examined concurrently (Laferton et al., 2020). Consistent with this, diary and ambulatory
   595→research indicates that interpersonal stressors tend to elicit stronger affective responses and
   596→are more strongly shaped by relatively stable individual characteristics. Importantly, this does
   597→not imply that workload stressors are unaffected by stress beliefs. Previous experience-
   598→sampling studies have demonstrated belief-related moderation effects in response to work-
   599→related stress (Casper et al., 2017; Daniels et al., 2006), and experimental and applied research
   600→has documented belief-dependent effects under academic and occupational stressors
   601→(e.g.,(Crum et al., 2013; Jamieson et al., 2010; Laferton et al., 2024). Taken together, the
   602→present findings suggest that the relative magnitude and consistency of belief-related
   603→moderation effects may vary as a function of stressor type, with particularly robust effects
   604→observed for social stressors in everyday life. By contrast, no concurrent associations were
   605→observed between stress beliefs and two stress regulation strategies specified by the stress
   606→optimization model (Crum et al., 2020)—situation selection (avoiding a stressful situation)
   607→and response modification (utilize the stress response). There were nominal trends suggesting
   608→
   609→24
   610→that higher positive stress beliefs may be linked to slightly stronger utilization of the stress
   611→response in general and under stressor exposure; however, these trends did not withstand
   612→multiple-comparison control and are therefore reported for transparency only. Targeted
   613→hypothesis tests in adequately powered EMA designs are warranted. To our knowledge, no
   614→prior study investigating stress responses to real life stressors has assessed these two
   615→components separately as formulated in the stress optimization model. However, there is
   616→evidence for an association of stress beliefs with approach-oriented coping assessed via
   617→composite scales, which in aggregate are more comparable to situation selection than to
   618→response utilization. In their EMA study, Casper et al. (2017) found that positive stress beliefs
   619→prospectively predicted higher approach coping on days with anticipated high workload.
   620→Other cross-sectional studies report similar links between stress beliefs and proactive coping
   621→(Keech et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). Moreover, students with more positive stress beliefs
   622→sought more feedback ahead of an anticipated social-evaluative stressor (Crum et al., 2013). A
   623→possible explanation is that situation selection is predominantly an anticipatory process;
   624→consequently, our concurrent (reactive) sampling likely undercaptured it relative to the
   625→anticipatory assessments used by Casper et al. (2017) and Crum et al. (2013). By contrast,
   626→response modification processes are more immediately tied to stressor occurrence. In the
   627→current study, the fixed schedule of three assessments per day may have lacked sensitivity to
   628→detect such in-the-moment changes, as prompts could occur up to four hours after a stressor.
   629→Future studies should employ event-related assessments to more precisely investigate the
   630→relationship between stress beliefs and response modification. In addition, exploratory
   631→analyses suggested that avoidance-based situation selection was associated with higher
   632→negative affect at the following within-day assessment, underlining its maladaptive nature
   633→within the Stress Optimization Model (Crum et al., 2020). Expanding on these temporal
   634→dynamics, cross-day exploratory analyses further indicated that high daily workload and high
   635→positive affect predicted a reduction in stress utilization on the subsequent day, suggesting a
   636→
   637→25
   638→potential reduction in regulatory engagement following high workload and high positive
   639→affect days. Moreover, exploratory growth curve models suggested that workload increased
   640→across study days, whereas the initial increase in utilization appeared to be largely attributable
   641→to this concurrent increase in workload.
   642→Some limitations and constraints on generality need to be considered when
   643→interpreting the results of the present study. First, although recruitment via university e-mail
   644→lists and research participation platforms may have introduced self-selection, several
   645→observations speak against substantial bias: mean stress-belief scores closely matched those
   646→reported in prior student samples; course hours, perceived workload, and social stress fell
   647→within typical student ranges; and the proportion with elevated psychological symptoms
   648→approximated rates observed in student population studies. Nevertheless, as typically seen in
   649→studies using online recruitment, participants predominantly identified as female, thus limiting
   650→the ecological validity for individuals of male gender. Future studies should replicate these
   651→findings in other populations and, ideally, in population-representative samples to more
   652→clearly establish generalizability. Although the EMA design resulted in 1939 data points
   653→among 104 participants, considering the conservative approach to multiple testing, the power
   654→of this study for detecting small effects is limited. Larger studies are needed to investigate
   655→smaller potential effects between stress beliefs and daily stress regulation strategies. The main
   656→strengths of the current study lie in the assessment of naturalistic stress processes on a daily
   657→basis. This design enabled the study of stress beliefs outside of the lab, while at the same time
   658→reducing recall bias compared to studies assessing self-report of stress variables
   659→retrospectively over several days. Specifically, the current study applied an interval contingent
   660→coverage EMA approach, prompting participants to report on stressors, affect and stress
   661→regulation strategies for the preceding 4 hours 3 times a day. This approach was chosen to
   662→minimize the likelihood of missing stressors, which occur less frequently (a common
   663→limitation of random sampling), while also avoiding the moment selection bias inherent to
   664→
   665→26
   666→event-contingent sampling—namely, the high likelihood of participants being unable or
   667→unwilling to self-initiate a survey during acute stress (Stone et al., 2023; Vaessen et al., 2023).
   668→Furthermore, in contrast to purely event-contingent designs, this interval-based approach
   669→enables the assessment of base rates for the variables of interest during non-stressor periods,
   670→which is essential for distinguishing stress-related changes from normal daily variations
   671→(Shiffman et al., 2008). However, this coverage approach introduces potential recall bias, as it
   672→captures short-term retrospective reporting of a 4-hour window rather than strictly momentary
   673→states (Shiffman et al., 2008). Although coverage EMA approaches are frequently used, it
   674→currently remains unclear how participants cognitively aggregate their experiences over such
   675→extended timeframes, meaning some degree of retrospective distortion cannot be completely
   676→ruled out (Stone et al., 2023). To address these methodological trade-offs, future research
   677→could benefit from combining event-contingent sampling with an interval-based coverage
   678→approach. Such a hybrid design would allow for the immediate assessment of acute stress
   679→reactions whenever feasible, while utilizing the coverage intervals as a fallback to capture
   680→missed events and maintain essential base rate assessments. Finally, to minimize participant
   681→burden during the high-frequency sampling protocol, two constraints regarding stressor
   682→measurement should be noted. First, the PDD assessed the extent of stressor occurrence rather
   683→than direct stressor intensity. Although prior research using the PDD indicates that these
   684→occurrence ratings are strongly associated with subjective stress intensity (Laferton et al.,
   685→2020), a direct momentary measure might have provided further insights. Second, as open-
   686→ended qualitative data were not collected, the specific nature of the events that participants
   687→classified as social or workload stressors remains unclear. Future research could incorporate
   688→brief qualitative assessments to disentangle how specific subtypes of stressors might
   689→differentially interact with stress beliefs. Third, as an additional constraint to minimize
   690→participant burden, only one end of the regulatory spectrum was assessed for each stress
   691→regulation strategy. Specifically, situation selection was measured solely via avoidance
   692→
   693→27
   694→(omitting approach-oriented behavior), and response modification was measured solely via
   695→utilization (omitting attempts to minimize the stress response). Future studies should
   696→incorporate the full spectrum of these domains, including approach and minimization
   697→strategies, to capture a more comprehensive picture of stress regulation. A further limitation is
   698→the exclusive reliance on self-reported data. Although the EMA design minimizes
   699→retrospective recall biases, subjective reports of stress regulation and stressor occurrence
   700→remain susceptible to individual reporting biases. Future studies should therefore aim to
   701→incorporate objective behavioral markers, such as digital phenotyping or physiological
   702→correlates, to corroborate these findings.
   703→In conclusion, this seven-day EMA study shows that stress beliefs moderate affective
   704→responses to real-life work and social stressors. Together with prior evidence, these findings
   705→provide compelling convergent support that stress beliefs shape affect under stress —
   706→identifying them as a promising intervention target, particularly given established links
   707→between affect and long-term health. By contrast, we did not observe concurrent associations
   708→with stress regulation strategies; nevertheless, the present results warrant focused,
   709→preregistered tests using EMA designs that separate anticipatory and reactive phases and
   710→incorporate event-related sampling to more sensitively detect potential stress beliefs–stress
   711→regulation pathways.
