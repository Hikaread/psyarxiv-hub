    15→Abstract
    16→How do individuals remain themselves while continuously changing? Contemporary psychological
    17→theories have primarily addressed this question through the concept of self-regulation, emphasizing
    18→the processes through which organisms adapt to internal and environmental demands. However,
    19→adaptation alone does not explain how individuals maintain a coherent sense of self across bodily,
    20→relational, and developmental change.
    21→The present paper proposes a theoretical framework of self-coherence that integrates insights from
    22→embodied cognition, predictive processing, phenomenology, identity research, and constructivist
    23→approaches to personality. Within this framework, self-coherence is conceptualized not as an
    24→additional self-construct, but as a higher-order organizational process through which embodied
    25→experience, identity processes, personal meaning organization, and self-regulatory mechanisms are
    26→dynamically integrated across time. Embodied processes provide a foundational sense of continuity,
    27→identity supports the integration of past, present, and future experiences, and personal meaning
    28→organizations are reinterpreted as strategies through which continuity is maintained despite change.
    29→Building on this perspective, the paper distinguishes self-coherence from self-regulation. Whereas
    30→self-regulation supports adaptive responding, self-coherence concerns the preservation of continuity
    31→and intelligibility across experience. Regulatory processes are therefore understood as mechanisms
    32→serving a more fundamental organizational task: maintaining a coherent sense of self over time.
    33→By positioning self-coherence as a higher-order organizational principle, the proposed framework
    34→offers a conceptual bridge between embodied, developmental, phenomenological, constructivist, and
    35→predictive accounts of selfhood, providing a unified framework for understanding how continuity is
    36→established, maintained, and restored across change.
    37→Keywords: Self-coherence; Temporal continuity; Identity; Personal Meaning Organizations;
    38→Embodied cognition; Predictive processing
    39→Highlights
    40→• Self-coherence is proposed as a higher-order organizational process
    41→• Continuity is framed as the primary organizational challenge of selfhood
    42→• Embodiment, identity, and meaning jointly support self-coherence
    43→• Self-coherence is distinguished conceptually from self-regulation
    44→• A temporal framework integrates diverse theories of selfhood
    45→1. Introduction
    46→The concept of self occupies a central position across psychology, cognitive science, phenomenology,
    47→and psychiatry. Despite decades of theoretical and empirical investigation, however, the nature of
    48→selfhood remains a matter of ongoing debate. Contemporary approaches have highlighted different
    49→dimensions of the self, including bodily experience, interoceptive awareness, predictive processing,
    50→identity development, autobiographical memory, and personal meaning-making. While these
    51→perspectives have generated substantial insights, they often address specific aspects of selfhood in
    52→2
    53→
    54→relative isolation, leaving open the broader question of how these processes contribute to the
    55→experience of being the same person over time.
    56→This question is particularly important because human experience is inherently dynamic. Bodily states
    57→fluctuate, emotions emerge and dissipate, social relationships evolve, and personal circumstances
    58→continuously change. Nevertheless, individuals generally experience themselves as persisting
    59→subjects whose lives unfold as coherent trajectories rather than as disconnected episodes. The
    60→challenge of selfhood is therefore not simply one of stability, but of continuity through change. This
    61→challenge has become increasingly relevant within contemporary psychological science, where
    62→advances in interoception, predictive processing, identity development, and phenomenological
    63→psychopathology have generated complementary yet largely disconnected accounts of how continuity
    64→is established and maintained.
    65→Several contemporary traditions have begun to address different components of this problem.
    66→Embodied and interoceptive accounts emphasize the role of bodily processes in shaping subjectivity
    67→and self-awareness (Craig, 2009; Garfinkel et al., 2015; Tsakiris, 2017). Predictive processing
    68→frameworks conceptualize the self as an inferential process emerging through the continuous
    69→regulation of bodily and environmental uncertainty (Barrett, 2017; Friston, 2010; Seth, 2021; Seth,
    70→2013). Developmental and narrative approaches describe identity as a mechanism through which
    71→individuals integrate past experiences, present commitments, and future aspirations into coherent life
    72→trajectories (Crocetti et al., 2023; Habermas & Bluck, 2000; McAdams & McLean, 2013).
    73→Constructivist traditions emphasize the role of personal meaning in organizing subjective experience
    74→(Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Guidano, 1987; Nardi & Bellantuono, 2008), while phenomenological
    75→approaches have highlighted the temporal structure of consciousness and the continuity of lived
    76→experience as fundamental dimensions of selfhood (Fuchs, 2017; Gallagher,
    77→2005).
    78→Although these traditions differ in their assumptions and levels of analysis, they converge on a
    79→common problem: how individuals maintain a coherent sense of self despite continuous bodily,
    80→relational, and environmental transformation. Existing theories identify mechanisms that contribute
    81→to this process, yet less attention has been devoted to developing an integrative framework capable
    82→of linking these mechanisms within a common organizational perspective.
    83→Importantly, the present framework does not propose self-coherence as an additional component of
    84→selfhood alongside embodiment, identity, or self-regulation. Rather, self-coherence is conceptualized
    85→as a higher-order organizational process through which these distinct dimensions are dynamically
    86→integrated across time. In this sense, self-coherence is not another element of the self, but a framework
    87→for understanding how multiple self-related processes collectively support continuity despite change.
    88→Although existing approaches have explained continuity from distinct levels of analysis—including
    89→bodily, narrative, relational, and regulatory perspectives—the present framework proposes that
    90→continuity itself constitutes the primary organizational problem of selfhood, with self-coherence
    91→representing the higher-order process through which these diverse mechanisms are integrated across
    92→time.
    93→Here we propose that self-coherence represents such a principle. Drawing on phenomenological,
    94→embodied, developmental, predictive, and constructivist perspectives, we define self-coherence as the
    95→dynamic capacity to maintain continuity and intelligibility of experience across time through the
    96→integration of embodied, affective, relational, and autobiographical processes. From this perspective,
    97→selfhood is neither a fixed entity nor a purely cognitive representation. Rather, it emerges as a
    98→temporally extended process through which continuity is preserved despite ongoing transformation.

   101→This formulation shifts attention from the question of what the self is to the question of how the self
   102→is maintained. We argue that bodily regulation, predictive inference, identity formation, interpersonal
   103→relationships, and personal meaning organization can all be understood as complementary
   104→mechanisms supporting the preservation of continuity across time. In particular, we propose that
   105→Personal Meaning Organizations may be reinterpreted as strategies of temporal self-maintenance and
   106→that self-regulation itself can be understood as serving a more fundamental organizational goal: the
   107→maintenance of self-coherence.
   108→The paper develops this argument in four steps. First, we conceptualize self-coherence as the primary
   109→adaptive challenge of selfhood. Second, we examine the embodied and relational foundations of
   110→continuity through interoception, predictive processing, and interpersonal regulation. Third, we
   111→discuss identity and Personal Meaning Organizations as complementary mechanisms of temporal
   112→self-maintenance. Finally, we distinguish self-coherence from self-regulation and argue that many
   113→regulatory processes may be understood as serving the broader task of preserving continuity of
   114→experience.
   115→By positioning self-coherence as a higher-order organizational principle, the proposed framework
   116→seeks to establish a conceptual bridge between phenomenological, embodied, developmental,
   117→constructivist, and predictive approaches to selfhood. More broadly, it offers a unified perspective on
   118→one of the most fundamental questions in psychological science: how individuals remain themselves
   119→while continuously changing.
   120→2. Self-Coherence as the Primary Organizational Challenge of Selfhood
   121→Traditional psychological approaches have often conceptualized selfhood in terms of cognitive
   122→representations, self-concepts, personality traits, or autobiographical narratives. While these
   123→perspectives have generated important insights, they often assume the existence of a coherent self
   124→rather than addressing the more fundamental question of how such coherence is continuously
   125→achieved and maintained.
   126→We propose that self-coherence constitutes the primary organizational problem that psychological
   127→systems must solve. Before individuals can regulate behavior, pursue goals, construct identities, or
   128→engage in social relationships, they must maintain continuity of experience across time. Selfhood
   129→therefore emerges not as a fixed entity but as an ongoing process through which continuity is
   130→preserved despite constant change.
   131→The idea that continuity constitutes a fundamental condition of selfhood has deep roots in
   132→phenomenological philosophy and psychiatry, both of which have emphasized the temporal
   133→organization of experience as a core feature of subjectivity.
   134→This perspective finds important roots in phenomenological analyses of consciousness. Husserl
   135→argued that conscious experience is never confined to isolated moments. Every present experience
   136→contains traces of what has just occurred (retention) and anticipations of what is about to occur
   137→(protention). Subjective experience therefore emerges through a continuous temporal synthesis
   138→linking past, present, and future. The self persists not because it remains unchanged but because
   139→experience is continuously integrated into a coherent temporal flow (Fuchs, 2017; Husserl, 1991).
   140→More recently, phenomenological psychiatry has emphasized the centrality of temporal continuity for
   141→psychological functioning. According to Fuchs, disturbances of selfhood frequently involve
   142→disruptions in the temporal organization of experience, resulting in difficulties integrating past
   143→experiences, present circumstances, and future possibilities into a coherent trajectory. From this
   144→4
   145→
   146→perspective, psychopathology can often be understood not simply as a disturbance of cognition or
   147→emotion, but as a disruption of experiential continuity itself (Fuchs, 2013). These phenomenological
   148→perspectives suggest that continuity is not simply one feature of selfhood among others, but a
   149→fundamental condition for the experience of being a persisting subject across time (Fuchs, 2017).
   150→The notion of self-coherence proposed here builds upon these insights while extending them into
   151→contemporary psychological theory. Self-coherence may be defined as the capacity to maintain
   152→experiential continuity across time through the dynamic integration of embodied, affective, relational,
   153→and autobiographical processes. Rather than representing a static property of personality, self-
   154→coherence constitutes an ongoing process through which individuals preserve continuity while
   155→simultaneously adapting to change.
   156→Accordingly, self-coherence should not be understood as a separate psychological subsystem or trait-
   157→like characteristic. Rather, it refers to the organizational process through which multiple dimensions
   158→of selfhood are coordinated into a sufficiently continuous and intelligible experience across time.
   159→Similar intuitions have emerged within constructivist clinical formulations, where psychological
   160→suffering has been described as arising when continuity among experiences, emotions, and self-
   161→perceptions becomes fragmented, requiring the reconstruction of a more integrated sense of self (De
   162→Marchis & Barca, 2014). From this perspective, psychopathology may be understood not only as the
   163→presence of symptoms, but also as a disruption in the processes through which experience remains
   164→coherent and intelligible over time.
   165→Importantly, this definition shifts the focus from stability to continuity. Psychological functioning
   166→does not require the self to remain unchanged. Rather, it requires sufficient continuity to allow
   167→individuals to experience themselves as the same person while simultaneously adapting to changing
   168→internal and external conditions. The adaptive challenge is therefore not permanence but coherence
   169→through transformation.
   170→From this perspective, self-regulation, emotion regulation, identity formation, and meaning-making
   171→can all be understood as mechanisms serving a more fundamental organizational goal: preserving
   172→continuity of experience across time. Self-coherence should not be confused with rigidity,
   173→consistency, or resistance to change. On the contrary, self-coherence refers to the capacity to preserve
   174→continuity while accommodating transformation. A coherent self is not a static self, but one that
   175→remains intelligible despite ongoing change.
   176→Whereas self-continuity typically refers to the subjective experience of being the same person over
   177→time, self-coherence refers to the broader organizational processes through which such continuity is
   178→constructed, maintained, and restored.
   179→3. Embodied and Relational Foundations of Self-Coherence
   180→If self-coherence represents a temporally extended process, an important question concerns the
   181→mechanisms through which continuity is achieved. Contemporary embodied, developmental, and
   182→predictive approaches suggest that the foundations of selfhood lie not primarily in abstract cognitive
   183→representations but in the ongoing regulation, interpretation, and integration of bodily experience.
   184→Embodied theories propose that subjective experience is fundamentally grounded in bodily processes
   185→(Craig, 2009; Gallagher, 2005; Tsakiris, 2017). Recent constructionist, predictive, and network
   186→physiology approaches further suggest that interoceptive processes do not merely provide information
   187→about physiological states but actively contribute to the integration of bodily, affective, and cognitive
   188→5
   189→
   190→dimensions of experience (Barrett, 2017; Candia-Rivera et al., 2024). Heartbeat, respiration, visceral
   191→sensations, fatigue, hunger, and bodily arousal constitute recurring features of experience that
   192→accompany the individual throughout life. Emerging evidence suggests that such processes may also
   193→contribute to the temporal organization of experience by linking bodily states to memories of the past,
   194→awareness of the present, and expectations about the future (Klamut & Weissenberger, 2026). Recent
   195→findings further indicate that ongoing visceral activity contributes to perception, attention, self-related
   196→processing, and the coordination of neural and cognitive dynamics (Rebollo et al., 2018; Tallon-
   197→Baudry et al., 2018, 2026). Continuity therefore emerges not from bodily sensations alone, but from
   198→their ongoing integration within predictive and meaning-making processes.
   199→At the same time, the body is not merely a source of continuity. It is also one of the primary sources
   200→of change in self-experience (Fuchs, 2017; Gallagher, 2005). Across development, individuals must
   201→continuously adapt to bodily transformations associated with growth, puberty, pregnancy,
   202→parenthood, aging, illness, and reproductive transitions. These changes often involve profound
   203→modifications in physiological regulation, emotional functioning, social roles, and self-understanding
   204→(Barrett, 2017; Fotopoulou & Tsakiris, 2017; Tsakiris, 2017). They also require individuals to revise
   205→and integrate new experiences into evolving life narratives and identities (Crocetti et al., 2023;
   206→Luyckx et al., 2006; McAdams & McLean, 2013; McLean & Syed, 2015).
   207→The challenge of selfhood therefore lies not in preserving continuity through an unchanging body,
   208→but in maintaining continuity despite ongoing bodily transformation. Adolescence, parenthood,
   209→menopause, aging, and illness all require individuals to incorporate changing bodily experiences into
   210→evolving models of who they are and who they are becoming. Embodiment contributes to self-
   211→coherence precisely because bodily change must be continuously integrated into a coherent sense of
   212→self. Recent phenomenological accounts similarly emphasize the reciprocal relationship between
   213→embodiment and narrativity, suggesting that bodily experience supports narrative continuity while
   214→simultaneously being interpreted and transformed through narrative self-understanding (Tanaka et
   215→al., 2026).
   216→Predictive processing and active inference frameworks provide a computational account of how such
   217→continuity may emerge (Friston, 2010; Hirsh, 2026; Seth, 2021). According to these models, the brain
   218→continuously generates predictions regarding bodily and environmental states and updates these
   219→predictions through incoming sensory information. Selfhood is therefore not a static representation
   220→but an active process of inference through which the organism continuously models its own bodily
   221→condition. From this perspective, continuity depends not only on the availability of bodily signals,
   222→but also on the capacity to generate relatively stable expectations about oneself across changing
   223→circumstances.
   224→Recent theoretical developments have extended this view by proposing that identity and self-
   225→experience themselves may be understood as temporally extended inferential processes aimed at
   226→maintaining coherent self-models across time (Hirsh, 2026). When such embodied self-models
   227→become disrupted, bodily sensations may lose their familiar meaning and predictability, contributing
   228→to disturbances in self-experience and continuity. Disturbances in interoceptive processing may
   229→therefore challenge not only bodily awareness but also the continuity through which individuals
   230→understand themselves across time (Barca et al., 2023; Barca & Pezzulo, 2020). Consistent with this
   231→view, recent phenomenological accounts of depersonalization suggest that disruptions in embodied
   232→temporal organization may contribute to experiences of estrangement from oneself and a diminished
   233→sense of continuity (Alekseeva & Ciaunica, 2025).
   234→The relationship between embodiment and selfhood is further enriched by developmental and
   235→relational perspectives. According to these approaches, bodily selfhood emerges not in isolation but
   236→6
   237→
   238→through interpersonal processes of affective regulation and embodied interaction (Fotopoulou &
   239→Tsakiris, 2017). Early caregiver–infant relationships provide a context within which bodily states
   240→become predictable, interpretable, and meaningful. The infant's developing experience of self is
   241→therefore shaped not only by internal bodily signals but also by their co-regulation within
   242→interpersonal relationships.
   243→This relational perspective suggests that self-coherence is initially co-constructed before becoming
   244→an individual capacity. Through repeated experiences of attunement, affective touch, and caregiver
   245→responsiveness, bodily sensations become associated with regulation, significance, and predictability.
   246→As a result, embodied continuity is established not only through physiological regulation but also
   247→through interpersonal experience. Emerging evidence further suggests that affective touch plays a
   248→particularly important role in shaping bodily self-awareness, emotional regulation, and social
   249→connectedness, thereby contributing to the development of coherent embodied self-experience
   250→(Crucianelli et al., 2018; Kirsch et al., 2018).
   251→Recent embodied accounts have further emphasized the role of movement and action in the
   252→construction of selfhood. From this perspective, self-experience emerges through ongoing
   253→interactions between bodily states, sensorimotor engagement, and predictive self-models (Barca,
   254→2025; Vaisvaser, 2025). Continuity is therefore not only experienced through the body but also
   255→enacted through the organism's active engagement with the world.
   256→Taken together, these perspectives suggest that self-coherence emerges through the dynamic
   257→integration of bodily regulation, predictive processing, movement, and interpersonal experience. The
   258→body provides a continuously evolving foundation through which experience remains anchored, while
   259→relational and predictive processes contribute to the interpretation and integration of change across
   260→time.
   261→However, bodily continuity alone cannot fully account for the complexity of human selfhood. Across
   262→development, individuals must also integrate changing bodily experiences into broader systems of
   263→identity, meaning, and autobiographical understanding. This observation raises a further question:
   264→how are changing experiences, relationships, and life transitions organized into a coherent sense of
   265→self over time?
   266→4. Identity and Personal Meaning Organizations as Mechanisms of Temporal Self-Maintenance
   267→While embodiment provides a foundation for continuity, bodily regulation alone cannot account for
   268→the complexity and stability of human selfhood. Human beings do not simply experience bodily
   269→continuity; they also construct coherent narratives, commitments, values, and meanings that extend
   270→across years and even decades. This higher level of organization is typically described through the
   271→concept of identity.
   272→Developmental and narrative approaches conceptualize identity as a process through which
   273→individuals integrate experiences into coherent life trajectories. Identity formation allows connections
   274→between past experiences, present commitments, and future aspirations, thereby supporting continuity
   275→across developmental transitions (Crocetti et al., 2023; Habermas & Bluck, 2000; McAdams &
   276→McLean, 2013). Rather than representing a fixed structure, identity constitutes an ongoing process of
   277→temporal integration.
   278→Phenomenological and constructivist perspectives converge with this view while emphasizing the
   279→embodied and relational nature of identity. Stanghellini and colleagues (Stanghellini et al., 2012) have
   280→argued that disturbances of identity often involve alterations in bodily self-experience, including
   281→7
   282→
   283→experiences of estrangement, objectification, and excessive dependence on external validation.
   284→Similarly, (Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009) conceptualize identity as emerging through the dynamic
   285→relationship between self-experience and lived situations. Identity therefore develops not
   286→independently of embodiment or interpersonal experience, but through their continuous integration
   287→within broader structures of meaning.
   288→From this perspective, identity may be understood as a mechanism of temporal integration through
   289→which embodied experiences become organized into coherent self-trajectories. Identity links bodily
   290→experience to autobiographical memory, interpersonal relationships, future projects, and personal
   291→values. Its primary function is not merely to answer the question "Who am I?", but to provide a
   292→coherent framework through which past experiences, present circumstances, and anticipated futures
   293→can be integrated into a meaningful whole.
   294→However, identity alone does not fully explain how continuity is maintained. Individuals may possess
   295→different identities, values, and autobiographical narratives, yet face a common adaptive challenge:
   296→preserving a coherent sense of self despite ongoing bodily, relational, and environmental change.
   297→Importantly, identity is neither singular nor immutable. Across the lifespan, individuals occupy
   298→multiple identity positions that evolve in response to developmental transitions and life events. A
   299→person may simultaneously identify as a parent, professional, partner, friend, or caregiver, while the
   300→relative salience of these identities changes over time. Major life transitions often require the
   301→incorporation of new identity dimensions into existing self-organizations. For example, the transition
   302→to parenthood requires individuals to integrate a new parental identity with previously established
   303→professional, relational, and personal commitments. The challenge is not merely acquiring a new
   304→identity, but reorganizing one's sense of self so that new and existing identities can coexist within a
   305→coherent life trajectory.
   306→Similarly, developmental transitions may challenge continuity even when identity itself remains
   307→intact. Parents experiencing the transition commonly described as the "empty nest" do not lose their
   308→identity as mothers or fathers when their children become autonomous and leave home. Rather, they
   309→may struggle to reorganize continuity when a central source of meaning, reciprocity, and daily
   310→structure is transformed. The psychological challenge is therefore not simply one of identity, but of
   311→integrating changing relational roles into a coherent and evolving sense of self.
   312→These examples suggest an important distinction. Identity primarily concerns the content of self-
   313→definition—who one is at a given moment in time. Self-coherence, in contrast, refers to the dynamic
   314→process through which changing identities, experiences, relationships, and life circumstances are
   315→integrated into a sufficiently continuous sense of self. Whereas identity answers the question "Who
   316→am I?", self-coherence addresses the broader question "How do I remain myself while who I am
   317→continues to change?"
   318→This distinction raises a further question. If identity contents continuously evolve across development,
   319→what allows individuals to preserve continuity despite these transformations? Identity describes what
   320→is integrated, but does not fully explain how such integration is achieved and maintained over time.
   321→This question points toward a broader level of psychological organization concerned not with the
   322→content of self-definition, but with the processes through which continuity is preserved across
   323→changing experiences. Not all individuals maintain continuity in the same way. Understanding these
   324→individual differences has been a central concern of constructivist approaches to personality.
   325→Constructivist approaches offer a useful perspective on this issue through Guidano's concept of
   326→Personal Meaning Organizations (PMOs). Traditionally described as relatively stable organizations
   327→8
   328→
   329→of emotional processing, personal meaning, and self-definition (Guidano, 1991; Guidano & Liotti,
   330→1983), PMOs have subsequently been elaborated within post-rationalist and phenomenological
   331→traditions as organizational frameworks through which individuals structure their experience of
   332→themselves and the world (Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Nardi & Bellantuono, 2008).
   333→Importantly, these individual differences are not conceptualized as pathological categories. Rather,
   334→they reflect alternative organizational styles through which individuals construct and maintain
   335→continuity of self-experience. Contemporary reinterpretations of PMOs have increasingly emphasized
   336→dimensions such as inward- and outward-oriented modes of self-organization, highlighting
   337→differences in the sources of information individuals rely upon to maintain a coherent sense of self
   338→(Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Nardi & Bellantuono, 2008)
   339→From this perspective, some individuals may achieve continuity primarily through the interpretation
   340→of internal bodily and emotional experiences, whereas others may rely more heavily on interpersonal
   341→recognition, social feedback, and relational contexts. These organizational differences are not
   342→inherently maladaptive. Rather, they represent alternative pathways through which self-coherence is
   343→maintained.
   344→Such pathways may influence not only vulnerability to specific life events but also the domains in
   345→which individuals seek meaning, fulfillment, and stability. Individuals whose sense of continuity is
   346→strongly grounded in interpersonal attunement and relational recognition may, in some cases, be
   347→drawn toward caregiving, teaching, counseling, healthcare, or other forms of engagement centered on
   348→human relationships. Others may derive coherence more strongly from personal mastery, autonomy,
   349→intellectual consistency, or the understanding of internal experience, leading them to pursue different
   350→forms of personal and professional engagement. Over time, these recurring preferences may
   351→contribute to characteristic life trajectories through which continuity is actively constructed and
   352→maintained. In this sense, organizational styles may shape not only how individuals respond to
   353→change, but also the kinds of environments, relationships, and life projects through which they seek
   354→coherence.
   355→At the same time, different pathways may be associated with different vulnerabilities. Organizational
   356→styles relying predominantly on external sources of validation may be particularly challenged by
   357→relational disruptions, rejection, or changes in social roles. Conversely, styles grounded more strongly
   358→in bodily and subjective experience may be especially sensitive to bodily transformations, illness, or
   359→disturbances in interoceptive processing. Personal meaning organizations therefore do not determine
   360→psychopathology directly; rather, they shape the conditions under which continuity becomes
   361→vulnerable and requires reorganization.
   362→The same organizational pathways that support continuity during periods of stability may become
   363→sources of vulnerability during developmental transitions that challenge the forms of experience on
   364→which continuity has traditionally relied. However, adaptive functioning may depend not only on the
   365→capacity to maintain continuity through established pathways, but also on the flexibility to reorganize
   366→continuity when those pathways become insufficient. Developmental transitions often require
   367→individuals to identify and integrate new sources of stability, meaning, and self-definition. In this
   368→sense, self-coherence is not achieved through the preservation of fixed forms of experience, but
   369→through the capacity to maintain continuity while reorganizing the foundations upon which continuity
   370→depends.
   371→This perspective reframes psychopathology not primarily as the failure of a particular organizational
   372→style, but as a difficulty in reorganizing continuity when previously reliable sources of self-coherence
   373→become unavailable.

   376→From the perspective developed here, PMOs may be understood as relatively stable organizational
   377→strategies through which individuals preferentially construct and maintain self-coherence across
   378→development. Although these organizational tendencies display a degree of stability across the
   379→lifespan, they should not be understood as fixed or immutable structures. Major developmental
   380→transitions, life events, and changing relational contexts may require individuals to reorganize the
   381→sources of continuity upon which self-coherence depends. Accordingly, continuity depends not only
   382→on organizational stability, but also on the capacity to reorganize established pathways of self-
   383→coherence in response to change. Rather than defining who individuals are, they shape how continuity
   384→is achieved by privileging particular sources of stability, such as bodily experience, interpersonal
   385→recognition, autobiographical narratives, or relational predictability. Identity provides a narrative
   386→answer to the question of who one is, whereas PMOs organize the pathways through which continuity
   387→is preserved despite change. Together, they support the maintenance of self-coherence across the
   388→lifespan.

   431→Although the processes described above have traditionally been studied within distinct theoretical
   432→traditions, they converge on a common adaptive function: the maintenance of self-coherence. This
   433→observation invites a reconsideration of one of psychology's central concepts—self-regulation.
   434→5. From Self-Regulation to Self-Coherence
   435→Taken together, the present framework suggests that self-coherence does not arise from any single
   436→psychological function. Rather, it emerges through the dynamic integration of embodied regulation,
   437→identity processes, and personal meaning organization within a temporally extended field of
   438→experience.
   439→The concept of self-regulation occupies a central position in contemporary psychological science.
   440→Across developmental psychology, personality theory, affective science, and clinical psychology,
   441→adaptive functioning is frequently described in terms of an individual's capacity to regulate emotions,
   442→11
   443→
   444→behaviors, thoughts, motivations, and physiological states. Psychological well-being is often
   445→associated with effective regulation, whereas psychopathology is conceptualized as a consequence of
   446→dysregulation.
   447→Although highly productive, this perspective may overlook a more fundamental organizational
   448→problem. Regulation presupposes the existence of a system whose continuity must first be maintained.
   449→Before individuals can regulate their behavior, they must preserve a sufficiently coherent experience
   450→of themselves as persisting subjects across time.

   472→Self-regulation refers to processes that modulate behavior, affect, cognition, and physiology in
   473→response to changing environmental and internal demands (Carver & Scheier, 2001). Its primary
   474→function is adaptation. Self-coherence, in contrast, refers to the maintenance of continuity and
   475→intelligibility across experience (Fuchs, 2017). Its primary function is not adaptation per se, but the
   476→preservation of a coherent sense of self despite ongoing change.
   477→This distinction becomes particularly evident during major life transitions. Consider an individual
   478→who loses a long-term relationship, retires from a valued professional role, or develops a chronic
   479→illness. In each case, successful adaptation requires more than behavioral or emotional regulation.
   480→Individuals must also re-establish continuity between who they were, who they are becoming, and
   481→how they understand their future. The central challenge is therefore not simply regulating emotional
   482→responses to change, but preserving coherence in the face of transformation.
   483→From this perspective, self-regulation and self-coherence represent distinct but complementary levels
   484→of analysis. Self-regulation explains how individuals respond to challenges; self-coherence explains
   485→how they remain themselves while doing so.
   486→12
   487→
   488→Importantly, these functions are deeply interconnected. Regulatory processes often contribute to the
   489→maintenance of self-coherence, while threats to self-coherence frequently trigger regulatory
   490→responses. Nevertheless, the distinction remains theoretically significant because it shifts the
   491→explanatory focus from what behavior accomplishes to what behavior protects.
   492→Many actions traditionally interpreted as regulatory strategies may be reinterpreted as attempts to
   493→preserve continuity of self-experience (Hirsh, 2026). Consider an individual who, following
   494→retirement, actively invests in new social activities, hobbies, or community roles. Such behaviors may
   495→regulate uncertainty and emotional distress, but they also help restore continuity by connecting past
   496→competencies and values with new forms of engagement. Self-regulation therefore contributes not
   497→only to adaptation, but also to the reconstruction of self-coherence following major life transitions.
   498→Individuals do not merely regulate anxiety, sadness, uncertainty, or physiological arousal; they
   499→regulate experiences that threaten the coherence of their self-models.
   500→This perspective offers a novel interpretation of uncertainty within predictive processing and active
   501→inference frameworks. Existing models suggest that organisms continuously strive to minimize
   502→uncertainty and maintain adaptive control over bodily and environmental states (Friston, 2010; A. K.
   503→Seth & Friston, 2016). However, uncertainty may become psychologically significant not merely
   504→because it impairs prediction, but because it threatens continuity. This interpretation is consistent with
   505→previous proposals suggesting that interoceptive uncertainty may contribute to disturbances in self-
   506→experience and motivate compensatory attempts to restore stability and predictability (Barca et al.,
   507→2023; Barca & Pezzulo, 2020). When individuals can no longer generate reliable expectations about
   508→themselves, their relationships, or their future trajectories, uncertainty becomes not only a cognitive
   509→challenge but also a challenge to selfhood. This is particularly evident during periods of major
   510→transition, loss, illness, or interpersonal disruption, when previously stable self-models become
   511→difficult to sustain.
   512→The resulting disruption affects not only behavioral adaptation but also the intelligibility of experience
   513→itself. From this perspective, uncertainty reduction can be understood as a means of preserving self-
   514→coherence rather than an end in itself.
   515→This formulation also helps explain why maladaptive behaviors often persist despite their apparent
   516→costs. Restrictive eating, compulsive rituals, excessive reassurance seeking, symptom monitoring,
   517→hypervigilance toward bodily sensations, or rigid interpersonal patterns may provide temporary
   518→experiences of predictability, stability, and continuity. Although such strategies frequently impair
   519→long-term adaptation, they may nevertheless be maintained because they protect continuity when
   520→ordinary mechanisms of self-coherence become fragile.
   521→In this sense, individuals may not simply regulate anxiety, sadness, or uncertainty; they may be
   522→attempting to regulate disruptions in the continuity of self-experience. Their persistence may therefore
   523→derive less from their regulatory effectiveness than from their capacity to provide temporary solutions
   524→to crises of self-coherence.
   525→Such a view resonates with phenomenological accounts of psychopathology, which frequently
   526→describe mental disorders as disruptions of experiential continuity and selfhood (Fuchs, 2013, 2017;
   527→Stanghellini et al., 2012), as well as with constructivist perspectives emphasizing the preservation of
   528→personal identity and meaning structures (Arciero & Bondolfi, 2009; Guidano, 1991). It also extends
   529→contemporary predictive and embodied theories by suggesting that the ultimate adaptive challenge is
   530→not merely reducing prediction error or regulating bodily states, but maintaining a coherent model of
   531→oneself across time.
   532→13
   533→
   534→From this perspective, self-regulation may be understood as one of several mechanisms serving a
   535→more fundamental organizational goal: the maintenance of self-coherence. Regulatory processes help
   536→individuals adapt to changing circumstances, but their broader psychological significance lies in
   537→supporting continuity across experience. The primary task of the self is therefore not merely to
   538→regulate behavior, emotion, or physiology, but to maintain continuity of experience while adapting to
   539→an ever-changing world.
   540→6. Future Directions
   541→The present framework also generates testable hypotheses regarding individual differences in the
   542→sources of continuity that individuals prioritize. Future research could examine whether disruptions
   543→in self-coherence are associated with distinct patterns of interoceptive processing, identity integration,
   544→autobiographical reasoning, and interpersonal functioning. Such investigations may help clarify
   545→whether self-coherence represents a transdiagnostic process linking multiple levels of psychological
   546→organization.
   547→A second challenge concerns the role of self-coherence in psychological adaptation and
   548→psychopathology. The proposed model suggests that disturbances in self-coherence may represent a
   549→transdiagnostic source of vulnerability, whereas therapeutic change may involve the restoration of
   550→continuity across embodied, relational, and autobiographical dimensions of experience. Longitudinal
   551→and process-oriented methodologies may be particularly useful for examining these hypotheses and
   552→for clarifying how self-coherence develops, fluctuates, and reorganizes across the lifespan.
   553→Future research should investigate whether self-coherence can be empirically modeled as a higher-
   554→order organizational process integrating constructs traditionally studied in isolation, including
   555→embodiment, identity, autobiographical reasoning, interpersonal functioning, and self-regulation.
   556→Such work will also need to determine the extent to which self-coherence can be empirically
   557→distinguished from related constructs such as self-continuity, identity integration, and self-concept
   558→clarity, thereby clarifying its unique contribution to contemporary theories of selfhood.
   559→7. Conclusion
   560→Existing theories have tended to explain continuity from a single level of analysis, whether bodily,
   561→cognitive, narrative, relational, or regulatory. The perspective advanced here suggests that continuity
   562→is not the outcome of any single psychological process. Rather, it emerges from the dynamic
   563→coordination of embodied, relational, narrative, and meaning-making systems across time.
   564→Accordingly, self-coherence is conceptualized as a fundamental organizational principle of selfhood.
   565→Rather than viewing the self as a static entity, personality trait, or cognitive representation, this model
   566→conceives selfhood as a temporally extended process through which continuity is maintained across
   567→changing bodily, emotional, relational, and environmental conditions.
   568→Within this view, embodiment, predictive processing, identity formation, personal meaning
   569→organization, and self-regulation represent complementary mechanisms operating at different levels
   570→of analysis. Self-coherence does not constitute an additional component of selfhood. Instead, it refers
   571→to the higher-order organizational process through which these mechanisms are integrated, allowing
   572→continuity to be established, maintained, and restored across changing circumstances.
   573→By bringing together phenomenological, embodied, predictive, developmental, and constructivist
   574→perspectives, the proposed model offers a common framework for understanding how individuals
   575→preserve a coherent sense of self while continuously adapting to change. Psychological adaptation
   576→14
   577→
   578→therefore depends not only on maintaining continuity, but also on the capacity to reorganize the
   579→sources of continuity when developmental transitions, relational transformations, or embodied
   580→changes challenge established forms of self-experience.
   581→From this perspective, the fundamental task of the self is not to resist change, but to remain coherent
   582→through it.
