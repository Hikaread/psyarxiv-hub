    13→    13→Abstract
    14→    14→Purpose
    15→    15→The study aimed to estimate the prevalence of social disconnection, depression, and anxiety
    16→    16→among Slovak mothers on parental leave. The secondary objective was to assess the
    17→    17→contribution of perceived loneliness and social isolation to depressive symptoms among
    18→    18→mothers.
    19→    19→
    20→    20→Methods
    21→    21→The final sample consisted of 410 mothers with a mean age of M = 33.4 and SD = 4.61. Data
    22→    22→were collected online via battery of standardized self-report questionnaires, focused on
    23→    23→depression, anxiety, perceived loneliness, and social isolation.
    24→    24→Results
    25→    25→The prevalence rates of moderately severe to severe depression (PHQ-9 ≥ 10) and anxiety
    26→    26→(GAD-7 ≥ 10) were 38.3% and 28.3%, respectively. The co-occurrence of both conditions
    27→    27→was observed in 23.2% of mothers. Elevated levels of loneliness were reported by 78.3% of
    28→    28→participants (UCLA-3 ≥ 6), while 26.8% were classified as socially isolated (LSNS-6 < 12),
    29→    29→indicating a high prevalence of both loneliness and social isolation in this sample. Regression
    30→    30→analysis showed that anxiety and loneliness were significant positive predictors of depressive
    31→    31→symptoms, with the models explaining 61–62% of the variance.
    32→    32→Conclusion
    33→    33→The result of the study revealed elevated rates of depression and anxiety among mothers on
    34→    34→parental leave. Psychological factors, particularly anxiety, loneliness, and social isolation,
    35→    35→played a more prominent role in explaining depressive symptoms than demographic variables,
    36→    36→including length of parental leave. The findings underscore the need for systematic screening
    37→    37→and accessible perinatal mental health care among mothers on parental leave.
    38→    38→Key words:
    39→    39→loneliness, social isolation, depression, anxiety, motherhood, postpartum, parental leave
    40→    40→Highlights:
    41→    41→• 38.3% of mothers on parental leave reported elevated depressive symptoms.
    42→    42→• 28.3% reported elevated anxiety symptoms; 23.2% had both depression and anxiety.
    43→    43→
    44→    44→• 78.3% reported loneliness; 26.8% were at risk of social isolation.
    45→    45→• Anxiety and loneliness were the strongest predictors of depressive symptoms.
    46→    46→• High psychological burden persisted throughout prolonged parental leave.
    47→    47→Introduction
    48→    48→Pregnancy and childbirth are major life events accompanied by profound psychological,
    49→    49→social, and biological changes. The transition to motherhood presents substantial challenges
    50→    50→across these domains (Saxbe et al. 2018; Olza et al. 2020). The perinatal period is also
    51→    51→recognized as a critical window for the onset or recurrence of common mental disorders,
    52→    52→particularly the most prevalent conditions such as depression and anxiety (Howard et al. 2014;
    53→    53→Jones et al. 2014; Fish-Williamson and Hahn-Holbrook 2023). Postpartum depression affects
    54→    54→approximately 19% of women during the first year after childbirth, although prevalence
    55→    55→estimates vary widely across studies (3%-44%) (Shorey et al. 2018; Motrico et al. 2022; Fish-
    56→    56→Williamson and Hahn-Holbrook 2023). Similarly, anxiety symptoms have been reported in
    57→    57→18% to 25% of women during pregnancy and in 15% to 21% in the postpartum period
    58→    58→(Dennis et al. 2017; Fawcett et al. 2019). Given their high prevalence and substantial impact,
    59→    59→these disorders represent a major public health concern. They are associated with adverse
    60→    60→pregnancy and birth outcomes (Rejnö et al. 2019; Jahan et al. 2021), impaired maternal
    61→    61→functioning, difficulties in the mother–infant relationship, poorer emotional and cognitive
    62→    62→development in offspring, greater behavioral and emotional problems in children (Slomian et
    63→    63→al. 2019; Saharoy et al. 2023; MacBeth et al. 2026), and increased health care utilization
    64→    64→(Pollack et al. 2022). From a socioeconomic perspective, severe perinatal mental health
    65→    65→conditions may increase caregiving demands on partners, contributing to income loss and
    66→    66→greater financial vulnerability for the family (British Red Cross 2016).
    67→    67→
    68→    68→Beyond depression and anxiety, the perinatal period is also characterized by increased
    69→    69→vulnerability to social disconnection, encompassing both the subjective experience of
    70→    70→loneliness and the objective state of social isolation. Both are closely linked to maternal
    71→    71→mental health and may both contribute to and exacerbate psychological distress and adverse
    72→    72→outcomes (Luoma et al. 2015; Kent-Marvick et al. 2022). In countries where young adults
    73→    73→often relocate for higher education or employment, internal mobility may coincide with the
    74→    74→transition to parenthood, potentially reduce social networks, decrease perceived social
    75→    75→support, and increase feelings of loneliness (Kent-Marvick et al. 2022). A British Red Cross
    76→    76→(2016) survey found that 32% of young parents reported feeling lonely "all the time" or
    77→    77→"often" during maternity leave. Across studies, loneliness has been reported in approximately
    78→    78→32%-42% of pregnant women and mothers, although prevalence estimates vary substantially
    79→    79→because of differences in study populations, measurement approaches, and sociocultural
    80→    80→contexts (Kent-Marvick et al. 2022). Maternal loneliness has been associated with greater
    81→    81→fatigue, stress, lower parental self-efficacy, reduced emotional availability, impaired emotion
    82→    82→regulation, and difficulties in the mother–infant relationship, which may further contribute to
    83→    83→feelings of isolation (Kruse et al. 2014; Tuominen et al. 2016; Kent-Marvick et al. 2022).
    84→    84→Biological evidence further suggests that social disconnection may act as an intense stress
    85→    85→signal, leading to dysregulation of neuroendocrine, immune, and autonomic systems involved
    86→    86→in maternal adaptation (Cacioppo et al. 2015) and increasing vulnerability to adverse mental
    87→    87→and physical health outcomes (Holt-Lunstad et al. 2017). In the postpartum period, both
    88→    88→loneliness and social isolation have been associated with depression, anxiety, and
    89→    89→posttraumatic stress symptoms (Kent-Marvick et al. 2022).
    90→    90→In Slovakia, previous research has primarily examined depressive symptoms during the early
    91→    91→postpartum period (up to 6-8 weeks after childbirth), with little attention to maternal mental
    92→    92→health throughout maternity and parental leave (Škodová et al. 2021; Ďuríčeková et al. 2024).
    93→    93→
    94→    94→Statutory maternity leave lasts 34 weeks, and parental leave may extend until the child
    95→    95→reaches 3 years of age. Countries with extended maternity and parental leave provide a unique
    96→    96→opportunity to examine maternal mental health beyond the early postpartum period, yet this
    97→    97→stage remains largely overlooked in the literature. The present study therefore aimed to
    98→    98→estimate the prevalence of depression, anxiety, loneliness, and social isolation among Slovak
    99→    99→mothers on maternity and parental leave and to identify associated psychological and
   100→   100→sociodemographic factors. A secondary aim was to examine the contribution of loneliness and
   101→   101→social isolation to depressive symptoms.
   102→   102→Materials and methods
   103→   103→Sample
   104→   104→Data were collected online between October 2025 and January 2026. Participants were
   105→   105→recruited through social media, pediatricians, maternity centers, online mothers' groups, and
   106→   106→non-profit organizations supporting families. All participants provided electronic informed
   107→   107→consent before completing the online survey. The survey was anonymous.
   108→   108→A total of 770 individuals responded to the study invitation; 637 provided informed consent
   109→   109→and initiated the survey, and 424 completed the questionnaires. After excluding nine fathers
   110→   110→and five adoptive mothers according to the predefined eligibility criteria, complete data from
   111→   111→410 biological mothers were included in the final analysis.
   112→   112→The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and approved by the
   113→   113→Ethical Review Board of the Faculty of Arts, Comenius University Bratislava (ER/56/2025).
   114→   114→
   115→   115→Measures
   116→   116→Depression
   117→   117→Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) (Kroenke et al. 2001) is a nine-item scale
   118→   118→assessing depressive symptoms over the previous two weeks. Items are rated from 0 (not at
   119→   119→all) to 3 (nearly every day), yielding a total score of 0–27. A score of ≥ 10 is considered
   120→   120→clinically significant, indicating at least moderate depressive symptom severity, while ≥ 15
   121→   121→indicates more severe symptoms (Kroenke et al. 2001; Hajduk et al. 2019).
   122→   122→Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) (Cox et al. 1987) is a 10-item screening tool
   123→   123→assessing depressive symptoms over the previous 7 days, particularly in the perinatal period.
   124→   124→Items are scored from 0 to 3, with total scores ranging from 0 to 30. A cut-off score of ≥ 13 is
   125→   125→considered clinically significant, representing moderate severity of depressive symptoms
   126→   126→(Matthey et al. 2006).
   127→   127→Anxiety
   128→   128→Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) (Spitzer et al. 2006) is a 7-item screening tool
   129→   129→assessing anxiety symptoms over the previous two weeks and are rated from 0 (not at all) to 3
   130→   130→(nearly every day), yielding a total score of 0–21. A cut-off score of ≥ 10 is considered
   131→   131→clinically significant, representing a moderate severity of anxiety symptoms, while ≥ 15
   132→   132→reflects more severe symptoms (Hajduk et al., 2019; Kroenke et al., 2010).
   133→   133→Loneliness
   134→   134→UCLA Loneliness Scale (UCLA-3) (Hughes et al. 2004) is a 3-item self-evaluation scale for
   135→   135→measuring loneliness. Items are rated from 1 (hardly ever) to 3 (often), with total scores
   136→   136→ranging from 3 to 9. Higher scores indicate greater perceived loneliness; a cut-off score of ≥ 6
   137→   137→indicates elevated loneliness (Steptoe et al. 2013).
   138→   138→
   139→   139→Social isolation
   140→   140→The Lubben Social Network Scale (LSNS-6) (Lubben et al. 2006a) is a screening tool, which
   141→   141→evaluates family and friend networks, including the number of close contacts, contact
   142→   142→frequency and availability of support. Scores range from 0 to 30, with lower scores indicating
   143→   143→greater social isolation. A cut-off score of <12 indicates risk of social isolation (Lubben et al.
   144→   144→2006b).
   145→   145→Direct measures of social disconnection
   146→   146→Social disconnection was additionally assessed using two direct questions adapted from the
   147→   147→EU Loneliness survey (Mauri et al. 2024): “How often have you felt lonely/isolated during the
   148→   148→last four weeks?” Responses were rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from never to
   149→   149→always.
   150→   150→Statistical Procedures
   151→   151→All statistical procedures were done using the program Jamovi (Version 2.6) (The jamovi
   152→   152→project 2024). Firstly, descriptive statistics were used to estimate the prevalence of depression
   153→   153→and anxiety. Due to violations of normality, Spearman's rank correlation coefficient was used
   154→   154→to assess the strength of relationships. Finally, two separate multiple linear regression models
   155→   155→were estimated: one for depressive symptoms measured by the PHQ-9 and another for
   156→   156→postpartum depression measured by the EPDS, based on demographic and psychopathological
   157→   157→variables. In both models, GAD-7, UCLA-3, and LSNS-6 scores were added simultaneously
   158→   158→in addition with demographic variables.
   159→   159→Results
   160→   160→The final sample consisted of 410 mothers who completed the questionnaire fully, with a
   161→   161→mean age of 33.4 (SD=4.61, range 22 - 44). 52.9% had one child, 33.2% were mothers of two
   162→   162→
   163→   163→children, 9.5% had three children, and 4.4% had four or more children. The mean length of
   164→   164→maternity leave was 34.9 months (SD = 28.0, median = 27.5, range 1–176 months). Most
   165→   165→women (88.3%) lived in a shared household with a partner or husband, while 9.8% lived as
   166→   166→single mothers. The sample was dominated by women with a high / university education
   167→   167→(83.7%), while women with middle and low education were less represented (14.9% resp.
   168→   168→1.5%). Due to the low frequency of participants in the lower categories, education was
   169→   169→recoded into a binary variable: low (middle and low education) versus high. Family
   170→   170→socioeconomic status was self-assessed by participants. Four respondents who selected
   171→   171→“Prefer not to answer” were excluded from the linear regression model. More detailed
   172→   172→sociodemographic and anamnestic characteristics are presented in Table 1.

   208→   208→Prevalence of depression, anxiety, loneliness, and social isolation
   209→   209→Based on established cut-off scores for depressive symptomatology, 36.6% of participants
   210→   210→scored above the EPDS threshold ≥ 13, 38.3% exceeded the PHQ-9 ≥ 10, and 17.1%
   211→   211→exceeded the more stringent criteria for severe intensity of symptoms, PHQ-9 ≥ 15.
   212→   212→Agreement between EPDS (cut-off ≥ 13) and PHQ-9 (cut-off ≥10) classifications was
   213→   213→examined using McNemar's test. The test indicated no significant difference in the proportion
   214→   214→of cases identified by the two measures, χ²(1, N = 410) = 0.65, p = .419, suggesting
   215→   215→comparable classification rates of elevated depressive symptoms. For anxiety, 28.3% of
   216→   216→participants scored above the GAD-7 ≥ 10, and 10.5% exceeded the cut-off of 15. Overall,
   217→   217→23.2% of participants met criteria for both depression and anxiety.
   218→   218→Regarding the direct measure of loneliness, the most frequent response was "sometimes"
   219→   219→(39.3%), followed by "most of the time" (24.1%). Only 8.8% of participants reported never
   220→   220→feeling lonely, whereas 4.9% reported feeling lonely all the time during the previous four
   221→   221→weeks. Similarly, for the direct measure of social isolation, the most frequent response was
   222→   222→"sometimes" (35.1%), followed by "most of the time" (28.0%). Only 13.7% of participants
   223→   223→reported never feeling socially isolated, whereas 7.1% reported feeling socially isolated all the
   224→   224→time during the previous four weeks.
   225→   225→
   226→   226→Consistent with these findings, standardized psychometric measures indicated high levels of
   227→   227→perceived loneliness and social isolation. Based on the UCLA-3, 78.3% of participants
   228→   228→reported elevated loneliness (score ≥ 6), while 26.8% were classified as being at risk of social
   229→   229→isolation according to the LSNS-6 (score < 12; see Table 2). Overall, 23.6% of participants
   230→   230→met criteria for both elevated loneliness and social isolation.

   295→   295→Discussion
   296→   296→The findings indicate a substantial psychological burden among mothers on parental leave in
   297→   297→Slovakia, with 38.3% of women reporting elevated depressive symptoms, 36.6% elevated
   298→   298→postpartum depressive symptoms, and 28.3% elevated anxiety. More severe symptoms were
   299→   299→reported by 17.1% for depression and 10.5% for anxiety symptoms.
   300→   300→Compared with previous research conducted in Slovakia, these prevalence estimates appear
   301→   301→higher. Earlier studies focusing on the early postpartum period reported lower rates of
   302→   302→depressive symptoms (6.1% of women shortly after childbirth and 11.6% at 6–8 weeks
   303→   303→postpartum) (Škodová et al. 2021). A longitudinal study of women affected by COVID-19
   304→   304→during pregnancy reported higher depressive symptoms at 6 weeks postpartum (17.8%),
   305→   305→although differences in recruitment, sample characteristics, and follow-up periods limit direct
   306→   306→comparisons (Holikova et al. 2026). The higher prevalence observed in the present study may
   307→   307→partly reflect the inclusion of mothers across a longer parental leave period, capturing later
   308→   308→phases of motherhood associated with cumulative psychosocial stressors. This interpretation
   309→   309→is consistent with evidence suggesting that depressive symptoms may increase over time after
   310→   310→childbirth (Shorey et al. 2018). The prevalence of anxiety symptoms also exceeded commonly
   311→   311→reported postnatal estimates and approached levels observed during periods of increased
   312→   312→societal stress, such as the COVID-19 pandemic (Caffieri et al. 2024). However, elevated
   313→   313→prevalence rates may partly reflect selection bias, as recruitment pathways may have
   314→   314→preferentially attracted women experiencing psychological distress (Kaźmierczak et al. 2023)
   315→   315→or with a greater interest in mental health. Conversely, perinatal screening studies suggest that
   316→   316→women with greater socioeconomic resources may be overrepresented, whereas women with
   317→   317→lower social support, adverse living conditions, chronic illness, exposure to intimate partner
   318→   318→violence, or minority backgrounds may be underrepresented despite higher perinatal mental
   319→   319→
   320→   320→health risks (Yang et al. 2022; Longoria et al. 2024; Barnwell et al. 2024). Furthermore, self-
   321→   321→report screening measures may yield higher prevalence estimates than structured clinical
   322→   322→interviews (Levis et al. 2019).
   323→   323→The strong agreement between the EPDS and PHQ-9 supports their suitability for assessing
   324→   324→depressive symptoms in maternal populations (Levis et al. 2019). However, the EPDS may
   325→   325→offer advantages in perinatal contexts because it focuses on emotional symptoms and avoids
   326→   326→somatic symptoms that may overlap with normal pregnancy and postpartum changes (Wang et
   327→   327→al. 2021).
   328→   328→Loneliness was highly prevalent in our sample, affecting 78.3% of participants, while 26.8%
   329→   329→were at risk of social isolation and 23.6% experienced both. These findings suggest that social
   330→   330→disconnection is a prominent feature of maternal experience during extended parental leave.
   331→   331→Following childbirth, family focus often shifts toward the newborn, leaving mothers with
   332→   332→fewer opportunities to maintain their own social and emotional connections. In addition,
   333→   333→major life transitions, including relocation for education or employment and the transition to
   334→   334→parenthood, together with reduced occupational engagement and limited psychosocial support
   335→   335→during extended parental leave, may contribute to increased loneliness (MPSVR SR 2022;
   336→   336→Kent-Marvick et al. 2022; Adlington et al. 2023).
   337→   337→Consistent with previous research, anxiety was the strongest predictor of depressive
   338→   338→symptoms, reflecting the established comorbidity between these conditions during the
   339→   339→perinatal period (Falah-Hassani et al. 2017; Howard et al. 2023). Loneliness also
   340→   340→independently predicted depressive symptoms, supporting previous longitudinal evidence
   341→   341→linking social disconnection with postpartum depression (Luoma et al. 2015; Adlington et al.
   342→   342→2023).
   343→   343→
   344→   344→Sociodemographic factors were not strong predictors of depressive symptoms, with only age
   345→   345→and education showing weak associations. Subjective socioeconomic status, number of
   346→   346→children, and length of parental leave were not significant predictors in the adjusted model.
   347→   347→These findings should be interpreted cautiously. In contrast to previous evidence linking
   348→   348→socioeconomic disadvantage to increased risk of perinatal depression (Yang et al. 2022;
   349→   349→Longoria et al. 2024), socioeconomic indicators showed limited associations with depressive
   350→   350→symptoms in our sample, which may reflect the sample’s relatively advantaged
   351→   351→socioeconomic profile. The absence of an association between parental leave duration and
   352→   352→depressive symptoms may suggest that the protective effects of parental leave (Hidalgo-
   353→   353→Padilla et al. 2023) plateau beyond the early postpartum period rather than increasing with
   354→   354→longer leave duration. Because maternal mental health during prolonged parental leave
   355→   355→remains understudied, these findings extend current evidence beyond the early postpartum
   356→   356→period.
   357→   357→From a clinical perspective, these findings support extending perinatal mental health
   358→   358→assessment beyond the early postpartum period and highlight loneliness as a potentially
   359→   359→modifiable target for intervention. Screening for loneliness, alongside depression and anxiety,
   360→   360→and interventions that strengthen peer support, community engagement, maternal self-
   361→   361→efficacy, and stigma reduction may improve maternal well-being (Hu et al. 2023; Naughton-
   362→   362→Doe et al. 2025). Future longitudinal research should examine the temporal relationship
   363→   363→between social disconnection and maternal mental health and evaluate interventions aimed at
   364→   364→reducing perinatal loneliness.
   365→   365→Several limitations should be acknowledged. The cross-sectional design prevents conclusions
   366→   366→regarding causality. Self-report measures may have overestimated prevalence, selection bias
   367→   367→cannot be excluded, and the study did not assess prior mental health history. The sample was
   368→   368→predominantly highly educated and partnered, which may limit generalizability to more
   369→   369→
   370→   370→vulnerable groups, including single mothers, migrant women, and women with lower
   371→   371→socioeconomic status. Additionally, the cultural and policy context of Slovakia, including
   372→   372→extended parental leave and availability of childcare and community services, may limit the
   373→   373→generalizability of the findings.
   374→   374→Conclusion
   375→   375→Mothers on parental leave in Slovakia experience a substantial burden of depressive and
   376→   376→anxiety symptoms alongside high levels of loneliness and social isolation. Together, these
   377→   377→findings suggest that maternal psychological vulnerability extends well beyond the early
   378→   378→postpartum period, particularly in settings with prolonged parental leave. Extending routine
   379→   379→maternal mental health assessment to include loneliness, alongside interventions that
   380→   380→strengthen social connectedness, may enhance opportunities for prevention and early
   381→   381→intervention.
