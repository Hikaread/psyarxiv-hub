    11→Abstract
    12→The interactional design of digital mental health systems shapes what people say, how they
    13→say it, and whose distress becomes legible to automated models. We designed M2SurveyBot,
    14→a multimodal chatbot developed with clinical psychologists, to examine whether interaction
    15→modality that is, typing versus speech, affects self-disclosure and depression screening. In a
    16→study with 455 U.S. participants, typed and spoken modalities yielded statistically
    17→indistinguishable predictive performance, while acoustic features alone carried near-zero
    18→signal. Critically, we find a robust SES-related calibration gap replicated across supervised
    19→models and GPT-4o zero-shot estimation: predictions track depression severity more closely
    20→for lower-SES participants, suggesting the gap reflects genuine socioeconomic differences in
    21→linguistic expression of distress. These findings challenge the assumption that multimodal
    22→collection produces more equitable screening, while establishing spoken interaction as a
    23→viable, accessible alternative to typed text, potentially lower-barrier for populations for
    24→whom typing poses challenges.
    25→Keywords: depression screening, chatbot, self-disclosure, multimodal depression
    26→detection, interaction mode, socioeconomic status, mental health, fairness, bias

    32→Introduction
    33→Mental health services remain inaccessible to the majority of people who need
    34→them (American Psychological Association, 2025; Mental Health America, 2025), and
    35→AI-assisted conversational tools are increasingly used to fill much of the resulting gap (Cho
    36→et al., 2023). Yet the AI models that underpin these tools were largely trained on social
    37→media language, and a critical limitation has emerged: they do not predict depression
    38→equally well across demographic groups (Aguirre et al., 2021; Rai et al., 2024). A structural
    39→explanation is that public platform expression is shaped by social desirability bias: people
    40→manage self-presentation for an audience in ways that reflect their social position (De Freitas,
    41→2024), suppressing or distorting disclosures of stigmatized experience. This raises a
    42→foundational design question: in a controlled, private setting, does differential model
    43→performance persist—and can design choices, including interaction modality, improve both
    44→disclosure and equity?
    45→These questions are worth testing because theoretical arguments support both
    46→modalities. Text affords anonymity, reflection time, and the “online disinhibition effect,”
    47→which can elicit more candid disclosure of stigmatized experience (Schober et al., 2015; Suler,
    48→2004). Spoken interaction may feel more natural, reducing the cognitive effort of composing
    49→responses thus lowering barriers to self-disclosure (Lucas et al., 2014; Rai et al., 2026), and
    50→may be essential for populations for whom typing poses barriers (Sehgal et al., 2025a, 2025b).
    51→Crucially, while multimodal approaches to mental health detection have been studied (Jiang
    52→et al., 2024; Khoo et al., 2024), prior work has focused on passive sensing and clinical
    53→recordings rather than interaction modality choice in conversational screening tools — and
    54→no prior work has examined whether the modality through which a user engages with a
    55→mental health chatbot interacts with socioeconomic status to shape both disclosure and
    56→model calibration.

    60→We developed M2SurveyBot, a multimodal chatbot designed with clinical
    61→psychologists to conduct semi-structured mental health interviews, and deployed it in a
    62→controlled study with 455 U.S. adults. We examine:
    63→• RQ1: Does interaction modality (text vs. audio) affect self-disclosure depth?
    64→• RQ2: Does modality affect depression prediction, and do paralinguistic features add
    65→signal beyond text?
    66→• RQ3: Does socioeconomic status moderate depression prediction across modalities?
    67→M2SurveyBot: A Multimodal Chatbot for Conducting Mental Health Interviews
    68→We developed M2SurveyBot, a multimodal conversational survey system designed to
    69→collect open-ended mental health narratives through text and audio interaction. The chatbot
    70→was developed in collaboration with clinical psychologists and administered a fixed sequence
    71→of eight questions covering emotional experiences, daily life, concerns, and future orientation
    72→(see Appendix A). To maintain conversational flow, GPT-4o generated brief follow-up
    73→prompts and empathetic affirmations to encourage sharing details, while avoiding clinical
    74→diagnosis or therapeutic claims.
    75→Interaction Modes. In text mode, participants read questions on screen and
    76→typed responses. In audio mode, questions were delivered via ElevenLabs text-to-speech;
    77→participants responded aloud, and responses were transcribed in real time using OpenAI
    78→Whisper Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), with transcripts displayed on-screen for
    79→participant correction.
    80→Safety and Quality Controls. A prominent disclaimer provided crisis resources
    81→(988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline). Responses were screened for self-harm language using a
    82→manually validated automated detection module using GPT-3.5 Turbo; flagged sessions were
    83→immediately terminated with referrals. Text fields disabled copy-paste to discourage
    84→pre-written material, and a RoBERTa-based relevance checker prompted clarification for
    85→off-topic responses.

    92→Sample Characteristics
    93→We recruited 831 U.S. adults aged 18+ via Prolific and CloudResearch. After quality
    94→checks (age > 90, word count < 300 (Rai et al., 2024), and PHQ score inconsistency > 5
    95→across sessions), 455 participants remained (345 text, 313 audio, 203 both; see Table A1).
    96→Participants completed two sessions. In each session, they first completed depression
    97→screening questionnaire PHQ-9 (excluding the question on self-harm) and demographic
    98→questions via Qualtrics, then engaged with M2SurveyBot in the assigned modality.
    99→Participants were randomly assigned to either audio-based or text-based interaction in their
   100→first session, then received the alternative modality in a follow-up session after 3 days.
   101→Interaction modality was counterbalanced across sessions to control for order effects. 14.46%
   102→of transcripts were flagged as incorrect, 97.09% of them subsequently edited them via the
   103→provided correction interface (Word error rate: M = 0.613,SD = 1.607; see Table A2 for
   104→examples). Participants who did not complete both sessions were matched for age and
   105→gender across conditions. All procedures were IRB-approved (#857296), and participants
   106→provided consent at the beginning of the session and were compensated at rate of $15/hr.
   107→Methods
   108→Measuring Self-Disclosure
   109→We employed three complementary approaches: (1) LIWC - first-person singular
   110→pronoun frequency (Boyd et al., 2022; Tausczik & Pennebaker, 2010), (2) pre-trained
   111→RoBERTa self-disclosure classifier that outputs continuous disclosure scores, computed
   112→at the message level (Reuel et al., 2022) and (3) GPT-4o rubric scoring on three
   113→dimensions: information, thought, and feeling (each on a 1–3 scale) (Fang et al., 2025) (see
   114→Appendix for prompt).

   118→Depression Risk Assessment
   119→Machine Learning with Linguistic Features.
   120→We built predictive models using four text-based feature representations:
   121→(1) RoBERTa-base embeddings(Liu et al., 2019); (2) LIWC category frequencies;
   122→(3) N-grams; and (4) LDA topic distributions (50 topics). For audio-condition
   123→participants, we additionally extracted 376 acoustic features using OpenSMILE (eGeMAPS
   124→feature set) (Eyben et al., 2010) and Librosa (McFee et al., 2024), aggregated to the
   125→participant level via statistics pooling (see appendix). We also constructed a combined
   126→spoken feature vector by concatenating transcript embeddings with acoustic features (768 +
   127→376 = 1,144 dimensions).
   128→We performed Elastic Net regression with default parameters (α = 1, L1-ratio = 0.5)
   129→with 10-fold cross-validation (stratified by SES status), reporting Pearson r with depression
   130→severity scores and MAE.
   131→LLM-Based Depression Severity Inference.
   132→We applied GPT-4o zero-shot to directly estimate depression severity scores from
   133→concatenated participant responses, using a structured prompt listing each PHQ-9 item on
   134→the standard 0–3 scale (see Appendix). This method was applied post-hoc and did not
   135→influence the live interaction.

   152→Results
   153→Self-Disclosure Does Not Differ by Modality (RQ1)
   154→Participants produced significantly more words in audio (M = 753, SD = 629) than
   155→text (M = 463, SD = 218) and spoke faster (51.8 vs. 34.3 words per minute). However,
   156→self-disclosure depth did not differ significantly between modalities on any ML-based or
   157→LLM-based measure (Table A7). Among dual completers, LIWC detected a small text
   158→advantage in text (∆M = 0.005, paired t = 3.17, p = .002), but this did not replicate across
   159→other disclosure measures, suggesting limited practical significance.
   160→Modality Does Not Improve Depression Prediction (RQ2)
   161→Text carries the signal; acoustic features do not.
   162→Typed text model trained on RoBERTa embeddings achieved the strongest
   163→correlation with reported depression scores (r = 0.496), followed closely by spoken
   164→transcripts (r = 0.445) (see Table 1). Acoustic features alone showed near-zero predictive
   165→validity (r = −0.023, p = .691). Combining transcript and acoustic features did not
   166→significantly improve the performance relative to transcript alone (r = 0.449 vs. r = 0.445).
   167→Among the 203 dual completers, pairwise Wilcoxon tests found no significant differences
   168→between any modality pair (See Table A6).
   169→LLM-based prediction confirms the pattern.
   170→GPT-4o zero-shot estimation yielded r = 0.583 for typed text and r = 0.522 for
   171→spoken transcripts (p < .001 for both), with negligible modality differences. The stronger
   172→performance of GPT-4o over supervised models may reflect its training across text, audio,
   173→and conversational data, making it more robust to transcript-like language.

   177→Content, not channel, drives prediction.
   178→LIWC analysis revealed 19 features positively associated (p < 0.05) with depression in
   179→typed text modalities and 13 features in spoken transcript (see Table A10). However,
   180→modality-specific markers emerged: spoken transcripts surfaced insight and certitude words
   181→(r ≈ 0.245 with depression in speech but near zero in typing), while typed text uniquely
   182→captured tentative language (r = 0.202). Despite these qualitative differences, the core
   183→depressive language signal is shared across modalities, and the overall predictive performance
   184→does not significantly differ.

   207→Discussion and Design Implications
   208→Modality does not improve screening. Multimodal approaches have been
   209→proposed to improve mental health screening robustness (Khoo et al., 2024). Our study
   210→provides direct empirical evidence against the hypothesis that switching from text to speech
   211→can improve depression risk assessment across SES in brief chatbot-led conversations. This
   212→aligns with previous findings (Jiang et al., 2024; Sadeghi et al., 2024). Across multiple
   213→measures of self-disclosure, multiple approaches to depression prediction, and both overall
   214→and SES-stratified analyses, we find no statistically significant advantage in this
   215→configuration for spoken over typed modality.
   216→More words does not translate to better prediction. A striking finding is the
   217→dissociation between quantity and quality of disclosure. Participants produced more words in
   218→audio and spoke at 51% faster rates (51.8 vs. 34.3 words per minute), yet this additional
   219→volume did not translate into deeper self-disclosure or better depression prediction. This
   220→suggests that the extra words produced in speech are largely filler—disfluencies, elaborations,
   221→and conversational padding that add length without adding diagnostic content. For system
   222→designers, this finding cautions against using word count or interaction duration as proxies
   223→for engagement quality in mental health chatbots.
   224→SES shapes model calibration. While models were equally accurate in absolute
   225→error magnitude across SES groups, they were differentially calibrated — a pattern that
   226→proved robust across two fundamentally different modeling approaches. The Elastic Net
   227→supervised model and GPT-4o zero-shot estimation independently converged on the same
   228→finding: predictions track reported depression severity more closely for lower-SES
   229→participants than higher-SES participants. The GPT-4o replication is particularly notable
   230→because it cannot be explained by unequal depression severity distributions in the training
   231→sample, lending stronger support to the interpretation that the gap reflects genuine
   232→socioeconomic differences in how depression is linguistically expressed.

   236→Implications for Mental Health System Design
   237→Our findings carry several practical implications for designers of mental health
   238→technologies:
   239→• Modality switching is not a solution to equity gaps. The intuitive appeal of
   240→“just add audio” as a design intervention for more inclusive mental health tools is not
   241→supported by our data. Equity-oriented designers should look beyond modality to
   242→address deeper structural factors in how depression is expressed and captured across
   243→populations.
   244→• Content analysis should take priority over acoustic analysis. In
   245→chatbot-mediated mental health assessment, investing in better natural language
   246→understanding of response content is likely more productive than incorporating
   247→acoustic or prosodic features for the relatively short, prompted conversational turns
   248→typical of screening chatbots.
   249→• Modality shapes which facets of distress become legible to automated
   250→models, with consequences for population-specific tool design. Spoken
   251→transcripts uniquely surfaced insight and certitude language, while typed text captured
   252→tentative language and health-related words — signals that may index different
   253→phenomenological aspects of depression, or different self-presentation norms across
   254→modalities. Designers targeting populations where somatic or uncertainty-laden
   255→expression predominates may find typed modalities better calibrated to those signals,
   256→while spoken interaction may be better suited to capturing reflective or insight-oriented
   257→distress. These are not equivalent channels, even when aggregate predictive
   258→performance is similar.
   259→These divergences also suggest that future evaluations of mental health chatbots
   260→should report modality-specific LIWC or linguistic profiles alongside accuracy metrics,
   261→rather than treating modality as a nuisance variable.

   265→• Subjective preferences and objective outcomes diverge. Participants
   266→completing both modalities slightly preferred audio interaction (M = 4.02 for audio vs
   267→3.95 for typed on a scale of 1 to 5; p > 0.5) and rated spoken interaction as more
   268→counselor-like than typed (M = 2.24 vs. 2.51; lower scores indicate greater
   269→counselor-likeness; p < 0.05), yet these preferences did not correspond to objectively
   270→better disclosure or prediction. System designers should be cautious about using user
   271→satisfaction as a proxy for assessment quality.
   272→• SES-aware model evaluation is essential. Our calibration findings suggest that
   273→aggregate prediction metrics can mask meaningful disparities in model behavior across
   274→socioeconomic groups. Evaluating depression prediction models should include
   275→SES-stratified calibration analysis, not just overall correlation.
   276→That said, our findings should not be read as evidence against audio interaction in all
   277→contexts. For populations including non-English speakers, older adults, and children where
   278→typing poses barriers, voice-based interaction may be essential for accessibility and
   279→participation.
   280→Limitations
   281→Several limitations qualify our findings. First, our audio condition was not a pure
   282→voice interaction: transcripts were displayed on-screen and participants could correct them,
   283→potentially attenuating modality differences. Second, acoustic features were extracted from
   284→consumer-grade remote recordings to mimic real world use. Finally, our SES composite
   285→(education + income median split) is a common but crude operationalization that does not
   286→capture wealth, occupation, or neighborhood-level factors. These limitations reinforce that
   287→our study does not show modality is irrelevant in all settings. Rather, in our semi-structured
   288→chatbot configuration, adding spoken interaction and acoustic features did not improve
   289→disclosure depth, prediction accuracy, or equity. As AI-assisted mental health tools are
   290→deployed at scale as informal support infrastructure, future work must examine SES effects

   543→Use the following guidelines:
   544→1) INFORMATION
   545→Level 1 (Score 1): No personal reference; only general/routine info. Example: “You need at least one
   546→month to travel in India.”
   547→Level2(Score2): Generalinformationaboutthewriter(e.g., age, occupation, familymembers, interests).
   548→Example: “I’m 25, and I work at a local bakery.”
   549→Level 3 (Score 3): Personal information that reveals something about the writer’s or close others’
   550→appearance, personal experiences, or problematic behaviors. Example: “I’ve struggled with insomnia for
   551→years and tried multiple medications.”
   552→2) THOUGHTS
   553→Level 1 (Score 1): No personal thoughts about the writer’s own life; only general ideas. Example: “I
   554→think feeding wild birds can be harmful.”
   555→Level 2 (Score 2): Personal thoughts about past events or future plans. Example: “I’d like to attend
   556→medical school someday.”
   557→Level 3 (Score 3): Personal or intimate thoughts relating to the writer’s characteristics, health, or deeply
   558→self-reflective ideas. Example: “I hate myself for lashing out at my friend and regret it deeply.”
   559→3) FEELINGS
   560→Level 1 (Score 1): No feelings are expressed. Example: “I bought groceries and cleaned my room today.”
   561→Level 2 (Score 2): Mild or moderate expressions of confusion, inconvenience, or ordinary frustrations.
   562→Example: “I was annoyed I couldn’t find a parking spot.”
   563→Level3(Score3): Expressionsofdeeporintenseemotionssuchashumiliation, agony, anxiety, depression,
   564→or fear. Example: “I’m terrified of failing my final exam and can’t sleep.”
   565→Important: If a message seems to qualify for multiple levels within the same category, choose the highest
   566→relevant level. Provide your scores in the format: Information (1–3), Thoughts (1–3), Feelings (1–3).
