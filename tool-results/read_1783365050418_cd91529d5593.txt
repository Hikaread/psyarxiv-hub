    21→Abstract
    22→Background. Idiographic approaches to case formulation often combine ecological
    23→momentary assessment (EMA) with network analysis. Traditional network models require large
    24→amounts of data and are largely theory-agnostic, whereas approaches based on perceived
    25→causal relationships are weakly constrained and highly exploratory. Both often generate
    26→unstable networks that are difficult to compare across individuals. We introduce Momentary
    27→Structured Networks (MSN), an EMA framework that combines theory-based structure with
    28→self-reported causal relations to generate individualised network representations.
    29→Method. Forty-six individuals with posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) completed EMA over
    30→three weeks (2–5 daily assessments). Participants reported perceived causal relations between
    31→personalised items drawn from the Ehlers and Clark cognitive model of PTSD. Directional edges
    32→were estimated as conditional probabilities and compared to association-based Ising models.
    33→Results. MSN networks consistently showed dense and complex structures, even with limited
    34→data. MSN identified most of the relations identified by Ising models while capturing additional
    35→ones, resulting in higher network complexity. Complexity was independent of the number of
    36→individual assessments, and network structure stabilised quickly, with over 90% of edges
    37→recovered using 60% of the data. A clinical case study showed how MSN can track changes in
    38→network dynamics during therapy and generate hypotheses about the processes underlying
    39→clinical improvement.
    40→Conclusion. MSN provides a practical approach for measuring case formulation and tracking
    41→change over time by reducing combinatorial complexity while retaining sensitivity to individual
    42→variation. Although demonstrated in PTSD, it can extend to other conditions and theoretical
    43→models, offering a new framework for evaluating clinical theory and therapeutic change.
    44→Keywords: Network analysis; Idiographic science; Case formulation; Psychological
    45→Mechanisms; Posttraumatic Stress Disorder; PTSD; Change processes; Nonlinear change in
    46→treatment; Psychological measurement

    48→Introduction
    49→Clinical psychological science is characterised by a mismatch between how distress is
    50→conceptualised in practice and how it is studied empirically and instantiated in clinical trials.
    51→Much of clinical practice is organised around case formulation [1]. Clinicians integrate an
    52→individual's experiences, beliefs, behaviours, and context into an explanatory account of how
    53→distress arises and is maintained, using this formulation to guide intervention and evaluate
    54→change over time. Case formulation is therefore inherently mechanistic (i.e., focused on the
    55→mechanisms driving psychopathology), individualised, and theory-guided [2–4]. In contrast,
    56→most clinical trials focus on symptom severity and symptom reduction, often treating
    57→symptoms and diagnoses as static outcomes rather than interacting components within a
    58→dynamic system [5,6]. Consequently, many of the processes central to clinical formulation
    59→remain largely absent from quantitative research. One reason for this gap is methodological:
    60→dynamic, interacting individual processes are difficult to operationalise and quantify
    61→systematically [7]. As a result, empirical models often fail to capture the mechanisms clinicians
    62→seek to understand and modify, limiting their utility for theory testing and inference at the
    63→individual level.
    64→Network approaches to psychopathology partially address this gap by conceptualising
    65→psychiatric disorders as systems of mutually interacting symptoms [8]. By modelling
    66→dependencies between symptoms, network models enable dynamic reasoning about how
    67→change may propagate through a system [9]. Idiographic networks aim to estimate such
    68→dynamics at the individual level, aligning in principle with the goals of case formulation [10–12].
    69→However, idiographic network approaches are typically exploratory and data-driven with no
    70→theoretical constraint aside from selecting which nodes participate in the network. This
    71→restricts their capacity to represent clinical formulations in several ways. First, idiographic
    72→network models are typically constructed in a largely exploratory manner, treating each
    73→individual as a blank slate and risk misidentifying key processes. Second, network algorithms
    74→are ‘data-hungry’, often requiring large numbers of observations for stable estimation [13],
    75→which limits feasibility in clinical settings and may exclude more burdened populations [14].
    76→Third, most network models are fundamentally association-based and are therefore insensitive
    77→to causal processes that do not manifest through covariance or are obscured by ceiling effects.
    78→Contemporaneous models capture co-occurrence without directional information, whereas
    79→temporal models impose directionality through lagged associations that rely on strong
    80→assumptions regarding timescale and process dynamics, many of which may be unrealistic in
    81→psychopathology, such as stationarity [15,16]. Even when direction is inferred, it reflects
    82→
    83→temporal precedence rather than causality [17]. Collectively, these limitations mean that
    84→current idiographic network approaches largely disregard existing theoretical knowledge
    85→regarding the components and relationships expected to underlie psychopathology.
    86→Consequently, the resulting networks are weakly constrained, unstable, difficult to interpret,
    87→and poorly aligned with clinical reasoning.
    88→One response to these limitations has been to move away from statistical inference
    89→altogether and instead ask patients directly how they believe their difficulties are connected.
    90→Longitudinal Perceived Causal Networks (L-PECAN) follows this logic [18] – rather than
    91→estimating associations from observed data, participants report which components they
    92→believe influence others, effectively constructing a quantified case formulation. Importantly,
    93→this approach reduces data requirements, incorporates patient knowledge, and can be
    94→implemented within ecological momentary assessment (EMA) designs. However, elicitation
    95→(i.e., asking patients to report perceived relationships between psychological processes) is
    96→typically unrestricted: participants can endorse relationships between any pair of components,
    97→often selected from a broad predefined list. This creates a large and weakly constrained space
    98→of possible networks with limited guidance regarding which relations are plausible, increasing
    99→reliance on introspective accuracy and consistency while placing substantial cognitive
   100→demands on participants. Empirical work suggests modest reliability and limited temporal
   101→stability [19].
   102→A different approach to addressing the limitations of idiographic science is the Prior
   103→Elicitation Module for Idiographic System Estimation (PREMISE) [11]. PREMISE represents an
   104→important advance by explicitly incorporating prior knowledge into network estimation, allowing
   105→patients and clinicians to jointly define network elements and estimate the strength of
   106→relationships, which are subsequently updated using EMA data within a Bayesian framework.
   107→While principled, this approach places substantial demands on patient insight and cognitive
   108→capacity early in treatment, when estimating relationship strength may be unreliable or
   109→infeasible, particularly among individuals experiencing high symptom burden [20–22]. In
   110→addition, despite its use of priors, PREMISE remains relatively data intensive, potentially limiting
   111→its feasibility in routine clinical settings [23]. Like L-PECAN, PREMISE also permits a largely
   112→unrestricted space of possible network structures with limited theoretical guidance, placing
   113→substantial weight on patients’ reflective capacities.
   114→To address the limitations of theory-agnostic networks, we recently developed an
   115→alternative approach that incorporates stronger theoretical structure into idiographic
   116→
   117→assessment [24,25]. Although generalisable in principle, we demonstrate this approach in the
   118→context of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Initially introduced in a proof-of-concept
   119→study by Blomfield et al. [25], this framework is grounded in the Ehlers and Clark cognitive
   120→model of PTSD [26], which proposes that persistent PTSD is maintained by a sense of current
   121→threat arising from negative appraisals of the trauma and its consequences, and by the nature
   122→of the trauma memory, which is poorly integrated and easily triggered. This threat is further
   123→maintained by maladaptive coping strategies such as avoidance and thought suppression.
   124→Accordingly, Hertz-Palmor et al. [24] measured mechanistic components such as negative
   125→appraisals, trauma memory characteristics, triggers, sense of current threat, and maladaptive
   126→coping strategies. Participants completed personalised EMA protocols after they selected
   127→individual items relevant to their experience, while remaining anchored by the predefined
   128→constructs of the model. This allowed individualised measurement within a shared theoretical
   129→framework. This approach improved interpretability and supported the model at the group level,
   130→while also revealing substantial heterogeneity in individual formulations. However, it remained
   131→dependent on statistical inference to estimate relationships between components, as
   132→associations were not theoretically constrained and were instead inferred using traditional
   133→network algorithms. Parallel computational simulations indicated limited power and precision,
   134→and many theoretically plausible relations were not detected [24]. Moreover, when compared
   135→against participants' own perceptions of symptom dynamics in the initial proof-of-concept
   136→evaluation [25], the inferred relations showed only limited agreement with perceived causal
   137→pathways. Directionality remained anchored in Granger-causal assumptions, and the approach
   138→retained the substantial data requirements and participant burden of traditional EMA.
   139→Here, we introduce Momentary Structured Networks (MSN), a method designed to
   140→address the abovementioned limitations by synthesising theory-guided structure with direct
   141→elicitation of perceived causal relations. Rather than treating network estimation as fully
   142→exploratory, MSN adopts a theory-driven approach in which elicitation is constrained by
   143→theoretical expectations (for example: in the Ehlers and Clark model – appraisals → threat →
   144→maladaptive strategies). We demonstrate this approach using the Ehlers and Clark model of
   145→PTSD as a proof-of-principle, though the framework is generalisable to other models and
   146→conditions. MSN resembles L-PECAN by embedding causal queries, but extends it by providing
   147→a structured, theory-driven framework. Participants report perceived causal relations between
   148→components while guided by theoretical predictions that prioritise plausible pathways while
   149→also allowing their disconfirmation. Directional relations are quantified as conditional
   150→
   151→probabilities (e.g., P(A→B | A)), yielding individualised networks that reflect both perceived
   152→associations and theoretical constraints.
   153→We tested MSN in a sample of 46 trauma-exposed individuals with either a PTSD
   154→diagnosis or high symptom severity indicative of probable PTSD, using EMA over three weeks
   155→(mean=42.6±36.5 observations per participant). We hypothesised that there would be: 1)
   156→substantial heterogeneity in individual networks; 2) greater structural complexity1 in MSN
   157→relative to association-based networks (Ising Models, used to derive networks from binary data
   158→[27,28]); 3) rapid stabilisation of network estimates, meaning that the final network structure
   159→would be achieved fast with relatively few observations; and, 4) directional edge preferences
   160→consistent with theoretical predictions. To illustrate the clinical utility of MSN, we present a
   161→detailed case study of one of these traumatised individuals who underwent Cognitive Therapy
   162→for PTSD in line with the Ehlers and Clark recommendations [29].
   163→Method
   164→Participants
   165→Participants were recruited from four sites. PTSD diagnosed participants were recruited through
   166→the National Health Service (NHS) in the United Kingdom, a stress clinic in Ontario, Canada,
   167→and Sheba Medical Center in Israel. A separate trauma-exposed cohort with clinical-level
   168→symptom severity was recruited from Tel Aviv University, Israel. The sample partially overlapped
   169→with Hertz-Palmor et al. [24]2. Inclusion criteria were endorsement of a traumatic event on the
   170→Life Events Checklist [LEC], coupled with either a formal diagnosis or meeting the clinical cutoff
   171→for probable PTSD on the PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5) – a PCL-5 score >33. Ethical
   172→approval was obtained from the University of Cambridge (PRE.2024.019), Tel Aviv University
   173→(0007074-2), and Sheba Medical Center (1666-24-SMC).
   174→Measures
   175→Five theory-driven constructs were assessed, based on the Ehlers and Clark model, including
   176→negative appraisals; characteristics of the trauma memory; trauma-related triggers; sense of
   177→current threat; and maladaptive coping strategies. For each construct, participants selected
   178→three items from validated questionnaires (see below) that best captured their day-to-day
   179→experiences. This constraint balanced personal relevance with participant burden. Analyses

   184→were conducted on these personalised subsets, aggregated at the construct level, rather than
   185→the full instruments. Accordingly, reliability estimates for the original questionnaires are not
   186→reported for the present sample.
   187→Negative appraisals were drawn from the Posttraumatic Cognitions Inventory (PTCI), a
   188→33-item measure assessing negative posttraumatic beliefs about the self and the world [30].
   189→Characteristics of the trauma memory were assessed using direct questions on recent intrusive
   190→memories and their characteristics, based on the Trauma Memory Quality Questionnaire
   191→(TMQQ), an 11-item measure capturing sensory and experiential features of traumatic
   192→recollections [31]. Triggers were elicited directly from participants during the item-selection
   193→phase, reflecting their highly idiosyncratic nature. Sense of current threat was measured using
   194→items indexing hypervigilance and physiological arousal from the PTSD Checklist for DSM-5
   195→(PCL-5; Blevins et al., 2015). Maladaptive coping strategies were selected based on examples
   196→described in the Ehlers and Clark model [26]. This approach to item personalisation has been
   197→employed in previous idiographic studies [24,25].
   198→Momentary Structured Network (MSN) Assessment
   199→MSN data were collected using questionnaires administered repeatedly via EMA. Based on the
   200→Ehlers and Clark model (Figure 1A), specific components were categorised as ‘upstream’
   201→(negative appraisals, trauma memory characteristics, and triggers) or ‘downstream’ (sense of
   202→current threat and maladaptive coping strategies). This distinction guided the structure and
   203→prioritisation of causal queries, which were constrained by the directional assumptions
   204→specified by the model.
   205→At each assessment, participants first indicated whether they had experienced any of
   206→the three upstream components (negative appraisals, trauma memory or triggers) since the
   207→previous assessment. The questions were phrased using their personalised items, with
   208→endorsement analysed at the component level (rather than specific items). If any of the three
   209→upstream components had been experienced, participants were asked whether it had led to a
   210→sense of current threat and whether it has caused any of the other upstream components3
   211→(Figure 1B). Thus, some relations were queried symmetrically (e.g., triggers ↔ appraisals),
   212→providing equal opportunity for either direction to be endorsed. In contrast, other relations were
   213→queried asymmetrically in line with theoretical predictions. For example, trauma memory →
   214→threat was queried whenever trauma memory was reported, whereas threat → trauma memory
   215→3 Each specific causal pathway was queried separately.
   216→
   217→was queried only when participants did not endorse the theoretically predicted trauma memory
   218→→ threat pathway. Sense of current threat was queried most frequently to reflect its central role
   219→in the model [24]. When a downstream component was endorsed—either sense of current
   220→threat or an upstream component that had itself been endorsed as the outcome of another
   221→component—participants were asked whether this led to using a maladaptive coping strategy. If
   222→no upstream component was endorsed, participants were still queried about current threat,
   223→whether it was caused by an upstream component or led to subsequent downstream effects,
   224→and whether maladaptive coping strategies were used. Structural constraints prohibited
   225→implausible relations (e.g., maladaptive coping strategies directly generating negative
   226→appraisals). See supplementary materials for an example of MSN querying from a single
   227→participant.
   228→Consistent with the model's assumption that threat mediates maladaptive responses,
   229→participants were three times more likely to be asked whether a sense of current threat led to
   230→maladaptive coping strategies than whether any other component directly caused them.
   231→Accordingly, maladaptive coping strategies were only permitted to form causal links with other
   232→components through their amplifying or attenuating effects on the sense of current threat.
   233→Queries concerning relations among upstream components were presented in a
   234→randomised order to ensure balanced opportunities for endorsement. Across repeated
   235→assessments (aggregated within individual), directional relations were quantified as conditional
   236→probabilities of individual contemporaneous dynamics, yielding individualised directed
   237→networks indexing the strength and asymmetry of perceived causal relations between
   238→components (see statistical analysis section).

   290→Procedure
   291→Onboarding session and item personalisation
   292→Participants completed a one-hour onboarding session with a researcher, conducted online or
   293→in person, which included informed consent, item selection, and psychoeducation.
   294→Participants first completed full-length versions of the measures listed above before selecting
   295→the three items per measure that best reflected their own experience of that underlying
   296→construct, choosing experiences, thoughts, or behaviours that occurred at least weekly and
   297→showed some variability. Item selection was supported by the researcher through clarification
   298→and procedural guidance, but final decisions were made by the participant. Freely elicited
   299→triggers were reviewed collaboratively to ensure a shared understanding of their definition and
   300→manifestation in daily life. Participants then received psychoeducation on the Ehlers and Clark
   301→model, delivered by the researcher or via pre-recorded videos (N=23 each), covering its core
   302→components and their role in maintaining post-traumatic stress. Following this, participants
   303→were given the opportunity to retain or modify their selected items. Finally, participants
   304→installed the m-Path app [33] and received instructions for completing the EMA protocol.
   305→Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA)
   306→EMA commenced the following day and continued for three weeks4. Participants received five
   307→notifications per day at fixed intervals (3 hours between each assessment) within preselected
   308→time windows. For most participants, three daily notifications included traditional EMA
   309→measures (not analysed here), alongside two MSN assessments scheduled around midday and
   310→18:00. A subset of participants (N=16; 34.8%) recruited later in the study received five daily
   311→MSN assessments (approximately 09:00, 12:00, 15:00, 18:00, and 21:00), with timing adjusted
   312→to individual schedules5. Data from participants who discontinued early were retained for
   313→analysis, consistent with the premise that MSN can yield informative network structures from
   314→fewer observations, albeit with reduced certainty. Clinical participants received £70 for the 3-
   315→week study, while students received 150 ILS (~£35) and course credit. Participants who
   316→withdrew early received partial compensation.
   317→4 One participant continued beyond the three-week period.
   318→5 As the protocol developed, later participants completed five daily MSN assessments to increase the
   319→number of observations and better align MSN with concurrent EMA assessments without substantially
   320→increasing participant burden.

   372→Statistical analysis
   373→Conditional directional probabilities
   374→Directional relations in MSN (e.g., appraisals → threat) were quantified as conditional
   375→probabilities across repeated assessments. For each ordered pair A→B, two complementary
   376→quantities were estimated, as follows:
   377→The parent-conditioned probability, P(A→B∣A), was defined as the proportion of
   378→queried instances of A in which B was reported as a downstream effect:
   379→𝑃(𝐴 → 𝐵)
   380→𝑃(𝐴 → 𝐵 ∣ 𝐴) =
   381→𝑃(𝐴)
   382→Where P(A→B) is the joint probability that A is endorsed and B is reported as its
   383→downstream effect, and P(A) is the probability that A is endorsed. For example, 𝑃(Appraisals →
   384→Threat ∣ Appraisals) represents the probability that, when appraisals are endorsed, they lead to
   385→a sense of current threat.
   386→The child-conditioned probability, 𝑃(𝐴 → 𝐵 ∣ 𝐵), was defined as the proportion of
   387→assessments in which 𝐴 was reported as causing 𝐵, among assessments in which 𝐵 was
   388→endorsed. For example, 𝑃(Appraisals → Threat ∣ Threat) reflects how often a sense of current
   389→threat is attributed to negative appraisals when threat is present.
   390→Whereas 𝑃(𝐴 → 𝐵 ∣ 𝐴) captures how often 𝐴 leads to 𝐵, 𝑃(𝐴 → 𝐵 ∣ 𝐵) captures how
   391→often 𝐵 is attributed to 𝐴 relative to other possible causes. Both probabilities were estimated
   392→for all theoretically permitted relations with two or more observations and used as edge weights
   393→in individualised MSNs. Agreement between the two probabilities was assessed using Pearson
   394→correlations, and within-person agreement was examined as a function of individual
   395→observations, to examine whether MSN estimation is constrained by data limitations.
   396→Directional edge asymmetry
   397→To assess whether MSN captures perceived directionality, conditional probabilities were
   398→compared for pairs of edges connecting the same components in opposite directions. For each
   399→pair, mean conditional probabilities and 95% confidence intervals were estimated across
   400→participants, and odds ratios (ORs) were computed for A→B relative to B→A. Analyses focused
   401→on relations between triggers, appraisals, and trauma memory, which were assigned symmetric
   402→
   403→a priori likelihoods. Edges involving threat or strategies were excluded due to asymmetric
   404→querying6.
   405→Network complexity
   406→Network complexity was quantified to capture the extent to which a network contained
   407→differentiated information beyond the mere presence or absence of connections. Complexity
   408→combined network density (the proportion of non-zero edges) with structural differentiation,
   409→indexed using normalized degree entropy (𝐻 ), which reflects how unevenly connections are
   410→norm
   411→distributed across nodes. Complexity was defined as:
   412→Complexity = Density×𝐻
   413→norm
   414→The resulting metric ranges from 0 to 1, where values closer to 0 indicate networks that
   415→are either sparse or structurally homogeneous (i.e., connections are distributed relatively
   416→evenly across components), whereas values closer to 1 indicate networks that are both densely
   417→connected and differentiated. Conceptually, higher complexity reflects a greater number of
   418→distinct and non-uniform maintaining mechanisms. Such formulations may offer richer
   419→opportunities for identifying intervention targets and tracking changes over the course of
   420→treatment, although the clinical utility of this metric remains to be established empirically.
   421→Complexity scores were compared across networks using analysis of variance
   422→(ANOVA). Because MSN networks are directional, complexity was also computed on undirected
   423→versions by collapsing directional edges using an either-or rule that retained the larger of the
   424→two directional edges. Associations between complexity and number of individual data were
   425→examined using Pearson correlations to assess the extent to which complexity depended on
   426→data volume.

   442→comparison benchmarks MSN against this standard, testing whether MSN captures additional
   443→structure beyond undirected co-occurrence. We compared the methods on edge-weight
   444→correlation, network complexity, and edge detection.
   445→Edge detection
   446→MSN and Ising networks were binarised based on the presence of non-zero edges. Sensitivity
   447→(recall; proportion of reference edges correctly identified), specificity (proportion of reference
   448→non-edges correctly identified), precision (proportion of identified edges that are present in the
   449→reference), and accuracy (overall proportion of correctly classified edges and non-edges) were
   450→computed for each individual, using each method as the reference, in turn. Metrics were
   451→summarised descriptively across participants.
   452→Network saturation
   453→Network saturation was assessed to determine how much data are required for MSN networks
   454→to stabilise, inspired by the approach taken by Burger et al. [18]. For each participant, networks
   455→were re-estimated sequentially using increasing proportions of the individual data, starting from
   456→the first observation and adding one observation at a time until the full sample was reached. At
   457→each step, uncertainty in edge estimates was quantified using 1,000 bootstrap resamples.
   458→Partial-data networks were then compared with the final network by evaluating edge-wise
   459→recovery, defined as whether the partial estimate fell within the 95% confidence interval of the
   460→final estimate. Saturation was quantified as the proportion of recovered edges at each data
   461→proportion.
   462→All analyses were conducted in R [35], version 4.5.1. Statistical tests were two-sided,
   463→and effect sizes are reported where applicable.

   465→Results
   466→The sample comprised 46 participants (age M±SD=33.2±13.2 years; 47.8% female, 50.0% male,
   467→2.2% non-binary; see Table 1 for sociodemographic characteristics). The PCL-5 score was
   468→51.7±12.7, with the most common trauma source being work-related trauma among police
   469→personnel (32.7%), followed by sexual violence (17.4%) and interpersonal trauma (13.0%); most
   470→participants reported direct exposure (80.4%) and/or exposure through a professional role
   471→(43.5%)7. Participants contributed a total of 1,958 observations (M±SD=42.6±36.5 per person;
   472→median [Md]±MAD=42.5±19.3) over 21.6±19.8 days, with a mean response rate of 0.76±0.27
   473→(Md±MAD=0.86±0.21). All participants produced non-empty MSN networks with at least one
   474→association between network elements.
   475→Parent- vs child-conditioned networks
   476→For most edges, parent-conditioned and child-conditioned estimates were highly correlated
   477→(r=.88, p<.0001; Sup. Figures S1-S2), indicating that the likelihood of a causal relation was
   478→broadly similar whether conditioning on the antecedent or the effect. Conceptually, the two
   479→capture complementary perspectives: parent-conditioned probabilities reflect forward
   480→propagation (what a component leads to), whereas child-conditioned probabilities reflect
   481→attribution (what explains an outcome). The parent-child correlation was not significantly
   482→related to the number of individual observations (r=.09, p=.56). Given their high convergence,
   483→we averaged the two methods to obtain a more stable summary of perceived directional
   484→relations for the main analyses.
   485→Directional edge asymmetry
   486→Clear directional preferences emerged for triggers → memory (W=0.308, 95% CI [0.24, 0.37])
   487→over memory → triggers (W=0.04, 95% CI [0.02, 0.07]; OR=20.1, 95% CI [8.60, 47.7]). To
   488→demonstrate how this dominance manifests on the individual level, participant 27 (see Sup.
   489→Figures S3 for individual MSNs), who experienced an interpersonal trauma, reported sensory
   490→triggers (specific sounds and smells) and an emotional trigger (feeling watched by others) that
   491→were much more likely to lead to intrusive traumatic memories (W=0.75) than be followed by
   492→them (W=0.11).
   493→7 Categories were not mutually exclusive
   494→8 Edge weights (W) represent the conditional probability for a causal effect between A→B, averaged across the
   495→conditional probability given the cause (A) or the outcome (B).

   497→A directional preference was also noted for triggers → appraisals (W=0.15, 95% CI [0.10,
   498→0.20]) over appraisals → triggers (W=0.03, 95% CI [0.01, 0.04]; OR=4.98, 95% CI [2.34, 11.1]),
   499→aligning with the theoretical predictions of Ehlers and Clark [26] and our hypotheses. For
   500→example, participant 43 experienced bereavement-related trauma following child loss, with
   501→sensory triggers including environmental cues (e.g., sounds, seeing children of a similar age),
   502→and experiencing positive emotions. These triggers were likely to lead to negative appraisals
   503→(W=0.42) about the self (“I can’t handle minor upsets”, “I was happy and now I’m miserable”)
   504→and the world (“I must always stay on guard”). The reverse direction, in which appraisals led to
   505→being triggered, was less likely (W=0.32), although still moderate.
   506→In contrast, no clear directional dominance was observed between appraisals and
   507→trauma memory: appraisals → memory (W=0.34, 95% CI [0.27, 0.41]) did not reliably exceed
   508→memory → appraisals (W=0.23, 95% CI [0.17, 0.28]; OR=0.55, 95% CI [0.22, 1.35]), also aligning
   509→with the theoretical proposition that these elements influence each other reciprocally (Figure
   510→2A). For example, participant 13, with assault-related trauma, showed a bidirectional pattern
   511→between negative appraisals and trauma memories. Negative appraisals (“The event happened
   512→because of me”, “Can’t know who will harm you”, “Must always be careful”) were about as
   513→likely to lead to trauma memories (W=0.39) as trauma memories were to activate these
   514→appraisals (W=0.42).
   515→Importantly, as shown in the individual MSN networks (Sup. Figures S3), the absence of
   516→a group-level directional preference did not reflect similar probabilities in both directions at the
   517→individual level. Rather, it arose from heterogeneity across participants. Some showed a clear
   518→preference for appraisals → memory (e.g., P3, P23, P42), whereas others showed the opposite
   519→pattern, with memory → appraisals (e.g., P14, P20, P24).
   520→MSN vs Ising Model
   521→Correlation and complexity. MSN networks differed from Ising networks. Although edge
   522→weights were significantly correlated, the association was weak (r=.16, p=.0005), indicating
   523→limited overlap between the two representations. MSN networks exhibited substantially greater
   524→complexity than Ising networks, with undirected MSN showing the highest values (Ising
   525→complexity: 0.15±0.18; directed MSN: 0.50±0.19; undirected MSN: 0.72 ± 0.21; F =7.55,
   526→(2,272)
   527→p<.0001, η²=.61; Figure 2B). Complexity in Ising networks correlated with complexity in both
   528→directed MSN (r=.36, p=.015) and undirected MSN (r=.37, p=.012). Ising complexity was
   529→positively correlated with individual sample size (r=.41, p=.004), whereas MSN complexity was
   530→not significantly related to sample size (r=.20, ps>.17), reflecting that MSN complexity shows
   531→
   532→independence from the amount of data individuals provide. Sensitivity analyses with
   533→unregularised Ising models confirmed these results (see supplementary). Figure 3 illustrates
   534→examples of MSN across different complexity levels.
   535→Edge detection. Using Ising as the reference, MSN showed high sensitivity (M±SE = 0.85±0.06)
   536→but low specificity (0.29±0.03) and precision (0.19±0.03), indicating that most Ising edges were
   537→recovered alongside many additional relations. Conversely, using MSN as the reference, Ising
   538→networks showed high specificity (0.89±0.04) and precision (0.85±0.06), but low sensitivity
   539→(0.19±0.03). These patterns indicate that MSN identifies more edges than the Ising model,
   540→reflecting higher sensitivity (Figure 2C). Overall accuracy was moderate (0.40±0.03). Sensitivity
   541→analyses with unregularised Ising networks showed similar metrics (see supplementary). This
   542→asymmetric pattern is consistent with the higher complexity of MSN, which captured most Ising
   543→edges but include additional relations.
   544→Sup. Figures S3 present individual-level visual comparisons between MSN and Ising
   545→networks, including density, complexity, and edge-weight correlations.
   546→Network saturation
   547→MSN networks converged rapidly toward their final structure (Figure 2D). With 70% of the data,
   548→networks closely approximated the full structure with high confidence (M±SD=0.99±0.12, 95%
   549→CI [0.97, 1.00]). Even with fewer observations, partial MSN captured a substantial proportion of
   550→the final structure: 0.93±0.26 (95% CI [0.88, 0.96]) of the final network structure at 60% of the
   551→data, 0.83±0.38 (95% CI [0.78, 0.88]) at 40%, and 0.60±0.49 (95% CI [0.53, 0.67]) at 20%.
   552→Notably, even with only 10% of the data, MSN captured over half of the final structure
   553→(M±SD=0.51±0.50, 95% CI [0.41, 0.59]). Overall, these results indicate high network stability
   554→and suggest that core structural features appear early, even with limited data.

   737→Interim discussion 1
   738→The results demonstrate the utility of MSN in a collated sample of 46 trauma-exposed
   739→individuals. MSN identified directional relations and complex network structures using
   740→relatively little data, showed higher sensitivity than Ising models, and converged rapidly to
   741→stable network estimates. These features make MSN well suited for clinical applications,
   742→particularly when monitoring change over time without imposing substantial burden on
   743→patients. Importantly, MSN goes beyond simply counting perceived causal links by estimating
   744→how likely one process leads to another, depending on whether we start from the cause or the
   745→outcome [36]. This allows relations to be examined from two complementary perspectives and
   746→supports testing theoretical predictions, such as whether some processes reinforce each other
   747→or operate more in one direction.
   748→Across analyses, MSN networks were stable, exhibited clear directional patterns, and
   749→identified meaningful differences between individual presentations. This potentially makes
   750→MSN particularly useful in clinical settings, as it provides a structured yet flexible approach for
   751→translating established theoretical models into individualised case formulations. Because
   752→these models are already familiar to clinicians and guide effective interventions, MSN offers an
   753→intuitive framework while still allowing individuals to diverge from expected patterns when their
   754→experiences differ, as reflected in the substantial heterogeneity of networks observed despite
   755→the shared theoretical framework and standardized assessment procedure. Compared with
   756→traditional Ising models, MSN recovered most of the same relations while identifying additional
   757→structure, resulting in richer and more informative networks.
   758→These findings suggest that MSN may provide a useful framework for informing
   759→treatment planning and evaluating therapeutic processes, even with relatively sparse data. In
   760→the next section, we illustrate the integration of MSN into routine clinical care through a case
   761→study of a client undergoing trauma-focused therapy, demonstrating how MSN can be used to
   762→track network change over time and support clinical decision-making.
   763→
   764→Case study: MSN in Cognitive Therapy for PTSD
   765→To illustrate the clinical utility of MSN, we present a case study of a client diagnosed with PTSD
   766→who received Cognitive Therapy for PTSD (CT-PTSD) [29] while completing repeated MSN
   767→assessments before, during, and after treatment (see client’s item selection in the
   768→supplementary materials).
   769→The client was a highly educated, high-functioning European male in his late thirties
   770→who sought treatment for trauma-related difficulties linked to the death of a family member
   771→during adolescence. His presentation was characterised by negative appraisals involving self-
   772→blame and guilt, avoidance of memories and reminders, and triggers associated with family
   773→contexts (e.g., gatherings, observing other families). He also reported heightened vigilance
   774→around safety and potential harm. As therapy progressed, the clinical formulation became
   775→more complex, with early family dynamics emerging as relevant contextual factors beyond the
   776→bereavement itself. The client identified negative schemas, rumination and intrusive memories
   777→as central components of his trauma experience and primarily relied on suppression and
   778→avoidance as coping strategies. He completed MSN and EMA assessments for a 2.5-week
   779→baseline period prior to treatment (five assessments per day: two MSN and three EMA), once
   780→daily during treatment and for one month following treatment, and an additional three weeks of
   781→intensive post-treatment assessment (five assessments per day; two MSN), yielding
   782→approximately two months of follow-up data. Participation included £140 compensation for
   783→pre- and post-treatment assessments.
   784→MSN networks were estimated on a weekly basis across pre-, within-, and post-
   785→treatment periods to examine shifts in causal dynamics between model components over time.
   786→We computed a network complexity score for each week and tested linear, quadratic and cubic
   787→trends9 by fitting polynomial regression models of increasing order, to observe their unfolding
   788→dynamics across time and per phase (baseline, during therapy, and follow-up). We
   789→hypothesised that as treatment progressed, network complexity would decrease, reflecting
   790→weaker influence of trauma components on one another. To evaluate temporal weakening or
   791→strengthening of specific causal relations, we fitted logistic regressions predicting the
   792→probability of a perceived causal edge conditional on the parent (antecedent) or child
   793→(outcome) component. Treatment phase, week, and their interaction were included as
   794→predictors to identify phase-specific week-by-week changes in causal strength. P-values were
   795→9 Linear, quadratic, and cubic trends test whether change over time follows a straight-line, curved, or
   796→more complex trajectory, respectively.

   900→Case study: interim discussion 2
   901→The case study demonstrates the practical application of MSN within a clinical context. MSN
   902→was used across three critical treatment phases. First, during pre-treatment assessment, it
   903→enabled the development of a data-informed clinical formulation and highlighted potential
   904→treatment targets. Second, MSN was used throughout therapy to track changes in network
   905→structure and evolving clinical dynamics over time. Finally, post-treatment assessment was
   906→used to evaluate treatment outcomes and their maintenance. Taken together, MSN provided a
   907→detailed understanding of clinical processes throughout treatment.
   908→The MSN revealed substantial changes in the network over time, both in overall network
   909→complexity and in specific processes. Most notably, we observed reductions in the influence of
   910→trauma memory and negative appraisals on one another, alongside weaker downstream effects
   911→on the sense of current threat and maladaptive coping strategies. These changes aligned with
   912→the therapeutic targets suggested by the baseline formulation networks and support the
   913→potential utility of MSN for both treatment planning and treatment monitoring.
   914→One particularly notable finding was that changes in network processes were non-
   915→linear. During early treatment, overall complexity and several specific associations initially
   916→strengthened rather than weakened. This pattern aligns with clinical theories of non-linear
   917→change [38–40], and specifically accounts suggesting that the early stages of treatment may be
   918→accompanied by temporary increases in distress before improvement occurs [41,42]. Although
   919→complexity decreased later in treatment, it increased again during mid-treatment, further
   920→emphasizing the non-linear nature of therapeutic change. Nevertheless, by the end of
   921→treatment, the network demonstrated lower overall complexity and weaker associations
   922→between key processes, consistent with clinical improvement.
   923→This case study illustrates how MSN can be translated from clinical research into
   924→routine care. One of MSN’s core principles is its ability to generate rich formulation networks
   925→while minimizing burden. Here, although the client completed MSN assessments twice per day
   926→(alongside three traditional EMA) during baseline and post-treatment phases, he completed
   927→assessments only once daily during therapy, which remained sufficient to generate informative
   928→network structures. This allowed MSN to be implemented across many weeks of treatment,
   929→providing detailed insight into the therapeutic process. This represents a major advantage
   930→relative to traditional EMA-based network approaches, suggesting that relatively low-burden
   931→assessments requiring only a few minutes per day—or any frequency jointly agreed upon by
   932→clinician and patient/client—may be sufficient to generate clinical formulation networks.
   933→
   934→Main discussion
   935→In this study, we introduce Momentary Structured Networks (MSN), a method for estimating
   936→perceived causal relations based on personalised data and theory-driven models of
   937→psychopathology, illustrated here using the psychiatric condition of PTSD and the Ehlers and
   938→Clark model of the disorder [26]. Using momentary assessments of perceived causal links, MSN
   939→generates individualised network representations that reflect a person’s case formulation. We
   940→show that these networks can be estimated with relatively little data, in some cases from only a
   941→few observations, while remaining rich and interpretable. Across individuals, MSN showed
   942→substantial complexity and consistent directional patterns that were largely independent of
   943→sample size, suggesting that meaningful structure can be captured without extensive data
   944→collection. The approach is also feasible in routine clinical practice, relying on brief repeated
   945→assessments that can be integrated into routine monitoring. Network saturation analyses
   946→indicated rapid convergence toward stable network structures, highlighting MSN’s potential
   947→utility early in treatment, when formulation is most critical. Compared with traditional
   948→association-based Ising models, MSN recovered most relations identified by those traditional
   949→methods while also capturing additional structure, resulting in higher overall complexity. A
   950→clinical case example illustrated how an MSN can be used to track changes over the course of
   951→treatment and to generate hypotheses about the processes underlying therapeutic gain.
   952→The advantages and limitations of MSN are best understood in relation to existing
   953→idiographic approaches, including traditional EMA-based networks and recent developments in
   954→perceived causal estimation, most notably L-PECAN [18,43] and PREMISE [11]. PREMISE uses
   955→Bayesian updating to combine prior knowledge with EMA data, but its reliance on patient insight
   956→while remaining agnostic to theory, alongside its relatively high data demands, may limit clinical
   957→feasibility. L-PECAN reduces burden by allowing patients to report perceived causal relations
   958→day to day without explicitly rating association strength [44]. However, its largely agnostic
   959→structure creates a broad and unconstrained space of possible relations, increasing cognitive
   960→demands, potentially reducing stability, and limiting personalisation and between-person
   961→comparison [45–47].
   962→MSN is designed to address these limitations by combining perceived causal elicitation
   963→with a theory-constrained, hypothesis-confirming framework. Rather than allowing unrestricted
   964→enumeration of possible relations, MSN enforces a structured model in which some
   965→associations are explored bidirectionally when theoretically plausible (e.g., trauma memory ↔
   966→negative appraisals), while others are queried asymmetrically or only when disconfirmed (e.g.,
   967→
   968→triggers → threat). This approach reduces combinatorial complexity while retaining sensitivity to
   969→individual variation. Anchoring idiographic elicitation in established theory provides an
   970→important advantage. Models such as Ehlers and Clark are grounded in decades of empirical
   971→and experimental work and have informed effective treatments, meaning that the relations they
   972→specify are both likely to be present and clinically relevant for many patients [48,49]. MSN uses
   973→this structure as a starting point, increasing the probability that reported relations are relevant
   974→to the specific clinical presentation while still allowing individuals to diverge from model
   975→predictions by endorsing alternative or disconfirming patterns. Although illustrated here using
   976→the Ehlers and Clark model, MSN is not tied to a specific theory and can be adapted to other
   977→frameworks by changing the components and queries.
   978→The case study illustrates the value of this approach in clinical care and treatment. MSN
   979→was able to track how specific relations changed over the course of therapy, showing which
   980→links weakened, which remained stable, and how the overall structure shifted with treatment.
   981→This makes it possible to move beyond symptom change and instead examine the processes
   982→driving improvement, generating hypotheses about what mechanisms are being targeted and
   983→when. Notably, even with one daily assessment, MSN remained interpretable, suggesting it can
   984→be implemented in routine care without placing excessive burden on patients.
   985→Limitations
   986→Several limitations should be considered. First, MSN is theory-grounded, and structuring
   987→elicitation around a specific model deliberately restricts the range of possible relations and
   988→privileges theory-consistent pathways over alternative explanations [46]. While this improves
   989→interpretability and reduces noise, it limits exploratory discovery. That is especially true for
   990→participants with comorbidity (e.g., PTSD and depression), where multiple interacting
   991→processes may require combining models across disorders or using transdiagnostic
   992→frameworks. That said, MSN can be adapted to test competing theories, and, critically, patients
   993→can always endorse relations that diverge from model predictions.
   994→Second, MSN captures perceived causal relations rather than causal mechanisms in
   995→the formal sense. As emphasized in causal inference frameworks [50], causal claims require
   996→assumptions and interventions beyond observational data. Moreover, estimated relations
   997→reflect momentary perceived influence rather than time-lagged dynamics. MSN should
   998→therefore be understood as capturing potential explanatory structures relevant to case
   999→formulation, rather than providing definitive causal inference. In addition, personalised items
  1000→
  1001→were fixed following the initial formulation phase, limiting the introduction of new components,
  1002→or even new aspects of measured constructs (e.g., new triggers), as therapy progressed.
  1003→Third, although the shared theoretical structure supports group-level analyses and
  1004→allows quantifying shared mechanisms (such as the percentage of participants presenting a
  1005→strong edge or a central component), MSN remains idiographic, and between-person
  1006→comparisons should be interpreted cautiously.
  1007→Finally, while we demonstrate MSN’s added value over association-based network
  1008→methods like Ising models, the comparison itself has limitations. Ising models may partly
  1009→reflect symptom severity rather than true co-variation [51], which should be considered when
  1010→interpreting differences between approaches.
  1011→Future directions
  1012→Future work should address these limitations by incorporating competing theoretical models or
  1013→extending the framework to transdiagnostic approaches [52], thereby enabling within- and
  1014→between-person comparisons of alternative explanatory frameworks and direct tests of theory-
  1015→driven versus exploratory elicitation strategies. Another possibility is to create a dataset of
  1016→which causal relations are likely or unlikely between a given set of symptoms or pathological
  1017→processes a specific patient presents with, across traditional diagnostic categories or disorder
  1018→models [53]. Beyond PTSD, MSN should be extended to other conditions and theoretical
  1019→frameworks to evaluate its generalisability and clinical utility. Greater flexibility could be
  1020→introduced by allowing dynamic updating of items as treatment progresses. Integration with
  1021→conversational agents based on large language models (LLMs) may further enhance MSN by
  1022→enabling flexible identification of formulation components and causal pathways from
  1023→naturalistic dialogue. Rather than relying on fixed EMA items, open-ended prompts could be
  1024→used and subsequently structured using AI annotation. While current applications of LLM in
  1025→mental health research have focused primarily on diagnosis and intervention [54,55], their use
  1026→in supporting case formulation represents a largely unexplored and potentially impactful
  1027→direction. Most importantly, future work should evaluate whether MSN and similar frameworks
  1028→improve treatment outcomes, inform clinical decision-making, support more appropriate levels
  1029→of care, and ultimately increase the efficiency of mental health services.
  1030→Conclusion
  1031→Momentary Structured Networks provide a novel framework for probing perceived causal
  1032→relations in real time. By constraining elicitation to theoretically plausible pathways, MSN
  1033→
  1034→reduces data demands and exploratory overload, yielding stable networks with interpretable
  1035→directional structure even from limited data. At the same time, MSN preserves sensitivity to
  1036→individual differences, allowing a variety of associations to emerge. Despite remaining
  1037→limitations, MSN offer a promising approach for Momentary Estimation of Structural Symptom
  1038→Interactions, assessing therapeutic change, and for formalising case formulation in a data-
  1039→informed and feasible manner.
