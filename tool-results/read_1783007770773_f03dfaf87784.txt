    13→Promoting well-doing: A Positive Psychology intervention for regulating
    14→performance momentum and thriving among high achievers
    15→The present study examined a novel journalling intervention designed to support
    16→subjective wellbeing and perceived work performance among high achievers.
    17→Sixty-seven university students reporting a grade average of B+ or higher
    18→completed a one-week, within-subjects pre–post study involving a daily ‘well
    19→done’ journalling task focused on small actions done well. Subjective wellbeing
    20→and perceived work performance were measured pre- and post- intervention; with
    21→journalling frequency and qualitative feedback collected afterwards. Findings
    22→indicated no detectable change in wellbeing from an elevated baseline, however,
    23→a significant improvement in perceived work performance was observed. Post-
    24→hoc analyses revealed a dose–response pattern, with stronger performance
    25→improvements among participants with frequent task engagement. Qualitative
    26→feedback was generally favourable, suggesting perceived benefits in positive
    27→affect, self-esteem, and motivation. Overall, results show preliminary support for
    28→‘well done’ journalling to enhance perceived performance in high-achieving
    29→students.
    30→Keywords: high achievers; positive psychology; work performance;
    31→interventions; achievement
    32→Introduction
    33→High achievers are individuals characterised by high personal standards, sustained goal
    34→commitment, and strong performance motivation (Hirt et al., 2021). A substantial body
    35→of research has demonstrated that high achievers tie their identity and self-worth to
    36→demonstrable accomplishment and competence (Crocker & Wolfe, 2001; Dweck &
    37→Leggett, 1988; Nerstad et al., 2018). While such individuals frequently attain
    38→objectively high levels of success across domains including sport, academia, and
    39→leadership (Dickinson & Dickinson, 2014; Luthar et al., 2020; Rassaby et al., 2021),
    40→In-Confidence
    41→
    42→3
    43→research indicates that they may simultaneously be vulnerable to reductions in
    44→subjective wellbeing (SWB; Barnard, 2008; Collin et al., 2020; Galbraith et al., 2015;
    45→Luthar et al., 2020; Rassaby et al., 2021).
    46→SWB refers to an individuals’ evaluation of their own life and typically
    47→encompasses life satisfaction, positive affect, and low negative affect (Bücker et al.,
    48→2018). Higher levels of SWB have been associated with greater intrinsic motivation,
    49→creativity, and work performance (Amabile & Kramer, 2011; Donaldson et al., 2022;
    50→Oswald et al., 2015; Ryan & Deci, 2001). Conversely, diminished SWB is linked to
    51→impaired functioning, maladaptive coping strategies, burnout, and increased risk of
    52→mental illness (Barnard, 2008; Collin et al., 2020). These effects appear particularly
    53→salient among high-achieving populations, including senior corporate leaders and high-
    54→performing university students, who report elevated levels of stress, perfectionistic
    55→concerns, anxiety, and emotional exhaustion (Galbraith et al., 2015; Ganesh et al., 2018;
    56→Rassaby et al., 2021).
    57→But why do high achievers exhibit declining SWB? Several decades of research
    58→have provided evidence that while many high achievers value personal growth, they are
    59→strongly orientated toward demonstrating competence relative to others (Dweck &
    60→Leggett, 1988; Nerstad et al., 2018). When combined with contingent self-worth, in
    61→which self-esteem depends on personally valued outcomes, this orientation can produce
    62→heightened sensitivity to achievement-relevant cues and outcomes (Ståhlberg et al.,
    63→2019). Over time, such patterns may encourage selective attention toward large
    64→accomplishments while rendering incremental or smaller achievements less salient
    65→(Crocker & Wolfe, 2001). Although this orientation may support goal achievement and
    66→persistence, it may also reduce opportunities to experience frequent, competence-
    67→affirming positive affect derived from everyday progress.
    68→In-Confidence
    69→
    70→4
    71→Studies on motivation and performance show that people often fixate on large
    72→goals or end results and underappreciate incremental progress unless it is made visible
    73→(Amabile & Kramer, 2011). Yet even minor achievements, when appraised as
    74→noteworthy, can reinforce a sense of competence and agency, thereby supporting
    75→intrinsic motivation (Bromberg-Martin et al., 2010; Nummenmaa & Niemi, 2004).
    76→Importantly, small achievements may generate more frequent and sustainable positive
    77→affect than larger achievements, as these can be accompanied by prolonged stress,
    78→heightened expectations, or an immediate escalation toward new goals (Weick, 1984).
    79→In sum, reflecting on small achievements may increase SWB and work performance,
    80→particularly among outcome-driven high achievers.
    81→Reflective writing or journalling has long been seen as an effective approach for
    82→encouraging such reflection (Hoult et al., 2025). Reflective writing interventions have
    83→been associated with increased confidence, motivation, and performance
    84→(Hatzigeorgiadis et al., 2009; Matsuo, 2022). In particular, gratitude-based journalling
    85→interventions have been consistently shown to increase SWB across diverse populations
    86→(Emmons & McCullough, 2003; Gander et al., 2016; Gander et al., 2017).
    87→The ‘Three Good Things’ exercise (Seligman et al., 2005), for example, is
    88→arguably one of the most well-supported positive psychology interventions of this kind.
    89→In this intervention, participants write down three things that went well each day and
    90→provide a written explanation of their causes. In the initial study, completing this task
    91→for one week produced sustained improvements in happiness and reductions in
    92→depressive symptoms for several months, particularly among those who continued the
    93→practice (Seligman et al., 2005).
    94→However, such interventions have not been adequately tested with high-
    95→achieving populations. In light of their dominant achievement orientation, it seems
    96→In-Confidence
    97→
    98→5
    99→possible that they may respond differently to open-ended reflection tasks. In particular,
   100→being instructed to reflect on what “went well” may inadvertently activate outcome-
   101→based evaluation, that is, draw attention to incomplete tasks (e.g., what has not been
   102→achieved on one's to-do list), as unfinished tasks tend to remain cognitively salient and
   103→more readily accessible than completed ones (Syrek & Antoni, 2014; Syrek et al.,
   104→2017). For individuals prone to high standards and achievement-contingent self-worth,
   105→such activation may intensify rumination and stress rather than alleviate it (Flett et al.,
   106→2002; Özhan & Yüksel, 2022; Schmeer, 2019). Furthermore, high achievers may
   107→discount smaller things that fail to meet their elevated standards, thereby undermining
   108→the intended effect (Collin et al., 2020; Galbraith et al., 2015; Madigan, 2019; Rassaby
   109→et al., 2021).
   110→These considerations suggest that while daily positive reflection may be
   111→beneficial in general populations, it may require adaptation for high achievers.
   112→Specifically, interventions that remain open-ended in what qualifies as “good” may
   113→inadvertently dilute or even counteract their intended effects. A more constrained
   114→approach may therefore be warranted.
   115→More particularly, encouraging reflection on small, modest ‘well done’
   116→moments—such as making the bed, choosing the stairs, or initiating a difficult
   117→conversation—behaviours that high achievers might not otherwise identify as worthy
   118→accomplishments, may, by shifting the focus away from the magnitude of achievement
   119→to a specific well done moment, be better positioned to increase SWB and perceived
   120→work performance in this population.

   124→Current study
   125→The current study tests this proposition by examining the effects of a brief, daily ‘well
   126→done’ journalling intervention on subjective wellbeing and perceived performance
   127→among high-achieving students. The following two hypotheses were proposed:
   128→H1: Participants will demonstrate an increase in subjective wellbeing following
   129→a week-long daily journalling intervention reflecting on activities they did well each
   130→day.
   131→H2: Participants will show an increase in perceived performance following a
   132→week-long daily journalling intervention reflecting on activities they did well each day.
   133→.
   134→Method
   135→All hypotheses, design, planned sample sizes, and the analysis plan were preregistered
   136→and are publicly available at https://aspredicted.org/d2ws-rfpg.pdf. An a priori power
   137→analysis was performed using G*Power 3.1.9.7 and indicated 41 participants were
   138→needed to detect improvements of a moderate effect size, d = 0.4, β = .80, α = .05, for
   139→this proof-of-concept study.
   140→Participants
   141→A total of 74 self-identified high-achieving first year psychology students were recruited
   142→to participate as part of their course work. Based on previous research, high achievers
   143→were defined a priori as students with a B+ average in the previous semester; exclusion
   144→criteria were a self-reported grade average below B+ and age under 18 years (Rassaby
   145→et al., 2021). Two participants were excluded after data collection, one who initially
   146→indicated a below B+ average but then re-entered the study reporting a higher grade to
   147→gain acceptance, and another which was identified as a bot by the Qualtrics software.

   151→Additionally, five participants did not complete the study, reducing the sample to 67
   152→participants – an attrition rate of 9.46%. No statistically significant differences were
   153→found between those retained and those lost to attrition. Of the final sample 59 (88.1%)
   154→identified as women and 61 (91.0%) were aged 18-24 years. Participants were offered
   155→course credits and entry in a prize draw in appreciation of their participation. The
   156→research was approved by the Human Research Ethics Committee of the University of
   157→Canterbury – HREC 2023/05/BL – and all participants provided consent before
   158→beginning the study. All procedures performed in the study were in accordance with the
   159→ethical standards of the 1964 Helsinki declaration and its later amendments or

   161→Measures
   162→Subjective wellbeing
   163→This was assessed at pre-test and post-test with the 8-item Flourishing Scale (FS; Diener
   164→et al., 2010). Participants indicated their agreement with wellbeing related statements
   165→using a 1-7 Likert scale (1 = “strongly disagree”, 7 = “strongly agree”), with the scores
   166→from all items summed as a measure of overall wellbeing. Example items include “I
   167→lead a purposeful and meaningful life” and “I am engaged and interested in my daily
   168→activities” (see appendix for the full scale). Scores may range from 8 (lowest possible
   169→wellbeing) to 56 (highest possible wellbeing). This is a brief measure which captures
   170→eudaimonic aspects of SWB without risking increased attrition due to respondent
   171→burden, and it has been validated with a New Zealand population (Diener et al., 2010;
   172→Hone et al., 2014; Rolstad et al., 2011). Reliability analysis showed good internal
   173→consistency (α = .85, M = 48.08, SD = 4.51).
   174→In-Confidence
   175→

   177→Work performance
   178→Perceived work performance was measured at pre-test and post-test using the Work
   179→Performance Scale (WPS; Crum, 2011), an 8-item measure that was adapted from the
   180→more expansive Role-Based Performance Scale developed by Welbourne et al. (1998).
   181→Example items include “quantity of work output” and “sustaining focus and
   182→engagement”, with participants rating each on a 1-5 Likert scale (1 = “needs much
   183→improvement”, 5 = “excellent”), and their overall performance equating to their mean
   184→score across all items (see appendix for the full scale). This scale has been used
   185→successfully in intervention studies to assess changes in productivity and work
   186→performance, including within New Zealand populations (Allan, 2023). Reliability
   187→analyses indicated good internal consistency (α = .76, M = 3.45, SD = 0.50).
   188→Journalling frequency
   189→At post-test, participants reported the number of days they completed the journalling
   190→task on a 0-7 scale (“On how many days did you complete the 'well done' moment
   191→journalling task?”).
   192→Open-ended feedback
   193→An optional free-text item at post-test captured qualitative feedback (“Give some
   194→feedback on the journalling task - was it helpful? Why?”).
   195→Design and procedure
   196→The study followed a within-subjects pre-post design. Participants were recruited via
   197→advertisement on the University of Canterbury’s Sona Systems participant management
   198→website, which linked participants to a Qualtrics survey that included study information
   199→In-Confidence
   200→
   201→9
   202→and an electronic consent form outlining the main elements of the research. Recruitment
   203→was open with self-selected rolling participation for 18 days (July 28th to August 14th).
   204→Data collection ended on August 22nd.
   205→After providing consent, participants completed the baseline (pre-test) WPS and
   206→FS measures before engaging in a one-week journalling exercise. Immediately
   207→following the intervention week, participants completed post-test WPS and FS
   208→measures, reported their journalling frequency and provided optional qualitative
   209→feedback in a second Qualtrics survey.
   210→Intervention
   211→Participants were asked to spend approximately 10 minutes every evening, reflecting on
   212→and journalling about three “well done moments” which were defined as “a small action
   213→or decision that usually goes unnoticed but deserves a pat on the back”, – the full
   214→instructions for the journalling activity are in the appendix – journalling could be
   215→completed in any preferred format; instructions were displayed in Qualtrics and emailed
   216→to participants after baseline completion.
   217→Prompts and reminders
   218→Three email reminders were sent during the intervention, on days three, five, and seven,
   219→with the final reminder also prompting return to Qualtrics to complete the post-test
   220→measures the following day. Participants had three days to complete the post-test
   221→measures; non-completion within this window was coded as attrition.

   325→Discussion
   326→The present study examined whether a daily journalling intervention requiring high
   327→achievers to reflect on small 'well done' moments would enhance subjective wellbeing
   328→(SWB; H1) and perceived work performance (H2) over the course of one week. While
   329→SWB did not increase over the one-week intervention period, results indicated
   330→improvements in perceived work performance, particularly among participants who
   331→engaged more frequently with the task. These findings suggest that structured reflection
   332→In-Confidence
   333→

   335→on modest, 'well done' moments may support performance perceptions in high-
   336→achieving individuals, even over a relatively short timeframe.
   337→Hypothesis 1 – subjective wellbeing
   338→H1 predicted that participants would demonstrate increased SWB following the
   339→intervention. This hypothesis was not supported and is not in keeping with prior
   340→research on positive psychology interventions. Specifically, no significant pre–post
   341→improvements in SWB were observed (Gander et al., 2016; Gander et al., 2017;
   342→Seligman et al., 2005; Southwell & Gould, 2017).
   343→Several factors may account for this pattern. First, baseline wellbeing levels in
   344→the present sample were high, limiting the capacity for measurable increases. Positive
   345→psychology interventions (PPIs) are often less effective among individuals who already
   346→report elevated wellbeing (Espinosa et al., 2025; Gander et al., 2016), and ceiling effects
   347→may therefore have constrained observable change. Second, the Flourishing Scale
   348→primarily captures eudaimonic aspects of wellbeing, which may be less sensitive to
   349→short-term fluctuations than affect-based measures commonly used in prior intervention
   350→research (Diener et al., 2010; Hone et al., 2014). It is possible that subtle affective shifts
   351→occurred but were not detected by the selected measure.
   352→It is also plausible that a one-week period is insufficient for broader changes in
   353→eudaimonic wellbeing to consolidate, particularly within high-achieving individuals
   354→whose standards and evaluative frameworks are deeply embedded. Future research
   355→incorporating longer intervention periods, affect-sensitive measures, and follow-up
   356→assessments will help clarify whether wellbeing benefits emerge over time.
   357→In-Confidence
   358→

   360→Hypothesis 2 – perceived work performance
   361→H2 predicted that participants would report increases in perceived work performance
   362→following the intervention. This hypothesis was supported. Although analytic
   363→assumptions required both parametric and non-parametric approaches, the overall
   364→pattern of findings indicated small but consistent pre–post improvements in perceived
   365→performance. Notably, a clear dose–response association emerged: participants who
   366→engaged more frequently in journalling demonstrated larger increases in perceived work
   367→performance, strengthening confidence that engagement with the intervention
   368→contributed to the observed change.
   369→Qualitative feedback converged with these quantitative findings. Many
   370→participants described increased awareness of daily achievements, greater motivation,
   371→and increased momentum in their work. While some participants initially reported
   372→ambiguity regarding what qualified as a 'well done' moment, this appeared to diminish
   373→over time as task fluency developed.
   374→The performance increases observed are theoretically coherent. Prior research
   375→suggests that daily progress is a central driver of engagement and motivation (Amabile
   376→& Kramer, 2011). Rather than relying on the impact of major achievements, the present
   377→intervention increased the frequency with which participants consciously noted modest
   378→accomplishments. Over repeated days, this accumulation of tiny successes may
   379→reinforce a sense of ongoing progress and personal effectiveness, consistent with
   380→models emphasising daily progress as a key driver of engagement and performance
   381→(Amabile & Kramer, 2011; Weick, 1984). In this view, magnitude may be less critical
   382→than regularity; that is, consistent recognition of small accomplishments may be
   383→sufficient to strengthen perceived capability and sustained effort on long-term goals.

   387→Limitations, and future directions
   388→This study provides initial evidence that reflecting on small 'well done' moments may
   389→support performance perceptions among high achievers. While no short-term changes in
   390→SWB were detected, the pattern of findings suggests that consistent engagement with
   391→the intervention is meaningfully related to perceived performance change.
   392→Several limitations should be addressed in future work. First, the within-subjects
   393→design did not include a control condition. Although the observed dose–response pattern
   394→strengthens confidence in the intervention’s relevance, causal inferences remain
   395→unwarranted without further evidence. Incorporating active comparison groups will be
   396→important for ruling out regression to the mean, expectancy effects, or time-related
   397→confounds. Including personality measures such as conscientiousness may also clarify
   398→whether individual differences account for variations in engagement and outcome
   399→change.
   400→Second, the sample consisted primarily of young women who were university
   401→students with relatively high baseline wellbeing, limiting generalisability. Replication in
   402→more diverse high-achieving populations, including business professionals or
   403→competitive athletes, will strengthen external validity. Future research should also
   404→incorporate affect-sensitive wellbeing measures and longer follow-up periods to better
   405→capture potential changes in SWB over time.
   406→Third, engagement and performance were assessed via self-report, and journal
   407→entries were not collected. While this approach reduced potential observation effects, it
   408→introduces the possibility of expectancy or social desirability bias. Future studies would
   409→benefit from incorporating objective or third-party indicators of performance, such as
   410→academic outcomes or supervisor ratings, as well as examining journal content to
   411→identify which categories of 'well done' moments are most impactful.

   415→Conclusion
   416→High achievers are often disciplined in tracking accomplishments, yet selective in what
   417→they count as an achievement. Major milestones are carefully recorded, while the daily
   418→stream of minor achievements frequently go unnoticed. The present study contributes to
   419→past work on reflection-based PPIs by demonstrating that systematically recognising
   420→those modest, everyday 'well done' moments may meaningfully shift how performance
   421→is experienced for high achiever populations.
   422→Although short-term changes in subjective wellbeing were not detected, these
   423→novel findings indicate that modest, everyday actions may affect how performance is
   424→experienced. When high achievers repeatedly acknowledge tiny, even mundane
   425→achievements, they may begin to experience greater work performance in consequence.
   426→Indeed, life is not constructed from occasional landmark events; it is built from
   427→moments so small they rarely register in memory. Recognising these moments, it seems,
   428→does not inflate achievement; it recalibrates perception, which in turn increases
   429→perceived performance. Further research will clarify how sustained attention to daily
   430→'well done' moments influences both performance and wellbeing across time, but the
   431→present study offers initial evidence that small actions, consistently noticed, are not
   432→trivial. They are impactful.

   638→Appendix
   639→Journaling task instructions
   640→In this daily journalling task, we ask you to consider your actions over the course of the
   641→day and reflect on moments you think were “well done”.
   642→What is a “Well Done” Moment?
   643→A “well done” moment is a small action or decision that usually goes unnoticed but
   644→deserves a pat on the back. It is not a large or typical achievement like finishing an
   645→assignment, getting an A+, or even completing your to-do list. Some examples could
   646→include:
   647→• You asked a question or spoke in class.
   648→• You took the stairs instead of the lift.
   649→• You went to bed earlier than usual.
   650→• You stayed calm during a difficult conversation.
   651→• You finally did something you’d been putting off.
   652→• You made your bed in the morning.
   653→• You did the dishes instead of leaving them.
   654→Please note: These are just examples. Your “well done” moments are whatever you
   655→consider to be well done.
   656→The Task
   657→Every night for one week:
   658→In-Confidence
   659→
   660→28
   661→• Take up to 10 minutes before bed.
   662→• Think back over your day.
   663→• Write down three “well done” moments.
   664→• For each one, write a few words about why you think it was well done
   665→The idea is to notice these small things you might usually overlook. You can use a
   666→notebook, a note on your phone, or type it up—whatever works best for you. You might
   667→want to keep a pen and paper near your bed as a reminder.
