    19→Revisiting the SciPop Scale: Measurement Alignment, Scoring Rules, and Cross-
    20→Context Comparability
    21→Abstract
    22→Science-related populism—populist attitudes toward scientific authority—has attracted
    23→growing scholarly attention. Although the SciPop Scale measures this construct, its cross-
    24→national psychometric properties remain underexamined. Using data from the Trust in Science
    25→and Science-Related Populism project (N = 69,341; 68 countries/regions) and the Swiss
    26→Science Barometer (N = 1,124), we evaluate factor structure and reliability, test multigroup
    27→invariance, apply alignment for approximate invariance, and compare scoring philosophies
    28→(Bollen vs. Goertz approach) on criterion validity. We further employ multidimensional item
    29→response theory models to provide complementary item-level diagnostics, including
    30→discrimination, threshold, item information, and differential item functioning. Results show
    31→that reliability is high and a four-factor model fits most broadly. Full measurement invariance
    32→holds across sex, age, education, residence, political orientation, and religiosity; partial
    33→invariance appears across regions, economic development, cultural values, and regime types.
    34→Cross-national comparability is limited but improves via measurement alignment. Different
    35→scoring methods identify different weakest dimensions. For criterion validity, Bollen-based
    36→aligned scores consistently outperform Goertz approach, yielding higher explanatory power in
    37→cross-national and cross-group analyses. Finally, we provide guidelines for researchers using
    38→the SciPop Scale and future directions for measuring science-related populism.
    39→Keywords: Science-related populism; the SciPop Scale; Measurement invariance;

    56→Introduction
    57→Global science populism
    58→Populist movements have been rising worldwide (Mede & Schäfer, 2020; Smith, 2019).
    59→Populists claim to represent the “virtuous people” in opposition to political elites and
    60→institutional authorities (Rooduijn, 2014). This anti-elite sentiment extends beyond politics to
    61→other institutions, including academic elites (Forchtner et al., 2018; Mede & Schäfer, 2020).
    62→This has given rise to science-related populism, which positions scientists and experts as a
    63→morally corrupt or detached group standing in opposition to the “virtuous people” (Mede &
    64→Schäfer, 2020). More broadly, science-related populism reflects how individuals evaluate
    65→expertise, institutional authority, and the legitimacy of elites, issues that have been important
    66→concerns in political psychology (Staerklé et al., 2022; Widmann, 2022).
    67→Science-related populism represents a domain-specific manifestation of populist attitudes
    68→while remaining empirically distinct from political populism (Eberl et al., 2023). Although the
    69→two are closely related, they differ in their substantive correlates, with political populism more
    70→strongly associated with trust in government and science-related populism more closely linked
    71→to trust in science (Eberl et al., 2023; Piterová & Loziak, 2025) (see Appendix A1 for a detailed
    72→comparison of the two constructs). Given its direct challenge to scientific authority (Mede &
    73→Schäfer, 2020), science-related populism is relevant for understanding public distrust in science
    74→(Elchardus & Spruyt, 2016; Merkley, 2020) anti-intellectualism (Hofstadter, 1963; Merkley,
    75→2020), and responses to global crises such as the COVID-19 pandemic and climate change
    76→(Bertin et al., 2020; Eberl et al., 2021; Klinger et al., 2022; Richardson et al., 2023).

    79→The SciPop scale and its cross-national applicability
    80→Despite increasing attention to anti-scientific attitudes, research on science-related
    81→populism remains limited due to the early stage of measurement development. Mede et al.
    82→(2021) introduced the SciPop Scale, a survey instrument designed to systematically capture
    83→science-related populist attitudes and provide a foundation for further empirical work. The
    84→SciPop Scale comprises four dimensions (Mede et al., 2021): (1) conceptions of ordinary
    85→people (PPL)—the belief that they rely on common sense, share values and interests, and are
    86→virtuous; (2) conceptions of the academic elite (ELI)—the belief that scientists and experts are
    87→immoral, produce useless knowledge, and conspire with other elites; (3) demands for decision
    88→(DEC)-making sovereignty—the claim that the people should influence scientists’ work; and
    89→(4) demands for truth-speaking sovereignty (TRU)—the claim that common-sense knowledge
    90→is superior to scientific knowledge. Building on this scale, Mede and colleagues (2025)
    91→conducted a series of studies, including the “Trust in Science and Science-Related Populism”
    92→(TISP) survey across 68 countries/regions and comparative research on global trust in scientists
    93→(Cologna et al., 2025), highlighting the need for further cross-cultural validation of the SciPop
    94→Scale.
    95→Importantly, cross-national variation concerns not only populism but also the societal role
    96→and epistemic status of science, both shaped by cultural and institutional contexts. Studies show
    97→substantial differences across countries in how science and scientists are understood (Younger-
    98→Khan, 2026) and in levels of scientific knowledge (Allum et al., 2008; Bauer et al., 2012). For
    99→example, Japan reports lower scientific knowledge and trust than the UK and the US (Miura et
   100→al., 2024). Large-scale surveys also show substantial variation in trust in science, with higher
   101→
   102→5
   103→levels in Australia, New Zealand, Northern Europe, and Central Asia, and lower levels in parts
   104→of Africa and South America (Gallup, 2019), although Cologna et al. (2025) find more nuanced
   105→regional patterns. Moreover, institutional arrangements shape scientific autonomy,
   106→transparency, and politicization, which influence how science is produced, communicated, and
   107→perceived (Efimov et al., 2024; Gittelman, 2006; Iordanou et al., 2026; Younger-Khan, 2026).
   108→For instance, politicization of science can foster public skepticism, contributing to cross-
   109→national variation in attitudes toward science (Rutjens et al., 2022). Taken together, these
   110→findings underscore the complexity of cross-national variation and the need for measurement
   111→equivalence before substantive interpretation. As Yang and Ma (2026) show, even with
   112→identical instruments, substantive conclusions may change after measurement alignment.
   113→Therefore, rigorous cross-national validation is necessary to ensure comparability of science-
   114→related populist attitudes across contexts. These issues have received increasing attention in
   115→political psychology, as they determine whether observed cross-national differences in attitudes
   116→toward science, expertise, and authority reflect substantive variation or measurement artifacts
   117→(Kusano et al., 2025; Sterner & Goretzko, 2026).
   118→Research gaps
   119→As a recently developed instrument, the SciPop Scale has undergone initial validation, yet
   120→several issues remain unresolved (Mede et al., 2021). In particular, its cross-national reliability
   121→and validity have not been comprehensively assessed, leaving important research gaps. First,
   122→the optimal factor structure of the SciPop Scale has not been firmly established. Existing studies
   123→have tested one-factor (Piterová, 2024), four-factor (Hawkins & Chinn, 2024; Mede et al.,
   124→2021), and second-order factor models (Mede et al., 2025), but have not systematically
   125→
   126→6
   127→compared these structures across subgroups (e.g., sex, age) or national contexts. Thus, whether
   128→a stable cross-cultural factor structure exists remains unclear.
   129→Second, evidence for measurement invariance across subgroups and countries/regions
   130→remains limited. While Piterová (2024) found good invariance across age, education, and sex
   131→groups in Slovakia, Mede et al. (2025) reported failures in metric and scalar invariance at the
   132→cross-national level without identifying specific regional patterns. This suggests that beyond
   133→demographic characteristics, broader cross-national and cross-cultural sources of variation
   134→remain underexplored. Insights from populism research indicate that populist attitudes vary
   135→systematically across geographic, economic, cultural, and political contexts. Regional
   136→comparisons show distinct patterns of populism across Europe, Latin America, Asia, and Africa
   137→(Hadiz & Chryssogelos, 2017), while economic and social development constitutes a key driver
   138→of populist attitudes (Guiso et al., 2017; Mansbach & Ferguson, 2021). For example, Guiso et
   139→al. (2017) found that economic anxieties such as declining income and unemployment strongly
   140→predict populist attitudes. Additionally, populism is also a result of cultural and value-based
   141→conflicts, as it often emerges in the context of cultural transformation conflicts, particularly in
   142→Protestant Europe and English-speaking countries/regions where self-expression values
   143→dominate (Norris & Inglehart, 2019). Finally, regime type also matters. Based on the Economist
   144→Intelligence Unit (EIU) Democracy Index, Pinto et al. (2017) found that populism is highest in
   145→hybrid regimes among EU member states, followed by flawed democracies, with authoritarian
   146→regimes occupying an intermediate position. Taken together, these findings highlight the need
   147→to further examine measurement invariance of science-related populism across these diverse
   148→contexts. Moreover, whether measurement alignment methods can provide a viable solution
   149→
   150→7
   151→for enabling valid cross-national comparisons remains an open question.
   152→Third, criterion validity of the SciPop Scale has not been systematically examined in
   153→cross-cultural settings. Prior studies show that science-related populism is negatively
   154→associated with perceived trustworthiness of science-related media coverage, perceived
   155→trustworthiness of scientists, beliefs in the promise of science, and vaccination likelihood
   156→(Kohler & Koinig, 2023; Mede et al., 2021), and positively associated with reservations toward
   157→science and conspiracy beliefs (Eberl et al., 2023; Mede et al., 2021), while showing no
   158→consistent link with technocratic preferences (Eberl et al., 2023). However, findings on trust in
   159→science remain mixed across studies. While some research reports significant negative
   160→associations (Cologna et al., 2025; Eberl et al., 2023; Mede et al., 2021; Piterová & Loziak,
   161→2025), others find no significant relationship (Mede et al., 2022; Zillich et al., 2024),
   162→underscoring the need for more rigorous cross-cultural validation.
   163→Another important but often overlooked research gap concerns the methodological
   164→foundations of SciPop scoring. Research on populist attitudes typically employs three scoring
   165→methods: Bollen, Goertz, and Sartori approach, each reflecting a different measurement
   166→philosophy (Wuttke et al., 2020). The Bollen approach, grounded in traditional psychometrics,
   167→treats multidimensional constructs as “compensatory”, meaning that a low score on one
   168→dimension can be offset by high scores on other dimensions (Bollen & Lennox, 1991). In
   169→contrast, the Goertz approach conceptualizes multidimensional constructs as “non-
   170→compensatory”, in which all dimensions must be simultaneously present and a deficiency in
   171→one dimension cannot be compensated by high scores in others; thus, overall strength is
   172→determined by the weakest dimension (Goertz, 2020). The Sartori approach also follows a non-
   173→
   174→8
   175→compensatory logic but treats the construct in binary terms by requiring all dimensions to meet
   176→a predefined threshold (Sartori, 1984). Empirically, although Bollen-based scoring is widely
   177→used (Wuttke et al., 2020), the Goertz approach is argued to better reflect populism as an
   178→attitudinal syndrome (Akkerman et al., 2014; Elchardus & Spruyt, 2016; Spruyt et al., 2016;
   179→Wuttke et al., 2020). By contrast, the Sartori approach is rarely employed in the populist
   180→attitudes literature because it treats populism as a binary concept and requires an explicit
   181→threshold to distinguish “populists” from “non-populists,” which may introduce arbitrariness
   182→when no clear cut-off exists (Wuttke, 2019; Wuttke et al., 2020). Accordingly, the present study
   183→only compares Bollen- and Goertz-based scoring approaches as alternative operationalizations
   184→of the SciPop construct.
   185→Inspired by political populism research, Mede et al. (2021) recommend the Goertz
   186→approach, i.e., taking lowest score among the four dimensions as an indicator of overall support
   187→for science-related populism. However, although this recommendation is grounded in a non-
   188→compensatory conceptualization, it has not been empirically evaluated using models that
   189→explicitly reflect this assumption, such as non-compensatory multidimensional item response
   190→theory (MIRT) models. As a result, the measurement implications of the non-compensatory
   191→structure remain insufficiently examined. In the absence of such validation, studies have
   192→adopted heterogeneous scoring practices. Some apply alternative implementations of the
   193→Bollen approach, such as computing simple mean scores (Hawkins & Chinn, 2024; Kohler &
   194→Koinig, 2023; Piterová & Loziak, 2025; Zillich et al., 2024), while others use confirmatory
   195→factor analysis (CFA) factor scores (Eberl et al., 2023; Mede & Schäfer, 2022). However,
   196→systematic comparisons of these scoring methods remain limited, and it is unclear how they
   197→
   198→9
   199→contribute to heterogeneous findings. Key questions therefore remain, including which
   200→approach is most appropriate and robust, and how scores differ before and after applying cross-
   201→national alignment methods.
   202→To address this issue, the present study computes SciPop dimension scores using three
   203→methods—mean scores, CFA factor scores, and alignment-based factor scores—and aggregates
   204→them using both the Goertz and Bollen approaches, resulting in six scoring variants (see Table
   205→1). A further issue is that the lowest-dimension score in the Goertz approach may differ
   206→depending on the scoring method used, potentially altering its substantive interpretation.
   207→Therefore, we also examine the consistency of the lowest-dimension across scoring approaches.
   208→Such comparisons are crucial for establishing measures of science-related populist attitudes
   209→that are comparable across cultural contexts. Finally, we address the limitation that the original
   210→scale development did not empirically test whether its non-compensatory theoretical
   211→assumptions align with item response theory (IRT)-based models. We report supplementary
   212→MIRT analyses in the online appendix, focusing on item-level properties, including
   213→discrimination, thresholds, item information, and differential item functioning (DIF) (see
   214→Appendix C).

   216→Research questions
   217→To address these research gaps, the study consists of two parts. Study 1, the central
   218→component of this paper, draws on TISP global survey data covering 68 countries/regions
   219→(Cologna et al., 2025; Mede et al., 2025). We assess the reliability, factor structure and
   220→measurement invariance across sex, age, education, place of residence, political orientation,
   221→
   222→10
   223→degree of religiosity, as well as at the national level. For cross-national analyses, we further
   224→categorize countries/regions by geography, economic development, political regime, and
   225→cultural values to test subgroup invariance. In addition, we employ measurement alignment to
   226→assess approximate invariance and compare aligned scores with several alternative scoring
   227→approaches. Finally, we examine criterion validity by testing associations with a range of
   228→related constructs, including perceived benefits of science, trust in scientific methods,
   229→confidence in scientists, and social dominance orientation.
   230→Study 2 uses nationally representative data from the Science Barometer Switzerland
   231→surveys conducted in 2019 and 2020 (Metag et al., 2022) to further test the factor structure and
   232→measurement invariance over time. This allows us to assess the robustness and provide a more
   233→stringent validation within the specific context of Switzerland. By doing so, we not only test
   234→the temporal and sample stability of the SciPop Scale, but also respond to the initial conceptual
   235→and empirical work of Mede et al. (2021), thereby extending its evidence base for criterion
   236→validity and applicability. Building on these two studies, we address the following research
   237→questions:
   238→RQ1: What is the optimal factor structure of the SciPop Scale across sex, age, education,
   239→residence, political orientation, religiosity, and countries/regions?
   240→RQ2: Does the SciPop Scale exhibit measurement invariance across these subgroups and
   241→across countries/regions?
   242→RQ3: How does the SciPop Scale correlate with other validated measures to assess its
   243→criterion validity?
   244→RQ4: Which scoring approach (See Table 1) provides the most valid basis for cross-
   245→
   246→11
   247→national comparison of SciPop Scale, particularly when using aligned factor scores?
   248→Together, these research questions are designed not only to evaluate the psychometric
   249→properties of the SciPop Scale, but also to derive evidence-based recommendations for its use
   250→in subgroup and cross-national research, and to inform future refinement of measures of
   251→science-related populist attitudes.
   252→Study 1
   253→Study 1 aims to systematically examine the reliability, factor structure, and measurement
   254→invariance of the SciPop Scale across diverse subgroups and countries/regions using the TISP
   255→global dataset. This study enables a global comparative perspective, allowing us to assess cross-
   256→national patterns and test the applicability of different scoring methods (mean scores, CFA
   257→factor scores, and alignment-based factor scores) under both the Goertz and Bollen approaches.
   258→In addition, we evaluate the criterion validity of the scale by testing its associations with related
   259→constructs. No preregistration was conducted for the study. All supplemental materials related
   260→to Study 1 are provided in Appendix A.
   261→Methods
   262→Data and sample
   263→Data for Study 1 were collected between November 2022 and August 2023 as part of the
   264→TISP Many Labs project (“Trust in Science and Science-Related Populism”). This preregistered,
   265→global cross-sectional online survey included 71,922 participants from 68 countries and regions
   266→(Mede et al., 2025). For our analyses, we used a cleaned dataset comprising 69,534 participants
   267→across 68 countries and regions.
   268→To ensure data quality and align with the study’s objectives, we conducted additional
   269→
   270→12
   271→rigorous data processing (see Figure A2). First, as science-related populism is our primary
   272→variable of interest, we excluded 193 participants with missing responses on the SciPop Scale.
   273→Second, we verified that there were no missing values for sex, age, education, or place of
   274→residence. Third, drawing on developmental psychology literature (Mehta et al., 2020; Reifman
   275→& Niehuis, 2023) we redefined age groups as follows: 18–29 years (emerging adulthood)
   276→(Arnett, 2000), 30–45 years (established adulthood) (Mehta et al., 2020), 46–65 years (middle
   277→adulthood) (Reifman & Niehuis, 2023) , and ≥66 years (late adulthood) (Vernon & Gage,
   278→2016). Fourth, following the coding practices adopted by major social surveys such as the
   279→European Social Survey (ESS; Kroh, 2007), American National Election Studies (ANES;
   280→Erikson et al., 2002; Kathleen, 1990), Comparative Research on Scientific Issues (CRSi; Belak
   281→& Goh, 2024), and Pew Research Center (Pew; The Pew Center, 2014, 2018), we recoded
   282→political orientation and religiosity into three-category variables based on the midpoints of their
   283→respective Likert scales. For political orientation, both the liberal–conservative and left–right
   284→self-placement scales were collapsed into three groups: scores of 1–2 were coded as Liberal or
   285→Left-leaning, a score of 3 as Moderate, and scores of 4–5 as Conservative or Right-leaning.
   286→Similarly, for religiosity, we classified respondents as lowly religious (scores of 1–2),
   287→moderately religious (score of 3), and highly religious (scores of 4–5).
   288→Finally, no countries/regions were excluded from the analysis because of insufficient
   289→sample size lower than the 227 cutoff (see Appendix Table A1). This cutoff was determined
   290→through a priori power analysis conducted for all tested models (one-factor, four-factor, second-
   291→order, and bifactor) using RMSEA-based criteria; the detailed procedures and assumptions are
   292→reported in Appendix A2. Since the smallest country/region sample in our dataset was 308
   293→
   294→13
   295→participants, all 68 countries/regions were retained for analysis. A post-hoc sensitivity analysis
   296→further confirmed the adequacy of the retained sample sizes, as reported in Appendix A2.
   297→After these steps, the final dataset consists of 69,341 participants from 68 countries and
   298→regions. To further account for heterogeneity in geography, economy, culture, and political
   299→regimes, we further categorized countries/regions into four subgroups, as measurement
   300→instruments may function more comparably across countries that are relatively similar along
   301→these dimensions (Zhou & Ma, 2025). These included six geographical clusters based on the
   302→United Nations’ geographic regions (United Nations, 1999), two economic groups based on the
   303→UNCTAD classification of Economic and Social Development (United Nations, 2022), seven
   304→cultural clusters based on the Inglehart–Welzel cultural map of the world (Inglehart & Welzel,
   305→2005), and four political regime types based on the EIU Democracy Index (Economist
   306→Intelligence Unit, 2023) (see Appendix Table A2).
   307→Measures
   308→Science-related populism. Science-related populist attitudes were assessed with the
   309→SciPop Scale (Mede et al., 2021). The scale consists of eight items rated on a 5-point Likert
   310→scale (strongly disagree – strongly agree). These items capture four conceptual dimensions of
   311→science-related populism (see Table 2). In terms of scoring, the present study computes
   312→dimension scores using three different methods: (1) mean scores, calculated as the unweighted
   313→arithmetic average of responses to the two items within each dimension; (2) CFA factor scores,
   314→estimated from a four-factor CFA model; and (3) alignment-based factor scores, obtained from
   315→the measurement alignment procedure to adjust for cross-national non-invariance when full
   316→measurement invariance cannot be established. For the latter, individual scores are computed
   317→
   318→14
   319→using country-specific aligned item parameters, such that item responses are first adjusted for
   320→intercept differences and then combined according to their corresponding factor loadings.
   321→Subsequently, the four-dimension scores were aggregated into an overall SciPop score using
   322→two distinct approaches: the Goertz approach, which takes the minimum value among the four
   323→dimensions, and the Bollen approach, which averages the four-dimension scores. This yields a
   324→total of six scoring variants for comparison (see Table 1).
   325→[insert Table 2 about here]
   326→Criterion variables. Criterion validity was assessed using four theoretically relevant
   327→variables: perceived benefits of science (Mede et al., 2025), trust in scientific methods
   328→(Achterberg et al., 2017; Mede et al., 2025), confidence in scientists (2019; Mede et al., 2025),
   329→and social dominance orientation (SDO) (Mede et al., 2025; Pratto et al., 2013). The first three
   330→variables are science-related single-item measures, while SDO provides a broader ideological
   331→criterion that is theoretically related to political ideology and science-related attitudes (Kerr &
   332→Wilson, 2021; Mayer et al., 2020). Details on item wording, response scales, coding, and
   333→limitations of these criterion variables are reported in Appendix A3.
   334→Statistical analysis
   335→The statistical analyses were conducted in six stages, as illustrated in Figure 1. First, we
   336→summarized the overall sample and subgroups and assessed scale reliability using Cronbach’s
   337→α and McDonald’s ω (DeVellis, 2012). Second, we evaluated the latent structure of the SciPop
   338→Scale using CFA, comparing one-factor (Piterová, 2024), four-factor (Mede et al., 2021),
   339→second-order four-factor (Cologna et al., 2025; Piterová, 2024), and bifactor models (see Figure
   340→2). Model fit was assessed using RMSEA, CFI, SRMR, and TLI, following established fit
   341→
   342→15
   343→criteria and the principle of parsimony (Falk & Muthukrishna, 2023) (see Appendix A4 for
   344→model specifications and selection criteria).
   345→[insert Figure 1 and 2 about here]
   346→Third, we tested measurement invariance using the four-factor model, which was selected
   347→as the most generalizable latent structure. Invariance was assessed across sex, age, education,
   348→place of residence, political orientation, religiosity, and countries/regions. To further examine
   349→cross-national heterogeneity, countries/regions were grouped by geography, economic
   350→development, cultural values, and political regime type. Measurement invariance was
   351→evaluated sequentially at the configural, metric, scalar, and strict levels using changes in CFI,
   352→RMSEA, TLI, and SRMR; when full invariance was not supported, partial invariance was
   353→examined (see Appendix A4 for grouping criteria, thresholds, and partial invariance
   354→procedures).
   355→Fourth, because partial measurement invariance could not be established at the full
   356→country/region level, we applied measurement alignment to assess approximate invariance and
   357→improve the comparability of SciPop scores across countries/regions under partial non-
   358→invariance (Asparouhov & Muthén, 2014; Fischer & Karl, 2019; Robitzsch, 2025). Alignment
   359→was conducted separately for the four SciPop dimensions, and aligned factor scores were
   360→extracted as one scoring alternative for subsequent analyses (see Appendix A4 for
   361→implementation details).
   362→Fifth, we examined the consistency of the Goertz logic across scoring methods by
   363→comparing whether unaligned mean scores, unaligned CFA factor scores, and alignment-
   364→adjusted factor scores identified the same lowest dimension. Sixth, we generated six SciPop
   365→
   366→16
   367→score variants by combining the three dimension-score types with both Goertz and Bollen
   368→aggregation approaches (see Table 1). We then assessed criterion validity using OLS
   369→regressions predicting perceived benefits of science, trust in scientific methods, confidence in
   370→scientists, and social dominance orientation. Differences between scoring approaches were
   371→evaluated using Fisher permutation tests with 1,000 bootstrap resamples and, as a robustness
   372→check, seemingly unrelated regression (SUR) models (Zellner, 1962) with Wald chi-square
   373→tests. As an additional robustness check, we calculated robustness values to assess the
   374→sensitivity of the observed associations to potential unobserved confounding. The rationale and
   375→technical details of coefficient comparisons and robustness-value analyses are reported in
   376→Appendix A4.
   377→Results
   378→Descriptive analysis and reliability
   379→An overview of the overall sample and individual country/region samples is presented in
   380→Appendix Table A3. As for scale reliability, the SciPop Scale presents strong internal
   381→consistency, with Cronbach’s α = 0.79 and McDonald’s ω = 0.83. These values indicate a high
   382→level of reliability.

   493→Since the SciPop Scale does not achieve full measurement invariance at the country level,
   494→we applied measurement alignment based on the four-factor model to assess approximate
   495→invariance across countries/regions. The alignment results provide strong support for
   496→approximate invariance (Table 4): across the four dimensions, the R² values for loadings exceed
   497→98% and for intercepts exceed 99% across the four dimensions (PPL, ELI, DEC, and TRU),

   501→2014). Importantly, alignment provides more robust estimates of latent means under conditions
   502→of partial measurement non-invariance, thereby improving the cross-group comparability of
   503→SciPop scores. Accordingly, the aligned factor scores are used as one scoring alternative in the
   504→subsequent criterion validity analyses.

   528→Goertz approach — unaligned mean scores (SciPop_Goertz_Mean), unaligned four-factor
   529→scores (SciPop_Goertz_Factor), and alignment-adjusted four-factor scores
   530→(SciPop_Goertz_Aligned) — as well as scores computed under the Bollen approach —
   531→unaligned mean scores (SciPop_Bollen_Mean), unaligned four-factor scores
   532→(SciPop_Bollen_Factor), and alignment-adjusted four-factor scores (SciPop_Bollen_Aligned).

   535→We then compared the coefficients and R² values across these different scoring strategies.
   536→[insert Figure 4 about here]
   537→As shown in Figure 4, across all criterion variables, SciPop scores show consistent and
   538→statistically significant associations in the theoretically expected directions: negative
   539→associations with perceived science benefit, trust in scientific methods, and trust in scientists,
   540→and positive associations with social dominance orientation. This pattern holds across all six
   541→scoring approaches. However, the magnitude of these associations varies across scoring

   548→than regression coefficients, these values should be interpreted as contextual indicators rather
   549→than as direct evaluative thresholds.

   551→the unaligned scores, mean-based scoring approaches generally show the strongest associations

   569→Discussion
   570→Study 1 provides a comprehensive psychometric evaluation of the SciPop Scale across 68
   571→countries and regions. We focused on its reliability, factor structure, and measurement
   572→invariance across sex, age, education, residence, political orientation, religiosity, and at the
   573→national level, including alignment testing across countries. Additionally, we examined the
   574→consistency of different scoring methods and their relationships with a range of related
   575→constructs to assess criterion validity. Through this work, we aim to evaluate the suitability of
   576→the SciPop Scale for cross-national research and to offer practical implications for its
   577→application.
   578→First, the SciPop Scale demonstrates high reliability, with the four-factor structure being
   579→the most widely applicable optimal model. Specifically, reliability analysis shows that the
   580→SciPop Scale exhibits high internal consistency. CFA results indicate that the four-factor model
   581→
   582→25
   583→outperforms other CFA models in the overall sample as well as in most subgroups. At the
   584→country/region level, however, 12 countries/regions do not reach acceptable model fit, and in
   585→13 others the best-fitting structures varied, including four-factor, second-order factor, and
   586→bifactor models. Overall, the four-factor structure remains the optimal model for the SciPop
   587→Scale on a theoretical level, while in some concrete scenarios (e.g., Georgia) adjustments may
   588→be necessary. Complementary item-level diagnostics based on MIRT further support the
   589→psychometric performance of the SciPop Scale (see Appendix C1). The items demonstrate
   590→moderate to high discrimination and provide complementary information across different
   591→ranges of the latent traits, suggesting good measurement precision across the four dimensions.
   592→These findings extend previous research. Earlier studies tested one-factor (Mede et al., 2025;
   593→Piterová, 2024), second-order factor (Mede et al., 2025; Piterová, 2024), or four-factor models
   594→(Hawkins & Chinn, 2024; Mede et al., 2021) separately, but rarely examined the optimal
   595→structure systematically across different subgroups. Moreover, most existing evidence has
   596→come from single-country samples, limiting opportunities for cross-national comparison. In
   597→contrast, this study not only evaluates the robustness and generalizability of the four-factor
   598→structure across different subgroups, but also provides broader empirical support for its cross-
   599→national applicability.
   600→Second, the SciPop Scale shows strong comparability across different sociodemographic
   601→subgroups, but its cross-national comparability is more limited. Specifically, full measurement
   602→invariance is established across sex, age, education, residence, political orientation, and
   603→religiosity. This result aligns with Piterová’s (2024) findings in the Slovak sample, while
   604→extending the evidence by demonstrating measurement invariance for the first time across
   605→
   606→26
   607→residence, political orientation, and religiosity. At the cross-national level, the measurement
   608→invariance tests offer important insights into the applicability of the SciPop Scale. In the full
   609→country sample, only configural and metric invariance are supported, while scalar and strict
   610→invariance do not hold. Consistent with prior cross-national research (Mede et al., 2025), this
   611→result suggests that the SciPop Scale faces certain limitations for direct country comparisons,
   612→likely due to differences in geography, economics, politics, and culture. Brief country-level
   613→diagnostics suggest that the observed non-invariance is unlikely to be primarily an artifact of
   614→response styles (see Appendix E2). Notably, complementary IRT-based DIF analyses revealed
   615→generally poor cross-group fit, suggesting that exact measurement invariance may be overly
   616→restrictive for large-scale cross-national ordinal data (see Appendix C2). To address these
   617→limitations, we further applied measurement alignment, which indicates that approximate
   618→invariance can be achieved across countries.
   619→In addition, we examined measurement invariance across groups defined by geographic,
   620→economic, cultural, and political contexts to explore whether stronger evidence of measurement
   621→invariance could be found within more homogeneous clusters. By geographic region, the
   622→SciPop Scale achieves full measurement invariance in Australia/Oceania and North America,
   623→and partial scalar invariance in Africa, Europe, and Latin America, suggesting a reasonable
   624→degree of comparability in those regions. By economic development, the SciPop Scale shows
   625→satisfactory comparability in both the Global South and the Global North. By cultural values,
   626→the scale demonstrates good cross-cultural comparability in Confucian, Protestant European,
   627→Catholic European, English-speaking, West South Asian, Latin American, Orthodox European,
   628→and African Islamic groups. Finally, by political regime type, the scale performs well across
   629→
   630→27
   631→Full Democracies, Flawed Democracies, Hybrid Regimes, and Authoritarian states. Taken
   632→together, these findings suggest that participants who share similar economic, political, or
   633→cultural contexts are more likely to interpret science-related populism in comparable ways.
   634→Third, the SciPop scores derived from different scoring methods are not consistent in
   635→identifying the weakest dimension. The findings show that mean scores tend to classify ELI as
   636→the lowest dimension, whereas factor scores and alignment-adjusted factor scores more often
   637→identify PPL as the lowest. The degree of consistency across methods also varies, with the
   638→highest agreement observed between mean scores and factor scores. These results indicate that
   639→the choice of scoring strategy substantially affects the identification of the weakest dimension,
   640→influencing not only the overall score distribution but also conclusions about which dimension
   641→is most fragile. Complementary multidimensional non-compensatory IRT analyses further
   642→revealed substantial instability in weakest-dimension identification across scoring approaches,
   643→suggesting important methodological challenges in operationalizing weakest-link logic (see
   644→Appendix C3).
   645→Fourth, SciPop scores derived from different scoring methods are all significantly
   646→associated with criterion variables (i.e., perceived science benefit, trust in scientific methods,
   647→confidence in scientists, and social dominance orientation), providing strong evidence of
   648→criterion validity. However, their explanatory power varies considerably. Overall, the simple
   649→mean-based scores perform best: SciPop_Bollen_Mean shows the largest coefficients for
   650→perceived scientific benefits and confidence in scientists, while SciPop_Goertz_Mean is most
   651→effective in explaining trust in the scientific methods, and yields the largest increase in R² across
   652→the four criterion variables. By contrast, factor scores and alignment-adjusted factor scores are
   653→
   654→28
   655→generally weaker. Notably, in the cross-national alignment context, SciPop_Bollen_Aligned
   656→shows significantly higher regression coefficients and greater R² increases than
   657→SciPop_Goertz_Aligned. These results suggest that for cross-national research, Bollen
   658→approach offers stronger explanatory power in terms of criterion validity.
   659→Study 2
   660→Study 2 aims to validate and extend the findings of Study 1. Using nationally
   661→representative Swiss data collected in 2019 and 2020, it examines whether the SciPop Scale
   662→remains structurally comparable within a single multilingual national context and across two
   663→measurement occasions. The Swiss data are useful for this purpose because they combine
   664→institutional stability with internal linguistic diversity (German-, French-, and Italian-speaking
   665→areas) and include two waves collected before and during the COVID-19 pandemic. In addition
   666→to providing a country-specific robustness check of the findings from Study 1, Study 2 focuses
   667→on three issues that Study 1 cannot directly address: within-country subgroup comparability,
   668→temporal measurement invariance, and whether the criterion validity of SciPop scores extends
   669→to broader political-psychological orientations beyond science-specific attitudes. Taken
   670→together, Study 2 complements Study 1 by evaluating the robustness of the SciPop Scale across
   671→different empirical structures—cross-national versus within-country, and cross-sectional
   672→versus longitudinal—thereby strengthening the overall conclusions of the study. Full methods
   673→and detailed results for Study 2 are reported in Appendix B.

   762→Discussion
   763→Study 2 assesses the psychometric properties of the SciPop Scale in Switzerland, including
   764→its factor structure, measurement invariance, scoring consistency, and criterion validity across
   765→a range of related constructs. Through this work, we aim to provide a more rigorous test of the
   766→SciPop Scale’s robustness within the Swiss context, while building on the initial conceptual
   767→and empirical work of Mede et al. (2021).
   768→First, both the four-factor and second-order factor structures of SciPop Scale emerge as
   769→the most widely applicable optimal models in the overall sample and most subgroups. In some
   770→subgroups, the optimal model is more varied. For example, in the female subgroup, the
   771→emerging and established adulthood subgroups, the German and French language subgroups,
   772→and the low- and high-religiosity subgroups, a bifactor model also fits well. In other subgroups,
   773→such as the late adulthood subgroup, no acceptable model is identified. In the Switzerland 2020
   774→overall sample and the Swiss panel data, only the four-factor model is selected as optimal.
   775→
   776→33
   777→These findings indicate that, within the specific Swiss context, the optimal factor structure of
   778→the SciPop Scale is more diverse.
   779→Second, the SciPop Scale demonstrates strong structural stability and comparability across
   780→sociodemographic subgroups and over time. Specifically, the four-factor model exhibits full
   781→measurement invariance across sex, age, education, language region, political orientation, and
   782→religiosity subgroups. Partial strict invariance is achieved for the education and region
   783→subgroups, and measurement alignment for these subgroups indicate that approximate
   784→invariance can be attained. The second-order factor model also shows good comparability
   785→across all subgroups, except for the language region subgroup, where measurement invariance
   786→cannot be established. Finally, the four-factor model shows full measurement invariance over
   787→time. To further explore potential sources of non-invariance at a more granular level, we
   788→conducted item-level differential item functioning (DIF) analyses based on multidimensional
   789→IRT models. The results indicate that several items—most notably ppl1, tru1, and tru2—exhibit
   790→evidence of measurement non-invariance across multiple subgroups. These findings suggest
   791→that, despite the overall robustness of the scale at the latent construct level, certain items may
   792→function differently across groups and therefore warrant further examination (see Appendix C2,
   793→for details).
   794→Third, the identification of the weakest dimension lacks stability across datasets and
   795→scoring methods. In Switzerland 2019 dataset, mean scores and region-aligned factor scores
   796→show high consistency (over 80%) and both identify ELI as the primary weakest dimension.
   797→However, other scoring methods in the same dataset highlight different dimensions, such as
   798→DEC or truth TRU, indicating that different approaches may emphasize different weaknesses.
   799→
   800→34
   801→In Switzerland 2020 dataset, consistency drops to around 70%, and the identified weakest
   802→dimension shifts from ELI to PPL. In the panel data, consistency falls below 50%, with
   803→different methods producing contradictory weakest dimensions across the two waves. This
   804→pattern may partly reflect the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, as the first wave (2019) was
   805→collected before the pandemic and the second wave (2020) during a period when science was
   806→highly salient in public discourse (Mede & Schäfer, 2022). Comparisons of lowest-scoring
   807→dimensions across waves suggest that the pandemic may have altered the relative salience of
   808→certain dimensions, increasing consistency for PPL and ELI while decreasing it for TRU (see
   809→Table B5 in Appendix B2). At the same time, given the relatively smaller size of the panel
   810→sample compared to the baseline cross-sectional data, these results should be interpreted
   811→cautiously. Overall, the findings provide suggestive evidence that the identification of the
   812→weakest dimension may depend on the scoring method, but strong conclusions about its
   813→temporal stability cannot yet be drawn. Further research with larger longitudinal samples is
   814→needed to assess its temporal consistency more conclusively.
   815→Fourth, most SciPop scoring methods show significant associations with a range of
   816→science attitude variables, providing support for the scale’s criterion validity. However, the
   817→choice of scoring strategy meaningfully affects the strength of these relationships. In general,
   818→aggregation approach based on Bollen method tends to show stronger associations with science
   819→attitudes than those based on the Goertz approach, suggesting that compensatory aggregation
   820→better captures overall variation in science-related attitudes. In addition, the comparison of
   821→alignment-adjusted scores highlights the importance of accounting for cross-group
   822→measurement differences. Region-aligned scores based on the Bollen approach tend to show
   823→
   824→35
   825→stronger relationships with science attitudes than their Goertz counterparts, suggesting that this
   826→strategy may better accommodate heterogeneity across linguistic contexts. By contrast,
   827→alignment based on education produces substantially weaker associations with criterion
   828→variables, possibly because it reduces the variation in SciPop scores associated with educational
   829→differences. Turning to political variables, associations between science-related populism and
   830→political attitudes are substantially weaker across all scoring methods, with no consistent
   831→evidence of a relationship with political trust. This divergence indicates that science-related
   832→populism may be more appropriately understood as a science-related attitudinal construct rather
   833→than a general political disposition.
   834→General Discussion
   835→The current study aims to examine the psychometric properties of the SciPop Scale using
   836→the TISP dataset across 68 countries and regions, as well as the Swiss Science Barometer
   837→dataset. Our research not only assesses the cross-national applicability of the SciPop Scale but
   838→also addresses broader issue regarding the measurement of populist attitudes.
   839→On one hand, we examine the applicability of the SciPop Scale’s psychometric properties.
   840→First, we find that the four-factor structure performs well overall across countries and various
   841→subgroups. However, when focusing on one specific national context (i.e., Switzerland), the
   842→optimal factor structure is more diverse, encompassing both the four-factor and second-order
   843→factor models. This suggests that contextual differences should be carefully considered when
   844→evaluating and applying the SciPop Scale. Second, the four-factor model demonstrates strong
   845→comparability across different sociodemographic subgroups, both in cross-national datasets
   846→and within Switzerland context, but full comparability across countries is limited. While
   847→
   848→36
   849→approximate invariance can be achieved through measurement alignment, some dimensions
   850→appear to contribute more to cross-group instability than others (see Appendix D). In particular,
   851→the PPL (i.e., conceptions of the ordinary people) dimension shows relatively weaker
   852→performance, both in terms of factor loadings and alignment results, suggesting potential
   853→concerns regarding its content validity. Conceptually, PPL appears to capture a more general
   854→form of people-centrism rather than attitudes directly related to science or scientific authority,
   855→which may reduce its consistency across contexts. This may partly explain why full cross-
   856→national comparability is difficult to achieve for the overall scale.
   857→On the other hand, our study also engages with broader questions concerning the
   858→philosophy of measuring populist attitudes. Conceptually, science-related populism is typically
   859→regarded as a non-compensatory construct (Mede et al., 2021), which assumes that all
   860→dimensions are necessary components of a construct, such that a low score on one dimension
   861→cannot be offset by high scores on other dimensions (Goertz, 2020; Wuttke et al., 2020). At the
   862→operational level, this logic is commonly implemented through the Goertz approach, in which
   863→the overall score is determined by the weakest dimension (Goertz, 2020; Wuttke et al., 2020).
   864→In practice, however, we find low consistency in identifying the weakest dimension across
   865→different scoring methods. This instability is best understood as an aggregation artefact specific
   866→to the Goertz approach. Because the Goertz score is defined by the lowest-scoring dimension,
   867→any change in the way dimension scores is estimated—whether by raw means, CFA factor
   868→scores, or alignment-adjusted factor scores—can change which dimension becomes the
   869→“weakest link.” Thus, the observed instability does not necessarily imply that the underlying
   870→construct itself is unstable; rather, it reflects the sensitivity of weakest-link scoring to the
   871→
   872→37
   873→preceding scoring procedure. This means that, under a non-compensatory framework,
   874→researchers should report not only the overall Goertz score but also which dimension
   875→determines that score and whether this identification is robust across scoring methods.
   876→Moreover, our findings show that, for alignment-adjusted scores, the Bollen approach (Bollen
   877→& Lennox, 1991) consistently outperforms the Goertz approach in predicting criterion variables.
   878→Since aligned scores are designed to enhance comparability across countries, this finding
   879→suggests that the compensatory approach may be more suitable for cross-national comparisons.
   880→These findings highlight two important issues. First, theoretical concepts and
   881→measurement strategies are not naturally aligned: the non-compensatory logic of populism may
   882→not be fully borne out in empirical data. This logic is inherited from the broader literature on
   883→populist attitudes, in which populism is understood as an attitudinal syndrome composed of
   884→jointly necessary dimensions (Wuttke et al., 2020), and is extended to science-related populism
   885→by Mede et al. (2021). Thus, the non-compensatory nature of science-related populism should
   886→be understood as a theoretically grounded measurement assumption rather than as an
   887→empirically established property. Second, the choice of scoring method is not merely a
   888→statistical technicality but a philosophical decision; researchers must weigh preserving
   889→theoretical logic against maximizing criterion validity. In other words, measuring science-
   890→related populism requires both acknowledging its theoretical non-compensatory nature and
   891→recognizing that real-world data may exhibit compensatory features. This tension offers
   892→methodological insights for future scale development and cross-cultural comparative research.
   893→Contributions and implications
   894→The present study makes several contributions and implications. First, we systematically
   895→
   896→38
   897→explored the optimal factor structure model across different groups and countries/regions using
   898→large-scale cross-national data and further examined cross-national validity through
   899→measurement invariance and alignment techniques. We not only extend existing evidence on
   900→the limited cross-national validity of the SciPop Scale (Mede et al., 2025) but also provide an
   901→empirical example for political psychology more broadly (Wuttke et al., 2020), highlighting
   902→the potential of alignment methods in cross-cultural measurement research. Second, we
   903→validated the criterion validity of the SciPop Scale in multiple contexts and demonstrated how
   904→different scoring strategies shape the interpretation of science-related populism. This finding
   905→offers a possible explanation for the mixed conclusions in previous research on science-related
   906→populism and underscores the need for greater caution in selecting and reporting scoring
   907→methods. Third, this is the first study to systematically compare the Goertz and Bollen scoring
   908→logics, along with their variants, in a large cross-national sample. Our findings reveal a key
   909→tension between the theoretical construct of science-related populism and its empirical
   910→performance. This tension warrants further consideration of whether the observed mismatch is
   911→specific to the SciPop Scale or reflects a broader feature of multidimensional constructs in
   912→political psychology.
   913→Beyond these methodological contributions, our findings also carry important theoretical
   914→implications. First, this study deepens our understanding of the internal structure and cross-
   915→national representation of science-related populism. Internally, the dimensions of science-
   916→related populism do not always constitute the construct in an equally stable or consistently
   917→aligned manner. Anti-elitism appears to play a more central role, whereas people-centrism
   918→shows weaker and less consistent associations with the other dimensions. Cross-nationally, the
   919→
   920→39
   921→failure to achieve full scalar invariance, together with poor model fit in some countries/regions,
   922→suggests that science-related populism is not understood or expressed in exactly the same way
   923→across contexts. Differences in scientific institutions, expert authority, political trust may shape
   924→the meaning and relative importance of its dimensions. Thus, science-related populism should
   925→not be treated as a fixed attitudinal structure that can be mechanically applied across all
   926→countries, but rather as a multidimensional political-psychological construct whose meaning is
   927→context-dependent.
   928→Second, because the non-compensatory conceptualization of science-related populism
   929→draws on the broader tradition of general populism research (Wuttke et al., 2020), our findings
   930→also contribute to the conceptualization of populist attitudes and other multidimensional
   931→political-psychological constructs. Populist attitudes are often understood as a non-
   932→compensatory attitudinal syndrome, in which core components must be simultaneously present
   933→(Wuttke et al., 2020). However, our findings suggest that this logic is sensitive to how
   934→dimensions are defined, measured, and empirically related. Changes in any dimension may
   935→affect the overall score, the identification of the weakest dimension, and the resulting
   936→theoretical interpretation. Therefore, non-compensatory logic should be treated as a theoretical
   937→assumption requiring empirical scrutiny rather than as a default premise of populism. This point
   938→is especially important given the lack of consensus on the dimensional structure of populism
   939→(Jami & Kemmelmeier, 2025; Pellegrini, 2023) and the varying empirical stability of its
   940→dimensions (Schimpf et al., 2024). More broadly, the same implication applies to other
   941→multidimensional constructs in political psychology and political behavior that adopt a non-
   942→compensatory logic, such as political extremism (Jungkunz et al., 2024), solid democratic
   943→
   944→40
   945→support (Moncagatta et al., 2023), and democratic knowledge (Wegscheider & Stark, 2020):
   946→when a construct is defined as consisting of multiple jointly necessary components, researchers
   947→should examine whether these components are equally necessary both theoretically and
   948→empirically.
   949→Guidelines for using the SciPop Scale
   950→We provide the following guidelines for researchers using the SciPop Scale:
   951→First, regarding model specification, the four-factor structure demonstrates relatively
   952→strong robustness across different contexts. We therefore recommend adopting it as the default
   953→model specification in cross-national research. In single-country studies, however, alternative
   954→specifications (e.g., a second-order factor model) may be considered depending on empirical
   955→performance. It should be noted that the SciPop Scale is not universally applicable across all
   956→contexts; for example, no acceptable CFA model was identified in Botswana, suggesting that
   957→the scale may not function adequately in certain settings.
   958→Second, measurement invariance testing is essential prior to cross-national comparisons.
   959→If scalar invariance cannot be established, comparisons of observed means across countries
   960→should be avoided, as such differences may reflect measurement bias rather than substantive
   961→variation. Meaningful comparisons of country-level means are only warranted when
   962→measurement invariance is satisfied (or approximately satisfied). When scalar invariance is not
   963→achieved, we recommend prioritizing measurement alignment methods to obtain comparable
   964→latent scores across countries. However, recent research indicates that not all factors can be
   965→successfully aligned in every context (e.g., Yang & Ma, 2026). In such cases, we suggest
   966→restricting comparisons to the factors that achieve acceptable alignment and clearly
   967→
   968→41
   969→acknowledging this limitation in the interpretation of results.
   970→Third, regarding scoring methods, we strongly recommend that researchers report both
   971→Goertz-based (non-compensatory) and Bollen-based (compensatory) scores to enhance the
   972→robustness and transparency of their findings. Although the SciPop Scale is theoretically
   973→grounded in a non-compensatory logic, our findings suggest that its empirical behavior may, in
   974→some contexts, be better captured by compensatory aggregation approaches. We additionally
   975→explored non-compensatory MIRT models and the multiplicative unified scale proposed by
   976→Silva et al. (2018), but these approaches showed lower stability and weaker associations with
   977→criteria variables (see Appendix C and Table A20). Overall, while we do not reject non-
   978→compensatory approaches such as the Goertz method, we advise caution in their application
   979→and recommend benchmarking them against compensatory alternatives, particularly in cross-
   980→national analyses.
   981→Fourth, when measurement invariance or alignment cannot be fully achieved, we
   982→recommend prioritizing the TRU and ELI dimensions as the core components for cross-group
   983→inference, as they demonstrate greater stability and more consistent associations with criterion
   984→variables across contexts (see Appendix D). In contrast, the PPL and DEC dimensions should
   985→be interpreted with caution due to their inconsistent relationships with other dimensions and
   986→with criterion variables. Although science-related populism is theoretically conceptualized as
   987→a multidimensional construct, the empirical contribution of each dimension is neither equally
   988→stable nor consistent. Therefore, focusing on a subset of empirically robust dimensions can
   989→serve as a useful complementary strategy in comparative research. For recommendations
   990→regarding future SciPop Scale development, please see the Appendix F.

   993→Limitations and future directions
   994→This study is subject to several limitations that open up opportunities for future research.
   995→First, our validation of criterion validity relied mainly on single-item indicators, partly because
   996→we did not test measurement invariance for all multi-item scale indicators and thus could not
   997→ensure their cross-cultural comparability. Future research should incorporate multi-item
   998→measures that have undergone invariance testing to capture criterion variables related to
   999→scientific populism more comprehensively. Second, in the data analyses, political orientation
  1000→and religiosity are recoded from 5-point scales into three categories. This practice follows
  1001→conventions in major social surveys (e.g., ESS, ANES, CRSi, Pew; Belak & Goh, 2024;
  1002→Kathleen, 1990; The Pew Center, 2014, 2018), enhancing comparability and simplifying model
  1003→estimation. However, this approach may lead to information loss by masking subtle distinctions
  1004→in continuous variables. Besides, cultural heterogeneity in how individuals interpret “middle”
  1005→or “extreme” categories may also affect cross-cultural comparability. Future work could adopt
  1006→more flexible strategies that combine categorical and continuous treatments. Third, although
  1007→we have conducted preliminary tests of the temporal stability of the SciPop Scale using panel
  1008→data, the relatively small sample size in Study 2 limits statistical power and the robustness of
  1009→conclusions, particularly regarding temporal stability. Therefore, future research should rely on
  1010→larger-scale longitudinal data to more rigorously examine cross-time invariance. Fourth,
  1011→although Study 1 used a large-scale cross-national dataset, differences in sampling procedures
  1012→and survey implementation across countries may have affected sample representativeness,
  1013→which could limit the generalizability and comparability of results across national contexts.
  1014→Future research should replicate these analyses using more strictly representative samples. Fifth,
  1015→
  1016→43
  1017→each SciPop dimension is measured by only two items, which may constrain diagnostic
  1018→assessments of residual structure and local dependence. Although robustness checks suggest
  1019→that our substantive conclusions are not disproportionately affected by any single two-item
  1020→factor (see Appendix E1), this limitation should be considered when interpreting the findings
  1021→and refining the scale. Finally, our findings indicate notable discrepancies between the Swiss-
  1022→specific results and those derived from cross-national data. Future research should extend
  1023→validation efforts to a broader range of national and regional contexts to better capture and
  1024→compare the manifestations of science-related populism across diverse sociocultural settings.
  1025→Conclusion
  1026→This study systematically examines the structural validity, measurement invariance, and
  1027→criterion validity of the SciPop Scale in both cross-national and specific national contexts,
  1028→while comparing the performance of different scoring strategies. The results indicate that,
  1029→although strict invariance is difficult to achieve, alignment methods can partially enhance the
  1030→feasibility of cross-national comparisons. Besides, different scoring methods substantially
  1031→influence empirical results and theoretical interpretations. In particular, when comparing the
  1032→Goertz and Bollen approach, the former adheres more closely to the non-compensatory
  1033→conceptual logic (Goertz, 2020; Wuttke et al., 2020) of science-related populism, whereas the
  1034→latter demonstrates greater robustness and explanatory power at the empirical level. Overall,
  1035→science-related populism presents both a challenge for empirical measurement and an
  1036→opportunity for theoretical reflection. Issues such as cross-national comparison, scoring method
  1037→selection, and the application of alignment techniques all involve ensuring conceptual
  1038→consistency across diverse sociocultural contexts. Thus, the implications of this study extend
  1039→
  1040→44
  1041→beyond the SciPop Scale itself. By using science-related populism as an empirical case, we
  1042→show that credible cross-context comparison in political psychology and science
  1043→communication requires not only broad samples and appropriate models, but also evidence that
  1044→the measured construct remains conceptually comparable across social, cultural, and political
  1045→settings (Yang & Ma, 2026). These insights offer broader implications for the development and
  1046→validation of cross-cultural measures for other complex political-psychological constructs.
