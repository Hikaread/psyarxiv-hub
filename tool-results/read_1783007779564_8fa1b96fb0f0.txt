    13‚Üí    13‚ÜíPromoting well-doing: A Positive Psychology intervention for regulating
    14‚Üí    14‚Üíperformance momentum and thriving among high achievers
    15‚Üí    15‚ÜíThe present study examined a novel journalling intervention designed to support
    16‚Üí    16‚Üísubjective wellbeing and perceived work performance among high achievers.
    17‚Üí    17‚ÜíSixty-seven university students reporting a grade average of B+ or higher
    18‚Üí    18‚Üícompleted a one-week, within-subjects pre‚Äìpost study involving a daily ‚Äòwell
    19‚Üí    19‚Üídone‚Äô journalling task focused on small actions done well. Subjective wellbeing
    20‚Üí    20‚Üíand perceived work performance were measured pre- and post- intervention; with
    21‚Üí    21‚Üíjournalling frequency and qualitative feedback collected afterwards. Findings
    22‚Üí    22‚Üíindicated no detectable change in wellbeing from an elevated baseline, however,
    23‚Üí    23‚Üía significant improvement in perceived work performance was observed. Post-
    24‚Üí    24‚Üíhoc analyses revealed a dose‚Äìresponse pattern, with stronger performance
    25‚Üí    25‚Üíimprovements among participants with frequent task engagement. Qualitative
    26‚Üí    26‚Üífeedback was generally favourable, suggesting perceived benefits in positive
    27‚Üí    27‚Üíaffect, self-esteem, and motivation. Overall, results show preliminary support for
    28‚Üí    28‚Üí‚Äòwell done‚Äô journalling to enhance perceived performance in high-achieving
    29‚Üí    29‚Üístudents.
    30‚Üí    30‚ÜíKeywords: high achievers; positive psychology; work performance;
    31‚Üí    31‚Üíinterventions; achievement
    32‚Üí    32‚ÜíIntroduction
    33‚Üí    33‚ÜíHigh achievers are individuals characterised by high personal standards, sustained goal
    34‚Üí    34‚Üícommitment, and strong performance motivation (Hirt et al., 2021). A substantial body
    35‚Üí    35‚Üíof research has demonstrated that high achievers tie their identity and self-worth to
    36‚Üí    36‚Üídemonstrable accomplishment and competence (Crocker & Wolfe, 2001; Dweck &
    37‚Üí    37‚ÜíLeggett, 1988; Nerstad et al., 2018). While such individuals frequently attain
    38‚Üí    38‚Üíobjectively high levels of success across domains including sport, academia, and
    39‚Üí    39‚Üíleadership (Dickinson & Dickinson, 2014; Luthar et al., 2020; Rassaby et al., 2021),
    40‚Üí    40‚ÜíIn-Confidence
    41‚Üí    41‚Üí
    42‚Üí    42‚Üí3
    43‚Üí    43‚Üíresearch indicates that they may simultaneously be vulnerable to reductions in
    44‚Üí    44‚Üísubjective wellbeing (SWB; Barnard, 2008; Collin et al., 2020; Galbraith et al., 2015;
    45‚Üí    45‚ÜíLuthar et al., 2020; Rassaby et al., 2021).
    46‚Üí    46‚ÜíSWB refers to an individuals‚Äô evaluation of their own life and typically
    47‚Üí    47‚Üíencompasses life satisfaction, positive affect, and low negative affect (B√ºcker et al.,
    48‚Üí    48‚Üí2018). Higher levels of SWB have been associated with greater intrinsic motivation,
    49‚Üí    49‚Üícreativity, and work performance (Amabile & Kramer, 2011; Donaldson et al., 2022;
    50‚Üí    50‚ÜíOswald et al., 2015; Ryan & Deci, 2001). Conversely, diminished SWB is linked to
    51‚Üí    51‚Üíimpaired functioning, maladaptive coping strategies, burnout, and increased risk of
    52‚Üí    52‚Üímental illness (Barnard, 2008; Collin et al., 2020). These effects appear particularly
    53‚Üí    53‚Üísalient among high-achieving populations, including senior corporate leaders and high-
    54‚Üí    54‚Üíperforming university students, who report elevated levels of stress, perfectionistic
    55‚Üí    55‚Üíconcerns, anxiety, and emotional exhaustion (Galbraith et al., 2015; Ganesh et al., 2018;
    56‚Üí    56‚ÜíRassaby et al., 2021).
    57‚Üí    57‚ÜíBut why do high achievers exhibit declining SWB? Several decades of research
    58‚Üí    58‚Üíhave provided evidence that while many high achievers value personal growth, they are
    59‚Üí    59‚Üístrongly orientated toward demonstrating competence relative to others (Dweck &
    60‚Üí    60‚ÜíLeggett, 1988; Nerstad et al., 2018). When combined with contingent self-worth, in
    61‚Üí    61‚Üíwhich self-esteem depends on personally valued outcomes, this orientation can produce
    62‚Üí    62‚Üíheightened sensitivity to achievement-relevant cues and outcomes (St√•hlberg et al.,
    63‚Üí    63‚Üí2019). Over time, such patterns may encourage selective attention toward large
    64‚Üí    64‚Üíaccomplishments while rendering incremental or smaller achievements less salient
    65‚Üí    65‚Üí(Crocker & Wolfe, 2001). Although this orientation may support goal achievement and
    66‚Üí    66‚Üípersistence, it may also reduce opportunities to experience frequent, competence-
    67‚Üí    67‚Üíaffirming positive affect derived from everyday progress.
    68‚Üí    68‚ÜíIn-Confidence
    69‚Üí    69‚Üí
    70‚Üí    70‚Üí4
    71‚Üí    71‚ÜíStudies on motivation and performance show that people often fixate on large
    72‚Üí    72‚Üígoals or end results and underappreciate incremental progress unless it is made visible
    73‚Üí    73‚Üí(Amabile & Kramer, 2011). Yet even minor achievements, when appraised as
    74‚Üí    74‚Üínoteworthy, can reinforce a sense of competence and agency, thereby supporting
    75‚Üí    75‚Üíintrinsic motivation (Bromberg-Martin et al., 2010; Nummenmaa & Niemi, 2004).
    76‚Üí    76‚ÜíImportantly, small achievements may generate more frequent and sustainable positive
    77‚Üí    77‚Üíaffect than larger achievements, as these can be accompanied by prolonged stress,
    78‚Üí    78‚Üíheightened expectations, or an immediate escalation toward new goals (Weick, 1984).
    79‚Üí    79‚ÜíIn sum, reflecting on small achievements may increase SWB and work performance,
    80‚Üí    80‚Üíparticularly among outcome-driven high achievers.
    81‚Üí    81‚ÜíReflective writing or journalling has long been seen as an effective approach for
    82‚Üí    82‚Üíencouraging such reflection (Hoult et al., 2025). Reflective writing interventions have
    83‚Üí    83‚Üíbeen associated with increased confidence, motivation, and performance
    84‚Üí    84‚Üí(Hatzigeorgiadis et al., 2009; Matsuo, 2022). In particular, gratitude-based journalling
    85‚Üí    85‚Üíinterventions have been consistently shown to increase SWB across diverse populations
    86‚Üí    86‚Üí(Emmons & McCullough, 2003; Gander et al., 2016; Gander et al., 2017).
    87‚Üí    87‚ÜíThe ‚ÄòThree Good Things‚Äô exercise (Seligman et al., 2005), for example, is
    88‚Üí    88‚Üíarguably one of the most well-supported positive psychology interventions of this kind.
    89‚Üí    89‚ÜíIn this intervention, participants write down three things that went well each day and
    90‚Üí    90‚Üíprovide a written explanation of their causes. In the initial study, completing this task
    91‚Üí    91‚Üífor one week produced sustained improvements in happiness and reductions in
    92‚Üí    92‚Üídepressive symptoms for several months, particularly among those who continued the
    93‚Üí    93‚Üípractice (Seligman et al., 2005).
    94‚Üí    94‚ÜíHowever, such interventions have not been adequately tested with high-
    95‚Üí    95‚Üíachieving populations. In light of their dominant achievement orientation, it seems
    96‚Üí    96‚ÜíIn-Confidence
    97‚Üí    97‚Üí
    98‚Üí    98‚Üí5
    99‚Üí    99‚Üípossible that they may respond differently to open-ended reflection tasks. In particular,
   100‚Üí   100‚Üíbeing instructed to reflect on what ‚Äúwent well‚Äù may inadvertently activate outcome-
   101‚Üí   101‚Üíbased evaluation, that is, draw attention to incomplete tasks (e.g., what has not been
   102‚Üí   102‚Üíachieved on one's to-do list), as unfinished tasks tend to remain cognitively salient and
   103‚Üí   103‚Üímore readily accessible than completed ones (Syrek & Antoni, 2014; Syrek et al.,
   104‚Üí   104‚Üí2017). For individuals prone to high standards and achievement-contingent self-worth,
   105‚Üí   105‚Üísuch activation may intensify rumination and stress rather than alleviate it (Flett et al.,
   106‚Üí   106‚Üí2002; √ñzhan & Y√ºksel, 2022; Schmeer, 2019). Furthermore, high achievers may
   107‚Üí   107‚Üídiscount smaller things that fail to meet their elevated standards, thereby undermining
   108‚Üí   108‚Üíthe intended effect (Collin et al., 2020; Galbraith et al., 2015; Madigan, 2019; Rassaby
   109‚Üí   109‚Üíet al., 2021).
   110‚Üí   110‚ÜíThese considerations suggest that while daily positive reflection may be
   111‚Üí   111‚Üíbeneficial in general populations, it may require adaptation for high achievers.
   112‚Üí   112‚ÜíSpecifically, interventions that remain open-ended in what qualifies as ‚Äúgood‚Äù may
   113‚Üí   113‚Üíinadvertently dilute or even counteract their intended effects. A more constrained
   114‚Üí   114‚Üíapproach may therefore be warranted.
   115‚Üí   115‚ÜíMore particularly, encouraging reflection on small, modest ‚Äòwell done‚Äô
   116‚Üí   116‚Üímoments‚Äîsuch as making the bed, choosing the stairs, or initiating a difficult
   117‚Üí   117‚Üíconversation‚Äîbehaviours that high achievers might not otherwise identify as worthy
   118‚Üí   118‚Üíaccomplishments, may, by shifting the focus away from the magnitude of achievement
   119‚Üí   119‚Üíto a specific well done moment, be better positioned to increase SWB and perceived
   120‚Üí   120‚Üíwork performance in this population.
   121‚Üí   121‚ÜíIn-Confidence
   122‚Üí   122‚Üí
   123‚Üí   123‚Üí6
   124‚Üí   124‚ÜíCurrent study
   125‚Üí   125‚ÜíThe current study tests this proposition by examining the effects of a brief, daily ‚Äòwell
   126‚Üí   126‚Üídone‚Äô journalling intervention on subjective wellbeing and perceived performance
   127‚Üí   127‚Üíamong high-achieving students. The following two hypotheses were proposed:
   128‚Üí   128‚ÜíH1: Participants will demonstrate an increase in subjective wellbeing following
   129‚Üí   129‚Üía week-long daily journalling intervention reflecting on activities they did well each
   130‚Üí   130‚Üíday.
   131‚Üí   131‚ÜíH2: Participants will show an increase in perceived performance following a
   132‚Üí   132‚Üíweek-long daily journalling intervention reflecting on activities they did well each day.
   133‚Üí   133‚Üí.

   161‚Üí   161‚ÜíMeasures
   162‚Üí   162‚ÜíSubjective wellbeing
   163‚Üí   163‚ÜíThis was assessed at pre-test and post-test with the 8-item Flourishing Scale (FS; Diener
   164‚Üí   164‚Üíet al., 2010). Participants indicated their agreement with wellbeing related statements
   165‚Üí   165‚Üíusing a 1-7 Likert scale (1 = ‚Äústrongly disagree‚Äù, 7 = ‚Äústrongly agree‚Äù), with the scores
   166‚Üí   166‚Üífrom all items summed as a measure of overall wellbeing. Example items include ‚ÄúI
   167‚Üí   167‚Üílead a purposeful and meaningful life‚Äù and ‚ÄúI am engaged and interested in my daily
   168‚Üí   168‚Üíactivities‚Äù (see appendix for the full scale). Scores may range from 8 (lowest possible
   169‚Üí   169‚Üíwellbeing) to 56 (highest possible wellbeing). This is a brief measure which captures
   170‚Üí   170‚Üíeudaimonic aspects of SWB without risking increased attrition due to respondent
   171‚Üí   171‚Üíburden, and it has been validated with a New Zealand population (Diener et al., 2010;
   172‚Üí   172‚ÜíHone et al., 2014; Rolstad et al., 2011). Reliability analysis showed good internal
   173‚Üí   173‚Üíconsistency (Œ± = .85, M = 48.08, SD = 4.51).

   177‚Üí   177‚ÜíWork performance

   183‚Üí   183‚Üíimprovement‚Äù, 5 = ‚Äúexcellent‚Äù), and their overall performance equating to their mean

   325‚Üí   325‚ÜíDiscussion
   326‚Üí   326‚ÜíThe present study examined whether a daily journalling intervention requiring high
   327‚Üí   327‚Üíachievers to reflect on small 'well done' moments would enhance subjective wellbeing
   328‚Üí   328‚Üí(SWB; H1) and perceived work performance (H2) over the course of one week. While
   329‚Üí   329‚ÜíSWB did not increase over the one-week intervention period, results indicated
   330‚Üí   330‚Üíimprovements in perceived work performance, particularly among participants who
   331‚Üí   331‚Üíengaged more frequently with the task. These findings suggest that structured reflection
   332‚Üí   332‚ÜíIn-Confidence
   333‚Üí   333‚Üí
   334‚Üí   334‚Üí15
   335‚Üí   335‚Üíon modest, 'well done' moments may support performance perceptions in high-
   336‚Üí   336‚Üíachieving individuals, even over a relatively short timeframe.
   337‚Üí   337‚ÜíHypothesis 1 ‚Äì subjective wellbeing
   338‚Üí   338‚ÜíH1 predicted that participants would demonstrate increased SWB following the
   339‚Üí   339‚Üíintervention. This hypothesis was not supported and is not in keeping with prior
   340‚Üí   340‚Üíresearch on positive psychology interventions. Specifically, no significant pre‚Äìpost
   341‚Üí   341‚Üíimprovements in SWB were observed (Gander et al., 2016; Gander et al., 2017;
   342‚Üí   342‚ÜíSeligman et al., 2005; Southwell & Gould, 2017).
   343‚Üí   343‚ÜíSeveral factors may account for this pattern. First, baseline wellbeing levels in
   344‚Üí   344‚Üíthe present sample were high, limiting the capacity for measurable increases. Positive
   345‚Üí   345‚Üípsychology interventions (PPIs) are often less effective among individuals who already
   346‚Üí   346‚Üíreport elevated wellbeing (Espinosa et al., 2025; Gander et al., 2016), and ceiling effects
   347‚Üí   347‚Üímay therefore have constrained observable change. Second, the Flourishing Scale
   348‚Üí   348‚Üíprimarily captures eudaimonic aspects of wellbeing, which may be less sensitive to
   349‚Üí   349‚Üíshort-term fluctuations than affect-based measures commonly used in prior intervention
   350‚Üí   350‚Üíresearch (Diener et al., 2010; Hone et al., 2014). It is possible that subtle affective shifts
   351‚Üí   351‚Üíoccurred but were not detected by the selected measure.
   352‚Üí   352‚ÜíIt is also plausible that a one-week period is insufficient for broader changes in
   353‚Üí   353‚Üíeudaimonic wellbeing to consolidate, particularly within high-achieving individuals
   354‚Üí   354‚Üíwhose standards and evaluative frameworks are deeply embedded. Future research
   355‚Üí   355‚Üíincorporating longer intervention periods, affect-sensitive measures, and follow-up
   356‚Üí   356‚Üíassessments will help clarify whether wellbeing benefits emerge over time.
   357‚Üí   357‚ÜíIn-Confidence
   358‚Üí   358‚Üí
   359‚Üí   359‚Üí16
   360‚Üí   360‚ÜíHypothesis 2 ‚Äì perceived work performance
   361‚Üí   361‚ÜíH2 predicted that participants would report increases in perceived work performance
   362‚Üí   362‚Üífollowing the intervention. This hypothesis was supported. Although analytic
   363‚Üí   363‚Üíassumptions required both parametric and non-parametric approaches, the overall
   364‚Üí   364‚Üípattern of findings indicated small but consistent pre‚Äìpost improvements in perceived
   365‚Üí   365‚Üíperformance. Notably, a clear dose‚Äìresponse association emerged: participants who
   366‚Üí   366‚Üíengaged more frequently in journalling demonstrated larger increases in perceived work
   367‚Üí   367‚Üíperformance, strengthening confidence that engagement with the intervention
   368‚Üí   368‚Üícontributed to the observed change.
   369‚Üí   369‚ÜíQualitative feedback converged with these quantitative findings. Many
   370‚Üí   370‚Üíparticipants described increased awareness of daily achievements, greater motivation,
   371‚Üí   371‚Üíand increased momentum in their work. While some participants initially reported
   372‚Üí   372‚Üíambiguity regarding what qualified as a 'well done' moment, this appeared to diminish
   373‚Üí   373‚Üíover time as task fluency developed.
   374‚Üí   374‚ÜíThe performance increases observed are theoretically coherent. Prior research
   375‚Üí   375‚Üísuggests that daily progress is a central driver of engagement and motivation (Amabile
   376‚Üí   376‚Üí& Kramer, 2011). Rather than relying on the impact of major achievements, the present
   377‚Üí   377‚Üíintervention increased the frequency with which participants consciously noted modest
   378‚Üí   378‚Üíaccomplishments. Over repeated days, this accumulation of tiny successes may
   379‚Üí   379‚Üíreinforce a sense of ongoing progress and personal effectiveness, consistent with
   380‚Üí   380‚Üímodels emphasising daily progress as a key driver of engagement and performance
   381‚Üí   381‚Üí(Amabile & Kramer, 2011; Weick, 1984). In this view, magnitude may be less critical
   382‚Üí   382‚Üíthan regularity; that is, consistent recognition of small accomplishments may be
   383‚Üí   383‚Üísufficient to strengthen perceived capability and sustained effort on long-term goals.

   387‚Üí   387‚ÜíLimitations, and future directions
   388‚Üí   388‚ÜíThis study provides initial evidence that reflecting on small 'well done' moments may
   389‚Üí   389‚Üísupport performance perceptions among high achievers. While no short-term changes in
   390‚Üí   390‚ÜíSWB were detected, the pattern of findings suggests that consistent engagement with
   391‚Üí   391‚Üíthe intervention is meaningfully related to perceived performance change.
   392‚Üí   392‚ÜíSeveral limitations should be addressed in future work. First, the within-subjects
   393‚Üí   393‚Üídesign did not include a control condition. Although the observed dose‚Äìresponse pattern
   394‚Üí   394‚Üístrengthens confidence in the intervention‚Äôs relevance, causal inferences remain
   395‚Üí   395‚Üíunwarranted without further evidence. Incorporating active comparison groups will be
   396‚Üí   396‚Üíimportant for ruling out regression to the mean, expectancy effects, or time-related
   397‚Üí   397‚Üíconfounds. Including personality measures such as conscientiousness may also clarify
   398‚Üí   398‚Üíwhether individual differences account for variations in engagement and outcome
   399‚Üí   399‚Üíchange.
   400‚Üí   400‚ÜíSecond, the sample consisted primarily of young women who were university
   401‚Üí   401‚Üístudents with relatively high baseline wellbeing, limiting generalisability. Replication in
   402‚Üí   402‚Üímore diverse high-achieving populations, including business professionals or
   403‚Üí   403‚Üícompetitive athletes, will strengthen external validity. Future research should also
   404‚Üí   404‚Üíincorporate affect-sensitive wellbeing measures and longer follow-up periods to better
   405‚Üí   405‚Üícapture potential changes in SWB over time.
   406‚Üí   406‚ÜíThird, engagement and performance were assessed via self-report, and journal
   407‚Üí   407‚Üíentries were not collected. While this approach reduced potential observation effects, it
   408‚Üí   408‚Üíintroduces the possibility of expectancy or social desirability bias. Future studies would
   409‚Üí   409‚Üíbenefit from incorporating objective or third-party indicators of performance, such as
   410‚Üí   410‚Üíacademic outcomes or supervisor ratings, as well as examining journal content to
   411‚Üí   411‚Üíidentify which categories of 'well done' moments are most impactful.

   415‚Üí   415‚ÜíConclusion
   416‚Üí   416‚ÜíHigh achievers are often disciplined in tracking accomplishments, yet selective in what
   417‚Üí   417‚Üíthey count as an achievement. Major milestones are carefully recorded, while the daily
   418‚Üí   418‚Üístream of minor achievements frequently go unnoticed. The present study contributes to
   419‚Üí   419‚Üípast work on reflection-based PPIs by demonstrating that systematically recognising
   420‚Üí   420‚Üíthose modest, everyday 'well done' moments may meaningfully shift how performance
   421‚Üí   421‚Üíis experienced for high achiever populations.
   422‚Üí   422‚ÜíAlthough short-term changes in subjective wellbeing were not detected, these
   423‚Üí   423‚Üínovel findings indicate that modest, everyday actions may affect how performance is
   424‚Üí   424‚Üíexperienced. When high achievers repeatedly acknowledge tiny, even mundane
   425‚Üí   425‚Üíachievements, they may begin to experience greater work performance in consequence.
   426‚Üí   426‚ÜíIndeed, life is not constructed from occasional landmark events; it is built from
   427‚Üí   427‚Üímoments so small they rarely register in memory. Recognising these moments, it seems,
   428‚Üí   428‚Üídoes not inflate achievement; it recalibrates perception, which in turn increases
   429‚Üí   429‚Üíperceived performance. Further research will clarify how sustained attention to daily
   430‚Üí   430‚Üí'well done' moments influences both performance and wellbeing across time, but the
   431‚Üí   431‚Üípresent study offers initial evidence that small actions, consistently noticed, are not
   432‚Üí   432‚Üítrivial. They are impactful.
