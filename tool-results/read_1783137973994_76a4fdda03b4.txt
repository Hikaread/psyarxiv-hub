     9→Purpose: Visual perceptual learning (VPL) holds translational promise, yet progress is
    10→constrained by limited reproducibility and accessibility. Having previously introduced PLFest, a
    11→cross-platform, calibrated psychophysical platform, and validated its basic visual-function
    12→measurements, we test here whether PLFest recovers canonical perceptual-learning signatures
    13→and reproduces established effects of attentional cueing on learning across multiple training
    14→paradigms.
    15→Methods: Seventy-seven observers across four sites completed ten daily sessions of a contrast
    16→discrimination task under one of four conditions using different attentional cueing: parafoveal
    17→training without a cue (PT), exogenous cueing (ExAT), endogenous cueing (EnAT), or
    18→multisensory auditory cueing (MF). Targets appeared at the fovea and at 5° eccentricity; contrast
    19→thresholds were measured at each location by independent staircases, and learning was quantified
    20→from exponential fits to log-thresholds. Transfer was assessed with a five-task battery (acuity,
    21→contrast sensitivity, CSF cutoff, spatial-frequency cutoff, visual search) administered before and
    22→after training.
    23→Results: Every condition produced reliable learning at both eccentricities, showing the canonical
    24→learning curve, with peripheral improvements of approximately 43% (PT), 50% (EnAT), 58%
    25→(MF), and 78% (ExAT). Learning magnitude significantly differed across conditions, a
    26→difference driven by ExAT, which roughly doubled the learning of the other three. Two
    27→observations emerged descriptively: exogenous cueing specifically collapsed the peripheral–
    28→foveal threshold gap, an effect absent under the equally valid endogenous cue, and observers
    29→with the largest trained learning tended to show broader change across the assessment battery,
    30→although this transfer reached significance only for peripheral spatial-frequency cutoff.
    31→Conclusions: Across four attentional paradigms, PLFest collected reliable, robust, and
    32→theoretically interpretable learning data, reproducing established attention effects. These findings
    33→position PLFest as a platform for large-scale, accessible VPL research and the development of
    34→translational training paradigms.
    35→
    36→1. Introduction
    37→Visual Perceptual learning (VPL), the lasting improvement in visual performance that follows
    38→training, is one of the most extensively studied forms of adult plasticity, and one of that holds the
    39→promise for effective translational intervention (Sagi, 2011). Over the last two decades, VPL-
    40→inspired training paradigms have been used to ameliorate deficits in myopia (Tan & Fong, 2008),
    41→amblyopia (Levi & Li, 2009), presbyopia (Polat et al., 2012), and central vision loss due to
    42→macular degeneration (Chung, 2011; Maniglia et al., 2016; Maniglia et al., 2020). VPL may
    43→unlock effective training interventions and help better understand mechanisms of plasticity in the
    44→visual brain, yet progress in the field has been constrained by two persistent problems:
    45→reproducibility and accessibility. Concerning the first, while the field has been driven by novel
    46→results of learning enhancement of generalization following specific paradigms, the VPL
    47→literature exhibits inconsistent ability to replicate key studies showing transfer of learning effects
    48→(Aberg & Herzog, 2010; Hung & Seitz, 2014; Liang et al., 2015; Xiao et al., 2008; Zhang &
    49→Yang, 2014; Zhang & Yu, 2016). A likely reason for these discrepancies can be traced to
    50→methodological and environmental variability across laboratories, including differences in
    51→display hardware, timing, calibration, paradigms, and measurement procedures, which often lack
    52→standardization. Concerning issues with accessibility, the field of VPL has been historically
    53→reliant on rigorous psychophysics measurements requiring specialized equipment and controlled
    54→laboratory settings. A typical consequence of these constraints is low statistical power and high
    55→methodological heterogeneity across laboratories, which severely increase the probability of
    56→false positives and inflated effect sizes (Green et al., 2019).
    57→To address both issues, we developed PLFest, a cross-platform, calibrated, adaptive
    58→psychophysical training and assessment platform with consistent stimulus presentation,
    59→automated data handling, and a common procedure that can be deployed identically across sites
    60→and devices (Jayakumar et al., 2024). By moving rigorous PL paradigms onto portable, self-
    61→contained hardware, the platform makes large-scale, multi-site, standardized data collection
    62→feasible while preserving the measurement quality on which PL research depends. A first step
    63→towards using PLFest as a tool for larger, theory-driven studies, is to establish that the platform
    64→can recover the standard signatures of perceptual learning and that it can do so across different
    65→training paradigms.
    66→In an earlier publication, we demonstrated that PLFest can reliably assess basic visual functions,
    67→namely, visual acuity and contrast sensitivity, with high validity and reliability when compared
    68→with standard psychophysical methods (Jayakumar et al., 2024).To further validate PLFest as a
    69→platform for large-scale data collection across a broad range of training protocols, we present
    70→data showing that PLFest can reliably reproduce well-established effects of attentional
    71→modulation previously reported in the visual science literature.
    72→Attention plays a key role in visual perception and learning, and it has long been proposed as a
    73→gate on plasticity: only stimuli that are attended, and thus sufficiently processed, are thought to
    74→drive learning (Ahissar & Hochstein, 2004; Roelfsema et al., 2010; Seitz & Watanabe, 2005;
    75→Seitz, 2017). Attention is a complex construct whose study can be traced back to the nineteenth
    76→century, with early discussions by William James. In the context of visual perception, several
    77→distinct forms of attention have been identified. Here, we focus on covert attention—the
    78→
    79→allocation of attention to a specific spatial location in the visual field without accompanying eye
    80→movements—and, in particular, on its two principal components: exogenous attention, which is
    81→reflexive, stimulus-driven, and transient (typically peaking around 100 ms), and endogenous
    82→attention, which is voluntary, goal-directed, and sustained (typically deploying over
    83→approximately 300 ms) (Carrasco, 2011; Carrasco & Barbot, 2014; Peñaloza & Ogmen, 2022).
    84→Both types of convert attention transiently enhance stimulus detection (Carrasco, 2011; Yeshurun
    85→& Carrasco, 1999), and their long-term effects in the mechanisms of perceptual learning is
    86→currently under investigation (Donovan & Carrasco, 2018; Donovan et al., 2015; Dosher et al.,
    87→2010; Szpiro & Carrasco, 2015).
    88→Indeed, a growing body of work demonstrates that different forms of attention can facilitate
    89→perceptual learning. For example, Szpiro and Carrasco (2015)showed that training with
    90→exogenous spatial cues produced learning that was absent under neutral training conditions.
    91→Critically, this benefit persisted when observers were subsequently tested without cues,
    92→indicating a lasting change in perceptual processing rather than a transient attentional
    93→enhancement. Exogenous attention has also been shown to facilitate the transfer of learning to
    94→untrained retinal locations (Donovan et al., 2015). Similar transfer effects have been observed
    95→with valid endogenous cues (Donovan & Carrasco, 2018), suggesting that both reflexive and
    96→voluntary forms of spatial attention can promote the generalization of perceptual learning.
    97→Beyond spatial attention, cross-modal attentional manipulations can also enhance learning: for
    98→example, task-congruent auditory cues have been shown to accelerate visual perceptual learning,
    99→likely by improving stimulus encoding and processing efficiency (Seitz et al., 2006). Together
   100→these studies provide a set of concrete, directional predictions against which we can compare the
   101→reliability of our new testing platform.
   102→Here, we used PLFest to train observers on a peripheral contrast discrimination task under four
   103→attentional manipulation. All tasks share an identical task structure and differ only in the cue
   104→element (Figure 1). A Parafoveal Training condition (PT) provided baseline without attentional
   105→manipulations. Three attentional conditions included a 100%-valid cue manipulation: Exogenous
   106→Attention Training (ExAT), with a brief peripheral cue at the target location; Endogenous
   107→Attention Training (EnAT), with a symbolic central cue indicating the target location; and
   108→Multisensory Facilitation (MF), in which a spatially localized auditory cue was used instead of
   109→the visual cue. In each task, stimuli were presented both at a central (foveal) location and at two
   110→parafoveal locations at 5° eccentricity, allowing learning to be characterized separately at each
   111→eccentricity. Generalization of learning was assessed by a battery of five pre- and post-training
   112→assessments, namely visual acuity, contrast sensitivity and its high-frequency cutoff, spatial-
   113→frequency cutoff, and visual search reaction time.
   114→Our aim in this report is to demonstrate that PLFest can collect reliable and robust learning data
   115→across four distinct attentional paradigms—that it recovers the standard perceptual learning curve
   116→in every condition, distinguishes conditions in a manner consistent with the prior attention
   117→literature, resolves individual differences in learning, and supports a principled analysis of
   118→transfer to untrained visual tasks. While the goal of this study is methodological rather than
   119→theoretical, two observations emerged from the available data that we report descriptively and
   120→consider in this paper: an exogenous-specific collapse of the peripheral–foveal threshold gap,
   121→
   122→and a consistent, directional relationship between the magnitude of trained learning and the
   123→breadth of change across the assessment battery.
   124→2. Methods
   125→2.1 Participants
   126→Data was collected in 77 participants (mean age = 22 years [SD = 3 years]) across 4 research
   127→sites: Northeastern University, University of California at Riverside, University of Wisconsin
   128→Madison, and Mills College at Northeastern University. Observers were assigned to one of four
   129→ten-session training conditions that shared a common task structure and differed only in the
   130→attentional manipulation (Figure 1).
   131→2.2 Stimuli and training conditions
   132→The Parafoveal Training condition (PT) served as the baseline against which the cued conditions
   133→were compared. In PT, targets appeared at a central (foveal) location and at one of two peripheral
   134→locations (5° eccentricity) trial-wise randomization of target location discouraged anticipatory
   135→eye movements. The three remaining conditions preserved this structure and added a 100%-valid
   136→cue. In Exogenous Attention Training (ExAT), small black dots (48 ms duration, 96 ms stimulus-
   137→onset asynchrony) appeared above the target location on each trial. In Endogenous Attention
   138→Training (EnAT), a symbolic central cue (the letters LL, CT, UR, indicating the lower-left,
   139→central target or upper-right diagonal; 150 ms duration, 500 ms stimulus-onset asynchrony)
   140→appeared at fixation. In Multisensory Facilitation (MF), the task structure of ExAT was kept but
   141→the visual cue was replaced with a spatially localized auditory cue (50 ms, 1000 Hz tone,
   142→spatialized by convolution with a generic head-related transfer function). After applying the
   143→inclusion criteria described below, the conditions comprised 18 (PT), 19 (ExAT), 20 (EnAT),
   144→and 20 (MF) observers contributing to the peripheral learning analyses.
   145→2.3 Exclusion criteria
   146→Thresholds exceeding 0.1 in normalized contrast units in the training session were treated as non-
   147→converged estimates—reflecting attentional lapses or failures to understand the task rather than
   148→genuine perceptual thresholds. This criterion was applied to all four conditions. Exclusion
   149→preceded the log transformation. So that each learning trajectory was estimated from enough
   150→points, observers having fewer than five valid training sessions were excluded from all learning
   151→analyses.

   161→2.4 Apparatus and procedure
   162→Training was administered through the PLFest application (Jayakumar et al., 2024) on Apple
   163→iPad Pro (2021) tablets (2048 × 2732 px, 120 Hz), mounted on stands at a 50 cm viewing
   164→distance with head and chin rests to stabilize fixation. Testing was conducted under low ambient
   165→illumination (0.9 cd/m²) with a background luminance of 75 cd/m², and responses were recorded
   166→with a numeric keypad.
   167→On each trial, a fixation period of 500 ms preceded the presentation of a Gabor target (6 cycles
   168→per degree) presented for 200 ms, after which observers reported the target's orientation. Targets
   169→
   170→appeared at the central location or at one of the two peripheral locations, and target contrast was
   171→controlled by an independent 3-down/1-up staircase at each location. On the first block of the
   172→first session the staircase was initialized at 30% contrast; on every subsequent session the
   173→staircase resumed from the last contrast value reached in the preceding session, so that session-
   174→to-session changes in threshold reflected consolidated performance rather than repeated re-
   175→initialization. Observers completed ten training sessions of approximately one hour on
   176→consecutive days. Each session comprised five blocks of 50 trial runs per target location (750
   177→trials per session in total), preceded by a brief practice phase, with two-minute breaks interleaved
   178→between blocks.
   179→2.5 Threshold estimation and learning quantification
   180→Contrast thresholds were estimated from staircase reversals at each location. To assess learning,
   181→we used the first block of each session and computed thresholds as the average of the last four
   182→staircase reversals at each location. Performance at the start of a session reflects learning retained
   183→from previous sessions while minimizing contamination from within-session fatigue, which can
   184→accumulate over the course of training. For each observer and session, peripheral performance
   185→was then summarized as a single estimate by averaging the thresholds from the two peripheral
   186→locations (left and right). Foveal performance was given by the central location. The exclusion
   187→criterion above was applied to these per-session periphery and fovea estimates. Because
   188→threshold and contrast-sensitivity measures are ratio-scaled, all subsequent analyses were
   189→performed on log transformed thresholds.
   190→10
   191→For each observer we represented learning as the magnitude of improvement across the ten
   192→sessions, derived from an exponential model fit to that observer's log thresholds: 𝑦(𝑡) = 𝑎+
   193→10
   194→𝑑 ∙𝑒−𝑡/𝜏, where t indexes session, a is the asymptote, d the amplitude, and τ the time constant.
   195→We summarized each observer's learning with the estimated fitted improvement—the difference
   196→between the fitted curve at the first and last observed sessions, expressed in log units. This
   197→10
   198→value captures the total improvement supported by the data within the observed training window
   199→and provides a single index of learning that is stable across observers regardless of differences in
   200→the underlying decay rate or asymptote. For descriptive reporting, log-unit improvements were
   201→also expressed as a bounded percentage, (1−10−Δ)×100, where Δ is the realized
   202→improvement in log units.

   204→2.6 Visual assessments
   205→Transfer of training was evaluated with a battery of five visual assessments administered before
   206→and after training, all delivered through the PLFest App: visual acuity (Sloan Landolt-C
   207→expressed in logMAR), contrast sensitivity (CSF) and its high-spatial-frequency cutoff (CSF
   208→cutoff), spatial-frequency cutoff (SF cutoff, in cycles per degree), and visual-search reaction time
   209→(target “T” among inverted “L” distractors). Contrast measures are reported as percent contrast.
   210→Except where noted, tasks used 3-down-1-up staircases and orientation-discrimination responses
   211→to Gabor stimuli. Details about the assessments task and stimulus properties can be find in (cite
   212→our recent assessment paper)
   213→2.7 Statistical analysis
   214→
   215→To test learning within each task manipulation we used a one-sample t-tests against zero (Holm-
   216→Bonferonni corrected); differences in peripheral learning magnitude across conditions were
   217→tested with Welch's ANOVA followed by Games–Howell post-hoc comparisons; and within-
   218→condition eccentricity effects (peripheral vs. fovea) were tested with paired t-tests.
   219→Observers were classified as learners or non-learners by a median split on the learning metric.
   220→This procedure was performed using with-condition or pooled data across the four trained
   221→conditions. For each of the five assessments, the pre-to-post change of learners and non-learners
   222→was compared as a learner × session interaction using Welch's t-test on per-observer change
   223→scores. The robustness of each interaction was evaluated by sliding the learner/non-learner
   224→classification threshold across a range of percentiles and by re-testing after regressing each
   225→measure on its baseline level.
   226→3. Results
   227→3.1.1 Reliable learning at both trained eccentricities in every condition
   228→Across every condition, contrast thresholds fell steeply over the first session and then declined
   229→more gradually toward an asymptote, the canonical form of a perceptual-learning curve (Lu &
   230→Dosher, 2022) (Figure 2). Peripheral improvement was reliably greater than zero in all four
   231→conditions (one-sample t-tests against zero, Holm-Bonferroni corrected): PT (mean improvement
   232→≈43%; t(15) = 6.30, p < 0.001), ExAT (≈78%; t(16) = 6.85, p < 0.001), EnAT (≈50%; t(17) =
   233→5.64, p < 0.001), and MF (≈58%; t(19) = 4.96, p < 0.001). Foveal thresholds likewise improved
   234→reliably in every condition.

   237→periphery (red) and fovea (dark blue) in each condition: PT, ExAT, EnAT, and MF. Thresholds were estimated
   238→from the first block of each session (average of the last four reversals per location), with peripheral estimates
   239→averaged across the two peripheral locations. Shaded regions denote ±1 SEM. Note the convergence of the
   240→peripheral and foveal curves in ExAT, absent in the other three conditions.
   241→Learning occurred across all eccentricities: pooled across conditions, both trained locations
   242→improved highly reliably (peripheral t(69) = 10.5, p < 0.001; foveal t(69) = 9.8, p < 0.001), and
   243→the two eccentricities did not differ in overall learning magnitude (peripheral ≈59% versus foveal
   244→≈58%; paired t(69) = 0.23, p = 0.82). Within individual conditions, the only reliable peripheral-
   245→over-foveal advantage was in ExAT (paired t(16) = 2.37, p = 0.031), the eccentricity dissociation
   246→considered below; the remaining conditions improved comparably across eccentricity. The task
   247→thus produced robust perceptual learning at both the fovea and the periphery.
   248→Although learning was present at both eccentricities, we focus the remaining analyses solely on
   249→the peripheral measure. Three considerations motivate this focus. First, peripheral thresholds
   250→were substantially higher at baseline than foveal thresholds (session-1 medians of approximately
   251→5.9% and 1.7% contrast, respectively), leaving more room for measurable improvement. Second,
   252→the periphery is where the conditions differed: as shown below, the inter-condition differences in
   253→learning were carried entirely by the peripheral measure, whereas foveal learning was
   254→comparable across conditions. Third, the peripheral estimate provides a single, well-defined
   255→
   256→index of the trained parafoveal locations that serves as the common learning metric across all
   257→parts of this report.
   258→3.1.2 Learning magnitude differed across conditions, driven by exogenous cueing
   259→The conditions differed in the magnitude of peripheral learning (Welch's ANOVA, F(3, 35.9) =
   260→4.98, p = 0.006). The difference was driven entirely by the exogenous-attention condition. Mean
   261→peripheral improvement was 0.66 log units (≈78%) in ExAT, roughly double that of the other
   262→10
   263→three conditions, which were closely comparable to one another: 0.25 log units (≈43%) in PT,
   264→10
   265→0.31 (≈50%) in EnAT, and 0.37 (≈58%) in MF. A contrasts test confirmed that ExAT exceeded
   266→PT (Δ = 0.41 log units, ≈59%; p = 0.004) and EnAT (Δ = 0.35, ≈55%; p = 0.019); the ExAT–
   267→10
   268→MF contrast was in the same direction but did not reach significance (Δ = 0.28, ≈44%; p = 0.12).
   269→Additionally, the three non-exogenous conditions did not differ from one another (all p > 0.43).
   270→3.1.3 The exogenous advantage is specific to the periphery and collapses the
   271→eccentricity gap
   272→A distinctive feature of the exogenous attention condition is evident in the learning curves
   273→themselves (Figure 2). In PT, EnAT, and MF, peripheral thresholds remained substantially
   274→elevated relative to foveal thresholds throughout training, preserving the eccentricity gap
   275→expected from the lower resolution of peripheral vision. In ExAT, by contrast, the peripheral and
   276→foveal curves converged: peripheral thresholds fell toward foveal levels, so that by the end of
   277→training the two were closely comparable. Consistent with this, peripheral improvement in ExAT
   278→(≈78%) substantially exceeded its foveal improvement (≈55%), the only condition with a reliable
   279→peripheral-over-foveal advantage (paired t(16) = 2.37, p = 0.031), whereas the remaining
   280→conditions improved more symmetrically across eccentricity. The EnAT, which employed an
   281→equally valid but symbolic central cue, preserved the full eccentricity gap—indicating that the
   282→collapse is specific to exogenous cueing rather than a general consequence of valid spatial
   283→cueing. Importantly, the observed ExAT effect is independent of eye movement as demonstrated
   284→by a control experiment using eye tracker conducted on a single session on PT and ExAT
   285→(supplementary material). We return to the interpretation of this dissociation, and to whether the
   286→peripheral enhancement reflects consolidated learning or an online attentional benefit, in the
   287→discussion; the present analyses establish the effect descriptively without adjudicating its
   288→mechanism.
   289→3.1.4 Heterogeneity of learning profiles across conditions
   290→Within each condition, observers spanned a wide range of learning outcomes, from steep
   291→threshold reductions to little or no measurable change (Figure 3). A within-condition median
   292→split on the peripheral learning metric separated this range into two readily distinguishable
   293→profiles in all four conditions. Learners showed the characteristic perceptual learning
   294→trajectory—a steep decline in peripheral threshold over the first inter-session interval followed
   295→by continued, more gradual improvement—whereas non-learners showed comparatively flat
   296→trajectories with little net change across training. Averaged across conditions, learners improved
   297→their peripheral thresholds by approximately 56–89% and non-learners by approximately 23–
   298→46%, with the two profiles clearly separated in every condition. The separation was present in
   299→
   300→the baseline condition (PT) as well as in the cued conditions, indicating that individual variation
   301→in learning is a general feature of the training rather than a property introduced by any particular
   302→attentional manipulation.
   303→Figure 3. Peripheral learning profiles for learners (blue) and non-learners (red) in each
   304→condition: PT, ExAT, EnAT, and MF. Faint traces show individual observers and thick traces show
   305→the group-mean trajectory for each subgroup. Observers were classified by a within-condition median
   306→split on the peripheral learning metric. Group sizes were PT (8 learners, 8 non-learners), ExAT (9, 8),
   307→EnAT (9, 9), and MF (10, 10). Thresholds are shown in percent contrast.
   308→The ExAT again stood out: its learners reached the lowest thresholds of any group, descending to
   309→near-foveal levels by the end of training, consistent with the eccentricity convergence reported in
   310→section 3.1.1.
   311→We note one feature of the classification relevant to its interpretation. In some conditions—most
   312→visibly ExAT and PT—observers classified as learners tended to begin training with higher
   313→peripheral thresholds than those classified as non-learners, whereas in EnAT and MF the two
   314→groups began at more similar baselines and diverged over training. Because the learning metric
   315→reflects the magnitude of threshold reduction, observers with higher starting thresholds have
   316→greater room to improve and are therefore more likely to be classified as learners, while
   317→observers already near floor at the outset can show little measurable change. The learner/non-
   318→
   319→learner distinction should accordingly be read as a descriptive summary of the observed range of
   320→learning trajectories, partly conditioned on baseline performance, rather than as a baseline-
   321→independent trait. This is one reason the continuous learning metric, rather than the median split,
   322→is used for the inferential analyses in Part 3.
   323→3.1.5 Pooled across conditions, learners show more transfer than non-learners across
   324→assessments––a consistent but not-yet-reliable pattern
   325→Next, we asked how transfer looks across learners versus non-learners. Engaging attention might
   326→promote generalization of learning, as previously shown in terms of retinal transfet (Carrasco
   327→Szpiro Donovan). To address this, we pooled observers across all four training conditions to
   328→maximize power and, using the peripheral learning metric defined above (the fitted exponential
   329→reduction in threshold across training), classified each observer as a learner or non-learner by a
   330→median split on that metric. For each of the five visual assessments we then compared the pre-to-
   331→post change of learners and non-learners (a learner × session interaction).
   332→The pattern was encouraging and directionally consistent: on every assessment, learners changed
   333→in the improving direction relative to non-learners, while non-learners tended to show little
   334→change or slight worsening over the same interval. The clearest difference was on peripheral
   335→spatial-frequency cutoff, where learners improved (24.6 → 26.8 cpd) while non-learners declined
   336→(23.0 → 20.0 cpd); this interaction was reliable (p = 0.005) and remained stable across a range of
   337→learner/non-learner cutoff thresholds rather than depending on the median split. A nominal
   338→interaction was also present for CSF cutoff (p = 0.035), with smaller, same-direction trends for
   339→acuity, CSF, and visual-search reaction time (all p > 0.28).
   340→We interpret this convergence cautiously. Because the analysis pools observers trained under
   341→different attentional manipulations into a single learner/non-learner contrast, and because the
   342→resulting per-group samples are modest, the cross-assessment pattern is best read as a consistent
   343→direction of effect rather than as established transfer: apart from peripheral SF cutoff, the
   344→individual interactions did not reach significance. The uniformity of the direction is nonetheless
   345→suggestive that observers who benefit most from peripheral training also tend to show broader
   346→visual change, and motivates collecting additional, condition-specific data to test whether this
   347→transfer is reliable and whether it differs by training manipulation.

   356→4. Discussion
   357→In this study we extended the validation of a novel, cross-platform application, PLFest, designed
   358→to promote large scale, accessible visual science research and training. Specifically, following a
   359→foundational validation study where we showed validity and reliability of basic visual function
   360→measurements (Jayakumar 2024), we here present training data with attentional manipulation
   361→paradigms applied to a standard contrast discrimination task. We specifically tested 4 attentional
   362→manipulations, including exogenous cueing, endogenous cueing, multisensory cueing, and
   363→control (no cue). Results showed that every condition produced reliable learning at both trained
   364→eccentricities (fovea and near periphery): contrast thresholds fell steeply over the first inter-
   365→session intervals and then asymptoted, tracing the standard perceptual-learning curve, with
   366→peripheral improvements ranging from ~43% (PT) to ~78% (ExAT). Second, the conditions
   367→differed in learning magnitude, in particular the exogenous attention condition roughly doubled
   368→the learning of the three other condition —a pattern consistent with prior reports that exogenous
   369→cueing potentiates learning (Szpiro & Carrasco, 2015). Third, ExAT alone nullified the
   370→peripheral–foveal performance gap, bringing peripheral performance to near-foveal levels, unlike
   371→the other conditions, in which foveal performance was systematically better than peripheral.
   372→Fourth, the platform provided a wide range of individual learning profiles within every condition
   373→and supported a transfer analysis that linked the magnitude of trained learning to the breadth of
   374→
   375→change across the assessment battery. Taken together, these results establish that PLFest collects
   376→reliable, robust, and theoretically interpretable learning data across paradigms.
   377→The most striking observation was the exogenous-specific collapse of the eccentricity gap.
   378→Several features of the data bear on its interpretation. The peripheral–foveal convergence in
   379→ExAT was present essentially from the first session rather than emerging gradually over training,
   380→which suggests that at least part of the effect reflects an online attentional enhancement of
   381→peripheral signals rather than a slow, consolidated change in perceptual sensitivity. This is
   382→mechanistically plausible: exogenous attention is known to boost the effective contrast and
   383→spatial resolution of stimuli at the cued location, and these benefits are largest in the periphery,
   384→where baseline resolution is lowest and there is the most headroom for enhancement (Carrasco,
   385→2011; Yeshurun & Carrasco, 1999). On this account, the valid peripheral cue in ExAT may have
   386→repeatedly elevated peripheral signal strength during training, narrowing the eccentricity gap as
   387→an attentional gain rather than as learning per se. At the same time, an enabling role for attention
   388→in learning cannot be excluded—exogenous attention has been proposed to push sensory
   389→stimulation past the threshold required for plasticity (Donovan et al., 2015; Szpiro & Carrasco,
   390→2015), so the elevated peripheral signal may also have facilitated genuine learning. The
   391→dissociation from EnAT is informative here because the endogenous cue was equally valid yet
   392→did not collapse the gap, the effect appears specific to the transient, stimulus-driven dynamics of
   393→exogenous attention rather than to spatial cue validity in general. Adjudicating the online-
   394→enhancement versus consolidated-learning accounts will require a within-task neutral-cue
   395→posttest, in which trained observers are re-tested without the cue (Szpiro & Carrasco, 2015); we
   396→identify this as the key next step.
   397→Several limitations constrain the conclusions that can be drawn from the present study. Because
   398→the requirements of an ongoing clinical trial limited the sample size available for condition-
   399→specific transfer analyses, our learner/non-learner transfer analysis was pooled across conditions.
   400→Although the observed effects were generally consistent in direction, they reached statistical
   401→significance only for peripheral spatial-frequency cutoff. Accordingly, the pattern of cross-
   402→assessment results should be interpreted as suggestive evidence of transfer rather than definitive
   403→proof of generalized learning. Furthermore, learner classification was partly influenced by
   404→baseline performance, as observers with higher initial thresholds had greater opportunity for
   405→improvement. Consequently, this classification should be viewed as a descriptive summary of the
   406→range of learning trajectories observed in our sample rather than as a baseline-independent
   407→characteristic of individual learners.
   408→These limitations are appropriate for a feasibility study and do not diminish the central
   409→conclusion of this work. Across four training paradigms, PLFest generated reliable, well-behaved
   410→learning data and successfully reproduced established effects from the perceptual learning
   411→literature. In particular, the platform reliably captured the distinct influences of exogenous,
   412→endogenous, and cross-modal attentional cueing on perceptual learning. These findings extend
   413→our previous validation of PLFest as a tool for measuring visual acuity and contrast sensitivity by
   414→demonstrating its ability to detect higher-level cognitive influences on learning and to support
   415→multisensory experimental manipulations. Together, these results position PLFest as a promising
   416→platform for large-scale, accessible investigations of perceptual learning mechanisms, as well as
   417→for the development and evaluation of translational training paradigms.
