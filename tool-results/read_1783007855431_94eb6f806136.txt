   900→concussions show compounded DMN hypoconnectivity,98 and reduced FPN-amygdala FC is observed in youth with
   901→any concussion history.99 Amygdala-focused studies in msTBI link frontoamygdala FC to affective
   902→dysregulation,201,202 while hippocampal network FC is associated with memory performance.199 Graph theory
   903→studies suggest that global network topology is disrupted, with modularity and segregation particularly
   904→affected.138,200 Age and sex may moderate the timing and extent of these alterations.200 The msTBI pediatric
   905→literature, while sparse, is broadly consistent with mTBI findings in showing mixed hyper- and hypoconnectivity
   906→across phases, with specific associations between FC alterations and both motor and cognitive
   907→outcomes.80,138,197,198
   908→Moderate-to-severe TBI
   909→This section summarizes findings across 46 rsfMRI studies that compare healthy individuals and
   910→individuals with msTBI. Because recovery in msTBI is more protracted than in mTBI, studies have focused on later
   911→
   912→windows of time post-injury. As mentioned in the introduction, for msTBI we consider "acute" to be 0-3 months
   913→post-injury, "subacute" 3-6 months, "early chronic" 6 months to 2 years, and "late chronic" any time beyond 2 years.
   914→Default mode and parietal memory networks
   915→DMN hypoconnectivity is generally reported in the acute phase. In patients with post-traumatic amnesia
   916→(PTA), reduced PCC-parahippocampal FC correlates with associative memory impairment and normalizes when
   917→patients emerge from PTA, although hyperconnectivity between PMN hubs and posterior DMN hubs is
   918→simultaneously observed.204 Peak serum S100B levels correlate negatively with DMN FC, linking injury severity to
   919→network disruption.205 In a pediatric cohort, increased DMN FC with the right dorsal premotor cortex is associated
   920→with worse motor performance, suggesting maladaptive DMN-motor coupling.80 In the subacute phase, DMN
   921→hyperconnectivity becomes more common, with increased FC between the PCC and MTL network and middle
   922→frontal regions,206,207 and PCC hypersynchrony with the anterior DMN alongside altered hippocampal FC with
   923→task-positive networks.208 However, one longitudinal study reports low but steeply increasing DMN FC at 6 months
   924→that peaks at approximately 1.5 years,209 suggesting that scan timing within the subacute window significantly
   925→influences results.
   926→In the early chronic phase (6 months - 2 years), DMN recovery trajectories diverge based on structural
   927→injury phenotypes and secondary behavioral symptoms.210–212 Some patients maintain compensatory
   928→hyperconnectivity potentially reflecting ongoing high metabolic demands,206,207,212 while others display
   929→hypoconnectivity linked to severe structural disconnections or comorbid depression.210,211 Some FC trajectories
   930→stabilize toward healthy control levels,209 or manifest as mixed patterns of functional reorganization.208 Several
   931→factors contribute to this divergence. Longitudinal data show a non-monotonic trajectory in which compensatory
   932→hyperconnectivity rises progressively from 6 months and peaks at approximately 1.5 years before reversing, so
   933→cross-sectional studies sampling at different points within this window can produce divergent findings even
   934→within similar cohorts. Structural injury severity also shapes recovery, with extensive white matter damage
   935→producing enduring hypoconnectivity reflecting permanent disconnection while relatively preserved structural
   936→integrity allows sustained compensatory upregulation. Comorbid depression further modulates FC, tracking
   937→patterns of DMN disruption that diverge from those driven by structural injury alone.
   938→In the late chronic phase, DMN outcomes are highly heterogeneous. FC may normalize completely,
   939→sometimes paired with a shift of compensatory activity to executive networks.209,213,214 Conversely, permanent
   940→white matter degradation can lead to enduring hypoconnectivity, particularly affecting long-range and
   941→interhemispheric DMN connections.93,122,201,215 Other patients show sustained compensatory hyperconnectivity
   942→correlating with long-term cognitive outcomes,100,216,217 or mixed patterns of reorganization tied to localized
   943→deficits.85,218,219 Age at injury modulates the response: aging TBI cohorts show declining DMN involvement as
   944→compensatory load shifts toward the FPN, consistent with the CRUNCH framework (Compensation-Related
   945→Utilization of Neural Circuits Hypothesis), which posits that aging brains recruit additional neural resources —
   946→particularly executive networks — to maintain cognitive performance.214 Finally, hyper- and hypoconnectivity
   947→findings can coexist within the same network depending on seed placement, with structural damage to the
   948→cingulum producing focal hypoconnectivity between core DMN nodes and diffuse hyperconnectivity in surrounding
   949→cortex.216
   950→Frontoparietal network
   951→In the acute phase, FPN findings depend heavily on what FC is correlated with. Studies linking FC to
   952→structural injury markers report hypoconnectivity: peak serum S100B levels correlate negatively with FPN FC in
   953→severe TBI patients in intensive care.205 In contrast, ICA-based analyses of patients in post-traumatic amnesia
   954→identify increased within-FPN FC,204 suggesting that intrinsic networks may upregulate acutely in response to the
   955→profound confusion of the PTA state. Subacutely, findings diverge. FPN hyperconnectivity is reported alongside
   956→evidence that highly connected hubs take on increased FC to support recovery,207 while FPN hypoconnectivity
   957→
   958→paired with DMN hyperconnectivity is reported in another study, suggesting a disrupted reciprocal relationship
   959→between task-positive and task-negative networks.206 One longitudinal study reports null FPN findings at this
   960→stage.209
   961→In the early chronic phase, divergent trajectories persist and largely reflect methodological choices.
   962→Whole-brain graph theory analyses report sustained FPN hyperconnectivity through the first year, with
   963→information routing concentrating in highly connected hubs.207,212 Seed-based analyses measuring specific
   964→long-range pathways report continued hypoconnectivity (dlPFC to posterior parietal regions)206 or a lack of
   965→focused network recruitment characterized by diffuse, aberrant suppression (greater negative FC) originating
   966→from the dlPFC.215 Another study reports mixed patterns reflecting altered FC between FPN hubs and medial
   967→temporal regions.208 A longitudinal mixed-effects analysis offers a reconciling timeline: within-FPN FC increases
   968→progressively from 6 months post-injury, peaks at approximately 1.5 years, and reverses thereafter,209 so
   969→cross-sectional studies sampling at different early-chronic timepoints can produce divergent findings even within
   970→similar cohorts. In the late chronic phase, hypoconnectivity is the dominant finding, reported across five studies
   971→that predominantly used seed-based approaches measuring long-range and inter-hemispheric connections — the
   972→specific tracts most vulnerable to chronic white matter degradation.93,213,220–222 Mixed FC patterns reported in three
   973→studies emerge from whole-brain connectome or global voxel-based analyses that capture both long-range
   974→disconnection and compensatory short-range upregulation, with loss of FPN segregation distinguishing patients
   975→with persistent symptoms from those without.85,219 Null results from longitudinal studies sampling at 3+ years
   976→post-injury209,223 are consistent with evidence that compensatory FPN hyperconnectivity peaks at approximately
   977→1.5 years before reversing, so later sampling may capture the FPN as earlier alterations transition back toward
   978→baseline. One study notes that despite persistent FPN abnormalities, chronic cognitive deficits are driven more by
   979→sensorimotor or DMN dysfunction than by the FPN.217 In an aging TBI cohort, FPN hyperconnectivity is reported
   980→alongside DMN normalization, interpreted as a shift in compensatory strategy toward executive networks in older
   981→adults.214
   982→Salience and cinguloopercular networks
   983→In the acute phase, SN hyperconnectivity is reported in patients with post-traumatic amnesia, with
   984→increased FC in core regions including the ACC and anterior insula.204 This contrasts with the predominantly
   985→hypoconnective pattern seen in the DMN and FPN at this timepoint, suggesting that SN hubs may be among the
   986→first to mount a compensatory response after injury. Subacutely, the anterior insula and ACC are identified as
   987→among the most highly recruited nodes, demonstrating robust hyperconnectivity.206,207 However, broader CON
   988→regions show more variable patterns, including some hypoconnectivity,206 and one longitudinal study reports null
   989→results at 6 months.209 This suggests that the compensatory response may be concentrated in core SN hubs rather
   990→than distributed across the broader network.
   991→In the early chronic phase, SN hyperconnectivity persists in some cohorts,206,207 with one study linking
   992→insula hyperconnectivity specifically to post-TBI depression.211 In the late chronic phase, hypoconnectivity is the
   993→dominant finding.213,220,221 This disconnection is tied to impaired self-awareness and performance monitoring.220
   994→Loss of network segregation between the CON, SN, and other networks is reported as a distinguishing feature of
   995→patients with persistent chronic symptoms.85 Null results are also reported, indicating that some patients achieve
   996→normalization.209,223 Across phases and despite variability in the direction of FC changes, the functional
   997→implications are relatively consistent: disrupted interactions between the SN and other control networks appear
   998→central to executive dysfunction after msTBI.220
   999→Dorsal and ventral attention networks
  1000→In the subacute phase, a pediatric cohort reports mixed DAN FC, with increased DAN-sensorimotor FC
  1001→alongside reduced DAN-caudate FC.80 In the early chronic phase, findings diverge: AN hyperconnectivity is
  1002→reported at approximately six month post-injury,212 while DAN hypoconnectivity centered on the intraparietal
  1003→
  1004→sulcus is reported in another study and linked to reduced structural integrity in the superior longitudinal
  1005→fasciculus.224 Mixed DAN FC patterns are also reported during this window.208 In the late chronic phase, outcomes
  1006→are heterogeneous. DAN hypoconnectivity is reported, including reduced DMN-DAN interactivity.93
  1007→Hyperconnectivity within a generalized AN is also reported,223 as are mixed DAN FC patterns in which loss of
  1008→network segregation distinguishes patients with persistent symptoms from those who have recovered.85 The DAN
  1009→does not show a clear dominant direction across phases, with hyper-, hypo-, and mixed findings all represented,
  1010→suggesting that the specific pattern may depend on chronicity, structural damage, and individual recovery
  1011→trajectory.
  1012→Somatomotor lateral and somatomotor dorsal networks
  1013→In the acute phase, SMN hypoconnectivity is reported in adults, with the degree of suppression correlating
  1014→with S100B levels.205 In a pediatric cohort, mixed FC patterns are observed between the SMN and attention
  1015→networks, as described in the DAN section above.80 No subacute studies were identified. In the early chronic
  1016→phase, mixed FC patterns are observed in a pediatric cohort, with reduced motor-caudate FC associated with
  1017→poorer response inhibition.198 In the late chronic phase, mixed FC is the most common finding,85,219 with loss of SMN
  1018→segregation from other networks reported as a feature distinguishing patients with persistent symptoms.85 Null
  1019→results are also reported, with one study noting that interhemispheric SMN FC is preserved even when cognitive
  1020→networks show severe disconnection.122,213 SMN hyperconnectivity is reported in one study, where increased
  1021→sensorimotor FC predicts worse attention deficits in TBI patients which is opposite to healthy controls, where
  1022→sensorimotor FC is negatively correlated with attention.217 This suggests that SMN hyperconnectivity may be
  1023→maladaptive rather than compensatory.
  1024→Auditory and visual networks
  1025→In the acute phase, hypoconnectivity in both the AUD and VIS networks is reported, with the degree of
  1026→suppression negatively correlated with peak S100B levels.205 No studies examined these networks in the subacute
  1027→or early chronic phases.
  1028→In the late chronic phase, VIS hypoconnectivity is the most consistent finding across seed-based studies
  1029→investigating long-range connections.218,219,222 Reduced interhemispheric FC between VIS regions and PFC is
  1030→associated with impaired facial-affect recognition, linking VIS disconnection to social and emotional processing
  1031→deficits.222 In a pediatric msTBI cohort, however, the opposite pattern is reported for a frontal–visual pathway:
  1032→positive (rather than the typical anticorrelated) FC between frontal regions and the fusiform gyrus, replicated
  1033→across two samples and interpreted as an immature or inefficient social-brain configuration.203 In contrast, one
  1034→study utilizing ICA reports VIS hyperconnectivity.217 This divergence may reflect a common topological
  1035→consequence of TBI, wherein long-range tracts connecting the visual cortex to higher-order cognitive and
  1036→affective centers are structurally impaired (resulting in between-network hypoconnectivity), while local
  1037→synchronization within the primary visual network itself is upregulated (intra-network hyperconnectivity). One
  1038→potential exception comes from a pediatric msTBI cohort reporting positive frontal–fusiform FC where uninjured
  1039→adolescents show anticorrelation;203 because this frontal–visual coupling normally shifts from positive to negative
  1040→across development, the finding may reflect an immature or delayed FC profile rather than a departure from the
  1041→adult long-range pattern. The AUD network has received little targeted investigation: null results are reported in
  1042→one study,217 while another reports mixed AUD and VIS FC patterns in which loss of network segregation, including
  1043→aberrant connections involving auditory and visual regions, differentiates patients with persistent symptoms from
  1044→those who have recovered.85
  1045→Parietooccipital and medial temporal lobe networks
  1046→There are no studies covering the PON. Other early chronic findings are mixed, with hippocampal
  1047→hyperconnectivity with proximal temporal and brainstem regions reported in one study,208 while reduced
  1048→
  1049→interhemispheric hippocampal FC is reported in another.215
  1050→In the late chronic phase, reduced left hippocampal-thalamic FC is associated with worse verbal memory
  1051→and learning in an adolescent cohort.199 Mixed FC patterns involving the hippocampus and frontal regions are
  1052→reported,219 and null results are observed in an aging remote TBI cohort, where hippocampal FC shows lower
  1053→reliability compared to cortical connections.214
  1054→Graph theory
  1055→Graph theory studies in msTBI show that injury alters global organizational properties of the brain, shifting
  1056→the balance between segregation and integration across the connectome.
  1057→In the early months of recovery, global topology is characterized by hyperconnectivity disproportionately
  1058→concentrated in highly connected cortical hubs.207 This hyperconnectivity is dynamic: overall connection strength
  1059→diminishes over the first six months as the network transitions toward a more efficient small-world configuration,7
  1060→and network cost-efficiency evolves over the first year, with hyperconnectivity peaking at approximately six
  1061→months before transitioning toward a more cost-efficient configuration.212
  1062→Chronically, the dominant finding is loss of efficiency and small-world topology.154 Reductions in global,
  1063→local, and cost efficiency are reported, driven by damage to long-range, interhemispheric, and between-network
  1064→connections.93 Comorbid depression further disrupts network topology, specifically affecting amygdala-related
  1065→circuits.122 Reduced network flexibility is also reported, with fewer transitions between distinct brain states
  1066→predicted by global hyperconnectivity, suggesting that over-reliance on core networks restricts dynamic range.225
  1067→Some topological shifts may be adaptive: heightened DMN-SN internetwork FC correlates with better
  1068→metacognitive accuracy,223 and increased local connection strength within right PPC alongside reduced variability
  1069→in modular structure has been reported, with right PPC modularity predicting cognitive performance and
  1070→suggesting that hyperconnectivity reflects shared responses to neurological disruption rather than breakdown in
  1071→local modular organization.226 Null results for hippocampal-subcortical graph metrics are reported in an aging
  1072→remote TBI cohort.214
  1073→Graph theory has also been applied to specific clinical questions. In pediatric TBI, atypical global
  1074→organization including lower modularity and higher small-worldness is reported years after injury and linked to
  1075→externalizing behavioral problems.138 Patients at risk for post-traumatic epilepsy show hyper-connected but
  1076→hypo-segregated networks with lower clustering coefficients, shifting toward a random configuration.227
  1077→Multimodal graph matching yields a structural connectome dissimilarity score that correlates with processing
  1078→speed, executive function, and PTA duration; functional connectome dissimilarity did not significantly correlate
  1079→with cognitive scores.228
  1080→Other
  1081→Several studies examined subcortical seeds. Disrupted corticostriatal FC, using data-driven subdivisions
  1082→of the caudate as seeds, is associated with executive dysfunction.221 Striatal FC is also altered more broadly, with
  1083→reduced striatal-visual cortex FC reported alongside task-induced subjective fatigue specifically linked to altered
  1084→striatal FC with the precuneus and cerebellum.218 In DAI, striatal subdivision seeds show reduced cortico-striatal
  1085→(ventral striatum-inferior frontal, dorsal putamen-anterior cingulate) and striatal-cerebellar FC, with increased
  1086→dorsal caudate-inferior parietal FC interpreted as compensatory; these FC values correlate with disability, motor,
  1087→and agitation scores.229 In a mixed-severity cohort, seed-based analyses of the substantia nigra identify reduced
  1088→FC with the superior medial frontal gyrus and left angular gyrus; FC between the substantia nigra and angular
  1089→gyrus is positively associated with anxiety symptoms and negatively associated with depressive symptoms
  1090→post-injury.149 In the first two weeks post-injury, divergent acute thalami- and hippocampi-cortical FC profiles
  1091→distinguish patients who go on to develop early versus late post-traumatic seizures, with early-seizure patients
  1092→showing hyperconnectivity in thalami- and hippocampi-cortical networks and late-seizure patients showing
  1093→reduced positive and increased negative hippocampal-cortical connectivity relative to seizure-free patients.230 In
  1094→
  1095→DAI, hypothalamic FC is dissociated: medial hypothalamus hyperconnectivity to the superior temporal gyrus and
  1096→operculum alongside lateral hypothalamus hypoconnectivity to the basal ganglia, cingulate, and middle frontal
  1097→cortex, with reduced lateral hypothalamus FC predicting cognitive state (MMSE).231 Homotopic (interhemispheric)
  1098→FC is reduced in TBI, reflecting disconnection between anatomically mirrored regions across hemispheres.232
  1099→Amygdala-focused studies consistently report altered FC with prefrontal and temporal regions. A trend toward
  1100→reduced right amygdala-rACC FC is observed in adolescents,201 and hyperconnectivity within medial and
  1101→ventrolateral amygdala networks predicts externalizing and internalizing behavioral problems.202 Amygdala
  1102→hyperconnectivity is also reported in patients with comorbid depressive symptoms.122 In a large cohort (n=79)
  1103→examining how FC scales with depressive symptom severity, higher depression scores are associated with
  1104→increased FC between limbic and cognitive-control regions and with decreased FC between the subgenual
  1105→anterior cingulate and ventral striatum and amygdala, interpreted as an inefficient cognitive-control strategy
  1106→alongside disrupted reward and emotion processing.233 Hypoconnectivity in facial-affect processing networks
  1107→involving the amygdala and temporal regions is associated with impaired facial-emotion recognition.222 Finally,
  1108→hyperconnectivity within a specific vmPFC-dACC circuit is reported as a compensatory mechanism providing
  1109→resilience against the development of apathy.141
  1110→Moderate-Severe TBI summary
  1111→The msTBI literature demonstrates persistent but heterogeneous disruption of large-scale brain FC, with
  1112→time since injury strongly influencing the direction and pattern of alterations. Acutely, hypoconnectivity
  1113→predominates in the DMN, FPN, SMN, AUD, VIS, and MTL,204,205 while mixed patterns and early hyperconnectivity
  1114→within posterior DMN hubs and the CON network are also observed.204 Subacutely, DMN and SN hyperconnectivity
  1115→becomes more common, concentrated in highly connected hubs,206,207 while the FPN shows divergent patterns
  1116→reflecting a disrupted reciprocal relationship between task-positive and task-negative networks.206,207 Recovery
  1117→trajectories diverge in the early chronic phase based on structural injury phenotypes, comorbidities, and individual
  1118→recovery status.206–209,211,212,224
  1119→In the late chronic phase, hypoconnectivity is the dominant finding across the FPN, SN, and DAN,93,213,220
  1120→while the DMN remains the most variable network with normalization, sustained hypoconnectivity, compensatory
  1121→hyperconnectivity, and mixed patterns all reported. Late chronic FC alterations are consistently tied to specific
  1122→clinical deficits including impaired performance monitoring,220 executive dysfunction,221 facial-affect
  1123→recognition,222 and memory.199 In aging TBI cohorts, compensatory activity shifts from the DMN to executive
  1124→networks.214
  1125→Graph theory studies provide the clearest temporal framework: early hyperconnectivity concentrated in
  1126→hub regions207 transitions toward chronic loss of efficiency and small-world topology,93,154 with network
  1127→cost-efficiency peaking around 6 months before seeking a more economical configuration.7,212 Multimodal graph
  1128→approaches show promise as quantitative biomarkers of whole-brain injury burden.228 Across networks and
  1129→phases, FC changes are repeatedly linked to deficits in memory, attention, executive control, and emotional
  1130→regulation, supporting the view that altered resting-state network organization is a core feature of msTBI.
  1131→Military-related TBI
  1132→This section summarizes 31 papers.
  1133→Over half a million post-9/11 veterans are estimated to have sustained mTBI, with approximately 75% of
  1134→theater-related head injuries involving explosive blast.234 The remaining injuries are due to blunt force. Due to
  1135→impracticalities of conducting research in theater, most military TBI studies focus on veterans and active-duty
  1136→service members (ADSMs) whose injuries occurred months to years before scanning. Only one study included
  1137→subjects in the postacute phase.235 Knowledge from acute timepoints is critical for understanding how to address
  1138→symptoms and mitigate the now well-documented chronic effects of military mTBI. Readings from portable
  1139→
  1140→quantitative EEG are brief to collect and could monitor brain health near to the times of combat and be related to
  1141→FC measured later to identify predictive biomarkers. In longitudinal studies, both of these could be investigated in
  1142→relation to chronic symptoms. Additionally, most networks remain understudied, leaving important gaps in our
  1143→understanding of how chronicity, military occupational specialty, and injury mechanism translate to network-level
  1144→dysfunction. Several abbreviations will be used in this section to describe specific or general military populations:
  1145→ADSMs as defined above, service members and veterans (SMVs), and special operations forces (SOFs).
  1146→Default mode and parietal memory networks
  1147→The DMN is the most extensively studied resting-state network in military mTBI with varying findings. The
  1148→PMN, anchored in precuneus and PCC, is frequently captured alongside posterior DMN (pDMN) findings rather than
  1149→examined as a separate target.
  1150→Hypoconnectivity within the DMN and between the DMN and other networks is reported. ICA identifies
  1151→reduced DMN FC with DLPFC and basal ganglia networks 236 in veterans with mTBI due to blast only. A seed analysis
  1152→identifies reduced pDMN FC (PCC/retrosplenial and lateral parietal) to visual cortex in ADSMs with mTBI.237
  1153→DMN-symptom relationships are reported across multiple cohorts: In veterans, greater pain interference
  1154→correlates with reduced medial prefrontal-to-posterior DMN FC,238 and in ADSMs, higher neurobehavioral and PTSD
  1155→symptom scores correlate inversely with DMN FC.239 Using ROI to ROI matrix FC, a trend for a negative association
  1156→between DMN FC and re-experiencing symptoms is reported in service members two months post-deployment.240
  1157→Hyperconnectivity is reported in a dual-regression ICA in veterans with mTBI only and not in those with
  1158→comorbid PTSD,241 suggesting that comorbid psychiatric conditions may modify or obscure DMN-specific TBI
  1159→signal; however, an ACC seed analysis identifies hyperconnectivity with PCC and precuneus and found the same
  1160→results whether or not Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5242 scores were a covariate.243
  1161→Three studies identify opposite patterns for the DMN. In an ICA with ADSMs, hyperconnectivity of the DMN
  1162→is reported with numerous nonfrontal areas, e.g., mid and PCC and cerebellum, while hypoconnectivity is found
  1163→within only precentral gyrus, inferior temporal gyrus, and caudate.244 A follow-up study also using ICA examined
  1164→PTSD symptoms in ADSMs and reports that all levels of symptom severity are associated with increased FC in the
  1165→anterior cingulate, and reduced FC in the medial frontal gyrus.245 DMN spreading anticorrelation pattern in
  1166→close-range blast exposure is discussed in SMN.246,247
  1167→A seed-based study reports null DMN findings in veterans,248 which could be due to small samples. A better
  1168→powered study reports no group differences in anterior and pDMN components in a network based ROI to ROI
  1169→analysis when comparing groups with high vs. low blast exposure in ADSMs and veterans with repetitive
  1170→subconcussions, although it did find group differences when examining the pDMN with magnetoenchephalography
  1171→(MEG), which was suggested to be more sensitive to repetitive subconcussions than FC network analysis.249
  1172→Frontoparietal network
  1173→Hypoconnectivity is the most consistently reported finding for the FPN, with reduced FC observed within
  1174→the FPN and between FPN hubs and DAN, SMN, and parietal regions.248,250 Broader reductions are reported both
  1175→within the FPN251 and in its interactions with the DMN and attention networks,236 and reduced frontal-visual FC
  1176→predicts worse executive function performance.252 Some of these effects are moderated by comorbid PTSD and
  1177→depression,248 a consideration that is particularly relevant in military cohorts.
  1178→A subset of studies report FPN hyperconnectivity, often interpreted as compensatory. In veterans with
  1179→blast-related mTBI, widespread hyperconnectivity from frontal working memory seeds is associated with better
  1180→working memory performance in the mTBI group but not in controls, suggesting recruitment of additional neural
  1181→resources to maintain executive function.253 Similarly, increased long-range FC localized to the left superior frontal
  1182→gyrus (SFG) is reported in Special Operations Forces (SOF) personnel with chronic subconcussive blast
  1183→exposure.254 One study directly comparing mTBI and PTSD finds opposing FPN signatures — left dlPFC–right
  1184→
  1185→middle frontal gyrus FC was increased in mTBI, but reduced in the PTSD group,237 further highlighting the
  1186→importance of disentangling these comorbidities.
  1187→Higher cumulative blast exposure in SOFs who had an average of over 9.5 million blast exposures has been
  1188→linked to converging structural and functional FPN alterations centered on the rACC, i.e., decreased FC, although
  1189→the FC results did not survive correction for multiple comparisons. 255 Because the rACC is active in other networks
  1190→(DMN, SN), these results apply to them as well and point out the extensiveness of the impact of this injured area.
  1191→Mixed FC patterns involving FPN hubs are reported,256,257 as are null results for associations between FPN FC and
  1192→self-reported executive dysfunction258 and for low vs high blast-related FPN differences using fMRI and MEG.249
  1193→The hypo/hyper divergence in the FPN tracks circuit geometry rather than analytic approach: analyses
  1194→frequently report hypoconnectivity across long-range and inter-network pathways,250 whereas hyperconnectivity
  1195→is typically localized to short-range or anterior-dominant frontal pathways253,254 suggesting that broad network
  1196→integration is degraded even as specific frontal hubs upregulate, potentially as a compensatory response.
