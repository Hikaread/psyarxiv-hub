    13‚Üí    13‚ÜíAbstract
    14‚Üí    14‚ÜíA prior study during COVID-19 lockdown (Heck et al., 2024) examined whether a pre-
    15‚Üí    15‚Üítest alcohol craving measurement influenced posttest assessment and tested its relationship with
    16‚Üí    16‚Üísocial desirability (SD) and demand effects (the consequences of participants trying to uncover
    17‚Üí    17‚Üíthe study‚Äôs aim). Results suggested that the first craving measurement increased its post-
    18‚Üí    18‚Üíexperimental levels in heavy drinkers, with no associations found for SD or demand effects.
    19‚Üí    19‚ÜíEngagement with the alcohol-related video was linked to craving. This study aimed to replicate
    20‚Üí    20‚Üíthese findings three years later outside the lockdown context.
    21‚Üí    21‚ÜíA hundred and forty-two psychology students were randomly allocated to either a ‚Äúpre-
    22‚Üí    22‚Üípost‚Äù or a ‚Äúpost-only‚Äù craving assessment group. They watched an alcohol-related video. SD,
    23‚Üí    23‚Üídemand effects, engagement, and alcohol consumption levels were also assessed.
    24‚Üí    24‚ÜíSeveral findings supported the robustness of effects observed in the initial study, while
    25‚Üí    25‚Üíothers diverged, offering new hypotheses. Heavier drinkers reported significantly higher
    26‚Üí    26‚Üícraving levels following video exposure and craving correlated moderately with video
    27‚Üí    27‚Üíengagement. No significant associations were found between craving and SD or demand
    28‚Üí    28‚Üícharacteristics. However, unlike the initial study, repeated measurements showed no significant
    29‚Üí    29‚Üíeffects or interactions with alcohol consumption.
    30‚Üí    30‚ÜíReanalysis of the original data highlighted a gender-based interaction, where the effect of
    31‚Üí    31‚Üíthe interaction between repeated craving measurements and alcohol consumption was
    32‚Üí    32‚Üísignificant in men but not in women. These results suggest partial replication of prior findings
    33‚Üí    33‚Üíand underscore the need to further explore contextual and demographic influences on alcohol
    34‚Üí    34‚Üícraving. Moreover, this study confirmed a significant role of video engagement in craving.
    35‚Üí    35‚ÜíKeywords: Alcohol craving measurement, demand characteristics, engagement, social
    36‚Üí    36‚Üídesirability.

    38‚Üí    38‚Üí3
    39‚Üí    39‚Üí1. Introduction
    40‚Üí    40‚ÜíCraving is a key feature of drug addiction. It has been widely studied since its inclusion
    41‚Üí    41‚Üías a diagnostic criterion in the DSM-V [1‚Äì7]. However, despite decades of research, its
    42‚Üí    42‚Üíusefulness and precise role in addiction is still debated [6,8]. Yet, the concept of craving is part
    43‚Üí    43‚Üíof most theoretical models of addiction and has been consistently shown to be implicated in
    44‚Üí    44‚Üídrug use and relapse [9]. The American Psychiatric Association defined craving as ‚Äúan intense
    45‚Üí    45‚Üíurge or desire for a drug‚Äù [10], although recent evidence shows that it also relates to non-
    46‚Üí    46‚Üísubstance-related behavioral addictions [11]. Based on extensive research and current
    47‚Üí    47‚Üítheoretical knowledge, it is now widely accepted that craving is a complex multidimensional
    48‚Üí    48‚Üíphenomenon, including automatic, cognitive, and physiological (interoceptive) components
    49‚Üí    49‚Üí[12,13]. Regarding alcohol use disorder (AUD) more specifically, craving has for instance been
    50‚Üí    50‚Üíshown to be associated with relapse [14‚Äì17, although see 18], disorder severity [19], and
    51‚Üí    51‚Üíattentional biases [20,21]. Given its crucial role, it is essential to measure craving as accurately
    52‚Üí    52‚Üías possible. Various methods were developed to measure alcohol craving [22], but the most
    53‚Üí    53‚Üíwidely used are self-reported scales.
    54‚Üí    54‚ÜíAs with other measurements based on self-reports, there are many potential sources of
    55‚Üí    55‚Üíbias that could impact craving accuracy measurement. Over 20 year ago, Sayette [23] already
    56‚Üí    56‚Üípointed out that craving research should take into account the multiple factors that could
    57‚Üí    57‚Üípotentially affect the measurement of craving through self-reported questionnaires, citing for
    58‚Üí    58‚Üíinstance social desirability (SD), response styles, and memory biases. We have recently argued
    59‚Üí    59‚Üíthat using self-reported craving scales repeatedly in the same experiment (as is usually done
    60‚Üí    60‚Üíwhen craving changes are expected after an experimental manipulation), could impair its proper
    61‚Üí    61‚Üíevaluation at the end of the experiment [24]. Indeed, the initial completion of the craving scale
    62‚Üí    62‚Üíby the participants could directly act as an alcohol cue, evoking alcohol consumption and
    63‚Üí    63‚Üítherefore possibly promoting or altering the subjective experience of craving [25]. In other
    64‚Üí    64‚Üí
    65‚Üí    65‚Üí4
    66‚Üí    66‚Üíwords, the initial craving scale could have a priming effect on alcohol craving. Additionally,
    67‚Üí    67‚Üíself-reported craving scales are generally very explicit and therefore possibly vulnerable to
    68‚Üí    68‚Üívarious experimental biases, such as social desirability and demand effects. Social desirability,
    69‚Üí    69‚Üíor the tendency to respond in a manner that makes one ‚Äúlook good‚Äù [26] might significantly
    70‚Üí    70‚Üíaffect the measurement of craving, although this has been little investigated. Demand effects
    71‚Üí    71‚Üíon the other hand, are related to the fact that participants are not passive responders to the
    72‚Üí    72‚Üíexperimental manipulations. They typically seek to offer a valuable contribution [27] and
    73‚Üí    73‚Üítherefore to behave according to their understanding of the aim of the study. As a consequence,
    74‚Üí    74‚Üíany explicit or implicit information about the purpose of the study is likely to affect their
    75‚Üí    75‚Üíresponses and behavior. All the explicit and implicit clues about the purpose of the study are
    76‚Üí    76‚Üícalled ‚Äúdemand characteristics‚Äù, because participants use these clues to identify what the
    77‚Üí    77‚Üíexperimenter is ‚Äúdemanding‚Äù of them. More recently, demand effects were conceptualized in
    78‚Üí    78‚Üíthree sub-dimensions [28]: cue interpretation (i.e., "the analysis and interpretation of these cues
    79‚Üí    79‚Üíby the participants to produce a set of conjectures about the purpose of the research"),
    80‚Üí    80‚Üísensitivity to demand characteristics (i.e., an expression of interest on behalf of the respondent,
    81‚Üí    81‚Üíseeking to make sense of the questioning situation with which they are confronted"), and
    82‚Üí    82‚Üíresponse motivation (i.e., "the willingness of the individuals being questioned to rely on their
    83‚Üí    83‚Üíinterpretation of the demand characteristics in order to engage in specific response behaviors).
    84‚Üí    84‚ÜíBased on these premises, the explicit nature of self-reported craving scales, especially when
    85‚Üí    85‚Üíadministered twice in the same experiment, is likely to induce specific demand effects in the
    86‚Üí    86‚Üíparticipants.
    87‚Üí    87‚ÜíIn a recent experiment, we investigated the impact of a pre-test measurement of alcohol
    88‚Üí    88‚Üícraving on its final assessment, in relation to social desirability and demand effects [24].
    89‚Üí    89‚ÜíParticipants were randomly allocated to two experimental groups: a ‚Äúpre-post‚Äù group in which
    90‚Üí    90‚Üícraving was assessed twice, before and after watching an alcohol-related video; or a ‚Äúpost-only‚Äù
    91‚Üí    91‚Üí
    92‚Üí    92‚Üí5
    93‚Üí    93‚Üígroup in which craving was assessed only after the video. Social desirability, demand effects,
    94‚Üí    94‚Üíengagement toward the video, and alcohol consumption levels were also assessed using
    95‚Üí    95‚Üívalidated questionnaires. The results showed that the levels of craving after watching an alcohol
    96‚Üí    96‚Üívideo were higher in heavier drinkers relative to low drinkers. Additionally, the pre-test
    97‚Üí    97‚Üíassessment of craving had a significant impact on post-test craving, but only in heavier alcohol
    98‚Üí    98‚Üídrinkers. More precisely, in high alcohol drinkers, alcohol craving was significantly higher after
    99‚Üí    99‚Üíthe video when craving was also assessed before the video. No such effect was observed in low
   100‚Üí   100‚Üíalcohol drinkers. In contrast, no relationship between self-reported craving and SD or demand
   101‚Üí   101‚Üíeffects was found in that study. Finally, exploratory analyses indicated that engagement toward
   102‚Üí   102‚Üíthe alcohol-related video was a significant mediator of subsequent craving increases.
   103‚Üí   103‚ÜíSince this initial study took place during the COVID-19 lockdown period and considering
   104‚Üí   104‚Üíits modest sample size, we aimed to assess the robustness of these findings in a non-lockdown
   105‚Üí   105‚Üícontext, using a larger sample size. We took advantage of the participant pool at the University
   106‚Üí   106‚Üíof Li√®ge to replicate the study on a sample of first-year psychology students.
   107‚Üí   107‚Üí2. Methods
   108‚Üí   108‚ÜíThe present study used the exact same design as the initial study (see [24]). The only
   109‚Üí   109‚Üímodification was the replacement of the DS36 social desirability scale [29] by the Social
   110‚Üí   110‚ÜíDesirability Scale-17 (SDS-17)[30], measuring social desirability with a one-factor structure
   111‚Üí   111‚Üíinstead of the two-factor structure (DS36). This modification was chosen to shorten the study
   112‚Üí   112‚Üíduration and because our primary interest was in 'impression management'‚Äîthe act of
   113‚Üí   113‚Üíconsciously presenting oneself in a favorable light to others‚Äîrather than 'self-deceptive
   114‚Üí   114‚Üíenhancement,' which involves unconsciously seeing oneself in a highly positive manner.
   115‚Üí   115‚ÜíParticipants were a hundred-and-forty-two 1st-year psychology students (92% female,
   116‚Üí   116‚Üímean age= 19.33, sd= 1.81), recruited through a ‚Äúparticipant pool‚Äù device of the Faculty of
   117‚Üí   117‚Üí
   118‚Üí   118‚Üí6
   119‚Üí   119‚ÜíPsychology at the University of Li√®ge. This high percentage of female participants reflects the
   120‚Üí   120‚Üígender distribution within the faculty. Students received course credit for their participation and
   121‚Üí   121‚Üítook part in an interactive educational session about the study. Exclusion criteria were strictly
   122‚Üí   122‚Üíthe same as in the initial study. Participants were randomly assigned to one of the two
   123‚Üí   123‚Üíexperimental groups: pre-post group (N= 72) vs. post-only group (N= 70). This study was
   124‚Üí   124‚Üíapproved by the Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Psychology, Speech and Language Therapy
   125‚Üí   125‚Üíand Education of the University (on October the 3rd 2023, reference number: 10968) and was
   126‚Üí   126‚Üíconducted in accordance with the ethical standards described in the Declaration of Helsinki
   127‚Üí   127‚Üí(1964) and institutional guidelines. The privacy rights of participants have been observed and
   128‚Üí   128‚Üíinformed consent was obtained.
   129‚Üí   129‚ÜíFollowing the initial study, we aimed for a sample size of at least 77 participants.
   130‚Üí   130‚ÜíHowever, given the participant pool setup, we anticipated obtaining a higher number of
   131‚Üí   131‚Üísubjects.
   132‚Üí   132‚ÜíStatistical analyses were run using R [31]. To examine the effect of repeated measures of
   133‚Üí   133‚Üícraving, alcohol consumption, and their interaction on final craving levels, a multiple linear
   134‚Üí   134‚Üíregression analysis was conducted. However, the normality of residuals was not achieved for
   135‚Üí   135‚Üíthis model. Consequently, craving data were square root transformed, which improved the
   136‚Üí   136‚Üídistribution significantly, but residual normality was still not met due to a floor effect (Shapiro-
   137‚Üí   137‚ÜíWilk p-value ranging from <.0001 to .007). Despite this, as homoscedasticity was satisfied
   138‚Üí   138‚Üí(Breusch-Pagan test: p=0.49), the same linear regression model as in the previous study was
   139‚Üí   139‚Üíapplied. To determine if there was a relationship between craving and engagement with the
   140‚Üí   140‚Üíalcohol-related video, similar to the previous study, a Pearson correlation coefficient was
   141‚Üí   141‚Üícomputed. Finally, Kendall‚Äôs Tau correlation coefficients were used to investigate social
   142‚Üí   142‚Üídesirability and demand characteristics.

   218‚Üí   218‚Üí4. Discussion
   219‚Üí   219‚ÜíThe primary objective of this study was to determine whether our initial findings could be
   220‚Üí   220‚Üíself-replicated outside the context of the covid-19 lockdown. The initial study examined
   221‚Üí   221‚Üíwhether repeated measurements of craving influenced its assessment after watching an alcohol-
   222‚Üí   222‚Üírelated video, considering social desirability and demand effects. The findings of the initial
   223‚Üí   223‚Üístudy revealed significant effects of the repeated measurement of craving, alcohol consumption
   224‚Üí   224‚Üílevel, and their interaction, suggesting that pre-experimental craving measurements elevated
   225‚Üí   225‚Üípost-experimental levels, but only in heavy drinkers. No evidence was found linking these
   226‚Üí   226‚Üí
   227‚Üí   227‚Üí11
   228‚Üí   228‚Üíchanges to social desirability or demand effects. Further analyses also indicated that the increase
   229‚Üí   229‚Üíin craving in heavy drinkers was mediated by a stronger engagement with the alcohol-related
   230‚Üí   230‚Üívideo.
   231‚Üí   231‚ÜíThe present study replicated most results of the initial study, strengthening the robustness
   232‚Üí   232‚Üíof the initial findings. In both studies, level of alcohol consumption significantly influenced
   233‚Üí   233‚Üícue-induced craving, and, when repeated assessments were conducted, craving levels increased
   234‚Üí   234‚Üífollowing cue exposure compared to pre-exposure levels, indicating successful craving
   235‚Üí   235‚Üíinduction. Similar effects were documented in the literature over recent decades [25,32‚Äì35].
   236‚Üí   236‚ÜíThese results demonstrate that the video cue exposure used in both studies successfully induced
   237‚Üí   237‚Üísubjective craving and effectively differentiated between light and heavy drinkers.
   238‚Üí   238‚ÜíNotably, the impact of engagement was also replicated, with a correlation of similar
   239‚Üí   239‚Üímagnitude in the two studies (initial study: r = .44; present study: r = .42). This finding provides
   240‚Üí   240‚Üífurther support for our hypothesis that the level of engagement with alcohol-related stimuli
   241‚Üí   241‚Üí(whether in a video format or other media) is a critical factor in craving induction.
   242‚Üí   242‚ÜíIn both studies, there was no indication of a statistically significant effect of social
   243‚Üí   243‚Üídesirability, or demand characteristics on cue-induced craving. This indicates that self-reported
   244‚Üí   244‚Üíalcohol craving was not significantly affected by participants' tendencies to present themselves
   245‚Üí   245‚Üíin a socially desirable manner or to conform to perceived experimental expectations. The
   246‚Üí   246‚Üíabsence of such biases enhances the validity of the experimental findings on craving, suggesting
   247‚Üí   247‚Üíthat the responses were mainly driven by experimental manipulations, such as alcohol cue
   248‚Üí   248‚Üíexposure. However, future research could further validate this conclusion by using implicit
   249‚Üí   249‚Üímeasures of craving or incorporating additional checks to detect potential subtle influences of
   250‚Üí   250‚Üísocial desirability and demand characteristics.
   251‚Üí   251‚Üí
   252‚Üí   252‚Üí12
   253‚Üí   253‚ÜíSince the effects of repeated measurements of craving were not replicated in the present
   254‚Üí   254‚Üístudy, we questioned what differences between the two studies might explain these
   255‚Üí   255‚Üídiscrepancies. A major difference was the gender ratio. Participants in the second study were
   256‚Üí   256‚Üírecruited from a "participant pool" in the Faculty of Psychology, resulting in a very high
   257‚Üí   257‚Üíproportion of female participants, reflecting the faculty's gender distribution. Consequently, we
   258‚Üí   258‚Üíreanalyzed the data from the initial study to explore potential gender effects. This analysis
   259‚Üí   259‚Üírevealed a significant gender effect (Figure 3) with a large interaction effect between alcohol
   260‚Üí   260‚Üíconsumption and repeated measurements in men, whereas this effect was absent in women.
   261‚Üí   261‚ÜíTherefore, the two studies yielded similar effects when gender is included in the analyses. We
   262‚Üí   262‚Üíalso checked whether differences in AUDIT-C scores or levels of engagement could act as
   263‚Üí   263‚Üíconfounding variables explaining the observed gender difference; however, this was not the
   264‚Üí   264‚Üícase in our data (AUDIT-C: W= 745, p=0.35, r= 0.11 CI95% -0.12 - 0.21; Engagement:
   265‚Üí   265‚ÜíW=680.5, p=0.83, r=0.026, CI 95%, -0.22 ‚Äì 0.05). However, these analyses were not part of
   266‚Üí   266‚Üíthe initial study design, and the sample sizes were relatively small (20 women and 15 men in
   267‚Üí   267‚Üíthe pre-post group, and 24 women and 15 men in the post-only group). Therefore, further
   268‚Üí   268‚Üíresearch is needed to confirm these conclusions.
   269‚Üí   269‚ÜíThis observed gender difference might be related to gender stereotypes. For women, the
   270‚Üí   270‚Üíreported levels of post-video craving were consistent with their usual alcohol consumption:
   271‚Üí   271‚Üílight drinkers reported low craving, while heavier drinkers reported higher levels of craving,
   272‚Üí   272‚Üíregardless of the repeated measurements. In contrast, a different pattern emerged for men. When
   273‚Üí   273‚Üícraving was assessed only after the video, the reported levels were unrelated to their drinking
   274‚Üí   274‚Üíhabits. Their responses seemed to be directly influenced by the cue exposure itself, potentially
   275‚Üí   275‚Üíactivating social stereotypes that portray heavy drinking as socially positive for men. However,
   276‚Üí   276‚Üíthe pre-video measurement of craving appeared to anchor their reported levels to their drinking
   277‚Üí   277‚Üí
   278‚Üí   278‚Üí13
   279‚Üí   279‚Üíhabits, subsequently influencing their post-video responses. This speculative explanation
   280‚Üí   280‚Üíclearly requires further confirmation in future studies.
   281‚Üí   281‚ÜíIn conclusion, the present results suggest that an initial pre-exposure craving measurement
   282‚Üí   282‚Üícould significantly impact the final craving assessment in men, but not in women. Moreover,
   283‚Üí   283‚Üíthe way individuals engage with alcohol-related videos is significantly related to increased
   284‚Üí   284‚Üícraving for alcohol afterwards. These findings also confirm the absence of social desirability
   285‚Üí   285‚Üíand demand effects observed in our previous study. Further investigations are needed to
   286‚Üí   286‚Üídetermine whether these effects of an initial craving measure are beneficial or represent a
   287‚Üí   287‚Üísignificant bias in craving studies, and why they may not apply to women.
