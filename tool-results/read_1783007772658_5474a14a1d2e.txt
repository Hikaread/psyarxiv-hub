    27→Abstract
    28→Objectives: To develop and implement a co-creation process for common language and multi-
    29→stakeholder engagement to produce a shared causal framework that helps identify meaningful
    30→research questions in the context of psychosocial processes in pain and guide data modelling
    31→decisions.
    32→Design: Participatory co-design study using facilitated workshops and iterative synthesis to
    33→develop a ‘Dream’ Directed Acyclic Graph (Dream-DAG). The Dream-DAG represented an
    34→idealised visual causal map unconstrained by available study measures or specific dataset
    35→limitations. We used a storm metaphor where different components of the storm (i.e., thunder
    36→or lightning) mirror the temporal and causal inference features in DAGs (i.e., exposure or
    37→confounding).
    38→Setting: A UK-based multidisciplinary pain research consortium involving researchers,
    39→clinicians and public contributors.
    40→Participants: 12 participants took part, including 4 experts by experience living with chronic
    41→pain and 8 professionals including a wide range of researchers (from quantitative and
    42→qualitative fields), clinicians and data scientists. Participants were invited because of their
    43→lived, clinical, methodological or data-related expertise relevant to psychosocial processes in
    44→pain.
    45→Results: Through the workshops we developed a shared language for causal thinking, a co-
    46→created Dream-DAG, and an operationalised set of hypothesised pathways to inform future
    47→data modelling within pain research. The variables and pathways of interest identified as part of
    48→this process cover a wide range of concepts considered relevant to the development and
    49→maintenance of pain. This included individual factors such as anxiety and loss of identity,
    50→interpersonal factors such as disruption to relationships, and the broader role of social worlds.
    51→Importantly, the DAG mapped the order of influences and highlighted disruption to relationships
    52→as a potential mediating indirect pathway between pain-related experiences and ongoing pain
    53→impact. The process also supported translation of experiential, clinical and methodological
    54→knowledge into testable research questions for use within existing life-course datasets.
    55→Conclusion: Collaboration using metaphor-based causal diagram development can support
    56→multidisciplinary pain research teams to integrate expertise from multiple viewpoints which is
    57→vital to ensure that research questions are meaningful to those with chronic pain. The resulting
    58→Dream-DAG provides a transparent, theory-informed and experience-informed framework for
    59→future empirical analyses, with operationalised and testable hypothesised pathways, although
    60→further work is needed to evaluate its transferability to other research teams, datasets and
    61→health contexts.
    62→Strengths and limitations of this study
    63→• The storm metaphor provided a shared, accessible language for causal diagram
    64→development, supporting equitable engagement between researchers and experts by
    65→experience.
    66→
    67→• The co-design process enabled people with lived experience of chronic pain to
    68→contribute to key decisions about concepts, temporal ordering and hypothesised causal
    69→pathways, shaping future research.
    70→• Developing a Dream-DAG before considering data constraints helped distinguish
    71→meaningful research questions from what could be measured in available data.
    72→• The approach produced a common framework that could guide analyses across
    73→different research teams and datasets within a multidisciplinary consortium.
    74→• The process involved a small number of contributors, and some causal features,
    75→including complex pathways, unavailable measures and effect modification, were
    76→difficult to represent fully within the DAG and storm metaphor.

    78→1. Introduction
    79→Context: Higher education institutions and external public and charity research funders are
    80→increasingly developing infrastructure and schemes to establish large multidisciplinary
    81→research hubs/consortia to tackle large health challenges, such as mental health and pain.
    82→Such consortia have the aim of maximising research potential through making more coherent
    83→and efficient use of existing resources. However, there are challenges in meaningful integration
    84→within teams with different expertise, including those with expertise by experience, those with
    85→data-driven or conceptually/clinically driven backgrounds. Members of such teams often have
    86→different perspectives, priorities and pressures as well as different languages but need to make
    87→collective decisions.
    88→The Consortium to Research Individual, Interpersonal and Social Influences in Pain (CRIISP) is
    89→one of four pain consortia within the Advanced Pain Discovery Platform, which has been funded
    90→through a joint initiative between UKRI (MRC, ESRC, BBSRC) and Arthritis UK. Here, we describe
    91→a process through a real-life worked example of one such challenge within CRIISP, where we
    92→carried out a consultation/engagement activity to guide our multidisciplinary team in developing
    93→a coherent analysis plan using different large datasets within one of its key work packages.
    94→Importantly, our aims were to ensure that data decisions were guided by meaningful research
    95→questions and informed by multiple perspectives, with each form of expertise valued equitably.
    96→The data problem in CRIISP: In research there are lots of different approaches to analyse and
    97→compile the ‘data’ that provides researchers and policy makers the ‘statistical findings’ that are
    98→ultimately used to inform care, policy and form current ‘evidence’. Changes in the variables
    99→selected for analysis and the order they appear in statistical models can drastically change the
   100→resulting inference and subsequent decision making. It is important therefore that data-analytic
   101→choices are directed by what is most relevant and meaningful to experiences and that the
   102→assumptions that are made in data models are understood and interrogated. The same
   103→variables modelled in different ways can give very different answers. Thus, such decisions
   104→should be co-produced by those with different forms of knowledge and interest. To do this,
   105→causal analytical models need to be transparent and accessible to those who hold vital
   106→experiential knowledge but may not be trained in epidemiology.
   107→Here, in the context of a large research consortium our objective was to investigate
   108→psychosocial processes involved in pain across several rich longitudinal cohort datasets. To
   109→meet this objective we present how researchers, clinicians and experts by experience can co-
   110→create a common data language and make joint decisions on data choice and analysis plans.
   111→Defining Data: Put simply, ‘data’ consists of numbers or categories that represent aspects of
   112→individuals’ experiences, attributes and characteristics across several ‘variables’. A variable is
   113→something that can vary from person to person. For example, age is a variable: each person in a
   114→dataset has a value for age. How age is recorded can vary between studies; it may be measured
   115→in days, months or years, or grouped into categories such as young, mid-life or older adult. Most
   116→analyses of data, in their simplest form, look at how two or more variables are connected. For
   117→example, we might ask whether people with higher depression scores also tend to report higher
   118→pain scores.
   119→
   120→However, the tested link between the two scores could be used to infer two different research
   121→questions: whether depression leads to later pain, or whether pain leads to later depression. In
   122→a statistical model, we may specify depression as the exposure (i.e. the risk factor, the cause,
   123→the X or in the equation) and pain as the outcome or dependent variable (i.e. Y in the equation).
   124→Alternatively, we could reverse this order and put the pain score first in our model. Although
   125→these two models imply different causal questions, it is impossible for the model alone to
   126→establish the direction of causation. This is why it is important to define the research question
   127→and hypothesised causal direction before analysing the data.
   128→Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) are a graphical tool used in epidemiology and mathematics to
   129→aid causal inference (1,2). They are being utilized across a range of health conditions (3),
   130→including more recently pain (4). DAGs are used to visualise the assumptions that we make
   131→about the causal relationship of interest and describe other variables we think might be related
   132→or influence that relationship. DAGs use rules to guide the researcher in identifying whether the
   133→causal connection of interest can be identified without bias and, if so, what must be done either
   134→in study design or data analysis to achieve this. Importantly, given the visual and theoretical
   135→nature of DAGs, they also open the door to two further opportunities: 1. Co-working because
   136→the construction of the DAG is task-oriented and interactive and 2. Translation and accessibility
   137→of complex epidemiological concepts in a visual way to allow true co-design in a common
   138→visual framework.
   139→In this study we focus on a ‘Dream-DAG’ to map hypothesised psychosocial processes involved
   140→in pain over time, of which there are many possibilities with complex inter-relationships.
   141→Drawing on De Paepe et al. (4), the ‘dream’ concept refers to developing a causal diagram under
   142→‘ideal’ circumstances before considering the practical constraints of data availability,
   143→measurement capacity, time, resources, statistical power (i.e., having a large enough sample
   144→size) or contextual variation. Instead, the Dream-DAG does not necessarily need to directly
   145→reflect reality and starts with a blank canvas to allow consideration of the causal question of
   146→interest. This is particularly relevant for psychosocial pain research, where many constructs,
   147→such as validation of pain symptoms are abstract and difficult to measure.
   148→In this article we describe the process towards a co-designed Dream-DAG within a large pain
   149→research consortium aiming to understand psychosocial pathways in pain. The process brought
   150→together people living with personal experience of living with chronic pain, through the project’s
   151→public involvement group described in detail elsewhere (5), alongside clinical, data and
   152→conceptual expertise. While previous work has incorporated varied stakeholders into DAG
   153→development in similar contexts (4), we adapted this process further by using a storm metaphor
   154→as a tool to translate the concepts and language of ‘data’ and causal inference, and break
   155→barriers between lived experience and research evidence generation.
   156→Specific aim and objectives
   157→Our aim was to develop a DAG to inform meaningful research questions linked to psychosocial
   158→mechanisms in pain.
   159→We had two key objectives to meet this aim:
   160→
   161→1. To develop a common language of ‘data’ and causal thinking that could reduce barriers
   162→between the experiences of those living with chronic pain and the translation of that
   163→experience into DAGs to design analysis
   164→2. To create a structured process to navigate and organise contributions within DAGs from
   165→a wide, mixed audience with equity (i.e. equal chance for decision making), where
   166→contributions from all are equally weighted in decision making and actions (i.e., what
   167→variables are included and in what order they appear).
   168→
   169→2. Approach
   170→We take a Consultation and Engagement Framework where public contributors (PCs) were
   171→active decision makers in the design and approach to our data modelling approach, rather than
   172→contributing as research participants. CRIISP has an existing public involvement framework,
   173→reported in detail by Grieve and colleagues (5). Public contributors were defined as individuals
   174→who have lived experience of chronic pain, either through their own pain experiences or through
   175→supporting someone else with chronic pain, such as in a caring role. For the engagement
   176→activities reported here we worked with the public contributors who were members of the big
   177→data work package development group (5).
   178→2. 1 Developing a common language for DAGs
   179→DAGs can identify variables that, if controlled for in the design or analysis phase, are sufficient
   180→to minimise confounding (non-causal paths) and some forms of selection bias (i.e. who is more
   181→likely to be in your dataset). DAGs have considerably improved our understanding of
   182→assumptions and biases in observational epidemiology (6). Online visualisation tools such as
   183→DAGitty (https://www.dagitty.net/) have also widened the use of DAGs by providing accessible
   184→ways to draw and analyse causal diagrams, identify implications for statistical adjustment, and
   185→translate causal assumptions into data analysis plans, see Textor et al. (7).
   186→Two key features of DAGs needed to be explained in accessible terms to support a shared
   187→language: they are ‘directed’ and ‘acyclic’.
   188→Directed means that the links (edges) between variables (nodes) have a direction. In other
   189→words, arrows point from one variable to another to represent a hypothesised causal direction.
   190→This can be challenging in psychosocial pain research because many concepts behind the data
   191→points, such as mood, pain and social relationships, have flexible temporality meaning they
   192→fluctuate over time and may influence each other in different ways at different points.
   193→Acyclic means that the diagram does not contain ‘loops’. An arrow cannot lead from a later
   194→consequence or descendant (outcome) back to an earlier ancestor (cause) within the same
   195→causal structure. This is important because this allows the relationships in the DAG to be
   196→assessed using statistical models underpinned by mathematical theory. However, the direction
   197→of the arrow is not determined by the data or the analysis. Instead, the directions are justified
   198→using knowledge around timing, theory or plausible mechanisms. For some variables this
   199→ordering may be straightforward. For example, genotype is determined at conception even if
   200→DNA is only measured much later in life. For psychosocial processes in pain, however, these
   201→decisions are often more complex because experiences are dynamic and reciprocal over time.
   202→2.2 Translating DAG features to a common language via a storm metaphor.
   203→These terms are central to how DAGs work, but can be difficult to engage with without
   204→translation into more familiar language. To support this translation, we used a storm metaphor
   205→to explain these two core features of DAGs: taking a direction and not going in a loop. We chose
   206→a metaphor that did not rely solely on technical or verbal explanations, but instead used familiar
   207→
   208→visual and sensory concepts that would be universally relatable and easy-to-understand for all
   209→ages, cultures, and genders.
   210→We also selected the storm metaphor because storms contain multiple components that can
   211→be used to represent features of DAGs.
   212→First, storm components have flexible temporality: for example, rain may occur before or after
   213→sunshine. This mirrors the challenge of deciding how variables should be ordered when there is
   214→no single obvious temporal sequence, and provides a tool for discussing and debating this
   215→order.
   216→Second, storms are dynamic: their components can change in intensity, such as rain being light
   217→or heavy. This allowed discussion of modifiable and changeable aspects of pain impact, such
   218→as family relationships or symptoms of anxiety, which may also vary in intensity over time.
   219→Third, storm components often carry a broadly understood valence: for example, rainbows may
   220→be viewed positively, rain negatively, and lightning as sudden or threatening. This provided a
   221→language for discussing prioritisation and direction of influence, including whether a concept
   222→may worsen or buffer another process (so both the rain and the cloud might link in the same
   223→direction to the thunder, but the rain makes it worse, and the rainbow makes it better).
   224→We proposed that this metaphor would help researchers, clinicians and experts by experience
   225→think together and co-construct statistical models. In doing so, it created conditions for new
   226→and creative discussion about how to prioritise and order variables in the way that DAG
   227→development requires. The building blocks of a DAG, and how each was translated into a
   228→building block of a storm, are shown in Box 1.
   229→
   230→BOX 1: Mirroring of Epidemiological Concepts with Storm Metaphor
   231→OUTCOMES:
   232→Epidemiology - This is the presenting problem, the end point, symptoms or health condition.
   233→Storm - The storm itself. How long it lasts/how it feels, the impact it has, which component (rain, thunder or
   234→a combination) is experienced as the problem.
   235→EXPOSURES:
   236→Epidemiology - These are possible causes, things that may have led to the problem, triggers/origins,
   237→underlying issues.
   238→Storm - The hot weather that set off the storm perhaps.
   239→MEDIATORS:
   240→Epidemiology - These are things that happen along the way, knock on effects that make things worse,
   241→‘triggers’ or ‘glimmers’ that make things better
   242→Storm - The rainbow or sun breaking through the rain cloud, if the psychological impact of the storm were our
   243→outcome.
   244→MODERATORS:
   245→Epidemiology - These are things that happen along the way, but not in a chain of events, they are outside
   246→influences.
   247→Storm - Elements of the environment that amplify noise or provide shelter.
   248→CONFOUNDERS:
   249→Epidemiology - Common causes of the exposure and outcome, but which do not directly link the pathway
   250→between the cause and the problem.
   251→Storm - the thunder in the rainstorm is also occurring but the thunder is not the part of the storm making you
   252→cold and wet. These factors are important because if you focus on them, it will not help (e.g., if you do not
   253→want to get wet, you need to avoid the rain not the thunder).

   284→2.4 Overall procedure of co-development of the initial DAG live workshops.
   285→At the start of each workshop, attendees were introduced to DAGs and to the concepts of
   286→outcomes, exposures, mediators and confounders using the storm metaphor, presented
   287→through a PowerPoint slide deck (see supplementary file). Each DAG component was then
   288→considered in turn. For each component, RP presented the relevant definition and metaphor,
   289→which remained visible on screen while attendees were given 5 minutes for individual reflection.
   290→During this time, attendees were asked to consider the definition and generate up to five
   291→possible ideas for variables or constructs that might fit. Attendees could record their ideas on
   292→physical or virtual post-it notes, share verbally, or enter into the online chat function.
   293→The session was divided equally across each component, with approximately 10–15 minutes
   294→spent on each. After individual reflection, attendees each outlined their ideas, listened to
   295→others’ suggestions and discussed as a group. For outcomes and exposures, attendees were
   296→then asked to prioritise just one variable or construct. For mediating, moderating and
   297→confounding variables, as many constructs as mentioned could be included.
   298→Discussion followed the structure of the DAG, beginning with the outcome, then identifying
   299→exposures, mediators and confounders. Particular attention was given to temporal ordering,
   300→with participants encouraged to consider when each variable would occur relative to the others,
   301→for example what happens first, what follows, and what may occur simultaneously. This helped
   302→distinguish between the components and ensure that proposed relationships were temporally
   303→plausible.
   304→Agreement was reached through an iterative process. Concepts suggested by multiple
   305→attendees were prioritised and, where possible, the most frequently mentioned option was
   306→taken forward. Where suggestions differed, attendees discussed whether they represented
   307→distinct concepts or different ways of describing a shared underlying idea. For outcomes and
   308→exposures, where only one variable was selected, the facilitator supported further discussion
   309→until a pragmatic consensus was reached. Then attendees considered when each concept
   310→occurred, how it related to other variables, and whether it should be represented as an
   311→exposure, mediator, moderator or confounder. Where disagreements occurred, the group
   312→discussed both the concept itself and its timing, considering alternative ways to represent or
   313→integrate it within the sequence of events. This process produced a shared set of variables and
   314→relationships that formed the building blocks for a coherent research question and subsequent
   315→DAG.
   316→The agreed variables and relationships were then entered into DAGitty, moving the discussion
   317→from generating and prioritising ideas to specification of the causal structure. The visual
   318→placement of variables was was used to reflect the agreed conceptual and temporal order,
   319→while arrows represented the hypothesised direction of influence. This step required attendees
   320→to define and decide each variable’s role from the perspective of the chosen exposure-outcome
   321→pathway, because the same concept may function differently across research questions. For
   322→example, a variable that acts as a mediator in one pathway may be an exposure or confounder
   323→in another.

   366→3.2 Description of the Experts by academic and/or clinical experience DAG
   367→The same set of steps was followed with 8 researchers, using the same definitions and building
   368→the DAG in DAGitty on a shared screen facilitated by RP. Attendees comprised of researchers
   369→associated with the CRIISP large cohort work package. This group included academics with
   370→expertise in pain psychology (e.g., EK, CRIISP PI; TB, CI), those with academic and clinical roles
   371→(e.g., AW, Consultant Clinical Psychologist; TP, anaesthetist and pain clinician), pain
   372→researchers employed on the project (e.g., AL, DV), and data scientists (LO).
   373→Step 1: During discussions around the developed DAG the experts by academic or clinical
   374→training initially listed possible pain outcomes from pain intensity, duration to pain impact.
   375→These were collated and the group were asked to rank in importance. There was universal
   376→
   377→agreement that pain impact should be prioritised as the outcome of most importance. However,
   378→different attendees emphasises different aspects of pain impact, including effects on work,
   379→self-care or social life. Rather than selecting one aspect, the group agreed that the DAG should
   380→represent pain impact as a multidimensional outcome comprising these three domains.
   381→Step 2: The broader work on CRIISP had already conducted a series of systematic reviews of the
   382→literature (8,9) and therefore lists of key variables from the literature were brought to the
   383→session.
   384→Steps 3-4: Although similar pathways may exist across domains (relationships may be
   385→impacted by all domains) the magnitude of this impact was potentially different across domains
   386→(i.e. stronger for pain that impacts social life). In addition, the role of relationships was initially a
   387→source of debate. Some attendees viewed relationships as a protective or contextual factor that
   388→could moderate the impact of pain, while others viewed relationships as mediating part of the
   389→causal chain linking pain to later outcomes. A proposed solution was to include change in
   390→relationships as the mediator, and prior social context as background. This is represented in the
   391→final agreed upon DAG (Figure 6).

   399→3.3 Consolidation/integration process
   400→Following the two workshops, we engaged in a process of consolidation with the aim of
   401→integrating and sense checking areas of similarities and differences between the DAGs from
   402→workshops 1 and 2, separating language differences from conceptual disagreements (see
   403→Figure 7).
   404→Figure 7. Comparison of DAGs as presented to each group with a key focus on mutually
   405→identified processes, highlighted in the purple circle.
   406→To achieve integration, the two DAGs were presented to each group in separate 1-hour online
   407→meetings: public contributors in the WDG were shown the researchers’ DAG and vice versa. The
   408→groups discussed commonalities and common languages. The WDG reflected and expanded
   409→their DAG, suggesting how some factors intertwined to have better or worse effects on pain. Key
   410→areas of overlap between the DAGs were identified. For example, for the WDG DAG outcome
   411→was ‘pain stopping you participating in life’, whereas for researchers ‘pain impact’ was
   412→identified, which was agreed to reflect the same construct. Similarly, the exposure of initial
   413→presentation of pain with impact in the WDG DAG can be represented by including pain with
   414→impact at time 1 and following up pain impact at time 2. The three impact domains identified by
   415→researchers were also agreed as relevant and best modelled in separate pathways (note: due to
   416→all domains including the same pathways for simplicity the integrated DAG is presented here),
   417→and the importance of pain uncertainty highlighted in the WDG DAG, which was not directly
   418→reflected in the researcher DAG, was incorporated within the pain cognitions and pain anxiety
   419→components.
   420→Feedback from discussions were integrated by RP, identifying possible common phrases. The
   421→WDG also were given offline ‘homework’ tasks to consider terms. Here, it was also explained
   422→that some of the bidirectional arrows initially included in the WDG DAG were not possible to
   423→model but were represented as change in variables e.g., multiple bidirectional arrows linked to
   424→confidence were represented as change in confidence/adaptation in the integrated DAG.
   425→3.4 The final integrated DAG
   426→
   427→The final integrated DAG was generated in DAGitty and presented in Figure 8. In this final model,
   428→confounding variables are selected as ‘adjusted’ for and represented in white. In our ‘Dream’
   429→model scenario we can measure all confounding variables and thus estimate the exposure-
   430→outcome relationship and mediating pathways accounting for all considered confounding. In
   431→reality, this may not be possible, and reselecting confounding variable as unmeasured
   432→highlights the paths that are impacted. Figure 9 presents an example of this, where culture is
   433→indicated as unobserved.
   434→The final DAG (Figure 8) was shared with the wider CRIISP team for feedback. Reflection
   435→opportunities were also given to both DAG groups to understand if the workshop process had
   436→achieved equity in decisions making. For example, the WDG group reflected on how the DAG
   437→process empowered them to see ‘where’ their experiences contributed, which also motivated
   438→them to engage further in the research.

   443→4. Discussion
   444→In summary, in using the storm metaphor and iterative visual mapping process we have
   445→demonstrated a structured approach for developing a common language around data and
   446→causal thinking, and for translating this into meaningful and testable DAGs. The process also
   447→supported equitable contribution from a wide and mixed group of attendees by creating
   448→opportunities for all participants to suggest, prioritise and discuss variables, as well as their
   449→placement within the DAG. In this way, decisions about what was included, how concepts were
   450→ordered, and how relationships were represented were shaped collectively, where public
   451→contribution and varied research / clinical opinion could be equally weighted. The following
   452→section outlines the key learning and reflections from the DAG generation exercise.
   453→4. 1 Action 1- Reconceptualising the ‘outcome’ as an ‘exposure-outcome’ relationship
   454→When discussing the ‘problem’ and separating it out from the rest of the mechanisms it became
   455→clear in both groups that the part people wanted to change most was not pain, but the impact
   456→pain has on life. Pain was therefore not viewed as the outcome, but rather ongoing pain that
   457→iteratively impacts lives was the focus. This reflects a temporal aspect to pain, and the
   458→conceptual approach taken by the CRIISP project around pain states and transition (10). It is
   459→thus an exposure-outcome relationship that we wanted to understand, how pain impacts on
   460→lives and how this unfolds over time i.e., gets better, gets worse or is maintained. Utilising the
   461→DAG approach required reconceptualising the outcome as an exposure-outcome relationship.
   462→In DAG terms, pain impact at time 1 (baseline) is the exposure, and the outcome is continued
   463→impact at time 2 (follow-up). Figure 10 demonstrates the detailed representation of the
   464→exposure to outcome parameterisation, that in the integrated DAG was collapsed across
   465→domains of impact and simplified to ‘extent of pain impact’.
   466→
   467→Figure 10. Representation of the exposure to outcome relationship in the DAG process. The
   468→figure shown is the ‘raw’ DAG from the workshops with variables labelled as they were in initial
   469→discussions before consolidation.
   470→4.2 The role of time and identification of key processes
   471→Another area of shared learning was the importance of time in the definition of our exposure and
   472→outcome. Time is a powerful tool in epidemiology, but its complexity can be overlooked. In
   473→DAGs, the timing and sequence of events are often used to guide the direction of arrows:
   474→causes should occur before their consequences. This can help researchers order variables,
   475→trace possible chains of events, and identify appropriate measures across longitudinal
   476→datasets.
   477→However, the order of events can become restrictive if time is taken too literally. For example,
   478→the point at which a life event occurs may differ from the point at which the event had its
   479→greatest impact. Similarly, a reaction to pain that has negative short-term consequence may
   480→have a positive longer-term impact. In the workshops, visual spacing was therefore used to
   481→represent the meaning and perceived impact of events rather than the exact temporal distance
   482→between them.
   483→This was reflected in the initial DAGs, where a large cluster of variables occupied substantial
   484→visual space because they represented the initial reaction to pain, even though this period may
   485→only have lasted a few days. Time is important to keep in mind in DAGs.
   486→
   487→4.3 Usefulness of the approach to disentangle proposed mediators and moderators
   488→The approach enabled the groups to map variables and together make difficult decisions on
   489→whether or not they are mediators or moderators. Key examples of this are given below.
   490→Figure 11. Zoomed in to one key prioritised pathway in the final integrated DAG
   491→4.3.1 Internal mediators (reactivity, confidence and identity)
   492→The initial variables discussed as part of the pathway ‘within’ the chain of events, therefore
   493→acting as mediators in epidemiology terms) involved feelings of uncertainty. Identity loss linked
   494→to uncertainty was placed in the more ‘immediate time’ close to the exposure variables by the
   495→WDG group and agreed in consolidation processes with researchers as an early mediating
   496→variable.
   497→4.3.2 Interpersonal mediators (relationships)
   498→The consideration of relational processes as a ‘mediator’ rather than a potential risk factor (i.e.
   499→exposure) or ‘moderator’ was also carefully considered. In epidemiological terms a moderator is
   500→external to the pathway, but in some way alters the strength or direction between two variables.
   501→The storm metaphor enabled the placement of relationships as a mediator, it provided the
   502→common language which meant public contributors clarified that relationships were not
   503→something outside of the ‘storm’ like a rainbow, but part of the chain of events ‘within’ the core
   504→
   505→sequence that links pain to its wider impact on peoples’ lives. The mechanisms informing the
   506→direction of the link between initial pain and social connections included not wanting to feel like
   507→a burden, pain symptom experience impacting social ‘performance’ and masking of pain.
   508→Representing relational impact as a mediator is then important because this implies that if
   509→psychosocial interventions focus on harnessing positive support in relationships, even if they
   510→don’t reduce pain, they could reduce pain impact.
   511→4.4 Mapping the differences between DAGs
   512→Comparing the two initial DAGs also allowed us to explore differences in how concepts were
   513→prioritised and represented between public contributors and researchers. For example,
   514→medication use, and side effects, were prominent in the experts-by-experience DAG, but not in
   515→the researcher DAG. On discussion, the group agreed that these concepts were partly captured
   516→through the broader concepts of intervention and physical state, with physical state understood
   517→as the combined manifestation of the pain itself and any medication and side effects.
   518→Conversely, sleep was explicitly included in the researchers DAG but not the initial experts-by-
   519→experience DAG. On consolidation the group felt this to be relevant to both the uncertainty and
   520→ongoing impact of pain.
   521→4.5 Comparison to previous co-designed DAGs:
   522→Previous researchers have developed a similar approach, and they share the same emphasis on
   523→the value of the perspective of those with lived experience of chronic pain, and the importance
   524→of ensuring that all aspects of DAGs are explained to those without a clinical or research
   525→background using accessible language (4). However, our approach differs due to the use of the
   526→storm metaphor which provides a systematic creative method to support inclusion. The
   527→metaphor translates the more conceptually challenging and technical features of DAGs (e.g.
   528→the difference between mediators, moderators and confounders) into more familiar concepts
   529→(e.g. the sun, shelter and thunder). This allowed multiple stakeholders to contribute
   530→meaningfully to the development of future research questions. In addition, it was suitable to the
   531→specific psychosocial mechanisms of interest to the consortium.
   532→4.6 Strengths
   533→This approach has several strengths. Firstly, the use of the storm metaphor created a common
   534→language that allowed researchers and experts by experience to have equity in the decisions
   535→made. It placed equal value on variable input as well as where they were placed in the DAGs. It
   536→also created a map to allow us to combine different parts of the mechanisms proposed by the
   537→DAG, but to then isolate individual pathways that can be tested in specific datasets. In the
   538→context of the CRIISP project this was important given different research teams were working on
   539→specific datasets, but the consortium as a whole sought to harmonise research questions,
   540→analytical approaches, and findings across datasets. Having a clear framework provided a way
   541→to test specific pathways where data allowed, while maintaining a common mission and
   542→structure for combining findings later on.
   543→An important by-product of this approach was wider engagement of public contributors, who
   544→had the opportunity to share decision-making power by shaping key choices in what variables
   545→were considered as outcomes and exposures, and the direction of arrows between them. It
   546→
   547→enabled those with lived experience of chronic pain to shape future research and to ensure it
   548→was meaningful to their experiences, while being accessible and enjoyable, which also
   549→increased general engagement with the CRIISP project. Ultimately, the approach has led to
   550→more impactful research. In this specific example, the produced DAG model has been
   551→empirically tested in real data and has led to evidence for important and previously unknown
   552→pathways highlighting the impact of pain in emerging adults on disrupting later social
   553→relationships (11). While the link between pain and relationships was already considered in the
   554→literature, the direction of the association (going from pain impact to relationships) and
   555→specification of relationships as a mediating variable was a direct consequence and change in
   556→model specification following the Dream-DAG co-design. Put simply, if not for the co-design
   557→framework the specific mediating pathway would not have been included.
   558→Limitations
   559→Some limitations of this approach should be noted. For example, DAGs can be incredibly
   560→complex, and it can be impossible to test some pathways due to unavailability of data (for
   561→example, there is no measure of uncertainty). Also, the concept of effect modification can be
   562→difficult to represent in DAGs. Although some guidance does exist (12) it is currently not a
   563→programmed feature in DAGItty. Furthermore, the use of the storm metaphor can also be seen
   564→as limited in its approach where, for example, features of the storm (rain, sun, wind etc) do not
   565→fully mirror the complexity and number of features of most causal models. There is scope,
   566→however, to consider the use of other metaphors in future research, as we hypothesise that it is
   567→not the metaphor itself per se, but rather the process that offers a simplified, universal and
   568→relatable concept that is de-personalised from one’s own story. Furthermore, only a small
   569→number of experts by experience and experts by academic or clinical training were included.
   570→While this was appropriate for the nature of the involvement activities, which required space for
   571→discussion and input, it may have limited the breadth of perspectives represented. The outlined
   572→process/framework can now inform future work that could build on this by engaging with more
   573→diverse groups of contributors.
   574→Conclusion
   575→The final integrated Dream-DAG poses an accessible, interactive consultation and engagement
   576→activity which mapped complex processes and produced unique and testable models. In future
   577→steps the CRIISP team are empirically testing research questions from the CRIISP Dream-DAG
   578→in existing data sets and guiding future decisions on measurement of missing concepts, such as
   579→identity and uncertainty of pain.
