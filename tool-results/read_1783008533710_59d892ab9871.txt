   201→   201→189 reciprocated by promoting proximity-seeking, courtship, sex, and caregiving. Obsessive thinking
   202→   202→190 about a loved one also means that stimuli about the loved one are regularly present, in turn activating
   203→   203→191 neurobiological responses to the loved one, reinforcing romantic love. Empirical support for the
   204→   204→5
   205→   205→
   206→   206→192 proximity-seeking function comes from Valshtein et al. (2020), who found that obsessive thinking
   207→   207→193 predicted prospective proximity-seeking behaviour (see Behaviour, below). This is consistent with
   208→   208→194 the proposal that obsessive thinking promotes reproductively relevant approach behaviour toward the
   209→   209→195 loved one.
   210→   210→196 Gonzaga et al. (2008) have shown that the experience of love in romantic relationships, but not
   211→   211→197 sexual desire, facilitates the suppression of thoughts about attractive alternatives and reduces memory
   212→   212→198 for their attractiveness-related features. It is plausible that obsessive thinking about the beloved
   213→   213→199 contributes to this effect by occupying cognitive resources that would otherwise be available for
   214→   214→200 attending to rival stimuli. However, participants in that study had been in relationships for an average
   215→   215→201 of nearly three years, meaning many were unlikely to still be experiencing romantic love and its
   216→   216→202 characteristic obsessive thinking. Whether this suppression effect is stronger during the early,
   217→   217→203 obsessive-thinking-intensive phase of romantic love remains untested.
   218→   218→204 4 Development Across the Lifespan
   219→   219→205 Romantic love expresses in juveniles, with it being documented in children as young as 4 years old
   220→   220→206 (Hatfield et al., 1988). Indications are that obsessive thinking about a loved one is present in these
   221→   221→207 children experiencing romantic love. The Juvenile Love Scale; (Greenwell, 1983; Hatfield & Young,
   222→   222→208 2009) includes two items assessing obsessive thinking: “Did you ever keep thinking about ___when
   223→   223→209 you wanted to stop and couldn’t?” and “I am always thinking about .” However, there is some
   224→   224→210 uncertainty about this as the studies validating the JLS were never peer reviewed and we are not
   225→   225→211 privy to child participants’ scores on those two specific items. Considerations of love in early
   226→   226→212 adolescence have been published (e.g., Montgomery & Sorell, 1998) but no documentation of
   227→   227→213 obsessive thinking appears to be present that we are aware of. Romantic love expresses with all its
   228→   228→214 features following puberty (Bode & Kushnick, 2021a). Understanding the age at which obsessive
   229→   229→215 thinking about a loved one expresses in romantic love is an area for targeted research.
   230→   230→216 Although obsessive thinking about a loved one is most often studied in prototypical romantic love
   231→   231→217 (i.e., in the first two years of a romantic relationship), obsessive thinking about a loved one also
   232→   232→218 persists in long-term romantic love. Among married individuals reporting being 'very intensely in
   233→   233→219 love' after more than ten years, thinking about the partner when apart was a significant correlate of
   234→   234→220 love intensity in both U.S. (O’Leary et al., 2011) and Spanish (Cuenca-Montesino et al., 2015)
   235→   235→221 samples. The frequency and content of obsessive thinking about a loved one across the duration of a
   236→   236→222 relationship has not been systematically investigated and represents a priority for future research.
   237→   237→223 5 Biological Mechanisms
   238→   238→224 Almost nothing is known about the biological mechanisms that cause obsessive thinking about a
   239→   239→225 loved one in romantic love. One fMRI study (Fujisaki et al., 2026) suggests that the anterior insula
   240→   240→226 (aINS) may play a role: in males in the early stages of a romantic relationship, the distinctiveness of
   241→   241→227 partner-specific neural representations in the aINS was positively associated with the frequency of
   242→   242→228 intrusive thoughts about a partner measured via experience sampling. This finding awaits replication
   243→   243→229 and extension to female samples.
   244→   244→230 In the late 1990s and early 2000s, Helen Fisher and others (Fisher, 2000; Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al.,
   245→   245→231 2002) confidently asserted that obsessive thinking was mechanistically similar to intrusive thinking
   246→   246→232 found in OCD. They attributed it to lower levels of serotonin during romantic love. Claimed evidence
   247→   247→233 for this included the first biological study of romantic love which measured circulating serotonin
   248→   248→6
   249→   249→
   250→   250→234 transporters in blood platelets (Marazziti et al., 1999). Since that time, however, it has become
   251→   251→235 apparent that this mechanistic explanation is almost certainly wrong.
   252→   252→236 The findings from the original study upon which this theory drew its initial support were
   253→   253→237 misinterpreted. Marazziti et al. (1999) found that the density of the serotonin transporter in blood
   254→   254→238 platelets was lower in people who has recently fallen in love and OCD patients than in control
   255→   255→239 participants. This was interpreted to indicate that individuals experiencing romantic love exhibited
   256→   256→240 higher circulating serotonin. Since that time, however, it has become known that lower serotonin
   257→   257→241 transporter levels are actually associated with higher levels of circulating serotonin (Jørgensen et al.,
   258→   258→242 2014; Mercado & Kilic, 2010). Although a later study (Langeslag et al., 2012) found lower levels of
   259→   259→243 serotonin in blood plasma and serum in males experiencing romantic love than male controls, it also
   260→   260→244 found higher plasma and serum serotonin levels in females experiencing romantic love than female
   261→   261→245 controls. Interestingly, in females experiencing romantic love, there was a significant positive
   262→   262→246 correlation between serum serotonin levels and scores on obsessive thinking about a loved one items
   263→   263→247 taken form the Passionate Love Scale (PLS; Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986). It should be noted though
   264→   264→248 that the sample size of this study was small. The final death knell for this theory comes from a
   265→   265→249 correlational study (Bode, Kowal, et al., 2025) finding that individuals experiencing romantic love do
   266→   266→250 not differ in obsessive thinking regardless of whether they are taking selective serotonin reuptake
   267→   267→251 inhibitors (SSRIs) or not. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors are a common treatment for OCD
   268→   268→252 (Del Casale et al., 2019). If they alleviate intrusive thinking in OCD, and intrusive thinking in OCD
   269→   269→253 and obsessive thinking in romantic love are mechanistically linked, it follows that SSRIs would
   270→   270→254 reduce obsessive thinking in romantic love. Because they don’t, this is strong evidence that the
   271→   271→255 mechanisms causing these two phenomena are different.
   272→   272→256 6 Ecology
   273→   273→257 There is some evidence that ecology (in the form of national-level indictors) can influence the
   274→   274→258 expression of obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love. In a cross-sectional study
   275→   275→259 investigating sex differences in romantic love using the Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode, Luoto, et
   276→   276→260 al., 2025), The United Nations Gender Inequality Index was used as a control in a hierarchical linear
   277→   277→261 regression predicting obsessive thinking about a loved one. In the final model, Gender Inequality
   278→   278→262 Index was the second largest predictor of obsessive thinking (after biological sex), indicating that
   279→   279→263 individuals in more gender equal countries experienced less obsessive thinking about a loved one.
   280→   280→264 This is consistent with another study (Sorokowski et al., 2023) demonstrating associations between
   281→   281→265 gender equality, modernisation and collectivism and three components of love in romantic
   282→   282→266 relationships (i.e., intimacy, passion, and commitment). This indicates that the cultural environment
   283→   283→267 may have a causal influence on how obsessive thinking out a loved one expresses. Future studies
   284→   284→268 should investigate the association between modernisation and collectivism on obsessive thinking
   285→   285→269 about a loved one.
   286→   286→270 7 Cognitions
   287→   287→271 According to Tennov (1979), obsessive thinking about the loved one (what she called the “limerent
   288→   288→272 object;” i.e., the person one feels limerence for) is a main component of romantic love. She relates it
   289→   289→273 to compulsive daydreaming about, preoccupation with, and uncontrollable fantasizing about the
   290→   290→274 limerent object. Likewise, Fisher (Fisher, 1998) and Fisher et al. (Fisher et al., 2002) list obsessive
   291→   291→275 thinking about a loved one as one of the characteristics of romantic love. Here, we review studies that
   292→   292→276 have assessed this cognitive aspect of romantic love.
   293→   293→7
   294→   294→
   295→   295→277 Several studies have assessed how much people think about their loved one. In a study of participants
   296→   296→278 who had ever experienced a romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), thoughts about the ex-partner
   297→   297→279 occurred several times per week on average and were fairly vivid, with nostalgia, loneliness, certain
   298→   298→280 songs, sadness, celebrations, anxiety, and boredom endorsed as common triggers. Among
   299→   299→281 participants currently in love, self-reported estimates of time spent thinking about the loved one have
   300→   300→282 ranged widely: 65% of waking hours in participants in love for 9 months or less (Langeslag et al.,
   301→   301→283 2012), 50–61% in participants in a relationship for 9 months or less (Nilakantan et al., 2014), 67% in
   302→   302→284 participants who were in love (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), 50% in young adults in love for less than
   303→   303→285 two years from 33 countries (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025), and 74% (Langeslag, Submitted) and 77%
   304→   304→286 (Evans et al. 2026) in participants experiencing limerence . Lower estimates of 21% and 49% come
   305→   305→287 from experience sampling studies in male participants in relationships of 7 months or less (Fujisaki et
   306→   306→288 al., 2026) and participants experiencing limerence (Evans et al., 2026), respectively. These lower
   307→   307→289 figures may reflect reduced memory bias from capturing experiences in real-time. In sum, average
   308→   308→290 self-reported thinking about the loved one ranged from 21 to 77% of the time across studies, with
   309→   309→291 individual responses ranging from 0 to 100% indicating that, for some individuals, obsessive thinking
   310→   310→292 about a loved one can be all-consuming.
   311→   311→293 Time spent thinking about the loved one has been positively associated with romantic love intensity
   312→   312→294 (r = .25; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025) and limerence intensity (r = .37; Langeslag, Submitted and r =
   313→   313→295 .39; Evans et al., 2026) but not with companionate love intensity (r = .003; Langeslag & Philippi,
   314→   314→296 2025). This indicates that obsessive thinking about a loved one is associated with romantic love
   315→   315→297 specifically rather than companionate love, although the correlations are far from perfect, and we
   316→   316→298 caution against using obsessive thinking about a loved one as a proxy for love intensity as has been
   317→   317→299 done in some studies (i.e., Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007). Obsessive
   318→   318→300 thinking about a loved one has also been shown to increase following threat to relatedness (i.e., the
   319→   319→301 basic psychological need to feel connected to, cared for by, and accepted by others) and negative
   320→   320→302 fantasizing about the loved one across a series of experimental studies using relatedness need
   321→   321→303 manipulations and directed fantasy tasks (Valshtein et al., 2020).
   322→   322→304 People think about their loved one during cognitive tasks unrelated to the loved one. In one study
   323→   323→305 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), participants who were in love reported thinking about their loved one
   324→   324→306 on 42% of thought probes and 34% of free responses during a sustained attention response task.
   325→   325→307 Interestingly, both measures were only weakly associated with obsessive thinking about a loved one
   326→   326→308 in general (both rs < .22), suggesting these two phenomena are only somewhat coupled. In studies of
   327→   327→309 participants who had experienced a break-up and were instructed to suppress thoughts of their ex-
   328→   328→310 partner (Baird et al., 2013), participants still thought about their ex-partner 14–22% of the time. High
   329→   329→311 cognitive load increased probe-caught (but not self-caught) thoughts about the ex-partner, and desire
   330→   330→312 to reconcile was positively associated with probe-caught thinking, suggesting that people who want
   331→   331→313 to reconcile think about their ex-partner more — and are less aware of doing so. The behavioural
   332→   332→314 consequences of this obsessive thinking about a loved one are addressed below.
   333→   333→315 8 Emotions
   334→   334→316 Some terms analogous with "obsessive thinking about a loved one" have negative connotation, (e.g.,
   335→   335→317 "intrusive thinking", "preoccupation", and "pathological obsession" (Fisher et al., 2002; Nilakantan et
   336→   336→318 al., 2014; Tennov, 1979). Intrusive thinking implies recurrent and persistent thoughts that are at least
   337→   337→319 sometimes unwanted and distressing, and that the individual attempts to ignore or suppress
   338→   338→320 (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Indeed, in the study of participants who had experienced a
   339→   339→321 romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), participants endorsed frightened, guilty, remorseful,
   340→   340→8
   341→   341→
   342→   342→322 ashamed, anxious, and grateful as common feelings when thinking about the ex-partner. However,
   343→   343→323 thinking about a loved one is not always bothersome. In a study of participants experiencing
   344→   344→324 limerence recruited through internet limerence communities (Langeslag, Submitted), the mean score
   345→   345→325 on a slider from "it bothers me" to "I enjoy it" was 41 out of 100, significantly below the midpoint.
   346→   346→326 Thinking about the loved one bothered them. In contrast, in a study of participants who were in love
   347→   347→327 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), the mean on the same slider was 84 out of 100, significantly above the
   348→   348→328 midpoint. These participants enjoyed thinking about their loved one.
   349→   349→329 What might determine whether thinking about the loved one has positive or negative emotional
   350→   350→330 effects? Across these two studies (Langeslag, Submitted; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), enjoyment of
   351→   351→331 thinking about the loved one did not correlate positively with romantic love intensity but did
   352→   352→332 correlate positively with companionate love intensity as measured with the IAS attachment scale
   353→   353→333 (Langeslag et al., 2013). Notably, only 5% of the limerence participants were in a romantic
   354→   354→334 relationship with their loved one, compared with 92% of the in-love participants, while time spent
   355→   355→335 thinking about the loved one did not differ drastically between the two samples (74% vs. 67%). It
   356→   356→336 therefore seems that whether people enjoy thinking about their loved one or whether it bothers them
   357→   357→337 depends more on companionate love intensity and whether they are in a romantic relationship with
   358→   358→338 the loved one than on how much they are thinking about them.
   359→   359→339 The rewarding character of thinking about a loved one is also evident in its analgesic effects. In two
   360→   360→340 studies (Nilakantan et al., 2014), participants experienced less pain from thermal stimuli when
   361→   361→341 viewing pictures of their romantic partner than of an acquaintance, and this analgesia was positively
   362→   362→342 correlated with the percentage of waking hours spent thinking about their partner. The mechanism
   363→   363→343 appears to be activation of brain reward regions rather than distraction (Younger et al., 2010),
   364→   364→344 consistent with the enjoyment many people report when thinking about their loved one.
   365→   365→345 There have also been some studies linking obsessive thinking about a loved one with features
   366→   366→346 analogous to symptoms of mood disorders. In one study (Bode & Kavanagh, 2025) using the
   367→   367→347 Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022), membership of clusters associated with
   368→   368→348 higher obsessive thinking about the loved one was positively associated with hypomanic-like features
   369→   369→349 (which are closely related to elevated mood (Faurholt-Jepsen et al., 2020)). This is consistent with
   370→   370→350 previous studies (Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007) that found a similar
   371→   371→351 association and two studies finding inconsistent associations with depression symptoms (Bajoghli et
   372→   372→352 al., 2013; Bajoghli et al., 2014).
   373→   373→353 9 Behaviour
   374→   374→354 Behavioural consequences of obsessive thinking about a loved one may be that it manifests as
   375→   375→355 reduced task performance and that it may lead to proximity-seeking behaviour. Several studies have
   376→   376→356 provided evidence that love produces behavioural distraction. Love intensity as measured with the
   377→   377→357 PLS was positively correlated with the interference effect in Stroop and flanker tasks (van
   378→   378→358 Steenbergen et al., 2014), and more obsessive thinking about a loved one in general was associated
   379→   379→359 with poorer and slower performance on a short-term memory task (Langeslag & van Strien, 2020)
   380→   380→360 and with reduced ability to withhold responses in a sustained attention response task (Langeslag &
   381→   381→361 Philippi, 2025). These studies show that love and thinking about a loved one in general (rather than
   382→   382→362 during the task specifically) are associated with behavioural distraction. In addition, the more
   383→   383→363 participants thought about their loved one during the sustained attention response task as rated by
   384→   384→364 coders, the slower their responses were (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and probe-caught thinking
   385→   385→365 about an ex-partner during reading was associated with reduced reading comprehension (Baird et al.,
   386→   386→9
   387→   387→
   388→   388→366 2013). Interestingly, self-caught thinking about the ex-partner was not associated with reduced
   389→   389→367 reading comprehension (Baird et al., 2013), which suggests that thoughts about the loved one that the
   390→   390→368 person is unaware of are particularly disruptive to performance. These studies suggest that thinking
   391→   391→369 about a loved one during an unrelated task specifically is distracting. Behavioural distraction has
   392→   392→370 clear implications for workplace productivity and engagement with the non-romantic social
   393→   393→371 environment.
   394→   394→372 Romantic love is also associated with proximity-seeking behaviours that vary in severity, with
   395→   395→373 stalking as an example of a severe proximity-seeking behaviour. Valshtein et al. (2020) assessed
   396→   396→374 prospective proximity-seeking behaviour in participants in a romantic relationship using 53 items
   397→   397→375 asking how likely they would be to engage in behaviours such as showing up to the loved one's house
   398→   398→376 unannounced and sending desperate messages. Across both studies, the score on a 12-item obsessive
   399→   399→377 thinking measure was positively associated with prospective proximity-seeking behaviour. We can’t
   400→   400→378 assert a causal relationship because the study was cross-sectional, but this is an avenue for future
   401→   401→379 research.
   402→   402→380 10 Discussion
   403→   403→381 Obsessive thinking about a loved one has been identified as a core feature of romantic love for more
   404→   404→382 than four decades (Tennov, 1979), is embedded in the field's most widely used measure of romantic
   405→   405→383 love (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986), and is regularly invoked in theoretical accounts of the phenomenon
   406→   406→384 (Bode, 2023; Fisher, 1998). Yet it has never been systematically reviewed. This article provides the
   407→   407→385 first such synthesis, organised using an Eight Perspectives approach (Bode & Kowal, 2026).
   408→   408→386 Obsessive thinking about a loved one appears to be a cross-culturally present feature of romantic love
   409→   409→387 that expresses across the lifespan, is associated with romantic love specifically rather than
   410→   410→388 companionate love, and has identifiable cognitive, emotional, and behavioural signatures. What
   411→   411→389 emerges, however, is that our understanding of this fundamental feature of romantic love remains
   412→   412→390 remarkably partial. Table 1 presents a summary of the main findings of this review.

   466→   466→414 A second observation is that the pathologising frame implied by terms such as "intrusive thinking"
   467→   467→415 and "pathological obsession" is difficult to reconcile with the available evidence. Selective serotonin

   469→   469→417 suggesting its mechanisms differ from those underlying OCD. Many people who are in love enjoy
   470→   470→418 thinking about their loved one (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and the phenomenon is associated with
   471→   471→419 analgesia (Nilakantan et al., 2014) and proximity-seeking behaviour (Valshtein et al., 2020)— both
   472→   472→420 consistent with adaptive function. These findings suggest obsessive thinking in romantic love is
   473→   473→421 better understood as a functional component of a mating-related motivational state than as a symptom
   474→   474→422 of dysfunction. We therefore favour the term "obsessive thinking about a loved one", which specifies
   475→   475→423 the object of thought rather than pathologising the thinker, and hope this review marks the start of a
   476→   476→424 broader shift toward this usage across the field.
   477→   477→425 Several limitations of the available evidence warrant mention. Studies of obsessive thinking about a
   478→   478→426 loved one in romantic love are dominated by Western, young-adult samples, limiting claims about
   479→   479→427 universality. Several load-bearing findings rest on single studies awaiting replication (e.g., Baird et
   480→   480→428 al., 2013; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025; Nilakantan et al., 2014; Valshtein et al., 2020), and many of
   481→   481→429 these studies are based on small samples and cross-sectional designs that limit causal inference.
   482→   482→430 Measurement is heavily reliant on self-report and, in particular, on three items from the Passionate
   483→   483→431 Love Scale (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986) that were not developed to assess obsessive thinking about a

   485→   485→433 behavioural measures, would strengthen the evidence base considerably. These limitations inform the
   486→   486→434 priorities for future research outlined below.

   524→   524→449 11 Conclusions
   525→   525→450 Obsessive thinking about a loved one is a defining feature of romantic love, yet research on it has
   526→   526→451 accumulated in a piecemeal fashion across disciplines that rarely speak to one another. This review
   527→   527→452 has used an Eight Perspectives approach to bring that scattered literature into a single structure,
   528→   528→453 clarify what is known, and make visible what is not. The phenotypic expression of obsessive thinking
   529→   529→454 is reasonably well characterised; its mechanisms, evolutionary functions, and ecology are not.
   530→   530→455 Addressing these gaps will require contributions from the evolutionary social and behavioural
   531→   531→456 sciences, neuroscience, genetics, and cross-cultural research — disciplines that have, to date, largely
   532→   532→457 overlooked this striking and ubiquitous feature of human mating psychology.
