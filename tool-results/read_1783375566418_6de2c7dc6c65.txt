   737→Language Analysis: Comparing Semantic Characteristics of Human and GPT Reappraisals
   738→With GPT-4 performing better than humans in producing reappraisals, a natural next step is to ask
   739→which features within GPT-4 reappraisals led to their perceived superiority. Using semantic analysis, we tried to
   740→answer three crucial questions regarding the essence of GPT-4 and human reappraisals. The first is to what
   741→extent GPT-4 and humans differ in terms of semantic similarity to the vignettes they were trying to reappraise,
   742→and is similarity to the vignette associated with reappraisal quality? This investigation was driven from lay
   743→criticism of GPT-4, which is that despite being eloquent and detailed, its responses may be less tuned in to the
   744→specific scenarios produced by the vignettes. Our analysis of data from our studies suggested that GPT-4 and
   745→humans did not differ significantly in their degree of semantic similarity to the vignettes, but that reappraisals
   746→that were closer to the vignette were generally of higher quality (see SI section “Semantic Distance to Vignette
   747→and Reappraisal Quality”).
   748→The second question we were interested in answering is whether one of the reasons GPT-4 reappraisals
   749→seemed better is because the words produced by GPT-4 were more complex or may be interpreted as produced
   750→by someone with higher intelligence. We therefore compared the language complexity between humans and
   751→GPT-4 in our studies. Results validated that GPT-4 used more complex language compared to humans, but that
   752→language complexity did not predict reappraisal quality, especially since specific reappraisal vocabulary was a
   753→stronger predictor (see below).
