    14→Abstract 
    15→Motivation is critical to successful academic functioning. A growing body of evidence details how motivation evolves throughout the lifetime, yet middle adulthood is rarely examined. Here we examined how trait apathy and fatigue, both of which manifest in diminished motivation, relate to self-reported academic functioning among university students aged 30 – 50. 332 students completed trait apathy and fatigue scales, measures of academic functioning (autonomous motivation, controlled motivation, study engagement, study effort, academic stress, dropout intentions, academic performance, and study satisfaction), and measures of family, household, work-related factors. Apathy and fatigue were related to almost all academic measures, yet apathy was more closely linked to academic functioning; in particular autonomous motivation, study engagement, and study satisfaction. Our results suggest that relatively stable individual differences in motivation among healthy adults, as captured by the measures of trait-apathy and fatigue, are closely linked to functioning in a critical area – academic development.

    23→Background
    24→Motivation drives everyday behaviours and is critical for successful academic functioning (Bonnelle et al., 2015; Cerasoli et al., 2014; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Academic motivation has been linked to higher study engagement, less procrastination, and the use of more sophisticated cognitive strategies (Howard et al., 2021; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). However, motivation is not static – it evolves throughout the lifetime (Cutler et al., 2024; Lockwood et al., 2021; Westbrook et al., 2013). In recent years, several highly cited studies have detailed how neural, cognitive, and personality factors that shape motivated behaviour evolve throughout the life course (Komarraju et al., 2009; Lockwood et al., 2021; Westbrook et al., 2013). These studies typically examine younger, still-maturing individuals, or late adulthood (Cutler et al., 2024; Katsantonis, 2025; Westbrook et al., 2013). Therefore, the prevalence of motivational deficits among fully mature but not aged adults is poorly understood, as are the potential consequences of these deficits on academic success, a critical area of healthy functioning.
    25→Apathy and Fatigue Result in Diminished Motivation 
    26→Apathy and fatigue, commonly experienced by healthy adults, result in lower motivation and diminished goal-directed activity (Ang et al., 2017; Jurgelis et al., 2021; Lockwood et al., 2017). Clinical apathy, defined as diminished motivation for emotional, physical, and cognitive activity, is a paradigmatic disorder of motivation (Husain & Roiser, 2018); whereas subclinical manifestations of apathy, also known as trait apathy, are common among generally healthy adults (Ang et al., 2017). Trait fatigue is a closely related construct characterised by a subjective sense of exhaustion (Jurgelis et al., 2021). Even though fatigue and apathy are similar conceptually (Le Heron et al., 2018; Müller & Apps, 2018) and tend to co-occur (Ang et al., 2017; Jurgelis et al., 2021), they may capture different manifestations of reduced motivation. Whereas apathy is characterized by difficulties initiating actions and diminished capacity to experience positive affect (anhedonia) (Husain & Roiser, 2018), fatigue is defined by a subjective sense of exhaustion and difficulties in sustaining effortful activity (Matthews et al., 2023; Ritvo et al., 1997; Smets et al., 1995). Fatigue and constructs closely linked to it, such as academic burnout, have been repeatedly linked to maladaptive academic outcomes; including higher academic stress, lower study engagement, and poorer academic performance (Doerr et al., 2015; Nagane, 2004; Schaufeli et al., 2002). Similarly, different operationalisations of apathy, such as degree apathy (Playfoot et al., 2024), sluggish cognitive tempo (Tamm et al., 2016), and teacher apathy (Hasanzadeh et al., 2024), have been linked to lower motivation, higher anxiety, and increased willingness to use generative artificial intelligence (AI) among students. 
    27→Unique Challenges Encountered by Older University Students
    28→        Most studies examining academic motivation focus on the population of traditional university students (e.g., Hanus & Fox, 2015; Kusurkar et al., 2013; Nteveros et al., 2020; Playfoot et al., 2024; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). The findings from these studies may not generalise to older university students, not only due to changes in motivational mechanisms with age (Cutler et al., 2024) but also to a different set of challenges encountered by older university students (Babb et al., 2021). Separately, some studies have focused on a category of non-traditional university students, typically defined as being 25 years and older (Augustine et al., 2018; Babb et al., 2021; Fiorilli et al., 2022; Markle, 2015). These studies cite the conflicts between academic demands and family or work obligations as key challenges to non-traditional students’ academic success and well-being (Augustine et al., 2018; Babb et al., 2021; Fiorilli et al., 2022; Markle, 2015). In the past, such identity conflict among non-traditional students has been linked to sleep problems and higher fatigue, yet the findings are not entirely consistent (Augustine et al., 2018; Fiorilli et al., 2022; Laberge et al., 2011). Hence it is unclear whether the factors that contribute to identity conflict, such as working full time and caring for underage children, result in an overall decline in motivation, as reflected by higher apathy or fatigue scores.  
    29→How Apathy Relates to Autonomous and Controlled Motivation
    30→A growing body of research details the effects of apathy on motivated behaviour (Bonnelle et al., 2016; Bonnelle et al., 2015; Husain & Roiser, 2018; Jurgelis et al., 2021; Lockwood et al., 2017), yet the link between trait apathy and academic motivation has not been examined. Self-determination theory (SDT) has proven critical in understanding how motivation shapes academic outcomes (Howard et al., 2021; Kusurkar et al., 2013; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Within SDT, a common distinction is made between autonomous motivation and controlled motivation, both of which, in the context of research on academic motivation, are often assessed using the Academic Self-Regulation Scale (ASRS) (Kusurkar et al., 2013; Ryan & Deci, 2020; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Autonomous motivation is characterised by an internal locus of control stemming from an innate interest in academic activities (e.g., “I’m studying because I’m highly interested in doing this”) or well-internalised extrinsic motivation in pursuit of a personally relevant goal (e.g., “I’m studying because it is personally important to me”). In contrast, controlled motivation is defined by an external perceived locus of causality (e.g., “I’m studying because that’s something others force me to do”). 
    31→There are interesting conceptual parallels between the distinction made in SDT between more autonomous and controlled types of motivation, and the pattern of deficits observed in apathy. Specifically, apathy is characterised by the struggle to complete self-initiated actions while simultaneously retaining the capacity to complete the tasks when externally prompted (Husain & Roiser, 2018). Hence, autonomous motivation, which is characterised by an internal locus of control, may show stronger links to apathy than controlled motivation, which is more reliant on external pressures (Ryan & Deci, 2017). This would have important practical implications given that autonomous motivation relates to more favourable academic outcomes than controlled motivation (Howard et al., 2021; Kusurkar et al., 2013; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009).
    32→Current Study
    33→In the current study, we aimed to understand how trait apathy and fatigue relate to academic functioning among university students aged 30 – 50. Within a sample of 332 participants, we assessed self-reported apathy and fatigue, a broad spectrum of academic measures (autonomous motivation, controlled motivation, study engagement, study effort, academic stress, dropout intentions, grades and exam performance, and study satisfaction), and factors related to family, household, studies, and work. First, we tested whether family, household, and work-related factors could account for either apathy or fatigue scores. We then examined how apathy and fatigue, separately and together, relate to academic variables.

    37→Method
    38→Participants
    39→The final sample consisted of 332 adult university students (211 female, 116 male, 5 other/prefer not to say) aged 30 - 50 years (M = 35.9, SD = 5.9) and with no prior history of neurological or psychiatric conditions. Via the online research platform Prolific, we recruited individuals residing in Australia, New Zealand, Canada, the United States of America, the United Kingdom, Ireland, and South Africa. Participants were compensated under Prolific ethical reward rates ($17.52 per hour). The study was approved by the School of Psychology’s Human Research Ethics Subcommittee at the University of Adelaide (HREC approval number 23/51). 
    40→A priori power analysis conducted using G*Power (version 3.1; Faul et al., 2009) indicated that a sample of at least 179 participants would offer sufficient power (1−β = .80) to detect a moderate correlation (.30) using a very conservative alpha level of α = .001 (two-sided), enabling us to correct for multiple tests. Given the available funding, we recruited a sample size well above this recommended threshold. 
    41→Measures
    42→Apathy Motivation Index. Apathy was measured using the Apathy Motivation Index (AMI; Ang et al., 2017). The AMI consists of 18 self-report items (e.g., “When I decide to do something, I am motivated to see it through to the end.”) rated on a 5-point Likert scale from 0 (“Completely untrue”) to 4 (“Completely true”). The AMI probes apathy across three dimensions - behavioural activation, social motivation, and emotional sensitivity, with the overall score reflecting the general propensity to experience apathy. The scale has well-established reliability and validity and is widely used to assess subclinical manifestations of apathy in the general population (Ang et al., 2017; Lockwood et al., 2017). We confirmed that the scale has good internal consistency (Cronbach’s α = .82).
    43→Modified Fatigue Impact Scale. Fatigue was measured using the Modified Fatigue Impact Scale (MFIS; Fisk et al., 1994; Ritvo et al., 1997). The MFIS consists of 21 self-report items (e.g., “I have been less able to complete tasks that require physical effort”) rated on a 5-point Likert scale from 0 (“Never”) to 4 (“Almost always”). The multidimensional MFIS assesses three facets of fatigue - physical, psychosocial, and cognitive. Higher overall scores indicate greater levels of trait fatigue. The MFIS is commonly used to assess sub-clinical fatigue (e.g., Ang et al., 2017; Jurgelis et al., 2021). It showed high internal consistency in the current study (α = .96).
    44→Academic Self-Regulation Scale. Autonomous and Controlled Motivation were measured using the Academic Self-Regulation Scale (ASRS; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). The scale asks participants to rate the importance of various factors as drivers of their academic motivation (“I am studying because …”). It contains four subscales – External Regulation (e.g., “Because that is something others (parents, friends, etc.) force me to do”), Introjected Regulation (e.g., “Because I would feel guilty if I didn’t study”), Identified Regulation (e.g., “Because it is personally important to me”), and Intrinsic Motivation (e.g., “Because I enjoy doing it”). The External and Introjected Regulation subscales are combined into a measure of Controlled Motivation, and the Identified Regulation and Intrinsic Motivation subscales are combined into a measure of Autonomous Motivation (Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). The ASRS is a well-established measure of academic motivation (e.g., Kusurkar et al., 2013; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Both Autonomous Motivation (α = .84) and Controlled Motivation (α = .85) subscales showed good internal consistency in the current study. 
    45→Study Engagement. We used the Utrecht Work Engagement Scale for Students (UWES-S) to assess study engagement (Schaufeli et al., 2002). The 14-item scale assesses students’ vigour, engagement, and absorption when studying (e.g., “Time flies when I’m studying”). Each item is rated on a 7-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“Never”) to 7 (“Always”). The measure has well-established validity and reliability (e.g., Cadime et al., 2016; Schaufeli et al., 2002). UWES-S showed high reliability in the current study (α = .93).
    46→Academic Effort. Academic Effort was measured using two single-item measures. The first item (“Study Effort – Rating”) asked participants to rate study effort on an 11-point Likert scale ranging from 0 (“No effort”) to 10 (“Maximum possible effort”) (Strauser et al., 2012). The second item (“Study Effort – Hours”) asked about the number of hours devoted to self-study per week (Kusurkar et al., 2013). Both measures have been used to quantify academic effort in past studies (Kusurkar et al., 2013; Strauser et al., 2012). 
    47→Perception of Academic Stress Scale. Academic stress was measured using the Perception of Academic Stress Scale (PASS; Bedewy & Gabriel, 2015). The PASS consists of 18 self-report items related to perceived sources of academic stress among university students. The phrasing of some items was modified to ensure that they were appropriate for use within the target population; for example, “The unrealistic expectations of my parents stress me out” was changed to “The unrealistic expectations of my family members stress me out”. Items were scored on a 5-point Likert scale from 1 (“Strongly disagree”) to 5 (“Strongly agree”). The PASS has established reliability and validity (Bedewy & Gabriel, 2015); it displayed good internal consistency in the current sample (α = .82)
    48→Dropout Intentions Scale. The intention to drop out was measured using the Dropout Intentions Scale (DIS; Hardre et al., 2019). The DIS consists of 7 self-report items related to intention to drop out in university students (e.g., “I intend to drop out of school”) rated on a 7-point Likert scale from 1 (“Strongly disagree”) to 7 (“Strongly agree”). The DIS has been validated (Hardre et al., 2019); our estimates confirmed good internal consistency (α = .86).
    49→Academic Performance. Academic performance was measured using three self-report survey items. The first item (“Performance – Grades”) was adapted from Vansteenkiste et al. (2009) (“What is your grade point average from the previous semester (or trimester/teaching period)?”). We converted grades from different grading systems into a common 4-point format using the publicly available guidelines (e.g., OECD, n.d.). Second, we asked participants to indicate the number of exams taken and passed in the previous teaching period to estimate the percentage of exams passed (“Performance – Exams %”) (Schaufeli et al., 2002). 
    50→Academic Satisfaction Scale. We used an adapted 21-item self-report measure to assess study satisfaction. The items assessed participants’ perceptions of curriculum, teaching practices, facilities, and the overall satisfaction with the studies (.e.g., “The curriculum helps to improve student required professional skills”). Participants rated each item on a 7-point Likert scale from 1 (“Completely disagree”) to 7 (“Completely agree”). As there is currently no established self-report measure suitable to assess student satisfaction across traditional on-campus studies and online studies, we used a measure based on two separate scales – the Postgraduate Nursing Student Academic Satisfaction Scale (PNSASS; Rahmatpour et al., 2021) and the Online Study Satisfaction Scale (OSSS; Eom & Ashill, 2016). Both original measures have well-established reliability and validity (Eom & Ashill, 2016; Rahmatpour et al., 2021). For more on the instrument construction see Supplementary Material 1. The final scale showed excellent internal consistency (α = .96).
    51→Demographic variables. We assessed the following employment, household, study, and demographic variables: the number of weekly employment hours (Employment Hours), the number of children under the age of 18 (Children), providing caregiving to other individuals e.g., elderly parents (Caregiving), the number of weekly hours spent in home duties (Home Duties - Hours), the level of studies i.e., undergraduate vs postgraduate (Postgraduate studies), completed study duration in years (Completed Study Duration), full time vs part time enrolment (Full Time Enrolment), studying entirely remotely vs a combination of on-campus and online activities (Remote Learning), gender (Gender), and age (Age). 
    52→Procedure and Design
    53→Data was collected in June 2022. Participants were recruited through Prolific (https://www.prolific.co) and completed the survey via Qualtrics. A participant information sheet and informed consent form were presented at the start of the survey. Participation was voluntary and anonymous. Contact details for counselling and support services were provided at the start and end of the survey should anyone experience distress due to participation in the study. Upon completion of the survey items, participants were presented with a debriefing statement providing them with contact details for the principal investigator and a School of Psychology Human Research Ethics Subcommittee member should they have had any questions or concerns related to the study or the ethical conduct of the research.
    54→To ensure data quality, we included three attention checks, embedded in self-report scales, asking participants to provide a pre-determined response to a given question (e.g., “Please respond ‘Strongly Agree’ to this question”). Participants who failed at least one attention check were excluded from the study. 
    55→Of the 343 individuals who started the survey, 332 were included in the final analysis (inclusion rate of 96.7%). Of the eleven respondents who started the survey but were excluded from the final analysis: two opened the survey but did not provide the informed consent and were not given the remainder of the survey, three failed at least one attention check, one fell outside the required age range, and five failed to provide a valid name for the university they attended (for instance, responding “Datacamp”). Further, of the 332 participants included in the final analyses, 71 did not provide responses on the exams passed and 89 did not provide responses on the Grade Point Average. The median survey completion time was 21.1 minutes.
    56→Statistical analyses
    57→                We used R (version 4.3.1; R Core Team, 2023) and JASP (version 0.17.3.0; JASP Team, 2019) statistical analysis packages to analyse the data. The study data is available on the Open Science Framework (OSF) at: https://osf.io/ht3fa/. 
    58→First, we examined how self-reported apathy and fatigue varied by demographic, household, or study-related factors. We used bivariate Pearson correlations to examine how apathy and fatigue correlated with continuous variables (e.g., age) and independent samples t-tests to compare how apathy and fatigue differed across the different levels of categorical variables (e.g., completing graduate vs. postgraduate studies). Next, we examined how apathy and fatigue separately relate to the measures of academic functioning using Pearson correlations. Finally, we used multiple linear regressions to compare how well apathy and fatigue predict the measures of academic functioning. We then assessed the equality of regression coefficients within each regression model using the approach outlined by Paternoster et al. (1998). Given the conceptual and statistical overlap between apathy and fatigue, we assessed multicollinearity. The estimates of Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) for apathy and fatigue were 1.03 – 1.04 across all models, well below the cutoff that would signal any risk of multicollinearity. 
    59→Throughout all our analyses, we controlled for multiple tests by applying corrections for false discovery rate (FDR) using the Linear Step Up procedure of Benjamini and Hochberg (1995) (α = .05). For instance, in the analyses examining the link between academic variables and apathy or fatigue, we corrected for 10 tests (corresponding to 10 different academic variables examined). Hence, for the most statistically significant association (i.e. the lowest p-value) the α was set to .005, for the second most statistically significant association α was set to .010 etc.

    67→The Prevalence of Apathy and Fatigue
    68→Apathy and fatigue scores were not related to most of the family, household, work, and study-related factors. After controlling for multiple tests, employment hours, having children, serving as a caregiver, and the amount of time devoted to home duties were not associated with either apathy or fatigue (Table 2). To account for potential differences in gender roles, we also calculated partial correlations for the variables Children, Caregiving, and Home Duties (Hours), controlling for gender – these analyses did not yield significant results either (see Supplementary Material 2). 
    69→Completed study duration, and the type of study delivery (remote vs in-person) were also not associated with either apathy or fatigue scores. After controlling for multiple tests, apathy was only associated with the level of study - students in postgraduate studies reported lower apathy scores than undergraduate students. Conversely, only gender and the type of enrolment were correlated to fatigue scores after correcting for multiple tests. Women reported higher fatigue scores than men, whereas students enrolled full-time exhibited higher fatigue scores than those enrolled part-time.

    73→Apathy And Fatigue Correlated with Diminished Academic Functioning
    74→Apathy and fatigue were separately associated with diminished academic functioning across a broad range of measures (Figure 1). Greater apathy was strongly correlated with lower study engagement and lower study satisfaction. Also notable were the associations between higher apathy scores and lower autonomous motivation, and lower study effort (rating). Finally, higher apathy scores were weakly correlated with lower controlled motivation, lower study effort (hours) as well as higher dropout intentions. 
    75→Higher fatigue scores showed a strong association with higher academic stress, moderate associations with lower study engagement and higher dropout intentions, and weak correlations with lower autonomous motivation, higher controlled motivation, lower study effort (rating), lower proportion of exams passed, and lower academic satisfaction.

    86→Discussion 
    87→A growing body of evidence shows how motivation evolves during the lifetime (Cutler et al., 2024; Katsantonis, 2025; Lockwood et al., 2021; Westbrook et al., 2013), yet middle adulthood is rarely investigated. In this study, we investigated how trait apathy and fatigue, both of which result in diminished motivation, relate to self-reported academic functioning among university students aged 30 – 50. We found that both apathy and fatigue were associated with almost all aspects of academic functioning – autonomous and controlled motivation, study engagement, study effort, dropout intentions, and academic satisfaction. The link of apathy and fatigue to the measures of academic performance, GPA and the percentage of exams passed was less clear. Further, academic stress was uniquely and strongly related to trait fatigue. Interestingly, trait apathy was more strongly correlated with academic variables, with the links to autonomous motivation, study engagement, and academic satisfaction being particularly noteworthy. This is notable, as unlike some previous studies examining the role of apathy in academic functioning (Hasanzadeh et al., 2024; Playfoot et al., 2024; Tamm et al., 2016), the measure of trait apathy has no items specific to academic functioning. Intriguingly, apathy and fatigue were largely independent of work and family related factors that are often identified as key challenges for older, non-traditional university students (Babb et al., 2021; Markle, 2015).
    88→Apathy Associated with Diminished Autonomous Motivation and Study Engagement
    89→We found that trait apathy was linked to reduced autonomous motivation and study engagement. Autonomous motivation is experienced as an intrinsic excitement and interest in academic activities or the pursuit of such activities as they help achieving personally important goals (Deci & Ryan, 2000; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Given that apathy is defined as a reduction in self-initiated behaviours and diminished satisfaction in previously enjoyable activities (Husain & Roiser, 2018; Robert et al., 2018), the clear link between apathy and autonomous motivation is not surprising. Nevertheless, it is noteworthy, as students who are more autonomously motivated study harder, employ more advanced cognitive strategies, and report greater academic satisfaction (Grolnick & Ryan, 1987; Kusurkar et al., 2013; Ryan & Deci, 2020; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Accordingly, we found that apathy was also associated with most measures of academic functioning. In contrast, the association between apathy and controlled motivation was far weaker. It has been shown that “high quality” motivation, i.e., the combination of high autonomous and low controlled motivation, is associated with the most favourable academic outcomes (Kusurkar et al., 2013; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Hence, trait apathy may serve as a risk factor for lower quality academic motivation.
    90→Why is apathy related to lower autonomous motivation? According to SDT, autonomous motivation is fostered by the social environment that satisfies the basic needs of autonomy, competence, and relatedness (Deci & Ryan, 2000; Ryan & Deci, 2020). We propose that higher apathy may limit a person’s capacity to satisfy basic needs. Apathy hinders the ability to make independent decisions and generate action options (Husain & Roiser, 2018)  – this may impair students’ capability to act autonomously. Trait apathy is also characterized by reduced emotional responsiveness to social interactions which may impair the ability to satisfy the need for connection (Ang et al., 2017; Husain & Roiser, 2018). Finally, by reducing the willingness to exert effort in pursuit of desired outcomes (Bonnelle et al., 2015; Jurgelis et al., 2021), apathy may reduce the capacity to satisfy the need for competence. Interventions targeting teaching practices have been shown to improve students’ need satisfaction and autonomous motivation (Chiu, 2022; Vasconcellos et al., 2020). If apathy hinders the capability to satisfy the basic needs, it may moderate the effectiveness of these interventions. Of course, given the cross-sectional nature of the current study we cannot rule out that the satisfaction of basic needs shapes trait apathy, especially given that the causal factors of apathy remain poorly understood (Bonnelle et al., 2015). 
    91→We also observed a strong negative association between apathy and study engagement, and only slightly weaker negative association between apathy and study effort (rating). This aligns with the recent studies showing that trait apathy reduces the willingness to exert effort in pursuit for rewarding outcomes (Bonnelle et al., 2016; Bonnelle et al., 2015; Jurgelis et al., 2021). 
    92→Apathy Associated with Lower Academic Satisfaction
    93→Our results showed a strong negative association between apathy and study satisfaction. This could reflect the diminished capacity to experience positive emotions and pleasure (anhedonia), often observed in apathy (Husain & Roiser, 2018). Alternatively, apathy may hinder the ability to successfully engage with the studies which in turn harms study satisfaction. Either way, the strong association between apathy and study satisfaction has important practical and scientific ramifications. Our results show that as a single factor, trait apathy may be amongst the strongest predictors of academic satisfaction (Eom & Ashill, 2016; Navarro et al., 2019). This finding is striking as unlike other factors explaining academic satisfaction (e.g., student-teacher interactions, course design), the Apathy Motivation Index probes general functioning and contains no items specific to academic endeavors, university characteristics, or study program (Ang et al., 2017; Eom & Ashill, 2016; Rahmatpour et al., 2021). Student satisfaction is often used as one of the measures of the quality of an academic program (Eom & Ashill, 2016; Rahmatpour et al., 2021). Given that trait apathy is a relatively stable personal predisposition, our findings suggest that to an extent, students’ academic satisfaction may be outside the control of universities administering study programs. Further, from a research standpoint, future studies examining predictors of study satisfaction may decide to control for trait apathy as a potential confounder. 
    94→Higher Fatigue Linked to Higher Academic Stress
    95→We found that fatigue was uniquely and strongly linked to higher academic stress. This replicates a well-established association. Academic stress has been linked not only to trait fatigue but also other related concepts including academic burnout and momentary fatigue (Doerr et al., 2015; Jiang et al., 2021). Nevertheless, this result is interesting as previous studies typically looked at younger university students or even school students (Doerr et al., 2015; Laberge et al., 2011; Tanaka et al., 2009). Still, our finding of the strong fatigue – stress link aligns with findings in younger student populations suggesting that age does not play a major role in this association. The strong correlation may also in part be explained by the findings that the link between fatigue and stress is bi-directional (Doerr et al., 2015). Fatigue results in heightened effort perception (Marcora & Staiano, 2010; Pageaux et al., 2015) and reduced willingness to exert effort (Jurgelis et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2021), also evidenced by our findings, which in turn can make it more difficult to successfully pursue studies and contribute to stress. Conversely, stress impairs sleep quality thus exacerbating fatigue (Carlson & Garland, 2005; Doerr et al., 2015). The fact that fatigue was associated with higher academic stress, as well as reduced study engagement, may also explain our finding that fatigue was correlated with higher intention to drop out.
    96→Apathy and Fatigue Scores Could not be Explained by Family and Work Factors
    97→Intriguingly, our results showed that apathy and fatigue were largely independent of family, household, and work-related factors including the number of children, other caregiving responsibilities, hours spent in household duties, and employment hours. This is noteworthy, as the conflict between academic demands and family or professional obligations is often identified as a key challenge for non-traditional university students (Augustine et al., 2018; Babb et al., 2021; Deutsch & Schmertz, 2011). We also ruled out the possibility that gender accounts for these associations. Despite being commonly brought up by non-traditional students, it appears that the link between identity conflict and diminished motivation is relatively weak and cannot be replicated in every study. For instance, Augustine et al. (2018) found that student mothers reported more fatigue in their daily activities than non-student mothers. However, this difference was relatively small, and the study benefited from a sample even larger than ours. Whereas Fiorilli et al. (2022) found no differences in burnout levels between working students and non-working students.
    98→It is possible that while experienced as salient by non-traditional students, identity conflict has limited impact due to the higher resilience and coping skills of non-traditional students. For instance, Babb et al. (2021) found that the more non-traditional student characteristics student displayed (e.g., working full-time, having dependents, being 25 years or older) the more resilience in the face of COVID lockdowns they displayed. Hence, non-traditional university students may only be taking on the challenges they feel capable of tackling. Alternatively, identity conflict may affect academic functioning without hurting the overall motivation, for instance, by reducing the time available for studying. 
    99→We also observed that apathy and fatigue scores were largely unrelated to course-related factors, including completed study duration and the mode of learning (online vs in-person). Previously, being further along one’s studies has been linked to higher burnout and emotional exhaustion among traditional university students (Liu et al., 2023; Nteveros et al., 2020; Turhan et al., 2023). The fact that we did not replicate this association may be explained by the higher resilience of non-traditional university students (Babb et al., 2021). However, we found that students in full time studies reported higher fatigue scores than part-time students - this may be the result of higher academic workload when studying full-time. Further, apathy was related to the study level, with postgraduate students reporting lower apathy scores than those in undergraduate studies. This may simply reflect higher underlying motivation among those who continue to pursue postgraduate studies. 
   100→Finally, we found that women reported higher fatigue scores, thus replicating a well-established gender difference both within academia and the general population (Bensing et al., 1999; Chen, 1986; Fiorilli et al., 2022). The links between age and apathy and fatigue were not statistically significant, which is not surprising given the restricted age range in our study. 
   101→Study Limitations and Future Directions
   102→It remains to be determined to what extent our novel findings will generalise to older and younger students. Our focus on students aged 30 – 50 was justified given that this group faces a different set of challenges than younger cohorts (Arnett et al., 2014; Babb et al., 2021), and the fact that motivation tends to evolve across the lifespan (Cutler et al., 2024; Lockwood et al., 2021). Nevertheless, future studies examining academic motivation, regardless of the age group examined, may benefit from an inclusion of measures of trait apathy and fatigue. 
   103→Given the cross-sectional nature of our study, no causal inferences can be made about the observed associations. In the future, longitudinal designs could help to clarify the causal links between demographic and work-related factors, trait apathy and fatigue, and academic functioning. Furthermore, we relied solely on self-report measures. Self-report measures are useful as they can capture inherently subjective experiences, such as personal reasons for studying or the sense of exhaustion associated with fatigue (Fisk et al., 1994; Ritvo et al., 1997; Vansteenkiste et al., 2009). Nevertheless, the exclusive use of self-report measures may result in an overestimate of the associations, given the common method variance, especially when the variables are measured at a single time-point. Using teacher and peer ratings of study engagement (e.g., Gleason et al., 2007), and longitudinal diary methods (e.g., Beymer & Robinson, 2022) could help to assess the robustness of our findings. Moreover, effort-based decision-making tasks recently emerged as a useful tool to index motivation (e.g., Westbrook et al., 2020). Future studies may consider using self-report measures in conjunction with effort-based decision-making tasks to further explore academic motivation and performance. Finally, GPA and exam performance were self-reported and harmonized across multiple national grading systems, which introduces significant measurement error. Future studies would benefit from acquiring student grade records directly from the universities (e.g., Kusurkar et al., 2013). 
   104→Conclusion
   105→The main conclusion of our study is that apathy and fatigue, two relatively stable predispositions that manifest in diminished motivation and reduced goal-directed activity, were associated with poorer self-reported academic functioning among students aged 30 – 50, an age group that has been often overlooked in extant literature.
