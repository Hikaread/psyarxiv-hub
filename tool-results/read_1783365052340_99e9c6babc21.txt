    19→Abstract
    20→Objective: Although the phenomenology of PTSD has been well documented, its underlying
    21→mechanisms remain only partially understood. Predictive processing accounts propose that
    22→danger-related beliefs formed during trauma may become resistant to change, thereby
    23→maintaining a sense of threat and contributing to PTSD symptoms even in objectively safe
    24→environments. The present experimental study examined the influence of a trauma-analogue
    25→stressor on updating of danger-related expectations and its association with analogue intrusive
    26→memories and resilience factors.
    27→Method: Healthy participants (N = 129) were randomly assigned to watch either a trauma
    28→film or a neutral control film. Belief updating was assessed using a trauma-adapted Bias
    29→Against Disconfirmatory Evidence (TR-BADE) task. Analogue PTSD symptoms were
    30→measured via an intrusion diary. In exploratory analyses, we decomposed the preregistered
    31→TR-BADE score into Negative Interpretation Bias (NIB) and Inflexible Interpretation
    32→Updating (IIU).
    33→Results:
    34→Contrary to the preregistered hypothesis, the trauma and neutral film groups did not differ in
    35→TR-BADE scores, NIB, or IIU. Within the trauma-film group, higher TR-BADE scores were
    36→positively associated with diary intrusions. NIB, but not IIU, predicted diary intrusions and
    37→was associated with lower Sense of Coherence (SoC). Mediation analyses further showed a
    38→significant indirect effect of SoC on diary intrusions via NIB.
    39→Conclusions:
    40→The results suggest that it is not the belief updating process per se that is involved in the
    41→formation of intrusive memories, but rather the extent to which negative, danger-related
    42→expectations form in the first place. Developing such expectations may mediate the link
    43→between SoC and analogue intrusive memories.
    44→Keywords: analogue trauma, belief updating, negative interpretation bias, predictive
    45→processing, posttraumatic stress disorder symptoms

    49→Exposure to potentially traumatic events is common across the globe (Kessler et al.,
    50→2017). In the World Mental Health Survey, 70.4% of respondents indicated that they had
    51→experienced at least one traumatic event during their lifetime (e.g. unexpected death of a
    52→loved one, witnessing death or serious injuries; Benjet et al., 2016). Surveys show that
    53→although exposure to potentially traumatic events is common, only a minority of individuals
    54→develop posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD; Koenen et al., 2017; Santiago et al., 2013).
    55→According to DSM-5, PTSD is characterized by re-experiencing symptoms such as intrusive
    56→memories, flashbacks, or nightmares (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). These
    57→intrusions typically consist of vivid sensory and emotional fragments of the trauma that arise
    58→spontaneously and can be triggered by internal or external stimuli, often outside of conscious
    59→control (Brewin, 2015; Krans et al., 2009a). In addition, the disorder is characterized by
    60→persistent avoidance of trauma-related thoughts, feelings, or reminders. The symptom profile
    61→further comprises negative alterations in cognition and mood (e.g., distorted beliefs,
    62→detachment, loss of positive affect) as well as hyperarousal and reactivity (e.g., irritability,
    63→hypervigilance, sleep disturbances; American Psychiatric Association, 2013; Ehlers & Clark,
    64→2000; Weston, 2014).
    65→Although the phenomenology of PTSD has been well documented, its underlying
    66→mechanisms remain only partially understood. The fact that not all individuals exposed to
    67→traumatic events develop PTSD symptoms raises the question of interindividual differences in
    68→vulnerability, suggesting that risk is determined not only by the trauma itself but also by how
    69→it is cognitively and perceptually processed afterwards (Brewin, 2014; Ehlers & Clark, 2000).
    70→Building on this perspective, recent theoretical approaches propose that trauma-related beliefs
    71→play a central role in the development and maintenance of PTSD (Kube et al., 2020;
    72→Wilkinson et al., 2017). Predictive processing has emerged as a popular theory in
    73→neuroscience, proposing that the human brain functions as an inference machine that
    74→continuously generates situation-specific expectations and updates these when confronted
    75→3
    76→
    77→4
    78→with new information (Clark, 2013; Friston, 2005, 2010). Expectations are formed on the
    79→basis of internal models - referred to as beliefs - which are in turn shaped by prior
    80→experiences. The information processing can be described as a hierarchical exchange between
    81→belief-based predictions and incoming sensory input. While higher levels of the hierarchy
    82→encode predictions derived from the current belief system, lower levels convey the actual
    83→sensory information arriving from the environment (Mesulam, 2008). This comparison allows
    84→the brain to detect mismatches between what was expected and what was perceived. These
    85→mismatches are referred to as prediction errors (Friston, 2010; Kube et al., 2020). Prediction
    86→errors play a crucial role in maintaining an accurate and adaptive internal belief system. When
    87→a prediction error occurs, it serves as feedback, signaling that the current model may need
    88→adjustment. Through this process, the brain continuously updates its predictions, enabling
    89→flexible learning and perception. However, not all prediction errors are treated equally: Their
    90→impact on updating depends on precision, which represents the estimated reliability or
    91→confidence assigned to either the prediction or the sensory evidence (O’Reilly et al., 2012;
    92→Paulus et al., 2019). If the precision of the sensory input is high, the internal model is readily
    93→adjusted in light of new information. Conversely, when prior beliefs are assigned excessive
    94→precision, incoming evidence is discounted, and the brain continues to rely on outdated or
    95→inaccurate expectations (Corlett et al., 2020).
    96→The predictive processing framework has recently been used to explain how PTSD
    97→symptoms may emerge after a potentially traumatic event (Kube et al., 2020; Wilkinson et al.,
    98→2017). The central assumption of these accounts is that traumatic events result in the encoding
    99→of danger-related hypotheses with very high precision. High precision is assumed to result
   100→since trauma-related beliefs are formed in life-threatening contexts, making them highly
   101→salient and existentially important and therefore more resistant to change. Consequently, even
   102→when individuals are later confronted with neutral or safe environments, these experiences are
   103→not weighted strongly enough to correct preexisting danger-related hypotheses. As a
   104→4
   105→
   106→5
   107→consequence, prediction errors that could signal safety are effectively ignored, preventing
   108→successful updating and leading the individual to persistently perceive the environment as
   109→threatening (Linson & Friston, 2019). This distorted weighting of precision and the
   110→persistence of trauma-related beliefs may explain the development of PTSD symptoms such
   111→as hypervigilance, avoidance, and intrusive re-experiencing (Brewin, 2014; Ehlers & Clark,
   112→2000). Therefore, Kube et al. (2020) conceptualize PTSD as a disorder of disrupted belief
   113→updating within a predictive processing framework. In this conceptualization, it is not
   114→regarded as central that people hold negative beliefs after trauma, but that these beliefs are
   115→unusually rigid and insufficiently updated when new information becomes available. This idea
   116→raises the question whether individuals who are able to adjust danger-related beliefs in light of
   117→disconfirmatory, safety-related information are less likely to develop PTSD symptoms than
   118→those whose beliefs remain stable despite such evidence (Kube et al., 2020).
   119→Empirical studies examining this question still remain limited. Sachschal et al. (2019)
   120→investigated how people with PTSD and a non-traumatized control group changed their
   121→impressions of strangers when they received positive versus negative information. Participants
   122→with PTSD were more inclined to revise their beliefs downward after negative feedback than
   123→to adjust them upward after positive feedback, whereas controls updated in a more balanced
   124→way. In a scenario-based study using the Trauma-related Bias Against Disconfirmatory
   125→Evidence (TR-BADE) paradigm, Haim-Nachum et al. (2024) asked participants with and
   126→without previous exposure to potentially traumatic events to imagine ambiguous situations
   127→that developed either towards a safe or dangerous ending. As the scenarios unfolded,
   128→participants repeatedly rated how plausible danger-related and neutral interpretations
   129→appeared. In both disconfirming-danger and disconfirming-safety scenario types, higher
   130→PTSD symptoms predicted reduced updating, whereas trauma exposure itself did not predict
   131→updating, even in the larger combined sample. Extending this line of research, Sopp et al.
   132→(2025) adapted the TR-BADE paradigm with a large sample of high-risk professionals and
   133→5
   134→
   135→6
   136→demonstrated that cumulative trauma exposure did not relate to interpretation updating, but
   137→that both negative interpretation bias and inflexible interpretation updating predicted PTSD
   138→symptom severity. Inflexible interpretation updating further moderated the association
   139→between trauma exposure and PTSD symptoms. Overall, these cross-sectional studies suggest
   140→that danger-related interpretations are more closely linked to PTSD symptom expression than
   141→to trauma exposure itself. The role of less flexible updating may be particularly relevant under
   142→conditions of repeated exposure. Haim-Nachum et al. (2024) explicitly noted that their cross-
   143→sectional design precludes causal inferences regarding whether altered belief updating
   144→contributes to PTSD symptom severity or instead reflects a consequence of symptoms, and
   145→they therefore called for prospective research. A similar limitation applies to Sopp et al.
   146→(2025) where the correlational design did not allow conclusions about the temporal ordering
   147→of updating inflexibility and PTSD symptoms, nor about whether updating can be altered by
   148→trauma-related stressors. To address these gaps, the present study was designed to extend the
   149→literature in an experimental analogue-trauma setting. Using the trauma film paradigm, we
   150→induced short-term trauma analogue reactions in healthy participants and assessed the
   151→following day how they updated danger-related interpretations in a trauma-adapted (TR-)
   152→BADE task that closely matched the film content. In a control group, participants viewed a
   153→matched neutral film compilation depicting comparable settings and thematic contexts but
   154→without traumatic content.
   155→Our primary aim was to examine the prospective influence of exposure to a trauma
   156→analogue stressor on updating danger-related expectations, and its association with analogue
   157→intrusive memories and PTSD symptoms. Specifically, we hypothesized that (H1) exposure to
   158→the trauma film (vs. a neutral control film) would reduce updating of danger-related
   159→expectations, and that (H2) within the trauma-film group, the extent of updating would be
   160→associated with the intensity of analogue intrusive memories.

   164→Building on the framework that emphasizes not only insufficient updating of danger-
   165→related beliefs in the face of disconfirmatory evidence but also the tendency to endorse
   166→negative interpretations in the first place, we further conducted exploratory analyses targeting
   167→these two components more directly. Specifically, we decomposed the preregistered TR-
   168→BADE score into two distinct components: Negative Interpretation Bias (NIB), reflecting the
   169→initial tendency to favor danger-related interpretations, and Inflexible Interpretation Updating
   170→(IIU), reflecting the failure to update these interpretations in light of disconfirmatory
   171→information. Building on this rationale, we explored whether these components of danger-
   172→related interpretation processing related to analogue PTSD-symptoms/intrusive memories.
   173→Finally, to strengthen the theoretical integration of the predictive processing
   174→framework with resilience research, we explored whether individual differences in NIB and
   175→IIU are associated with established resilience factors (trait resilience, Sense of Coherence,
   176→Locus of Control) and, in turn, with analogue intrusive memories. More specifically, we
   177→examined whether a bias toward danger-related interpretations and their revision in light of
   178→disconfirmatory evidence may represent a proximal resilience mechanism that helps explain
   179→how broader resilience-related resources translate into symptom outcomes. This aligns with
   180→resilience frameworks (Kalisch et al., 2015) proposing that protective factors exert their
   181→effects via shared proximal cognitive mechanisms (e.g., appraisal tendencies shaping the
   182→interpretation of stress-related information). To this end, we examined associations between
   183→well-established resilience factors with danger-related interpretation bias and inflexible
   184→interpretation updating and explored whether these processes mediate the link between
   185→resilience resources and analogue intrusive memories following trauma.

   251→Film presentation
   252→In order to investigate how exposure to trauma influences belief updating, we used the
   253→Trauma-Film Paradigm (TFP). This procedure involves presenting participants with a
   254→compilation of highly distressing film scenes, which reliably induces analogue trauma
   255→responses. Such responses typically include short-term increases in negative affect, stress,
   256→intrusive memories, anxiety, and avoidance behavior (Holmes & Bourne, 2008; James et al.,
   257→2016; Krans et al., 2009b). The paradigm enables the examination of trauma-like reactions in
   258→a controlled and standardized laboratory setting without causing enduring harm. It is therefore
   259→widely recognized as a valid method to investigate trauma-related processes (Holmes &
   260→Bourne, 2008; James et al., 2016).
   261→Individuals were randomly assigned to one of two groups. Both groups watched a ~20-
   262→minute film consisting of five sequences of approximately four minutes each. In the trauma
   263→condition the film sequences contained explicit portrayals of interpersonal or accidental
   264→trauma, adapted from commercial movies. In contrast, the neutral condition comprised five

   269→included a nightclub scene depicting a violent altercation with explicit injuries, whereas the
   270→neutral film showed a girl dancing in a nightclub. To minimize order effects, the five

   274→TR-BADE Task
   275→Belief updating was assessed using a novel version of the Trauma-related Bias Against
   276→Disconfirmatory Evidence Task (TR-BADE-Task; Woodward et al., 2006; Everaert et al.,
   277→2018). For the current study, we designed a version of the task, resembling sequences from
   278→the previously presented films (e.g., a night club scene). Participants were instructed to
   279→imagine the scenarios as if they were personally experiencing them. Each scenario consisted
   280→of three consecutive pieces of information, followed by several possible interpretations of the
   281→situation. Scenarios initially suggested potential threat to oneself or significant others. In
   282→disconfirming-danger scenarios, the first two statements suggested a threatening outcome,
   283→while the final statement pointed towards a neutral outcome (see Figure 2). After each
   284→statement, participants rated four possible interpretations: one true interpretation, one absurd
   285→interpretation, and two lure interpretations. The absurd option remained implausible
   286→throughout the scenario. Lures appeared plausible during the initial stages of the scenarios but
   287→suddenly became implausible when the final (neutral) information was presented. In contrast,
   288→the true interpretation was initially rather implausible but became the most plausible
   289→explanation after the final statement. Plausibility ratings were provided on a visual analogue
   290→scale ranging from 0 (“poor”) to 100 (“excellent”). The task included five disconfirming-
   291→danger scenarios, which required updating an initially threatening interpretation in light of
   292→neutral evidence, and three confirming-danger scenarios, in which the threatening
   293→interpretation remained plausible throughout the scenario. The latter served as filler trials,
   294→11
   295→
   296→12
   297→aiming to prevent participants from gaining explicit insights into the predictable structure of
   298→the scenarios, and were thus not included in the analyses. All scenarios and response options
   299→were presented in randomized order.
   300→In the preregistration, the TR-BADE score was registered as the main outcome,
   301→reflecting the mean reduction in plausibility ratings of interpretations from Statement 2 to
   302→Statement 3 across all disconfirming-danger scenarios (Woodward et al., 2006). This score is
   303→intended to reflect flexible belief updating in response to disconfirmatory evidence. However,
   304→in our previous study (Sopp et al., 2025), we encountered a critical limitation of this
   305→composite score: It conflates two distinct cognitive processes - a general bias toward negative
   306→interpretations and a lack of updating flexibility. Specifically, participants with a stronger
   307→initial negative bias (i.e., higher plausibility ratings of negative interpretations at Statement 2)
   308→may exhibit greater change scores without reflecting their genuine updating ability. To
   309→address this issue, we followed Sopp et al. (2025) by applying the principal component
   310→analysis (PCA) procedure recommended by Everaert et al. (2018) to extract two distinct
   311→indices for use alongside the preregistered TR-BADE score.
   312→(1) Negative Interpretation Bias (NIB) was computed as the mean plausibility rating
   313→of danger-related lure interpretations after Statement 2, capturing a general tendency to favor
   314→danger-related interpretations.
   315→(2) Inflexible Interpretation Updating (IIU) was computed as the average reduction in
   316→the lure ratings from Statement 2 to the neutral Statement 3.
   317→All analyses involving NIB and IIU were not part of the preregistered plan and are
   318→thus reported as exploratory.

   392→Data analysis
   393→All analyses were conducted using IBM SPSS Statistics 30. The Type I error level was set to
   394→α = .05 (two-tailed) for all analyses. In addition, assumptions for parametric tests (normality,
   395→homogeneity of variance, and linearity for regression models) were examined.
   396→To test Hypothesis #1, namely that exposure to the trauma film (vs. neutral film) would
   397→reduce updating of danger-related expectations, we compared the trauma and neutral film
   398→groups on the TR-BADE score using an independent-samples t-test. Effect sizes for group
   399→differences are reported as Cohen’s d with 95% confidence intervals. To test Hypothesis #2,
   400→15
   401→
   402→16
   403→which predicted that reduced belief updating in the trauma film group would be associated
   404→with more intense analogue intrusive memories, analyses were restricted to participants in the
   405→trauma condition. We ran separate linear regression analyses with the intrusion indices (diary
   406→intrusion index and IPT intrusion index) as dependent variables and the TR-BADE score as
   407→predictor. For each model, we report standardized regression coefficients (β), corresponding t-
   408→values, and adjusted R² as an index of explained variance.
   409→In exploratory follow-up analyses, we repeated the main tests of Hypotheses 1 and 2
   410→using IIU and NIB instead of the TR-BADE score in order to differentiate the effects of
   411→negative interpretation bias and interpretation inflexibility in joint analyses. Furthermore, we
   412→explored associations between Trait Resilience, Sense of Coherence, and Locus of Control
   413→and outcomes of the TR-BADE task (IIU, NIB), as well as analogue intrusive memories using
   414→Pearson product–moment correlations. In addition, we conducted an exploratory mediation
   415→analysis using PROCESS (Model 4, 5,000 bootstrap samples) to test whether NIB mediates
   416→the association between Sense of Coherence and diary intrusions.
   417→Results
   418→Manipulation Check
   419→To test whether the trauma film elicited the intended increase in state anxiety, we
   420→conducted a mixed-design ANOVA with time (pre vs. post trauma film presentation) as a
   421→within-subject factor and group (trauma vs. neutral) as a between-subject factor. The analysis

   531→Discussion
   532→This study examined the impact of exposure to an analogue traumatic stressor on
   533→belief updating and intrusive memories. Specifically, we investigated whether exposure to an
   534→analogue trauma would reduce belief updating, and whether reduced updating would be
   535→associated with more intense intrusive memories. In addition, we explored whether belief
   536→updating may act as a proximal mechanism linking general resilience factors with analogue
   537→PTSD symptoms.
   538→Contrary to our preregistered hypotheses, belief updating (as indexed by the TR-
   539→BADE score) was not impaired by exposure to the trauma film. Accordingly, our results align
   540→with previous findings (Haim-Nachum et al., 2024; Sopp et al., 2025), suggesting that trauma
   541→exposure itself may not necessarily elicit systematic impairments in belief updating. Belief
   542→updating tendencies may rather represent relatively stable cognitive styles that are not easily
   543→21
   544→
   545→22
   546→shifted by a single analogue stressor in a healthy population. It is also possible that, although
   547→the trauma film paradigm reliably elicited intrusive memories, it did not capture the intensity,
   548→personal threat, or repeated exposure that characterize real-life trauma and may be necessary
   549→to shift danger-related beliefs as proposed by predictive-processing accounts. Taken together,
   550→these findings highlight the need for longitudinal field research in trauma-exposed individuals
   551→to test whether changes in belief updating precede, follow, or maintain posttraumatic
   552→symptoms.
   553→Our second hypothesis concerned the association between belief updating and
   554→analogue intrusive memories. We had expected that reduced belief updating would be
   555→associated with more intrusive memories in the trauma-film group. By contrast, the
   556→preregistered analyses indicated that higher TR-BADE scores were related to a higher diary
   557→intrusion index, while TR-BADE scores did not predict intrusive memories in the IPT. At first
   558→glance, this appears inconsistent with the assumption that flexible updating should act as a
   559→protective factor against trauma-related symptoms. However, follow-up analyses revealed that
   560→the positive association between TR-BADE and diary intrusions likely originated from an
   561→underlying association between NIB and diary intrusions. Examining separate indices, we
   562→found that a higher NIB was significantly associated with a higher diary intrusion index,
   563→whereas IIU showed no significant association with either diary or IPT intrusions. Taken
   564→together, these results suggest that a general tendency to interpret ambiguous situations as
   565→threatening seems to be more closely linked to analogue intrusive memories than the failure to
   566→update danger-related hypotheses once disconfirmatory information is available.
   567→These findings align with the results of previous research using the TR-BADE
   568→paradigm. In particular, Sopp et al. (2025) reported stronger and more consistent associations
   569→between NIB and trauma-related symptoms than for IIU, suggesting that a tendency to
   570→prioritize threatening interpretations of ambiguous information may be more closely tied to
   571→symptom expression than updating per se.
   572→22
   573→
   574→23
   575→At the etiology model level, these findings provide limited support for predictive
   576→processing accounts suggesting that belief updating is a key mechanism in PTSD. Instead,
   577→they are more consistent with established cognitive models that highlight the role of threat-
   578→focused appraisals in generating a persistent sense of current threat, thereby maintaining
   579→posttraumatic stress symptoms (Brewin, 2014; Ehlers & Clark, 2000). In this view, NIB may
   580→capture a proximal cognitive vulnerability that maps onto appraisal-based mechanisms,
   581→whereas IIU may play a more limited or context dependent role.
   582→One possibility is that updating inflexibility may only become clinically relevant once
   583→danger-related appraisals and trauma-memory networks have been repeatedly activated over
   584→time. In other words, inflexible interpretation updating may become more detectable after
   585→repeated activation and consolidation over time, which may explain why a single analogue
   586→trauma is insufficient to reveal robust updating deficits. Accordingly, such effects may be
   587→more evident in chronic PTSD, where symptoms are repeatedly triggered and maintained
   588→across time. In chronic PTSD, persistent re-experiencing and hypervigilance are thought to be
   589→maintained by a persistent sense of current threat, such that the environment continues to be
   590→perceived as dangerous even in objectively safe contexts (Leone et al., 2022). Within such a
   591→threat-focused context, re-experiencing, avoidance, and hypervigilance may repeatedly cue
   592→danger-confirming interpretations, and thereby strengthen threat-related expectations and
   593→stabilize them against corrective, safety-consistent evidence. Under these conditions,
   594→information that signals safety may be given less weight, so inflexible updating may function
   595→as a maintenance mechanism rather than an immediate impairment following trauma. This
   596→would imply that IIU may be more detectable in samples marked by sustained or repeated
   597→trauma exposure. This possibility should be explored further by future research.
   598→Guided by resilience frameworks positing that broad resilience resources operate
   599→through cognitive mechanisms (Kalisch et al., 2015), we explored whether NIB and IIU may
   600→constitute such mechanisms. Specifically, we examined associations of NIB/IIU with
   601→23
   602→
   603→24
   604→established resilience factors (trait Resilience, Sense of Coherence, Locus of Control) and
   605→with analogue intrusive memories. In line with previous research demonstrating that NIB is
   606→associated with PTSD symptoms (Sopp et al., 2025), our findings extend this relationship to
   607→intrusive memories specifically. We found that NIB was linked to intrusive memories as well
   608→as SoC. Moreover, our mediation analysis showed that the indirect effect from SoC to
   609→intrusions via NIB was significant, whereas the direct effect was not. This pattern is consistent
   610→with the idea that individuals who perceive their environment as comprehensible and
   611→meaningful are less likely to adopt strongly threat-focused interpretations, which may reduce
   612→the occurrence of intrusive memories after an analogue traumatic stressor. In other words,
   613→SoC might protect against intrusions at least partly by buffering NIB. In line with this
   614→interpretation, studies show that SoC may protect mental health by shaping how people
   615→interpret stressful experiences. For example, in motor-vehicle accident survivors, SoC
   616→predicted later PTSD symptom severity indirectly via negative posttraumatic cognitions
   617→(Kaźmierczak et al., 2016). This suggests that individuals with higher SoC may be less likely
   618→to develop and maintain strongly threat-focused appraisals and negative meanings about
   619→themselves over time. This interpretation aligns with resilience frameworks proposing that
   620→heterogeneous resilience factors converge on shared proximal mechanisms, such as appraisal
   621→and interpretation processes, that shape how stress-related information is processed (Kalisch
   622→et al., 2015).
   623→The present study provides an important methodological extension of previous
   624→research by using an ecologically valid analogue-trauma paradigm with a direct trauma-
   625→versus-neutral comparison rather than questionnaire-based approaches, allowing a more
   626→differentiated distinction between an initial bias toward danger-related interpretations and
   627→inflexible interpretation updating in response to disconfirmatory evidence.
   628→Although the present findings are preliminary, they provide some theoretical
   629→implications. First, they suggest that, in an analogue trauma context, NIB may be more
   630→24
   631→
   632→25
   633→closely tied to analogue PTSD symptoms than IIU itself. While this finding does not
   634→contradict predictive-processing models of PTSD, it aligns more closely with established
   635→cognitive accounts emphasizing danger-related appraisals and interpretation biases, rather
   636→than a primary updating deficit. Furthermore, our results support the notion that resilience-
   637→related factors are not only associated with better psychological outcomes in general but may
   638→exert their effects through negative interpretation bias in the aftermath of adverse events.
   639→Limitations
   640→The study has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the
   641→findings. First, the study used an analogue trauma paradigm in a healthy, predominantly
   642→student sample, which limits the generalizability to individuals with clinical PTSD or
   643→(repeated) real-life trauma exposure. Future studies should therefore aim to recruit more
   644→heterogeneous samples, including individuals with substantial trauma histories and higher
   645→symptom severity.
   646→Second, potential participants were informed in advance that aversive film material
   647→would be presented, which may have led to self-selection of individuals who felt confident in
   648→their ability to tolerate such content and may therefore have attenuated any effects of the
   649→analogue trauma on belief updating. Although the trauma film paradigm is a well-established
   650→method to induce analogue trauma responses under controlled conditions. At the same time,
   651→all participants were informed in advance that aversive film material would be presented,
   652→which may have led to self-selection of individuals who felt confident in their ability to
   653→tolerate such content and may therefore have attenuated any effects of the analogue trauma on
   654→belief updating. Although the trauma film paradigm is a well-established method to induce
   655→analogue trauma responses under controlled conditions.
   656→Third, the TR-BADE task itself comes with methodological limitations. The scenarios
   657→were designed to approximate trauma-related themes and to resemble the film sequences, but
   658→they nevertheless remain hypothetical scenarios. Their effectiveness depends on participants’
   659→25
   660→
   661→26
   662→ability and willingness to imagine themselves in the described situations and to identify with
   663→the unfolding scenario. It is therefore uncertain to what degree the observed interpretation
   664→patterns reflect how people would update beliefs in real life. At the same time, the TR-BADE
   665→paradigm is a well-established and process-oriented measure that captures interpretation
   666→dynamics and has been successfully applied in various contexts (Everaert et al., 2018).
   667→Finally, analyses involving NIB, IIU, and resilience-related variables were exploratory
   668→and only registered as such. Moreover, effect sizes were mostly in the small-to-moderate
   669→range. Replication in independent samples, including clinical populations, is thus needed to
   670→clarify the robustness and scope of our findings.
   671→Conclusion
   672→In sum, this study did not provide evidence that analogue traumatic stress directly
   673→disrupts belief updating. Instead, the results highlight negative interpretation bias as a
   674→potential pathway linking general resilience factors - specifically Sense of Coherence - and
   675→analogue PTSD symptoms. Future research should examine whether similar patterns emerge
   676→in clinical samples and whether interventions that reduce negative interpretation bias can
   677→support symptom reduction after trauma.
