    50‚Üí    50‚Üí    50‚ÜíSelf-control is essential for achieving long-term goals, maintaining healthy habits, and
    51‚Üí    51‚Üí    51‚Üínavigating daily responsibilities. It enables people to resist immediate temptations in favor of
    52‚Üí    52‚Üí    52‚Üímore meaningful long-term outcomes, regardless of whether that means sticking to a diet,
    53‚Üí    53‚Üí    53‚Üístaying focused at work, or managing personal finances effectively. Self-control refers to the
    54‚Üí    54‚Üí    54‚Üíability to override one‚Äôs inner responses, as well as to interrupt undesired behavioral
    55‚Üí    55‚Üí    55‚Üítendencies (such as impulses) and refrain from acting on them (Hofmann et al., 2014;
    56‚Üí    56‚Üí    56‚ÜíTangney et al., 2004). Previous research has shown that higher trait self-control is
    57‚Üí    57‚Üí    57‚Üíconsistently linked to diverse domains, including better academic performance, healthier
    58‚Üí    58‚Üí    58‚Üílifestyles, better relationships, and greater well-being (De Ridder et al., 2012). However,
    59‚Üí    59‚Üí    59‚Üíemerging research suggests that beyond this stable trait, people‚Äôs beliefs about self-control
    60‚Üí    60‚Üí    60‚Üímay also play a role in shaping their self-regulatory success.
    61‚Üí    61‚Üí    61‚Üí
    62‚Üí    62‚Üí    62‚ÜíSELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 4
    63‚Üí    63‚Üí    63‚ÜíBeliefs ‚Äì people‚Äôs ideas and thoughts about the nature of self-control and how it
    64‚Üí    64‚Üí    64‚Üíoperates ‚Äì have been shown to be key determinants for behavior in many contexts. For
    65‚Üí    65‚Üí    65‚Üíinstance, beliefs about one‚Äôs self-control capacity can influence the choice for healthy
    66‚Üí    66‚Üí    66‚Üíbehaviors (Hagger, 2025), and whether effort is believed to be enjoyable can affect the level
    67‚Üí    67‚Üí    67‚Üíof effort individuals are willing to devote to their tasks (Mlynski et al., 2025). Beliefs about
    68‚Üí    68‚Üí    68‚Üíself-control may influence how individuals approach self-control tasks, such as the valence
    69‚Üí    69‚Üí    69‚Üíthey associate with the task (e.g., whether positive or negative) and the emotions they
    70‚Üí    70‚Üí    70‚Üíexperience (e.g., whether pleasant or unpleasant). Additionally, self-control beliefs can
    71‚Üí    71‚Üí    71‚Üíinfluence the way individuals respond to self-control challenges, such as in determining
    72‚Üí    72‚Üí    72‚Üíwhich strategies to use and whether to rely on willpower of the individuals. Prior research has
    73‚Üí    73‚Üí    73‚Üílargely focused on implicit beliefs about self-control, particularly whether people consider
    74‚Üí    74‚Üí    74‚Üíself-control as a limited resource (versus non-limited) (Job et al., 2010) or a fixed trait (versus
    75‚Üí    75‚Üí    75‚Üía malleable skill) (Mukhopadhyay & Johar, 2005). These self-control beliefs have been
    76‚Üí    76‚Üí    76‚Üíshown to be associated with greater persistence on challenging tasks, healthier behaviors,
    77‚Üí    77‚Üí    77‚Üíhigher academic achievement, lower procrastination, stronger relationships, and more pro-
    78‚Üí    78‚Üí    78‚Üíenvironmental behaviors (Bernecker & Job, 2020; Di Maio et al., 2020; Francis et al., 2023;
    79‚Üí    79‚Üí    79‚ÜíHagger et al., 2019; Jankowski & Job, 2023; Job et al., 2010, 2015; Mukhopadhyay & Johar,
    80‚Üí    80‚Üí    80‚Üí2005).
    81‚Üí    81‚Üí    81‚ÜíBeyond Implicit Theories: A Broader Set of Self-Control Beliefs
    82‚Üí    82‚Üí    82‚ÜíRecent work has identified a broader set of lay beliefs about self-control, expanding
    83‚Üí    83‚Üí    83‚Üíbeyond the implicit limited or malleable dimensions. These include beliefs about the
    84‚Üí    84‚Üí    84‚Üístrategic, moral, pleasant, and success-related nature of self-control (Berm√∫dez et al., 2023a;
    85‚Üí    85‚Üí    85‚ÜíGennara et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025; Murray et al., 2023; Vaughn & Burkins, 2023). For
    86‚Üí    86‚Üí    86‚Üíexample, some individuals may believe self-control relies on willpower, while others
    87‚Üí    87‚Üí    87‚Üíemphasize its strategic nature (e.g., by avoiding temptations and setting specific action plans).
    88‚Üí    88‚Üí    88‚Üí
    89‚Üí    89‚Üí    89‚ÜíSELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 5
    90‚Üí    90‚Üí    90‚ÜíAgain, others may view self-control as inherently effortful and requiring persistence, or
    91‚Üí    91‚Üí    91‚Üíregard it as a source of pleasure or a reflection of morality. An experimental study comparing
    92‚Üí    92‚Üí    92‚Üíwillpower beliefs and strategy beliefs indicated that most people tend to associate self-control
    93‚Üí    93‚Üí    93‚Üíprimarily with willpower rather than strategy (Gennara et al., 2023). When describing
    94‚Üí    94‚Üí    94‚Üíindividuals with high trait self-control, people perceived them as more likely to exert
    95‚Üí    95‚Üí    95‚Üíwillpower (e.g., resisting temptation by sheer willpower) rather than as using self-control
    96‚Üí    96‚Üí    96‚Üístrategies (e.g., bypassing a tempting shop). Moreover, these beliefs were shown to influence
    97‚Üí    97‚Üí    97‚Üípeople‚Äôs intentions to apply willpower or self-control strategies in resolving self-control
    98‚Üí    98‚Üí    98‚Üíchallenges. Participants who were prompted to endorse the strategic nature of self-control
    99‚Üí    99‚Üí    99‚Üíintended to use strategies more than those whose beliefs about self-control as willpower were
   100‚Üí   100‚Üí   100‚Üíhighlighted (Gennara et al., 2023).
   101‚Üí   101‚Üí   101‚ÜíAnother study examined people‚Äôs self-control beliefs through linguistic analysis of
   102‚Üí   102‚Üí   102‚Üídescriptions of personal self-control experiences. People often described self-control in
   103‚Üí   103‚Üí   103‚Üírelation to power, risk, and achievement, but far less frequently in terms of positive emotions
   104‚Üí   104‚Üí   104‚Üíand the experience of pleasure (Vaughn & Burkins, 2023). Recently, a comprehensive
   105‚Üí   105‚Üí   105‚Üíframework of self-control beliefs has been proposed, identifying 14 distinct components. This
   106‚Üí   106‚Üí   106‚Üístudy directly asked participants about their views on self-control and found that people
   107‚Üí   107‚Üí   107‚Üícommonly perceive self-control as difficult and important, often requires commitment and
   108‚Üí   108‚Üí   108‚Üípersistence to achieve, but also seems unpleasant for many (Li et al., 2025), thus confirming
   109‚Üí   109‚Üí   109‚Üíthe results of the linguistic study by Vaughn and Burkins (2023). These beliefs cover different
   110‚Üí   110‚Üí   110‚Üífacets of people‚Äôs thoughts about self-control and may significantly impact how individuals
   111‚Üí   111‚Üí   111‚Üíexert self-control, yet apart from studies on the belief that self-control is a limited resource,
   112‚Üí   112‚Üí   112‚Üíno studies have examined the impact of the broader set of beliefs on life outcomes that may
   113‚Üí   113‚Üí   113‚Üíbenefit from self-control.
   114‚Üí   114‚Üí   114‚Üí
   115‚Üí   115‚Üí   115‚ÜíSELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 6
   116‚Üí   116‚Üí   116‚ÜíIn the present study, we will address this gap. Importantly, we will examine to what
   117‚Üí   117‚Üí   117‚Üíextent self-control beliefs add explanatory power to the influence of self-control capacity
   118‚Üí   118‚Üí   118‚Üí(i.e., trait self-control) and metacognition in predicting self-control outcomes. Trait self-
   119‚Üí   119‚Üí   119‚Üícontrol and self-control metacognition ‚Äì how individuals monitor and regulate their own self-
   120‚Üí   120‚Üí   120‚Üícontrol processes and strategies (Hennecke & B√ºrgler, 2023) ‚Äì are known to influence
   121‚Üí   121‚Üí   121‚Üíoutcomes in various domains (B√ºrgler & Hennecke, 2024; De Ridder et al., 2012; Hofmann
   122‚Üí   122‚Üí   122‚Üíet al., 2014). To assess the impact of self-control beliefs, it is important to investigate the
   123‚Üí   123‚Üí   123‚Üípredictive power of beliefs in relation to trait self-control and self-control metacognition as
   124‚Üí   124‚Üí   124‚Üírelated but distinct concepts. Previous studies attest to the notion that self-control beliefs can
   125‚Üí   125‚Üí   125‚Üíbe distinguished from trait self-control. For instance, a weak correlation was found between
   126‚Üí   126‚Üí   126‚Üíthe belief that self-control is a limited resource and trait self-control (Hagger et al., 2019),
   127‚Üí   127‚Üí   127‚Üíimplying that what people believe about self-control is relatively independent from their self-
   128‚Üí   128‚Üí   128‚Üícontrol capacity. Therefore, we aim to examine the power of self-control beliefs in predicting
   129‚Üí   129‚Üí   129‚Üílife outcomes, especially in comparison to that of trait self-control and metacognition.
   130‚Üí   130‚Üí   130‚ÜíCurrent Study
   131‚Üí   131‚Üí   131‚ÜíIn this pre-registered study, we first aimed to reduce an initial mapping of 14 self-

   134‚Üí   134‚Üí   134‚Üíparticipants about what defines self-control, capturing key aspects of self-control (Li et al.,

   136‚Üí   136‚Üí   136‚Üíunexplored. Given their conceptual associations, some components may naturally cluster into
   137‚Üí   137‚Üí   137‚Üíbroader, higher-order belief factors, resulting in a more comprehensive framework. We
   138‚Üí   138‚Üí   138‚Üítherefore hypothesized that the 14 self-control beliefs would be reproduced (H1a) and could
   139‚Üí   139‚Üí   139‚Üíbe structured into higher-order beliefs (H1b). Furthermore, we hypothesized that self-control
   140‚Üí   140‚Üí   140‚Üíbeliefs could be distinguished from trait self-control and metacognition (H2).
   141‚Üí   141‚Üí   141‚Üí
   142‚Üí   142‚Üí   142‚ÜíSELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 7

   253‚Üí   253‚Üí   253‚Üíperformed a confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) using the R package lavaan version 0.6-19
   254‚Üí   254‚Üí   254‚Üí(Rosseel, 2012) to assess the 14-component model of self-control beliefs. To validate the
   255‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚Üímodel further, we compared it with a one-factor model, where all items loaded onto a single
   256‚Üí   256‚Üí   256‚Üífactor. Maximum likelihood estimation was used to estimate model parameters, and a fixed
   257‚Üí   257‚Üí   257‚Üífactor model method was employed, with the variances of latent variables set to 1 for model
   258‚Üí   258‚Üí   258‚Üíidentification. Correlations among all latent variables were allowed to be estimated. Model fit
   259‚Üí   259‚Üí   259‚Üíwas evaluated using several fit indices, including the Comparative Fit Index (CFI), the
   260‚Üí   260‚Üí   260‚ÜíTucker-Lewis Index (TLI), the Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR), and the
   261‚Üí   261‚Üí   261‚ÜíRoot Mean Square Error of Approximation (RMSEA) (Browne & Cudeck, 1992; Hu &
   262‚Üí   262‚Üí   262‚ÜíBentler, 1999; Kline, 2023).
   263‚Üí   263‚Üí   263‚ÜíNext, we employed a network analysis to identify potential higher-order belief factors
   264‚Üí   264‚Üí   264‚Üíof self-control beliefs. Network analysis is a method that effectively communicates
   265‚Üí   265‚Üí   265‚Üímultivariate patterns of dependency, making it particularly suitable for exploring high-
   266‚Üí   266‚Üí   266‚Üídimensional data when there is limited prior theory regarding the relationships between
   267‚Üí   267‚Üí   267‚Üívariables (Borsboom et al., 2021). Recently, it has been widely used in research on
   268‚Üí   268‚Üí   268‚Üí2 Three hundred and seventy participants reported both of their weight and height. Weight and height data
   269‚Üí   269‚Üí   269‚Üíwere manually corrected before calculating means. Implausible responses, such as 3.5 cm for height and 400
   270‚Üí   270‚Üí   270‚Üíkg for weight, were excluded from the analysis.
   271‚Üí   271‚Üí   271‚Üí
   272‚Üí   272‚Üí   272‚ÜíSELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 12
   273‚Üí   273‚Üí   273‚Üípersonality (Cramer et al., 2012), attitude and beliefs (Brandt et al., 2019; Dalege et al.,
   274‚Üí   274‚Üí   274‚Üí2018), and mental health (Blanken et al., 2019; Borsboom & Cramer, 2013). A network
   275‚Üí   275‚Üí   275‚Üíessentially consists of nodes, typically representing entities, and edges that reflect the
   276‚Üí   276‚Üí   276‚Üírelationships between them. In the current study, the 14 self-control belief components were
   277‚Üí   277‚Üí   277‚Üíset as nodes. To visualize the associations among them, we applied the EBICglasso model in
   278‚Üí   278‚Üí   278‚ÜíJASP, which automatically sets small connections to zero, thus reducing relatively weak
   279‚Üí   279‚Üí   279‚Üíassociations and producing a more parsimonious network (Epskamp & Fried, 2018; Friedman
   280‚Üí   280‚Üí   280‚Üíet al., 2008). A thick and direct edge represents a strong and direct association between two
   281‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚Üínodes. We examined the clustering patterns in the network to identify potential higher-order
   282‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚Üíbelief factors (Borsboom et al., 2021; Costantini et al., 2015, 2020; Golino & Epskamp,
   283‚Üí   283‚Üí   283‚Üí2017). Additionally, we reported node strength, betweenness, and closeness to assess the
   284‚Üí   284‚Üí   284‚Üícentrality of the self-control belief components. Correlation stability coefficient (CS-
   285‚Üí   285‚Üí   285‚Üícoefficient) was calculated using the R package bootnet to evaluate the stability of node
   286‚Üí   286‚Üí   286‚Üícentrality, with a preferred threshold of above 0.5 (Epskamp et al., 2018).
   287‚Üí   287‚Üí   287‚ÜíWe further conducted an exploratory factor analysis (EFA) using JASP to examine
   288‚Üí   288‚Üí   288‚Üíhow specific items loaded onto the clusters. Factor analysis and network analysis are not
   289‚Üí   289‚Üí   289‚Üímutually exclusive but rather complementary; while network analysis visualizes patterns of
   290‚Üí   290‚Üí   290‚Üíassociations, factor analysis helps to identify groups of closely connected components
   291‚Üí   291‚Üí   291‚Üí(Cramer et al., 2012). This combined approach allowed us to identify potential higher-order
   292‚Üí   292‚Üí   292‚Üíbelief factors with greater robustness and obtain more accurate scores for each factor based
   293‚Üí   293‚Üí   293‚Üíon specific items. We used JASP‚Äôs default settings for EFA, using parallel analysis, with a
   294‚Üí   294‚Üí   294‚Üíminimum factor loading threshold of .40. Factor rotation was set to oblique (oblimin),
   295‚Üí   295‚Üí   295‚Üíallowing for correlations among factors.
   296‚Üí   296‚Üí   296‚ÜíBased on the results, we subsequently performed a second higher-order CFA to
   297‚Üí   297‚Üí   297‚Üíexplore and examine the higher-order factors of self-control beliefs. Consistent with the
   298‚Üí   298‚Üí   298‚Üí
   299‚Üí   299‚Üí   299‚ÜíSELF-CONTROL BELIEFS 13
   300‚Üí   300‚Üí   300‚Üíprevious CFA, we evaluated the model fit based on fit indices, such as CFI, TLI, SRMR, and
   301‚Üí   301‚Üí   301‚ÜíRMSEA.
   302‚Üí   302‚Üí   302‚ÜíTo investigate the distinction between self-control beliefs and trait self-control (H2),
   303‚Üí   303‚Üí   303‚Üíwe calculated Pearson‚Äôs r correlations between the higher-order belief factors and other self-

   306‚Üí   306‚Üí   306‚Üícontrol, and metacognition were combined, with a three-factor model treating them as distinct
   307‚Üí   307‚Üí   307‚Üíconstructs. Furthermore, we employed structural equation modeling (SEM) to assess whether
   308‚Üí   308‚Üí   308‚Üíself-control beliefs, trait self-control, and metacognition independently predicted self-control
   309‚Üí   309‚Üí   309‚Üíoutcomes. If these constructs contributed independently to the outcomes, this would support
   310‚Üí   310‚Üí   310‚Üíour hypothesis.
   311‚Üí   311‚Üí   311‚ÜíFinally, to examine the predictive power of the higher-order belief factors on life
   312‚Üí   312‚Üí   312‚Üíoutcomes, we conducted a hierarchical regression analysis with six life outcomes as

   446‚Üí   446‚Üí   446‚ÜíDiscussion
   447‚Üí   447‚Üí   447‚ÜíSelf-control plays a crucial role in individuals' ability to regulate their behaviors, resist
   448‚Üí   448‚Üí   448‚Üítemptations, and achieve long-term goals. While trait self-control has been extensively
   449‚Üí   449‚Üí   449‚Üístudied, emerging research suggests that beliefs about the nature of self-control may also
   450‚Üí   450‚Üí   450‚Üíinfluence self-control outcomes. The overall aim of this study was to examine the predictive
   451‚Üí   451‚Üí   451‚Üípower of beliefs about self-control across various life domains. To achieve this aim, we
   452‚Üí   452‚Üí   452‚Üísought to reproduce a set of 14 previously identified self-control beliefs and test a higher-
   453‚Üí   453‚Üí   453‚Üíorder structure of core beliefs. As expected, our findings provided support for the 14-
