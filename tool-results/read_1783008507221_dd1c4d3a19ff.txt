    12→10 Abstract:
    13→11 Obsessive thinking about a loved one (i.e., constant, involuntary, repetitive thoughts about the loved
    14→12 one) is a defining feature of romantic love yet remains markedly under-researched. This article
    15→13 provides the first review of obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love, organising the
    16→14 disparate literature using an "Eight Perspectives" approach spanning evolutionary history,
    17→15 evolutionary functions, development, mechanisms, ecology, cognitions, emotions, and behaviour. We
    18→16 argue that obsessive thinking about a loved one likely emerged by co-opting mother-infant bonding
    19→17 circuitry, appears across cultures and across the lifespan, and may have evolved to promote
    20→18 proximity-seeking and sustained orientation toward a loved one. Contrary to long-standing claims,
    21→19 the mechanisms underlying obsessive thinking in romantic love are unlikely to be shared with
    22→20 obsessive-compulsive disorder. People who are in love think about their loved one for approximately
    23→21 50% of their waking hours, and this thinking is associated with romantic (but not companionate)
    24→22 love, distraction, proximity-seeking behaviour, and analgesia. Whether such thinking is experienced
    25→23 as bothersome or enjoyable depends less on its frequency than on companionate love and
    26→24 reciprocation. We conclude by identifying priorities for future research across all eight perspectives.

    36→33 1 Introduction
    37→34 Obsessive thinking about a loved one is a commonly cited feature of romantic love (Bajoghli et al.,
    38→35 2014; Bode, 2023; Bode & Kushnick, 2021b; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007; Fisher, 2000;
    39→36 Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al., 2002; Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986; Langeslag et al., 2012; Nilakantan et al.,
    40→37 2014). Romantic love, generally, and obsessive thinking, in particular, is under-researched. This
    41→38 article provides the first review, to our knowledge, of obsessive thinking in romantic love. It uses an
    42→39 “eight perspectives” framework to outline the evolutionary history, development across the lifespan,
    43→40 mechanisms, cognitions, emotions, behaviour, ecology, and evolutionary functions of obsessive
    44→41 thinking about a loved one. In so doing, it provides a summary of the state of the art of research on
    45→42 obsessive thinking in romantic love. We conclude by outlining areas for future research that, if
    46→43 pursued, will substantially increase our understanding of this fundamental feature of romantic love.
    47→44 1.1 Romantic Love
    48→45 Romantic love has been defined in line with the English definition (Collins, 2025), American
    49→46 Psychological Association definition (American Psychological Association, 2018), the conventions
    50→47 in biopsychology (Bode & Kowal, 2023) and evolutionary social and behavioural sciences (Bode,
    51→48 2026; Bode, Kavanagh, et al., 2025), and the meaning conveyed by one of the founders of
    52→49 relationship science (Berscheid, 2010):
    53→50 […] a motivational state typically associated with a desire for long-term mating with a
    54→51 particular individual. It occurs across the lifespan and is associated with distinctive cognitive,
    55→52 emotional, behavioral, social, genetic, neural, and endocrine activity in both sexes. Throughout
    56→53 much of the life course, it serves mate choice, courtship, sex, and pair-bonding functions. It is a
    57→54 suite of adaptations and by-products that arose sometime during the recent evolutionary history
    58→55 of humans (Bode & Kushnick, 2021b, p. 21).
    59→56 Romantic love is commonly associated with the early stages of a romantic relationship (Bode &
    60→57 Kowal, 2023) but can exist in the absence of a romantic relationship (Bringle et al., 2013; Hill et al.,
    61→58 1997) or after many years or decades (Acevedo & Aron, 2009; Acevedo et al., 2012; O’Leary et al.,
    62→59 2011). Romantic love decreases with relationship duration and progression (Langeslag et al., 2013).
    63→60 It is sometimes referred to as “passionate love,” “infatuation”, or “limerence” by some psychologists
    64→61 and as being “in love” by the general population.
    65→62 Romantic love can be contrasted with companionate love (sometimes also referred to as
    66→63 “attachment”). Companionate love is the comforting feeling of emotional bonding with another
    67→64 person that is a much calmer state than romantic love (Berscheid, 2010; Fisher, 1998; Hatfield,
    68→65 1988). It is a feature of an established pair bond. Companionate love takes time to develop, so it
    69→66 increases with relationship duration and progression (Langeslag et al., 2013).
    70→67 1.2 Obsessive Thinking About a Loved One
    71→68 Obsessive thinking about a loved one has been identified as a common aspect of romantic love since
    72→69 early work on the topic. Dorothy Tennov (Tennov, 1979), in her seminal book, Love and Limerence,
    73→70 described obsessive thinking as a necessary component of intense romantic love (which she referred
    74→71 to as limerence). She referred to obsessive thinking about a loved one as “intrusive thinking” and
    75→72 describes it as constant, involuntary, and repetitive. The importance of obsessive thinking to
    76→2
    77→
    78→73 romantic love has been reinforced through its inclusion in the most common measure of the intensity
    79→74 of romantic love (Passionate Love Scale; (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986), early theorising (Fisher, 2000;
    80→75 Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al., 2002), empirical studies (e.g., Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015;
    81→76 Brand et al., 2007; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025; Langeslag et al., 2012; Nilakantan et al., 2014), and
    82→77 recent work on the semantics (Bode & Kushnick, 2021b) and theory (Bode, 2023) of romantic love.
    83→78 The specific cognitive features of obsessive thinking about a loved one are considered in detail
    84→79 below, however, it is commonly operationalised in terms of percentage of waking hours spent
    85→80 thinking about a loved one (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022; Langeslag et al., 2012) and items from the
    86→81 Passionate Love Scale1 (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986; e.g., Acevedo & Aron, 2009; Langeslag et al.,
    87→82 2012). Some researchers have used three items adapted from the Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive
    88→83 Scale (YBOCS) about thinking about a loved one as a proxy for love intensity in their studies (e.g.,
    89→84 Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007).
    90→85 We are not referring to obsessive love (or mania), in which “The lover is jealous, full of doubt about
    91→86 the partner’s sincerity and commitment, subject to physical symptoms such as inability to eat and
    92→87 sleep, experiences acute excitement alternating with debilitating depression” (Hendrick & Hendrick,
    93→88 1992, p. 66). Only one of the items of the Mania subscale of the Love Attitudes Scale (Hendrick &
    94→89 Hendrick, 1992) assesses obsessive thinking about a loved one (i.e., “When I am in love, I have
    95→90 trouble concentrating on anything else”), while the other items focus more on anxiety about whether
    96→91 the loved one is committed (e.g., “When things aren't right with my lover and me, my stomach gets
    97→92 upset”). Indeed, an obsessive love style is associated with anxious/ambivalent attachment styles
    98→93 (Ahmadi et al., 2013; Honari & Saremi, 2015; Levy & Davis, 1988). While an obsessive love style
    99→94 may be accompanied by obsessive thinking about a loved one, it may not be the main aspect of it.
   100→95 And obsessive thinking about a loved one may not be exclusively associated with an obsessive love
   101→96 style or mania but may occur in other types of love as well.
   102→97 We are also not referring to a specific symptom of obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), namely
   103→98 obsessions focused on romantic relationships. That type of obsession has been assessed using the
   104→99 Relationship Obsessive Compulsive Inventory (ROCI; Doron et al., 2012), which consists of three
   105→100 factors: feelings towards one’s partner (e.g., “I continuously reassess whether I really love my
   106→101 partner”), one’s perception of partner’s feelings (e.g., “I continuously doubt my partner’s love for
   107→102 me”), and one’s appraisal of the “rightness” of the relationship (e.g., “I check and recheck whether
   108→103 my relationship feels right”). These OCD symptoms are obsessions about love feelings and the
   109→104 relationship, rather than obsessive thinking about a loved one.
   110→105 1.3 “Eight Perspectives” Approach
   111→106 This review uses an "Eight Perspectives" approach (Bode & Kowal, 2026) to describe obsessive
   112→107 thinking about a loved one in romantic love. The Eight Perspectives approach extends Tinbergen's
   113→108 Four Questions (Tinbergen, 1963; i.e., evolutionary history, evolutionary functions, development
   114→109 across the lifespan, and mechanisms) by adding dedicated consideration of ecology, emotions,
   115→110 cognitions, and behaviour (Bode & Kowal, 2026). Together, these eight perspectives span ultimate
   116→111 causes (why a phenomenon evolved), proximate causes (how it works), and phenotypic outcomes
   117→112 (how it is expressed), and are aligned with the analytical traditions of ethology, ecology, psychology,
   118→113 and behavioural science. The approach was designed to provide useful, simple, and flexible
   119→3
   120→1 Items 5, 19, and 21 of the PLS (i.e., “Sometimes I feel I can’t control my thoughts; they are obsessively on _____.”
   121→“_____ always seems to be on my mind.”, and “I sometimes find it difficult to concentrate on work because thoughts of
   122→_____ occupy my mind.”)
   123→
   124→114 organisation of findings about complex human psychobehavioural phenomena, and the present
   125→115 review represents its first application since its publication. As will become apparent, the approach is
   126→116 well suited to obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love; it organises a disparate literature
   127→117 into an interpretable structure, makes gaps in knowledge immediately visible, and invites
   128→118 contributions from researchers across disciplines who might not otherwise see their work as
   129→119 connected.
   130→120 The eight perspectives are presented in an order that traces the causes and consequences of the
   131→121 biological, ecological, psychological, and behavioural activity involved in obsessive thinking about a
   132→122 loved one. Ultimate causes come first: evolutionary history situates the phenomenon in deep time,
   133→123 and evolutionary functions identify the adaptive problems it may have solved. Proximate causes
   134→124 follow: development describes how obsessive thinking emerges and changes across the lifespan,
   135→125 biological mechanisms describe the biological and psychological processes that produce it, and
   136→126 ecology describes how the surrounding environment shapes its expression. The phenotypic outcomes
   137→127 are presented last: cognitions and emotions are the most direct expressions of the proximate
   138→128 mechanisms, and these in turn give rise to behaviour. Figure 1 presents the eight perspectives in the
   139→129 order in which they appear.

   143→133 2 Evolutionary History
   144→134 There is not much to say about the evolutionary history of obsessive thinking about a loved one. To
   145→135 our knowledge, only one scientific text has commented on the evolution of obsessive thinking. Bode
   146→136 (A. Bode, 2023), in an hypothesis and theory article, suggested that romantic love evolved by co-
   147→137 opting mother-infant bonding. He suggests that three components of mother infant bonding (i.e.,
   148→138 bonding attraction, obsessive thinking, and attachment) were replicated from mother-infant bonding
   149→139 and took on a new function, romantic love. More recent work refines the theory by suggesting that
   150→140 genes were not replicated, but rather, regulatory DNA responsible for particular neural circuitry was
   151→141 co-opted (Felesina & Bode, 2026). The theory is yet to be tested, but the available evidence upon
   152→142 which the theory is based is convincing. The theory suggests that processes of mutation and co-
   153→143 option provide a reproductive and survival benefit to individuals expressing obsessive thinking in
   154→144 romantic love and natural selection made it a ubiquitous feature of modern humans (Homo sapiens).
   155→145 Although no one has directly investigated it, it appears that obsessive thinking is present in romantic
   156→146 love cross-culturally and across ethnic groups. The publicly available Romantic Love Survey 2022
   157→147 (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022) included data from 1,556 people self-reporting experiencing romantic love
   158→4
   159→
   160→148 in 33 countries, mainly in North America, Western and Central Europe, and South Africa. All
   161→149 indications are that obsessive thinking is present in all the countries sampled, with participants in that
   162→150 survey reporting that they thought about their loved one on average 49.75% of their waking hours.
   163→151 The lowest proportion of time spent thinking about their loved one was among Austrians (n = 10)
   164→152 with an average of 35.10% of waking hours. While this is indicative of cross cultural and ethnic
   165→153 expression, it will be necessary to demonstrate the presence of obsessive thinking about a loved one
   166→154 in a greater number of ethnic groups, including in small scale societies, before we can confidently
   167→155 claim it is a universal.
   168→156 That obsessive thinking about a loved one appears to be a feature of romantic love cross-culturally
   169→157 indicates that it almost certainly emerged prior to the emergence of modern humans at least by
   170→158 300,00 years ago (Stringer, 2016). Romantic love serves the function of promoting social monogamy,
   171→159 and social monogamy is associated with reduced sexual dimorphism (Alexander et al., 1979). This
   172→160 occurred most drastically during the reign of Homo erectus, the commencement of which has been
   173→161 dated to about 2 MYA (Antón, 2003). If obsessive thinking about a loved one emerged at the same
   174→162 time as the original suite of adaptations and by-products that constitute romantic love (Felesina &
   175→163 Bode, 2026), we can assume it first emerged within the past 1.5-2 million years (Plavcan, 2012;
   176→164 Villmoare et al., 2019).
   177→165 One additional feature of obsessive thinking about a loved one’s evolutionary history relates to
   178→166 sexual selection. Females have been shown to think about their partner noticeably more than males
   179→167 (females = 54% of waking hours cf males = 45% (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025), and in that study,
   180→168 biological sex explained 4% of the variation in how long individuals spend of their waking hours
   181→169 thinking about their loved one. The authors suggest this may be the result of sexual selection,
   182→170 although we can’t discount it could be partially explained by sex differences in mechanisms that play
   183→171 a role in mother-infant bonding. It may be that females are more suited to obsessive thinking about
   184→172 their loved one because they have refined this process in relation to infants over the past 200 million
   185→173 years (Grossnickle et al., 2019).
   186→174 3 Evolutionary Functions
   187→175 There has not been much theorising or empirical testing about the evolutionary functions of obsessive
   188→176 thinking about a loved one in romantic love. Drawing on the likely evolutionary history of romantic
   189→177 love, Bode (2023) highlights that obsessive thinking about an infant ensures that the mother does not
   190→178 forget about her infant, and can in turn engage in the activities (i.e., caregiving) necessary to keep it
   191→179 alive. The specific evolutionary functions in romantic love, however, are less clear. Individuals in
   192→180 love think about their loved one about 50% of their waking hours (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025),
   193→181 indicating that this intensity may have been optimally adaptive in the environment in which romantic
   194→182 love emerged and evolved (Waynforth, 2023). Why we need to think about our partner that much is a
   195→183 mystery when it seems feasible that thinking about them only 10% of our waking hours in a regular
   196→184 manner would have a similar effect.
   197→185 Tennov (Tennov, 1979) suggested obsessive thinking about a loved one maintains focus on the loved
   198→186 one, sustains the emotional intensity of the loving experience, and keeps the person oriented toward
   199→187 pursuing or securing reciprocation. Focussing energies on the loved one (Fisher, 1998) is also likely
   200→188 to have direct benefits to reproductive fitness in circumstances when the love can be, or is,
   201→189 reciprocated by promoting proximity-seeking, courtship, sex, and caregiving. Obsessive thinking
   202→190 about a loved one also means that stimuli about the loved one are regularly present, in turn activating
   203→191 neurobiological responses to the loved one, reinforcing romantic love. Empirical support for the
   204→5
   205→
   206→192 proximity-seeking function comes from Valshtein et al. (2020), who found that obsessive thinking
   207→193 predicted prospective proximity-seeking behaviour (see Behaviour, below). This is consistent with
   208→194 the proposal that obsessive thinking promotes reproductively relevant approach behaviour toward the
   209→195 loved one.
   210→196 Gonzaga et al. (2008) have shown that the experience of love in romantic relationships, but not
   211→197 sexual desire, facilitates the suppression of thoughts about attractive alternatives and reduces memory
   212→198 for their attractiveness-related features. It is plausible that obsessive thinking about the beloved
   213→199 contributes to this effect by occupying cognitive resources that would otherwise be available for
   214→200 attending to rival stimuli. However, participants in that study had been in relationships for an average
   215→201 of nearly three years, meaning many were unlikely to still be experiencing romantic love and its
   216→202 characteristic obsessive thinking. Whether this suppression effect is stronger during the early,
   217→203 obsessive-thinking-intensive phase of romantic love remains untested.
   218→204 4 Development Across the Lifespan
   219→205 Romantic love expresses in juveniles, with it being documented in children as young as 4 years old
   220→206 (Hatfield et al., 1988). Indications are that obsessive thinking about a loved one is present in these
   221→207 children experiencing romantic love. The Juvenile Love Scale; (Greenwell, 1983; Hatfield & Young,
   222→208 2009) includes two items assessing obsessive thinking: “Did you ever keep thinking about ___when
   223→209 you wanted to stop and couldn’t?” and “I am always thinking about .” However, there is some
   224→210 uncertainty about this as the studies validating the JLS were never peer reviewed and we are not
   225→211 privy to child participants’ scores on those two specific items. Considerations of love in early
   226→212 adolescence have been published (e.g., Montgomery & Sorell, 1998) but no documentation of
   227→213 obsessive thinking appears to be present that we are aware of. Romantic love expresses with all its
   228→214 features following puberty (Bode & Kushnick, 2021a). Understanding the age at which obsessive
   229→215 thinking about a loved one expresses in romantic love is an area for targeted research.
   230→216 Although obsessive thinking about a loved one is most often studied in prototypical romantic love
   231→217 (i.e., in the first two years of a romantic relationship), obsessive thinking about a loved one also
   232→218 persists in long-term romantic love. Among married individuals reporting being 'very intensely in
   233→219 love' after more than ten years, thinking about the partner when apart was a significant correlate of
   234→220 love intensity in both U.S. (O’Leary et al., 2011) and Spanish (Cuenca-Montesino et al., 2015)
   235→221 samples. The frequency and content of obsessive thinking about a loved one across the duration of a
   236→222 relationship has not been systematically investigated and represents a priority for future research.
   237→223 5 Biological Mechanisms
   238→224 Almost nothing is known about the biological mechanisms that cause obsessive thinking about a
   239→225 loved one in romantic love. One fMRI study (Fujisaki et al., 2026) suggests that the anterior insula
   240→226 (aINS) may play a role: in males in the early stages of a romantic relationship, the distinctiveness of
   241→227 partner-specific neural representations in the aINS was positively associated with the frequency of
   242→228 intrusive thoughts about a partner measured via experience sampling. This finding awaits replication
   243→229 and extension to female samples.
   244→230 In the late 1990s and early 2000s, Helen Fisher and others (Fisher, 2000; Fisher, 1998; Fisher et al.,
   245→231 2002) confidently asserted that obsessive thinking was mechanistically similar to intrusive thinking
   246→232 found in OCD. They attributed it to lower levels of serotonin during romantic love. Claimed evidence
   247→233 for this included the first biological study of romantic love which measured circulating serotonin
   248→6
   249→
   250→234 transporters in blood platelets (Marazziti et al., 1999). Since that time, however, it has become
   251→235 apparent that this mechanistic explanation is almost certainly wrong.
   252→236 The findings from the original study upon which this theory drew its initial support were
   253→237 misinterpreted. Marazziti et al. (1999) found that the density of the serotonin transporter in blood
   254→238 platelets was lower in people who has recently fallen in love and OCD patients than in control
   255→239 participants. This was interpreted to indicate that individuals experiencing romantic love exhibited
   256→240 higher circulating serotonin. Since that time, however, it has become known that lower serotonin
   257→241 transporter levels are actually associated with higher levels of circulating serotonin (Jørgensen et al.,
   258→242 2014; Mercado & Kilic, 2010). Although a later study (Langeslag et al., 2012) found lower levels of
   259→243 serotonin in blood plasma and serum in males experiencing romantic love than male controls, it also
   260→244 found higher plasma and serum serotonin levels in females experiencing romantic love than female
   261→245 controls. Interestingly, in females experiencing romantic love, there was a significant positive
   262→246 correlation between serum serotonin levels and scores on obsessive thinking about a loved one items
   263→247 taken form the Passionate Love Scale (PLS; Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986). It should be noted though
   264→248 that the sample size of this study was small. The final death knell for this theory comes from a
   265→249 correlational study (Bode, Kowal, et al., 2025) finding that individuals experiencing romantic love do
   266→250 not differ in obsessive thinking regardless of whether they are taking selective serotonin reuptake
   267→251 inhibitors (SSRIs) or not. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors are a common treatment for OCD
   268→252 (Del Casale et al., 2019). If they alleviate intrusive thinking in OCD, and intrusive thinking in OCD
   269→253 and obsessive thinking in romantic love are mechanistically linked, it follows that SSRIs would
   270→254 reduce obsessive thinking in romantic love. Because they don’t, this is strong evidence that the
   271→255 mechanisms causing these two phenomena are different.
   272→256 6 Ecology
   273→257 There is some evidence that ecology (in the form of national-level indictors) can influence the
   274→258 expression of obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love. In a cross-sectional study
   275→259 investigating sex differences in romantic love using the Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode, Luoto, et
   276→260 al., 2025), The United Nations Gender Inequality Index was used as a control in a hierarchical linear
   277→261 regression predicting obsessive thinking about a loved one. In the final model, Gender Inequality
   278→262 Index was the second largest predictor of obsessive thinking (after biological sex), indicating that
   279→263 individuals in more gender equal countries experienced less obsessive thinking about a loved one.
   280→264 This is consistent with another study (Sorokowski et al., 2023) demonstrating associations between
   281→265 gender equality, modernisation and collectivism and three components of love in romantic
   282→266 relationships (i.e., intimacy, passion, and commitment). This indicates that the cultural environment
   283→267 may have a causal influence on how obsessive thinking out a loved one expresses. Future studies
   284→268 should investigate the association between modernisation and collectivism on obsessive thinking
   285→269 about a loved one.
   286→270 7 Cognitions
   287→271 According to Tennov (1979), obsessive thinking about the loved one (what she called the “limerent
   288→272 object;” i.e., the person one feels limerence for) is a main component of romantic love. She relates it
   289→273 to compulsive daydreaming about, preoccupation with, and uncontrollable fantasizing about the
   290→274 limerent object. Likewise, Fisher (Fisher, 1998) and Fisher et al. (Fisher et al., 2002) list obsessive
   291→275 thinking about a loved one as one of the characteristics of romantic love. Here, we review studies that
   292→276 have assessed this cognitive aspect of romantic love.
   293→7
   294→
   295→277 Several studies have assessed how much people think about their loved one. In a study of participants
   296→278 who had ever experienced a romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), thoughts about the ex-partner
   297→279 occurred several times per week on average and were fairly vivid, with nostalgia, loneliness, certain
   298→280 songs, sadness, celebrations, anxiety, and boredom endorsed as common triggers. Among
   299→281 participants currently in love, self-reported estimates of time spent thinking about the loved one have
   300→282 ranged widely: 65% of waking hours in participants in love for 9 months or less (Langeslag et al.,
   301→283 2012), 50–61% in participants in a relationship for 9 months or less (Nilakantan et al., 2014), 67% in
   302→284 participants who were in love (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), 50% in young adults in love for less than
   303→285 two years from 33 countries (Bode, Luoto, et al., 2025), and 74% (Langeslag, Submitted) and 77%
   304→286 (Evans et al. 2026) in participants experiencing limerence . Lower estimates of 21% and 49% come
   305→287 from experience sampling studies in male participants in relationships of 7 months or less (Fujisaki et
   306→288 al., 2026) and participants experiencing limerence (Evans et al., 2026), respectively. These lower
   307→289 figures may reflect reduced memory bias from capturing experiences in real-time. In sum, average
   308→290 self-reported thinking about the loved one ranged from 21 to 77% of the time across studies, with
   309→291 individual responses ranging from 0 to 100% indicating that, for some individuals, obsessive thinking
   310→292 about a loved one can be all-consuming.
   311→293 Time spent thinking about the loved one has been positively associated with romantic love intensity
   312→294 (r = .25; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025) and limerence intensity (r = .37; Langeslag, Submitted and r =
   313→295 .39; Evans et al., 2026) but not with companionate love intensity (r = .003; Langeslag & Philippi,
   314→296 2025). This indicates that obsessive thinking about a loved one is associated with romantic love
   315→297 specifically rather than companionate love, although the correlations are far from perfect, and we
   316→298 caution against using obsessive thinking about a loved one as a proxy for love intensity as has been
   317→299 done in some studies (i.e., Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007). Obsessive
   318→300 thinking about a loved one has also been shown to increase following threat to relatedness (i.e., the
   319→301 basic psychological need to feel connected to, cared for by, and accepted by others) and negative
   320→302 fantasizing about the loved one across a series of experimental studies using relatedness need
   321→303 manipulations and directed fantasy tasks (Valshtein et al., 2020).
   322→304 People think about their loved one during cognitive tasks unrelated to the loved one. In one study
   323→305 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), participants who were in love reported thinking about their loved one
   324→306 on 42% of thought probes and 34% of free responses during a sustained attention response task.
   325→307 Interestingly, both measures were only weakly associated with obsessive thinking about a loved one
   326→308 in general (both rs < .22), suggesting these two phenomena are only somewhat coupled. In studies of
   327→309 participants who had experienced a break-up and were instructed to suppress thoughts of their ex-
   328→310 partner (Baird et al., 2013), participants still thought about their ex-partner 14–22% of the time. High
   329→311 cognitive load increased probe-caught (but not self-caught) thoughts about the ex-partner, and desire
   330→312 to reconcile was positively associated with probe-caught thinking, suggesting that people who want
   331→313 to reconcile think about their ex-partner more — and are less aware of doing so. The behavioural
   332→314 consequences of this obsessive thinking about a loved one are addressed below.
   333→315 8 Emotions
   334→316 Some terms analogous with "obsessive thinking about a loved one" have negative connotation, (e.g.,
   335→317 "intrusive thinking", "preoccupation", and "pathological obsession" (Fisher et al., 2002; Nilakantan et
   336→318 al., 2014; Tennov, 1979). Intrusive thinking implies recurrent and persistent thoughts that are at least
   337→319 sometimes unwanted and distressing, and that the individual attempts to ignore or suppress
   338→320 (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Indeed, in the study of participants who had experienced a
   339→321 romantic break-up (Farber et al., 2025), participants endorsed frightened, guilty, remorseful,
   340→8
   341→
   342→322 ashamed, anxious, and grateful as common feelings when thinking about the ex-partner. However,
   343→323 thinking about a loved one is not always bothersome. In a study of participants experiencing
   344→324 limerence recruited through internet limerence communities (Langeslag, Submitted), the mean score
   345→325 on a slider from "it bothers me" to "I enjoy it" was 41 out of 100, significantly below the midpoint.
   346→326 Thinking about the loved one bothered them. In contrast, in a study of participants who were in love
   347→327 (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), the mean on the same slider was 84 out of 100, significantly above the
   348→328 midpoint. These participants enjoyed thinking about their loved one.
   349→329 What might determine whether thinking about the loved one has positive or negative emotional
   350→330 effects? Across these two studies (Langeslag, Submitted; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), enjoyment of
   351→331 thinking about the loved one did not correlate positively with romantic love intensity but did
   352→332 correlate positively with companionate love intensity as measured with the IAS attachment scale
   353→333 (Langeslag et al., 2013). Notably, only 5% of the limerence participants were in a romantic
   354→334 relationship with their loved one, compared with 92% of the in-love participants, while time spent
   355→335 thinking about the loved one did not differ drastically between the two samples (74% vs. 67%). It
   356→336 therefore seems that whether people enjoy thinking about their loved one or whether it bothers them
   357→337 depends more on companionate love intensity and whether they are in a romantic relationship with
   358→338 the loved one than on how much they are thinking about them.
   359→339 The rewarding character of thinking about a loved one is also evident in its analgesic effects. In two
   360→340 studies (Nilakantan et al., 2014), participants experienced less pain from thermal stimuli when
   361→341 viewing pictures of their romantic partner than of an acquaintance, and this analgesia was positively
   362→342 correlated with the percentage of waking hours spent thinking about their partner. The mechanism
   363→343 appears to be activation of brain reward regions rather than distraction (Younger et al., 2010),
   364→344 consistent with the enjoyment many people report when thinking about their loved one.
   365→345 There have also been some studies linking obsessive thinking about a loved one with features
   366→346 analogous to symptoms of mood disorders. In one study (Bode & Kavanagh, 2025) using the
   367→347 Romantic Love Survey 2022 (Bode & Kavanagh, 2022), membership of clusters associated with
   368→348 higher obsessive thinking about the loved one was positively associated with hypomanic-like features
   369→349 (which are closely related to elevated mood (Faurholt-Jepsen et al., 2020)). This is consistent with
   370→350 previous studies (Bajoghli et al., 2014; Brand et al., 2015; Brand et al., 2007) that found a similar
   371→351 association and two studies finding inconsistent associations with depression symptoms (Bajoghli et
   372→352 al., 2013; Bajoghli et al., 2014).
   373→353 9 Behaviour
   374→354 Behavioural consequences of obsessive thinking about a loved one may be that it manifests as
   375→355 reduced task performance and that it may lead to proximity-seeking behaviour. Several studies have
   376→356 provided evidence that love produces behavioural distraction. Love intensity as measured with the
   377→357 PLS was positively correlated with the interference effect in Stroop and flanker tasks (van
   378→358 Steenbergen et al., 2014), and more obsessive thinking about a loved one in general was associated
   379→359 with poorer and slower performance on a short-term memory task (Langeslag & van Strien, 2020)
   380→360 and with reduced ability to withhold responses in a sustained attention response task (Langeslag &
   381→361 Philippi, 2025). These studies show that love and thinking about a loved one in general (rather than
   382→362 during the task specifically) are associated with behavioural distraction. In addition, the more
   383→363 participants thought about their loved one during the sustained attention response task as rated by
   384→364 coders, the slower their responses were (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and probe-caught thinking
   385→365 about an ex-partner during reading was associated with reduced reading comprehension (Baird et al.,
   386→9
   387→
   388→366 2013). Interestingly, self-caught thinking about the ex-partner was not associated with reduced
   389→367 reading comprehension (Baird et al., 2013), which suggests that thoughts about the loved one that the
   390→368 person is unaware of are particularly disruptive to performance. These studies suggest that thinking
   391→369 about a loved one during an unrelated task specifically is distracting. Behavioural distraction has
   392→370 clear implications for workplace productivity and engagement with the non-romantic social
   393→371 environment.
   394→372 Romantic love is also associated with proximity-seeking behaviours that vary in severity, with
   395→373 stalking as an example of a severe proximity-seeking behaviour. Valshtein et al. (2020) assessed
   396→374 prospective proximity-seeking behaviour in participants in a romantic relationship using 53 items
   397→375 asking how likely they would be to engage in behaviours such as showing up to the loved one's house
   398→376 unannounced and sending desperate messages. Across both studies, the score on a 12-item obsessive
   399→377 thinking measure was positively associated with prospective proximity-seeking behaviour. We can’t
   400→378 assert a causal relationship because the study was cross-sectional, but this is an avenue for future
   401→379 research.
   402→380 10 Discussion
   403→381 Obsessive thinking about a loved one has been identified as a core feature of romantic love for more
   404→382 than four decades (Tennov, 1979), is embedded in the field's most widely used measure of romantic
   405→383 love (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986), and is regularly invoked in theoretical accounts of the phenomenon
   406→384 (Bode, 2023; Fisher, 1998). Yet it has never been systematically reviewed. This article provides the
   407→385 first such synthesis, organised using an Eight Perspectives approach (Bode & Kowal, 2026).
   408→386 Obsessive thinking about a loved one appears to be a cross-culturally present feature of romantic love
   409→387 that expresses across the lifespan, is associated with romantic love specifically rather than
   410→388 companionate love, and has identifiable cognitive, emotional, and behavioural signatures. What
   411→389 emerges, however, is that our understanding of this fundamental feature of romantic love remains
   412→390 remarkably partial. Table 1 presents a summary of the main findings of this review.

   456→406 across perspectives. Cognitions, emotions, and behaviour (i.e., the phenotypic outcomes) are
   457→407 comparatively well-characterised, with evidence for frequency estimates, valence determinants,

   461→409 functions, and ecology are largely empty. This pattern maps directly onto disciplinary silos:
   462→410 psychology has done the phenotypic work, while evolutionary biology, neuroscience, and cross-
   463→411 cultural research have scarcely engaged with obsessive thinking about a loved one in romantic love.
   464→412 The framework makes this imbalance visible and, we hope, provides a structure through which
   465→413 researchers across disciplines can locate their current and potential contributions.
   466→414 A second observation is that the pathologising frame implied by terms such as "intrusive thinking"
   467→415 and "pathological obsession" is difficult to reconcile with the available evidence. Selective serotonin
   468→416 reuptake inhibitors do not reduce obsessive thinking about a loved one (Bode, Kowal, et al., 2025),
   469→417 suggesting its mechanisms differ from those underlying OCD. Many people who are in love enjoy
   470→418 thinking about their loved one (Langeslag & Philippi, 2025), and the phenomenon is associated with
   471→419 analgesia (Nilakantan et al., 2014) and proximity-seeking behaviour (Valshtein et al., 2020)— both
   472→420 consistent with adaptive function. These findings suggest obsessive thinking in romantic love is
   473→421 better understood as a functional component of a mating-related motivational state than as a symptom
   474→422 of dysfunction. We therefore favour the term "obsessive thinking about a loved one", which specifies
   475→423 the object of thought rather than pathologising the thinker, and hope this review marks the start of a
   476→424 broader shift toward this usage across the field.
   477→425 Several limitations of the available evidence warrant mention. Studies of obsessive thinking about a
   478→426 loved one in romantic love are dominated by Western, young-adult samples, limiting claims about
   479→427 universality. Several load-bearing findings rest on single studies awaiting replication (e.g., Baird et
   480→428 al., 2013; Langeslag & Philippi, 2025; Nilakantan et al., 2014; Valshtein et al., 2020), and many of
   481→429 these studies are based on small samples and cross-sectional designs that limit causal inference.
   482→430 Measurement is heavily reliant on self-report and, in particular, on three items from the Passionate
   483→431 Love Scale (Hatfield & Sprecher, 1986) that were not developed to assess obsessive thinking about a
   484→432 loved one specifically. Dedicated instruments, combined with experience-sampling methods and
   485→433 behavioural measures, would strengthen the evidence base considerably. These limitations inform the
   486→434 priorities for future research outlined below.

   524→449 11 Conclusions
   525→450 Obsessive thinking about a loved one is a defining feature of romantic love, yet research on it has
   526→451 accumulated in a piecemeal fashion across disciplines that rarely speak to one another. This review
   527→452 has used an Eight Perspectives approach to bring that scattered literature into a single structure,
   528→453 clarify what is known, and make visible what is not. The phenotypic expression of obsessive thinking
   529→454 is reasonably well characterised; its mechanisms, evolutionary functions, and ecology are not.
   530→455 Addressing these gaps will require contributions from the evolutionary social and behavioural
   531→456 sciences, neuroscience, genetics, and cross-cultural research — disciplines that have, to date, largely
   532→457 overlooked this striking and ubiquitous feature of human mating psychology.
