    22‚Üí18 Abstract
    23‚Üí19 Background: There is growing evidence that cognitive dysfunction plays a role in the
    24‚Üí20 emergence and persistence of antisocial behavior, and it is increasingly implicated as relevant for
    25‚Üí21 risk assessment and treatment within the Risk-Need-Responsivity (RNR) framework. However,
    26‚Üí22 this evidence comes predominantly from prison and inpatient populations. Whether similar
    27‚Üí23 patterns hold in forensic outpatient settings remains largely unknown. Moreover, existing studies
    28‚Üí24 often rely on normative groups that are not matched for age, sex, and education, and tend to
    29‚Üí25 focus on single cognitive domains rather than administering comprehensive test batteries.
    30‚Üí26 Methods: This protocol paper describes the design and methods of the FOCUS study,
    31‚Üí27 which aims to map cognitive functioning in forensic outpatients and examine its associations
    32‚Üí28 with aggression and treatment outcomes. The study compares forensic outpatients (n = 100) to a
    33‚Üí29 normative group (n = 100, matched on sex, age, and educational level). All participants complete
    34‚Üí30 a comprehensive computerized neuropsychological test battery covering seven cognitive
    35‚Üí31 domains: intelligence, attention, language, executive functioning, social cognition, memory, and
    36‚Üí32 decision making. Aggression is assessed using both a self-report questionnaire (Reactive
    37‚Üí33 Proactive Questionnaire; RPQ) and a behavioral task (Competitive Reaction Time Task; CRTT).
    38‚Üí34 We have four research aims: (1) to compare the cognitive profile of forensic outpatients to a
    39‚Üí35 matched normative group; (2) to examine the correspondence between self-reported and task-
    40‚Üí36 based aggression; (3) to examine associations between neuropsychological test scores and
    41‚Üí37 aggression; and (4) to examine associations between neuropsychological test scores and
    42‚Üí38 treatment outcomes, including recidivism risk, treatment non-attendance, dropout, and actual
    43‚Üí39 recidivism.
    44‚Üí2
    45‚Üí
    46‚Üí40 Discussion: The FOCUS study is the first to administer a comprehensive
    47‚Üí41 neuropsychological test battery in a forensic outpatient sample with a matched normative
    48‚Üí42 comparison group. Findings are expected to advance understanding of cognitive functioning
    49‚Üí43 within the RNR framework and to inform more individualized forensic treatment planning.
    50‚Üí44 Introduction
    51‚Üí45 Studies from various disciplines such as neuropsychology, developmental psychology,
    52‚Üí46 and neuropsychiatry have demonstrated that cognitive functioning plays a role in the emergence
    53‚Üí47 and persistence of antisocial and offending behavior (1‚Äì5). The term 'offending' refers to
    54‚Üí48 behaviors that violate laws, regulations, or societal standards, which may result in legal
    55‚Üí49 consequences. In this study, we focus on individuals receiving forensic outpatient treatment,
    56‚Üí50 including those who have engaged in offending behavior as well as those considered at risk of
    57‚Üí51 doing so. Individuals in offender populations are more likely than the general population to
    58‚Üí52 experience cognitive difficulties, including impairments in attention, executive functions,
    59‚Üí53 memory, language, and social cognition (2,6‚Äì10). These difficulties are also reflected in the
    60‚Üí54 overrepresentation of conditions such as acquired brain injury, intellectual disability, ADHD, and
    61‚Üí55 externalizing disorders in offender populations (11‚Äì15).
    62‚Üí56 Within forensic mental health care, there is a growing awareness of the potential
    63‚Üí57 relevance of cognitive functioning within the Risk-Need-Responsivity framework (RNR) (16).
    64‚Üí58 The RNR-model remains the dominant framework shaping forensic treatment. This model
    65‚Üí59 describes three key principles of effective offender rehabilitation. First, the Risk principle
    66‚Üí60 emphasizes aligning treatment intensity and duration with the individual‚Äôs risk of recidivism.
    67‚Üí61 Second, the Need principle targets dynamic risk factors that contribute to offending behavior.
    68‚Üí3
    69‚Üí
    70‚Üí62 The third principle, Responsivity, focuses on tailoring interventions to individual characteristics
    71‚Üí63 that affect the success of treatment, such as learning style, motivation, or cognitive abilities.
    72‚Üí64 Regarding the risk principle, there is preliminary evidence that incorporating
    73‚Üí65 neurocognitive measures may improve the predictive value of risk assessment (17,18). Although
    74‚Üí66 current risk assessment tools incorporate diverse psychological and social criminogenic factors,
    75‚Üí67 their predictive accuracy remains modest, with area-under-the-curve values typically around 0.70
    76‚Üí68 (19). Cognitive functioning is thought to underlie several of the Central Eight criminogenic
    77‚Üí69 needs, the eight most consistent predictors of recidivism (20), by influencing the behavioral
    78‚Üí70 patterns and self-regulatory processes that these needs reflect. The Central Eight include history
    79‚Üí71 of antisocial behavior, antisocial personality patterns, antisocial attitudes, antisocial associates,
    80‚Üí72 and difficulties in the domains of family, work, leisure, and substance use. For example,
    81‚Üí73 executive functioning difficulties - including poor inhibition, working memory, and cognitive
    82‚Üí74 flexibility - are associated with the self-regulation problems, impulsivity, and difficulty learning
    83‚Üí75 from feedback that underlie antisocial behavior and substance abuse (20‚Äì22). Language and
    84‚Üí76 learning difficulties may also affect the ability to process feedback, understand the consequences
    85‚Üí77 of one's actions, and engage in pro-social communication (14,23). However, domains such as
    86‚Üí78 social cognition are not explicitly captured within the Central Eight framework, suggesting that
    87‚Üí79 current risk assessment practice may insufficiently account for the full range of cognitive
    88‚Üí80 difficulties relevant to offending behavior. Regarding the Responsivity principle, difficulties in
    89‚Üí81 cognitive functions like attention, memory, and language processing may hinder patients‚Äô
    90‚Üí82 treatment responsiveness by limiting their ability to follow structured interventions or engage
    91‚Üí83 meaningfully in treatment (24), potentially resulting in higher rates of no-show or dropout. In
    92‚Üí84 short, while the RNR-model and the Central Eight provide a robust framework for forensic
    93‚Üí4
    94‚Üí
    95‚Üí85 treatment, it remains unclear whether a more explicit integration of cognitive functioning would
    96‚Üí86 improve the accuracy of risk assessment or the effectiveness of treatment. The current study aims
    97‚Üí87 to address this question.
    98‚Üí88 Before examining how cognitive functioning relates to risk assessment and treatment
    99‚Üí89 outcomes, a basic empirical understanding of cognitive functioning in forensic outpatient
   100‚Üí90 populations is needed. The few studies conducted in forensic outpatients suggest cognitive
   101‚Üí91 difficulties may be less prominent than in prison and inpatient samples (25), though the
   102‚Üí92 outpatient population remains understudied (6). In addition, many studies use non-offending
   103‚Üí93 normative groups that are not matched for age, gender and education, while it is well known that
   104‚Üí94 these variables are related to test performance (26). Furthermore, few studies have administered
   105‚Üí95 comprehensive test batteries covering multiple cognitive domains. As a result, there is currently
   106‚Üí96 no clear picture of how cognitive functioning is distributed across forensic outpatients.
   107‚Üí97 Cognitive functioning is also thought to be relevant for understanding aggression, with
   108‚Üí98 difficulties in social cognition and executive functioning linked to hostile attributions and poor
   109‚Üí99 behavioral regulation (27‚Äì31). However, measuring aggression in forensic populations presents
   110‚Üí100 its own challenges. Most studies rely exclusively on self-report measures of aggression, which
   111‚Üí101 may underestimate true levels due to social desirability bias and limited self-insight (32,33).
   112‚Üí102 Behavioral tasks offer a complementary approach; however, correlations between self-reported
   113‚Üí103 and behaviorally measured aggression tend to be modest in general populations (r = .16-.42)
   114‚Üí104 (34‚Äì36), suggesting that these measures tap partially distinct aspects of aggression. Using both
   115‚Üí105 measures is therefore informative: it allows us to examine not only levels of aggression but also
   116‚Üí106 the degree to which self-report and behavioral assessment correlate.
   117‚Üí5
   118‚Üí
   119‚Üí107 The current study addresses these gaps by systematically assessing cognitive functioning
   120‚Üí108 in a sample of adult forensic outpatients and comparing their performance to an age, gender and
   121‚Üí109 education matched normative group without a criminal history. In addition, associations between
   122‚Üí110 cognitive test performance and aggression will be examined, as well as the potential of cognitive
   123‚Üí111 functioning to inform treatment-related outcomes including recidivism risk, changes in
   124‚Üí112 recidivism risk over the course of treatment, actual recidivism, no-shows, and dropout. By
   125‚Üí113 linking cognitive profiles to these outcomes, the study aims to enhance understanding of the role
   126‚Üí114 of cognitive functioning within the Risk-Need-Responsivity framework in forensic mental health
   127‚Üí115 care.
   128‚Üí116 More specifically, this study aims to answer four research questions:
   129‚Üí117 1. How does the cognitive functioning profile of forensic outpatients compare to a
   130‚Üí118 normative group without a criminal history?
   131‚Üí119 2. What is the correspondence between self-reported aggression and aggression measured
   132‚Üí120 using a behavioral task?
   133‚Üí121 3. Are neuropsychological test scores associated with levels of aggression?
   134‚Üí122 4. Are neuropsychological test scores associated with treatment related outcomes (i.e., risk
   135‚Üí123 of recidivism, changes in recidivism-risk over time, actual recidivism, dropout, and no-
   136‚Üí124 show)?
   137‚Üí125 Regarding RQ1, we hypothesize that forensic outpatients will more frequently show a
   138‚Üí126 deviant cognitive profile as determined using Multivariate Normative Comparisons (MNC), a
   139‚Üí127 method that evaluates whether an individual's pattern of test scores deviates significantly from a
   140‚Üí128 normative reference group. Based on prior literature, particular deviations are expected in
   141‚Üí129 executive functioning and social cognition. Regarding RQ2, we expect to find small to medium
   142‚Üí6
   143‚Üí
   144‚Üí130 (r = 0.20-0.40) correlations between self-reported aggression (Reactive Proactive Questionnaire;
   145‚Üí131 RPQ) (37) and task-based aggression (Competitive Reaction Time Task; CRTT) (38), indicating
   146‚Üí132 partial but not total overlap between the two measures. Regarding RQ3, we hypothesize that
   147‚Üí133 lower neuropsychological test performance will be associated with higher levels of aggression.
   148‚Üí134 Regarding RQ4, given the limited statistical power of these analyses, the following hypotheses
   149‚Üí135 are considered exploratory: lower cognitive test performance is expected to be associated with
   150‚Üí136 higher levels of dropout and no-show, higher recidivism risk, smaller reductions in recidivism
   151‚Üí137 risk during treatment, and higher levels of actual recidivism.
   152‚Üí138 Materials and Methods
   153‚Üí139 The FOCUS project was preregistered in four separate preregistrations to ensure clear
   154‚Üí140 specification of each research aim, with corresponding hypotheses and analysis plans
   155‚Üí141 (AsPredicted RQ1: #258783, RQ2: #258784, RQ3: #258785, RQ4: #258788).
   156‚Üí142 Study population
   157‚Üí143 The study consists of two participant groups. The first group is recruited at a Dutch
   158‚Üí144 outpatient forensic treatment center (N = 100). At this clinic, outpatients are treated who have
   159‚Üí145 displayed criminal behavior or are at risk to do so. General exclusion criteria for treatment at the
   160‚Üí146 treatment center are: 1) sensitivity to crisis, liability to act out dangerously towards oneself or
   161‚Üí147 others, requiring immediate intervention, e.g. suicidality, serious self-harm, psychosis, 2)
   162‚Üí148 substance abuse of a severity that requires clinical treatment or which therapists deem to need
   163‚Üí149 treatment before any other interventions take place, and 3) offenders who commit crimes from a
   164‚Üí150 psychosis are referred to a psychiatry center or clinic.
   165‚Üí151 The second group is a normative group without a criminal history (N = 100). The
   166‚Üí152 normative sample is recruited using frequency matching to ensure that the distribution of sex,
   167‚Üí7
   168‚Üí
   169‚Üí153 age (18-24, 25-34, 35-44, 45-54, 55-64, ‚â•65), and educational level (low, medium, high) is
   170‚Üí154 comparable between groups. If perfect cell-level matching cannot be achieved, overall group-
   171‚Üí155 level equivalence on these variables will be verified, and all analyses will statistically control for
   172‚Üí156 age, sex, and educational level to account for any differences. The normative group is recruited
   173‚Üí157 through social media, the networks of researchers and research assistants, and the snowballing
   174‚Üí158 approach.
   175‚Üí159 Inclusion and exclusion criteria
   176‚Üí160 All participants must have sufficient command of the Dutch language and must be 18
   177‚Üí161 years of age or older. Exclusion criteria for the FOCUS study are: intellectual disability, severe
   178‚Üí162 substance use disorder (defined as inability to abstain from alcohol or illicit drugs for 24 hours
   179‚Üí163 prior to assessment), and visual or auditory impairments that preclude participation. Eligibility is
   180‚Üí164 determined in consultation with the therapist. Participants are asked to refrain from alcohol and
   181‚Üí165 drugs in the 24 hours before assessment; self-reported compliance is verified in the general
   182‚Üí166 questionnaire.
   183‚Üí167 Procedures
   184‚Üí168 A neuropsychological test battery is administered using the testing platform Mindsware
   185‚Üí169 (39,40), a computerized assessment platform designed for standardized neuropsychological
   186‚Üí170 testing. The test battery was constructed based on a systematic selection procedure. Cognitive
   187‚Üí171 domains were included if they were rated as at least moderately important by a forensic
   188‚Üí172 neuropsychological expert panel and demonstrated a meta-analytic effect size of ‚â• 0.25 in
   189‚Üí173 offender populations [masked for blind review; masked for blind review]. Tests were selected
   190‚Üí174 based on meta-analytic effect sizes, expert ratings, suitability for digital administration, and
   191‚Üí175 psychometric properties. The final battery was reviewed by panels of forensic
   192‚Üí8
   193‚Üí
   194‚Üí176 neuropsychologists and clinical neuropsychological researchers. To minimize effect of fatigue
   195‚Üí177 and declined motivation, participants are assigned to one of three task orders (see Supplementary
   196‚Üí178 Table S1). The test administration is done by the principal researcher and/or a research assistant.
   197‚Üí179 Total administration time is 120-180 minutes. As neuropsychological testing may be experienced
   198‚Üí180 as demanding, participants are permitted to take breaks as needed or to complete the assessment
   199‚Üí181 across multiple sessions. During the test administration, the researcher stays present to answer
   200‚Üí182 questions. To ensure standardized administration, the researcher does not provide additional task
   201‚Üí183 instructions or explanations beyond repeating the on-screen instructions. The test administrator
   202‚Üí184 writes down observations or any technical difficulties that might occur.

   272‚Üí193 Other measures
   273‚Üí194 To measure risk of recidivism, the Forensic Outpatient Risk Evaluation (FORE) (59) is scored by
   274‚Üí195 the therapist at the start of the treatment and every subsequent six months. The FORE consists of
   275‚Üí196 17 items that are scored on a 5-point scale (0-4) and results in a total score between 0 and 68
   276‚Üí197 points, with higher scores indicating higher risk of recidivism.
   277‚Üí198 Actual recidivism will be retrieved from the WODC (Wetenschappelijk Onderzoek- en
   278‚Üí199 Documentatiecentrum), the research and documentation center of the Dutch Ministry of Justice,
   279‚Üí200 which maintains national records of criminal convictions and offenses. This will be retrieved 2.5
   280‚Üí201 years after completion of the data collection, to ensure sufficient follow up time. Researchers
   281‚Üí202 extracting WODC and patient file data will have access to identifying information (e.g., name,
   282‚Üí203 date of birth) necessary to match records to participants; this information will be removed and
   283‚Üí204 replaced with the participant's study code immediately after matching, consistent with the
   284‚Üí205 pseudonymization procedures described in the Data Management section.
   285‚Üí206 Treatment adherence will be measured by two indicators. First, the percentage no-shows
   286‚Üí207 will be calculated by dividing the number of no-shows by the number of planned sessions after
   287‚Üí208 the cognitive assessment. A session is categorized as a no-show if the patient cancels less than 24
   288‚Üí209 hours in advance or does not attend without notice or permission. Because participants are
   289‚Üí210 recruited at different stages of treatment, the number of post-assessment sessions will vary
   290‚Üí211 between participants; this variability is acknowledged as a limitation of the study design. Second,
   291‚Üí11
   292‚Üí
   293‚Üí212 outpatients will be categorized as either dropout (outpatients who terminated their treatment
   294‚Üí213 prematurely) or completers (outpatients who completed their treatment as planned).
   295‚Üí214 In addition, the following variables will be extracted from the electronic patient files:
   296‚Üí215 type of offences, type of treatment program, DSM-5 diagnosis, start date of treatment, and (if
   297‚Üí216 applicable) end date of treatment. This data will be extracted after recruitment is completed.
   298‚Üí217 Data management
   299‚Üí218 A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) was conducted prior to data collection.
   300‚Üí219 Data are stored on a secured file server located in the Netherlands, managed by the participating
   301‚Üí220 treatment center, which holds NEN 7510 certification for information security in healthcare.
   302‚Üí221 Data are pseudonymized; only researchers at the treatment center can link participant codes to
   303‚Üí222 personal identifiers. Access is restricted to project team members via role-based permissions and
   304‚Üí223 two-factor authentication.
   305‚Üí224 Statistical analysis
   306‚Üí225 The analyses will be conducted in R (60). Full analysis details, including data
   307‚Üí226 preprocessing, outlier handling, and assumption checks, are specified in the preregistrations
   308‚Üí227 (AsPredicted #258783, #258784, #258785, #258788). Assumptions for the intended analyses
   309‚Üí228 will be checked. In case of multiple comparisons, Bonferroni-Holm correction (RQ1) or False
   310‚Üí229 Discovery Rate (FDR) correction (RQ2, 3 and 4) will be applied.
   311‚Üí230 RQ1: Multivariate Normative Comparisons
   312‚Üí231 To compare the cognitive functioning of both groups, Multivariate Normative
   313‚Üí232 Comparisons (MNC) will be conducted. Our approach is based on previously described methods,
   314‚Üí233 applying MNC to identify deviant cognitive profiles at the individual level (61,62). The
   315‚Üí234 percentage of deviant profiles in the forensic and normative group will be compared using a chi-
   316‚Üí12
   317‚Üí
   318‚Üí235 square test or Fisher‚Äôs exact test. Multiple Case Series Analysis (MCSA) (63) will be conducted
   319‚Üí236 to test the hypothesis that forensic outpatients will more frequently show deviant performance on
   320‚Üí237 individual neuropsychological tests than individuals without a criminal history.
   321‚Üí238 RQ2: Correspondence aggression measures
   322‚Üí239 Pearson correlations will be analyzed between RPQ (total, reactive, proactive) and CRTT (total,
   323‚Üí240 reactive, proactive) scores.
   324‚Üí241 RQ3: Regression analyses ‚Äì aggression
   325‚Üí242 Multivariate multiple regression will be conducted with the four aggression outcomes
   326‚Üí243 (RPQ-reactive, RPQ-proactive, CRTT-reactive, CRTT-proactive) as dependent variables.
   327‚Üí244 Cognitive domain composites will be entered as primary predictors, followed by age, sex, and
   328‚Üí245 education as covariates.
   329‚Üí246 RQ4: Regression analyses ‚Äì treatment outcomes
   330‚Üí247 Separate regression analyses will be conducted for each treatment outcome, with
   331‚Üí248 cognitive domain composites as predictors (step 1) and age, sex, and education as covariates
   332‚Üí249 (step 2). Four treatment outcomes will be analyzed. First, baseline recidivism risk will be
   333‚Üí250 examined using linear regression with the FORE risk score as the dependent variable. Second,
   334‚Üí251 change in recidivism risk over the course of treatment will be examined using a linear mixed-
   335‚Üí252 effects model, with time (baseline vs. follow-up) as a within-subjects factor, a random intercept
   336‚Üí253 for participant, and time √ó cognitive composite interaction terms to test whether lower cognitive
   337‚Üí254 performance is related to smaller risk reduction. Third, treatment no-show will be examined
   338‚Üí255 using linear regression with percentage of missed appointments (after cognitive testing) as the
   339‚Üí256 outcome. Fourth, treatment dropout will be examined using logistic regression. Finally, actual
   340‚Üí257 recidivism will be examined at a later stage using survival analysis (Cox proportional hazards
   341‚Üí13
   342‚Üí
   343‚Üí258 regression), with time to recidivism since the start of treatment as the dependent variable, once
   344‚Üí259 sufficient follow-up time (2.5 years) has passed.
   345‚Üí260 Ethics approval and consent to participate
   346‚Üí261 The Medical Ethics Review Committee of Amsterdam University Medical Centers
   347‚Üí262 confirmed that the Medical Research Involving Human Subjects Act (WMO) does not apply to
   348‚Üí263 the FOCUS study (ref. no 2025.0517). The FOCUS project complies with the guidelines
   349‚Üí264 formulated by the Ethics Review Board (FMG-UvA), University of Amsterdam, The
   350‚Üí265 Netherlands, and has been approved by the aforementioned Ethics Review Board (ref. no FMG-
   351‚Üí266 10138).
   352‚Üí267 Outpatients who meet the inclusion and exclusion criteria, as assessed in consultation
   353‚Üí268 with their therapist, receive an information flyer/letter about the study. In the normative group,
   354‚Üí269 potential participants receive the flyer/letter either through a research assistant or they can
   355‚Üí270 download it online. If they express interest, a researcher or research assistant subsequently
   356‚Üí271 contacts them to provide further information and answer questions. Participants are given at least
   357‚Üí272 one week to consider their decision. Prior to participation, participants have the opportunity to
   358‚Üí273 review the information letter and ask questions, and subsequently, sign the digital informed
   359‚Üí274 consent form. For the outpatient group, it is made clear that their decision to participate does not
   360‚Üí275 influence their treatment. Participation in this study is entirely voluntary. Participants may

   363‚Üí278 Power and Sample Size
   364‚Üí279 For multivariate normative comparisons a large sample size (i.e. ‚â• 100) is preferred (61).
   365‚Üí280 Therefore, we aim to recruit 100 forensic outpatients and 100 participants in the normative
   366‚Üí14
   367‚Üí
   368‚Üí281 group. For the regression analyses (RQ3 and RQ4), no comparable regression-based effect size
   369‚Üí282 estimate is available in the literature; we therefore assume f¬≤ = 0.10 as a small-to-medium
   370‚Üí283 benchmark (64). Post-hoc power analysis (65) indicated adequate power for RQ3 (N = 200;
   371‚Üí284 power = .87) but limited power for RQ4 (N = 100; power = 0.50). RQ4 analyses should therefore
   372‚Üí285 be interpreted as exploratory and hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. Where
   373‚Üí286 necessary, domain composites may be merged to reduce the number of predictors and improve
   374‚Üí287 power, consistent with the preregistrations.
   375‚Üí288 Data collection started in May 2025 (normative group) and September 2025 (forensic
   376‚Üí289 group) and is planned to continue until the end of 2026, or until 100 participants are included in
   377‚Üí290 both groups. The first publication on study outcomes is expected in 2027.
   378‚Üí291 Missing Data
   379‚Üí292 All questions and tests in Mindsware are mandatory to ensure complete data collection
   380‚Üí293 and valid multivariate analysis. If variables are missing, they will be imputed using full
   381‚Üí294 information maximum likelihood (FIML) estimation in a multilevel framework, as recommended
   382‚Üí295 previously (66). FIML can be applied as long as sufficient information from other variables is
   383‚Üí296 available and the missingness is assumed to be at random. If FIML is not feasible for certain
   384‚Üí297 analyses, multiple imputation may be considered as a backup approach. Participants with fewer
   385‚Üí298 than 50% of cognitive test scores available will be excluded from analysis. Where appropriate,
   386‚Üí299 test scores will be adjusted for age, sex, and education level before computing MNC
   387‚Üí300 classification, consistent with normative correction procedures described previously (62).

   390‚Üí301 Discussion
   391‚Üí302 The FOCUS-study is the first to administer a comprehensive digital neuropsychological
   392‚Üí303 test battery in an outpatient forensic population and matched normative group. In addition, as the
   393‚Üí304 tests are all computerized, we can standardize instructions and test administration.
   394‚Üí305 The results of RQ1, which examines the cognitive functioning profile of forensic
   395‚Üí306 outpatients compared to a matched normative group, will give insight into the extent and nature
   396‚Üí307 of cognitive difficulties in the forensic outpatient population. Findings may reveal three broad
   397‚Üí308 patterns. First, cognitive difficulties may be widespread across multiple domains, which would
   398‚Üí309 argue for more comprehensive assessment as a routine part of forensic treatment planning.
   399‚Üí310 Second, difficulties may be concentrated in specific domains such as executive functioning or
   400‚Üí311 social cognition, which would support more targeted assessment protocols and help identify
   401‚Üí312 which cognitive functions are most relevant for treatment adherence and recidivism risk within
   402‚Üí313 the RNR framework. Third, cognitive functioning may be generally intact, which would
   403‚Üí314 challenge the idea that cognitive difficulties are common in this population. Such a finding could
   404‚Üí315 also suggest that previous studies have overestimated the magnitude of cognitive differences
   405‚Üí316 between offenders and non-offenders, as many previous studies compare groups that are not
   406‚Üí317 matched on age, sex, and education (6) and rarely control for other potential confounders such as
   407‚Üí318 intellectual disabilities, acquired brain injury, socioeconomic status or psychiatric diagnosis. The
   408‚Üí319 FOCUS study addresses several of these limitations by excluding individuals with intellectual
   409‚Üí320 disability, using a matched normative group, and measuring psychiatric diagnosis as a covariate.
   410‚Üí321 However, potential confounding from factors such as socioeconomic status cannot be fully
   411‚Üí322 excluded.
   412‚Üí16
   413‚Üí
   414‚Üí323 By linking cognitive test scores to aggression, no-show, dropout, and recidivism (risk)
   415‚Üí324 (RQ3 and RQ4), the FOCUS study contributes to application of the Risk-Need-Responsivity
   416‚Üí325 model. Despite incorporating diverse psychological and social criminogenic factors, current risk
   417‚Üí326 assessment tools show modest predictive accuracy (19,67). If cognitive functioning is found to
   418‚Üí327 predict risk of recidivism, these findings could support the inclusion of neuropsychological
   419‚Üí328 outcomes in forensic risk assessment instruments. If cognitive difficulties are associated with
   420‚Üí329 treatment no-show or dropout, they could serve as an early indicator of individuals at risk of
   421‚Üí330 disengaging from treatment. Conversely, if cognitive functioning does not predict recidivism risk
   422‚Üí331 or treatment dropout, this does not preclude its relevance for treatment planning. Even in the
   423‚Üí332 absence of predictive value, cognitive difficulties may still function as a responsivity factor,
   424‚Üí333 informing how treatment is delivered.
   425‚Üí334 The comparison between self-reported and behaviorally measured aggression (RQ2) will
   426‚Üí335 provide methodological guidance for future research. Rather than assuming either measure is
   427‚Üí336 superior, examining the convergence between self-reported and task-based aggression scores will
   428‚Üí337 clarify whether these measures capture overlapping or distinct aspects of aggression in forensic
   429‚Üí338 outpatients, informing which approach is most appropriate for future use.
   430‚Üí339 Taken together, the FOCUS-project will contribute to a more comprehensive
   431‚Üí340 understanding of cognitive functioning in forensic outpatients and its relevance for aggression
   432‚Üí341 and treatment-related outcomes. The findings have the potential to inform both clinical practice
   433‚Üí342 and research, ultimately contributing to more individualized and effective treatment of
   434‚Üí343 aggression and other antisocial behavior.
